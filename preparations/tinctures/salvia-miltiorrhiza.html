Salvia miltiorrhiza tincture for medicinal use

Salvia miltiorrhiza tincture is a concentrated liquid extract made from the roots of the Salvia miltiorrhiza plant, commonly known as Chinese red sage.

It is widely used in traditional Chinese medicine and herbalism for its purported health benefits. This tincture is valued for its potential to support cardiovascular health, improve circulation, and reduce inflammation. It is often taken internally in small doses to address conditions such as poor circulation and menstrual issues.

Additionally, it may be used externally for skin conditions and wound healing.

Health Benefits

Salvia miltiorrhiza tincture relieves chest pain by improving blood circulation and reducing oxidative stress in the cardiovascular system.

It lowers blood pressure through its vasodilatory effects, which help relax blood vessels and ease the workload on the heart. The tincture also reduces inflammation by inhibiting pro-inflammatory pathways, supporting overall heart health. Additionally, it lowers cholesterol levels by promoting the metabolism of lipids and preventing the buildup of arterial plaques.

Finally, it treats heart failure by enhancing cardiac function and improving oxygen delivery to heart tissues.

Bioactive Constituents

Salvia miltiorrhiza tincture contains flavonoids, which are known for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Saponins contribute to the tincture's ability to support cardiovascular health and immune function. Triterpenoids, another key component, exhibit anti-inflammatory and antitumor activities. Polyphenols in the tincture help in scavenging free radicals and protecting cells from oxidative stress.

Sesquiterpenoids further enhance the tincture's therapeutic potential by promoting blood circulation and reducing inflammation.

Plant Parts

Salvia miltiorrhiza tincture is primarily made from the roots of the plant, which contain the highest concentration of active compounds such as tanshinones.

The roots are typically harvested during the autumn season when their medicinal properties are most potent. While the leaves are sometimes used, they are less common and contribute fewer active components compared to the roots. The root extract is often combined with alcohol to create the tincture, enhancing its potency and shelf life.
