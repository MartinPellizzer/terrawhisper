What is jaundice and how does it affect your body?

Jaundice is a condition characterized by the yellowing of the skin and the whites of the eyes, caused by high levels of bilirubin in the blood.

Bilirubin is a yellow pigment that results from the normal breakdown of red blood cells. When the liver is unable to process bilirubin effectively, it accumulates in the bloodstream, leading to the visible yellow discoloration. Jaundice can be a sign of various underlying issues, such as liver disease, blockage of the bile ducts, or hemolytic anemia.

It can affect multiple systems in the body, including the liver, gallbladder, and digestive system, and may lead to more serious complications if left untreated.

Underlying causes and risk factors of jaundice

Jaundice, characterized by the yellowing of the skin and eyes, is primarily caused by an elevation in bilirubin levels in the blood.

This can occur due to increased bilirubin production, impaired liver function, or obstruction in the bile ducts. Underlying causes include liver diseases such as hepatitis, cirrhosis, or liver failure, which hinder the liver's ability to process bilirubin. Risk factors encompass alcohol abuse, viral hepatitis infections, gallstones, and certain genetic disorders like Gilbert's syndrome.

Additionally, newborns are at risk due to immature liver function, leading to physiological jaundice in the first few days of life.
