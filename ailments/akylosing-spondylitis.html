What is ankylosing spondylitis and how does it affect your body?

Ankylosing spondylitis is a type of inflammatory arthritis that primarily affects the spine, causing chronic inflammation of the joints and ligaments.

This condition leads to the fusion of the vertebrae, resulting in a rigid, inflexible spine that can make movement difficult. The inflammation often begins in the sacroiliac joints and can spread to other areas of the spine, causing pain and stiffness, especially in the lower back. It may also affect other parts of the body, including the eyes, lungs, and heart, leading to complications such as uveitis or reduced lung capacity.

Over time, the condition can significantly impact a person's quality of life, affecting mobility, posture, and overall physical function.

Underlying causes and risk factors of ankylosing spondylitis

Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) is primarily driven by genetic factors, with the HLA-B27 gene being the most significant known risk factor, although not all individuals with this gene develop the disease.

Environmental triggers, such as infections, may also play a role in initiating the inflammatory process in susceptible individuals. The condition is more common in men and typically begins in early adulthood, suggesting a complex interplay between genetics and environmental influences. Inflammation in the axial skeleton leads to the formation of new bone, resulting in spinal fusion and reduced mobility.

Other risk factors include a family history of the disease, smoking, and possibly gut microbiome imbalances, which may contribute to immune system dysregulation.
