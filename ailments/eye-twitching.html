What is eye twitching and how does it affect your body?

Eye twitching, also known as blepharospasm, is a common condition characterized by involuntary, repetitive contractions of the eyelid muscles, typically affecting the upper or lower eyelid.

It is often caused by factors such as stress, fatigue, lack of sleep, excessive caffeine intake, or eye strain from prolonged screen use. While generally harmless, persistent eye twitching can be a sign of more serious neurological conditions, such as essential tremor or Parkinson's disease, especially if it spreads to other parts of the body. The condition may lead to discomfort, irritation, or even temporary vision impairment if the twitching is severe or prolonged.

In most cases, reducing stress and improving lifestyle habits can help alleviate symptoms, though medical intervention may be necessary for chronic or severe cases.

Underlying causes and risk factors of eye twitching

Eye twitching, or blepharospasm, is often caused by a combination of neurological, physiological, and environmental factors.

Common underlying causes include stress, fatigue, and excessive caffeine or alcohol consumption, which can overstimulate the nervous system and lead to involuntary muscle contractions. Neurological conditions such as Parkinson's disease or multiple sclerosis may also contribute by affecting the brain's control over muscle movement. Additionally, eye twitching can be linked to dry eyes, eye strain from prolonged screen use, or nutritional deficiencies like magnesium or potassium.

While occasional twitching is usually harmless, persistent or severe cases may indicate a more serious underlying condition requiring medical evaluation.
