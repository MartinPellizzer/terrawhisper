10 Best Medicinal Preparations of Harpagophytum procumbens (Devil’S Claw)

Harpagophytum procumbens, also known as devil's claw, is a plant that has been used in traditional medicine for centuries, particularly in Southern Africa.

Its roots are used to make various medicinal preparations, such as tisanes and tinctures. A tisane, or herbal tea, is made by steeping dried or fresh devil's claw root in hot water. This soothing beverage is often used to alleviate digestive complaints, such as loss of appetite, nausea, and stomach pain.

A tincture, on the other hand, is a more concentrated extract of the plant, made by soaking the roots in alcohol or glycerin. Tinctures are typically taken in small doses, and are used to treat a wider range of ailments, including arthritis, muscular pain, and fever. Both tisanes and tinctures of Harpagophytum procumbens are believed to have anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, making them popular natural remedies for a variety of conditions.

However, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before starting any new medicinal regimen.

1. Dry extract

Harpagophytum Procumbens dry extract is a standardized form of the Devil's Claw plant, produced by drying and extracting the secondary metabolites from its roots and underground stems.

The main health benefits of this preparation include its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, making it a popular natural remedy for conditions such as arthritis, lower back pain, and musculoskeletal disorders. The roots and underground stems of Harpagophytum procumbens are the primary plant parts used in its preparation. To make the dry extract, the roots and underground stems are harvested, cleaned, chopped, and then subjected to a series of extraction processes using solvents such as ethanol or water.

The final product is a standardized extract that contains a consistent level of active compounds, such as harpagosides. When using Harpagophytum procumbens dry extract, it is important to follow the recommended dosage and not exceed it, as high doses can cause side effects such as diarrhea, stomach upset, and nausea. Additionally, it is not recommended for use during pregnancy or breastfeeding, and should be used with caution in individuals with gallstones or stomach ulcers.

It is also important to note that Harpagophytum procumbens may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), so it is essential to consult with a healthcare provider before using this preparation.
