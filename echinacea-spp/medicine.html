Echinacea spp. (Echinacea) Medicinal Guide

Echinacea spp., commonly known as coneflower, is a popular herbal remedy known for its ability to boost the immune system and reduce symptoms of the common cold and flu.

The main constituents of Echinacea include alkamides, cichoric acid, essential oils, and polysaccharides, which are thought to have anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and immune-stimulating properties. Preparations of Echinacea can be made from its above-ground parts or roots, and are available in various forms such as teas, tinctures, capsules, and ointments.

While Echinacea is generally considered safe, some people may experience side effects such as stomach upset, dizziness, and allergic reactions. It is important to note that Echinacea should not be taken by individuals with autoimmune diseases, as it can stimulate the immune system and worsen symptoms.

Additionally, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before taking Echinacea if you are pregnant, breastfeeding, or taking other medications.

Echinacea spp.

In rare instances, echinacea can cause an allergic reaction, resulting in difficulty breathing, temporary numbness or tingling in the mouth, and swelling of the throat or tongue.

Therefore, it is crucial to follow the recommended dosage and consult with a healthcare provider before taking echinacea, especially for those with allergies or underlying health conditions.

What are the precautions to take when using Echinacea spp. (Echinacea) medicinally?

Before using Echinacea spp.

for medicinal purposes, it's important to take some precautions such as consulting a healthcare provider first, as they can provide personalized advice. It's crucial to check for plant allergies, and avoid using Echinacea if you have a known allergy or if you are pregnant or breastfeeding. If you have an auto-immune disorder, Echinacea may not be safe for you, and it's essential to discontinue its use before surgery and closely monitor blood clotting. Limit usage to 8 weeks, avoid using it in children under 2, and always check for medication interactions.

When starting to use Echinacea, start with lower doses, stay hydrated, and observe for allergic reactions. If your immune system is suppressed, avoid using Echinacea, and do not consume alcohol while using it. Discontinue its use if a rash occurs, monitor blood sugar levels, and consult a doctor for infants. If you are on immunosuppressive drugs or using other herbal supplements, avoid using Echinacea without consulting a doctor.

Discontinue its use if no improvement is seen in 1 week.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Echinacea spp. medicinally.

  • 1. Consult a healthcare provider first: It's important to get professional advice before starting any new medicinal herb, as they can provide guidance based on your health history and current medications.
  • 2. Check for plant allergies: If you're allergic to other plants in the Asteraceae family (such as ragweed, chrysanthemums, or marigolds), you may also be allergic to Echinacea.
  • 3. Avoid in pregnancy or breastfeeding: Echinacea's effects on fetal development and infants are not well-studied, so it's best to err on the side of caution and avoid using it during pregnancy or breastfeeding.
  • 4. Do not use with auto-immune disorders: Echinacea can stimulate the immune system, which could worsen auto-immune conditions like rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, or multiple sclerosis.
  • 5. Discontinue before surgery: To avoid potential interactions with anesthesia or other medications, stop using Echinacea at least two weeks before a scheduled surgery.
  • 6. Monitor blood clotting closely: Echinacea may affect blood clotting, so if you have a bleeding disorder or are taking blood-thinning medications, monitor your condition closely.
  • 7. Limit usage to 8 weeks: Long-term use of Echinacea may lead to reduced effectiveness and potential liver toxicity, so it's recommended to limit usage to 8 weeks with a break of at least one week in between.
  • 8. Avoid in children under 2: Echinacea's safety and efficacy in children under 2 have not been established, so it's best to avoid giving it to them.
  • 9. Check for medication interactions: Echinacea can interact with various medications, including immunosuppressants, certain antihistamines, and some chemotherapy drugs, so consult a healthcare provider if you're taking any medications.
  • 10. Start with lower doses: To assess your tolerance and reduce the risk of adverse reactions, begin with a lower dose of Echinacea and gradually increase as needed.
