Taxonomy

Achillea millefolium, commonly known as yarrow, belongs to the plant kingdom, classified as Eukaryota. It falls within the phylum Angiosperms (or Magnoliophyta), indicating that it's a flowering plant. Among flowering plants, it is categorized as a Eudicot, which is a class of dicotyledonous plants.

In a more specific taxonomic context, yarrow is placed in the order Asterales, a diverse group that includes daisies and sunflowers. Yarrow's family is Asteraceae, one of the largest and most widespread plant families in the world, encompassing a wide array of daisy-like flowers.

The genus of yarrow is Achillea, a name rooted in Greek mythology, associated with the hero Achilles who, according to legend, used yarrow to treat wounds. Finally, at the species level, it is known as Achillea millefolium, emphasizing its unique and distinct characteristics within the Achillea genus.

Common Names

Achillea millefolium, commonly known as yarrow, goes by various names due to its distinctive characteristics and historical uses. This herbaceous plant features clusters of small, white or pink flowers and finely divided leaves. It's often recognized as "Milfoil" or "Thousand-leaf" because of its abundant and finely segmented foliage.

One historical alias, "Soldier's woundwort," hints at its role in battlefield medicine. Yarrow was traditionally employed to treat wounds, hence the name that emphasizes its relevance to soldiers. Another practical use gave rise to the name "Nosebleed plant." Yarrow can constrict blood vessels, aiding in the cessation of nosebleeds.

The moniker "Old man's pepper" could be attributed to its spicy aroma, somewhat reminiscent of pepper. The term "Staunchweed" underlines its historical function as a hemostatic agent, effectively stopping bleeding. Its Latin name, "millefolium," means "thousand leaves," which is the origin of several of its common names, like "Thousand-leaf."

"Sanguinary" also highlights its historical association with blood due to its Latin root, "sanguis," meaning blood. "Western yarrow" distinguishes the plant's regional occurrence, indicating its presence in western parts of its native range. Lastly, "Knight's milfoil" might allude to the historical use by knights for wound treatment, aligning with its association with soldiers and battlefield care.

Here's a list of the most common names of Achillea millefolium with a brief description for each name.

  • Common yarrow: A widely recognized herb with clusters of small white or pink flowers and feathery leaves, often used in traditional medicine.
  • Milfoil: Another name for yarrow, describing its finely divided leaves.
  • Thousand-leaf: Referring to the plant's highly divided and numerous leaves.
  • Soldier's woundwort: Historically used to treat wounds on the battlefield, as its scientific name "millefolium" means "thousand leaves" in Latin.
  • Nosebleed plant: Called so due to its traditional use to help stop nosebleeds, as it can constrict blood vessels.
  • Old man's pepper: A nickname possibly derived from its spicy aroma, reminiscent of pepper.
  • Staunchweed: Named for its traditional use to staunch or stop bleeding.
  • Sanguinary: Derived from "sanguis," meaning blood, highlighting its historical use in treating bleeding wounds.
  • Western yarrow: A regional variation of the common name yarrow, indicating its occurrence in the western parts of its native range.
  • Knight's milfoil: Possibly named after knights who might have used it for treating wounds on the battlefield, in line with the name "soldier's woundwort."
