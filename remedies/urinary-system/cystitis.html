Cystitis: Causes, Medicinal Herbs and Herbal Preparations

Cystitis is a common urinary tract infection (UTI) characterized by inflammation of the bladder, often causing symptoms such as painful urination, frequent urination, and blood in the urine.

Typically caused by bacterial infections, especially E. coli, cystitis can also be triggered by factors like poor hygiene, kidney stones, or certain medical conditions. If left untreated, cystitis can lead to serious health complications, including kidney damage and sepsis. To alleviate cystitis symptoms, herbal remedies like uva ursi, juniper berries, and marshmallow root can be used in teas, capsules, or infusions, while also practicing good hygiene and staying hydrated.

However, it is crucial to consult a healthcare professional before using medicinal herbs, especially if pregnant or breastfeeding, as some may interact with medications or exacerbate conditions.

This article explains in detail what are the causes of cystitis, what medicinal herbs to use to relieve this problem and how to prepare these herbs to get the best results.

What are the main causes of cystitis?

The main causes of cystitis are bacterial infections, particularly Escherichia coli (E. coli), which account for approximately 80-90% of cases, often entering the urinary tract through the urethra during sexual intercourse or through poor hygiene.

Non-bacterial causes include interstitial cystitis, a condition characterized by chronic bladder pain and inflammation, and foreign body irritants such as spermicides, bubble baths, and tight clothing. Other risk factors include age, with cystitis more common in women over 40, and certain medical conditions, such as diabetes, kidney stones, and neurological disorders. Additionally, catheterization, urinary tract abnormalities, and genetic predisposition can also contribute to cystitis.

Hormonal changes, such as those experienced during pregnancy or menopause, can also increase the risk of cystitis due to changes in urine pH and bladder function.

  • UTI: Urinary tract infections are the most common cause of cystitis, typically resulting from bacteria, such as Escherichia coli, entering the urinary tract and multiplying, leading to inflammation and infection of the bladder.
  • E. coli: Escherichia coli, a common gut bacterium, can enter the urinary tract through the urethra and cause cystitis, often due to its ability to adhere to and colonize the bladder and urinary tract tissues.
  • Kidney stones: Kidney stones can obstruct the flow of urine, leading to a buildup of bacteria and toxins in the bladder, which can cause inflammation and infection, resulting in cystitis.
  • Diabetes: Diabetes can increase the risk of cystitis by altering the immune system's ability to fight off infections, as well as by increasing the concentration of glucose in the urine, which can promote bacterial growth.
  • Hormonal changes: Hormonal changes during pregnancy, menopause, or using hormonal birth control can alter the pH of the urine, making it more susceptible to bacterial growth and increasing the risk of cystitis.
  • Weakened immune system: A weakened immune system, often resulting from chronic illness, chemotherapy, or immunosuppressive medications, can impair the body's ability to fight off infections, increasing the risk of cystitis.
  • Catheter use: Using a urinary catheter can increase the risk of cystitis by providing a pathway for bacteria to enter the urinary tract and colonize the bladder.
  • Anal sex: Engaging in anal sex can increase the risk of cystitis by introducing bacteria from the anus into the urinary tract.
  • Spinal cord injury: Spinal cord injuries can result in incomplete bladder emptying, leading to a buildup of bacteria and toxins in the bladder, which can cause inflammation and infection, resulting in cystitis.
