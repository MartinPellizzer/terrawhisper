What is benign prostatic hypertrophy and how it affects your life?

Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH) is a common condition that affects millions of men worldwide.

Characterized by an enlarged prostate gland, BPH can significantly impact daily life, causing discomfort, inconvenience, and even embarrassment. The symptoms of BPH can be debilitating, making it difficult to perform simple tasks such as getting out of bed or using the bathroom without hesitation.

For instance, frequent nighttime awakenings to use the restroom can disrupt sleep patterns, leaving one feeling fatigued and groggy during the day. Additionally, a slow stream or difficulty urinating can make even the most mundane activities, like taking a shower or going for a walk, feel like an insurmountable challenge. Furthermore, BPH can also cause urinary retention, leading to painful attempts to force urine out of the body, and may even increase the risk of kidney damage if left untreated.

As a result, men with BPH often experience reduced quality of life, feeling anxious, depressed, or withdrawn due to their inability to control their bladder function.

What are the main causes of benign prostatic hypertrophy?

The main causes of benign prostatic hypertrophy are a combination of hormonal and age-related changes in the body.

As men age, their levels of dihydrotestosterone (DHT), a potent form of testosterone, increase, leading to stimulation of prostate cell growth. Additionally, the normal aging process leads to a decline in the balance between androgens and estrogens, causing an imbalance that can stimulate prostate cell proliferation. Furthermore, genetics play a role, with some men being more susceptible to developing BPH due to inherited traits.

Other contributing factors include obesity, physical inactivity, and certain medical conditions such as high blood pressure and diabetes, which can increase the risk of developing BPH.

The most common causes of benign prostatic hypertrophy are listed below.

  • Aging: As men age, their prostate gland naturally grows and can lead to BPH symptoms.
  • Hormonal Imbalance: An imbalance of hormones, particularly dihydrotestosterone (DHT), can stimulate the growth of the prostate gland.
  • Family History: If a family member has a history of BPH, an individual may be more likely to develop it as well.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese has been linked to increased risk of developing BPH.
  • Low Testosterone: Low levels of testosterone can contribute to BPH symptoms.
  • Genetics: Certain genetic predispositions can increase the likelihood of developing BPH.
  • Circulatory Problems: Reduced blood flow to the prostate gland can lead to an overgrowth of tissue and symptoms.
  • Nutritional Factors: A diet high in saturated fats, refined sugars, and processed foods may contribute to inflammation and cell growth.
  • Chronic Prostatitis: Chronic inflammation of the prostate gland due to bacterial infection or other factors can lead to BPH.
  • Erectile Dysfunction: Men with erectile dysfunction (ED) are more likely to develop BPH.
