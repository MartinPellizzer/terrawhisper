10 best herbal teas for high cholesterol

Herbal teas for high cholesterol are caffeine-free beverages made from various plants, flowers, and spices, which can help manage high cholesterol levels.

These teas often contain beneficial compounds that inhibit cholesterol absorption, increase bile excretion, and promote overall heart health. Some popular herbal teas for high cholesterol include hawthorn, holy basil, and black or green tea. For example, hawthorn tea is rich in antioxidants that help reduce LDL (bad) cholesterol, while holy basil tea has anti-inflammatory properties that may lower triglycerides and cholesterol levels.

Regularly consuming these teas can contribute to improved heart health, reduced risk of cardiovascular diseases, and an overall enhanced quality of life.

The following article describes in detail the most important teas for high cholesterol, including medicinal properties, parts of herbs to use, and recipes for preparations.

1. White tea

White tea helps with high cholesterol because it contains high levels of antioxidants, particularly catechins, which have been shown to inhibit the absorption of cholesterol in the body.

These antioxidants also help to reduce the oxidative stress that can damage blood vessels and lead to plaque buildup. Furthermore, white tea has anti-inflammatory properties, which can help to reduce inflammation in the arteries and improve overall heart health.

Regular consumption of white tea may help to lower both LDL (bad) cholesterol and total cholesterol levels, reducing the risk of heart disease and other complications associated with high cholesterol.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in white tea that aid with high cholesterol.

  • Anti-oxidative properties: White tea contains high levels of antioxidants, such as flavonoids and polyphenols, which help to reduce oxidative stress and inflammation in the body, thereby preventing the oxidation of LDL cholesterol and reducing the risk of plaque buildup in the arteries.
  • Anti-inflammatory properties: White tea has potent anti-inflammatory effects, which can help to reduce inflammation in the cardiovascular system, thereby reducing the risk of inflammation-induced plaque buildup and lowering cholesterol levels.
  • Anti-atherogenic properties: White tea has been shown to inhibit the formation of atherosclerotic plaques, which are caused by the buildup of cholesterol and other substances in the arteries, by reducing the oxidation of LDL cholesterol and preventing the formation of foam cells.
  • Hypolipidemic properties: White tea has been found to have hypolipidemic effects, which means it can help to lower lipid levels in the blood, including cholesterol and triglycerides, by increasing the breakdown and excretion of cholesterol.
  • Vasodilatory properties: White tea has vasodilatory effects, which means it can help to widen blood vessels and improve blood flow, thereby reducing blood pressure and reducing the risk of cardiovascular disease, which is often associated with high cholesterol levels. The active constituents responsible for these properties include catechins, theaflavins, thearubigins, and other polyphenols.

The list below shows the primary parts of white tea used to make teas for high cholesterol.

  • Leaves: Rich in antioxidants, white tea leaves can help reduce the absorption of cholesterol in the body.
  • Buds: High in catechins, white tea buds can help lower LDL (bad) cholesterol levels and increase HDL (good) cholesterol levels.
  • Stems: Containing flavonoids, white tea stems can help prevent the oxidation of LDL cholesterol, reducing the risk of plaque buildup in arteries.
  • Flowers: With anti-inflammatory properties, white tea flowers can help improve blood circulation and reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease.
