What is high cholesterol and how it affects your life?

High cholesterol is a condition where you have too much of a fatty substance called cholesterol in your blood.

This excess cholesterol can lead to the formation of plaques in your arteries, causing them to narrow and harden, a condition known as atherosclerosis. This can result in various negative consequences on your health and quality of life.

For instance, high cholesterol can increase your risk of heart disease, heart attacks, and strokes, which can be life-threatening. Moreover, it can lead to peripheral artery disease, where your limbs, particularly your legs, don't receive enough blood and oxygen, causing pain, cramping, and difficulty walking. Additionally, high cholesterol can cause gallstones and even lead to memory loss, dementia, and depression.

Overall, uncontrolled high cholesterol can significantly impair your health, well-being, and longevity, making it essential to monitor and manage it effectively.

What are the main causes of high cholesterol?

The main causes of high cholesterol are an unhealthy diet, lack of physical activity, obesity, smoking, and genetic factors.

An unhealthy diet that is high in saturated and trans fats, cholesterol, and refined sugars can increase cholesterol levels in the blood. Lack of physical activity can also lead to high cholesterol levels, as regular exercise helps to increase the "good" HDL cholesterol and reduce the "bad" LDL cholesterol.

Obesity is another major cause of high cholesterol, as excess body weight can increase LDL cholesterol and decrease HDL cholesterol. Smoking damages the walls of the blood vessels, making them more prone to accumulating cholesterol and other deposits.

Finally, genetic factors can also play a role in high cholesterol, as some people may have a genetic predisposition to high cholesterol levels.

The most common causes of high cholesterol are listed below.

  • Poor diet: Consuming a diet high in saturated and trans fats, cholesterol, and refined sugars can increase cholesterol levels.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese can lead to high cholesterol levels, as excess weight can increase the production of cholesterol in the body.
  • Lack of physical activity: A sedentary lifestyle can contribute to high cholesterol levels by reducing the body's ability to remove LDL (bad) cholesterol from the bloodstream.
  • Smoking: Smoking damages the walls of the arteries, making them more prone to accumulating cholesterol and fat, which can lead to high cholesterol levels.
  • Diabetes: High blood sugar levels can lead to increased production of cholesterol in the body, which can result in high cholesterol levels.
  • Genetics: Inherited genetic conditions such as familial hypercholesterolemia can cause high cholesterol levels, even in people who follow a healthy lifestyle.
  • Age: Cholesterol levels tend to rise with age, as the body produces more cholesterol as it gets older.
  • Gender: Men are more likely to have high cholesterol levels than women, especially after menopause.
  • Certain medications: Some medications, such as steroids, beta blockers, and diuretics, can increase cholesterol levels.
  • Stress: High levels of stress can increase cholesterol levels by affecting the body's hormonal balance and leading to unhealthy lifestyle choices, such as overeating and lack of exercise.
