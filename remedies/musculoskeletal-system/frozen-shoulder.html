What is frozen shoulder and how it affects your life?

Frozen shoulder, also known as adhesive capsulitis, is a debilitating condition that causes stiffness and pain in the shoulder joint.

It can affect anyone, but it's most common in people over 40 years old. Living with frozen shoulder can be a nightmare, as even simple tasks become daunting challenges. For instance, getting dressed or brushing your hair requires a significant amount of effort and pain, making everyday grooming a laborious task.

Going up or down stairs becomes an exercise in agony, and even sleeping can be uncomfortable due to the constant ache. Social activities are also impacted, as simply reaching out to shake hands or hug loved ones becomes a painful experience. Furthermore, frozen shoulder can make it difficult to drive, cook, or engage in hobbies that require normal range of motion, leading to feelings of frustration and isolation. The condition can also affect work performance, making it challenging to perform daily job tasks or even attend meetings without discomfort.

Overall, frozen shoulder is a condition that can significantly alter one's quality of life, leaving individuals feeling limited, restricted, and miserable.

What are the main causes of frozen shoulder?

The main causes of frozen shoulder, also known as adhesive capsulitis, are still not fully understood, but several factors have been identified that may contribute to its development.

These include inflammation, scarring, and stiffness in the connective tissue surrounding the shoulder joint, which can lead to a reduction in mobility and range of motion. Other potential triggers include injury or trauma to the shoulder, diabetes, thyroid disorders, cardiovascular disease, and surgery, particularly shoulder surgery. Additionally, genetic predisposition, age (frozen shoulder is more common in people over 40), and hormonal changes may also play a role.

In some cases, frozen shoulder can occur as a result of an underlying medical condition or as a side effect of certain medications.

The most common causes of frozen shoulder are listed below.

  • Idiopathic Frozen Shoulder: No known underlying cause or reason, making up about 70% of all cases.
  • Trauma or Injury: A direct blow to the shoulder or surrounding area can cause inflammation and adhesion of the tendons, leading to stiffness.
  • Disease (Diabetes, Thyroid Disorder, etc.): Certain medical conditions such as diabetes, thyroid disorders, and cardiovascular disease can increase the risk of developing frozen shoulder.
  • Surgery or Anesthesia: Patients who have undergone surgery or anesthesia may experience a higher risk of developing frozen shoulder due to the stress on the shoulder joint.
  • Shoulder Instability: Shoulder instability can cause inflammation and adhesion in the tendons, leading to stiffness.
  • Overuse or Repetitive Strain Injury: Repeatedly performing activities that involve heavy lifting, bending, or twisting can put excessive strain on the shoulder joints and tendons, increasing the risk of frozen shoulder.
  • Cervical Spine Problems: Conditions such as cervical spine stenosis or herniated discs in the neck can cause referred pain and stiffness in the shoulder area.
  • Muscle Imbalance or Weakness: Muscle imbalances or weaknesses in the shoulder region can lead to altered movement patterns, resulting in inflammation and adhesion of the tendons.
  • Lack of Exercise or Sedentary Lifestyle: A lack of regular exercise and a sedentary lifestyle can contribute to stiffness and decreased range of motion in the shoulder joints.
  • Hormonal Changes (Pregnancy, Menopause, etc.): Hormonal fluctuations during certain stages of life, such as pregnancy or menopause, can affect joint stability and increase the risk of developing frozen shoulder.
