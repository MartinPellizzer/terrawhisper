1. Scrophularia nodosa

Figwort tinctures helps with osteoporosis because it is rich in bioactive compounds that stimulate bone growth and density.

The tannins present in figwort have been shown to inhibit the activity of enzymes involved in bone resorption, while the flavonoids enhance collagen synthesis, leading to improved bone strength and reduced risk of fractures.

Additionally, figwort's anti-inflammatory properties may help reduce joint pain and inflammation associated with osteoporosis, making it a valuable adjunctive therapy for managing this condition.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in figwort tinctures that aid with osteoporosis.

  • Anti-inflammatory and anti-arthritic compounds: Figwort contains bioactive compounds that reduce inflammation and alleviate arthritic pain, which can be particularly beneficial for osteoporosis patients who may experience joint pain and swelling.
  • Bone-strengthening flavonoids: Figwort tinctures contain flavonoids that have been shown to improve bone density by stimulating osteoblasts (bone-building cells) and inhibiting osteoclasts (bone-resorbing cells), which can help prevent further bone loss and fractures associated with osteoporosis.
  • Antioxidant and free radical scavenging activities: Figwort's antioxidant properties help protect bones from oxidative stress and damage caused by free radicals, which can contribute to the development of osteoporosis and other degenerative bone diseases.

The list below shows the primary parts of figwort used to make tinctures for osteoporosis.

  • Roots: Rich in bioactive compounds, including alkaloids and glycosides, which have been shown to help regulate bone metabolism and density.
  • Rhyzomes: Contains saponins, flavonoids, and other bioactives that may help stimulate collagen synthesis and improve bone health.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic figwort for osteoporosis.

  1. Harvest scrophularia nodosa roots in the morning after dew has evaporated and dry them thoroughly within 24 hours.
  2. Cut dried scrophularia nodosa roots into small pieces and combine with 60% vodka or grain alcohol at a ratio of 1:3.
  3. Allow the mixture to macerate for 14-21 days, shaking the container daily, then strain through cheesecloth into dark glass bottles.
  4. Filter the resulting tincture through coffee filters or fine-mesh strainers to remove any remaining particles and sediment.
  5. Store the final figwort tincture in a cool dark place, protected from light and heat, for up to 2 years before use as recommended dosages of 20-30 drops twice daily.
