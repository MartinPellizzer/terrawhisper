What is bunion and how it affects your life?

A bunion is a painful and unsightly bump that forms at the base of the big toe, causing significant discomfort and disruption to daily life.

The condition can make even simple activities like walking, standing, or dressing a struggle. For instance, wearing shoes becomes a daunting task as they rub against the bony protrusion, exacerbating the pain and swelling. Simple chores like vacuuming or mopping can become exhausting due to the constant throbbing in the foot.

Even everyday tasks like going for a walk or playing with children are hindered by the limited mobility and sharp pain. Moreover, the appearance of the bunion can be embarrassing and affect one's self-esteem, making it difficult to feel confident in social situations or at work. Furthermore, the condition can also impact mental health, leading to feelings of frustration and anxiety as simple tasks become daunting challenges.

As a result, people with bunions often find themselves avoiding activities they once enjoyed, feeling isolated and limited by their condition.

What are the main causes of bunion?

The main causes of bunion are a combination of genetic and biomechanical factors.

One of the primary causes is the inheritance of a tendency to develop bunions, as some people may be more prone to this condition due to their family history. Another significant factor is the way our feet function, particularly the way we walk and move.

For instance, people who have flat feet or high arches are more likely to develop bunions over time. Additionally, poor foot mechanics, such as overpronation (when the foot rolls inward) or supination (when the foot rolls outward), can also contribute to the development of bunions.

Furthermore, shoes that don't fit properly, especially those with narrow toes boxes or high heels, can put additional pressure on the toe joints and exacerbate bunion formation.

The most common causes of bunion are listed below.

  • Genetics: Bunions can be inherited from your parents, and if you have a family history of bunions, you may be more likely to develop one.
  • Abnormal Foot Mechanics: Abnormalities in foot mechanics, such as overpronation or supination, can put additional stress on the joints and lead to the development of a bunion.
  • Wearing High Heels: Wearing high heels regularly can cause the toes to be squeezed into an unnatural position, leading to pressure and irritation on the joint that forms a bunion.
  • Wearing Narrow or Tight Shoes: Wearing shoes that are too narrow or tight can put pressure on the joint, causing it to become inflamed and develop into a bunion.
  • Obesity: Excess weight can put additional stress on the joints, including those in the foot, making it more likely for a bunion to develop.
  • Trauma: Injuries to the foot or ankle, such as stubbing your toe or spraining your ankle, can cause inflammation and irritation that can lead to the development of a bunion.
  • Arthritis: Certain types of arthritis, such as rheumatoid arthritis or osteoarthritis, can increase the risk of developing a bunion.
  • Poor Foot Biomechanics: Abnormalities in the way the foot functions, such as a condition called pes cavus (high arch), can put additional stress on the joints and lead to the development of a bunion.
  • Hormonal Changes: Hormonal changes during pregnancy or menopause can cause swelling and inflammation in the feet, making it more likely for a bunion to develop.
  • Aging: As we age, the tendons and ligaments in our feet can become less flexible, leading to an increased risk of developing bunions.
