What is ankylosing spondylitis and how it affects your life?

Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) is a chronic autoimmune condition that causes inflammation in the joints of the spine, leading to stiffness, pain, and eventually, fusion of the vertebrae.

This debilitating disease affects not only physical mobility but also daily life, making even the simplest tasks a challenge. For example, getting out of bed can be an ordeal as the body struggles to straighten up due to limited flexibility in the neck and back. Simple activities like walking or going for a run become impossible due to severe back pain and stiffness.

Even dressing and grooming can be difficult as the fingers and hands may swell and ache. Furthermore, AS can lead to fatigue, making it hard to maintain employment or attend social events, isolating individuals from loved ones and community. It can also impact mental health, causing depression and anxiety due to feelings of hopelessness and frustration with limited mobility. Additionally, AS can affect one's appearance, as the condition can cause hunching and stooping, leading to low self-esteem and body image issues.

Overall, ankylosing spondylitis significantly affects every aspect of life, making even the most mundane tasks a daunting challenge.

What are the main causes of ankylosing spondylitis?

The main causes of ankylosing spondylitis are still not fully understood, but research suggests that it is a complex interaction between genetic and environmental factors.

It is believed that people who have a family history of the condition or share similar genetic markers may be more likely to develop ankylosing spondylitis. Additionally, certain genes such as HLA-B27 play a significant role in increasing the risk of developing the condition.

Environmental triggers, such as viral infections like Reiter's syndrome, may also trigger the onset of symptoms in individuals who are genetically predisposed. Furthermore, other factors such as smoking and obesity may also contribute to the development and progression of ankylosing spondylitis.

Overall, a combination of genetic and environmental factors likely plays a role in the development of this chronic inflammatory condition.

The most common causes of ankylosing spondylitis are listed below.

  • Genetics: AS is associated with specific genetic markers, particularly HLA-B27, which increases an individual's risk of developing the condition.
  • Family History: Having a family history of AS or other spondyloarthritis conditions increases one's likelihood of developing AS.
  • Infection: Certain bacterial infections, such as Chlamydia trachomatis, may trigger AS in some individuals.
  • Viral Infections: Viral infections, such as Epstein-Barr virus and human herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8), have been linked to the development of AS.
  • Environmental Factors: Exposure to environmental toxins or stress can contribute to the development of AS in susceptible individuals.
  • Imbalances in Gut Bacteria: Research suggests that an imbalance in gut bacteria, also known as dysbiosis, may play a role in the development of AS.
  • Smoking: Smoking has been shown to increase the risk and severity of AS, possibly due to its impact on inflammation and tissue damage.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese can contribute to inflammation and joint damage in individuals with AS.
  • Age: AS is most common among young adults, typically between the ages of 15 and 30.
  • Hormonal Imbalance: Hormonal fluctuations, particularly during puberty and menopause, may trigger AS in some individuals.
