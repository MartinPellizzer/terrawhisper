What is stye and how it affects your life?

A stye, also known as a hordeolum, is a painful and swollen lump that forms on the eyelid, typically at the base of the eyelashes.

The mere presence of a stye can be debilitating, as it often causes discomfort, redness, and swelling in the affected area. This can lead to difficulty opening or closing the eye, making everyday activities such as reading, watching TV, or even sleeping a challenge. Moreover, the appearance of a stye can also have negative effects on one's self-esteem, causing anxiety and embarrassment when interacting with others.

For instance, an individual with a stye may avoid social gatherings or cancel plans to hide their affected eye. Styes can also disrupt daily routines, making it difficult to drive, work, or engage in hobbies that require clear vision. Furthermore, the constant itching and discomfort caused by a stye can lead to fatigue, headaches, and irritability.

In extreme cases, styes can even affect one's ability to perform simple tasks, such as applying makeup or wearing contact lenses, making daily life feel like an uphill battle.

What are the main causes of stye?

The main causes of stye are a combination of factors that lead to the blockage and inflammation of the oil glands or sweat glands on the eyelid.

One of the primary causes is bacterial infection, which can occur when bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus enter the eyelid through small cuts or abrasions and multiply rapidly, causing inflammation and pus formation. Another contributing factor is blockage of the oil glands due to a buildup of dead skin cells, sebum, and other debris, which can lead to clogged pores and inflammation.

Additionally, hormonal changes, stress, fatigue, and poor eyelid hygiene can also contribute to the development of stye by weakening the immune system and making the eyelid more susceptible to infection.

The most common causes of stye are listed below.

  • Blocked oil glands: The oil glands on the eyelid produce sebum, which can become trapped and clog the gland's opening, leading to inflammation and a sty.
  • Bacterial infection: Bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus can infect the oil gland or hair follicle, causing the formation of a pustule or abscess that appears as a sty.
  • Poor eye hygiene: Failing to clean the eyes properly can lead to a buildup of bacteria and other debris, increasing the risk of developing a sty.
  • Makeup and mascara use: Sharing makeup or using expired products can introduce bacteria into the eyes and increase the risk of styes.
  • Allergic reactions: Allergies to contact lens solutions, eye drops, or other substances can cause inflammation and irritation in the eyelid, leading to a sty.
  • Dry eye syndrome: Dryness and irritation caused by dry eye syndrome can lead to the formation of a sty as the body tries to protect the eyes from further damage.
  • Seborrheic dermatitis: This common skin condition causes redness and flaking on the eyelids, which can increase the risk of developing a sty.
  • Rosacea: A skin condition that affects the face, including the eyelids, rosacea can cause inflammation, redness, and acne-like bumps.
  • Hormonal changes: Hormonal fluctuations during pregnancy or menstruation can lead to increased oil production in the eyes, which can clog glands and cause styes.
  • Weakened immune system: A weakened immune system due to illness or medication can make it harder for the body to fight off bacterial infections that can cause styes.
