1. Yarrow tincture

Yarrow tincture helps with dry skin because it contains anti-inflammatory and moisturizing properties.

Additionally, yarrow tincture is a natural emollient, helping to lock in moisture and keep the skin hydrated.

This makes it an ideal natural remedy for dry, flaky, and itchy skin, providing relief and comfort.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in yarrow tincture that aid with dry skin.

  • Astringent: Yarrow tincture's astringent property tightens and tones skin, reducing dryness and flakiness, due to its tannin content.
  • Anti-inflammatory: Yarrow tincture's anti-inflammatory property soothes irritated dry skin, reducing redness and inflammation, due to its lactone content.
  • Antimicrobial: Yarrow tincture's antimicrobial property protects dry skin from infection, promoting skin health, due to its sesquiterpene lactone content.
  • Emollient: Yarrow tincture's emollient property softens and moisturizes dry skin, improving skin texture, due to its volatile oil content.
  • Circulation-stimulating: Yarrow tincture's circulation-stimulating property increases blood flow to dry skin, promoting nutrient and oxygen supply, due to its azulene content.

The list below shows the primary parts of yarrow tincture used to make tinctures for dry skin.

  • Flowers: Rich in antioxidants, anti-inflammatory properties, and essential oils, yarrow flowers help soothe dry, irritated skin.
  • Leaves: Containing tannins and flavonoids, yarrow leaves provide astringent and emollient qualities, beneficial for dry skin.
  • Stems: High in volatile oils and bitter compounds, yarrow stems aid in skin hydration and support skin barrier function.
  • Rhyzomes: With high levels of azulene, yarrow rhyzomes offer anti-inflammatory and skin-soothing properties, ideal for dry skin.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic yarrow tincture for dry skin.

  1. Chop 2 ounces of dried yarrow flowers and leaves, placing them in a clean glass jar.
  2. Pour 16 ounces of 80-proof vodka or rum over the herbs, ensuring they are fully submerged.
  3. Seal the jar tightly and store it in a cool, dark place for 2-6 weeks, shaking it daily.
  4. Strain the tincture through cheesecloth into a clean jar or bottle, discarding the spent herbs.
  5. Label the tincture with the ingredients and date, storing it in a cool, dark location for up to a year.
