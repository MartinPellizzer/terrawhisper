What is mouth ulcers and how it affects your life?

Mouth ulcers, also known as canker sores, are small, shallow lesions that develop on the soft tissues inside your mouth or gums.

They can be white, gray, or yellow in color, surrounded by a red border. Mouth ulcers can cause significant discomfort, making it painful to talk, eat, drink, or brush your teeth.

In severe cases, they may even interfere with your ability to sleep or concentrate. The constant pain and discomfort can lead to a loss of appetite, weight loss, and fatigue.

Additionally, mouth ulcers can affect your social life, causing embarrassment and self-consciousness about your appearance, which can impact your confidence and overall quality of life.

What are the main causes of mouth ulcers?

The main causes of mouth ulcers are minor injuries to the mouth, stress or hormonal changes, nutritional deficiencies, and sensitivity to certain types of food or chemicals.

Minor injuries can occur from accidentally biting the inside of the cheek, braces or dentures, or even from harsh tooth brushing. Emotional stress and hormonal changes can also trigger the development of mouth ulcers. Additionally, a lack of essential vitamins and minerals, such as vitamin B12, iron, and folic acid, can make a person more prone to developing mouth ulcers.

Certain foods and chemicals, including coffee, chocolate, spicy foods, and acidic fruits, can also irritate the mouth and cause ulcers in some individuals.

The most common causes of mouth ulcers are listed below.

  • Mechanical trauma: Mouth ulcers can be caused by accidental bites, braces, dentures, or rough brushing. These ulcers are typically found on the sides of the tongue or inside of the cheek.
  • Nutritional deficiencies: Lack of essential vitamins, such as B-12, zinc, folate, or iron, can lead to mouth ulcers. A balanced diet and supplements, if recommended by a healthcare professional, can help prevent these ulcers.
  • Stress or hormonal changes: Emotional stress, hormonal fluctuations, and lack of sleep can weaken the immune system, making individuals more susceptible to mouth ulcers.
  • Allergic reactions or sensitivities: Certain foods, toothpastes, or mouthwashes can cause allergic reactions or sensitivities, leading to mouth ulcers. Common food triggers include acidic fruits, spicy foods, and nuts.
  • Celiac disease or gluten sensitivity: People with celiac disease or gluten sensitivity may develop mouth ulcers as a reaction to gluten. A gluten-free diet may help alleviate this issue.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD): Conditions like Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis can cause mouth ulcers as a symptom of the underlying inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Canker sores (aphthous ulcers): These are recurring mouth ulcers with no known cause. They usually appear as small, round, or oval sores with a white or yellow center and a red border.
  • Oral lichen planus: This is a chronic inflammatory condition that can cause mouth ulcers. It is characterized by lacy white patches on the inside of the cheeks or tongue.
  • Oral cancer or precancerous conditions: Persistent mouth ulcers that do not heal within three weeks may be a sign of oral cancer or a precancerous condition. Regular dental check-ups are essential for early detection and treatment.
  • Certain medications: Some medications, such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) or chemotherapy drugs, can increase the risk of developing mouth ulcers as a side effect.
