What is periodontal disease and how it affects your life?

Periodontal disease, also known as gum disease, is a serious infection of the gums and other tissues that surround and support the teeth.

It is typically caused by plaque, a sticky film of bacteria that constantly forms on our teeth. When left untreated, periodontal disease can lead to a number of negative consequences for an individual's life.

For example, it can cause teeth to loosen or fall out, resulting in the need for dentures or dental implants. Additionally, the infection can spread to other parts of the body, increasing the risk of developing heart disease, stroke, and other systemic conditions. Periodontal disease can also cause bad breath, painful chewing, and receding gums, all of which can negatively impact an individual's self-esteem and quality of life.

Furthermore, it can lead to difficulty in speaking, swallowing and even malnutrition.

What are the main causes of periodontal disease?

The main causes of periodontal disease are bacterial plaque and tartar buildup on the teeth.

When plaque, a sticky film of bacteria, is not removed through regular brushing and flossing, it can harden into tartar, which is difficult to remove and can cause inflammation of the gums. This inflammation, known as gingivitis, is the early stage of periodontal disease. If left untreated, gingivitis can progress to periodontitis, a more severe form of gum disease that can cause damage to the soft tissue and bone that support the teeth, leading to tooth loss.

Other factors that can increase the risk of periodontal disease include smoking, diabetes, certain medications, and a family history of the disease.

The most common causes of periodontal disease are listed below.

  • Poor Oral Hygiene: Inadequate brushing, flossing, and rinsing can lead to plaque buildup, which causes gum inflammation and periodontal disease.
  • Smoking or Using Tobacco: Smoking or using tobacco products can weaken the immune system, making it harder for the body to fight off gum infections, and can also cause tooth discoloration, bad breath, and other oral health problems.
  • Genetic Predisposition: Some people are genetically predisposed to periodontal disease, meaning they are more susceptible to gum infections and may require more frequent dental checkups and cleanings.
  • Hormonal Changes: Hormonal fluctuations during pregnancy, menopause, and puberty can increase the sensitivity of gum tissues, making them more prone to gum disease.
  • Stress: Stress can weaken the immune system and make it more difficult for the body to fight off infections, including gum infections.
  • Poor Nutrition: A diet lacking in essential vitamins and minerals can weaken the immune system and make it more difficult for the body to fight off gum infections.
  • Grinding or Clenching Teeth: Grinding or clenching teeth can put excessive force on the gums and teeth, causing gum recession and increasing the risk of periodontal disease.
  • Certain Medications: Certain medications, such as antidepressants, anticonvulsants, and some heart medications, can reduce saliva flow, leading to dry mouth and increasing the risk of gum disease.
  • Diabetes: People with diabetes are more susceptible to infections, including gum infections, due to high blood sugar levels.
  • Age: As people age, their gums naturally recede, making them more susceptible to gum disease. Additionally, older adults may have a harder time maintaining good oral hygiene, increasing their risk of periodontal disease.
