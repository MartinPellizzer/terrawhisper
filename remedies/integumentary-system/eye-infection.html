What to know about eye infection before treating it with medicinal herbs

An eye infection, also known as conjunctivitis, is an inflammation or irritation of the thin membrane covering the white part of the eyeball and the inside of the eyelids.

It can be caused by bacteria, viruses, or allergies and can lead to discomfort, redness, and blurry vision. Eye infections can significantly impact daily life, causing fatigue, decreased productivity, and social isolation.

What is eye infection and how it affects your life?

An eye infection can be a debilitating and frustrating experience that significantly impacts one's daily life.

It can cause symptoms such as redness, swelling, discharge, itching, and blurred vision, making even simple tasks like reading, watching TV, or using digital devices a challenge. Moreover, an eye infection can make it difficult to perform routine activities like driving, working, or even doing everyday chores, leading to feelings of frustration and helplessness.

It can also disrupt sleep patterns due to discomfort and itchiness, affecting one's mood and overall well-being. Social interactions and outings may become limited as the infection makes it difficult to wear makeup, contact lenses, or even keep glasses clean. Moreover, an eye infection can also increase sensitivity to light, making it hard to tolerate bright lighting conditions, which can further exacerbate discomfort and fatigue.

As a result, an eye infection can take a toll on one's personal and professional life, causing significant distress and disruption.

What are the main causes of eye infection?

The main causes of eye infection are a combination of factors that can lead to the growth and multiplication of bacteria, viruses, or fungi in the eyes.

Bacterial conjunctivitis, for instance, is often caused by touching the eyes with contaminated hands, sharing makeup or contact lenses, or having poor hygiene. Viral conjunctivitis, on the other hand, is typically spread through direct contact with an infected person's mucus or respiratory droplets. Fungal infections can occur when bacteria or viruses cause a secondary infection in individuals who are already experiencing dry eyes or have weakened immune systems.

Other risk factors for eye infection include poor ventilation, overcrowding, and inadequate hygiene practices, particularly among children.

The most common causes of eye infection are listed below.

  • Conjunctivitis: Also known as pink eye, this is an inflammation or infection of the thin membrane covering the white part of the eye and the inside of the eyelids.
  • Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA): A type of bacterial infection that can cause conjunctivitis, especially in people with a weakened immune system or those who have recently had surgery.
  • Chlamydia trachomatis: A bacterium that can cause sexually transmitted conjunctivitis and other eye infections.
  • Adenovirus: A common virus that can cause pink eye, as well as other respiratory infections such as the common cold.
  • HSV (Herpes Simplex Virus): A virus that can cause recurrent eye infections, including shingles and herpes keratitis.
  • Viral conjunctivitis: A highly contagious infection caused by various viruses, often spread through close contact or contaminated surfaces.
  • Bacterial conjunctivitis: An infection caused by a variety of bacteria, such as Streptococcus pneumoniae or Haemophilus influenzae, which can be transmitted through direct contact with an infected person's eye secretions.
  • Fungal keratitis: A fungal infection that can cause inflammation and ulcers on the cornea, often associated with trauma to the eye or wearing contact lenses.
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa: A bacteria that can cause a type of conjunctivitis known as pseudomonal keratitis, which is common in people who wear contact lenses or have a weakened immune system.
  • Acanthamoeba keratitis: An infection caused by a parasite called Acanthamoeba, which can be found in contaminated water and can cause a type of corneal ulcers.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for eye infection?

When using herbal remedies for an eye infection, it's essential to exercise caution and follow proper guidelines to avoid exacerbating the condition or causing additional harm.

First and foremost, consult with an eye care professional or a healthcare provider before attempting to treat an eye infection with herbs, as they can provide personalized guidance and recommend the most effective treatment approach. Additionally, ensure that you obtain high-quality, pure herbal products from reputable sources, and follow the recommended dosages carefully.

Some herbal remedies may interact with prescription medications or worsen certain medical conditions, so be aware of any potential contraindications. Furthermore, avoid touching your eyes after handling herbs or applying eye drops to prevent contamination, and wash your hands thoroughly before and after application.

Finally, monitor your symptoms closely and discontinue use if you experience any adverse reactions or worsening of the infection.
