What is ingrown toenail and how it affects your life?

An ingrown toenail is a painful and debilitating condition that can significantly disrupt daily life.

It occurs when the edges of the toenail grow into the surrounding skin, causing redness, swelling, and intense discomfort. Simple activities such as walking or standing become torture as every step feels like a knife piercing the skin. Everyday tasks like dressing, bathing, or even sleeping are compromised due to the constant pain and pressure.

Social interactions and outings become awkward as individuals struggle to hide their affected foot from others, leading to feelings of embarrassment and isolation. Even simple exercises like running or playing sports become impossible, leaving one feeling frustrated and demotivated. Moreover, the condition can also lead to infection, which may require antibiotic treatment, further adding to the inconvenience.

The constant discomfort and anxiety surrounding an ingrown toenail can affect not only physical well-being but also mental health, causing irritability, stress, and overall decreased quality of life.

What are the main causes of ingrown toenail?

The main causes of ingrown toenail are a combination of genetic, environmental, and lifestyle factors.

One of the primary causes is poor nail trimming habits, where the nail is cut too short or at an angle, causing the nail to grow into the skin instead of straight out. Another common cause is genetics, as some people may have naturally curved nails that are more prone to ingrowth.

Tight shoes or socks can also contribute to ingrown toenails by putting pressure on the toe and causing the nail to curve downward. Furthermore, fungal infections such as athlete's foot can weaken the nail, making it more susceptible to becoming ingrown.

Lastly, improper footwear or foot mechanics can also lead to excessive pressure on the toes, increasing the risk of ingrown toenails.

The most common causes of ingrown toenail are listed below.

  • Improper Trimming: Cutting the nail too short, cutting it at an angle, or cutting into the corners can cause the nail to grow back into the skin.
  • Tight Shoes: Wearing shoes that are too tight can cause pressure on the toes and lead to ingrown toenails.
  • Genetic Predisposition: Some people may be more prone to developing ingrown toenails due to their genetic makeup.
  • Poor Foot Mechanics: Abnormalities in the way the foot functions, such as overpronation or flat feet, can put additional pressure on the toes and lead to ingrown toenails.
  • Fungal Infections: Fungal infections, such as athlete's foot, can cause thickening of the nail and increase the risk of ingrown toenails.
  • Paronychia: An infection of the skin around the nail bed, caused by bacteria or fungi, can lead to inflammation and swelling that can cause ingrown toenails.
  • Diabetes: People with diabetes are more susceptible to developing foot problems, including ingrown toenails, due to nerve damage and poor circulation.
  • Trauma: Injury to the toe, such as a cut or puncture wound, can cause irritation and inflammation of the nail bed that can lead to an ingrown toenail.
  • Nerve Damage: Nerve damage, such as from a condition like peripheral neuropathy, can cause numbness and tingling in the toes that can make it difficult to notice pain or discomfort until it becomes severe.
  • Hormonal Changes: Hormonal fluctuations during pregnancy or menopause can cause changes in nail growth patterns, leading to an increased risk of ingrown toenails.
