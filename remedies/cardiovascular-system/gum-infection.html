What is gum infection and how it affects your life?

Gum infection, also known as periodontitis, is a chronic bacterial infection that can have devastating effects on one's overall health and quality of life.

When left untreated, gum infection can cause pain, bleeding, and swelling in the gums, leading to tooth loss and even spread to other parts of the body through the bloodstream. For instance, it can lead to difficulty eating or speaking due to tooth sensitivity, making everyday tasks a challenge.

Moreover, gum infection has been linked to an increased risk of heart disease, respiratory infections, and diabetes, making it a significant concern for individuals with pre-existing medical conditions. Furthermore, it can also affect one's self-esteem and confidence, leading to feelings of embarrassment or shame when interacting with others, social or professional. Additionally, the constant discomfort and pain can disrupt sleep patterns, affecting mood and overall well-being.

Moreover, gum infection can also lead to bad breath, yellowing or darkening of teeth, and even changes in facial structure due to bone loss, affecting one's appearance and overall quality of life.

What are the main causes of gum infection?

The main causes of gum infection are a combination of factors that contribute to an imbalance in the oral cavity.

Plaque, a sticky film of bacteria, is the primary culprit behind gum infection, also known as periodontal disease. When plaque builds up on teeth and below the gum line, it can cause inflammation and irritation, leading to redness, swelling, and bleeding of the gums.

Other contributing factors include poor oral hygiene habits, such as infrequent brushing and flossing, which allow plaque to accumulate unchecked. Additionally, smoking, diabetes, genetics, and certain medications can increase an individual's risk of developing gum infection.

Furthermore, a diet high in sugars and starches can also contribute to the development of gum disease, as bacteria feed on these substances and produce acid that erodes tooth enamel and exacerbates inflammation.

The most common causes of gum infection are listed below.

  • Poor Oral Hygiene: Failure to brush and floss regularly allows plaque and tartar to build up on teeth, leading to gum inflammation and infection.
  • Smoking: Smoking decreases blood flow to the gums, making them more susceptible to infection and damage.
  • Tartar Buildup: A buildup of tartar (hardened plaque) below the gum line can cause irritation and lead to gum infection.
  • Gingivitis: Mild inflammation of the gums caused by poor oral hygiene or other factors, which if left untreated, can progress to periodontitis.
  • Hormonal Changes: Hormonal fluctuations during pregnancy, menstruation, or menopause can increase blood flow to the gums, making them more susceptible to infection.
  • Genetic Predisposition: Some people may be born with a genetic predisposition to gum disease, which can make them more prone to infection.
  • Diabetes: Uncontrolled diabetes increases the risk of developing periodontitis due to compromised immune function and blood flow.
  • Stress: Chronic stress can weaken the immune system, making it harder for the body to fight off infections, including gum infections.
  • Nutritional Deficiencies: Deficiencies in vitamins like C and D, as well as minerals like calcium and phosphorus, can increase the risk of developing gum disease.
  • Certain Medications: Certain medications such as antidepressants, steroids, and certain antibiotics can cause dry mouth, which can lead to an increased risk of gum infection.
