What is dry mouth and how it affects your life?

Dry mouth, also known as xerostomia, is a condition characterized by a persistent lack of moisture in the mouth.

This can have a significant impact on daily life, causing a range of uncomfortable and debilitating symptoms. For example, speaking and eating can become challenging due to the difficulty in forming words and swallowing saliva.

The sensation of dryness and stickiness in the mouth and throat can be embarrassing and frustrating, making it hard to concentrate or enjoy social activities like meals with friends or family. Additionally, dry mouth can lead to bad breath, which can erode self-confidence and make individuals feel anxious or self-conscious about their appearance. Furthermore, it can also increase the risk of tooth decay, gum disease, and other oral health issues due to the lack of saliva's natural antibacterial properties.

Moreover, dry mouth can disrupt sleep patterns, leading to fatigue and irritability, and even affect overall quality of life by making everyday tasks like reading or watching TV feel tiresome and uncomfortable.

What are the main causes of dry mouth?

The main causes of dry mouth are a complex combination of factors, including medical conditions, medications, and lifestyle habits.

Certain medical conditions such as diabetes, kidney disease, Parkinson's disease, and Sjogren's syndrome can cause dry mouth due to the lack of saliva production or decreased saliva quality. Medications like antihistamines, antidepressants, and decongestants can also contribute to dry mouth as a side effect. Additionally, lifestyle habits such as frequent snacking, sucking on candies or tobacco products, and using mouthwash can all lead to dry mouth.

Moreover, age-related changes in salivary gland function can also cause dry mouth, particularly in older adults.

The most common causes of dry mouth are listed below.

  • Aging: As we age, saliva production decreases, leading to dry mouth. This can be a natural part of aging or a side effect of certain medications.
  • Medications: Many prescription and over-the-counter medications can cause dry mouth as a side effect, including antihistamines, decongestants, painkillers, and diuretics.
  • Dehydration: Not drinking enough water or fluids can lead to dehydration, which can cause dry mouth.
  • Gum disease: Gum disease (periodontitis) can cause inflammation in the gums and saliva glands, leading to decreased saliva production and dry mouth.
  • Diabetes: People with diabetes are more prone to dry mouth due to nerve damage that affects saliva production.
  • Hormonal changes: Hormonal fluctuations during menopause or pregnancy can affect saliva production, leading to dry mouth.
  • Radiation therapy: Radiation treatment for cancer can cause dry mouth as a side effect, particularly in the head and neck area.
  • Anxiety and stress: Stress and anxiety can trigger dry mouth by increasing cortisol levels, which can alter saliva production.
  • Smoking and tobacco use: Smoking and using other tobacco products can reduce saliva production and lead to dry mouth.
  • Autoimmune disorders: Certain autoimmune disorders, such as Sjögren's syndrome, can cause inflammation in the salivary glands and lead to dry mouth.
