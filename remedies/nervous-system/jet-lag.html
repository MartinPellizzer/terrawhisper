What is jet lag and how it affects your life?

Jet lag is a debilitating phenomenon that can wreak havoc on one's daily routine, leaving even the most well-adjusted individuals feeling like zombies.

When we travel across time zones, our bodies struggle to adjust to the new sleep-wake cycle, resulting in fatigue, disorientation, and a host of other unpleasant symptoms. For instance, you might find yourself stumbling through airport security, struggling to focus at work or school, or simply unable to muster the energy to cook a simple meal.

The effects can be far-reaching, causing irritability and mood swings, making it difficult to enjoy even your favorite activities or spend quality time with loved ones. You may experience headaches, nausea, or digestive issues, leaving you feeling like you've been hit by a truck. And just when you think things can't get any worse, the jet lagged brain fog sets in, making it challenging to concentrate, remember important tasks, or even recall your own name.

It's no wonder that jet lag has been known to ruin romantic vacations, business trips, and family reunions alike – it's a travel nemesis that can leave you feeling like you're stuck in perpetual limbo.

What are the main causes of jet lag?

The main causes of jet lag are disruptions to an individual's natural sleep-wake cycle, also known as their circadian rhythm, caused by crossing multiple time zones while traveling.

When we travel across time zones, our body's internal clock struggles to adjust to the new local time, leading to a mismatch between our body's natural rhythms and the new environment's daily routine. This can result in feelings of fatigue, disorientation, and difficulty sleeping or staying awake at normal times.

Additionally, jet lag is also exacerbated by factors such as changes in light exposure, meal times, and physical activity, which further disrupts our bodily functions and makes it harder to adapt to the new time zone.

The most common causes of jet lag are listed below.

  • Travel Across Multiple Time Zones: Traveling across multiple time zones can disrupt your body's natural circadian rhythms, making it difficult to adjust to a new sleep-wake cycle.
  • Poor Sleep Habits Before Travel: Poor sleep habits before traveling, such as not getting enough sleep or having an irregular sleep schedule, can make jet lag worse.
  • Inconsistent Wake-Up Times: Inconsistent wake-up times during travel, such as sleeping in late or going to bed too early, can confuse your body's internal clock and exacerbate jet lag.
  • Screen Time Before Bed: Using screens (e.g., phones, tablets, or laptops) before bedtime can suppress melatonin production, making it harder to fall asleep and worsening jet lag symptoms.
  • Cabin Pressure and Dry Air: The low air pressure and dry air on airplanes can disrupt your body's natural sleep patterns and lead to fatigue, insomnia, and other jet lag symptoms.
  • Disruptive Travel Schedules: Irregular travel schedules, such as flying during non-traditional hours or with frequent layovers, can make it harder for your body to adjust to a new time zone.
  • Lack of Physical Activity: Limited physical activity during travel, such as long periods of sitting or lack of exercise, can contribute to fatigue, sleepiness, and other jet lag symptoms.
  • Circadian Rhythm Disruptions: Disruptions to your natural circadian rhythm, such as exposure to bright lights in the middle of the night or excessive noise, can confuse your body's internal clock and lead to jet lag.
  • Stress and Anxiety: High levels of stress and anxiety during travel, caused by factors like turbulence, long lines at customs, or language barriers, can make it harder for your body to adjust to a new time zone.
  • Medication Side Effects: Certain medications, such as sedatives or antidepressants, can interfere with sleep patterns and worsen jet lag symptoms.
