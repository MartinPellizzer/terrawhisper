What is flu and how it affects your life?

The flu, also known as influenza, is a highly contagious viral infection that primarily attacks the respiratory system.

Once contracted, it can lead to a variety of uncomfortable and sometimes severe symptoms, such as fever, chills, body aches, fatigue, sore throat, and cough. These symptoms can have a significant negative impact on an individual's daily life, causing them to miss work or school, and experience a decrease in productivity and energy levels. Furthermore, the flu can also lead to more serious complications, such as pneumonia, bronchitis, and ear infections, which may require hospitalization and can even be life-threatening.

Additionally, the flu can also have a social impact, as it is easily spread through coughing, sneezing, and touching, which can lead to the infection of others, particularly those who are more vulnerable, such as young children, the elderly, and people with weakened immune systems.

What are the main causes of flu?

The main causes of flu are infection with influenza viruses.

These viruses spread mainly by tiny droplets made when people with flu cough, sneeze or talk. These droplets can land in the mouths or noses of people nearby or possibly be inhaled into the lungs.

Less often, a person might also get flu by touching a surface or object that has flu virus on it and then touching their own mouth, nose, or possibly their eyes. Flu viruses can survive on surfaces for a few hours or up to a day. People with flu are most contagious in the first three to four days after their illness begins, but some otherwise healthy adults may be able to infect others beginning 1 day before symptoms develop and up to 5 to 7 days after becoming sick.

Children and some people with weakened immune systems may be able to infect others for an even longer time.

The most common causes of flu are listed below.

  • Influenza virus: The flu is caused by infection with influenza viruses A, B, or C. These viruses spread mainly from person to person when people with the flu cough, sneeze, or talk and release droplets containing their virus into the air.
  • Poor hand hygiene: Not washing hands frequently or properly can lead to the spread of flu viruses from contaminated surfaces to the mouth, nose, or eyes.
  • Close contact with infected individuals: Spending time in close proximity to people who have the flu, especially in crowded areas or during travel, increases the risk of infection.
  • Lack of vaccination: Not getting an annual flu shot leaves individuals without immunity to the circulating flu viruses, making them more susceptible to infection.
  • Weakened immune system: People with weakened immune systems, such as those with HIV/AIDS, undergoing cancer treatment, or taking immunosuppressive drugs, are at higher risk of developing the flu.
  • Chronic medical conditions: People with chronic illnesses, such as asthma, diabetes, or heart disease, are more vulnerable to the flu due to their weakened immune systems.
  • Age: Young children and older adults are at higher risk of developing the flu due to their weaker immune systems and closer contact with others in schools or care facilities.
  • Living in close quarters: People living in close quarters, such as college dormitories, military barracks, or nursing homes, are at increased risk of flu transmission due to the high concentration of people in a small area.
  • Touching contaminated objects: Flu viruses can survive on surfaces for several hours, making it easy to contract the flu by touching contaminated objects and then touching the mouth, nose, or eyes.
  • Traveling: Air travel, in particular, can increase the risk of flu transmission due to the recirculation of air in the cabin and the close proximity of passengers.
