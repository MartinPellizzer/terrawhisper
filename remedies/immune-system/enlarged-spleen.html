What is enlarged spleen and how it affects your life?

An enlarged spleen, also known as splenomegaly, is a condition where the spleen becomes larger than its normal size.

This can have a significant impact on daily life, making even simple tasks challenging and exhausting. For instance, an enlarged spleen can cause chronic pain and discomfort in the abdomen, back, and sides, making it difficult to engage in physical activities or enjoy hobbies.

It can also lead to fatigue, weakness, and shortness of breath, making everyday tasks like walking or climbing stairs a struggle. Furthermore, an enlarged spleen can compress nearby organs, such as the stomach, small intestine, and pancreas, leading to symptoms like nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Additionally, it can cause bruising and bleeding easily due to its increased size, making even minor injuries or medical procedures more complicated.

Overall, living with an enlarged spleen requires constant management of pain, fatigue, and other symptoms, which can significantly affect one's quality of life and ability to participate in daily activities.

What are the main causes of enlarged spleen?

The main causes of enlarged spleen are a complex array of factors, including infections, inflammatory conditions, and autoimmune disorders.

Infections such as malaria, mononucleosis (mono), and viral hepatitis can cause enlargement of the spleen. Chronic inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) can also lead to splenomegaly.

Additionally, some people may experience an enlarged spleen due to autoimmune disorders such as idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP), a condition in which the immune system attacks platelets. Other less common causes of enlarged spleen include blood cancers like leukemia and lymphoma, liver cirrhosis, and certain medications that can cause splenomegaly as a side effect.

In some cases, an enlarged spleen may also be a symptom of a underlying genetic disorder or congenital condition.

The most common causes of enlarged spleen are listed below.

  • Mononucleosis (Mono): Also known as infectious mononucleosis, Mono is a viral infection caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). Enlargement of the spleen can occur due to the body's reaction to the infection.
  • Cancer: Certain types of cancer, such as leukemia and lymphoma, can cause the spleen to become enlarged as it tries to filter out abnormal cells from the bloodstream.
  • Trauma or injury: Physical trauma to the abdomen or a blow to the area can cause the spleen to rupture and become enlarged due to inflammation.
  • Autoimmune disorders: Conditions like lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, and scleroderma can lead to an enlarged spleen as the immune system mistakenly attacks healthy tissues.
  • Viral hepatitis: Infection with viruses such as hepatitis B or C can cause inflammation and enlargement of the liver and spleen.
  • Cirrhosis: Advanced liver disease (cirrhosis) can cause the spleen to become enlarged due to increased blood pressure and scarring in the liver.
  • Infections like malaria, abscesses, or sepsis: Bacterial infections such as malarial fever, splenic abscesses, or bloodstream infection (sepsis) can cause inflammation and enlargement of the spleen.
  • Bleeding disorders: Conditions like hemophilia A or von Willebrand disease can lead to bleeding within the spleen, causing it to become enlarged.
  • Liver diseases: Certain liver conditions, such as liver cirrhosis or portal hypertension, can cause the spleen to enlarge due to increased blood pressure and scarring in the liver.
  • Infiltrative disorders: Conditions like Gaucher disease (a rare genetic disorder) or amyloidosis (a condition where abnormal proteins accumulate in tissues) can cause abnormal substances to build up within the spleen, leading to its enlargement.
