Runny Nose: Causes, Medicinal Herbs and Herbal Preparations

A runny nose, medically known as rhinorrhea, is the excessive production of thin nasal discharge, often accompanied by congestion, sneezing, and discomfort.

Caused by viral infections, allergies, cold weather, or irritants like dust, smoke, or strong odors, a runny nose can lead to sinus infections, headaches, and respiratory problems. To alleviate symptoms, herbal remedies like echinacea, eucalyptus, and peppermint can be used to soothe the nasal passages.

These herbs can be consumed as teas, taken as supplements, or applied topically as nasal sprays, but it is essential to consult with a healthcare professional before using medicinal herbs, especially if you have underlying health conditions or allergies.

This article explains in detail what are the causes of runny nose, what medicinal herbs to use to relieve this problem and how to prepare these herbs to get the best results.

What are the main causes of runny nose?

The main causes of runny nose are the excessive production of mucus, triggered by various factors such as viral infections, allergies, and environmental irritants.

Viruses like the common cold, flu, and respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) can stimulate the nasal mucosa to produce excess mucus, leading to a runny nose. Allergies, particularly those caused by pollen, dust mites, and pet dander, can also trigger an overproduction of mucus. In addition, environmental irritants like tobacco smoke, pollution, and strong odors can stimulate the nasal mucosa and cause a runny nose.

Hormonal changes, such as those experienced during pregnancy or menstruation, can also lead to increased mucus production and a runny nose.

  • Allergies: Allergies cause the release of histamine in the body, leading to the production of excess mucus and the inflammation of the nasal passages, resulting in a runny nose.
  • Cold: A cold virus infects the cells lining the nasal passages, causing the production of excess mucus and the inflammation of the nasal passages, leading to a runny nose.
  • Sinusitis: Sinusitis, an inflammation or infection of the sinuses, causes the production of excess mucus and the blockage of the nasal passages, leading to a runny nose.
  • Pregnancy: Hormonal changes during pregnancy cause the nasal passages to produce excess mucus, leading to a runny nose.
  • Anxiety: Anxiety can cause the body to produce excess mucus and lead to a runny nose due to the increased production of stress hormones.
  • Hormonal Changes: Hormonal changes during menstruation, menopause, or puberty can cause the nasal passages to produce excess mucus, leading to a runny nose.
  • Smoking: Smoking damages the cilia in the nasal passages, impairing the body's ability to remove excess mucus and leading to a runny nose.
  • Air Pollution: Inhaling air pollutants, such as particulate matter and nitrogen dioxide, can cause inflammation in the nasal passages and lead to a runny nose.
  • Irritants: Inhaling irritants, such as dust, pollen, and chemicals, can cause the nasal passages to produce excess mucus and lead to a runny nose.
  • Food Allergies: Food allergies can cause the release of histamine in the body, leading to the production of excess mucus and the inflammation of the nasal passages, resulting in a runny nose.
