What to know about cough before treating it with medicinal herbs

A cough is an involuntary contraction of the diaphragm, often caused by irritation or inflammation in the throat, lungs, or airways.

Chronic coughing can significantly disrupt daily life, causing fatigue, insomnia, and social isolation. Common causes include allergies, respiratory infections, asthma, and acid reflux.

To alleviate symptoms, medicinal herbs like Thyme, Ginger, and Licorice root have been used for centuries. These herbs can be consumed as teas, tinctures, or infusions. When using herbs medicinally, it's essential to note that individual reactions may vary, and potential interactions with medications should be considered.

Consult with a healthcare professional before self-medicating with herbal remedies.

What is cough and how it affects your life?

A persistent cough can be a debilitating and frustrating affliction that severely impacts one's daily life.

It can disrupt sleep, leaving individuals feeling exhausted and irritable for days to come. Simple tasks like getting out of bed or taking a shower become Herculean efforts as the constant hacking and clearing of the throat take its toll on energy levels.

At work, a cough can make it difficult to focus and concentrate, leading to decreased productivity and potentially affecting job performance. Social events and gatherings become daunting challenges, as the need to constantly excuse oneself to cough or cover one's mouth becomes an awkward and embarrassing ritual. Even simple pleasures like enjoying a meal or watching a movie are ruined by the constant interruptions of the cough.

Additionally, a cough can lead to embarrassment in public, anxiety and stress, and even physical discomfort due to increased respiratory effort, further exacerbating the negative impact on daily life.

What are the main causes of cough?

The main causes of cough are a multitude of factors that can irritate and inflame the throat, lungs, and airways.

Respiratory infections such as common colds, flu, bronchitis, and pneumonia are among the most common causes of coughing. Inflammation or irritation of the trachea, larynx, and bronchi can also trigger a persistent cough, often caused by allergies, asthma, or exposure to environmental irritants like tobacco smoke, pollution, or strong chemicals. Additionally, gastrointestinal reflux disease (GERD), sinus infections, and acid reflux can radiate pain to the chest and throat, leading to a chronic cough.

Furthermore, certain medications, such as ACE inhibitors and beta-blockers, can also cause coughing as a side effect.

The most common causes of cough are listed below.

  • Common Cold: A viral infection that affects the upper respiratory tract, causing inflammation and irritation in the throat and lungs, leading to a characteristic cough.
  • Acid Reflux (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease): Stomach acid flowing back up into the esophagus can irritate the throat, triggering a cough, often worse at night or after eating spicy foods.
  • Asthma: Chronic inflammation of the airways leads to recurring episodes of wheezing, coughing, and shortness of breath, often triggered by allergens, pollution, or stress.
  • Pneumonia: Bacterial infection of the lungs causes inflammation, leading to a dry, hacking cough that may worsen over time.
  • Bronchitis: Inflammation of the bronchi (airways) due to infection, allergies, or environmental factors, resulting in a persistent, productive cough.
  • Allergies: Exposure to allergens like pollen, dust, pet dander, or mold triggers an allergic reaction, causing inflammation and irritation in the throat and lungs, leading to a cough.
  • Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV): A common viral infection affecting infants and young children, characterized by a runny nose, cough, and wheezing.
  • Lung Infections (Pleurisy or Pleuritis): Inflammation of the lining surrounding the lungs can cause a sharp, stabbing pain in the chest and a dry, hacking cough.
  • COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease): Chronic inflammation and damage to the airways due to smoking, pollution, or other environmental factors, leading to persistent coughing and shortness of breath.
  • Sinusitis: Infection or inflammation of the sinuses can cause postnasal drip, which irritates the throat and triggers a cough.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for cough?

When using herbal remedies to treat a cough, it's essential to exercise caution and take certain precautions.

Some herbs, such as eucalyptus and licorice root, can interact with medications or worsen certain health conditions, such as high blood pressure or diabetes. Additionally, some herbs may not be suitable for children or pregnant women. It's crucial to consult with a healthcare provider before using any herbal remedy, especially if you have underlying medical conditions or are taking prescription medications.

Furthermore, ensure that the herbal remedy is purchased from a reputable source and follows proper preparation and dosing instructions to minimize the risk of adverse effects.

The most important precautions to take when using herbal remedies for cough are listed below.

  • Check the ingredients carefully to ensure you are not allergic to any of them
  • Consult a healthcare professional before giving herbal remedies to children or pregnant women
  • Follow the recommended dosage and preparation instructions carefully
  • Keep track of your symptoms and adjust the dosage as needed
  • Monitor your blood pressure and heart rate regularly when using herbal remedies
  • Avoid combining herbal remedies with prescription medications without consulting a doctor
  • Report any adverse reactions to your healthcare professional immediately
