Pertussis, also known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious bacterial infection caused by Bordetella pertussis that attacks the respiratory system, leading to severe coughing fits and potentially life-threatening complications.

The bacteria spreads through airborne droplets and close contact with an infected person. What is pertussis and how it affects your life?

Pertussis, also known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease that can have a devastating impact on an individual's daily life.

The relentless coughing fits that accompany this illness can be exhausting, making it difficult to sleep, eat, or even think clearly. Simple tasks like dressing, bathing, and caring for oneself become daunting challenges, as the constant hacking and choking make every movement feel like an Olympic feat.

Social interactions become a nightmare, as the incessant coughing makes it hard to concentrate in meetings, classes, or conversations. Even simple outings like grocery shopping or going for a walk become arduous endeavors, as the need to frequently clear one's airways becomes a constant distraction.

The emotional toll of pertussis is not to be underestimated either - the feelings of frustration, embarrassment, and isolation that come with having such a debilitating illness can take a significant toll on mental health and overall well-being.

What are the main causes of pertussis?

The main causes of pertussis are complex and multifaceted, but can be attributed to a combination of factors.

The bacterium Bordetella pertussis is responsible for causing the disease, and it is highly contagious, spreading through the air when an infected person talks, coughs, or sneezes. Additionally, poor vaccination coverage and waning immunity among adults are significant contributing factors, as they can lead to increased transmission of the disease. Other risk factors include lack of access to healthcare, inadequate infection control practices in healthcare settings, and crowding, which can facilitate the spread of the bacteria.

Furthermore, pertussis can also occur when the vaccine's effectiveness wears off over time or when there is a lapse in vaccination coverage, allowing the disease to resurface.

The most common causes of pertussis are listed below.

  • Close Contact: Pertussis spreads easily from person to person, especially among family members and caregivers who come into close contact with an infected individual.
  • Poor Vaccination Rates: When vaccination rates are low in a community, the risk of pertussis outbreaks increases, as more individuals are susceptible to infection.
  • Vaccine Efficacy Waning: Even with proper vaccination, immunity against pertussis can wane over time, making individuals more susceptible to infection, especially among adults and older children.
  • Inadequate Booster Shots: Failure to receive recommended booster shots or inadequate dosing of the vaccine can leave individuals vulnerable to infection.
  • Waning Immunity in Adolescents and Adults: Pertussis vaccines are most effective in protecting against severe disease in young children, but immunity wanes over time, leaving adolescents and adults at higher risk for infection.
  • Circulation of New Strains: Mutations in the bacteria that cause pertussis can lead to new strains that evade vaccine-induced immunity, making infections more common.
  • Travel and Exposure: Traveling abroad or being exposed to crowded areas, such as airports or public transportation, increases the risk of contracting pertussis from an infected individual.
  • Poor Hygiene Practices: Poor handwashing habits, failure to cover mouths when coughing, and not avoiding close contact with sick individuals can contribute to transmission.
  • Increased Respiratory Viral Co-Infections: When a person contracts multiple viral respiratory infections at once (e.g., flu and RSV), they are more susceptible to pertussis due to compromised immune function.
  • Urbanization and Increased Population Density: The close proximity of individuals in urban areas can facilitate the spread of pertussis, making cities more prone to outbreaks.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for pertussis?

When considering the use of herbal remedies for pertussis, it is essential to exercise caution and consult with a healthcare professional first.

While some herbs may have antibacterial properties that could potentially help alleviate symptoms, they should not be relied upon as the sole treatment for this highly contagious respiratory infection. It's crucial to ensure that any herbal remedy does not interact with prescribed antibiotics or exacerbate the condition. Furthermore, some herbal remedies may not be suitable for certain populations, such as pregnant women or individuals with compromised immune systems.

To avoid delays in seeking medical attention and to ensure proper diagnosis and treatment, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare professional before using any herbal remedy for pertussis symptoms.
