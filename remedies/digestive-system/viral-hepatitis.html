What is viral hepatitis and how it affects your life?

Viral hepatitis is a group of infectious diseases caused by various viruses that primarily target the liver, leading to inflammation and damage.

These viruses include Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each with unique modes of transmission and clinical manifestations. The infection can result in a wide range of negative impacts on one's life, such as debilitating symptoms like jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and loss of appetite.

Severe cases may cause liver failure, cirrhosis, and even death. Additionally, viral hepatitis can lead to long-term complications, such as an increased risk of liver cancer and the need for liver transplantation.

The infection can also have significant psychological and social consequences, including isolation, stigma, and financial burden due to medical expenses and lost productivity.

What are the main causes of viral hepatitis?

The main causes of viral hepatitis are five specific viruses, labeled as types A, B, C, D, and E. Each type is responsible for different modes of transmission and severity of illness.

Hepatitis A virus (HAV) is typically acquired through the ingestion of contaminated food or water, while Hepatitis B virus (HBV) is often transmitted through contact with infected blood, semen, or other bodily fluids. Hepatitis C virus (HCV) is primarily spread through sharing of needles or other equipment used for injecting drugs, and less commonly through sexual contact or from mother to child during childbirth.

Hepatitis D virus (HDV) is contracted only by people who are already infected with HBV. Lastly, Hepatitis E virus (HEV) is usually spread through contaminated food or water, similar to HAV.

Understanding these causes is crucial for taking appropriate measures to prevent infection and manage the spread of viral hepatitis.

The most common causes of viral hepatitis are listed below.

  • Hepatitis A virus (HAV): HAV is primarily spread through consumption of contaminated food and water or direct contact with an infected person. It can cause acute hepatitis, but it rarely leads to chronic hepatitis.
  • Hepatitis B virus (HBV): HBV is transmitted through contact with infectious body fluids, such as blood, semen, and vaginal fluids. It can cause both acute and chronic hepatitis, which may lead to severe complications like cirrhosis and liver cancer.
  • Hepatitis C virus (HCV): HCV is spread through contact with infected blood, most commonly through sharing needles or other equipment used for injecting drugs. It often causes chronic hepatitis, which can result in long-term health problems.
  • Hepatitis D virus (HDV): HDV requires the presence of HBV to cause infection, as it uses HBV's envelope proteins for transmission. HDV can accelerate the progression of liver disease in individuals with HBV.
  • Hepatitis E virus (HEV): HEV is primarily spread through contaminated food and water, similar to HAV. It usually causes acute hepatitis, but in some cases, it can lead to chronic hepatitis in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Alcohol consumption: Excessive alcohol consumption can cause alcoholic hepatitis, which is inflammation of the liver due to alcohol abuse. Over time, this can lead to scarring and cirrhosis.
  • Fatty liver disease: Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) and non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) can cause inflammation and damage to the liver, leading to hepatitis.
  • Toxins and medications: Certain toxins, chemicals, and medications can cause hepatitis as a side effect. This includes some chemotherapy drugs, certain antibiotics, and over-the-counter medications like acetaminophen (Tylenol) when taken in excessive amounts.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis: Autoimmune hepatitis is a condition where the body's immune system attacks the liver, causing inflammation and damage. This can lead to chronic hepatitis if left untreated.
  • Genetic disorders: Certain genetic disorders, such as Wilson's disease and hemochromatosis, can cause hepatitis due to the accumulation of copper and iron, respectively, in the liver.
