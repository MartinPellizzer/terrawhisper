What is viral hepatitis and how it affects your life?

Viral hepatitis is a debilitating and often chronic condition that can wreak havoc on one's physical, emotional, and social well-being.

This insidious disease can cause fatigue, weakness, loss of appetite, nausea and vomiting, abdominal pain, dark urine, pale or clay-colored stools, and jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes), among other symptoms. As a result, individuals with viral hepatitis often struggle to maintain their daily routines, leading to missed workdays, cancelled social engagements, and a general sense of malaise.

Moreover, the constant discomfort and fatigue can lead to feelings of depression, anxiety, and irritability, further isolating those affected. In some cases, the disease can also cause liver damage or failure, requiring costly medical treatment and even liver transplantation in severe instances. Furthermore, viral hepatitis can also have a significant impact on personal relationships, as individuals may become withdrawn, irritable, or unable to perform their usual roles and responsibilities, causing stress and strain on loved ones.

Ultimately, living with viral hepatitis can be a daunting and overwhelming experience that demands significant lifestyle adjustments and compromises one's overall quality of life.

What are the main causes of viral hepatitis?

The main causes of viral hepatitis are typically classified into five different types, each caused by a specific virus.

Hepatitis A is primarily spread through contaminated food and water, as well as person-to-person contact through fecal-oral transmission. Hepatitis B is commonly transmitted from mother to child during childbirth, as well as through sharing needles or other equipment for injectable drugs, and sexual contact with an infected individual.

Hepatitis C is mainly acquired through direct blood-to-blood contact, such as through sharing contaminated needles or medical equipment, or receiving a tainted blood transfusion. Hepatitis D is a rare form of the disease that only affects individuals who are already infected with hepatitis B, and is typically spread through shared syringes or other injection equipment.

Hepatitis E is primarily caused by consuming contaminated food or water, especially in areas with poor sanitation.

The most common causes of viral hepatitis are listed below.

  • Hepatitis A: Caused by the hepatitis A virus (HAV), it is typically spread through contaminated food and water, close contact with an infected person, or sharing personal items.
  • Hepatitis B: Caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV), it can be transmitted through blood transfusions, needle-sharing for drug use, mother-to-child transmission during childbirth, or sexual contact.
  • Hepatitis C: Caused by the hepatitis C virus (HCV), it is primarily spread through blood-to-blood contact, such as sharing needles for injection drug use, getting a tattoo or piercing with unsterilized equipment, or having an infected organ transplant.
  • Sexual transmission: All types of viral hepatitis can be transmitted through sexual contact, including HAV, HBV, and HCV.
  • Sharing personal care items: Sharing toothbrushes, razors, or other personal care items with someone who has the virus can spread it.
  • Contaminated blood products: Receiving blood transfusions or organ transplants from infected donors can transmit HAV, HBV, or HCV.
  • Mother-to-child transmission: Infants born to mothers with HBV or HCV can acquire the infection during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding.
  • Needlestick injuries: Healthcare workers who are accidentally stuck with a needle contaminated with infected blood can contract viral hepatitis.
  • Unsterilized medical equipment: Using medical equipment that is not properly sterilized can spread viral hepatitis in healthcare settings.
  • Poor hygiene and sanitation: Failing to maintain proper hygiene and sanitation, such as washing hands regularly or using clean water for cooking and drinking, can increase the risk of transmission.
