What is hiatus hernia and how it affects your life?

A hiatus hernia occurs when a part of the stomach protrudes upward through the diaphragm, the muscle that separates the chest and abdominal cavities.

This can result in uncomfortable and sometimes painful symptoms that negatively impact a person's life. For example, a hiatus hernia can cause acid reflux, where stomach acid flows back up into the esophagus, leading to heartburn, chest pain, and difficulty swallowing.

Additionally, it can result in feelings of bloating and discomfort after eating, as well as nausea and vomiting. In severe cases, a hiatus hernia can even lead to the development of ulcers or strictures in the esophagus, further exacerbating symptoms and requiring medical intervention.

Overall, a hiatus hernia can significantly impact a person's quality of life, causing chronic discomfort and limiting their ability to engage in daily activities.

What are the main causes of hiatus hernia?

The main causes of hiatus hernia are increased pressure in the abdomen and weakness in the diaphragm.

Hiatus hernia occurs when a part of the stomach protrudes through an opening in the diaphragm, the muscle that separates the chest and abdomen. This condition is often caused by factors that increase pressure in the abdomen, such as obesity, pregnancy, coughing, vomiting, straining during bowel movements, and long-term heavy lifting.

Additionally, age-related wear and tear can cause the diaphragm to weaken, making it easier for the stomach to push through the opening, leading to a hiatus hernia.

The most common causes of hiatus hernia are listed below.

  • Increased abdominal pressure: Chronic increased pressure in the abdomen can cause the stomach to push up through the diaphragm, resulting in a hiatal hernia. This can be caused by obesity, pregnancy, straining during bowel movements, or frequent heavy lifting.
  • Aging: The aging process can cause the muscles in the diaphragm to weaken, allowing the stomach to bulge up through the opening. This is the most common cause of hiatal hernias in people over 50.
  • Congenital factors: Some people are born with a larger than normal hiatal opening, which can increase the risk of a hiatal hernia.
  • Obesity: Excess body weight, particularly around the abdomen, can put increased pressure on the abdominal cavity and contribute to the development of a hiatal hernia.
  • Smoking: Chronic smoking can weaken the muscles in the diaphragm and increase the risk of a hiatal hernia.
  • Poor posture: Chronic slouching or hunching over can put increased pressure on the abdomen and contribute to the development of a hiatal hernia.
  • Strenuous activity: Activities that involve repeated or sudden increases in abdominal pressure, such as heavy lifting or high-impact exercises, can increase the risk of a hiatal hernia.
  • Constipation: Chronic constipation can cause straining during bowel movements, which can put increased pressure on the abdomen and contribute to the development of a hiatal hernia.
  • Trauma: Injuries to the chest or abdomen, such as those caused by car accidents or falls, can damage the muscles in the diaphragm and increase the risk of a hiatal hernia.
  • Previous surgery: Hiatal hernias can sometimes occur as a complication of previous surgeries in the abdominal or chest area.
