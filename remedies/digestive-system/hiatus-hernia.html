What is hiatus hernia and how it affects your life?

A hiatus hernia occurs when a portion of the stomach bulges up into the chest through an opening in the diaphragm.

This common condition can have a significant impact on daily life, causing a range of uncomfortable and debilitating symptoms. For instance, sufferers may experience frequent heartburn and regurgitation of food, which can disrupt meal times and make eating out or social dining events a daunting task.

The constant burning sensation in the chest and throat can also lead to difficulty sleeping, as lying down or bending over exacerbates the discomfort. Additionally, hiatus hernia patients may notice weight loss due to the pain and difficulty eating, leading to feelings of embarrassment and frustration. Furthermore, the chronic acid reflux can cause digestive problems like bloating, gas, and stomach pain, making it challenging to engage in physical activity or participate in hobbies that require prolonged sitting or standing.

As a result, hiatus hernia patients may feel isolated, anxious, and limited in their daily activities, affecting not only their quality of life but also their mental well-being and overall sense of happiness.

What are the main causes of hiatus hernia?

The main causes of hiatus hernia are a combination of anatomical and physiological factors that lead to the protrusion of the stomach into the thoracic cavity through the esophageal hiatus.

One of the primary causes is weakening of the diaphragmatic muscle and surrounding connective tissue, which can occur due to aging, obesity, pregnancy, or repeated straining during coughing, constipation, or heavy lifting. Additionally, genetic predisposition, hiatal hernia in family members, and other conditions such as GERD (gastroesophageal reflux disease) can also contribute to the development of a hiatus hernia.

Furthermore, anatomical abnormalities, such as a larger than normal esophageal hiatus or a shorter-than-normal length of the lower esophagus, can increase the risk of a hiatus hernia forming.

The most common causes of hiatus hernia are listed below.

  • Increased Intrabdominal Pressure: This can be caused by obesity, pregnancy, or straining during bowel movements, which can lead to a weakening of the muscles and tissues that hold the stomach in place.
  • Aging: As people age, the connective tissue that holds the stomach in place can weaken, leading to a hiatus hernia.
  • Obesity: Excess weight can put additional pressure on the stomach and esophagus, causing the stomach to bulge up through the diaphragm.
  • Pregnancy: The growing uterus during pregnancy can push the stomach upwards, putting strain on the diaphragm and increasing the risk of a hiatus hernia.
  • Chronic Coughing: Persistent coughing can lead to straining, which can cause the stomach to bulge up through the diaphragm.
  • Lifting Heavy Objects: Lifting heavy objects can put additional pressure on the stomach and esophagus, leading to a hiatus hernia.
  • Weakened Diaphragm Muscle: A weakened diaphragm muscle due to age, injury, or disease can cause the stomach to bulge up through the opening in the diaphragm.
  • Connective Tissue Disorders: Conditions such as Marfan syndrome or Ehlers-Danlos syndrome can affect the connective tissue that holds the stomach in place, increasing the risk of a hiatus hernia.
  • Trauma: Injury to the chest or abdominal area can cause the stomach to bulge up through the diaphragm and become trapped outside the abdominal cavity.
  • Genetic Predisposition: Some people may be born with a predisposition to developing a hiatus hernia due to their genetic makeup, which can lead to a weakened connective tissue or other structural abnormalities.
