What is peptic ulcers and how it affects your life?

Peptic ulcers are open sores that develop on the lining of the stomach or the first part of the small intestine, also known as the duodenum.

These ulcers can cause various negative impacts on a person's life. For instance, they can result in severe abdominal pain, which can disrupt daily activities, work, and sleep patterns.

Additionally, peptic ulcers can lead to loss of appetite, weight loss, and malnutrition due to difficulty in eating or digesting food. In severe cases, they can cause internal bleeding, leading to anemia, fatigue, and weakness. Peptic ulcers can also increase the risk of infection and perforation of the stomach or intestinal walls, requiring hospitalization and surgery.

Furthermore, they can result in emotional distress, anxiety, and depression, affecting a person's overall quality of life.

What are the main causes of peptic ulcers?

The main causes of peptic ulcers are bacterial infection and long-term use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs).

The bacterium Helicobacter pylori is commonly found in the stomach and can cause inflammation and infection, leading to the development of peptic ulcers. Prolonged use of NSAIDs, such as aspirin and ibuprofen, can also irritate and erode the lining of the stomach and duodenum, resulting in peptic ulcers. Additionally, while less common, excessive alcohol consumption, smoking, and stress can contribute to the development of peptic ulcers.

However, it is important to note that a combination of factors, rather than a single cause, often leads to the formation of peptic ulcers.

The most common causes of peptic ulcers are listed below.

  • Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) infection: This bacterium can cause inflammation and infection in the stomach lining, leading to the development of peptic ulcers.
  • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs): Regular use of NSAIDs, such as aspirin, ibuprofen, and naproxen, can irritate and erode the mucous lining of the stomach and duodenum, causing peptic ulcers.
  • Smoking: Smoking increases the production of stomach acid, reduces the stomach's ability to heal, and can exacerbate the effects of H. pylori infection, all of which contribute to peptic ulcer development.
  • Alcohol consumption: Excessive alcohol consumption can irritate and erode the mucous lining of the stomach and duodenum, increasing the risk of peptic ulcers.
  • Genetic factors: Individuals with a family history of peptic ulcers are more likely to develop them due to inherited factors that affect stomach acid production and mucous lining resistance.
  • Stress: While stress alone does not directly cause peptic ulcers, it can exacerbate the effects of H. pylori infection and NSAID use, increasing the risk of ulcer formation.
  • Advanced age: The risk of peptic ulcers increases with age due to factors such as decreased mucous lining resistance and increased likelihood of H. pylori infection.
  • Poor diet: Consuming a diet high in spicy, fatty, or fried foods can irritate the stomach lining and potentially contribute to peptic ulcer development. However, diet is not a primary cause of ulcers.
  • Radiation therapy: Exposure to radiation, such as during cancer treatment, can increase the risk of peptic ulcers by damaging the mucous lining of the stomach and duodenum.
  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: This rare condition involves the overproduction of gastrin, a hormone that stimulates the stomach to produce acid, leading to an increased risk of peptic ulcers.
