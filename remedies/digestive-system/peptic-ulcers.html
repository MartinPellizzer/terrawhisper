What is peptic ulcers and how it affects your life?

Peptic ulcers are painful sores that develop in the lining of the stomach or small intestine, causing debilitating symptoms that can significantly impact daily life.

The constant burning sensation and gnawing pain can disrupt sleep patterns, making it difficult to get a good night's rest and leaving you feeling fatigued and irritable during the day. The nausea and vomiting associated with peptic ulcers can lead to dehydration, malnutrition, and weight loss, causing individuals to feel weak and lacking in energy.

Social activities become a challenge as the discomfort and pain make it difficult to eat in front of others or engage in outdoor activities, leading to feelings of isolation and depression. Furthermore, the constant discomfort and worry about when the next attack will come can cause anxiety and stress, affecting personal relationships and work performance.

In extreme cases, peptic ulcers can even lead to serious complications such as bleeding, perforation, or obstruction of the digestive tract, which can require hospitalization and surgery, putting a significant strain on one's mental and emotional well-being.

What are the main causes of peptic ulcers?

The main causes of peptic ulcers are a combination of factors that disrupt the delicate balance between the digestive system's lining and the digestive juices produced by the stomach and duodenum.

One of the primary culprits is Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) bacteria, which infects the mucous membranes of the stomach and duodenum, causing inflammation and damage to the lining. Another key factor is non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), such as aspirin and ibuprofen, which can irritate the stomach lining and cause ulcers.

Additionally, excessive consumption of spicy or acidic foods, smoking, and stress can also contribute to the development of peptic ulcers by reducing the natural protective mucus layer in the digestive tract and increasing the production of stomach acid.

The most common causes of peptic ulcers are listed below.

  • Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) infection: H. pylori is a type of bacteria that infects the lining of the stomach and duodenum, causing inflammation and damage to the mucous membranes, leading to peptic ulcer formation.
  • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs): Long-term use of NSAIDs such as aspirin, ibuprofen, and naproxen can irritate the stomach lining and cause ulcers, especially when taken in high doses or for prolonged periods.
  • Corticosteroids: High-dose corticosteroid therapy can suppress the production of stomach acid and increase the risk of peptic ulcer development, particularly in the duodenum.
  • Anticoagulant medications: Regular use of anticoagulant medications such as warfarin (Coumadin) can increase the risk of bleeding from existing ulcers or developing new ones.
  • Bicarbonate and acid reflux: Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), also known as heartburn, can cause stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus, leading to inflammation and peptic ulcer formation in the lower part of the esophagus.
  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: A rare condition characterized by overproduction of gastrin, a hormone that stimulates the release of gastric acid, leading to hypergastrinemia and peptic ulcers.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD): Chronic inflammation in the gut associated with conditions like Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis can increase the risk of developing peptic ulcers, especially in the duodenum.
  • Viral infections: Certain viruses such as cytomegalovirus (CMV) and herpes simplex virus (HSV) can cause inflammatory responses that lead to peptic ulcer formation.
  • Fungal infections: Fungal overgrowth in the gut, often associated with antibiotic use or weakened immune systems, can also contribute to peptic ulcers.
  • Cigarette smoking: Chronic smoking has been linked to an increased risk of developing peptic ulcers due to its effects on blood flow and stomach lining integrity.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for peptic ulcers?

When using herbal remedies for peptic ulcers, it is essential to exercise caution and follow certain precautions.

Always consult with a healthcare professional before starting any new treatment, including herbal supplements, especially if you are taking medications or have a pre-existing medical condition. Some herbs, such as licorice root and ginger, can interact with blood thinners, diabetes medications, and blood pressure medications, which may exacerbate underlying conditions. Additionally, certain herbs like slippery elm and marshmallow root can cause allergic reactions or interact with other herbal supplements, so monitor your body's response closely.

Start with a low dose and gradually increase as needed, and be aware that herbal remedies may not be regulated by the FDA, which means their quality and purity cannot be guaranteed.
