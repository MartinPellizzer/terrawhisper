What to know about heartburn before treating it with medicinal herbs

Heartburn, also known as acid reflux, is a burning sensation in the chest and throat caused by stomach acid flowing back up into the esophagus.

If left untreated, it can lead to digestive problems, sleep disturbances, and even anxiety and depression. Common causes of heartburn include eating spicy or fatty foods, lying down after meals, and obesity.

Medicinal herbs such as ginger, marshmallow root, and aloe vera can provide relief from heartburn symptoms when consumed as teas, capsules, or supplements. However, it's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before using herbs medicinally, especially if you're taking other medications or have underlying health conditions.

Precautions include starting with small doses, monitoring for side effects, and combining herbs with conventional treatments under medical supervision.

What is heartburn and how it affects your life?

Heartburn is a painful and uncomfortable condition that can significantly disrupt daily life.

It's characterized by a burning sensation in the chest, often radiating up to the neck, throat, or arms. For those who suffer from it, heartburn can be a constant companion, making even simple activities a challenge.

Imagine being unable to enjoy your favorite foods without worrying about the impending discomfort, or struggling to get a good night's sleep due to persistent pain and discomfort. Heartburn can also affect one's work and social life, as it may become difficult to concentrate at meetings or participate in conversations without experiencing excruciating pain. It can even impact personal relationships, as loved ones may grow frustrated with the constant complaining or avoidance of activities that trigger symptoms.

Additionally, heartburn can lead to fatigue, anxiety, and depression, making it a debilitating condition that can consume one's life.

What are the main causes of heartburn?

The main causes of heartburn are typically related to the movement and relaxation of the muscles in the esophagus, stomach, and diaphragm.

One of the most common triggers is gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), which occurs when the ring-like muscle at the end of the esophagus fails to close properly, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus. Other causes include eating large or fatty meals that put pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter and cause it to relax, as well as lying down after eating, which can cause stomach contents to flow back up into the esophagus more easily.

Additionally, certain foods and drinks, such as citrus fruits, tomatoes, chocolate, and caffeine, can weaken the lower esophageal sphincter and trigger heartburn symptoms in some individuals.

The most common causes of heartburn are listed below.

  • Eating large or heavy meals: Consuming large amounts of food, especially foods that are high in fat, spice, or acidity, can put pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), causing it to relax and allow stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
  • Foods that trigger heartburn: Certain foods such as citrus fruits, tomatoes, chocolate, spicy dishes, and fried or fatty foods can irritate the lining of the esophagus and trigger heartburn symptoms.
  • Lying down after eating: Lying down too soon after eating can cause stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus, especially if you eat a large meal.
  • Being overweight or obese: Excess weight can put pressure on the LES, causing it to relax and allow stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
  • Pregnancy: Hormonal changes during pregnancy can cause the muscles in the esophagus to relax, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus and trigger heartburn symptoms.
  • Smoking: Smoking can weaken the LES and reduce the lower esophageal sphincter's ability to function properly, leading to heartburn symptoms.
  • Drinking too much coffee or tea: Consuming large amounts of caffeine can stimulate the muscles in the stomach and increase acid production, which can lead to heartburn symptoms.
  • Eating late at night: Eating a large meal close to bedtime can cause stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus while you're lying down, triggering heartburn symptoms.
  • Stress and anxiety: Stress and anxiety can exacerbate existing heartburn symptoms by increasing stomach acid production and relaxing the LES.
  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD): GERD is a chronic condition in which the contents of the stomach flow back up into the esophagus, causing heartburn symptoms.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for heartburn?

When using herbal remedies for heartburn, it's essential to exercise caution and follow certain precautions.

For instance, consult with your healthcare provider before taking any herbal supplements, especially if you're already taking medications or have a history of underlying health conditions such as kidney disease or stomach ulcers. Some herbs like ginger, licorice root, and marshmallow root may interact with medications or worsen symptoms in certain individuals.

Additionally, be aware that herbal remedies are not regulated by the FDA, so their quality and potency can vary significantly. Look for products from reputable manufacturers and follow the recommended dosages carefully to minimize the risk of adverse effects.

Furthermore, if your heartburn symptoms persist or worsen despite trying herbal remedies, seek medical attention to rule out any underlying conditions that may require medical treatment.
