What is heartburn and how it affects your life?

Heartburn, also known as acid reflux, is a condition where stomach acid flows back up into the esophagus, causing a burning sensation in the chest or throat.

This uncomfortable feeling can negatively impact various aspects of a person's life. For instance, heartburn can disrupt sleep patterns, leading to fatigue and difficulty concentrating during the day.

It may also cause pain and discomfort during meals, making eating less enjoyable and potentially leading to weight loss or malnutrition. Additionally, heartburn can affect social interactions, as individuals may feel self-conscious about experiencing symptoms in public places or while eating with others. Furthermore, frequent or severe heartburn can result in missed work or school days, negatively impacting productivity and academic performance.

Overall, heartburn can significantly diminish a person's quality of life, causing physical discomfort, sleep disturbances, and emotional distress.

What are the main causes of heartburn?

The main causes of heartburn are associated with the digestive system.

Heartburn, also known as acid reflux, occurs when stomach acid flows back up into the esophagus, causing a burning sensation in the chest. This can be caused by several factors, but the most common ones include overeating or eating fatty or spicy foods, which can increase stomach acid production.

Additionally, lying down or bending over soon after eating can also cause heartburn, as it makes it easier for stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus. Other factors that can contribute to heartburn include being overweight, smoking, and pregnancy.

Certain medications, such as those used to treat asthma and high blood pressure, can also increase the risk of heartburn.

The most common causes of heartburn are listed below.

  • Overeating: Consuming large amounts of food at once can lead to heartburn as the stomach becomes stretched and has to produce more acid to digest the larger quantity of food.
  • Certain foods: Some foods and drinks, such as spicy foods, fatty foods, citrus fruits, tomato-based products, chocolate, caffeine, and carbonated drinks, can relax the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) and allow stomach acid to leak into the esophagus.
  • Lying down after eating: Gravity helps keep stomach acid from backing up into the esophagus. Lying down soon after eating can cause stomach acid to rise and lead to heartburn.
  • Obesity: Excess weight, particularly around the abdomen, can increase pressure on the stomach and cause stomach acid to back up into the esophagus.
  • Tight clothing: Wearing tight clothing, especially around the waist, can put pressure on the abdomen and cause stomach acid to back up into the esophagus.
  • Smoking: Smoking can weaken the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) and increase the production of stomach acid, both of which can contribute to heartburn.
  • Certain medications: Some medications, such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), antibiotics, and some blood pressure medications, can irritate the esophagus and cause heartburn.
  • Stress and anxiety: Stress and anxiety can increase the production of stomach acid and make heartburn worse.
  • Pregnancy: Hormonal changes and the growing uterus can put pressure on the stomach and cause stomach acid to back up into the esophagus, leading to heartburn.
  • Hiatal hernia: A hiatal hernia occurs when a small part of the stomach protrudes through the diaphragm and into the chest cavity. This can allow stomach acid to leak into the esophagus and cause heartburn.
