What is acid reflux and how it affects your life?

Acid reflux, also known as gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), is a condition where the stomach acid flows back up into the esophagus, causing irritation and inflammation.

This can result in a variety of uncomfortable and painful symptoms, such as heartburn, chest pain, difficulty swallowing, regurgitation of food or sour liquid, and a persistent cough or sore throat. In addition to the physical discomfort, acid reflux can also have a negative impact on a person's quality of life.

For example, it can cause sleep disturbances, leading to fatigue and difficulty concentrating during the day. It can also affect a person's ability to enjoy food, as certain foods and drinks can trigger symptoms. Furthermore, the constant need to be near a bathroom or to have antacids on hand can be embarrassing and limiting.

Acid reflux can also lead to more serious health problems, such as esophageal ulcers and Barrett's esophagus, if left untreated.

What are the main causes of acid reflux?

The main causes of acid reflux are lifestyle factors and certain medical conditions.

One common cause is frequent or overeating, which can lead to increased pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), the muscle that controls the opening between the esophagus and stomach. This can cause the LES to relax, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus.

Additionally, certain foods and drinks, such as fatty or fried foods, citrus fruits, chocolate, carbonated drinks, and alcohol, can trigger acid reflux by relaxing the LES or increasing the production of stomach acid. Obesity and pregnancy can also increase the risk of acid reflux by putting additional pressure on the stomach.

Furthermore, certain medical conditions such as hiatal hernia, gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), and scleroderma can weaken or damage the LES, leading to acid reflux.

The most common causes of acid reflux are listed below.

  • Overeating: Consuming large amounts of food can lead to acid reflux as the stomach has to produce more acid to digest the larger quantity of food, which can result in acid backing up into the esophagus.
  • Certain foods: Certain foods and drinks, such as spicy foods, fatty foods, citrus fruits, tomato-based foods, chocolate, caffeine, carbonated drinks, and alcohol, can relax the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) and allow acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese can increase the risk of acid reflux as excess weight puts pressure on the abdomen, which can cause the LES to open and allow acid to escape.
  • Lying down after eating: Lying down or bending over after eating can cause acid to flow back up into the esophagus, especially if the person has recently consumed a large meal or spicy or fatty foods.
  • Hiatal hernia: A hiatal hernia occurs when a portion of the stomach protrudes through the diaphragm and into the chest cavity, which can cause the LES to relax and allow acid to reflux into the esophagus.
  • Pregnancy: Pregnancy can cause acid reflux due to the pressure of the growing fetus on the stomach, which can cause the LES to relax and allow acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
  • Smoking: Smoking can weaken the LES and increase the production of stomach acid, both of which can contribute to acid reflux.
  • Certain medications: Certain medications, such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), aspirin, certain antibiotics, and blood pressure medications, can irritate the lining of the esophagus and increase the risk of acid reflux.
  • Stress and anxiety: Stress and anxiety can trigger acid reflux by increasing the production of stomach acid and causing the LES to relax.
  • Eating close to bedtime: Eating close to bedtime can increase the risk of acid reflux as the person lies down shortly after eating, which can cause acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
