What is gerd and how it affects your life?

Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is a chronic condition characterized by the frequent flow of stomach acid backing up into the esophagus, causing symptoms such as heartburn, chest pain, difficulty swallowing, and regurgitation of food or sour liquid.

These symptoms can significantly impact a person's quality of life, causing discomfort, pain, and embarrassment in social situations. For example, someone with GERD may experience heartburn during meals, preventing them from enjoying their food.

They may also experience regurgitation of stomach acid, leading to an unpleasant taste in their mouth and bad breath. Chronic GERD can also cause damage to the esophagus, leading to complications such as esophagitis, ulcers, and a precancerous condition called Barrett's esophagus.

Additionally, GERD can disrupt sleep, causing insomnia and daytime fatigue, and can lead to anxiety and depression due to the constant discomfort and pain.

What are the main causes of gerd?

The main causes of GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease) are attributed to factors that increase pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), a muscle that acts as a valve between the esophagus and stomach.

These factors include obesity, pregnancy, hiatal hernia, and certain lifestyle habits such as smoking, alcohol consumption, and eating large meals or lying down immediately after eating. Additionally, certain foods can relax the LES, including fatty and fried foods, chocolate, garlic, onions, caffeine, carbonated drinks, and citrus fruits or juices.

These factors can cause stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus, leading to the symptoms of GERD such as heartburn, chest pain, difficulty swallowing, and regurgitation.

The most common causes of gerd are listed below.

  • Overeating: Eating large meals can cause the stomach to produce excessive acid, leading to GERD symptoms.
  • Certain foods and drinks: High-fat foods, spicy foods, citrus fruits, chocolate, tomato-based products, garlic, onions, caffeine, carbonated drinks, and alcohol can relax the lower esophageal sphincter and allow stomach acid to flow back into the esophagus.
  • Obesity: Excess weight puts pressure on the abdomen, pushing up the stomach and causing acid to back up into the esophagus.
  • Hiatal hernia: A hiatal hernia occurs when a part of the stomach protrudes through the diaphragm into the chest cavity, which can cause acid reflux.
  • Pregnancy: The growing uterus can put pressure on the stomach, causing acid to back up into the esophagus.
  • Smoking: Nicotine can relax the lower esophageal sphincter, allowing stomach acid to flow back into the esophagus.
  • Certain medications: Certain medications, such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), aspirin, certain antibiotics, and blood pressure medications, can irritate the esophagus and cause GERD symptoms.
  • Lying down or bending over after eating: These positions can cause stomach acid to flow back into the esophagus.
  • Eating close to bedtime: Lying down shortly after eating can cause stomach acid to back up into the esophagus.
  • Stress and anxiety: While stress and anxiety do not directly cause GERD, they can exacerbate symptoms and trigger acid reflux.
