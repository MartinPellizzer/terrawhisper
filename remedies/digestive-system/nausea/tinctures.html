1. Zingiber officinale

Ginger tinctures helps with nausea because of its unique ability to interact with the digestive system.

The bioactive compounds present in ginger, such as gingerols and shogaols, have natural anti-inflammatory properties that help to reduce inflammation and alleviate symptoms associated with nausea, including stomach discomfort and bloating.

Additionally, ginger's warming properties help to stimulate digestion and relieve feelings of queasiness, providing quick relief from nausea caused by various triggers, including motion sickness, morning sickness during pregnancy, or food poisoning.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in ginger tinctures that aid with nausea.

  • Volatile oils: Ginger's volatile oils, such as gingerol and shogaol, have natural anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, which can help to reduce inflammation in the stomach and intestines that may be contributing to nausea.
  • Shogaol: Shogaol is a bioactive compound found in ginger that has been shown to have potent anti-emetic properties, meaning it can help to alleviate vomiting and nausea by blocking serotonin receptors in the brain.
  • Gingerols: Gingerols are another type of active compound found in ginger that have been shown to have anti-inflammatory and pain-relieving effects, which can help to reduce nausea caused by inflammation or irritation in the digestive tract.

The list below shows the primary parts of ginger tincture used to make tinctures for nausea.

  • Rhyzomes: The thick, underground stems of the ginger plant are rich in bioactive compounds that have anti-inflammatory and digestive properties, making them effective in soothing nausea.
  • Roots: The roots of the ginger plant contain a high concentration of shogaols and gingerols, which are responsible for its medicinal properties and can help alleviate nausea.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic ginger tincture for nausea.

  1. Harvest fresh ginger root from your garden or store-bought organic variety for processing.
  2. Chop the harvested ginger into small pieces weighing approximately 50 grams and transfer to a glass jar.
  3. Fill the jar with 95% ethanol at room temperature for a 4-hour maceration period allowing solutes to dissolve.
  4. Store the jar in a cool dark place for 14 days allowing the mixture to steep and extract active compounds slowly.
  5. Strain the liquid through cheesecloth or a coffee filter into another glass container discarding solids and label the tincture for future use.
