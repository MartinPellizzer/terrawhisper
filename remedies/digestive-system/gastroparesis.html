What is gastroparesis and how it affects your life?

Gastroparesis is a debilitating condition where the stomach muscles weaken, causing food to remain in the stomach for an extended period, leading to a range of distressing and inconvenient symptoms.

For those affected, this means living with persistent nausea, vomiting, bloating, abdominal pain, and discomfort that can last for hours or even days after eating. Daily activities become a struggle as they must constantly monitor their food intake, fearing a sudden onset of vomiting or stomach cramps.

Social gatherings, family dinners, and special occasions become stressful events as they worry about being able to eat without experiencing debilitating symptoms. Even simple tasks like grocery shopping or cooking meals become daunting challenges as they must navigate around food triggers and plan for emergency snacks. Sleep quality suffers due to the constant discomfort and bloating, leaving them feeling fatigued and irritable.

Simple pleasures like enjoying a favorite meal or snack are replaced with anxiety and dread, making everyday life feel like an exhausting struggle.

What are the main causes of gastroparesis?

The main causes of gastroparesis are a combination of gastrointestinal and systemic factors.

Idiopathic gastroparesis, also known as diabetic gastroparesis, is the most common form, accounting for approximately 60% of cases. This type of gastroparesis occurs when the muscles in the stomach become weak or are damaged, leading to delayed gastric emptying.

Other causes include diabetes, Parkinson's disease, and use of certain medications such as antidepressants and antipsychotics, which can slow down gastric motility. Additionally, gastroparesis can also be caused by hormonal imbalances, such as hypothyroidism, and gastrointestinal conditions like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).

In some cases, gastroparesis may occur due to a combination of these factors or as a side effect of other underlying medical conditions.

The most common causes of gastroparesis are listed below.

  • Diabetes: Gastroparesis is a common complication of diabetes, particularly type 1 diabetes. High blood sugar levels can damage the nerves that control digestion, leading to slowed gastric emptying and symptoms of gastroparesis.
  • Gastrointestinal surgery: Certain surgeries, such as gastric bypass or laparoscopic adjustable gastric banding (LAGB), can cause gastroparesis by damaging the stomach muscles or nerves.
  • Hormonal changes: Hormonal fluctuations during pregnancy, menopause, or polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) can affect digestion and lead to slowed gastric emptying.
  • Nerve damage: Damage to the vagus nerve or other nerves that control digestion can cause gastroparesis by disrupting normal digestive function.
  • Smoking: Smoking can damage the stomach muscles and nerves, leading to gastroparesis.
  • Medications: Certain medications, such as anticholinergics, antidepressants, and painkillers, can slow gastric emptying and contribute to gastroparesis.
  • Gastrointestinal infection: Infections such as gastritis or gastroenteritis can cause inflammation in the stomach and intestines, leading to gastroparesis.
  • Systemic sclerosis: This autoimmune disorder can damage the nerves and muscles of the gastrointestinal tract, leading to slowed gastric emptying.
  • Parkinson's disease: People with Parkinson's disease are at higher risk of developing gastroparesis due to nerve damage caused by the condition.
  • Vagus nerve stimulation therapy: In some cases, vagus nerve stimulation therapy for epilepsy or depression can cause gastroparesis as a side effect.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for gastroparesis?

When considering the use of herbal remedies for gastroparesis, it is essential to exercise caution and consult with a healthcare provider first.

Some herbs can interact with medications or exacerbate underlying conditions, potentially worsening symptoms. For instance, St. John's Wort may reduce the effectiveness of certain antidepressants, while ginkgo biloba could interfere with blood thinners.

Additionally, some herbal remedies may have unregulated dosing and quality control issues, increasing the risk of adverse effects. Before using any herbal remedy, it is crucial to discuss potential interactions and side effects with a healthcare provider, and start with low doses under close supervision. It is also important to choose high-quality products from reputable manufacturers and follow recommended dosage guidelines carefully.
