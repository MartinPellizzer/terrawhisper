What is gastroparesis and how it affects your life?

Gastroparesis is a gastrointestinal disorder characterized by the slow emptying of the stomach, despite the absence of a blockage.

This condition can have a significant impact on a person's life, causing a wide range of negative symptoms. For example, individuals with gastroparesis may experience nausea, vomiting, and chronic abdominal pain, making it difficult to maintain a consistent diet and causing significant weight loss.

Additionally, they may also have to deal with malnutrition, dehydration, and fatigue due to poor absorption of nutrients. Furthermore, the symptoms of gastroparesis can lead to social isolation, anxiety, and depression as individuals may become fearful of eating or experience discomfort in social situations involving food.

In some cases, gastroparesis can also cause life-threatening complications, such as bacterial infections and esophageal damage, requiring hospitalization and intensive medical treatment.

What are the main causes of gastroparesis?

The main causes of gastroparesis are damage to the vagus nerve, which controls the movement of muscles in the digestive tract, and diabetes.

The vagus nerve can be damaged due to surgery, infection, or trauma. When the vagus nerve is damaged, the muscles in the stomach and intestines may not work properly, leading to gastroparesis.

Diabetes is another common cause of gastroparesis. High blood sugar levels can damage the nerves and muscles in the stomach, impairing its ability to empty food.

Other possible causes of gastroparesis include certain medications, Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, and scleroderma.

The most common causes of gastroparesis are listed below.

  • Diabetes: Long-term uncontrolled diabetes can damage the vagus nerve, leading to delayed gastric emptying.
  • Viral infections: Certain viral infections, such as the norovirus or rotavirus, can cause inflammation of the stomach lining and lead to gastroparesis.
  • Post-surgical complications: Gastroparesis can occur as a complication following certain surgeries, including gastric bypass or other upper gastrointestinal surgeries.
  • Medications: Certain medications, particularly narcotics, anticholinergics, and some antidepressants, can slow gastric emptying and contribute to gastroparesis.
  • Parkinson's disease: Parkinson's disease and other neurological disorders can affect the autonomic nervous system, leading to gastroparesis.
  • Amyloidosis: Amyloidosis is a condition where an abnormal protein called amyloid builds up in organs and tissues, including the stomach, leading to gastroparesis.
  • Scleroderma: Scleroderma is a connective tissue disorder that can cause thickening and hardening of the stomach wall, leading to delayed gastric emptying.
  • Radiation therapy: Radiation therapy for cancer treatment can damage the stomach and vagus nerve, resulting in gastroparesis.
  • Hypothyroidism: An underactive thyroid gland (hypothyroidism) can slow down digestion and contribute to gastroparesis.
  • Eating disorders: Eating disorders, such as anorexia nervosa or bulimia, can cause delayed gastric emptying and contribute to gastroparesis.
