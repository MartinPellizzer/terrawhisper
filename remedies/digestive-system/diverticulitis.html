What is diverticulitis and how it affects your life?

Diverticulitis is a condition that occurs when small pouches or sacs, called diverticula, form in the wall of the colon and become inflamed or infected.

This can lead to various negative impacts on a person's life. For instance, diverticulitis can cause severe abdominal pain and cramping, which can make it difficult to perform daily activities or even move around comfortably.

Additionally, it can lead to fever, nausea, and vomiting, all of which can result in missed work or school days. In more severe cases, diverticulitis can cause complications such as abscesses, perforations, or blockages in the intestine, which may require hospitalization and surgery.

Moreover, the fear of recurring symptoms can cause anxiety and stress, affecting a person's mental health and overall quality of life.

What are the main causes of diverticulitis?

The main causes of diverticulitis are thought to be a low-fiber diet and increased pressure within the colon.

A low-fiber diet can lead to constipation and hard stools, which can cause straining during bowel movements and increase pressure within the colon. Over time, this increased pressure can cause weak spots in the colon wall to bulge out and form small pouches called diverticula. When these pouches become inflamed or infected, it is known as diverticulitis.

Other factors that may contribute to the development of diverticulitis include obesity, smoking, and a lack of physical activity.

The most common causes of diverticulitis are listed below.

  • Low-fiber diet: A diet lacking in sufficient amounts of fiber can lead to constipation and increased pressure in the colon, which can cause diverticula to form and increase the risk of diverticulitis.
  • Lack of physical activity: Sedentary lifestyle can slow down the digestion process and increase the pressure in the colon, leading to diverticulitis.
  • Obesity: Excess body weight, particularly abdominal obesity, can increase the risk of developing diverticulitis.
  • Age: The risk of diverticulitis increases with age, as the walls of the colon can weaken over time.
  • Smoking: Smoking can increase inflammation and decrease blood flow to the colon, increasing the risk of diverticulitis.
  • Certain medications: Long-term use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), corticosteroids, and opioids can increase the risk of diverticulitis.
  • Genetics: A family history of diverticulitis may increase the risk of developing the condition.
  • Gastrointestinal disorders: Conditions such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), chronic constipation, and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) can increase the risk of diverticulitis.
  • Suppressed immune system: A weakened immune system due to conditions such as HIV/AIDS or immunosuppressive medications can increase the risk of diverticulitis.
  • Previous history of diverticulitis: A history of previous episodes of diverticulitis can increase the risk of recurrent episodes.
