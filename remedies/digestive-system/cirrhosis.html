What is cirrhosis and how it affects your life?

Cirrhosis is a severe liver disease that can have a profound impact on an individual's quality of life.

Characterized by scar tissue that replaces healthy liver tissue, cirrhosis can lead to a range of debilitating symptoms and complications. One of the most significant effects of cirrhosis is fatigue, which can make it difficult to perform even the simplest daily tasks, such as getting out of bed or taking a shower. Digestive problems, including bloating, abdominal pain, and diarrhea or constipation, are also common, making mealtime a constant struggle.

As cirrhosis progresses, liver function begins to decline, leading to issues with blood clotting, which can result in frequent nosebleeds, bruising easily, and poor wound healing. Additionally, cirrhosis can cause yellowing of the skin (jaundice), dark urine, and pale stools, which can significantly affect an individual's self-esteem and body image. Furthermore, the disease can also lead to memory loss, confusion, and mood changes, making it challenging to maintain relationships and perform tasks that require focus and concentration.

Overall, cirrhosis can turn daily life into a constant battle, leaving individuals feeling exhausted, frustrated, and defeated.

What are the main causes of cirrhosis?

The main causes of cirrhosis are chronic liver damage and scarring that can occur due to a variety of factors.

Alcoholic liver disease, which is caused by long-term excessive consumption of alcohol, is one of the most common causes of cirrhosis. Other causes include viral hepatitis B and C infections, which can lead to inflammation and scarring in the liver.

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), which is often associated with obesity, diabetes, and metabolic syndrome, is also a major risk factor for cirrhosis. Additionally, certain medications, such as acetaminophen overdose or long-term use of medications like methotrexate, can cause liver damage that may lead to cirrhosis.

In some cases, inherited disorders such as alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency can also increase the risk of developing cirrhosis.

The most common causes of cirrhosis are listed below.

  • Alcoholism: Chronic heavy drinking can lead to liver damage and scarring, ultimately causing cirrhosis.
  • Viral Hepatitis (A, B, and C): Infections from these viruses can cause chronic inflammation and scarring in the liver, leading to cirrhosis.
  • Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD): A condition characterized by excess fat buildup in the liver, often associated with obesity, diabetes, and high cholesterol.
  • Autoimmune Hepatitis: An immune system disorder that attacks the liver, leading to inflammation and scarring.
  • Primary Biliary Cholangitis: A chronic autoimmune disease that damages the bile ducts in the liver, causing cirrhosis and liver failure.
  • Hemochromatosis: A genetic disorder that causes excess iron absorption, which can lead to liver damage and cirrhosis if left untreated.
  • Wilson's Disease: A rare genetic disorder that causes an accumulation of copper in the liver, leading to liver damage and cirrhosis.
  • Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency: An inherited disorder that affects protein production, causing liver damage and scarring.
  • Schistosomiasis: Infection with parasitic worms (schistosomes) that live in the bloodstream, which can cause liver damage and cirrhosis.
  • Non-viral Hepatitis: A group of causes not related to viral infections, including medication-induced liver injury, metabolic disorders, and other conditions.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for cirrhosis?

When considering the use of herbal remedies for cirrhosis, it is essential to exercise caution and consult with a healthcare provider first.

Some herbal remedies may interact with prescription medications or worsen liver function, particularly in individuals with compromised liver health. Certain herbs like St. John's Wort, Ginkgo biloba, and SAMe can decrease the effectiveness of medications used to treat cirrhosis, such as antivirals and immunosuppressants.

Additionally, herbal remedies like milk thistle and turmeric may cause allergic reactions or interact with blood thinners in people with liver disease. Therefore, it is crucial to discuss any planned use of herbal remedies with a healthcare provider, who can help weigh the potential benefits against the risks and recommend appropriate dosages and combinations.

Furthermore, choose high-quality products from reputable manufacturers and follow proper preparation and storage guidelines to minimize exposure to contaminants or adulterants.

The most important precautions to take when using herbal remedies for cirrhosis are listed below.

  • Consult before taking any herbal remedy, as some may interact with prescription medications or worsen liver function in people with cirrhosis.
  • Research thoroughly to ensure the herb you choose has been scientifically studied and proven safe for liver health.
  • Start with small doses and gradually increase as needed, under medical supervision if possible, to avoid overwhelming your liver.
  • Be aware of potential allergic reactions or side effects and stop taking the remedy immediately if any adverse symptoms occur.
  • Monitor blood tests and liver function regularly while using herbal remedies to detect any potential harm.
  • Inform your healthcare provider about all herbal supplements you are taking, as they may affect treatment plans or medication dosages.
  • Consider consulting a licensed practitioner of herbal medicine, such as an ND (naturopathic doctor) or herbalist, for personalized guidance.
  • Be cautious when choosing products that claim to "cure" cirrhosis or liver disease, as there is no scientific evidence to support these claims.
  • Keep in mind that the quality and potency of herbal supplements can vary greatly between brands and sources, so choose reputable manufacturers whenever possible.
  • Avoid using unproven remedies or those with unknown ingredients, which may put your health at risk.
