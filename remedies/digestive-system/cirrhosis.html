What is cirrhosis and how it affects your life?

Cirrhosis is a chronic liver disease characterized by the replacement of healthy liver tissue with scar tissue, leading to the loss of liver function.

This condition can have numerous negative impacts on a person's life. For instance, it can cause fatigue, weakness, and loss of appetite, making it difficult to maintain a normal daily routine or enjoy meals. Cirrhosis can also lead to fluid accumulation in the abdomen, known as ascites, which can result in discomfort and difficulty moving.

Additionally, it can result in jaundice, a condition characterized by the yellowing of the skin and eyes. Moreover, cirrhosis can increase the risk of developing liver cancer and other life-threatening complications. As the disease progresses, it can lead to liver failure, which may require a liver transplant.

All these factors can significantly reduce a person's quality of life, making it essential to seek medical attention and manage the condition promptly.

What are the main causes of cirrhosis?

The main causes of cirrhosis are long-term damage to the liver due to alcohol abuse and chronic viral hepatitis (hepatitis B and C).

Other causes include non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), which is linked to obesity, diabetes, and high cholesterol levels, as well as autoimmune hepatitis, genetic disorders such as hemochromatosis and Wilson's disease, and prolonged exposure to certain toxins and medications.

Cirrhosis is a progressive disease, and as the liver tries to repair itself, scar tissue forms, eventually leading to liver failure if left untreated.

The most common causes of cirrhosis are listed below.

  • Hepatitis C: A viral infection that attacks the liver, causing inflammation and damage, which can lead to cirrhosis over time.
  • Hepatitis B: Another viral infection that can cause liver damage and cirrhosis, especially if left untreated.
  • Alcohol abuse: Long-term, heavy drinking of alcohol can cause alcoholic liver disease, which can progress to cirrhosis.
  • Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD): A condition characterized by the accumulation of fat in the liver, which can cause inflammation and cirrhosis.
  • Hepatitis D: A viral infection that only occurs in people who already have hepatitis B, and can accelerate the progression to cirrhosis.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis: A condition in which the body's immune system attacks the liver, causing inflammation and cirrhosis.
  • Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC): A rare, chronic liver disease that slowly destroys the bile ducts in the liver, leading to cirrhosis.
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC): A rare, chronic liver disease that causes inflammation and scarring of the bile ducts, which can result in cirrhosis.
  • Hemochromatosis: An inherited disorder that causes the body to absorb too much iron, which can accumulate in the liver and cause cirrhosis.
  • Wilson's disease: A genetic disorder that affects copper metabolism, causing copper to accumulate in the liver and other organs, leading to cirrhosis.
