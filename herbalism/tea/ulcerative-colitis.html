10 best herbal teas for ulcerative colitis

For those with ulcerative colitis, certain herbal teas may provide relief and promote gut health.

Slippery elm tea is known for its soothing properties, which can help protect and heal the inflamed gut lining. Marshmallow root tea has a similar effect, creating a protective barrier in the digestive tract.

Chamomile tea is another option, as it has anti-inflammatory properties that may reduce inflammation in the gut. Additionally, its calming effect can help alleviate stress and anxiety related to the condition.

However, it is essential to consult with a healthcare provider before incorporating herbal teas into a treatment plan, as some herbs can interact with medications or worsen symptoms.

1. Slippery Elm

Slippery elm tea provides relief for ulcerative colitis due to its demulcent property, which involves a softening or soothing effect.

This tea contains mucilage, a substance that turns into a gel when mixed with water, creating a protective barrier over the gut's lining. By coating the irritated tissues, slippery elm tea lessens inflammation and eases painful digestive symptoms. Furthermore, this tea's antioxidant qualities help combat harmful free radicals, reducing damage in the colon.

Lastly, slippery elm tea encourages the production of more mucus in the gastrointestinal tract, supporting the body's natural healing processes.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in slippery elm tea that help with ulcerative colitis.

  • Bark: The bark of the slippery elm plant contains mucilage, a substance that becomes a slick gel when mixed with water. This gel can coat the digestive tract, potentially protecting it from irritation and reducing symptoms of ulcerative colitis.
  • Leaves: Slippery elm leaves also contain mucilage. In tea form, these leaves can help soothe the gastrointestinal tract, potentially reducing inflammation associated with ulcerative colitis.
  • Roots: While not as commonly used as the bark or leaves, the roots of the slippery elm plant also contain mucilage. As a result, they can be used in tea to help coat and soothe the digestive tract in a similar way.
  • Buds: Slippery elm buds are a lesser-known source of the plant's mucilage. When brewed into a tea, these buds can help form a soothing coating in the digestive tract, potentially alleviating symptoms of ulcerative colitis.

Furthermore, the tea's antioxidant qualities impede oxidative stress, contributing to the overall well-being of individuals combating ulcerative colitis.

3. Chamomile

Chamomile tea contains anti-inflammatory properties due to its high concentration of flavonoids, particularly a compound called apigenin.

Apigenin inhibits the release of inflammatory chemicals, thereby reducing inflammation in the gut. This directly counteracts the primary symptom of ulcerative colitis, which is inflammation in the colon.

Additionally, chamomile tea boasts antispasmodic properties that soothe the intestines, alleviating the abdominal pain and cramping associated with ulcerative colitis. Moreover, it promotes gastrointestinal health by enhancing the quality of mucus lining the gut, fostering a more robust barrier against harmful bacteria.

Consequently, chamomile tea's multifaceted therapeutic attributes make it a valuable asset in managing ulcerative colitis.

4. Licorice

Licorice tea's effectiveness in alleviating ulcerative colitis symptoms is a direct result of its active components, particularly glycyrrhizin and flavonoids.

These substances help decrease inflammation in the digestive tract, providing soothing relief. Consequently, the consumption of licorice tea contributes to the restoration of a healthy gut environment, diminishing the discomfort and distress associated with ulcerative colitis.

However, it's crucial to consult with a healthcare professional before incorporating licorice tea into a treatment regimen, as excessive consumption of glycyrrhizin may lead to side effects such as high blood pressure and potassium depletion.
