10 Best Herbal Teas For Breastfeeding

Herbal teas, particularly those with fenugreek, blessed thistle, and fennel, are known to increase milk production in breastfeeding mothers.

This is due to the phytoestrogen content in these herbs, which can stimulate mammary gland growth and milk production. Additionally, these herbs can help to strengthen uterine contractions during labor and postpartum, making them beneficial for both prenatal and postnatal use. Furthermore, herbal teas can provide hydration and soothe digestive issues, which can be helpful for both the mother and the baby.

However, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before consuming herbal teas during breastfeeding, as some herbs can have adverse effects or interact with medications.

1. Fenugreek

Fenugreek tea helps with breastfeeding because it contains phytoestrogens, which can increase milk production by mimicking the effects of estrogen in the body.

These compounds stimulate the growth of mammary gland tissue, leading to an increased supply of breast milk. Additionally, fenugreek has been found to contain galactagogues, or substances that promote lactation, which can help new mothers establish a strong milk supply. Furthermore, fenugreek tea is a popular natural remedy for boosting milk production, and many breastfeeding mothers report seeing an improvement in their milk supply within 24 to 72 hours of starting to drink it.

However, it's important to note that while fenugreek tea is generally considered safe, it may cause side effects like bloating, gas, and diarrhea in some people, and pregnant women should avoid it due to its potential to induce labor.

The following procedure explains how to make fenugreek tea for breastfeeding.

  1. In a tea pot, add 1 teaspoon of dried fenugreek seeds to 1 cup of boiling water, then let it steep for 10-15 minutes to allow the active compounds to be released.
  2. Strain the tea to remove the seeds, then add honey or sugar to taste, as the natural bitterness of fenugreek can be off-putting to some.
  3. Warm up the tea if necessary, as it should be consumed while still hot to maximize the benefits and make it more soothing for breastfeeding.
  4. Drink 2-3 cups of fenugreek tea daily, ideally between meals, to help increase breast milk supply and ensure regular and consistent intake.
  5. Monitor your body's response, as fenugreek can have side effects such as bloating, gas, and diarrhea, and adjust the dosage or discontinue use if necessary. Consult with a healthcare provider before using fenugreek if you have any pre-existing medical conditions or are taking any medications.

2. Blessed Thistle

Blessed thistle tea helps with breastfeeding because it is known to be a galactagogue, a substance that promotes milk flow.

The tea is made from the leaves of the blessed thistle plant, which has been used traditionally to increase milk production in breastfeeding mothers. The active compounds in blessed thistle, such as flavonoids and sesquiterpene lactones, are believed to stimulate the production of milk-producing hormones in the body. Additionally, the tea is considered safe for both mother and baby, making it a popular choice among breastfeeding mothers.

However, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before taking blessed thistle or any other herbal supplement to ensure it is safe and appropriate for individual circumstances.

The following procedure explains how to make blessed thistle tea for breastfeeding.

  1. To make blessed thistle tea for breastfeeding, start by boiling 8 ounces of water and letting it cool down for a few minutes until it reaches a comfortable temperature for brewing.
  2. In a tea infuser or teapot with a built-in strainer, add 1-2 teaspoons of dried blessed thistle flowers and leaves.
  3. Pour the hot water over the blessed thistle and let it steep for 5-10 minutes to extract the beneficial compounds and achieve the desired strength of flavor.
  4. After steeping, remove the tea infuser or strain the tea to discard the spent blessed thistle.
  5. For best results, enjoy 2-3 cups of blessed thistle tea daily, adding honey or lemon to taste if desired. Remember to consult with a healthcare provider before using herbal teas for breastfeeding.

3. Fennel

Fennel tea helps with breastfeeding because it contains a natural compound called anethole, which is known to increase milk flow and promote breast milk production.

This herbal remedy has a long history of use in promoting lactation and soothing digestive issues in both mothers and infants. Fennel tea may also help alleviate common breastfeeding challenges, such as engorgement and mastitis, by promoting the release of hormones that aid in let-down and milk ejection. Furthermore, its galactagogue properties can help mothers maintain a sufficient milk supply, ensuring their babies receive the necessary nutrients for healthy growth and development.

Lastly, fennel tea is a caffeine-free, non-pharmacological alternative that is generally considered safe for both mothers and infants, making it an attractive option for those seeking natural support during the breastfeeding journey.

The following procedure explains how to make fennel tea for breastfeeding.

  1. Thoroughly rinse and roughly chop 1 tablespoon of fennel seeds, then add them to 1 cup of boiling water, allowing the seeds to steep for 10-15 minutes before straining the mixture to remove the seeds.
  2. While the fennel tea is still warm, squeeze half of a lemon into the tea, releasing the juice and pulp into the liquid, then stir well to combine the citrus flavor.
  3. Optional: add a small slice of ginger root or a cinnamon stick during the steeping process for added flavor and potential additional benefits.
  4. Allow the tea to cool slightly, then taste and add honey or another sweetener to taste, if desired, stirring until fully dissolved.
  5. Drink 2-3 cups of this tea daily, ideally between feedings, to potentially help increase milk supply and soothe digestion for both mother and baby.

4. Nettle

Nettle tea helps with breastfeeding because it is rich in nutrients and minerals, including vitamin K, calcium, and iron, which can support milk production and the overall health of both the mother and baby.

The tea's anti-inflammatory properties may also help reduce inflammation and discomfort in the breasts associated with breastfeeding. Furthermore, nettle tea is known to boost iron levels, which can help prevent anemia in new mothers, a common issue that can affect milk supply. Additionally, nettle tea has a mild diuretic effect, which can help eliminate excess fluids in the body and support the let-down reflex during breastfeeding.

However, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before consuming nettle tea or any other herbal remedy while breastfeeding.

The following procedure explains how to make nettle tea for breastfeeding.

  1. Gather 2-3 teaspoons of fresh nettle leaves (Urtica dioica) or 1-2 teaspoons of dried nettle leaves, rinse well, and place them in a tea infuser or a reusable tea bag.
  2. Bring 1 cup of fresh water to a boil, then pour it over the nettle-filled infuser in a teapot or a heat-resistant mug.
  3. Allow the tea to steep for 5-10 minutes, depending on your desired strength and taste, ensuring the nutrients from the nettle leaves are well-infused.
  4. Remove the tea infuser or tea bag, and let the tea cool down to a comfortable temperature, ideally around 98.6-100.4°F (37-38°C), to maintain breastmilk quality.
  5. Sip and enjoy the nettle tea as part of your breastfeeding routine, ensuring proper hydration and potential health benefits for you and your baby.

5. Alfalfa

Alfalfa tea helps with breastfeeding because it is rich in phytoestrogens, which are known to increase milk production in breastfeeding mothers.

The tea also contains essential vitamins and minerals, such as vitamin K, calcium, and iron, which are necessary for both the mother and the baby's health. Additionally, alfalfa tea has been used traditionally to treat low milk supply, and its consumption can help new mothers increase their milk supply over time. Furthermore, alfalfa tea is caffeine-free and can be a great alternative to other caffeinated beverages that may interfere with breastfeeding.

However, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before consuming alfalfa tea or any other herbal supplement to ensure its safety and effectiveness.

The following procedure explains how to make alfalfa tea for breastfeeding.

  1. To make alfalfa tea for breastfeeding, start by boiling 1 cup of water and adding 1-2 teaspoons of dried alfalfa leaves; let it steep for 5-10 minutes.
  2. Strain the tea to remove the leaves, then allow the liquid to cool down to a comfortable drinking temperature.
  3. Optionally, add a natural sweetener like honey or stevia to taste.
  4. Drink 2-3 cups of alfalfa tea daily, preferably between meals.
  5. Remember to consult with a healthcare provider before consuming alfalfa tea if you have any health concerns or are taking medication.

6. Goat'S Rue

Goat's rue tea helps with breastfeeding because it contains a compound called galegine, which is believed to stimulate milk production in breastfeeding mothers.

This herbal tea has been traditionally used by women to increase their milk supply and is often recommended by herbalists and lactation consultants. Goat's rue tea is also thought to have a galactagogue effect, which means it can enhance the flow of breast milk. Furthermore, it is caffeine-free and can be safely consumed in moderation during breastfeeding.

However, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before taking goat's rue tea or any other herbal supplement to ensure its safety and effectiveness.

The following procedure explains how to make goat's rue tea for breastfeeding.

  1. To make goat's rue tea for breastfeeding, bring one cup of water to a boil, then add 1 teaspoon of dried goat's rue leaves and let it steep for 10-15 minutes.
  2. Strain the tea to remove the leaves, then allow the liquid to cool down to a drinkable temperature.
  3. Optional: add honey or another sweetener to taste, as goat's rue has a slightly bitter flavor.
  4. Drink three cups of this tea daily, preferably between meals, to help increase milk supply.
  5. Continue this regimen for several days to two weeks, and monitor your breast milk production to see if there is an improvement. If you do not notice a change, discontinue use or consult a healthcare professional for further guidance.

7. Marshmallow Root

Marshmallow root tea helps with breastfeeding because it is known to increase milk production in breastfeeding mothers.

This is due to the tea's high mucilage content, which can help to stimulate the let-down reflex and increase milk flow. Additionally, marshmallow root tea has anti-inflammatory properties, which can help to reduce inflammation and discomfort in the breasts that can sometimes occur during breastfeeding. The tea is also considered safe for both the mother and the baby, making it a popular choice among breastfeeding mothers.

Furthermore, marshmallow root tea is caffeine-free and can help to promote hydration, which is essential for breast milk production.

The following procedure explains how to make marshmallow root tea for breastfeeding.

  1. To make marshmallow root tea for breastfeeding, start by boiling 1 quart of water in a pot, then add 1-2 teaspoons of dried marshmallow root and let it steep for 3-5 minutes.
  2. After steeping, turn off the heat and cover the pot, allowing the tea to cool down to a safe temperature for drinking.
  3. Strain the tea to remove the marshmallow root pieces, then pour the liquid into a glass or mug.
  4. For best results, drink 2-3 cups of marshmallow root tea per day, spacing them out evenly throughout the day.
  5. If you prefer a sweeter taste, add a small amount of honey to the tea, but avoid adding sugar or artificial sweeteners.

8. Milk Thistle

Milk thistle tea helps with breastfeeding because it contains silymarin, a flavonoid that is known to increase milk production in lactating women.

This compound stimulates the mammary glands, promoting the secretion of breast milk. Moreover, milk thistle is considered safe for both the mother and the baby, making it a popular choice among breastfeeding mothers. Additionally, milk thistle has been shown to have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, which can help support the overall health and well-being of both the mother and the baby during breastfeeding.

However, it is always recommended that breastfeeding mothers consult with their healthcare provider before taking any herbal supplements, including milk thistle tea.

The following procedure explains how to make milk thistle tea for breastfeeding.

  1. To make milk thistle tea for breastfeeding, start by boiling 8 ounces of water and adding 1-2 teaspoons of dried milk thistle seeds to a tea infuser or teapot.
  2. Allow the seeds to steep in the hot water for 10-15 minutes to extract the beneficial compounds and achieve a strong, fragrant tea.
  3. After steeping, remove the tea infuser or strain the tea to discard the spent seeds, leaving only the milk thistle-infused liquid.
  4. For added flavor and benefits, consider adding 1 teaspoon of honey and a squeeze of lemon juice to the tea before stirring well.
  5. Serve the milk thistle tea warm and enjoy it up to three times a day, as needed, to support breastfeeding and overall health.

9. Red Raspberry Leaf

Red raspberry leaf tea helps with breastfeeding because it contains fragarine, a compound known to tone and strengthen the uterus, which can lead to improved milk production.

Additionally, this herbal tea is rich in vitamins and minerals, such as vitamin B complex, calcium, and iron, that support postpartum recovery and overall lactation health. The tea's soothing properties may also alleviate common breastfeeding discomforts, like engorgement and mastitis, by promoting let-down and milk flow. Furthermore, regular consumption of red raspberry leaf tea has been anecdotally linked to increased breast milk supply, making it a popular choice among breastfeeding mothers.

However, it is essential to consult a healthcare provider before incorporating red raspberry leaf tea or any other herbal remedy into a breastfeeding routine.

The following procedure explains how to make red raspberry leaf tea for breastfeeding.

  1. Bring 1 cup of water to a boil, then add 1-2 teaspoons of dried red raspberry leaf (depending on desired strength); reduce heat and simmer, covered, for 10-15 minutes.
  2. Turn off heat and let the tea steep for an additional 5-10 minutes to increase the potency of the medicinal compounds.
  3. Strain the tea through a fine-mesh sieve into a mug, discarding the used leaves.
  4. Optional: add honey or lemon to taste, but avoid adding sugar or artificial sweeteners.
  5. Drink 1-3 cups of this tea daily, preferably between meals, to support breastfeeding and promote postpartum recovery.

10. Chamomile

Chamomile tea helps with breastfeeding because it contains phytoestrogens, which can increase milk supply in breastfeeding mothers.

Additionally, chamomile has calming properties that can help both the mother and the baby relax, reducing stress and anxiety. The tea is also caffeine-free, making it a safe choice for breastfeeding mothers. Furthermore, chamomile tea has anti-inflammatory properties that can help soothe engorged breasts and alleviate discomfort during breastfeeding.

Finally, chamomile tea is a natural and herbal remedy, making it a popular choice among breastfeeding mothers who prefer to avoid chemical-based medications.

The following procedure explains how to make chamomile tea for breastfeeding.

  1. To make chamomile tea for breastfeeding, start by boiling 8 ounces of water and adding 1-2 teaspoons of dried chamomile flowers or 1 chamomile tea bag to a cup.
  2. Allow the chamomile to steep in the hot water for 5-10 minutes to extract the flavor and medicinal properties.
  3. While the tea is still warm, add 1-2 teaspoons of honey or sugar to taste, if desired.
  4. Carefully strain out the chamomile flowers, if using, before drinking.
  5. Drink 1-3 cups of chamomile tea daily, as needed, to help promote milk production and provide relaxation. However, it's always best to consult with a healthcare provider before consuming herbal teas while breastfeeding.
