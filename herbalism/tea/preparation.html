How To Make Effective Medicinal Herbal Tea In 5 Simple Steps (Beginner Guide)

Herbal tea offers many health benefits thanks to antioxidants, vitamins, and minerals. For example, Chamomile, Peppermint, and Ginger tea support digestion, aid relaxation, boost immunity, reduce inflammation, promote better sleep, assist weight management, and can even alleviate stress

The process of making effective medicinal herbal tea involves several steps. The steps listed below summarize the entire process and are best suited for beginners who are approacing herbal remedies for the first time.

  1. Choose the appropriate herb based on its benefits
  2. Purchase tea bags over bulk herbs (for convenience and quality assurance)
  3. Boil water (best if filtered) using a pot or a kettle to better extract beneficial compounds from the herbs
  4. Steep the tea bag in the boiled water for 5 minutes
  5. Avoid adding sugar or sweeteners, as they can counteract the tea's health benefits

In this article you'll get a detailed description of all the above step. By the end of this practical guide, you'll know how to make effective medicinal herbal tea in a fast and easy way.

2. Buy the herb

When you've selected the right herb that solves your problem, it's time to visit a grocery store and buy that herb. More specifically, buy the herb packaged as tea bags.

WARNING: Don't buy loose herbs from a herbalist's shop if you are a beginner. There's a high chance you're going to have a bad experience if you don't know how to assess their quality. So, stick with buying tea bags until you become more experienced.

Commercial tea bags are convenient and easy to use. They are easily portable, accessible, and have a long shelf life. They are consistent in flavor and potency, and they are cost-effective in small quantities.

What are the pros and cons of buying loose herbs in bulk?

Pros of cultivating your own herbs:

  • Cost-effective
  • Customization
  • Reduced Packaging Waste
  • Freshness and Quality
  • Versatility

Cons of cultivating your own herbs:

  • Storage Challenges
  • Limited Shelf Life
  • Risk of Contamination
  • Lack of Variety
  • Initial Investment

On the good side, it's cheaper buying loose herbs in bulk because you get more for less money, and you can mix herbs together however you like. Plus, there's less waste from packaging. Also, you might get fresher and better-quality herbs.

But it's not all easy. Storing them properly can be tricky, and they can lose their flavor or strength over time. There's also a risk of them getting dirty or spoiled if not handled carefully (from both you and the seller). Sometimes, you might not find all the herbs you want in bulk, and it might cost more at first because you're buying a lot.

The following list gives you practical advice and tips for buying loose herbs in bulk.

  • Research and Plan: Know your needs beforehand. Research the herbs you want to buy, their uses, and the quantity you'll require.
  • Quality Check: Look for reputable sources or stores that offer high-quality herbs. Check for freshness, aroma, and color before purchasing.
  • Buy in Moderation: Purchase quantities that you can reasonably use within a reasonable timeframe to avoid spoilage or loss of potency.
  • Proper Storage: Ensure proper storage in airtight containers, away from light, heat, and moisture to maintain freshness and potency. Label containers for easy identification.
  • Check for Contaminants: Inspect the herbs for any signs of contamination, such as mold, insects, or debris. Ensure hygiene and cleanliness during handling.
  • Consider Variety: While buying in bulk, consider the variety of herbs available and whether they suit your requirements. Some herbs might be more readily available in pre-packaged form.
  • Ask Questions: Don't hesitate to ask the seller about the herb's origin, cultivation methods, and any other queries you might have regarding the product.
  • Compare Prices: Compare prices from different suppliers to ensure you're getting a good deal on the bulk purchase.
  • Start Small: If you're new to buying bulk herbs, start with smaller quantities to test quality and how quickly you can use them.
  • Use Gradually: Incorporate the herbs into your routine gradually, allowing you to understand their potency and how they complement your needs.
  • Experiment and Enjoy: Have fun experimenting with different blends and uses for your bulk herbs. Enjoy the process of creating custom combinations.

What are the pros and cons of cultivating your own herbs?

Pros of cultivating your own herbs:

  • Freshness
  • Cost-saving

  • Customization
  • Therapeutic benefits

Cons of cultivating your own herbs:

  • Time-consuming
  • Space limitations
  • Seasonal challenges
  • Learning curve

Growing your own herbs has its advantages. You get fresh herbs right when you need them, making your food taste better. It saves money too, as you don't have to buy them all the time.

You can be sure they're good quality, because you're the one growing them. It's easy to grab what you need from your garden or pots at home. You can grow the herbs you like best and try new ones too.

Taking care of plants can also make you feel good. But, it takes time and effort to look after them properly. You might not have enough space for all the herbs you want. Weather changes can affect how well they grow.

It might take a while to learn how to take care of each herb right. Bugs and diseases can harm your plants if you're not careful. And at the start, you need to buy stuff like pots and soil, which can cost some money.

Here's a list of tips to help you cultivate your own herbs successfully:

  • Choose the Right Location: Select a spot with adequate sunlight (most herbs need at least 6 hours of sunlight daily) and good drainage for your herb garden or pots.
  • Start with Good Quality Soil: Use well-draining, nutrient-rich soil or potting mix suitable for herbs. Consider adding compost for extra nutrients.
  • Select Herbs Wisely: Begin with a few easy-to-grow herbs like basil, mint, rosemary, or thyme. Research their specific needs regarding sunlight, water, and space.
  • Watering Carefully: Ensure consistent moisture for herbs without waterlogging. Water when the top inch of soil feels dry, but avoid overwatering, as some herbs are sensitive to excessive moisture.
  • Pruning and Harvesting: Regularly prune herbs to encourage growth and prevent them from becoming too leggy. Harvest leaves frequently but avoid taking more than a third of the plant at once to promote continuous growth.
  • Fertilize Appropriately: Use organic fertilizers or compost to provide nutrients to your herbs. Follow recommended guidelines for application and frequency.
  • Pest and Disease Control: Monitor your plants regularly for pests and diseases. Use organic pest control methods or natural remedies to address issues without harmful chemicals.
  • Container Size and Placement: If planting in pots, choose containers with sufficient drainage and appropriate size for each herb's root system. Ensure proper spacing to prevent overcrowding.

  • Seasonal Care: Herbs may require different care during various seasons. Protect them from frost or extreme weather conditions, and consider bringing potted herbs indoors in colder climates.

  • Continuous Learning: Keep learning about your herbs' needs and growth patterns. Experiment with different techniques and observe what works best for your specific plants.

But, it's risky because if you mistake one plant for another, it can be dangerous. Some herbs might get harmed if too many people take them from nature.

Also, not all herbs are easy to find, and the quality might change from place to place. Sometimes, there are rules about picking plants from the wild, so you must follow them to avoid problems.

Here's a list of tips for harvesting herbs in the wild:

  • Learn plant identification: Before picking any wild herbs, thoroughly research and understand their characteristics to avoid misidentification with poisonous plants.
  • Use reliable guides or experts: Consult field guides, experienced foragers, or local experts to learn about safe harvesting practices and to confirm plant identification.
  • Respect regulations: Check local regulations and laws regarding foraging in the area. Obtain necessary permits and avoid protected or sensitive areas.
  • Harvest sustainably: Only collect herbs in quantities that won't harm the plant population. Take no more than what you need and avoid damaging the surrounding environment.
  • Choose clean areas: Harvest herbs from locations free of pollution, pesticides, or other contaminants that could affect their safety for consumption.
  • Be mindful of ecosystems: Respect the balance of nature by not disrupting habitats or ecosystems while harvesting. Avoid trampling on other plants or disturbing wildlife.
  • Ethical foraging: Practice ethical foraging by not uprooting entire plants unnecessarily. Instead, harvest leaves, flowers, or seeds carefully, allowing the plant to regrow.
  • Time your harvest: Gather herbs at the right time. Research the best season or time of day for optimal flavor and potency.
  • Bring necessary tools: Carry appropriate tools like scissors, small knives, or baskets to harvest herbs gently and efficiently.
  • Leave no trace: Clean up after harvesting by leaving the area as undisturbed as possible. Dispose of any waste properly and avoid leaving a visible impact on the environment.

3. Boil water

To make a medicinal tea that's effective and packed with active constituents, you need to boil water first.

To boil water, simply choose a pot or a kettle and heat it until it reaches 100°C (212°F). You can use the stove, an induction plate, or electricity to make it boil, depending on the type of container you're using. You can even put a cup filled with water inside a microwave if you want to boil it fast.

As for the water, use cold tap water if it's good quality. Otherwise, filter and purify the water before using it. In alternative, you can purchase water bottled water.

It's important to boil water to make herbal tea because it helps sterilize it, making it safer to use. Boiling water aids in extracting beneficial compounds from medicinal herbs and brings out the full flavor of the herbs.

How to boil water using a microwave?

To boil water using a microwave, follow these steps:

  1. Choose a microwave-safe container suitable for heating liquids, like glass or ceramic.
  2. Fill the container with the desired amount of water, leaving enough space at the top to prevent boiling over.
  3. If preferred, add a microwave-safe object (like a wooden stick or microwave-safe tea bag) to prevent superheating, which can cause the water to explode when disturbed.
  4. Place a microwave-safe lid or microwave-safe plastic wrap over the container to prevent splattering.
  5. Put the container in the center of the microwave.
  6. Set the microwave to high power.
  7. Heat the water in short intervals, usually starting with 2-3 minutes.
  8. After each interval, carefully remove the container using oven mitts or a towel and gently stir the water to distribute the heat.
  9. Continue heating in short intervals, checking the water's temperature until it reaches the desired boiling point.
  10. Use caution when handling the hot container and water, and let it rest for a moment before removing it from the microwave.

Warning: Some metals can literally melt or catch fire inside a microwave. Make sure you are using only 100% microwave-safe containers.

Note: While this procedure may seem complicated, don't be scared. The microwave is probably one of the easiest an fastest way to boil water. If you already have a microwave at home, use it by all means.

How to boil water using an electric kettle?

Here's a step-by-step guide to boiling water using an electric kettle:

  1. Fill the electric kettle with the desired amount of water, ensuring not to surpass the maximum fill line indicated inside the kettle.
  2. Close the lid securely to prevent any spillage.
  3. Place the kettle on the power base and plug it into a suitable power outlet.
  4. Turn on the electric kettle using the power switch usually located on the handle or base.
  5. Wait for the water to come to a rolling boil. You'll typically hear a click or see an indicator light turning off once the water reaches boiling temperature.
  6. Once the water is boiled, switch off or unplug the kettle.
  7. Carefully grasp the handle and pour the hot water as needed, being cautious to avoid any spills or burns.
  8. After use, empty any remaining water and allow the kettle to cool before cleaning or storing it.

How to boil water using an induction cooktop?

Follow these steps to boil water using an induction cooktop:

  1. Place an induction-compatible pot or kettle onto the cooktop.
  2. Ensure the pot's base matches the cooktop's size for proper heating.
  3. Fill the pot with the desired amount of water, leaving some space to prevent overflow.
  4. Turn on the induction cooktop by pressing the power button.
  5. Select the "Boil" or highest temperature setting if available; otherwise, choose the maximum heat level.
  6. Place the pot or kettle containing water on the cooktop's designated heating zone.
  7. Press the "Start" or "On" button to begin heating the water.
  8. The induction cooktop will generate heat directly into the pot, rapidly heating the water.
  9. Monitor the water closely. It should start bubbling and eventually reach a rolling boil.
  10. Once the water reaches a vigorous boil, turn off the cooktop and carefully remove the pot using oven mitts or pot holders.

How to boil water using a stove?

To boil water using a stove, follow these steps:

  1. Place a pot or kettle filled with the desired amount of water on the stove burner.
  2. Ensure the burner is off before placing the pot on it.
  3. Turn the stove knob to high heat.
  4. Wait for the water to reach a rolling boil; this is indicated by bubbles rapidly breaking the surface.
  5. Keep an eye on the pot to prevent overflowing.
  6. Once boiling, reduce the heat to maintain a steady simmer or turn off the burner if you've reached the desired boiling point.
  7. Use caution when handling the hot pot or kettle.

How to boil water using a campfire?

If you are on an adventure in the wild you can boil water using a campfire:

  1. Collect firewood and arrange it in a safe, contained area, creating a suitable campfire pit.
  2. Place small, dry tinder at the center of the pit and stack kindling around it in a teepee or log cabin structure.
  3. Use a lighter or matches to ignite the tinder carefully, ensuring the flames catch onto the kindling.
  4. Gradually add larger pieces of firewood to maintain a steady flame, allowing it to burn down to hot embers.
  5. Fill a heat-resistant pot or container with the water you intend to boil. Ensure it's not overfilled to prevent spillage.
  6. Position the pot over the hot embers, using a grill or heat-proof surface if available. Alternatively, suspend the pot over the fire using a tripod or suitable support.
  7. Monitor the water closely, and as the heat increases, bubbles start forming at the bottom and rising to the surface.
  8. Once the water reaches a rolling boil (bubbles vigorously break the surface), remove it from the fire carefully, using heat-resistant gloves or tools.

4. Steep the herb

For this step, take a cup and put the tea bag inside. Then, fill the cup with boiling water. You want to fill most of the cup with boiling water, don't leave the cup half empty.

Now, leave the tea bag steeping for 5 minutes. This steeping time is well-suited for most herbs. Some herbs require more steeping time than others to extract more constituents but stick with 5 minutes for this tutorial. Don't overcomplicate this process.

If you want to use a timer, that's great. But if you don't want to, it's fine too. You don't need to be perfect at this point.

An additional tip is to stir the tea bag inside the hot water 1 or 2 times during these 5 minutes. This way, the tea bag releases more active compounds and the tea becomes stronger in flavor and medicinal efficacy.

5. Don't add sugar/sweeteners

Never add sugar or sweeteners in medicinal herbal teas. Use sugar and sweeteners only when making syrup.

The negative effects of sugar can negate the health benefits of teas, depending on how effectively your metabolism processes the sugar. For example, scientific studies confirm that sugar induces inflammation in the body and one teaspoon of sugar is enough to negate any anti-inflammatory benefit your medicinal tea can bring you.

Even worse, artificial sweeteners like aspartame, saccharin, and sucralose have been associated with various health problems, including headaches, digestive issues, and even cancer. So, if you believe you can replace sugar with sweeteners because they are low in calories (aka "healthy"), think twice because their dangerous side effects are just not worth it.

Drink your medicinal tea plain vanilla. Learn to appreciate its aroma. You may not like the taste at first, but your palate will adjust to that taste in a week or so and you will start to appreciate the flavor.
