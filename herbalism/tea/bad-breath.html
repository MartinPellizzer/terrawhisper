10 Best Herbal Teas For Bad Breath

Herbal teas, particularly those made from ingredients like cloves, cinnamon, and peppermint, can help with bad breath due to their antibacterial properties that reduce the number of odor-causing bacteria in the mouth.

The warm temperature of the tea can also stimulate saliva production, which helps to wash away food particles and bacteria. Additionally, some herbal teas, such as chamomile and lemon balm, have anti-inflammatory effects that can help soothe irritated gums and reduce inflammation in the mouth. Furthermore, the strong flavors of some herbal teas can temporarily mask any lingering bad breath, providing a quick and natural solution for freshening breath.

Lastly, herbal teas are often caffeine-free and sugar-free, making them a healthier alternative to other breath-freshening options like coffee or sweetened gum.

1. Parsley

Parsley tea helps with bad breath because it contains high levels of chlorophyll and antioxidants, which neutralize odor-causing bacteria in the mouth.

Chlorophyll is an excellent natural deodorizer that binds to volatile sulfur compounds responsible for bad breath. Sipping parsley tea throughout the day can help keep your breath fresh, as the herbal infusion continuously battles harmful oral bacteria.

Additionally, parsley tea's anti-inflammatory properties may reduce gum inflammation and promote overall dental health, contributing to improved oral hygiene and long-term prevention of bad breath.

The following procedure explains how to make parsley tea for bad breath.

  1. Gather 1 cup of fresh parsley leaves and rinse them thoroughly under cold water to remove any dirt or impurities.
  2. Coarsely chop the parsley leaves and place them in a teapot or heat-resistant glass container.
  3. Boil 2 cups of water in a separate pot and pour it over the chopped parsley leaves.
  4. Allow the mixture to steep for 10-15 minutes, then strain out the parsley leaves.
  5. Let the parsley tea cool down to a drinkable temperature, then sip it slowly, swishing it around in your mouth to ensure it reaches all areas of your mouth. Drink 1-2 cups of parsley tea daily to help freshen your breath.

2. Chamomile

Chamomile tea helps with bad breath because it contains antimicrobial properties that inhibit the growth of bacteria in the mouth, specifically those responsible for producing foul odors.

The calming effect of chamomile also reduces inflammation and gum disease, which can contribute to bad breath. Its delicate flavor masks unpleasant mouth odors while promoting saliva production, which naturally cleanses the mouth and neutralizes acidity.

Lastly, regular consumption of chamomile tea may improve digestive health, reducing the likelihood of regurgitation or other digestive issues that can cause bad breath.

The following procedure explains how to make chamomile tea for bad breath.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water in a saucepan on medium heat.
  2. Add 1-2 teaspoons of dried chamomile flowers (or 1 chamomile tea bag) to the boiling water.
  3. Reduce heat to low, cover the saucepan, and let it simmer for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the chamomile flowers, and let it cool down to a drinkable temperature.
  5. Sip the chamomile tea slowly, allowing it to swish around your mouth before swallowing, for a minimum of 5 minutes. Repeat as needed up to 3 times a day.

3. Fennel Seed

Fennel seed tea helps with bad breath because it contains anethole, a natural compound known for its antimicrobial properties.

Anethole inhibits the growth of bacteria that cause bad breath by disrupting their metabolic processes. Additionally, fennel seed tea is rich in antioxidants, which help neutralize odor-causing compounds in the mouth. Its pleasant sweet and licorice-like taste also helps freshen breath and mask unpleasant odors temporarily.

Regular consumption of fennel seed tea can contribute to overall oral health by promoting saliva production, which naturally cleanses the mouth and neutralizes acids that lead to bad breath.

The following procedure explains how to make fennel seed tea for bad breath.

  1. Boil 1-2 teaspoons of fennel seeds in 1 cup of water for 10-15 minutes.
  2. Strain the mixture to remove the seeds, leaving only the fennel-infused water.
  3. Allow the fennel tea to cool down to a drinkable temperature.
  4. Optionally, add a natural sweetener like honey to taste.
  5. Sip the fennel seed tea slowly after meals or as needed to help freshen breath.

The following procedure explains how to make mint tea for bad breath.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water in a saucepan on high heat.
  2. Once boiling, reduce heat to low and add 1 cup of fresh mint leaves or 1 teaspoon of dried mint leaves.
  3. Let the mixture steep for 5-10 minutes, then remove from heat.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the mint leaves.
  5. Allow the tea to cool to a drinkable temperature, then enjoy! For best results, drink 2-3 times a day after meals.

5. Cardamom

Cardamom tea helps with bad breath because Its key ingredient, cardamom, has antibacterial properties that eliminate the bacteria causing bad breath in the mouth.

The warm temperature of the tea allows for increased blood flow to the gums and mouth area, promoting oral health and freshness. Consuming cardamom tea regularly can also help reduce inflammation in the mouth, which is a common cause of bad breath. Additionally, the aroma of the tea can help mask any lingering odors in the mouth, providing an instant solution for social situations.

Regular consumption of cardamom tea can lead to long-term benefits, including improved digestion and overall oral hygiene, further contributing to fresh breath.

The following procedure explains how to make cardamom tea for bad breath.

  1. Crush 2 cardamom pods to release the seeds, then grind the seeds into a powder.
  2. In a small saucepan, combine 2 cups of water, 1 teaspoon of grated ginger, and 1/4 teaspoon of the ground cardamom seeds.
  3. Bring the mixture to a boil over high heat, then reduce heat to low and let it simmer for 10 minutes.
  4. Remove the saucepan from heat and stir in 1 tablespoon of honey and the juice of half a lemon.
  5. Strain the tea through a fine-mesh sieve into a cup, let it cool to a drinkable temperature, and enjoy.

6. Cinnamon

Cinnamon tea helps with bad breath because it contains antibacterial properties that fight the bacteria responsible for causing bad breath.

Cinnamon is also rich in antioxidants, which help to neutralize the volatile sulfur compounds (VSCs) that contribute to bad breath. The warm temperature of the tea aids in saliva production, which helps to wash away food particles and bacteria in the mouth. Moreover, the sweet and spicy flavor of cinnamon tea can mask mild cases of bad breath while also stimulating the production of saliva for a fresher feeling in the mouth.

Regular consumption of cinnamon tea as part of good oral hygiene habits can help prevent and reduce bad breath.

The following procedure explains how to make cinnamon tea for bad breath.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water in a saucepan and add 1 cinnamon stick or 1/2 teaspoon of ground cinnamon.
  2. Reduce heat and let it simmer for 10-15 minutes to allow the cinnamon to release its essential oils.
  3. Strain the tea into a cup to remove any cinnamon residue.
  4. Optional: Add a sweetener of your choice (honey, stevia, etc.) to taste, but avoid sugar as it can promote bacterial growth.
  5. Sip the tea slowly, allowing it to coat your mouth and throat, and breathe in the vapors for maximum benefits. Repeat as needed throughout the day.

7. Ginger

Ginger tea helps with bad breath because it contains compounds that fight bacteria in the mouth, specifically gingerol and shogaol.

These antimicrobial agents work to eliminate the sulfur compounds responsible for bad breath by inhibiting the growth of bacteria in the mouth. Additionally, ginger tea's anti-inflammatory properties help reduce inflammation in the mouth, which can contribute to bad breath.

Lastly, drinking ginger tea regularly can increase saliva production, which naturally helps wash away food particles and bacteria that cause bad breath.

The following procedure explains how to make ginger tea for bad breath.

  1. Peel and thinly slice 1 inch of fresh ginger, then place it in a pot with 2 cups of water.
  2. Bring the water to a boil, then reduce heat and let the ginger simmer for 10-15 minutes.
  3. Remove the pot from heat, and add 1-2 teaspoons of honey and the juice of half a lemon.
  4. Stir the mixture until the honey dissolves, then strain the tea into a glass or mug.
  5. Sip the ginger tea slowly, allowing it to coat your mouth and throat for at least 5 minutes before swallowing.

8. Cloves

Cloves tea helps with bad breath because it contains eugenol, an essential oil that has strong antimicrobial and antioxidant properties.

This compound effectively kills the bacteria responsible for producing foul odors in the mouth. The warm temperature of cloves tea also aids in saliva production, which helps rinse away food particles and bacteria that contribute to bad breath. Additionally, the aromatic nature of cloves tea masks unpleasant mouth odors, providing an instant freshness sensation.

Long-term use of cloves tea can help reduce inflammation in the gums and improve overall oral health, further contributing to a fresher and healthier mouth.

The following procedure explains how to make cloves tea for bad breath.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water in a saucepan on the stove.
  2. Add 2-3 whole cloves to the boiling water.
  3. Reduce heat and let the mixture simmer for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Remove from heat, strain the liquid into a cup, and let it cool down for a few minutes.
  5. Slowly sip the warm clove tea, allowing it to coat your mouth and throat for fresh breath.

9. Anise

Anise tea helps with bad breath because anise seeds, from which the tea is made, contain compounds that have antibacterial and antimicrobial properties.

These compounds help to kill the bacteria in the mouth that cause bad breath. Additionally, anise tea can stimulate the production of saliva, which helps to wash away food particles and bacteria.

Furthermore, anise tea has a strong, distinct flavor that can help to mask the smell of bad breath. Drinking anise tea regularly can help to maintain oral hygiene and reduce the incidence of bad breath.

However, it is important to note that anise tea should not be used as a substitute for regular brushing, flossing, and dental check-ups.

The following procedure explains how to make anise tea for bad breath.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water in a saucepan on the stove.
  2. Add 1 teaspoon of anise seeds to the boiling water.
  3. Reduce heat to a simmer and let the anise seeds steep for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the anise seeds.
  5. Drink the anise tea slowly, allowing it to coat your mouth and throat for maximum breath-freshening effect.

10. Sage

Sage tea helps with bad breath because of its natural antibacterial properties, which eliminate the bacteria responsible for producing foul odors in the mouth.

The essential oils found in sage leaves, such as camphor and beta-caryophyllene, kill oral microbes that contribute to bad breath. Regular consumption of sage tea also increases saliva production, which naturally cleanses the mouth and washes away bacteria. Additionally, sage tea's anti-inflammatory properties help reduce gum inflammation and promote healthy gums, further contributing to improved oral health and reduced bad breath.

Overall, sage tea is a natural and effective remedy for bad breath due to its antibacterial, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory properties.

The following procedure explains how to make sage tea for bad breath.

  1. Heat one cup of water in a saucepan over high heat until it reaches a boil.
  2. Add one teaspoon of dried sage leaves or one fresh sage leaf to the boiling water.
  3. Reduce heat to low, cover the saucepan, and let the sage leaves steep for 5-10 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the sage leaves.
  5. Allow the tea to cool slightly, then drink it slowly, allowing it to coat your mouth and freshen your breath.
