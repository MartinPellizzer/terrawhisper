10 Best Herbal Teas For Constipation

Herbal teas, particularly those made from plants like senna, psyllium, and licorice, can help alleviate constipation due to their natural laxative properties.

These herbs contain compounds that stimulate the intestines, promoting muscle contractions that move stool through the digestive tract. Additionally, some herbal teas can help increase the water content in the intestines, softening stool and making it easier to pass.

Furthermore, these teas often contain antispasmodic properties that can help relax the muscles in the intestines, reducing discomfort and pain associated with constipation. However, it's important to note that while herbal teas can be helpful, they should not be used as a substitute for medical treatment, especially in cases of chronic or severe constipation.

As with any treatment, it's always best to consult with a healthcare professional before use.

1. Slippery Elm

Slippery elm tea helps with constipation because it contains mucilage, a substance that becomes a slick, gel-like solution when mixed with water.

This gel-like substance can help lubricate and soften the stool, making it easier to pass. Additionally, the mucilage can help soothe the gastrointestinal tract, reducing inflammation and irritation that can contribute to constipation.

Slippery elm tea is also a natural source of fiber, which can help add bulk to the stool and promote regularity. The combination of these effects makes slippery elm tea a gentle and effective remedy for constipation.

However, it is important to note that while slippery elm tea can be helpful for occasional constipation, it is not a substitute for seeking medical attention for chronic or severe constipation.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in slippery elm tea that help with constipation.

  1. Mucilage: Slippery elm's high mucilage content forms a slick gel when mixed with water, which coats and soothes the digestive tract, helping to stimulate relaxed muscle movements and relieve constipation.
  2. Tannins: The tannins in slippery elm have an astringent effect, helping to tone and gently constrict the tissues of the digestive tract, promoting normal bowel movements.
  3. Starch: Slippery elm's demulcent starch soothes and lubricates the intestinal lining, easing the passage of stool and alleviating constipation.
  4. Fiber: Slippery elm bark is a good source of soluble fiber, which absorbs water and expands in the gut, promoting regular bowel movements and softening stool for easy passage.

The following list reveals what parts of slippery elm are most commonly used to make medicinal tea for constipation.

  1. Bark: The outer covering of the slippery elm tree is used due to its high mucilage content, which helps to relieve constipation by providing a protective coating to the digestive tract and promoting bowel movements.
  2. Inner Bark: Containing large amounts of mucilage, the inner bark is used in medicinal teas for constipation, as it can help soothe irritated tissues and add bulk to stools for easy passage.
  3. Leaves: Slippery elm leaves are occasionally used in tea preparations, as their mucilage content helps lubricate the intestines, easing the passage of stools in cases of constipation.
  4. Seeds: While less common, slippery elm seeds can be used in teas for constipation, as the seeds contain mucilage, which helps increase the moisture content in the stools and encourages bowel movements.

The following procedure explains how to make slippery elm tea for constipation.

  1. "In a pot, combine 1 cup of water and 1 teaspoon of dried slippery elm bark."
  2. "Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce heat and let it simmer for 5-10 minutes."
  3. "Cover the pot and allow the tea to steep for an additional 10 minutes."
  4. "Strain the tea to remove the bark, then let it cool to a drinkable temperature."
  5. "Drink 1-3 cups of slippery elm tea daily for constipation relief."

3. Psyllium

Psyllium tea helps with constipation because it contains psyllium husk, a natural source of soluble fiber.

This fiber, when mixed with water, forms a gel-like substance that softens and adds bulk to stools, making them easier to pass. The tea also stimulates the muscles in the intestines, promoting bowel movements.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in psyllium tea that help with constipation.

  1. Fiber: Psyllium husk is a soluble fiber that absorbs water and forms a gel-like substance, which adds bulk to stools and helps regulate bowel movements, thereby alleviating constipation.
  2. Mucilage: Psyllium mucilage provides a soothing effect on the gastrointestinal tract and acts as a natural lubricant, facilitating the passage of stools through the intestines and reducing constipation symptoms.
  3. Arabinoxylan: As a type of hemicellulose, arabinoxylan also contributes to the fiber content of psyllium, promoting regular bowel movements and aiding in the management of constipation.

The following list reveals what parts of psyllium are most commonly used to make medicinal tea for constipation.

  1. Seeds: Psyllium seeds are the primary constituent used to make medicinal tea for constipation, as they contain mucilage that expands in the gut, promoting bowel movements.
  2. Leaves: Psyllium leaves, although not as potent as the seeds, can also be used in medicinal tea to alleviate constipation due to their mild laxative effect.
  3. Flowers: Psyllium flowers can be included in medicinal tea for constipation because they contain mucilage, which helps to soften stool and ease bowel movements.

The following procedure explains how to make psyllium tea for constipation.

  1. In a cup, mix 1 teaspoon of psyllium husk powder with 8 ounces of warm water.
  2. Stir briskly for 30 seconds to ensure powders fully dissolves and doesn't clump.
  3. Let the mixture sit for 2 minutes to allow psyllium to absorb some water and swell.
  4. Add a squeeze of lemon juice or a dash of cinnamon for flavor, if desired.
  5. Drink immediately, following with an additional glass of water, and prepare for relaxation as the psyllium works its way through your digestive system.

4. Senna

Senna tea helps with constipation because it contains anthraquinone glycosides, which are natural laxatives.

These compounds promote the contraction of intestinal muscles, thereby accelerating the process of digestion and alleviating constipation. Furthermore, senna tea can increase the volume and softness of stool, making it easier to pass. It is important to note that senna tea should be used sparingly and under the guidance of a healthcare professional, as overuse can lead to dependence and potential electrolyte imbalances.

Additionally, senna tea may interact with certain medications, so it is crucial to inform one's healthcare provider before using it.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in senna tea that help with constipation.

  1. Sennosides: These are the main active constituents in senna that have a laxative effect. They work by stimulating the intestinal lining, promoting muscle contractions in the intestines, and increasing bowel movements.
  2. Anthraquinones: This class of compounds in senna contribute to its laxative effect by irritating the lining of the intestines and promoting the secretion of fluids, which helps to soften stool and ease its passage through the digestive tract.
  3. Mucilage: The mucilage content in senna provides a soothing effect on the gastrointestinal system and helps to protect the mucous membrane of the digestive tract. This can help to alleviate some of the discomfort associated with constipation.
  4. Flavonoids: Flavonoids in senna possess anti-inflammatory properties which can help to reduce inflammation in the digestive tract, promoting regular bowel movements and alleviating constipation symptoms.

The following list reveals what parts of senna are most commonly used to make medicinal tea for constipation.

  1. Leaves: The senna plant's leaves are commonly used in medicinal tea for constipation because they contain anthraquinone glycosides, which stimulate the intestines and promote bowel movements.
  2. Pods: Senna pods, also used in the tea, have similar anthraquinone glycosides, helping increase intestinal muscle contractions and alleviating constipation symptoms.

The following procedure explains how to make senna tea for constipation.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water in a kettle or pot, allowing it to reach a rolling boil.
  2. Add 1-2 teaspoons of dried senna leaves or 1 senna tea bag to a cup or teapot.
  3. Pour the boiling water over the senna leaves or tea bag, ensuring they are fully submerged.
  4. Steep the tea for 5-10 minutes, depending on desired strength, then remove the leaves or tea bag.
  5. Consume the tea while still warm, preferably before bedtime, for overnight relief of constipation.

The following procedure explains how to make ginger tea for constipation.

  1. Peel and chop 1 inch of fresh ginger into small pieces, then bring 2 cups of water to a boil.
  2. Add the chopped ginger to the boiling water, reduce heat to low and let it simmer for 10-15 minutes.
  3. Remove from heat, then add 1 teaspoon of honey and 1/2 lemon juice for flavor, let it cool down for 3-5 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea to remove ginger pieces, then pour it into a cup.
  5. Sip and drink the warm ginger tea slowly to soothe constipation.
