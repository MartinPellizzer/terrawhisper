What are the main causes of jaundice?

The list below provides the primary causes of jaundice.

  • Hemolysis: Red blood cells are broken down excessively, releasing excess bilirubin into the bloodstream, which builds up and causes jaundice.
  • Liver disease: Impaired liver function prevents the liver from properly processing bilirubin, allowing it to accumulate in the blood and cause jaundice.
  • Gallstones: Blockages in the bile ducts prevent bilirubin from being secreted into the intestine, causing it to build up in the bloodstream and leading to jaundice.
  • Pancreatitis: Inflammation of the pancreas disrupts bile flow and liver function, leading to a buildup of bilirubin in the blood and causing jaundice.
  • Gilbert syndrome: A genetic disorder that impairs the liver's ability to process bilirubin, resulting in excessive levels in the bloodstream and jaundice.
  • Newborn jaundice: The liver in newborns takes time to mature and efficiently process bilirubin, leading to a temporary buildup and jaundice.
  • Blockage of bile ducts: Obstructions in the bile ducts prevent bilirubin from being excreted into the intestine, causing it to accumulate in the blood and lead to jaundice.
  • Medications: Certain drugs, such as chlorpromazine, can inhibit the excretion of bilirubin, leading to its buildup in the bloodstream and jaundice.
  • Crigler-Najjar syndrome: A rare genetic disorder that severely impairs the liver's ability to process bilirubin, resulting in extremely high levels in the bloodstream and jaundice.
  • Cholestasis: Reduced bile flow, often due to liver or bile duct problems, prevents bilirubin from being excreted into the intestine, leading to its accumulation in the blood and jaundice.
