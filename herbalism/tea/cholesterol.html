10 Best Herbal Teas For Cholesterol

Herbal teas, particularly those made from ingredients like hawthorn, holy basil, and ginger, have been found to help lower cholesterol levels.

These herbs contain antioxidants and anti-inflammatory compounds that can help reduce the absorption of cholesterol in the gut and increase its excretion from the body. Additionally, some herbal teas can help improve liver function, which plays a crucial role in processing and eliminating cholesterol. However, it's important to note that while herbal teas can be a helpful addition to a cholesterol-lowering diet and lifestyle, they are not a substitute for medical treatment or a healthy lifestyle.

As with any supplement, it's always a good idea to speak with a healthcare provider before incorporating herbal teas into a regular routine.

1. Green Tea

Green tea helps with cholesterol because it contains bioactive compounds, particularly catechins, that can inhibit the absorption of cholesterol in the gut, thereby reducing the overall cholesterol levels in the body.

Moreover, green tea has been found to increase the excretion of cholesterol-rich bile, which can also help in lowering cholesterol levels. Additionally, green tea has been shown to reduce inflammation, which is a significant factor in the development of high cholesterol and cardiovascular disease. The antioxidant properties of green tea may also prevent the oxidation of LDL cholesterol, which is a critical step in the development of plaque in the arteries.

Therefore, incorporating green tea into a balanced diet and healthy lifestyle can be an effective strategy for managing cholesterol levels and promoting cardiovascular health.

The following procedure explaing how to make green tea tea for cholesterol.

  1. Bring 8 ounces of filtered water to a boil, then let it cool down to 160-170°F (71-77°C), as boiling water can damage the catechins in green tea, making it less effective for cholesterol reduction.
  2. In a ceramic or glass teapot or cup, place 1-2 teaspoons of high-quality green tea leaves (like matcha, sencha, or gyokuro) depending on your taste preference.
  3. Pour the heated water over the green tea leaves, ensuring they are fully submerged. Allow the tea to steep for 2-3 minutes for a balanced flavor and optimal cholesterol-lowering benefits.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the spent tea leaves. For added cholesterol-lowering effects, consider adding a slice of lemon or a pinch of turmeric.
  5. Drink the green tea while it's still warm, ideally 2-3 cups daily, as part of a balanced diet and healthy lifestyle to help manage cholesterol levels.

2. Holy Basil

Holy basil tea helps with cholesterol because it contains ursolic acid and other bioactive compounds that have been shown to lower cholesterol levels.

These compounds work by reducing the absorption of cholesterol in the gut and increasing the breakdown and excretion of cholesterol in the liver. Additionally, holy basil tea has antioxidant properties that can help prevent the oxidation of LDL cholesterol, which is a major risk factor for heart disease. Furthermore, holy basil tea has anti-inflammatory effects that may help reduce inflammation in the arteries, which can contribute to the development of high cholesterol.

However, it's important to note that while holy basil tea may be helpful in managing cholesterol levels, it should not be used as a substitute for prescribed medications or a healthy lifestyle.

The following procedure explaing how to make holy basil tea for cholesterol.

  1. In a mortar and pestle, grind 2 tablespoons of dried holy basil leaves (also known as tulsi) until they become a coarse powder, then transfer to a teapot.
  2. Boil 4 cups of water in a separate pot, then pour the hot water over the ground holy basil leaves in the teapot, covering and steeping for 5-10 minutes.
  3. Strain the tea through a fine-mesh sieve into a teacup or mug, pressing the leaves gently to extract all liquid.
  4. Optional: add a small amount of raw honey or a squeeze of lemon to taste, but avoid adding milk or sugar.
  5. Drink the tea while warm, ideally once or twice a day, after consulting with a healthcare provider for proper dosage and potential interactions with existing medications.

3. Turmeric

Turmeric tea helps with cholesterol because it contains curcumin, a compound known for its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

This compound has been found to lower levels of LDL (bad) cholesterol and increase HDL (good) cholesterol, thus improving overall cardiovascular health. Moreover, curcumin inhibits the oxidation of LDL cholesterol, which is a significant factor in the development of plaques in the arteries. Additionally, turmeric tea can help reduce inflammation in the body, which is often a contributing factor to high cholesterol levels.

However, it is important to note that while turmeric tea can be a beneficial addition to a healthy lifestyle, it should not replace a balanced diet, regular exercise, and any prescribed medications for managing cholesterol levels.

The following procedure explaing how to make turmeric tea for cholesterol.

  1. In a saucepan, combine 2 cups of water, 1 teaspoon of grated fresh turmeric or 1/2 teaspoon of ground turmeric, 1/2 teaspoon of grated ginger, and a pinch of black pepper (to enhance absorption); bring the mixture to a boil.
  2. Reduce heat and let it simmer for 10 minutes, allowing the water to absorb the properties of the spices.
  3. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the solids.
  4. Stir in 1-2 teaspoons of honey or maple syrup, depending on your preferred level of sweetness, and a squeeze of lemon juice for added flavor and vitamin C.
  5. Enjoy your turmeric tea while it's still warm, up to three times a day for best results in managing cholesterol levels.

4. Ginger

Ginger tea helps with cholesterol because it contains antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties that work to lower the levels of LDL (bad) cholesterol in the body.

The active compounds in ginger, such as gingerols and shogaols, can inhibit the production of cholesterol in the liver and improve overall liver function. Additionally, ginger has been shown to increase HDL (good) cholesterol levels, which can help remove LDL cholesterol from the bloodstream. Furthermore, ginger's natural blood-thinning properties can prevent the formation of blood clots, reducing the risk of heart disease associated with high cholesterol.

Overall, regular consumption of ginger tea can support healthy cholesterol levels and promote cardiovascular health.

The following procedure explaing how to make ginger tea for cholesterol.

  1. Peel and finely chop 1 inch of fresh ginger root, then combine in a saucepan with 4 cups of water, bringing to a boil; reduce heat and simmer, covered, for 15 minutes to create a ginger-infused water.
  2. Remove from heat and stir in 1 teaspoon of honey and 1 tablespoon of freshly squeezed lemon juice for added flavor and vitamin C.
  3. Allow the mixture to cool for 5 minutes, then strain into a teapot or mug to remove ginger pieces.
  4. Pour the ginger-infused tea into a cup and add an optional cholesterol-lowering supplement like 200mg of red yeast rice extract or 1000mg of fish oil capsule.
  5. Enjoy your homemade ginger tea for cholesterol, drinking 2-3 times daily, 30 minutes before meals, for best results.

5. Cinnamon

Cinnamon tea helps with cholesterol because it contains antioxidants and compounds that inhibit the oxidation of low-density lipoprotein (LDL), or "bad" cholesterol, preventing it from forming plaque in the arteries.

One of the key active components in cinnamon, called cinnamaldehyde, has been found to reduce levels of total cholesterol, LDL, and triglycerides in the blood. Moreover, cinnamon may improve insulin sensitivity, which can indirectly help lower cholesterol by reducing insulin resistance and promoting healthier eating habits. Regular consumption of cinnamon tea can also increase the excretion of bile acids, which may help lower cholesterol levels by reducing the amount of cholesterol available for the production of new bile acids.

Lastly, cinnamon tea has anti-inflammatory properties that can help reduce inflammation in the body, which is associated with high cholesterol and cardiovascular disease.

The following procedure explaing how to make cinnamon tea for cholesterol.

  1. In a small pot, bring 1 cup of water to a boil, then add 1 cinnamon stick and 1 teaspoon of honey, reducing heat to a simmer for 10 minutes to allow the nutrients to infuse.
  2. After 10 minutes, remove the pot from heat and add 1 teaspoon of lemon juice, stirring well to combine the ingredients.
  3. Carefully pour the hot tea through a fine mesh strainer into a cup, to remove the cinnamon stick and any solids.
  4. To ensure proper dilution, add an additional 1/2 cup of hot water to the cup, stirring gently to mix.
  5. Drink the cinnamon tea while warm, up to three times a day for best results in maintaining healthy cholesterol levels.

6. Black Tea

Black tea helps with cholesterol because it contains catechins, a type of flavonoid antioxidant that has been shown to improve cholesterol levels.

Specifically, these antioxidants can help reduce the absorption of cholesterol in the body and increase the excretion of cholesterol in the feces. Additionally, black tea has been found to inhibit the activity of cholesteryl ester transfer protein (CETP), an enzyme that contributes to the development of harmful cholesterol.

By reducing the activity of CETP, black tea can help maintain healthy levels of high-density lipoprotein (HDL), or "good" cholesterol. Furthermore, regular consumption of black tea has been associated with a lower risk of developing cardiovascular disease, which is often linked to high cholesterol levels.

However, it's important to note that black tea should be consumed as part of a balanced diet and a healthy lifestyle to effectively manage cholesterol levels.

The following procedure explaing how to make black tea tea for cholesterol.

  1. Bring 4 cups of water to a boil, then reduce heat to a simmer and add 2 teaspoons of black tea leaves, allowing them to steep for 3-5 minutes for optimal flavor and cholesterol-lowering benefits.
  2. In a separate pot, heat 2 cups of low-fat milk with 1 cinnamon stick and 1 teaspoon of honey on medium heat until warm, but not boiling.
  3. Slowly combine the black tea and the warmed milk mixture in a large teapot or pitcher, mixing gently to ensure even distribution of heat and flavors.
  4. Add 1 tablespoon of lemon juice to the mixture, which helps to increase the absorption of cholesterol-lowering compounds found in black tea.
  5. Serve the black tea for cholesterol hot, dividing it evenly among 3-4 cups, and enjoy daily as part of a balanced diet and healthy lifestyle to help manage cholesterol levels.

7. Oolong

Oolong tea helps with cholesterol because it contains antioxidants, specifically polymerized polyphenols, which have been shown to reduce the levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL), or "bad" cholesterol, while increasing high-density lipoprotein (HDL), or "good" cholesterol.

These polyphenols work by blocking the absorption of cholesterol in the digestive system, allowing it to be excreted from the body. Additionally, oolong tea has been found to stimulate the metabolism, aiding in weight loss, which can indirectly help lower cholesterol levels. However, it is important to note that oolong tea should not be used as a substitute for prescribed medications or a healthy lifestyle, but rather as a complement to a balanced diet and regular exercise.

More research is needed to fully understand the long-term effects of oolong tea on cholesterol levels and overall heart health.

The following procedure explaing how to make oolong tea for cholesterol.

  1. Bring 1 liter of fresh, cold water to a boil, then let it cool down to 195-205 degrees Fahrenheit (90-96 degrees Celsius); add 10 grams of high-quality oolong tea leaves to a teapot.
  2. Pour the heated water over the tea leaves, ensuring they are fully submerged; let the tea steep for 3-5 minutes to allow the flavors and antioxidants to infuse, which can help lower cholesterol levels.
  3. Strain the tea into a cup or teapot, discarding the spent tea leaves; for multiple infusions, increase the steeping time by 30 seconds for each subsequent brew.
  4. Enjoy the warm oolong tea without added sweeteners, as they can negate some of the health benefits; drink 2-3 cups daily for best results in managing cholesterol levels.
  5. For optimal cholesterol reduction, combine oolong tea consumption with a balanced diet, regular exercise, and a healthy lifestyle.

8. Rooibos

Rooibos tea helps with cholesterol because it contains unique antioxidants, such as aspalathin and quercetin, which have been shown to have a positive effect on cholesterol levels.

These antioxidants work by inhibiting the absorption of cholesterol in the bloodstream and promoting the conversion of bad cholesterol (LDL) into good cholesterol (HDL). Additionally, rooibos tea has anti-inflammatory properties that can help reduce inflammation in the arteries, which is a major contributor to high cholesterol. Furthermore, rooibos tea has been shown to increase the excretion of cholesterol in the feces, further reducing the levels of cholesterol in the body.

Overall, regular consumption of rooibos tea can help maintain healthy cholesterol levels and support cardiovascular health.

The following procedure explaing how to make rooibos tea for cholesterol.

  1. In a teapot, combine 1 liter of fresh water, 1 heaping tablespoon of loose rooibos tea leaves, and 1 cinnamon stick, then bring to a boil over medium-high heat.
  2. Once boiling, reduce heat to low, cover the teapot, and let it simmer for 10-15 minutes to allow the antioxidants to develop and cholesterol-lowering properties to be released.
  3. After simmering, remove the teapot from heat and mix in 1 tablespoon of raw honey and 1 teaspoon of lemon juice for added flavor and additional health benefits.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup or mug, discarding the used tea leaves and cinnamon stick.
  5. Serve the rooibos tea warm and enjoy up to 3 cups a day as part of a balanced diet to help manage cholesterol levels.

9. Hibiscus

Hibiscus tea helps with cholesterol because it contains high levels of antioxidants that can help to reduce the absorption of LDL (bad) cholesterol in the body.

These antioxidants, known as anthocyanins, can also increase the levels of HDL (good) cholesterol, which helps to clear out LDL cholesterol from the bloodstream. Additionally, hibiscus tea has anti-inflammatory properties that can help to prevent the buildup of plaque in the arteries, reducing the risk of heart disease and stroke. Studies have shown that regular consumption of hibiscus tea can lead to significant reductions in both total cholesterol and LDL cholesterol levels.

Furthermore, hibiscus tea is a natural and caffeine-free alternative to other beverages, making it a healthy choice for those looking to manage their cholesterol levels.

The following procedure explaing how to make hibiscus tea for cholesterol.

  1. In a 2-quart pot, bring 4 cups of water to a boil, then remove from heat, add 3 tablespoons of dried hibiscus flowers, cover, and let steep for 15-20 minutes.
  2. After steeping, strain the tea to remove the hibiscus flowers and discard them.
  3. Return the tea to the pot, add 2 cinnamon sticks and 1 tablespoon of raw honey, then reheat the tea to a simmer.
  4. Once simmering, crack 5 whole black peppercorns and add them to the pot, then let the tea simmer for an additional 5 minutes.
  5. Remove the pot from the heat, add 1 tablespoon of lemon juice, stir well, and serve the hibiscus tea hot for a cholesterol-lowering beverage.

10. Rosemary

Rosemary tea helps with cholesterol because it contains carnosic acid, a compound that has been shown to inhibit the oxidation of LDL cholesterol, which is often referred to as "bad" cholesterol.

This action can help prevent plaque buildup in the arteries, reducing the risk of heart disease. Additionally, rosemary tea has been found to increase HDL cholesterol, or "good" cholesterol, which helps remove LDL cholesterol from the bloodstream.

Furthermore, rosemary contains antioxidants that can help reduce inflammation and improve blood vessel function, both of which are important for maintaining healthy cholesterol levels. However, it's important to note that while rosemary tea may be beneficial for cholesterol levels, it should not be used as a substitute for a healthy diet and lifestyle.

As with any supplement, it's always best to consult with a healthcare provider before incorporating rosemary tea into your routine.

The following procedure explaing how to make rosemary tea for cholesterol.

  1. In a teapot, combine 1 liter of water and 3-4 sprigs of fresh rosemary, or 2-3 teaspoons of dried rosemary leaves, and bring to a boil.
  2. Reduce heat to low, cover, and let the mixture simmer for 10-15 minutes to allow the rosemary to release its nutrients and flavors.
  3. Strain the tea to remove the rosemary leaves, and let it cool down to a drinkable temperature.
  4. For added cholesterol-lowering benefits, squeeze half a lemon (about 2 tablespoons of fresh lemon juice) into the tea.
  5. Serve the rosemary tea in cups, and enjoy it 2-3 times a day, preferably after meals.
