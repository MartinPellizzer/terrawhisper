10 Best Herbal Teas For Cats

Herbal teas, when properly prepared and given in appropriate amounts, can provide certain benefits for cats.

Some herbal teas contain soothing ingredients like chamomile or valerian root, which may help reduce stress and anxiety in cats. Additionally, some herbal teas can aid in digestion and alleviate mild stomach issues, making them helpful for cats with occasional tummy troubles.

However, it's crucial to consult with a veterinarian before administering any herbal remedies to cats, as some herbs can be toxic or interact negatively with medications. Also, it's important to note that herbal teas for cats should be caffeine-free, as caffeine is harmful to cats.

Lastly, herbal teas should never replace proper veterinary care or prescribed medications for cats with chronic health issues.

1. Chamomile

Chamomile tea helps with cats because it contains calming properties that can soothe and relax anxious or stressed felines.

Its anti-inflammatory and pain-relieving components can also alleviate digestive issues such as nausea, vomiting, and stomach aches in cats. Furthermore, chamomile tea's antispasmodic effects can help reduce spasms in the intestines and improve overall gut health. Additionally, its soothing properties can help heal skin irritations and promote wound healing when applied topically.

However, it is crucial to consult with a veterinarian before administering chamomile tea to cats, as excessive consumption can lead to adverse effects.

The following procedure explaing how to make chamomile tea for cats.

  1. Steep 1 teaspoon of dried chamomile flowers in 8 ounces of boiling water for 10 minutes, then strain to remove the flowers, allowing the tea to cool down to a comfortable temperature for your cat.
  2. Consult with a veterinarian to determine the appropriate dosage based on your cat's weight, typically around 1/4 teaspoon per 5 pounds of body weight, up to three times a day.
  3. If your cat is new to chamomile, introduce the tea gradually by mixing a small amount with their regular food or offering it in a shallow dish.
  4. Monitor your cat's reaction to the chamomile tea, watching for any signs of improvement in their health or adverse reactions.
  5. Always provide fresh water alongside the chamomile tea, and store any leftovers in the refrigerator for up to 3 days.

2. Valerian

Valerian tea helps with cats because it contains actinidine, a compound that acts as a natural sedative and mood booster, which can help reduce anxiety and stress in cats.

This compound also attracts cats due to its similarity to catnip, making the tea a useful tool for training or calming cats during stressful situations. Additionally, valerian tea can promote relaxation and better sleep in cats, making it beneficial for cats who have trouble sleeping or experience restlessness. However, it's important to note that not all cats react to valerian in the same way, and some may not experience any effects.

As with any new addition to a cat's diet or routine, it's always best to consult with a veterinarian before giving valerian tea to a cat.

The following procedure explaing how to make valerian tea for cats.

  1. Steep 1 teaspoon of dried valerian root in 8 ounces of boiling water for 10-15 minutes to create a valerian tea; let the tea cool down to a warm temperature that is safe for your cat to drink.
  2. Optional: Add a small amount of catnip to the tea for additional relaxation benefits, as catnip is often enjoyed by cats and can complement the calming effects of valerian root.
  3. Strain the tea to remove the valerian root and catnip particles, if applicable, to ensure a smooth and comfortable drinking experience for your cat.
  4. Pour the strained tea into a shallow dish, making sure it is not too hot to avoid burning your cat's mouth or throat.
  5. Serve the tea to your cat in a calm and quiet environment, encouraging them to drink it as a natural and soothing way to promote relaxation and stress relief.

3. Catnip

Catnip tea helps with cats because it contains nepetalactone, the same chemical that is found in catnip plants.

This compound binds to receptors in a cat's nose, leading to a range of behaviors such as purring, rolling around, and increased playfulness. While cats cannot ingest catnip in its plant form to experience these effects, consuming it as a tea allows them to benefit from its properties. However, it's important to note that not all cats are affected by catnip, and some may not respond to catnip tea at all.

Additionally, catnip tea should be used as a complementary treatment and not as a substitute for professional veterinary care.

The following procedure explaing how to make catnip tea for cats.

  1. Gather 1 cup of fresh catnip leaves or 1/4 cup of dried catnip, 2 cups of water, and a tea infuser or strainer; ensure catnip is organic and cat-safe.
  2. Pour the water into a saucepan and bring it to a boil over medium-high heat.
  3. Once boiling, add the catnip to the tea infuser or a reusable tea bag, then place it in the boiling water.
  4. Reduce heat to low, let it simmer for 10-15 minutes to extract the essential oils, then remove from heat.
  5. Allow the catnip tea to cool down to a warm temperature, remove the infuser, and serve it to your cat in a shallow bowl.

4. Lavender

Lavender tea helps with cats because it contains nepetalactone, a compound also found in catnip, which is known to produce a calming effect in cats.

This chemical triggers sensors in a cat's nose, leading to relaxation and even mild sedation. The resultant behavior can range from sniffing, licking, and chewing, to a state of mental euphoria and increased playfulness in some cats. However, it's important to note that not all cats respond to nepetalactone, and even those that do may not exhibit a response every time.

Furthermore, while lavender tea is safe for cats in small amounts, it should never be used as a substitute for professional veterinary care.

The following procedure explaing how to make lavender tea for cats.

  1. Steep 1 teaspoon of dried culinary-grade lavender in 8 ounces of boiling water for 5-10 minutes to create a lavender tea base, then let it cool down to a comfortable temperature for your cat.
  2. Consult with a veterinarian to determine the appropriate dosage based on your cat's weight, typically around 1/4-1/2 teaspoon per 10 pounds of body weight.
  3. Strain the lavender tea to remove any solid particles and discard the used lavender.
  4. Slowly introduce the lavender tea to your cat's diet, starting with a small amount and gradually increasing over several days.
  5. Serve the lavender tea as a refreshing and potentially calming beverage for your cat, either alone or mixed with a small amount of wet cat food.

5. Lemon Balm

Lemon balm tea helps with cats because it contains antiviral and anti-inflammatory properties that can soothe and heal respiratory infections and other viral ailments commonly affecting cats.

Its calming effects can also reduce stress and anxiety in cats, promoting relaxation and overall well-being. Furthermore, lemon balm tea has been shown to have a mild sedative effect, which can be beneficial for cats during stressful situations, such as travel or boarding. Additionally, its natural antioxidants can support the immune system, making it a great option for overall cat health.

However, it's important to consult with a veterinarian before giving any new herbal remedies to cats, as some herbs can be toxic to them.

The following procedure explaing how to make lemon balm tea for cats.

  1. Steep 1 teaspoon of dried lemon balm leaves (or 2 teaspoons of fresh leaves) in 8 ounces of boiling water for 5-10 minutes to extract the beneficial compounds and create a concentrated tea.
  2. Allow the tea to cool down to a comfortable drinking temperature, as hot tea can burn your cat's mouth and throat.
  3. Strain the tea using a fine-mesh strainer or cheesecloth to remove the leaves and any potential choking hazards.
  4. Carefully pour the strained tea into a shallow, easy-to-clean dish, ensuring it's not too deep or difficult for your cat to access.
  5. Offer the lemon balm tea to your cat in a calm, stress-free environment, encouraging them to drink it as a natural remedy for anxiety, stress, or digestive issues. Remember, not all cats will enjoy or tolerate lemon balm tea, so monitor your cat's behavior and reactions closely.

6. Peppermint

Peppermint tea helps with cats because it may provide relief for digestive issues in cats, such as nausea, gas, and indigestion, due to its calming and antispasmodic properties.

However, it's important to note that cats should not drink peppermint tea directly, as it contains essential oils that can be toxic to them. Instead, a few drops of peppermint oil diluted in water can be used as a natural remedy for cats, but only under the guidance of a veterinarian. Furthermore, peppermint tea can be beneficial for cat owners, as it may help reduce stress and anxiety, creating a more calming environment for their feline friends.

Lastly, while peppermint tea can offer potential benefits for cats, it should never replace proper veterinary care and should only be used as a complementary treatment.

The following procedure explaing how to make peppermint tea for cats.

  1. Steep 1 teaspoon of dried peppermint leaves in 1 cup of boiling water for 10 minutes, then let the tea cool down to a comfortable temperature for your cat.
  2. Strain the tea to remove the leaves and discard them, leaving only the liquid.
  3. Consult with a veterinarian to determine the appropriate dosage for your cat based on its weight, usually 1-2 teaspoons for every 10 pounds of body weight.
  4. Slowly introduce the peppermint tea to your cat's diet, starting with a small amount and gradually increasing, to monitor for any adverse reactions.
  5. Serve the peppermint tea to your cat in a shallow dish, and store any leftovers in the refrigerator for up to 3 days.

7. Ginger

Ginger tea helps with cats because it contains natural anti-inflammatory and digestive properties that can soothe a cat's stomach and alleviate nausea.

The soothing warmth of the tea can also help to alleviate discomfort in cats suffering from respiratory issues, such as feline upper respiratory infections. Additionally, ginger's natural antiviral and antibacterial properties may help to boost a cat's immune system and aid in recovery from illness. However, it's important to note that ginger tea should only be given to cats in moderation and with the guidance of a veterinarian, as excessive consumption can lead to digestive upset.

Lastly, it's worth mentioning that while ginger tea can be a helpful supplement for cats, it should not be used as a substitute for veterinary care or medication.

The following procedure explaing how to make ginger tea for cats.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water and add 1 teaspoon of fresh, finely grated ginger; let it simmer for 5 minutes.
  2. Turn off the heat, add 1 green tea bag, and let it steep for 2-3 minutes before removing the tea bag.
  3. Allow the ginger-tea mixture to cool down to a warm temperature, suitable for your cat to drink.
  4. Pour the ginger tea into a bowl, ensuring it's not too hot, and gently introduce it to your cat.
  5. Discard any leftover tea after 24 hours and store it in the refrigerator if needed.

8. Dandelion

Dandelion tea helps with cats because it supports their digestive health, promotes liver function, and acts as a natural diuretic, which can be beneficial for urinary tract health.

Additionally, it contains vitamins and minerals that contribute to overall feline well-being. However, it's important to consult with a veterinarian before starting any new herbal remedies, as some cats may have allergies or sensitivities to certain plants. Always use dandelion tea specifically designed for pets, as human versions may contain additives harmful to cats.

Lastly, ensure the dandelions are sourced from a chemical-free environment to avoid potential toxin exposure.

The following procedure explaing how to make dandelion tea for cats.

  1. Gather 1 teaspoon of dried dandelion flowers and leaves, ensuring they are organic and free of chemicals, and rinse them under cool water to remove any remaining dirt or impurities.
  2. Heat 1 cup of water to boiling point, then pour it over the dried dandelion flowers and leaves in a glass or ceramic teapot.
  3. Allow the tea to steep for 10-15 minutes to extract the beneficial compounds and create a strong infusion.
  4. Strain the tea using a fine-mesh strainer or a coffee filter to remove the plant material, then let it cool down to a comfortable temperature for your cat.
  5. Serve the dandelion tea to your cat in a clean bowl, offering small amounts at a time and monitoring their reaction to ensure they tolerate it well.

9. Nettle

Nettle tea helps with cats because it contains nutrients and properties that can improve a cat's overall health.

It is rich in vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants that can support a cat's immune system and promote healthy skin and coat. Nettle tea has anti-inflammatory properties that can help reduce inflammation in cats, which can be beneficial for cats with allergies or other inflammatory conditions. Additionally, nettle tea may help to alleviate symptoms of urinary tract infections in cats, as it has natural diuretic and antibacterial properties.

However, it is important to consult with a veterinarian before giving nettle tea to cats, as it may not be suitable for all cats and could interact with certain medications.

The following procedure explaing how to make nettle tea for cats.

  1. Steep 1 teaspoon of dried nettle leaves in 8 ounces of boiling water for 10-15 minutes; ensure the water is not bubbling hot as it may burn your cat's mouth.
  2. Allow the tea to cool down to a warm temperature, about room temperature or slightly warmer, to ensure it's safe and comfortable for your cat to drink.
  3. Strain the tea using a fine mesh strainer or cheesecloth to remove any solid particles and ensure a smooth drink for your cat.
  4. Pour the strained nettle tea into a clean, shallow bowl, making sure it's not too deep to prevent any accidental spills or drowning hazards for your cat.
  5. Offer the nettle tea to your cat in a calm and quiet environment, encouraging them to drink it as a natural supplement, but do not force it if they resist.

10. Red Clover

Red clover tea helps with cats because it has natural anti-inflammatory properties, making it an effective aid in managing respiratory issues and skin problems that commonly affect felines.

The isoflavones present in red clover tea can help balance hormones, potentially reducing the risk of feline reproductive system issues. Furthermore, red clover tea's antioxidant properties support overall immune system health in cats, contributing to their overall well-being. Additionally, it can promote skin healing and soothe irritations, providing relief for cats with allergies or dermatitis.

However, it's crucial to consult with a veterinarian before administering red clover tea or any herbal remedy to cats, as improper usage can lead to adverse effects.

The following procedure explaing how to make red clover tea for cats.

  1. Steep 1 teaspoon of dried red clover flowers in 8 ounces of boiling water for 10-15 minutes; allow the tea to cool down to a comfortable temperature.
  2. Strain the tea to remove the flowers and discard them.
  3. Do not add any sweeteners or flavorings, as they can be harmful to cats.
  4. Consult with a veterinarian to determine the appropriate dosage for your cat, based on its weight and health status.
  5. Serve the tea to your cat in a clean bowl, allowing it to drink as much or as little as it wants.
