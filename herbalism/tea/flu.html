10 best herbal teas for flu

When it comes to herbal teas for the flu, there are several varieties that can help soothe symptoms and promote recovery.

Peppermint tea, known for its anti-inflammatory properties, can help reduce fever and alleviate sore throat pain. Ginger tea is another excellent option, as it can help ease nausea and soothe a cough.

Additionally, chamomile tea has anti-inflammatory and calming effects, which may help promote better sleep and reduce congestion. Lastly, echinacea tea is a popular choice for flu recovery as it can boost the immune system and help shorten the duration of illness.

However, it's important to consult with a healthcare professional before consuming herbal teas as they can interact with certain medications or medical conditions.

1. Elderberry

Elderberry tea provides relief from flu due to its antiviral properties, which directly combat the influenza virus.

This tea contains high levels of vitamin C and anthocyanidins, both of which strengthen the immune system, helping it counteract the flu. Moreover, elderberry tea's antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties alleviate symptoms such as fever, sore throat, and muscle aches. Additionally, it inhibits the virus's ability to replicate, effectively shortening the duration of the flu.

Lastly, its soothing nature eases congestion and coughing, providing further relief.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in elderberry tea that help with flu.

  • Flowers: Rich in anthocyanins, the flowers help alleviate flu symptoms by possessing anti-inflammatory and antiviral properties.
  • Berries: Packed with vitamin C and antioxidants, the berries boost the immune system and fight off flu viruses.
  • Bark: Containing high tannin levels, the bark soothes throats and alleviates coughs, commonly associated with the flu.
  • Leaves: Loaded with flavonoids, the leaves exhibit antiviral and antimicrobial effects, providing relief from flu-related symptoms.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic elderberry tea for flu.

  1. In a saucepan, combine 1 cup of elderberries, 3 cups of water, and 2 inches of sliced ginger root; bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 30 minutes.
  2. Strain the mixture to remove solids, then stir in honey to taste and let cool slightly.
  3. Add the juice of 1 lemon and 1/4 teaspoon of cinnamon to the tea.
  4. Pour the tea into a mug and optionally top off with more hot water to desired strength.
  5. Sip and enjoy for soothing flu relief.

2. Echinacea

Echinacea tea assists with flu because it contains active compounds, such as alkamides and polysaccharides, which boost the immune system by increasing the number of white blood cells.

These compounds found in echinacea tea inhibit the attachment and entry of the virus into cells, thus reducing the virus's ability to replicate. Moreover, echinacea tea's anti-inflammatory properties alleviate flu symptoms, such as sore throat, cough, and fever, by decreasing the production of inflammatory chemicals in the body. Additionally, echinacea tea's antioxidant properties protect cells from oxidative damage caused by the flu virus, promoting faster recovery.

Ultimately, echinacea tea's immune-boosting, antiviral, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties make it an effective remedy for managing flu symptoms and promoting recovery.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in echinacea tea that help with flu.

  • Roots: Contain numerous beneficial compounds, such as cichoric acid and echinacoside, which have immune-boosting and anti-inflammatory properties, making them valuable for fighting off flu symptoms.
  • Leaves: Rich in vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants, providing essential nutrients to help strengthen the immune system and alleviate flu-related symptoms.
  • Flowers: Packed with flavonoids and other antioxidants that support overall immune function and help reduce inflammation caused by the flu.
  • Buds: High in rosmarinic acid, a potent anti-inflammatory compound, which aids in soothing sore throats and easing other flu-related aches and pains.
  • Seeds: Contain essential oils with antimicrobial and antiviral properties, providing additional support in eliminating the flu virus from the body.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic echinacea tea for flu.

  1. In a saucepan, combine 2 cups of water and 2 tablespoons of dried echinacea flowers or 4 echinacea tea bags.
  2. Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce heat and let it simmer for 15-20 minutes.
  3. Remove the saucepan from heat and take out the tea bags (if used).
  4. Strain the liquid to remove echinacea flowers (if used) and let it cool for a few minutes.
  5. Pour the tea into a cup, add honey or lemon to taste, and enjoy.

3. Peppermint

Peppermint tea soothes sore throats and relieves congestion due to its anti-inflammatory and expectorant properties, derived from its menthol component.

This tea's warm temperature helps to thin mucus, making it easier to expel, while its menthol content alleviates stuffy noses by acting as a natural decongestant. Furthermore, peppermint tea's antiviral qualities hinder flu-causing viruses' proliferation, bolstering the immune system.

Lastly, this herbal tea's calming effects on the gastrointestinal tract address nausea and indigestion often accompanying the flu.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in peppermint tea that help with flu.

  • Leaves: Rich in menthol and rosmarinic acid, peppermint leaves help break up phlegm and provide relief from congestion.
  • Flowers: High in antioxidants, peppermint flowers support immune function and soothe sore throats.
  • Seeds: Packed with essential oils, peppermint seeds aid in reducing fever and inflammation.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic peppermint tea for flu.

  1. In a teapot, add boiling water to 1 teaspoon of dried peppermint leaves and let it steep for 10 minutes.
  2. Strain the tea into a cup to remove the leaves.
  3. Squeeze half a lemon into the tea for added vitamin C.
  4. Stir in 1-2 teaspoons of honey for soothing a sore throat.
  5. Drink the tea while warm, up to three times a day, for relief of flu symptoms.

4. Ginger

Ginger tea offers valuable help with the flu due to its potent natural properties.

Ginger contains active compounds, such as gingerols and shogaols, which possess antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antiviral effects. These properties work together to alleviate flu symptoms by reducing inflammation in the body, soothing sore throats, and easing congestion. Furthermore, ginger aids in strengthening the immune system, making it more effective at fighting off the influenza virus.

Right below you will find a list of the most important active constituents in ginger tea that help with flu.

  • Rhizomes: Ginger rhizomes are a common ingredient in medicinal tea for flu due to their antiviral and anti-inflammatory properties.
  • Roots: Ginger roots contain compounds that alleviate nausea, sore throat, and fatigue caused by the flu.
  • Leaves: Ginger leaves have antioxidant and antimicrobial properties that help clear flu symptoms.
  • Flowers: Ginger flowers are rich in polyphenols, which have antiviral and expectorant effects beneficial for treating the flu.
  • Seeds: Ginger seeds are used in medicinal teas for their warming and diaphoretic properties, which help reduce fever during the flu.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic ginger tea for flu.

  1. Peel and chop 1-2 inches of fresh ginger root into small slices, then place in a pot.
  2. Pour 4 cups of water into the pot and bring to a boil.
  3. Reduce heat and let the ginger simmer for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Strain the ginger tea into a cup, then add honey and lemon juice to taste.
  5. Drink the tea while warm, up to three times a day for flu relief.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic cinnamon tea for flu.

  1. In a pot, boil 1 cup of water for 3-5 minutes.
  2. Stir in 1 cinnamon stick and let it simmer for 10-15 minutes.
  3. Remove cinnamon stick and add 1 teaspoon of honey, mix until fully dissolved.
  4. Optionally, squeeze in some lemon juice for added vitamin C.
  5. Pour the tea through a strainer into a cup and enjoy while warm.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic sage tea for flu.

  3. Remove the pot from heat and strain the liquid to separate the leaves.
  4. Add honey or lemon to taste, if desired, and stir until fully dissolved.
  5. Pour the sage tea into a cup and enjoy while warm, up to three times a day.
