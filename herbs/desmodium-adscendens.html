Scientific and Botanical Profile

Desmodium, with botanical name Desmodium adscendens, is a leguminous plant belonging to the Fabaceae family, commonly known as the legume or pea family.

Native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Americas, it is widely distributed and has been introduced to various parts of the world for its nutritional and medicinal value. Morphologically, Desmodium adscendens is a climbing herb with trifoliate leaves, each leaflet being ovate to elliptical in shape, and it produces small, yellow to pale pink flowers that are often clustered in racemes. The plant is also referred to by numerous common names such as Babylonian Spinach, Climbing Desmodium, Velvet Bean, and Touch-Me-Not, reflecting its widespread use and unique characteristics.

Additionally, it is known as Sensitive Plant, Mimosa Plant, and Climbing Bignay, highlighting its sensitivity to touch and its role in traditional agriculture and herbal medicine.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Desmodium adscendens has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used as an infusion or applied topically for skin conditions. Due to limited scientific evidence, it is advised to use desmodium adscendens sparingly and for short durations, unless otherwise directed by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Desmodium adscendens can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory, antidiabetic, and antimicrobial properties, but it must be used with caution.

While generally considered safe when taken in appropriate doses, it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals. It is important to note that desmodium adscendens may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting blood sugar or the liver, and should not be combined with other herbs without professional guidance. Special populations, including pregnant or breastfeeding women and individuals with chronic illnesses, should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data and potential risks.

To ensure safe use, always consult a healthcare provider before taking desmodium adscendens, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Desmodium adscendens grows best in well-drained, loamy soils with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires consistent moisture during its growing season. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates, and regular watering is essential, especially during dry periods, while avoiding waterlogging to prevent root rot. For optimal growth, it should be planted in rows with adequate spacing to allow for air circulation and to reduce the risk of disease. Harvesting should occur when the leaves are fully developed but before flowering, typically 60 to 90 days after planting, using sharp scissors or pruning shears to cut the stems just above the base.

To preserve its potency, the harvested plant should be shade-dried in a well-ventilated area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain its medicinal properties.
