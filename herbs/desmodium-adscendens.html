What is Desmodium (Desmodium Adscendens)?

Desmodium adscendens, also known as climbing desmodium, is a flowering plant native to tropical and subtropical regions, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and digestive properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Desmodium is Desmodium adscendens. This plant belongs to the family Fabaceae, order Fabales, class Fabales, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. It is commonly known as "sensitive plant" in South America and "touch-me-not" in parts of Africa. The plant is also referred to as "milkweed" in some regions of Southeast Asia. Synonyms include Desmodium gyrans and Desmodium subulatum, with regional names like "sensitive plant" and "touch-me-not" being widely recognized.

Plant Description and Morphology

Desmodium is a climbing legume native to tropical and subtropical regions, typically growing as a perennial herb reaching heights of 1 to 3 meters.

Its stems are slender, twining, and often branched, with a rough texture and a faint, spicy aroma. The leaves are trifoliate, with three oval to heart-shaped leaflets that are smooth and slightly hairy on the underside. The flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, and have a distinctive pink to purple color with five petals arranged in a butterfly-like pattern.

The root system consists of deep taproots and numerous lateral roots, which help in nitrogen fixation and soil stabilization.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Desmodium is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, particularly in countries such as South Africa, Kenya, and Tanzania.

It is also found in parts of Asia, including India and China, and in the Caribbean islands. The plant thrives in warm climates with well-defined wet and dry seasons. It prefers well-drained soils and full sun to partial shade.

Desmodium is commonly found in grasslands, savannas, and disturbed soils in its native habitats.

The primary plant parts used medicinally in Desmodium adscendens are the leaves, stems, and roots.

The leaves are harvested when fully expanded and dried for use in traditional remedies. The stems are often collected during the flowering season and used similarly to the leaves. Roots are typically harvested in the early stages of growth and dried for their medicinal properties.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Desmodium adscendens is generally considered safe for most individuals, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort or allergic reactions in some cases.
