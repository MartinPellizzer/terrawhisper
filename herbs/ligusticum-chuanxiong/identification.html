Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Chuan Xiong is Ligusticum chuanxiong. It was first described by the botanist Carl von Linné in 1790. This name is widely accepted and used in botanical literature. Synonyms include Angelica sinensis and Ligusticum wallichii, though these are now considered separate species.

Morphological Characteristics

Chuan Xiong is a perennial herb with a taproot system. It has a straight, cylindrical stem that is hollow and has a slight fragrance. The leaves are basal, alternate, and have a pinnate structure with serrated edges. The inflorescence is a compound umbel, with small, white flowers that have a subtle scent. The fruit is a dry, three-lobed achene with a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Chuan Xiong can be distinguished by its distinctive purple-red coloration on the rhizome and roots, which is absent in similar species. Its leaves are broadly lanceolate with prominent veins, and the flowers are typically pink to purple, setting it apart from other Ligusticum species. The presence of a strong aromatic odor when crushed is another key diagnostic feature.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Chuan Xiong is native to southern China, particularly the Sichuan and Hubei provinces. It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters and warm summers. The plant prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It is typically found at altitudes between 500 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Chuan Xiong typically blooms from May to July. Seed formation occurs from August to September. For identification purposes, the herb is harvested when the stems are mature and the flowers have fully opened, usually between June and August.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Chuan Xiong belongs to the genus Ligusticum in the family Apiaceae. Its closest relatives include species like Ligusticum sinense and Ligusticum wallichii. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, reflecting their close phylogenetic relationships within the genus.
