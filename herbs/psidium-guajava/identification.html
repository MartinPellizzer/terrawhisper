Botanical Identification of Guava (Psidium guajava)

To identify Guava (Psidium guajava) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Guava belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Solanales, family Solanaceae, genus Psidium, and species Psidium guajava. These classifications help identify its botanical relationships and place it within the plant kingdom.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Psidium guajava is the scientific name for guava, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the Myrtaceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Psidium cattleyanum and Psidium littorale. Common names vary globally, with "guava" being the most widely recognized. Regional and historical names reflect its long-standing use in traditional medicine and cuisine.

Morphological Characteristics

Guava is a small, evergreen tree or shrub with a shallow, fibrous root system. Its stem is upright, often branching at the base, and covered with rough, whitish bark. Leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptic, with a glossy green upper surface and a lighter underside. Inflorescences are axillary clusters, bearing small, white or pinkish flowers with five petals and numerous stamens. The fruit is a tropical berry, typically round to oblong, with a thick, aromatic skin and a juicy, segmented interior containing multiple seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Guava can be distinguished by its opposite, ovate leaves with a slightly serrated margin, and its clusters of small, white, five-petaled flowers. The fruit is typically round, green when unripe, and turns yellow or red when mature, with a thick, fragrant skin and a juicy, sweet flesh. These features help differentiate it from similar species like physalis or cape gooseberry.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Guava is native to the Americas, particularly Central and South America, and parts of the Caribbean. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates with warm temperatures and ample sunlight. The plant prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes ranging from sea level up to 1,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Guava trees typically bloom from late spring to early summer, producing small white or pink flowers. Seed formation occurs approximately 2-3 months after flowering. Harvesting takes place 4-6 months after flowering, usually in late summer to early autumn, when the fruit is fully colored and firm.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Psidium guajava belongs to the Myrtaceae family and is closely related to species like Psidium cattleyanum and Psidium lucidum. It shares characteristics with other members of the Psidium genus, such as similar leaf structures and flower morphology. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary placement within the broader context of tropical plants used in herbal medicine.
