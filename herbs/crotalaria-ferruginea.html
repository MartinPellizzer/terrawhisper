Crotalaria ferruginea

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crotalaria ferruginea has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, woody stems that are densely covered in ferruginous, or rusty-colored, stellate hairs. The leaves are typically pinnate with several pairs of oblong to elliptical leaflets that also exhibit a velvety texture due to their indumentum. Small, bright yellow flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves, featuring a distinct papilionaceous structure typical of the Fabaceae family. The fruit is a compressed, bristly legume pod that contains multiple seeds protected by a dense layer of brownish pubescence. These morphological traits, particularly the rusty hairiness, serve as key diagnostic features for distinguishing this species from related taxa.

This plant is native to the dry, open habitats of East Africa, specifically found within countries such as Kenya, Tanzania, and Ethiopia. Its distribution is often linked to acacia savannas and scrublands that characterize these tropical regions. Within these ecosystems, the species occupies specific niches where sunlight is abundant and competition from dense forest canopy is minimal.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Crotalaria ferruginea can cause severe liver damage and death due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids within its tissues. These toxic compounds are known to induce hepatic veno-occlusive disease, which obstructs the small veins in the liver. Once ingested, the alkaloids undergo metabolic activation in the body to form highly reactive pyrroles. These substances cause progressive inflammation and fibrosis that can lead to permanent organ failure. Continuous exposure to this plant poses a significant risk to livestock and humans alike.
