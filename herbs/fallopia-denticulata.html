Fallopia denticulata

What's the taxonomical classification of Fallopia denticulata?

Fallopia denticulata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Caryophyllales, it is placed in the family Polygonaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Fallopia and is specifically designated as the species denticulata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Fallopia denticulata has erect, hollow, reddish-brown stems that can reach heights of up to two meters and feature distinct swollen nodes. The leaves are broadly ovate or heart-shaped with serrated margins and a prominent vein pattern. Small, creamy-white flowers grow in dense, drooping clusters called racemes that emerge from the leaf axils. These blossoms eventually develop into small, dark, nut-like fruits that characterize the plant's reproductive structure. The root system is composed of creeping rhizomes that allow the plant to spread extensively through the soil.

What are the pharmacological activities of Fallopia denticulata?

This plant has anti-influenza neuraminidase inhibitory activity, meaning its extracts can block the enzyme used by the influenza virus to spread between cells. These specific pharmacological actions are primarily attributed to the presence of bioactive polyphenolic compounds found within its roots. Research indicates that these components can effectively interfere with the viral life cycle by preventing the release of new viral particles. Beyond its antiviral properties, the plant also exhibits significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory potential. These combined activities make Fallopia denticulata a subject of interest for developing natural therapeutic agents against respiratory infections.

As discussed in a study, Fallopia denticulata exhibits the best neuraminidase inhibition activity among tested extracts. Analysis of the metabolic components through ultra-performance liquid chromatography-time-of-flight mass spectrometry identified 21 compounds, including 3 organic acids, 11 flavonoids, 1 coumarin, and 6 others. Of these identified components, 12 compounds were discovered for the first time in Fallopia denticulata. Molecular docking confirmed that the two better bioactive compounds are (-) -epicatechin gallate and (+) -catechin. These findings suggest that Fallopia denticulata could be used as a potential material for new anti-influenza drugs.
