Euphorbia kansuensis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia kansuensis has succulent, cylindrical stems that are green in color and typically feature small, pointed tubercles along their surface. These stems function as primary water storage organs to help the plant survive in arid environments. The leaves are relatively small and are often deciduous, appearing briefly during periods of moisture before falling off. Tiny, inconspicuous flowers are produced in specialized structures known as cyathia, which are characteristic of the Euphorbia genus. The overall growth habit is compact and shrub-like, reflecting its adaptation to rocky or desert landscapes.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the island of Kanchanaburi in Thailand, where it grows in specific localized environments. Its distribution is highly restricted to certain rocky outcrops and limestone formations within this single province. Because it is found in such a narrow range, the species is considered geographically limited. The unique soil compositions of these specific areas are essential for its survival. Consequently, the plant does not occur naturally in any other parts of Southeast Asia.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Euphorbia kansuensis can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and intense eye inflammation if its milky white latex comes into contact with human tissue. This toxic sap contains diterpene esters that act as potent irritants upon contact with the skin. If the liquid enters the eyes, it may lead to temporary blindness or significant corneal damage. Ingesting any part of the plant can result in painful swelling of the mouth and throat, nausea, and gastrointestinal distress. Protective gear should always be worn when handling this species to prevent accidental exposure to these harmful substances.
