Botanical Identification of Black-Eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta)

To identify Black-Eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Black-Eyed Susan, or Rudbeckia hirta, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Rudbeckia, and species Rudbeckia hirta. These classifications reflect its role as a flowering plant in the sunflower family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Rudbeckia hirta is the scientific name for Black-Eyed Susan. It was first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. Common names include Black-Eyed Susan, Brown-eye Susan, and Goatsbread. It is also known as Yellowdisk in some regions and has historical references as a remedy in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Rudbeckia hirta is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and can grow up to 1.5 meters tall. Leaves are alternate, heart-shaped, and covered in fine hairs. The inflorescence is a composite flower head with yellow ray florets surrounding brown disc florets. The fruit is a dry, hairy achene with a pappus for wind dispersal.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Black-Eyed Susan can be distinguished by its yellow-orange petals and dark brown central disk. It has rough, hairy leaves and grows up to 3 feet tall. It blooms in late summer and is commonly found in open fields and along roadsides. It is often confused with other rudbeckias but is easily identified by its distinct color contrast and leaf texture.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Black-Eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta) is native to eastern North America, including the United States and Canada. It thrives in temperate climates with full sun and well-drained soil. It prefers loamy or sandy soils and is commonly found at low to mid-elevation regions.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Black-Eyed Susan blooms from late spring to early fall, typically between May and September. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the most accurate time to observe key features is during its blooming period, from June through August.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Rudbeckia hirta is closely related to other members of the genus Rudbeckia, which belongs to the family Asteraceae. It shares key traits with other sunflower relatives, such as composite flower heads and alternate leaf arrangements. Its phylogenetic placement reflects its evolutionary ties to other composite plants within the tribe Heliantheae.
