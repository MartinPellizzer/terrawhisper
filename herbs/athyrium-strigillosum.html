Athyrium strigillosum

What's the taxonomical classification of Athyrium strigillosum?

Athyrium strigillosum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida, it falls under the subclass Polypodiinae and the order Polypodiales. Its taxonomic journey continues through the family Aspleniaceae, specifically within the genus Athyrium, ultimately reaching its designation as the species strigillosum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Athyrium strigillosum has large, bipinnate to tripinnate fronds that typically measure between 30 and 80 centimeters in length with lanceolate to oblong-lanceolate leaflets.

The stipe is dark brown and features a distinct groove on the upper surface. Each pinna is subdivided into smaller pinnules that possess finely serrated or slightly lobed margins. The underside of the mature blades is characterized by prominent sori located on the margins, which are protected by a thin, reflexed indusium.

These fern structures emerge from a creeping rhizome that is covered in pale, brownish scales.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Athyrium strigillosum targets inflammatory signaling proteins such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and various pro-inflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). The bioactive compounds within the plant, including flavonoids and phenolic acids, inhibit these enzymes to reduce the production of prostaglandins and nitric oxide. These molecular interactions help suppress the activation of the NF-kappaB signaling pathway, which is a primary regulator of the immune response. By modulating these specific protein activities, the plant exerts potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects in biological systems. Such targeted inhibition of enzymatic pathways provides the underlying mechanism for its traditional medicinal uses in treating swelling and pain.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Athyrium strigillosum can cause contact dermatitis, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals upon direct physical contact with its fronds. The plant contains various secondary metabolites that may trigger localized redness or itching if the foliage touches the skin. Some people might experience swelling or a rash if they handle the fern without protective gloves. While systemic toxicity is not widely documented, the primary risk involves external dermatological distress. Caution is recommended for those with known sensitivities to common fern species.
