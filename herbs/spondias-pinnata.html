Spondias pinnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Spondias pinnata?

Spondias pinnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Sapindales. This plant is a representative of the family Anacardiaceae, which places it within the genus Spondias. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by its species name, pinnata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Spondias pinnata has pinnately compound leaves with 5 to 15 pairs of oblong leaflets that possess entire margins and an acute apex. These leaves are arranged alternately along woody branches that can develop prominent thorns. The tree produces small, five-petaled white or yellowish flowers that grow in large, fragrant terminal panicles. After flowering, it develops fleshy, drupaceous fruits characterized by a smooth skin and a single large seed. The bark is generally smooth and greyish, providing a stark contrast to the dense green canopy.

Because it prefers well-drained soils, its natural range is often concentrated in areas with seasonal monsoon patterns. What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing diabetes, including type 1 diabetes mellitus, through its various bioactive compounds. Researchers have explored its potential to regulate blood glucose levels and improve insulin sensitivity in patients. The fruit and leaves contain phytochemicals that may assist in stabilizing glycemic indices. Some studies suggest that the plant's antioxidant properties help mitigate the oxidative stress often associated with chronic diabetic complications. These therapeutic applications make it a subject of significant interest in ethnobotanical research.

According to a study published by "Current drug discovery technologies", Spondias pinnata is used for its β-cell regenerative potential in the treatment of type 1 diabetes mellitus. In the investigation, alloxan monohydrate was used at 150 mg/kg to induce diabetes in Wisstar albino rats. The treatment groups received Spondias pinnata at a dosage of 1.0 g/kg for a duration of 30 days. Results showed a significant increase in the concentrations of serum insulin and C-peptide in the treated rats (p < 0.05). This plant extract exerted β-cell regenerative potential in diabetic rats and could be valued as a natural agent for prompting β-cell regeneration in vivo.
