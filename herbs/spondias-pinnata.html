Spondias pinnata

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Spondias pinnata has pinnately compound leaves with 5 to 15 pairs of oblong leaflets that possess entire margins and an acute apex. These leaves are arranged alternately along woody branches that can develop prominent thorns. The tree produces small, five-petaled white or yellowish flowers that grow in large, fragrant terminal panicles. After flowering, it develops fleshy, drupaceous fruits characterized by a smooth skin and a single large seed. The bark is generally smooth and greyish, providing a stark contrast to the dense green canopy.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning from India and Sri Lanka to parts of China and Indochina. It thrives primarily in coastal areas and low-lying plains where the climate remains warm and humid throughout the year. Because it prefers well-drained soils, its natural range is often concentrated in areas with seasonal monsoon patterns. The species has also been introduced to other tropical territories across the globe due to its edible fruit. Consequently, its distribution has expanded beyond its original evolutionary boundaries into many island ecosystems.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Spondias pinnata can cause allergic reactions such as contact dermatitis, oral irritation, or digestive upset in sensitive individuals. The high sugar content in the ripe fruit may lead to blood glucose spikes if consumed in excessive amounts. Some people might experience mild gastrointestinal discomfort or bloating after eating the pulp. Additionally, the latex found in the plant parts can trigger skin rashes or localized swelling upon direct contact. Careful moderation and testing for sensitivities are recommended when incorporating this fruit into a diet.
