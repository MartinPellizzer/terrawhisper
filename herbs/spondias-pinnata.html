Spondias pinnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Spondias pinnata?

Spondias pinnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Sapindales. This plant is a representative of the family Anacardiaceae, which places it within the genus Spondias. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by its species name, pinnata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Spondias pinnata has pinnately compound leaves with 5 to 15 pairs of oblong leaflets that possess entire margins and an acute apex.

These leaves are arranged alternately along woody branches that can develop prominent thorns. The tree produces small, five-petaled white or yellowish flowers that grow in large, fragrant terminal panicles. After flowering, it develops fleshy, drupaceous fruits characterized by a smooth skin and a single large seed.

The bark is generally smooth and greyish, providing a stark contrast to the dense green canopy.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Spondias pinnata targets enzymes such as cyclooxygenase, lipoxygenase, and alpha-glucosidase to exert its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antidiabetic effects. The bioactive phytochemicals found in the plant, including flavonoids and tannins, inhibit the cyclooxygenase pathway to reduce the production of pro-inflammatory mediators. Additionally, these compounds interact with alpha-glucosidase to slow down carbohydrate absorption, which helps in regulating blood glucose levels. They also mitigate oxidative stress by scavenging free radicals and modulating antioxidant enzyme activities within cellular environments. These specific protein interactions underpin the plant's diverse pharmacological potential in treating metabolic and inflammatory disorders.

What are the herbal preparations of Spondias pinnata?

Spondias pinnata is prepared as decoctions, infusions, juices, syrups, powders, and poultices using its various fleshy fruits, leaves, bark, and seeds. The fruit is commonly processed into fresh juices or fermented into alcoholic beverages to serve as a refreshing tonic. Leaves are often boiled in water to create medicinal teas or crushed into a paste for topical applications. Bark extracts are frequently used in decoctions to address specific systemic ailments. What side-effects this plant can have?

Spondias pinnata can cause allergic reactions such as contact dermatitis, oral irritation, or digestive upset in sensitive individuals. The high sugar content in the ripe fruit may lead to blood glucose spikes if consumed in excessive amounts. Some people might experience mild gastrointestinal discomfort or bloating after eating the pulp. Additionally, the latex found in the plant parts can trigger skin rashes or localized swelling upon direct contact. Careful moderation and testing for sensitivities are recommended when incorporating this fruit into a diet.
