What's the taxonomical classification of Oxytropis bicolor?

Oxytropis bicolor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent through the order Fabales, it is placed within the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Oxytropis, with its specific identification being the species bicolor.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxytropis bicolor has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect stems that reach heights of 10 to 40 centimeters and are covered in fine, silvery-white hairs.

The leaves are pinnately compound, typically featuring 7 to 15 narrow, linear leaflets that contribute to a soft, pubescent appearance. Its most distinctive morphological feature is the bicolor flower, which displays a deep purple or violet standard petal contrasted against lighter, often whitish or pale lavender wing and keel petals. The calyx is notably hairy and serves to protect the developing reproductive structures within the legume family.

Small, light-colored pods follow the blooming period, completing the plant's reproductive morphology.

      What are the herbal preparations of Oxytropis bicolor?

      Oxytropis bicolor is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice for medicinal use. The roots are typically boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction used for internal ailments. For external applications, the dried plant material can be crushed and mixed with water to form a poultice. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the delivery of the plant's bioactive constituents to specific health needs.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Oxytropis bicolor can cause severe poisoning in livestock including cattle, sheep, and horses due to its high concentration of toxic alkaloids. These alkaloids primarily target the central nervous system and can lead to symptoms such as muscle tremors, staggering, and loss of coordination. If the plant is ingested in large quantities, animals may experience profound weakness or even sudden death. Chronic exposure to the plant often results in permanent neurological damage or reproductive issues in grazing herds. Because the toxins are potent, even small amounts consumed during early growth stages can be fatal to sensitive species.
