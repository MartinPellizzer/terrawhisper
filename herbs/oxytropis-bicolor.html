Oxytropis bicolor

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxytropis bicolor has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect stems that reach heights of 10 to 40 centimeters and are covered in fine, silvery-white hairs. The leaves are pinnately compound, typically featuring 7 to 15 narrow, linear leaflets that contribute to a soft, pubescent appearance. Its most distinctive morphological feature is the bicolor flower, which displays a deep purple or violet standard petal contrasted against lighter, often whitish or pale lavender wing and keel petals. The calyx is notably hairy and serves to protect the developing reproductive structures within the legume family. Small, light-colored pods follow the blooming period, completing the plant's reproductive morphology.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Oxytropis bicolor can cause severe poisoning in livestock including cattle, sheep, and horses due to its high concentration of toxic alkaloids. These alkaloids primarily target the central nervous system and can lead to symptoms such as muscle tremors, staggering, and loss of coordination. If the plant is ingested in large quantities, animals may experience profound weakness or even sudden death. Chronic exposure to the plant often results in permanent neurological damage or reproductive issues in grazing herds. Because the toxins are potent, even small amounts consumed during early growth stages can be fatal to sensitive species.
