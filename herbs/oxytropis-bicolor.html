Oxytropis bicolor

What's the taxonomical classification of Oxytropis bicolor?

Oxytropis bicolor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent through the order Fabales, it is placed within the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Oxytropis, with its specific identification being the species bicolor.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Oxytropis bicolor contains various alkaloids, including quinolizidine alkaloids, which serve as the primary bioactive constituents of the plant. These specific nitrogenous compounds are characteristic of the Fabaceae family and often exhibit complex pharmacological activities. Some studies suggest that these alkaloids may possess neurotoxic or antimicrobial properties depending on their specific chemical structures. The concentration and type of these compounds can vary significantly based on the plant's environmental conditions and growth stage. Researchers examine these chemical profiles to understand the potential for developing new therapeutic agents or identifying natural toxins.

What are the herbal preparations of Oxytropis bicolor?

Oxytropis bicolor is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice for medicinal use. The roots are typically boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction used for internal ailments. For external applications, the dried plant material can be crushed and mixed with water to form a poultice. Infusions are often made by steeping the softer parts of the plant in hot liquid to extract delicate compounds. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the delivery of the plant's bioactive constituents to specific health needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Oxytropis bicolor can cause severe poisoning in livestock including cattle, sheep, and horses due to its high concentration of toxic alkaloids. These alkaloids primarily target the central nervous system and can lead to symptoms such as muscle tremors, staggering, and loss of coordination. If the plant is ingested in large quantities, animals may experience profound weakness or even sudden death. Chronic exposure to the plant often results in permanent neurological damage or reproductive issues in grazing herds. Because the toxins are potent, even small amounts consumed during early growth stages can be fatal to sensitive species.
