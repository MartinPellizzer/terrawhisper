Caltha palustris

What's the taxonomical classification of Caltha palustris?

Caltha palustris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Ranunculales. It is further categorized under the family Ranunculaceae, which places it within the genus Caltha. Finally, its specific identification is completed by its species name, palustris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Caltha palustris has kidney-shaped, bright green leaves that are glossy, cordate to reniform in shape, and possess crenate or wavy margins. These leaves grow on long, fleshy petioles that emerge from a rhizomatous root system. The plant produces clusters of bright yellow, actinomorphic flowers with five to nine distinct petals that surround a central cluster of numerous stamens. Each flower is supported by a sturdy, succulent stem that can reach heights of up to thirty centimeters. The overall structure is characterized by its lush, herbaceous foliage and vibrant, cup-shaped blooms.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the northern regions of the Northern Hemisphere, ranging across much of Europe, northern Asia, and North America. It is commonly found in temperate climates where moist or marshy environments are abundant. In North America, its distribution extends from the Arctic tundra down through the northern United States. It frequently populates wetlands, such as bogs, fens, and the edges of ponds, throughout these latitudes. This wide-reaching presence is primarily dictated by the availability of saturated soils and cool temperature profiles.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires consistently waterlogged soil or shallow standing water to thrive in a wetland or bog environment.

It should be planted in full sun to partial shade to ensure healthy growth and vibrant flowering. Because it is a perennial that spreads via rhizomes, providing ample space in a pond margin or a dedicated bog garden is essential. Regular moisture is necessary to prevent the root system from drying out entirely.

Once established, it is relatively low-maintenance as long as its aquatic habitat remains stable.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Caltha palustris contains medicinal properties primarily derived from its whole aerial parts, including the leaves and stems. These plant components are traditionally utilized in herbal preparations to address various ailments such as skin irritations and inflammatory conditions. The leaves are often processed into poultices or infusions to treat external wounds or topical swellings. It is important to note that the plant contains protoanemonin, a toxic substance that requires careful preparation to mitigate potential harm. Consequently, most medicinal applications involve drying or specific processing methods to ensure safety during use.

What are the pharmacological activities of Caltha palustris?

This plant has significant pharmacological properties including potent antioxidant and cytotoxic activities. The antioxidant potential of Caltha palustris helps neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. Research also indicates that specific extracts from the plant exhibit cytotoxicity against various cancer cell lines. These bioactive compounds work by inducing cell death in targeted malignant cells while protecting healthy tissue from oxidative damage. Such multifaceted biological actions make this species a subject of interest for future drug development.

According to a study published by "International journal of molecular sciences", Caltha palustris extracts demonstrated a dual antioxidant and cytotoxicity effect. This species was one of 16 Ranunculaceae species assessed from 19 ethanolic extracts to evaluate in vitro antioxidant, antiproliferative, and antiparasitic potential. While the study noted that all tested extracts showed no or low toxicity against the FHs 74Int normal cell line, with IC50 values ranging from 152.9 to >512 µg/mL, the specific efficacy for Caltha palustris was characterized by its dual effects. These findings suggest that such plant extracts may serve as potential candidates for the development of novel antioxidant or antiproliferative compounds.
