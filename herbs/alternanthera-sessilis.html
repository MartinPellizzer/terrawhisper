Alternanthera sessilis

What's the taxonomical classification of Alternanthera sessilis?

Alternanthera sessilis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Caryophyllales. This plant is a member of the family Amaranthaceae, falling under the genus Alternanthera, and is specifically identified by its species name, sessilis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Alternanthethera sessilis has prostrate, creeping stems that branch extensively from a central root system and possess small, elliptical to lanceolate leaves measuring roughly 1 to 3 centimeters in length. The leaves are typically arranged oppositely along the stems and exhibit a smooth or slightly hairy texture on their surfaces. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in dense, axillary clusters that lack prominent petals. These plants often develop a reddish or purplish tint on their foliage and stems depending on light exposure. The root system is shallow but efficient at anchoring the plant as it spreads across the soil surface.

What are the pharmacological activities of Alternanthera sessilis?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, anti-oxidant, and anti-diabetic properties. Its chemical constituents allow it to effectively reduce pain and suppress inflammatory responses in biological systems. The plant also demonstrates significant capacity to inhibit the growth of various harmful microorganisms. Furthermore, its high antioxidant content helps neutralize free radicals and mitigate oxidative stress within cells. These combined therapeutic effects also contribute to its potential utility in managing blood glucose levels for anti-diabetic treatment.

According to a study published by "BMC complementary and alternative medicine", the methanol extract of the aerial parts of Alternanthera sessilis demonstrates significant antihyperglycemic and analgesic activities. In oral glucose tolerance tests, administration of the extract at doses of 50, 100, 200, and 400 mg per kg body weight reduced blood glucose levels by 22.9, 30.7, 45.4, and 46.1%, respectively. For analgesic activity in mice, these same four doses reduced the number of abdominal writhes by 27.6, 37.9, 41.4, and 44.8%, respectively. These results validate the folk medicinal use of the plant for the alleviation of severe pain. The findings suggest that the plant may serve as a potential source for blood sugar lowering drugs.
