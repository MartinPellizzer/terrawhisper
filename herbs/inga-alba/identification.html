Botanical Identification of White Inga (Inga alba)

To identify White Inga (Inga alba) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

White Inga, or Inga alba, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of flowering plants, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Inga, and species Inga alba. This classification places it within the legume family, known for nitrogen-fixing capabilities and diverse ecological roles.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name of White Inga is Inga alba, a species within the legume family. It was first described by the botanist A. DC. in 1825. This name is widely accepted and used in scientific literature. Synonyms and common names vary by region, but the scientific name remains the standard for identification and study.

Morphological Characteristics

White Inga (Inga alba) is a small to medium-sized tree with a spreading growth habit. It has a shallow, fibrous root system that supports its rapid growth. The stem is straight and often branched, with a smooth, greyish-brown bark. Its leaves are alternate, pinnately compound, and have a delicate, feathery appearance. The inflorescence consists of axillary clusters, with small, white flowers that emit a faint fragrance. The fruit is a flattened pod containing several seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

Diagnostic Identification Features

White Inga (Inga alba) can be distinguished by its compound leaves with 7-13 pairs of elliptic to ovate leaflets, white to pale yellow flowers in dense clusters, and small, round, yellow fruits. It differs from similar species like Inga olearum by its leaf shape and flower color, and from Inga insignis by its fruit size and pubescence.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

White Inga (Inga alba) is native to tropical regions of South America, particularly Brazil, Colombia, and Peru. It thrives in warm, humid climates with high rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils and is commonly found at low to mid-altitudes, typically between 200 and 1,000 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

White Inga (Inga alba) blooms throughout the wet season, typically from April to October, depending on regional rainfall. Seed formation occurs 6-8 months after flowering, usually between October and March. For identification purposes, harvest timing is best during the dry season when leaves are less abundant, making botanical features more visible.

Phylogenetic Relationships

White Inga (Inga alba) is closely related to other species within the Inga genus, which belongs to the legume family Fabaceae. It shares key family traits such as pod fruits and nitrogen-fixing abilities. Its closest relatives include other Inga species like Inga obovata and Inga vera, which are also used in traditional herbal medicine.
