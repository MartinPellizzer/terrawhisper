Botanical Identification

Sea Buckthorn, scientifically known as Hippophae rhamnoides, is a deciduous shrub belonging to the Elaeagnaceae family. It is native to central and eastern Europe and parts of Asia. Common names include Sea Buckthorn and Chinese Tea Tree, with variations in different regions. The plant is a perennial shrub with yellow berries, alternate leaves, and small yellow flowers. It has several synonyms in herbal literature, including Elaeagnus rhamnoides.

Active Compounds

Sea Buckthorn contains key bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, and tannins, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds work synergistically to reduce inflammation, support antioxidant activity, and enhance skin and digestive health. Their combined effects make Sea Buckthorn a valuable component in herbal medicine.

  • Flavonoid
  • Phenolic acid
  • Tannin

Therapeutic Indications

System Condidtion Action
Respiratory Cough, asthma, chronic cough Expectorant, bronchodilator, immunostimulant
Integumentary Skin infections, scabies, eczema Antimicrobial, antiparasitic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant
Immune Inflammation, immune deficiency Anti-inflammatory, immunostimulant

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for respiratory issues and as a general tonic.

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and to extract nutrients from the bark.

Oil infusion: Used for skin conditions and as a topical treatment for wounds.

Safety Profile

Sea Buckthorn is generally safe when used in recommended doses. However, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals. Prolonged use or high doses could lead to adverse effects, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with known allergies.
