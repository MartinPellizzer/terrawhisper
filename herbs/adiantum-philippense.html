What's the taxonomical classification of Adiantum philippense?

Adiantum philippense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Polypodiinae and the order Polypodiales. The plant is a member of the family Pteridaceae and is specifically placed within the genus Adiantum, with its unique identity defined by the species name philippense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Adiantum philippense has delicate, bipinnate to tripinnate fronds characterized by thin, dark, wire-like stipes and small, fan-shaped, translucent leaflets with entire margins. The pinnules are typically bright green and possess a distinctively papery texture that feels light to the touch. These leaflets are arranged along slender, branching rachises that give the fern its characteristic airy and lacy appearance. At the edges of the leaflets, the spores are housed in specialized marginal sori protected by thin, dark-colored indusia. This complex arrangement of fine textures and dark vascular structures defines its unique morphological profile.

What are the pharmacological activities of Adiantum philippense?

This plant has antimicrobial activities that allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. These properties are primarily attributed to the presence of bioactive secondary metabolites found within its leaves. Researchers have studied these compounds to understand how they disrupt bacterial or fungal cell membranes. By leveraging these natural chemical defenses, the plant serves as a significant subject for developing new therapeutic agents. This biological capability makes it a valuable resource in the field of ethnopharmacology.

According to a study published by "Journal, genetic engineering & biotechnology", the medicinal plant Adiantum philippense L. hosts the fungal endophyte Nigrospora sphaerica within its leaf segment. This endophyte produces the antimicrobial metabolite phomalactone, which exhibits a broad spectrum of activity against human and phytopathogenic bacteria and fungi. The antimicrobial activity of phomalactone was found to be highest against Escherichia coli by disc diffusion assay. The MIC was found to be significant against both Escherichia coli and Xanthomonas campestris, as well as the dermatophyte Candida albicans at 150 μg/ml. This work represents the first report regarding the antibacterial activity of phomalactone.

It is generally advised to handle the species with caution to avoid these adverse reactions.
