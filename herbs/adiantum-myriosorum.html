Adiantum myriosorum

What's the taxonomical classification of Adiantum myriosorum?

Adiantum myriosorum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is positioned in the subclass Polypodiinae and falls under the order Polypodiales. This fern is a member of the family Pteridaceae, specifically categorized within the genus Adiantum, with its unique identification as the species myriosorum.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Adiantum myriosorum contains bioactive compounds primarily located in its delicate, dissected fronds which are harvested for traditional medicinal purposes. These fern leaves are often utilized in folk medicine to treat various ailments, including respiratory issues and skin irritations. Some practitioners prepare decoctions or infusions from the foliage to alleviate coughs or congestion. The plant's chemical profile within these parts is believed to contribute to its therapeutic potential. Local communities frequently rely on the whole aerial parts of the plant for their medicinal applications.

      What are the herbal preparations of Adiantum myriosorum?

      Adiantum myriosorum is prepared primarily as a decoction, a concentrated liquid extract made by boiling the plant material in water. This method is frequently used to extract the medicinal properties of the fern fronds for therapeutic purposes. In some traditional practices, the plant may also be processed into a fine powder through drying and grinding. This powder can then be incorporated into other substances or consumed directly. These various forms allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended use.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Adiantum myriosorum can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or respiratory distress if its spores or dust are inhaled. While generally considered safe as an ornamental fern, sensitive individuals may experience redness or itching upon direct contact with the foliage. Some people might also develop a rash if they handle the plant frequently without protection. There is limited clinical data regarding systemic toxicity, but caution is advised for those with known plant allergies. Always wash your hands after handling the leaves to minimize any potential adverse reactions.
