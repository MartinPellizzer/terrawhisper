Botanical Identification of Indian Frankincense (Commiphora wightii)

To identify Indian Frankincense (Commiphora wightii) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Commiphora wightii belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Burserales, family Burseraceae, genus Commiphora, and species Commiphora wightii. This classification places it within the group of flowering plants and highlights its relationship to other resin-producing trees.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Commiphora wightii is the scientific name for Indian Frankincense. It was first described by the botanist William Wight, hence the author citation is Wight. This species has several synonyms, including Commiphora gileadensis and Commiphora mollicoma. It is known by various common names such as Indian frankincense, giloy, and khadira. Regional and historical names vary, reflecting its long use in traditional medicine across South Asia.

Morphological Characteristics

Commiphora wightii is a small to medium-sized shrub or tree with a spreading growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly in the soil. The stem is woody and branched, with a rough, fissured bark. Leaves are alternate, elliptical, and have a leathery texture with a smooth margin. The inflorescence consists of short, clustered spikes. Flowers are small, greenish-yellow, and borne in clusters. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Commiphora wightii can be distinguished by its unique bark with longitudinal fissures, pale yellow resinous exudate, and elliptical leaves with a smooth margin. It differs from similar species like Commiphora myrrha by its lighter coloration and distinct leaf shape. The presence of a strong, aromatic resin scent also aids in identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Commiphora wightii, or Indian Frankincense, is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly India, Nepal, and Bhutan. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates with high humidity. The plant prefers well-drained sandy or loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 500 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Commiphora wightii blooms from March to May, producing small, yellowish-green flowers. Seed formation occurs between June and September. Harvesting of the resin, used for identification purposes, is typically done between November and January when the resin is most abundant and mature.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Commiphora wightii is closely related to species like Commiphora officinalis and Commiphora arabica. It belongs to the Burseraceae family, sharing traits such as resin production and leaf structure with other members of the genus. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary history and medicinal properties.
