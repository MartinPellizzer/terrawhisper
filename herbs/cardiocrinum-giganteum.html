Cardiocrinum giganteum

What's the taxonomical classification of Cardiocrinum giganteum?

Cardiocrinum giganteum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Liliales. It is further categorized under the family Liliaceae, which places it within the genus Cardiocrinum, ultimately identifying it by its specific species name, giganteum.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds abscisic acid, gibberellin, glucose, starch, and trans-zeatin. These specific substances function as vital plant hormones that regulate various physiological processes and growth stages. The presence of glucose and starch provides essential energy sources for the organism's metabolic functions. Additionally, the cytokinins such as trans-zeatin play a crucial role in cell division and development. Together, these chemical components contribute to the complex biological profile of the species.

According to a study published by "Planta", Cardiocrinum giganteum is a perennial herbaceous bulbous plant with unique medicinal value. During the seed variable temperature stratification process, the content of glucose, gibberellin, and trans-zeatin increases, while starch and abscisic acid decrease. Research involving 16 hub genes and 24 transcription factors identified involvement in glucose and trans-zeatin metabolism. Additionally, 14 hub genes and 8 transcription factors were associated with abscisic acid and starch metabolism. These metabolic processes occur within pathways such as the glycolysis pathway, tricarboxylly acid cycle, and pentose phosphate pathway.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. It is also known to induce neurological and muscular symptoms including dizziness and muscle weakness. Most critically, the toxic alkaloids within the plant may lead to dangerous cardiac arrhythmias. These various side effects highlight the significant toxicity associated with consuming any part of the species.
