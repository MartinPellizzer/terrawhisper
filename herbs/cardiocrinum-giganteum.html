Cardiocrinum giganteum

What's the taxonomical classification of Cardiocrinum giganteum?

Cardiocrinum giganteum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Liliales. It is further categorized under the family Liliaceae, which places it within the genus Cardiocrinum, ultimately identifying it by its specific species name, giganteum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cardiocrinum giganteum has large, fleshy, ovate leaves that grow in a basal rosette and a tall, robust scape topped by a large, solitary, bell-shaped white flower. The leaves are thick and succulent, often reaching significant lengths to maximize light absorption in their shaded forest habitats. Its inflorescence consists of a single, massive bloom characterized by long, spreading tepals and a prominent central column. The plant typically emerges from a substantial, bulbous underground storage organ that provides energy for its dramatic flowering cycle. These structural features allow the species to thrive in moist, tropical environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cardiocrinum giganteum contains medicinal alkaloids primarily located within its bulb. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal and nutritional compounds abscisic acid, gibberellin, glucose, starch, and trans-zeatin. The hormones abscisic acid and gibberellin play critical roles in regulating the plant's growth cycles and stress responses. Trans-zeatin acts as a potent cytokinin that promotes cell division and developmental processes. For energy storage and metabolic function, the plant utilizes glucose as a primary sugar and starch as a long-term carbohydrate reserve. These diverse chemical components work together to maintain the biological vitality of the organism.

As discussed in a study, Cardiocrinum giganteum is a perennial herbaceous bulbous plant with unique medicinal value. The plant undergoes a 17-18-month seed morphophysiological dormancy period. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. It is also known to induce neurological and muscular symptoms including dizziness and muscle weakness. Most critically, the toxic alkaloids within the plant may lead to dangerous cardiac arrhythmias. These various side effects highlight the significant toxicity associated with consuming any part of the species.
