Lithocarpus chrysocomus

What's the taxonomical classification of Lithocarpus chrysocomus?

Lithocarpus chrysocomus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its hierarchical descent, it falls into the order Fagales and the family Fagaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Lithocarpus, and its specific species name, chrysocomus.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Lithocarpus chrysocomus contains medicinal compounds primarily derived from its bark and leaves. The tannins and polyphenols extracted from the bark are often utilized for their astringent and antimicrobial properties. Researchers have investigated the leaves for their potential antioxidant activities and bioactive metabolites. These specific plant parts are frequently studied in traditional medicine for treating various inflammatory conditions. Such botanical components offer significant interest for the development of new therapeutic agents.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Lithocarpus chrysocomus targets inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and various pro-inflammatory cytokines including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha). The plant contains diverse polyphenolic compounds and tannins that exert their medicinal effects by inhibiting these specific enzymatic pathways. By modulating the NF-kappaB signaling pathway, its bioactive constituents can effectively reduce the production of oxidative stress markers within cellular environments. Furthermore, its phytochemical profile shows potential for interacting with various protein kinases involved in cell proliferation and survival. These complex molecular interactions underpin the plant's observed antioxidant and anti-inflammatory pharmacological properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Lithocarpus chrysocomus?

Lithocarpus chrysocomus has medicinal properties characterized by its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities derived from its concentrated polyphenolic compounds. The plant contains significant levels of tannins and flavonoids which help neutralize free radicals in the body. These bioactive components work together to reduce oxidative stress and suppress inflammatory responses in various tissues. Additionally, the extracts demonstrate an ability to inhibit the growth of certain pathogenic bacteria and fungi. This chemical profile makes the species a subject of interest for developing natural therapeutic agents.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat gastrointestinal issues such as diarrhea, stomachaches, and dysentery through its decoctions.

The bark and leaves are often boiled to create medicinal teas that soothe digestive distress. These preparations are also applied topically to aid in the healing of skin inflammations or minor wounds. Some traditional practitioners utilize the plant to help manage fever and general body aches.

These various applications rely on the specific chemical properties found within the plant's tissues.

    What are the herbal preparations of Lithocarpus chrysocomus?

    Lithocarpus chrysocomus is prepared as a decoction by boiling the dried leaves, bark, or acorns in water to extract its bioactive compounds. This aqueous extract serves as the primary method for traditional medicinal use in various regional practices. Some practitioners also utilize the plant material to create concentrated tinctures through alcohol-based extraction methods. Additionally, the pulverized acorns can be processed into powders for inclusion in nutritional supplements or topical applications. These diverse preparation forms allow for different delivery methods depending on the desired therapeutic effect.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Lithocarpus chrysocomus can cause adverse reactions such as gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, or allergic responses if ingested or applied topically. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many species within the Fagaceae family contain tannins that may irritate the digestive lining. Consuming large quantities of unprocessed plant material could lead to nausea or stomach cramping. Individuals with known sensitivities to oak or beech trees should exercise caution when handling this plant to avoid contact dermatitis. Always consult a medical professional before attempting to use any wild flora for medicinal purposes.
