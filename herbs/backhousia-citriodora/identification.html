Botanical Identification of Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora)

To identify Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Lemon Myrtle, or Backhousia citriodora, is classified within the Plantae kingdom, Magnoliophyta division, Eudicots class, Sapindales order, Myrtaceae family, and the genus Backhousia. Its scientific name identifies it as a species within this genus.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Lemon Myrtle is Backhousia citriodora, first described by the botanist Robert Brown in 1810. It is also known by several synonyms, including Backhousia attenuata and Backhousia myrtifolia. Common names include Lemon Myrtle, Lemonwood, and Citron Myrtle, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Lemon Myrtle is a small evergreen shrub with a compact growth habit. It has a shallow root system, allowing it to thrive in well-drained soils. The stems are woody and branched, supporting its dense foliage. Leaves are narrow, lance-shaped, and have a fragrant, lemony scent. The inflorescence consists of small, clustered flowers that are white to pale yellow. The fruit is a small, capsule-like structure containing numerous tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Lemon Myrtle can be distinguished by its distinctive lemon-scented leaves, which are narrow and lance-shaped with a smooth margin. It differs from similar species like Backhousia citriodora var. macrophylla by having smaller, more pointed leaves and a more intense citrus aroma. The leaves also tend to be more rigid and less hairy compared to related species.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Lemon Myrtle is native to eastern Australia, particularly Queensland and New South Wales. It thrives in subtropical to temperate climates with mild winters. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at elevations between 300 and 800 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Lemon Myrtle blooms from September to November, producing small white flowers. Seed formation occurs between December and February. For identification purposes, the leaves are harvested when they are fully mature, typically between March and June.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is closely related to species within the genus Backhousia, sharing familial traits with the Myrtaceae family. It exhibits similarities in morphology and chemical composition with other members of this family, particularly in its aromatic leaves and essential oil profile. Its phylogenetic position highlights its evolutionary ties to other aromatic myrtle species.
