Ipomoea cairica

What's the taxonomical classification of Ipomoea cairica?

Ipomoea cairica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Solanales. This plant is a member of the family Convolvulaceae and is further categorized under the genus Ipomoea, specifically identified by the species name cairica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ipomoea cairica has climbing or trailing herbaceous stems that reach lengths of up to 15 meters and bear heart-shaped, bright green leaves with smooth margins. The leaves are typically arranged alternately along the vine and feature long petioles that connect the blades to the stem. Its flowers are funnel-shaped and display a vibrant violet or pale blue color with a yellowish throat. These blossoms emerge from axillary clusters and are supported by slender pedicels. The plant produces small, globose capsules that contain numerous dark seeds upon maturation.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ipomoea cairica?

This plant has antidiabetic, cytotoxic, larvicidal, and α-glucosidase inhibitory activities that demonstrate its broad pharmacological potential. Its antidiabetic properties are largely driven by its ability to function as an α-glucosidase inhibitory agent, which helps manage blood glucose levels after meals. Additionally, the plant exhibits significant cytotoxic effects that make it a subject of interest for potential cancer research. The larvicidal activity of its extracts offers a natural approach to controlling mosquito populations and preventing disease transmission. These diverse biological actions suggest that Ipomoea cairica contains complex bioactive compounds suitable for further medicinal development.

As discussed in a study, six partially acylated pentaasaccharide resin glycosides named cairicosides A-F were isolated from the aerial parts of Ipomoea cairica. These compounds were characterized as a group of macrolactones of simonic acid A. The lactonization site of 11S-hydroxyhexadecanoic acid was bound to the second saccharide moiety at C-3 in cairicosides A-E, whereas it was at C-2 in cairicoside F. Compounds cairicosides A-E exhibited moderate cytotoxicity against a small panel of human tumor cell lines. These specific activities were recorded with IC50 values in the range of 4.28-14.31μM.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds cairicoside A, cairicoside B, cairicoside C, kaempferol 7-O-α-L-rhamnopyranoside, and quercetin 3-O-α-L-arabino-pyranoside. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their diverse biological activities and potential therapeutic benefits. The presence of various glycosides and flavonoids suggests that the plant possesses significant antioxidant properties. Researchers analyze these secondary metabolites to understand how they contribute to the overall medicinal profile of the species. Such detailed chemical compositions are essential for developing new pharmacological treatments.

As discussed in a study, six pentasaccharide resin glycosides were isolated from the aerial parts of Ipomoea cairica. These compounds include four new acylated pentasaccharide resin glycosides named cairicoside I-IV (1-4) and two known compounds, cairicoside A (5) and cairicoside C (6). The core of these 6 compounds was simonic acid A, with the aglycone being (11S)-hydroxyhexadecanoic acid. The lactonization site of the aglycone was bonded to the second saccharide moiety at C-2 in compounds 1-4 and at C-3 in compounds 5-6. Additionally, compounds 1 and 5, as well as 4 and 6, were identified as two pairs of isomers. All 4 of the new compounds (1-4) demonstrated strong inhibitory activity against α-glucosidase.
