Solanum incanum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum incanum?

Solanum incanum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized into the order Solanales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Solanaceae, falling under the genus Solanum, and is specifically identified by its species name, incanum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum incanum has a perennial, woody shrub morphology characterized by spreading branches covered in dense, spreading prickles and stellate hairs. The leaves are typically large, alternate, and deeply lobed with a grayish-green color derived from the fine pubescence on their surfaces. Small, white or pale lavender flowers emerge in cymes, featuring five petals and prominent yellow anthers. The fruit consists of globose berries that transition from green to a bright yellow when fully mature. These berries are often surrounded by persistent calyces and contain numerous small, flat seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to various regions across Africa, the Middle East, and parts of the Indian subcontinent. It thrives in a wide range of habitats, including arid shrublands, dry savannas, and disturbed areas near human settlements. Its distribution spans from the Mediterranean coast southward through much of sub-Saharan Africa. The species has also established itself in parts of South Asia due to its ability to adapt to diverse climates. Because it is often considered a weed, it frequently spreads along roadsides and agricultural lands throughout these territories.

What are the therapeutic actions of Solanum incanum?

Solanum incanum has therapeutic properties that include bitter, purgative, emetic, anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic actions. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the alkaloid solamargine along with a diverse array of flavonoids, glycosides, phenols, and saponins. These chemical constituents work together to provide the plant with various biological activities. The presence of phenols and flavonoids often contributes to significant antioxidant properties within the organism. Additionally, the glycosides and saponins play crucial roles in its complex chemical defense mechanisms. Each of these medicinal compounds offers unique potential for pharmacological research and therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, solamarginine purified from Solanum incanum triggers the gene expression of human TNFR I. This compound possesses potent cytotoxicity to human hepatocyte Hep3B and normal skin fibroblast cells. The gene expression of TNFR I was up-regulated within 30 min of solamarginine treatment. Following a 3-h incubation with solamarginine, the sub-G1 cell stage was drastically increased. These results evidence that solamarginine arises cell death by apoptosis.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe toxicity characterized by nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and intense abdominal pain. Ingestion often leads to neurological disturbances such as dizziness, confusion, and vivid hallucinations. Physical symptoms also include tachycardia and mydriasis, which results in significantly dilated pupils. Additionally, victims frequently experience a persistent sensation of dry mouth.
