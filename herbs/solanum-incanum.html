Solanum incanum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum incanum?

Solanum incanum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized into the order Solanales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Solanaceae, falling under the genus Solanum, and is specifically identified by its species name, incanum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum incanum has a perennial, woody shrub morphology characterized by spreading branches covered in dense, spreading prickles and stellate hairs. The leaves are typically large, alternate, and deeply lobed with a grayish-green color derived from the fine pubescence on their surfaces. Small, white or pale lavender flowers emerge in cymes, featuring five petals and prominent yellow anthers. The fruit consists of globose berries that transition from green to a bright yellow when fully mature. These berries are often surrounded by persistent calyces and contain numerous small, flat seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to various regions across Africa, the Middle East, and parts of the Indian subcontinent. It thrives in a wide range of habitats, including arid shrublands, dry savannas, and disturbed areas near human settlements. Its distribution spans from the Mediterranean coast southward through much of sub-Saharan Africa. The species has also established itself in parts of South Asia due to its ability to adapt to diverse climates. Because it is often considered a weed, it frequently spreads along roadsides and agricultural lands throughout these territories.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Solanum incanum contains medicinal compounds located in its leaves, roots, fruits, and stems. The leaves are frequently crushed or boiled to create poultices and infusions for treating skin infections and inflammation. Extracts from the roots are often utilized in traditional medicine to address digestive issues and respiratory ailments. The berries can be processed for various therapeutic applications, though they must be used with extreme caution due to their toxicity. Various parts of the plant are also decocted to manage pain and fever in several regional folk practices.

What are the pharmacological activities of Solanum incanum?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, antifungal, antimalarial, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities. Various phytochemical constituents within its leaves, roots, and fruits contribute to these diverse biological effects. The presence of alkaloids and steroidal saponins allows the plant to inhibit the growth of numerous pathogenic microorganisms. Research also suggests that specific extracts can target malaria parasites and demonstrate significant potential in suppressing cancer cell proliferation. These multifaceted properties make Solanum incanum a subject of intense interest for drug development and traditional medicine.

As discussed in a study, Solanum incanum was found to be one of the most active plants tested among 17 plants from the Arabian Peninsula. The methanol extracts of Solanum incanum demonstrated very high activities against two or more test organisms at low concentrations ranging from 0.5 to 20 mg/ml. Specifically, the extracts of the leaves of S. incanum were among the most active nematicides. This plant showed significant nematicidal activity at a concentration of 0.5 mg/ml.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compound solamarine along with a diverse profile of alkaloids, flavonoids, glycosides, and saponins. These chemical constituents work together to provide various therapeutic properties used in traditional medicine. The alkaloids and flavonoids are often recognized for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Additionally, the glycosides and saponins contribute to the plant's complex biochemical interaction with biological systems. These various compounds make the plant a significant subject for pharmacological study.

As discussed in a study, Solamargine purified from Solanum incanum triggers the gene expression of human TNFR I which may lead to cell apoptosis. This component possesses a potent cytotoxicity to human hepatocyte (Hep3B) and normal skin fibroblast, where inhibition curves for both cells essentially overlapped. A sub-G1 cell stage was drastically increased after 3-h incubation with solamargine. Furthermore, the gene expression of TNFR I was up-regulated within 30 min of solamargine treatment. The overexpression of TNF receptor I may be related to the mechanism of cytotoxicity of solamargine.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating type-2 diabetes, diabetes, diarrhoea, skin and wound infections, and wounds. Various parts of the plant are prepared into medicinal decoctions or pastes to manage blood sugar levels. Its antimicrobial properties make it effective for addressing various skin and wound infections. Additionally, the plant is frequently applied topically to facilitate the healing of open wounds. Ingestion of specific extracts can also help control symptoms related to diarrhoea.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion, decoction, tincture, poultice, or macerated oil to treat various ailments. An infusion of the leaves is often used to soothe topical inflammation, while a decoction of the roots may be prepared for internal digestive issues. Finally, a macerated oil created by soaking the plant parts in a carrier oil serves as a soothing base for topical salves.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe toxicity characterized by nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and intense abdominal pain. Ingestion often leads to neurological disturbances such as dizziness, confusion, and vivid hallucinations. Physical symptoms also include tachycardia and mydriasis, which results in significantly dilated pupils. Additionally, victims frequently experience a persistent sensation of dry mouth.
