Botanical Identification of Mexican Angelica (Ligusticum porteri)

To identify Mexican Angelica (Ligusticum porteri) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Mexican Angelica, or Ligusticum porteri, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Ligusticum, and species Ligusticum porteri. It is a member of the carrot family, known for its aromatic properties and traditional use in herbal medicine.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Ligusticum porteri is the scientific name for Mexican Angelica. It was first described by the botanist Thomas Nuttall in 1818. The species has several synonyms, including Heracleum porteri and Angelica porteri. It is also known by various common names such as Mexican Angelica and Angelica. Regional and historical names reflect its traditional use in herbal medicine across different cultures.

Morphological Characteristics

Mexican Angelica is a biennial herb with a taproot system. It grows up to 2 meters tall, with smooth, hollow, and aromatic stems. Its leaves are compound, with a broad, heart-shaped base and finely divided upper leaves. The inflorescence is a terminal umbel, with small white to greenish flowers. The fruit is a dry, two-lobed schizocarp, containing one seed each.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Mexican Angelica can be distinguished by its finely divided, fern-like leaves with a strong anise scent. It has small greenish-white flowers in clustered umbels and a hollow, aromatic stem. It differs from similar species like Ligusticum cubense by its more slender leaves and distinct leaf arrangement. The presence of a strong, spicy fragrance is also a key identifying trait.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Mexican Angelica is native to the southern United States and northern Mexico. It thrives in temperate to subtropical climates. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It is commonly found at elevations between 1,000 and 2,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Mexican Angelica blooms from May to July, with seed formation occurring in late summer to early fall. Harvesting for identification purposes is best done during the flowering stage, typically between June and August, when the plant's characteristics are most distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Mexican Angelica, or Ligusticum porteri, is closely related to other members of the Carrot family (Apiaceae). It shares key characteristics with other Ligusticum species, such as similar leaf structures and aromatic properties. Its phylogenetic placement highlights its evolutionary ties to other angelicas and related herbs within the same family.
