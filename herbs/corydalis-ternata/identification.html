Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Corydalis ternata is the scientific name for Spring Corydalis. It was first described by the botanist Thomas Anderson in 1836. The species has several synonyms, including Corydalis biflora and Corydalis fragilis. It is also known by various common names such as spring lousewort and fragrant corydalis.

Morphological Characteristics

Spring Corydalis (Corydalis ternata) is a low-growing herb with a rhizomatous root system. It has slender, branched stems that rise from the ground. Its leaves are alternate, lanceolate, and slightly hairy. The plant produces a terminal raceme of small, pinkish flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing several small, dark seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Spring Corydalis (Corydalis ternata) can be distinguished by its slender, branching stems, narrow lance-shaped leaves, and clusters of small, pinkish-purple flowers. It differs from similar species like Corydalis fragrans by having fewer petals and a more elongated corolla. The leaves are more linear and the flower stems are shorter, making these features key for accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Spring Corydalis (Corydalis ternata) is native to regions in East Asia, including China, Japan, and Korea. It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and mild winters. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and is commonly found at elevations between 500 and 1500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Spring Corydalis (Corydalis ternata) blooms from April to June, with seed formation occurring from July to August. Harvesting for identification purposes is best done in late summer when the plant is fully mature, ensuring accurate identification based on its developmental stages.
