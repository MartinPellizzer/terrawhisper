Codonopsis pilosula

What's the taxonomical classification of Codonopsis pilosula?

Codonopsis pilosula belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This specific plant is a part of the family Campanulaceae, which defines its broader botanical grouping. Within this family, it is categorized under the genus Codonoopsis, with the specific designation of the species pilosula.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Codonopsis pilosula has narrow, linear leaves that are densely covered in fine, soft hairs and small, tubular flowers that transition from greenish-white to a pale yellowish hue. The foliage grows in a basal rosette pattern, creating a compact mound of textured greenery. Each leaf features a slightly serrated edge and a distinctively pubescent surface that gives the plant a velvety appearance. The inflorescence consists of small, delicate blossoms held on slender, upright stems. These flowers often cluster in small groups to provide subtle color against the dense leaves.

What are the pharmacological activities of Codonopsis pilosula?

This plant has potent antioxidant, antimicrobial, anti-tumor, and anti-cancer pharmacological activities derived from its bioactive chemical constituents. Research indicates that its extracts can effectively neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage through antioxidant mechanisms. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates significant antimicrobial properties by inhibiting the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Studies also highlight its anti-cancer and anti-tumor potential, suggesting it may interfere with the proliferation of malignant cells. These diverse therapeutic qualities make Codonoopsis pilosula a subject of interest in modern drug discovery.

As discussed in a study, exopolysaccharides isolated from the endophytic strain 14-DS-1 of Codonoopsis pilosula, known as DSPS, demonstrate significant anti-cancer activities. These DSPS are predominately composed of 6 monosaccharides and function by inducing macrophage activation and polarization through the promotion of TNF-alpha and nitric oxide production. The treatment promotes macrophage infiltration while simultaneously suppressing cancer cell migration. Furthermore, DSPS exposure leads to S-phase arrest and apoptosis in cancer cells. Immunofluorescence staining also revealed that treatment with DSPS results in defects in spindle orientation and positioning.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Codonoopsis pilosula polysaccharide (CPPPS), cyanidin, galangin, lobetyolin, and syringin. These bioactive substances work together to provide various therapeutic benefits to the human body. For instance, the presence of cyanidin and galangin often contributes to antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Lobetyolin and syringin are also recognized for their potential roles in supporting metabolic and neurological health. Furthermore, the specific polysaccharide CPPPS is frequently studied for its unique immunomodulatory properties.

As discussed in a study, Codonoopsis pilosula is a traditional medicinal herb containing flavonoid compounds such as Cyanidin and Galangin. Metabolomic and transcriptomic analyses revealed that leaves contained the highest number of differentially accumulated metabolites related to flavonoids, reaching up to 23. The leaves also harbored the most differentially expressed flavonoid-related genes, with up to 54. Furthermore, the roots treated with NaHCO3 contained up to 14 types of flavonoids. The leaves treated with NaHCO3 contained up to 20 types of differentially expressed flavonoid-related genes.
