Botanical Identification of Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis)

To identify Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Licorice, or Glycyrrhiza officinalis, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Glycyrrhiza, and species Glycyrrhiza officinalis. This classification places it within the legume family, reflecting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Licorice is Glycyrrhiza officinalis, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Glycyrrhiza glabra and Glycyrrhiza uralensis. Common names such as sweet root and licorice root are widely used, while regional and historical names reflect its long-standing use in traditional medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Licorice is a perennial herb with a deep taproot system. It has erect, cylindrical stems that can grow up to 1 meter tall. Its leaves are alternate, pinnately compound, and have serrated margins. The plant produces a racemose inflorescence with small, yellowish-white flowers. The fruit is a legume containing several oblong seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Licorice can be distinguished by its stout, cylindrical root with a light brown, corky exterior and a yellowish interior. The leaves are opposite, pinnate, with 13-23 pairs of oval, slightly hairy leaflets. The flowers are pale yellow, clustered in racemes, and the fruit is a hairy, inflated pod. These features help differentiate it from similar species like wild licorice.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Glycyrrhiza officinalis is native to temperate regions of Europe, western Asia, and parts of northern Africa. It thrives in dry, sunny climates with well-drained soils. It is commonly found at altitudes between 500 and 2000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Licorice blooms in late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. Harvesting for identification purposes is best done in late summer to early fall when the plant is mature and the roots are fully developed.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Glycyrrhiza officinalis belongs to the legume family, Fabaceae, and is closely related to other genera like Astragalus and Hedysarum. It shares key family traits such as pod fruits and compound leaves. Its closest relatives are species within the same genus, differing mainly in habitat and chemical composition.
