Lonicera tangutica

What's the taxonomical classification of Lonicera tangutica?

Lonicera tangutica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, falling under the order Dipsacales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Caprifoliaceae, which places it within the genus Lonicera. Ultimately, the specific identity of this plant is defined by its species designation, tangutica.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Lonicera tangutica targets inflammatory mediators such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha, interleukin-6, and various cyclooxygenase enzymes to exert its therapeutic effects. The plant contains bioactive compounds like chlorogenic acid and various flavonoids that inhibit the activation of nuclear factor-kappa B pathways. By suppressing these signaling cascades, the extracts reduce the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines in various cellular models. These interactions also extend to modulating nitric oxide synthase activity, which helps mitigate oxidative stress and tissue inflammation. Such molecular mechanisms underpin the plant's traditional uses in treating respiratory infections and skin inflammations.

What are the therapeutic actions of Lonicera tangutica?

Lonicera tangutica has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and antioxidant properties that are primarily utilized in traditional medicine to treat respiratory infections and skin ailments. What are the herbal preparations of Lonicera tangutica?

Lonicera tangutica is prepared primarily as a decoction made by boiling the dried stems and leaves in water to extract its medicinal components. Practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of concentrated herbal extracts or tinctures created through alcohol-based maceration. Some traditional applications involve using the plant in powdered form to be mixed into topical pastes for skin ailments. These various preparations allow the active compounds to be delivered through either oral ingestion or external application. The specific form chosen depends heavily on whether the goal is to treat internal inflammation or external irritations.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Lonicera tangutica can cause gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and potential toxicity if ingested in large quantities. The plant contains various chemical compounds that may trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain upon consumption. Direct contact with the stems or leaves might result in localized dermatitis or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Because specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, caution is necessary when handling the species. Always consult a medical professional if accidental ingestion or skin exposure occurs.
