Belamcanda chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Belamcanda chinensis?

Belamcanda chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further classified under the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Asparagales. This plant is a member of the family Iridaceae, falling into the genus Belamcanda, and is specifically identified by its species name, chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Belamcanda chinensis has long, linear, grass-like leaves that grow in basal clumps and reach heights of up to 60 centimeters. The plant produces slender, arching stems that terminate in clusters of trumpet-shaped flowers varying in color from yellow to orange or red. Each blossom features six tepals and prominent stamens that contribute to its striking visual appearance. Following the flowering stage, the plant develops elongated, three-valved seed capsules that split open to reveal small, black, shiny seeds. These seeds are often dispersed by birds or gravity to facilitate new growth.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across China, Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and parts of Southeast Asia. It thrives primarily in temperate and subtropical regions where it can find suitable moisture levels. While its natural range is centered in these Asian territories, it has been widely introduced to other parts of the world for ornamental purposes. In many non-native locations, it is commonly found in gardens and parks due to its hardiness and aesthetic appeal. This widespread cultivation has allowed the species to establish a presence in various global climates.

What are the pharmacological activities of Belamcanda chinensis?

This plant has demonstrated diverse pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, anti-nociceptive, anticancer, and antioxidant properties. Its anti-inflammatory and anti-nociceptive effects work together to reduce pain perception and swelling in biological systems. The antioxidant capabilities of the plant help neutralize free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative damage. Additionally, research indicates that specific compounds within the species possess anticancer potential by inhibiting tumor cell growth. These combined therapeutic attributes make the plant a significant subject for medicinal chemistry studies.

As discussed in a study, the ethyl acetate extract of Belamcanda chinensis (EAEBc) demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory activity in RAW264.7 cells by reducing LPS-induced NO generation and suppressing the production of TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6 in a concentration-dependent manner. In vivo, EAEBc downregulates serum TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, NO, iNOS, and PGE2 contents in mice with LPS-induced inflammation in a dose-dependent manner. This extract also showed anti-inflammatory activity in both carrageenan-induced paw edema and xylene ear edema tests. Intragastric administration of EAEBc at test doses of 100 and 200 mg/kg inhibited nociception and capillary permeability induced by acetic acid. Furthermore, EAEBc exerted analgesic effects in formalin and hot plate tests and reversed changes in NO, PGE2, 5-HT, β-EP, substance P, and NE levels in a mouse model of formalin-induced pain.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Belamcandaoids A-N, Irisflorentin, Tectoridin, Tectorigenin, and various isoflavones. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of the species. The Belamcandaoids represent a unique group of steroidal saponins found within the plant tissues. Additionally, the presence of Irisflorentin and isoflavones supports its traditional use in medicinal applications. Tectoridin and Tectorigenin further enhance the complex biochemical makeup of this botanical specimen.

As discussed in a study, fourteen new triterpenoids known as belamcandaoids A-N (1-14) were isolated from the seeds of Belamcanda chinensis. All of these compounds except for 1 and 2 are identified as 3,4-seco-triterpenoids belonging to the fernane type. Biological evaluation results showed that compound 3 could reduce fibronectin expression in TGF-β1 induced kidney proximal tubular cells. Additionally, compound 13 was found to reduce collagen I expression in these same cells.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach pain, and dizziness if ingested. These adverse reactions typically stem from the presence of toxic compounds within the plant tissues. Most gastrointestinal distress symptoms occur shortly after the consumption of the leaves or seeds. Some individuals may also experience neurological symptoms like dizziness following exposure. It is important to avoid all parts of the plant to prevent these painful side effects.
