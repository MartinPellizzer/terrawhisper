Begonia taiwaniana

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Begonia taiwaniana has succulent, asymmetrical leaves that are broadly ovate to cordate in shape and feature distinct, irregularly serrated margins. The leaf surfaces often display a subtle texture with prominent venation patterns that radiate from the base. Its stems are fleshy and typically exhibit a reddish or brownish pigmentation depending on light exposure. The inflorescences consist of small, delicate flowers that emerge from the leaf axils in a clustered arrangement. These flowers are primarily white or pale pink and serve as a key identifying characteristic of the species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the high-altitude montane forests of Taiwan, specifically occurring in the mountainous regions of the island. It typically inhabits moist, shaded environments found at elevations ranging from 1,000 to over 2,000 meters above sea level. These populations are often localized to specific microclimates within the subtropical cloud forests. Due to this restricted range, the species is geographically isolated from other members of its genus found on the mainland. The unique topography of Taiwan's central mountain ranges plays a critical role in maintaining its specialized habitat.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Begonia taiwaniana can cause skin irritation, mouth inflammation, and stomach distress if it comes into contact with or is ingested by humans or animals. The plant contains oxalic acid, which acts as a chemical irritant to sensitive mucosal membranes. Contact with the leaves or stems may result in redness, itching, or a burning sensation on the skin. If swallowed, the toxins can lead to painful swelling of the throat and digestive upset. Pet owners should be especially cautious as ingestion can cause similar adverse reactions in domestic animals.
