Spatholobus uniauritus

What's the taxonomical classification of Spatholobus uniauritus?

Spatholobus uniauritus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Fabales and is a member of the family Fabaceae. Ultimately, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Spatholobus, with the unique species designation of uniauritus.

Spatholobus uniauritus has potent antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties that are primarily utilized to treat skin infections, swelling, and localized pain. What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including stomach aches, intestinal worms, malaria, and skin infections. Local communities often utilize its decoctions to alleviate digestive distress and clear parasitic infestations from the gut. The plant also possesses medicinal properties that help reduce the symptoms associated with malaria. Additionally, topical applications of its extracts are applied to manage various dermatological issues. These traditional uses highlight its importance in regional herbal medicine.

What are the herbal preparations of Spatholobus uniauritus?

Spatholobus uniauritus is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a macerated extract using various parts of the plant including the bark, leaves, and roots. These liquid preparations are often administered orally to treat a range of medicinal conditions. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a potent decoction for systemic ailments. Additionally, the leaves may be steeped in warm water to produce a milder infusion. Some practitioners also utilize macerations where plant materials are soaked in alcohol or oil to extract specific bioactive compounds.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Spatholobus uniauritus can cause skin irritation, digestive upset, and potential allergic reactions if consumed or handled without caution. The specific chemical compounds within the plant may trigger localized dermatitis when the sap comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. Ingesting even small amounts can lead to nausea, abdominal cramping, or vomiting in susceptible individuals. While clinical studies are limited, the presence of bioactive alkaloids suggests a need for careful management to avoid toxic effects. Users should always exercise extreme caution and consult medical professionals before any exposure.
