Scientific and Botanical Profile

Incised Notopterygium, with botanical name Notopterygium incisium, is a member of the family Apiaceae, known for its aromatic and medicinal properties.

Native to various regions including parts of Asia, the Middle East, and the Mediterranean, this plant thrives in temperate climates and is often found in open woodlands and meadows. Morphologically, it is characterized by its finely cut, fern-like leaves that are often described as "incised," and its small, clustered white or greenish flowers that bloom in summer. The plant is also known by a variety of common names such as Shooting Star, Moorland Caper, Indian Caper, and Chinese Angelica, reflecting its widespread use in traditional medicine and its distinctive appearance.

Additionally, it is referred to as Devil's Backbone, Fairy Wand, and Pineapple Weed, among others, highlighting its cultural and ecological significance across different regions.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Notopterygium incisium has a variety of forms available, including fresh roots, dried roots, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules, allowing for flexible use depending on the intended application.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping the dried roots in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the roots for a longer period to extract more active compounds. Topical applications include using the essential oil diluted in a carrier oil or applying the powder directly to the skin for localized effects. For adults, a typical dosage ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried root per day, divided into two or three doses, while children should only use it under medical supervision due to limited safety data.

It is recommended to use notopterygium incisium for short durations, typically no longer than two weeks, to minimize potential side effects and ensure safe long-term use.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Notopterygium incisium can be used in traditional medicine for its purported therapeutic effects, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, and allergic reactions in some individuals. There are limited studies on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with anticoagulants or medications affecting the liver, as it may enhance their effects. Special populations, including pregnant or breastfeeding women and those with chronic illnesses, should avoid its use due to insufficient evidence on safety and potential risks to the fetus or infant.

To ensure safe use, consult a qualified healthcare provider before taking notopterygium incisium, and follow recommended dosages to minimize adverse effects.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Notopterygium incisium grows in temperate climates, preferring well-drained, loamy soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH.

It thrives in full sun to partial shade and requires consistent moisture, though it is somewhat drought-tolerant once established. Regular watering during the growing season and mulching to retain soil moisture are essential for optimal growth. To ensure healthy development, it is important to weed regularly and provide support for its climbing habit.

The plant should be pruned in early spring to encourage new growth and prevent overcrowding.
