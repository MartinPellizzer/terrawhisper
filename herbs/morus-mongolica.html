Morus mongolica

What's the taxonomical classification of Morus mongolica?

Morus mongolica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, the plant is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It follows the order Rosales and is a member of the family Moraceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Morus and the species mongolica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Morus mongolica has deciduous, alternate leaves that are typically ovate or elliptic with serrated margins and can range from entire to irregularly lobed. The leaf texture is often somewhat leathery, and the venation is prominent on the undersides. Its stems are woody and branched, eventually forming a medium-sized tree or large shrub. The inflorescence consists of small, inconspicuous flowers that develop into aggregate fleshy fruits known as multiple fruits. These fruits are oblong to ellipsoid and change color as they ripen from green to red or dark purple.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating microbial infections, inflammatory diseases, respiratory ailments, and various skin disorders. The bioactive compounds found in its leaves and bark possess strong antibacterial and antifungal properties that combat pathogens. It is frequently utilized in traditional medicine to soothe coughs and alleviate symptoms of asthma or bronchitis. Additionally, its antioxidant capacity helps reduce systemic inflammation and promotes wound healing. These therapeutic applications make it a versatile resource in herbal pharmacology.

According to a study published by "Phytomedicine : international journal of phytotherapy and phytopharmacology", prenylated flavonoids from Morus mongolica Schneider can be used to treat microbial infection. These compounds show potential as antimicrobial agents and anti-inflammatory agents. The study evaluated the antimicrobial activity of 18 prenylated flavonoids against 4 bacterial and 2 fungal microorganisms. Specific flavonoids such as kuwanon C, mulberrofuran G, albanol B, and kenusanone A demonstrated strong antibacterial activity with 5-30 microg/ml of MICs. Additionally, certain substances like morusin, sanggenon B and D, kaziol B, kurarinone, kenusanone C, and isosophoranone were effective against gram positive bacteria.
