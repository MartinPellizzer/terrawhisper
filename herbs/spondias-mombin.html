Spondias mombin

What's the taxonomical classification of Spondias mombin?

Spondias mombin belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Sapindales and the family Anacardiaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Spondias, and its specific species, mombin.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Spondias mombin has an alternate, pinnately compound leaf structure with 5 to 15 elliptical leaflets that possess entire margins and acute tips. The tree reaches heights of up to 30 meters and features a sturdy, greyish-brown trunk with a spreading canopy. Small, fragrant, yellowish-white flowers emerge in large clusters from the terminal ends of the branches. These blossoms eventually develop into smooth, oval drupes that transition from green to a bright yellow or orange color when ripe. The plant is characterized by its ability to produce a dense, woody architecture that supports heavy fruit loads.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating type 2 diabetes mellitus, helminthosis, malaria, dysentery, and diarrhoea. Various parts of the plant, such as the leaves and bark, contain bioactive compounds that target these specific ailments. For instance, its medicinal properties are frequently utilized to manage blood sugar levels in diabetic patients. The plant also serves as a traditional remedy for gastrointestinal issues like dysentery and diarrhoea. Additionally, it is employed in folk medicine to combat parasitic infections like helminthosis and febrile illnesses such as malaria.

As discussed in a study, aqueous extracts from the Brazilian medicinal plant Spondias mombin L. were evaluated for intestinal disaccharidase and α-glucosidase inhibition. The chemical profile of the plant was examined using UPLC-HRMS to search for hypoglycemic compounds. While other species in the study showed stronger activities, Spondias mombin was one of five plants evaluated for its potential in treating Type 2 diabetes mellitus. These findings contribute to the search for bioactive compounds for developing plant-based antidiabetic agents.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic skin reactions, gastrointestinal distress, and potential complications for individuals with specific metabolic conditions if consumed excessively. The latex found within the fruit and bark may trigger contact dermatitis or localized itching upon contact with sensitive skin. Consuming large quantities of the fruit can lead to stomach upset, diarrhea, or nausea due to its high fiber and sugar content. Furthermore, because the plant is high in natural sugars, individuals managing diabetes should monitor their glucose levels closely. There is also limited clinical data regarding the long-term systemic effects of its medicinal components on human organ function.
