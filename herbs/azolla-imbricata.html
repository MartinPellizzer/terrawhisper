Azolla imbricata

What's the taxonomical classification of Azolla imbricata?

Azolla imbricata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Polypodiinae. Moving through its specific hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Salviniales and the family Salviniaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is completed by its placement in the genus Azolla, specifically identifying it as the species imbricaata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Azolla imbricaata has small, scale-like leaves that overlap tightly like shingles on a roof and a branched, creeping stem structure.

These tiny leaves are arranged in two rows along the stem to facilitate efficient light absorption. Each leaf consists of a dorsal lobe and a ventral lobe that form a compact, flattened shape. The plant also features specialized cavities within its leaf structure that house symbiotic cyanobacteria.

This unique morphology allows the floating fern to thrive in aquatic environments while managing buoyancy and nutrient uptake.

      What are the herbal preparations of Azolla imbricata?

      Azolla imbricata is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a paste for topical applications. The dried plant material is frequently ground into a fine powder to be mixed with water or honey for medicinal consumption. When used externally, the fresh aquatic fern is crushed into a poultice to treat skin irritations or wounds.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Azolla imbricata can cause skin irritation or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals through direct contact with its surface. While the plant is often used in aquaculture and as a fertilizer, certain people may experience localized itching or redness. There is also a potential risk of respiratory irritation if large quantities of the dried plant are inhaled in a dusty environment. Because scientific studies on its human toxicity are limited, cautious handling is recommended for those with known sensitivities. Monitoring for these symptoms is essential when integrating the plant into agricultural or decorative practices.
