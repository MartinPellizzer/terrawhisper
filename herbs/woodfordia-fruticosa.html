Woodfordia fruticosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Woodfordia fruticosa?

Woodfordia fruticosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Myrtales. This plant is a member of the family Lythraceae, falling under the genus Woodfordia, and is specifically identified by the species name fruticosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Woodfordia fruticosa has an erect, woody shrub habit characterized by slender, reddish-brown branches and oppositely arranged, ovate to lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage typically features a rough, scabrous texture on the upper surface and a paler underside. Small, tubular flowers emerge in dense, terminal clusters, displaying a bright pink to rose-colored hue. These blossoms are accompanied by persistent, cup-shaped calyces that remain on the plant after the petals fall. The plant produces small, globose capsules that split open to release numerous tiny seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of Indonesia. It thrives primarily in seasonally dry deciduous forests and scrublands where sunlight is abundant. Its distribution is often linked to open landscapes and hilly terrains characterized by a distinct dry season. You can frequently find it growing in disturbed areas or along forest edges throughout these warm climates. What are the pharmacological activities of Woodfordia fruticosa?

This plant has potent analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antimicrobial, and antioxidant pharmacological activities. The presence of specific phytochemicals allows it to effectively reduce pain and swelling in biological systems. Its strong antimicrobial and antibacterial properties make it useful for inhibiting the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, the plant acts as an antioxidant by neutralizing free radicals to prevent cellular damage. These diverse medicinal properties support its extensive use in traditional healing practices.

As discussed in a study, crude alkaloids extracted from Woodfordia fruticosa were screened for acute toxicity and found to be safe up to a dose of 1250 mg/kg body weight in mice. These alkaloids demonstrated prominent analgesic potentials through the inhibition of both peripheral and central nervous system mechanisms. In testing, the alkaloids significantly reduced abdominal constrictions and increased the time for paw licking responses in both phases. The study also observed a significant raise in latency time in nociception models with a value of P ≤ 0.05. Additionally, the antinociceptive response was significantly attenuated by pretreatment with naloxone, suggesting the involvement of opioid receptors.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds ellagic acid, gallic acid, woodfordin C, ceonothein A, and ceonothein B. These bioactive substances are primarily classified as polyphenols which provide significant antioxidant properties to the organism. Ellagic acid and gallic acid are well-known for their potential to combat oxidative stress and inflammation within human cells. The presence of woodfordin C further enhances the chemical complexity of the plant's therapeutic profile. Additionally, the specific flavonoids ceonothein A and ceonothein B contribute to the unique medicinal efficacy found in its extracts.

As discussed in a study, the Woodfordia fruticosa extract identified as SBE-80 contains the constituent ellagic acid. This specific compound inhibits BACE-1 with an IC50 value of 16 µM. Within a library of 105 extracts, the SBE-80 extract displayed significant inhibition of AChE, BChE, and BACE-1. When administered at a dose of 25 mg/kg QD x 9 (PO), the SBE-80 extract displayed memory-enhancing activity in Swiss albino mice. The study also notes that treatment with SBE-80 inhibits AChE in-vivo while showing a non-significant decrease in serum TBARS.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, gastric irritation, and dizziness in some individuals. These adverse reactions often occur when the plant is ingested in improper doses or without appropriate preparation. Gastric irritation may manifest as stomach pain or discomfort shortly after consumption. Some people may also experience lightheadedness or dizziness as a systemic response. Maintaining caution is essential because these digestive and neurological symptoms can vary in severity.
