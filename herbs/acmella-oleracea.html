Acmella oleracea

What's the taxonomical classification of Acmella oleracea?

Acmella oleracea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Asterales. This plant is a part of the widely recognized family Asteraceae, which is further categorized into the genus Acmella, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species oleracea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acmella oleacea has a decumbent to erect herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of up to 50 centimeters and features oppositely arranged, ovate to elliptical leaves with entire or slightly serrated margins. The foliage is characterized by a soft, pubescent texture that covers both the upper and lower surfaces of the blades. Its inflorescence consists of small, yellowish flower heads arranged in dense, terminal clusters or capitula. These flower heads are supported by green involucral bracts and contain numerous tiny tubular florets. The plant's overall structure is compact, often forming low-growing mats in its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical and tropical regions of East and Southeast Asia, specifically found in parts of China, Taiwan, and Vietnam. It thrives primarily in mountainous areas and forest edges where humidity remains relatively high. The species is often localized to specific altitudinal zones within these temperate and tropical landscapes. Its distribution patterns are closely linked to the availability of shaded, moist environments common in these regions. Consequently, its range is restricted by the particular climatic requirements of its preferred habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Acmella oleracea?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, antifungal, and anti-parasitic pharmacological activities. These diverse biological properties are attributed to the presence of various bioactive secondary metabolites found within its tissues. Specifically, its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize harmful free radicals to prevent cellular damage. The plant also demonstrates significant efficacy in inhibiting the growth of various bacteria and fungi. Furthermore, its anti-parasitic actions provide a natural defense against different types of parasitic organisms.

As discussed in a study, Acmella oleracea leaves extract (AOM) exhibits potent anti-inflammatory activity by reducing the production of IL-6, TNF-α, NO, and LDs in macrophages by 96.66 ± 1.95%, 99.21 ± 3.82%, 100%, and 67.51 ± 0.72%, respectively. The antioxidant capacity of AOM is demonstrated by IC50 values of 44.50 ± 4.46 and 127.60 ± 14.42 μg/mL in DPPH and NO radical inhibition assays, alongside a 63.69 ± 3.47% inhibition of lipid peroxidation and a 69.03 ± 3.85% decrease in ROS production. Regarding antimicrobial properties, MIC values for AOM range from 1000 to 125 μg/mL, with growth inhibition of 77.17 ± 1.50% for S. aureus and 62.36 ± 1.01% for P. aeruginosa. Biofilm adhesion was significantly reduced by 44.71 ± 4.44% for S. aureus, 95.50 ± 6.37% for P. aeruginosa, and 51.83 ± 1.50% for mixed biofilms. Furthermore, AOM enhances the in vitro migration of L929 fibroblasts by 97.86 ± 0.82%, supporting its potential for wound healing.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains 14 alkamides, spilanthol, lupeol (fagarasterol), quercetin, and (E)-caryophyllene. These specific medicinal compounds work together to provide various therapeutic benefits to the body. The presence of spilanthol is often associated with numbing and analgesic properties. Additionally, the antioxidant effects of quercetin and the anti-inflammatory potential of (E)-caryophyllene contribute to its medicinal profile. These chemical constituents make the plant a subject of interest for pharmacological research.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract of Acmella oleracea inflorescences, known as AoE, contains 14 annotated alkamides. HPLC-DAD analysis of AoE revealed a peak with a substantial relative area of approximately 85% which was isolated and identified as spilanthol. This plant is utilized for its local anesthetic, insecticidal, antiparasitic, and anthelmintic attributes. In vitro testing showed that AoE caused mortality of adult schistosomes with an EC50 value of 32.6 μg/mL. When administered orally at 400 mg/kg, AoE resulted in a significant reduction in worm burden of 28%.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause local irritation, allergic reactions, nausea, dizziness, and stomach upset in individuals who consume it or touch it. Direct skin contact with the leaves may trigger redness or itching as a sign of local irritation. Some people may experience a systemic allergic reaction characterized by hives or respiratory distress. If ingested, the plant is known to lead to stomach upset and persistent nausea. Additionally, some users have reported feeling sudden dizziness following exposure.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Allergic reaction
  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Stomach upset
