Scientific and Botanical Profile

Cape Gooseberry, with botanical name Acmella oleracea, is a flowering plant native to South America, Central America, Mexico, the Caribbean, Brazil, Venezuela, and Colombia.

It belongs to the Acanthaceae family and is commonly known by a variety of names including Spiny Sowthistle, Acmella Lettuce, Shrimp Plant, Nasturtium, Golden Berries, Garden Shrimp Plant, Spurge, Cornflower, Golden Gooseberry, Lawn Buttercup, Water Cress, Rattlebox, Acuba, Mexican Prickly Lettuce, False Shrimp Plant, Crown Of Thorns, Bachelor's Buttons, Spurflower, Spatterdock, Mexican Rattlebox, Crown Of Thorns Plant, Common Cornflower, Papaya, Spiny Rattlebox, and Wild Lettuce. Morphologically, it is an annual herb with slender, hairy stems and alternate leaves that are ovate to lanceolate in shape, often with spiny margins.

The plant produces small, yellowish flowers that are clustered in umbels, followed by distinctive, spiny, green to yellowish fruits that resemble tiny berries.

History and Cultural Relevance

Cape Gooseberry was used by indigenous communities in South America for centuries as both a food source and a medicinal plant.

Native to the Andean region, it has been an integral part of the traditional medicine systems of the Quechua and Aymara peoples, who utilized its leaves and fruits to treat ailments such as inflammation, digestive issues, and skin conditions. In some Andean cultures, the plant holds symbolic significance, often featured in rituals and ceremonies related to fertility, protection, and spiritual healing. Its bright orange berries are also used in folk traditions to ward off evil spirits and promote good luck, particularly during harvest festivals and religious observances.

Today, traditional remedies derived from Cape Gooseberry, such as its anti-inflammatory properties, continue to be recognized in alternative medicine and are being studied for their potential in modern pharmacology.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Acmella oleracea grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without waterlogging.

It thrives in warm climates and should be planted in early spring after the last frost, with spacing of about 30 cm between plants to allow for adequate air circulation. Regular weeding and mulching help control weeds and retain soil moisture, while periodic fertilization with a balanced organic fertilizer supports healthy growth and flowering. Harvesting should occur when the plant has reached maturity, typically 60 to 90 days after planting, by cutting the entire plant at the base to encourage regrowth.
