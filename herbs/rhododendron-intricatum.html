Rhododendron intricatum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rhododendron intricatum has a low-growing, spreading growth habit characterized by densely branched, woody stems that form thick mats or shrubs up to 0.5 meters in height. The leaves are small, evergreen, and elliptical in shape, typically measuring between 1 and 3 centimeters in length. These leaves possess a leathery texture and are arranged alternately along the intricate, twiggy branches. During the flowering season, the plant produces small, bell-shaped blossoms that are usually pale pink to white. The overall structure of the plant is defined by its tangled, interlacing branch pattern which gives it its specific name.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Rhododendron intricatum can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential death if the leaves or flowers are ingested due to high concentrations of grayanotoxins. These toxins disrupt the sodium channels in cell membranes, which can lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. In more serious cases, a person may experience a significant drop in blood pressure, a slow or irregular heartbeat, and dizziness. Some individuals might also suffer from muscle weakness or temporary paralysis following consumption. Because of these dangerous physiological effects, this plant should never be consumed by humans or livestock.
