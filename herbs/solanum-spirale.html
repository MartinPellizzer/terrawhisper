Solanum spirale

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum spirale has a woody, perennial growth habit characterized by stems that exhibit a distinct spiral or twisting pattern. The leaves are typically alternate and can vary in shape from ovate to lanceolate with entire or slightly wavy margins. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the axils, often displaying a pale coloration typical of the Solanaceae family. The plant produces small berries that develop from the fertilized ovaries of the flowers. These morphological traits collectively distinguish the species from other members of the Solanum genus.

      Solanum spirale is prepared as decoctions, infusions, tinctures, or topical poultices depending on the intended therapeutic application.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Solanum spirale can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential respiratory failure due to the presence of toxic glycoalkaloids. Ingestion of the plant often leads to immediate symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and intense abdominal cramping. As toxins enter the bloodstream, individuals may experience dizziness, confusion, or even hallucinations. In extreme cases, the chemical compounds can interfere with nerve signaling, leading to paralysis or cardiac irregularities. Prompt medical intervention is necessary if any part of this plant is consumed accidentally.
