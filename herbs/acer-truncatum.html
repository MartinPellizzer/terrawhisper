Acer truncatum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer truncatum has opposite, pinnately compound leaves composed of 7 to 11 leaflets that are typically elliptic to obovate in shape with serrated margins. The leaflets feature acute to acuminate apices and are often characterized by a slightly asymmetrical base. The plant exhibits a deciduous habit with bark that becomes fissured and grayish as the tree matures. During the flowering season, small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves. The fruit consists of paired, winged samaras that are relatively small and turn reddish or brown upon ripening.

This plant is native to central and western China, where it thrives in diverse terrains ranging from mountain slopes to river valleys. Its natural range encompasses several provinces including Sichuan, Gansu, and Shaanxi. Within these regions, the species is typically found in temperate deciduous forests and along hilly landscapes. It often inhabits specific ecological niches characterized by well-drained soils and varying elevations. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Acer truncatum targets enzymes and signaling proteins involved in inflammatory, oxidative, and metabolic pathways, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines. The bioactive polyphenols and fatty acids within the plant act as ligands that modulate the activity of nuclear factor-kappa B to reduce systemic inflammation. Additionally, these compounds interact with peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors to regulate lipid metabolism and improve insulin sensitivity. Antioxidant properties are achieved through the direct scavenging of free radicals and the upregulation of endogenous antioxidant enzymes like superoxide dismutase. These diverse molecular interactions collectively contribute to the plant's potential therapeutic effects in managing cardiovascular disease and metabolic syndrome.
