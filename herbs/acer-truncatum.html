Acer truncatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Acer truncatum?

Acer truncatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Sapindales and belongs to the family Sapindaceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Acer, specifically identified as the species truncatum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer truncatum has opposite, pinnately compound leaves composed of 7 to 11 leaflets that are typically elliptic to obovate in shape with serrated margins. The leaflets feature acute to acuminate apices and are often characterized by a slightly asymmetrical base. The plant exhibits a deciduous habit with bark that becomes fissured and grayish as the tree matures. During the flowering season, small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves. The fruit consists of paired, winged samaras that are relatively small and turn reddish or brown upon ripening.

This plant is native to central and western China, where it thrives in diverse terrains ranging from mountain slopes to river valleys. Its natural range encompasses several provinces including Sichuan, Gansu, and Shaanxi. Within these regions, the species is typically found in temperate deciduous forests and along hilly landscapes. What are the pharmacological activities of Acer truncatum?

This plant has potent pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, antitumor, and cytotoxic properties. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. The antibacterial effects allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant cytotoxic potential that contributes to its observed antitumor activities. These diverse medicinal qualities make Acer truncatum a subject of intense pharmaceutical interest.

As discussed in a study, Acer truncatum is a medicinal herb that contains high concentrations of penta-O-galloyl-β-D-glucose (PGG). This specific compound demonstrates anti-inflammatory activity. In concentrations of 5 μM-20 μM, PGG demonstrated statistically significant inhibition of ROS generation, IL-8 secretion and β2 integrin expression in stimulated neutrophils. The inhibition of L-selectin expression by PGG resulted in prevention in neutrophils' endothelial attachment.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds catechin, chlorogenic acid, procyanidin B2, procyanidin B3, and quercetin-3-O-alpha-L-rhamnoside. These specific polyphenols contribute to the plant's diverse biological activities and antioxidant properties. Catechin and chlorogenic acid are well-known for their ability to mitigate oxidative stress within cells. Furthermore, the procyanidins B2 and B3 are potent tannins that support cardiovascular health. Finally, the presence of quercetin-3-O-alpha-L-rhamnoside adds to the complex chemical profile used in therapeutic studies.

As discussed in a study, Acer truncatum is an important medicinal plant resource in China. A total of 98 compounds were characterized or tentatively identified, including 63 compounds reported from this species for the first time. Through multiple orthogonal partial least-squares discriminant analyses, 52 compounds were identified as potential marker compounds differentiating the various plant parts. The variable influence on projection score revealed that catechin, procyanidins B2 or B3, and procyanidins C1 or C2 are significant metabolites in ABa extracts. These specific metabolites likely contribute to the antioxidant and cytotoxic activities of the plant.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cardiovascular diseases, cerebrovascular diseases, hyperlipidemia, inflammation, and skin trauma. Its bioactive compounds help lower lipid levels in the blood to manage hyperlipidemia effectively. These properties also provide protective effects against damage related to cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases. Additionally, the plant contains potent agents that reduce systemic inflammation throughout the body. Topical applications of its extracts can also assist in the healing process of skin trauma.

As discussed in a study, Acer truncatum Bunge is a multifunctional plant in northern China that has been traditionally used by Mongolian, Tibetan, and Korean linguistic groups to prevent cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases. Different parts of the plant, including leaves, fruits, and bark, are used as herbal medicine to treat conditions such as hyperpiesia and hyperlipidemia. Phytochemical studies have isolated or identified a total of 288 compounds in Acer truncatum, which include polyphenols, organic acids, lipids, and biological volatile organic compounds. This plant contains various active ingredients, especially nervonic acid, which is an important component in delaying brain aging. The ethnobotanical, phytochemical, and pharmacological information related to the plant was collated from data spanning from 1949 to March 2021.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions, gastrointestinal discomfort, or skin irritation in sensitive individuals. While many studies focus on its nutritional benefits, consuming large quantities may lead to stomach upset or nausea. Some people might experience dermatological issues such as redness or itching if direct contact occurs with the sap or leaves. There is currently limited clinical research regarding long-term toxicity in humans, so caution is advised when using it as a supplement.
