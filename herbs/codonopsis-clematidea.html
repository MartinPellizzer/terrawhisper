Codonopsis clematidea

What's the taxonomical classification of Codonopsis clematidea?

Codonopsis clematidea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further categorized under the order Asterales. It is a member of the family Campanulaceae, which contains the genus Codonoopsis, and is specifically identified by the species name clematidea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Codonopsis clematidea has large, heart-shaped leaves that are deeply lobed and possess a dark green, almost blackish coloration with prominent veins. These foliage elements emerge from a creeping rhizome and can grow quite substantial in size. The plant produces delicate, star-shaped flowers that typically feature white or pale petals with darker central markings. Each bloom is supported by a slender stem that rises above the dense leaf canopy. This combination of dark, dramatic foliage and light floral accents creates a striking visual contrast.

What are the pharmacological activities of Codonopsis clematidea?

This plant has demonstrated significant antioxidant, anti-platelet activation, and anti-thrombotic pharmacological activities through its unique phytochemical profile. Research indicates that the bioactive compounds within Codonopsis clematiidea effectively neutralize free radicals to prevent oxidative stress. These chemical constituents also work to inhibit the aggregation of platelets in the bloodstream. By preventing such aggregation, the plant exhibits a potent anti-thrombotic effect that helps mitigate clot formation. These combined properties suggest that the species holds considerable potential for developing cardiovascular therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic root extract of Codonopsis clematidea prevents a hypoxia induced procogaulant state by inhibiting Lyn kinase activation. This extract showed improved antioxidant status and improvements in bleeding time and in vitro coagulation assays including pT, aPTT, and INR. In animals supplemented with the root extract, decreased concentrations of D-Dimers, the platelet activation marker CD41, and serum concentrations of Thromboxane A2 were observed. The extract was effective in preventing hypoxia induced platelet activation by inhibiting Lyn kinase despite an increase in the concentration of the activating ligand vWF. Studies involved adult male Sprague-Dawley rats weighing 230-250 g exposed to hypoxia simulating an altitude of 7600 m (282 mmHg) for 3, 7, 14, and 21 days.
