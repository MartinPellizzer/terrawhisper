Begonia wilsonii

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Begonia wilsonii has fleshy, succulent stems that support asymmetric, ovate leaves with serrated margins and a distinctive reddish or purplish underside. The leaf surfaces often exhibit a textured, slightly rugose appearance with prominent venation patterns. Small, delicate flowers emerge from the leaf axils, typically appearing in clusters of white or pale pink petals. The plant's rhizomatous growth habit allows it to spread across moist, shaded forest floors. These morphological features collectively allow the species to thrive in humid, low-light environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and western China, specifically within the provinces of Hubei, Sichuan, and Guizhou. It typically thrives in the shaded, humid environments found along rocky cliffs and forest floors at moderate elevations. The species is often restricted to specific microclimates where moisture levels remain consistent throughout the year. Because its habitat is tied to these unique geological formations, its range is fragmented rather than continuous. This localized distribution makes the plant a specialized component of its specific montane ecosystems.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Begonia wilsonii can cause skin irritation, eye inflammation, and gastrointestinal distress if its tissues are ingested or touched. The plant contains oxalic acid, which is a common irritant found in many begonia species. When the sap comes into contact with sensitive skin, it may result in redness, itching, or a burning sensation. If the leaves are eaten, individuals might experience nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. It is important to wear gloves when handling the plant to prevent these adverse physical reactions.
