Botanical Identification of Carob (Ceratonia siliqua)

To identify Carob (Ceratonia siliqua) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Carob, or Ceratonia siliqua, is classified within the plant kingdom, specifically in the division of flowering plants. It belongs to the class Fabales, order Fabales, and family Fabaceae, commonly known as the legume family. The genus Ceratonia contains a few species, with C. siliqua being the most notable for its edible pods and medicinal uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Carob is Ceratonia siliqua, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Ceratonia siliquastrum and Ceratonia siliquastrum var. siliqua. Common names include carob, locust bean, and Indian almond. Regional and historical names vary, reflecting its widespread use and cultural significance across different regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Carob is a small to medium-sized tree with a spreading crown. Its root system is deep and fibrous, allowing it to thrive in dry environments. The stem is straight, branching, and has a smooth, greyish bark. Leaves are alternate, simple, and trifoliate, with a leathery texture. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small, yellowish-green flowers. The fruit is a legume pod, containing sweet, sticky seeds used in herbal medicine.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Carob can be distinguished by its smooth, dark brown pods that split open to reveal glossy, edible seeds. It has compound leaves with small, oval leaflets and produces yellowish flowers. It is often confused with locust trees but lacks the thorns and has a distinct, sweet aroma.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Carob trees are native to the Mediterranean region, including southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. They thrive in temperate to subtropical climates with mild winters and hot, dry summers. They prefer well-drained soils, often found in rocky or limestone areas. They typically grow between sea level and 1,500 meters in elevation.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Carob trees bloom in late winter to early spring, typically between January and April, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in the summer months, usually from June to August. Harvesting takes place in the fall, around September to November, when the pods are fully mature and ready for collection.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Carob belongs to the genus Ceratonia within the legume family Fabaceae. Its closest relatives include species like the Persian lilac (Chionanthus retusus) and the genus Pithecolobium. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, reflecting their close phylogenetic relationships within the Fabaceae family.
