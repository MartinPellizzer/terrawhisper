Phyllanthus glaucus

What's the taxonomical classification of Phyllanthus glaucus?

Phyllanthus glaucus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is classified under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls within the order Malpighiales. The plant is a member of the family Phyllanthaceae, specifically situated within the genus Phyllanthus, and is identified by its specific species name, glaucus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Phyllanthus glaucus has a woody, shrubby growth habit characterized by small, elliptical leaves that exhibit a distinct glaucous, blue-gray waxy coating on both surfaces. The leaves are arranged in a distichous pattern along the branches, giving the stems a feathery or pinnate appearance. Its stems are typically slender and can become somewhat woody at the base as the plant matures. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in the leaf axils, often appearing as tiny yellowish or greenish structures. The plant typically develops small, globose fruits that follow the flowering stage.

What are the pharmacological activities of Phyllanthus glaucus?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antiproliferative, and cytotoxic properties. The antioxidant potential of Phyllanthus glaucus helps neutralize harmful free radicals that cause cellular damage. Its anti-inflammatory actions suggest a capacity to reduce swelling and biochemical markers of inflammation in biological systems. Furthermore, the plant exhibits antiproliferative effects that can inhibit the uncontrolled growth of certain cells. These antiproliferative qualities are closely linked to its observed cytotoxic activities against various cancer cell lines.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", three new cleistanthane diterpenoids known as phyllanglins A-C (1-3) were isolated from the roots of Phyllanthus glaucus. This plant also yielded a new natural product called 4-acetyl-bergenin (4) and three known compounds (5-7). These isolated compounds, which include the unusual phyllanglins A-C featuring phenylacetylene moieties, were all evaluated for their anti-inflammatory activities.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cervical cancer, inflammation, and various other inflammatory conditions. Researchers have investigated its potential bioactive compounds for their ability to combat malignant cells in reproductive tissues. The plant also contains phytochemicals that help reduce swelling and pain throughout the body. By modulating immune responses, it serves as a traditional remedy for managing systemic inflammatory conditions. This diverse range of medicinal properties makes it a significant subject in ethnobotanical studies.

According to a study published by "PloS one", Phyllanthus glaucus contains securinine and allosecurinine which were evaluated for their antiproliferative potency against human cervical cancer HeLa cells. The securinine and the alkaloid fraction obtained from the plant showed significant potency in inhibiting HeLa cell growth with IC50 values of 7.02 ± 0.52 μg/ml (32.3 μM) and 25.46 ± 1.79 μg/ml, respectively. The study found that securinine induces apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner and causes cell cycle arrest in the S phase. This process is associated with increased ROS positive cells, depolarization of cells, and the stimulation of ERK1/2, caspase-9, and caspase-3/7 activities. The research indicates that these effects involve the mitochondrial pathway in programmed cell death.
