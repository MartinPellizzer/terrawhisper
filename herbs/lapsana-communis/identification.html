Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Lapsana communis is the scientific name for the common plant known as common hawk's-beard. It was first described by the botanist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck in 1785. The species has several synonyms, including Crepis communis and Crepis lapponica, reflecting historical taxonomic revisions. It is also known by various common names such as hawk's-beard and common sow thistle.

Morphological Characteristics

Lapsana communis is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy. Leaves are alternate, heart-shaped with toothed margins. The plant produces a branched inflorescence of small yellow flowers. The fruits are small achenes enclosed in a pappus, aiding in wind dispersal.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Lapsana communis can be distinguished by its serrated, lance-shaped leaves and yellow flowers with five petals. It lacks the hairy stems of similar species and has a distinct, branched root system. Its leaves are usually larger and more pointed compared to related plants like Erigeron. The plant also produces small, oval seeds in a burr-like structure.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Lapsana communis is native to Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and is commonly found in meadows and along roadsides. It typically grows at low to mid-altitudes, up to around 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Lapsana communis blooms from June to August, producing yellow flowers in clusters. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during its flowering period for accurate botanical recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Lapsana communis is closely related to species within the Asteraceae family, particularly those in the genus Lapsana. It shares key characteristics with other members of its genus, such as leaf morphology and floral structure. These relationships help classify it within the broader context of its family and highlight its evolutionary ties to other flowering plants.
