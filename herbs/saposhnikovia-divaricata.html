Saposhnikovia divaricata

What's the taxonomical classification of Saposhnikovia divaricata?

Saposhnikovia divaricata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized into the order Apiales. It is a member of the family Apiaceae, which places it within the genus Saposhnikovia, ultimately defining its specific identity as the species divaricata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Saposhnikovia divaricata has an erect, herbaceous perennial habit characterized by a woody taproot and stems that can reach heights of up to 1.5 meters. The leaves are pinnately compound, displaying serrated margins and a distinctively bright green color. During the flowering season, the plant produces numerous small, yellowish-white flowers clustered in large, terminal umbels. These blossoms are supported by long peduncles that emerge from the upper nodes of the stem. The plant's overall structure is robust, often forming dense clumps in its natural habitat.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds decursin, decursinol angelate, cimifugin, imperatoriin, and isoimperatorin. These bioactive molecules are primary constituents found within the root of the species. Decursin and decursinol angelate are often studied for their neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of cimifugin and various coumarins like imperatorin contributes to the plant's complex chemical profile. Together, these specific substances drive many of the traditional therapeutic applications associated with the plant.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Saposhnikovia divaricata contain four coumarins, specifically anomalin (1), 5-methoxy-7-(3,3-dimethylallyloxy)coumarin (2), decursin (3), and decursinol angelate (4), along with four chromones identified as cimifugin (5), prim-O-glucosylcimifugin (6), 3'- O-angeloylhamaudol (7), and sec-O-glucosylhamaudol (8). Compounds 1-5 and 7 were classified as well-absorbed, whereas compounds 6 and 8 were categorized as moderately absorbed. The transport of compounds 1-7 increased linearly over a time period up to 180 min. These transport observations occurred within a specific concentration test range of 10-200 µM.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and general gastrointestinal irritation when ingested. Some individuals may also experience dizziness as a neurological side effect. There are serious concerns regarding potential hepatotoxicity, which indicates possible damage to the liver. Additionally, the plant carries a risk of potential nephrotoxicity that could harm the kidneys. These adverse reactions highlight the need for caution when handling or consuming this species.
