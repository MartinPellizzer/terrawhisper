Salix cathayana

What's the taxonomical classification of Salix cathayana?

Salix cathayana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Malpighiales. The plant is a member of the family Salicaceae, falling under the genus Salix, and is specifically identified by the species epithet cathayana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salix cathayana has long, lanceolate leaves that are typically 5 to 15 centimeters in length, featuring finely serrated margins and an acute apex.

The leaf surfaces often exhibit a distinct pubescence on the underside, providing a soft texture. Its branches are characterized by a yellowish-brown bark that becomes more fissured as the specimen matures. The catkins emerge in early spring, appearing as elongated, cylindrical structures that facilitate wind pollination.

These reproductive clusters are supported by short pedicels emerging from the woody stems.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Salix cathayana contains salicin in its bark and leaves, which serves as the primary medicinal component for treating pain and inflammation. The bark is most frequently harvested for its chemical properties because it contains high concentrations of glycosides. These compounds are metabolized in the human body into salicylic acid, providing a natural precursor to modern aspirin. Practitioners also utilize the leaf extracts to help manage fever and reduce swelling in various ailments. Because the potency can vary depending on the season, careful processing of the plant material is essential for therapeutic use.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains salicylates, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and tannins which serve as its primary medicinal compounds.

The presence of salicylates provides significant analgesic and anti-inflammatory properties similar to those found in other willow species. Flavonoids within the plant contribute antioxidant activity that helps protect cells from oxidative stress. Additionally, phenolic acids play a crucial role in providing antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory benefits.

Finally, the tannins in Salix cathayana act as astringents that can help reduce inflammation and aid in tissue repair.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Salix cathayana targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, specifically COX-1 and COX-2, to inhibit the synthesis of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins. The primary bioactive constituent, salicin, is metabolized into salicylic acid which acts as a potent inhibitor of these enzymes within the arachidonic acid pathway. By suppressing COX activity, the plant effectively reduces the production of inflammatory mediators that drive pain and swelling. This mechanism also extends to the modulation of various cytokine pathways that regulate the immune response. Consequently, these specific protein interactions underpin the plant's documented analgesic and antipyretic medicinal properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Salix cathayana?

Salix cathayana has analgesic, antipyretic, and anti-inflammatory properties derived from its high concentration of salicin.

This natural compound acts as a precursor to salicylic acid, which inhibits the production of prostaglandins in the body. By reducing these biochemical mediators, the plant effectively manages pain and lowers elevated body temperatures. Its ability to suppress inflammatory responses makes it useful for treating swelling and joint discomfort.

These therapeutic actions provide a biological mechanism similar to that found in modern aspirin.

  • Anti-inflammatory

    What are the herbal preparations of Salix cathayana?

    Salix cathayana is prepared as aqueous decoctions, topical poultices, or concentrated tinctures derived from its bark and leaf components. The bark is most commonly simmered in water to extract salicin for oral consumption. Practitioners often apply crushed leaves directly to the skin as a poultice to address localized inflammation. Additionally, alcohol-based tinctures are frequently used to create standardized extracts for more consistent dosing. These diverse forms allow the plant's active compounds to be utilized for both internal systemic relief and external dermatological applications.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Salix cathayana can cause allergic reactions such as skin rashes, gastrointestinal upset, and potential bleeding issues due to its high salicylate content. Because this plant contains compounds similar to aspirin, individuals with aspirin sensitivities may experience severe adverse reactions. It can also increase the risk of stomach ulcers or gastrointestinal bleeding if consumed in excessive amounts. People taking blood-thinning medications should avoid it to prevent dangerous interactions. Furthermore, its chemical properties make it unsafe for children due to the risk of Reye's syndrome.
