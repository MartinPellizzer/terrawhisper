Botanical Identification of Myrrh (Commiphora myrrha)

To identify Myrrh (Commiphora myrrha) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Myrrh (Commiphora myrrha) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Myrrh, or Commiphora myrrha, is classified within the plant kingdom. It belongs to the division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Burserales, family Burseraceae, genus Commiphora, and species Commiphora myrrha. This classification reflects its botanical relationships and evolutionary lineage.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Myrrh is Commiphora myrrha, a member of the Burseraceae family. It was first described by the botanist Carl von Linné in 1753. Common names include myrrh, gum myrrh, and olibanum, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Myrrh, or Commiphora myrrha, is a small to medium-sized shrub or tree. It has a shallow root system and a branched stem that becomes woody with age. The leaves are alternate, simple, and oval-shaped with a leathery texture. The plant produces small, clustered inflorescences with yellow-green flowers. The fruit is a capsule that contains several small, flat seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Myrrh, or Commiphora myrrha, can be distinguished by its aromatic, reddish resinous exudate, which solidifies into a gum-like substance. It is typically found in tropical regions, often in dry, sandy soils. The plant has simple, alternate leaves and produces small, inconspicuous yellow flowers. Its resin is commonly used in traditional medicine and incense.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Myrrh (Commiphora myrrha) is native to regions in eastern Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and parts of southern Asia. It thrives in arid to semi-arid climates with hot, dry summers and mild winters. The plant prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It typically grows at elevations between 500 and 1,500 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Myrrh (Commiphora myrrha) blooms during the dry season, typically between December and April. Seed formation occurs after flowering, usually in the following months. Harvesting of the resin is done when the tree is mature, usually between 10 to 15 years old, to ensure quality and sustainability.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Myrrh, Commiphora myrrha, is closely related to species like Commiphora molmol and Commiphora erythraea. It belongs to the genus Commiphora within the Burseraceae family, sharing traits such as resin production and aromatic properties. These relationships help in understanding its botanical classification and medicinal uses.
