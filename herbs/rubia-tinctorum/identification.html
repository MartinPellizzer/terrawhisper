Botanical Identification of Madder (Rubia tinctorum)

To identify Madder (Rubia tinctorum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Madder, or Rubia tinctorum, is classified under the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rubiales, family Rubiaceae, genus Rubia, and species Rubia tinctorum. This classification places it within the flowering plants and highlights its relationship to other plants in the Rubiaceae family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Rubia tinctorum is the scientific name for Madder, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Rubia graveolens and Rubia pachyphylla. Common names include Madder root, Rubia, and Redroot, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Madder is a perennial herb with a taproot system. It has upright, branched stems that are hairy and reddish. Its leaves are alternate, heart-shaped, and have a rough texture. The plant produces a spikelike inflorescence with small, clustered flowers. The flowers are pale pink to white and bloom in late spring. The fruit is a capsule containing small, dark seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Madder can be distinguished by its distinctive red roots and leaves with serrated edges. It has opposite leaves and produces small, yellowish flowers in clusters. It is often confused with other Rubia species, but its deep red roots and specific leaf shape help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Madder is native to southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with mild summers and cool winters. It prefers well-drained soils, often found in grasslands or along riverbanks. It typically grows at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Madder blooms from June to August, producing bright red flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest the roots is in autumn, typically between September and November, when the plant has fully matured.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Rubia tinctorum belongs to the Rubiaceae family, closely related to other Rubia species. It shares morphological and genetic traits with its genus relatives, such as R. cordifolia and R. manitobensis. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary placement and medicinal properties.
