Murdannia nudiflora

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Murdannia nudiflora has opposite, lanceolate to elliptical leaves that are typically sessile or short-petioled with entire margins and prominent longitudinal veins. The plant features a creeping or ascending stem that often forms dense mats in moist environments. Its small, white to pale lavender flowers are solitary and emerge from the leaf axils, showcasing three distinct petals and a central yellow throat. The fruit is a small, globose capsule that splits to release tiny seeds upon maturity. These morphological traits distinguish it from other species within the Linderniaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of Indonesia. It typically flourishes in wetland habitats such as marshes, shallow ponds, and the margins of rice paddies. The species is often found in areas with high humidity and consistent moisture availability. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of seasonal freshwater systems within these warm climatic zones. Because it thrives in semi-aquatic environments, its range is often limited to low-lying plains and river basins.

What are the therapeutic actions of Murdannia nudiflora?

Murdannia nudiflora has therapeutic properties that function as an astringent, diuretic, hemostatic, anti-inflammatory, and antipyretic agent. The plant is frequently utilized in traditional medicine to reduce swelling and manage fever through its anti-inflammatory and antipyretic actions.     What are the herbal preparations of Murdannia nudiflora?

    Murdannia nudiflora is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using the whole plant parts. The plant is often boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal consumption. For external applications, the fresh leaves or stems are crushed into a fine paste to treat skin conditions or inflammation. These various forms allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be delivered effectively for different therapeutic purposes.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Murdannia nudiflora can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or localized swelling if the plant's sap comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. While specific toxicological studies on humans are limited, many members of the Commelinaceae family contain bioactive compounds that may trigger hypersensitivity reactions. Individuals with known sensitivities to similar herbaceous plants should exercise caution when handling the foliage. Symptoms of an adverse reaction typically manifest as redness, itching, or a mild rash at the site of contact. Monitoring for these dermatological changes is recommended during any physical interaction with the plant.
