Botanical Identification of St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum)

To identify St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

St. John'S Wort (Hypericum perforatum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

St. John's Wort, Hypericum perforatum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Garryales, family Clusiaceae, genus Hypericum, and species Hypericum perforatum. These classifications reflect its place within the broader plant kingdom and its specific taxonomic group.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of St. John's Wort is Hypericum perforatum, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by various synonyms, including Hypericum hirtum and Hypericum olympicum. Common names such as St. John's Wort, Klamath Weed, and Mayweed reflect its historical and regional significance. This name has remained largely consistent across different regions and historical periods.

Morphological Characteristics

St. John's Wort is a flowering plant with a prostrate to upright growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it in soil. Its stems are square and hairy, often branching at the base. Leaves are opposite, oval, and dotted with translucent glands. The inflorescence is a dense cluster of yellow flowers with five petals and numerous stamens. The fruit is a capsule containing small, dark seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

St. John's Wort can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow-orange flowers with reddish veins, opposite leaf arrangement, and distinctive stipules. It also has translucent dots on the leaves and produces small, red-brown seeds in cylindrical capsules. These features help differentiate it from similar species like Hypericum ulmifolium.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

St. John's Wort is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with full sun. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It typically grows at low to mid-altitudes, often in meadows or along roadsides.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

St. John's Wort blooms from June to August, producing bright yellow flowers with red stamens. Seed formation occurs in late summer, typically around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is most identifiable during its flowering stage, which is key for accurate botanical recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

St. John's Wort belongs to the genus Hypericum in the family Hypericaceae. Its closest relatives include Hypericum species such as Hypericum hirsutum and Hypericum maculatum. Within the genus, it shares traits like yellow flowers and opposite leaves. The family Hypericaceae is characterized by its aromatic leaves and showy flowers, which are key identifiers for distinguishing St. John's Wort from other plants.
