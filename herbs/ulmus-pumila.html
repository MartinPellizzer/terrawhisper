Ulmus pumila

What's the taxonomical classification of Ulmus pumila?

Ulmus pumila belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it resides in the order Rosales and is a member of the family Ulmaceae. Specifically, it is identified by the genus Ulmus and is defined by the species epithet pumila.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ulmus pumila has small, ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and asymmetrical bases that grow on slender, spreading branches. The bark of the mature tree is dark gray and becomes deeply fissured with age. Its flowers are small, inconspicuous, and typically appear in early spring before the leaves fully expand. The plant produces small, winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal of the seeds. This species is characterized by its rapid growth and relatively narrow, spreading crown.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a vast territory spanning East Asia, including China, Korea, Japan, Mongolia, and parts of Russia. It thrives across diverse landscapes ranging from temperate forests to arid steppe environments. Due to its incredible hardiness and adaptability, it has been widely introduced to many other regions around the world. In many non-native areas, it can be found growing in urban settings or along riverbanks. This widespread distribution is largely facilitated by its ability to tolerate various soil types and extreme temperature fluctuations.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ulmus pumila?

Ulmus pumila has therapeutic properties that include acting as an astringent, diaphoric, demulcent, and tonic to support various bodily functions. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive substances including flavonoids, alkaloids, and terpenoids that contribute to its therapeutic profile. The presence of flavonoid compounds specifically provides potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the plant's chemical structure. These molecules work by neutralizing free radicals and reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. Additionally, the complex synergy between these various phytochemicals enhances the overall medicinal efficacy of the species. Researchers study these specific chemical constituents to develop new pharmaceutical treatments for various ailments.

Due to these higher flavonoid contents, the Ulmus pumila group exhibited lower thiobarbituric acid-reactive substances after 14 d of cold storage (p<0.05). Regarding sensory results, the Ulmus pumila group had lower lightness and higher yellowness values on the surface region (p<0.05). This group also had higher flavor and lower off-flavor intensities compared to the control (p<0.05), leading to higher preference by trained panelists (p<0.001).

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause allergic reactions in people sensitive to tree pollen, specifically manifesting as hay fever, sneezing, and itchy eyes. The fine pollen released from its flowers during the spring season is a common trigger for respiratory irritation. Some individuals may also experience skin irritation or contact dermatitis if they come into direct contact with certain parts of the tree. While it is not considered highly toxic to humans, the seasonal allergen load can be significant in urban environments. Monitoring your personal sensitivity to wind-borne allergens is recommended when near these trees.
