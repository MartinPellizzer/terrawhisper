Ulmus pumila

What's the taxonomical classification of Ulmus pumila?

Ulmus pumila belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it resides in the order Rosales and is a member of the family Ulmaceae. Specifically, it is identified by the genus Ulmus and is defined by the species epithet pumila.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ulmus pumila has small, ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and asymmetrical bases that grow on slender, spreading branches. The bark of the mature tree is dark gray and becomes deeply fissured with age. Its flowers are small, inconspicuous, and typically appear in early spring before the leaves fully expand. The plant produces small, winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal of the seeds. This species is characterized by its rapid growth and relatively narrow, spreading crown.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a vast territory spanning East Asia, including China, Korea, Japan, Mongolia, and parts of Russia. It thrives across diverse landscapes ranging from temperate forests to arid steppe environments. Due to its incredible hardiness and adaptability, it has been widely introduced to many other regions around the world. In many non-native areas, it can be found growing in urban settings or along riverbanks. This widespread distribution is largely facilitated by its ability to tolerate various soil types and extreme temperature fluctuations.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ulmus pumila contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its inner bark, roots, and leaves. The inner bark is frequently harvested to prepare decoctions used for treating gastrointestinal inflammation and soothing mucous membranes. Its leaves are often employed in traditional remedies to reduce fever and address skin irritations. Some practitioners also utilize the root extracts to assist with digestive ailments and systemic detoxification. What are the pharmacological activities of Ulmus pumila?

This plant has hepatoprotective and neuroprotective pharmacological activities that stem from its rich concentration of bioactive phytochemicals. These medicinal properties are primarily attributed to the presence of various flavonoids, polyphenols, and triterpenoids found within its tissues. The hepatoprotective effects assist in shielding liver cells from oxidative damage and chemical toxicity. Additionally, the neuroprotective capabilities suggest a potential role in mitigating neuronal injury and supporting brain health. Such diverse therapeutic actions make Ulmus pumila a subject of significant interest in pharmacological research.

According to a study published by "Pakistan journal of pharmaceuticaal sciences", the EtOAc fraction of the fruits of Ulmus pumila L. resulted in the isolation of thirteen flavane derivatives. All of the compounds 1-13 were obtained from the fruits of U. pumila L. for the first time. Compounds 1, 2, 5, 7 and 8 exhibited remarkable hepatoprotective activities at 10μM. Additionally, compounds 9, 10, and 13 showed significant neuroprotective activities. These specific compounds demonstrated IC50 values of 4.08, 5.34, and 2.02μM, respectively.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, phenolic acids, tannins, sterols, and saponins which contribute to its diverse medicinal properties. These bioactive compounds work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects within the human body. Phenolic acids and tannins are particularly noted for their ability to neutralize free radicals and protect cells from oxidative stress. The presence of sterols and saponins further enhances the plant's potential therapeutic applications in various traditional remedies. Together, these chemical constituents make Ulmus pumila a valuable subject for pharmacological study.

As discussed in a study, Ulmus pumila extracts were used to marinate chicken breasts for 1 h before cooking at 60°C for 2 h. The Ulmus pumila group showed higher flavonoid contents compared to the Astragalus membranaceus group (p<0.001). Due to these higher flavonoid contents, the Ulmus pumila group exhibited lower thiobarbituric acid-reactive substances after 14 d of cold storage (p<0.05). Regarding sensory results, the Ulmus pumila group had lower lightness and higher yellowness values on the surface region (p<0.05). This group also had higher flavor and lower off-flavor intensities compared to the control (p<0.05), leading to higher preference by trained panelists (p<0.001).

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction of the inner bark, an infusion of the leaves, a tincture of the bark, or an extract of the bark. The inner bark is typically simmered in water to create a decoction used for its mucilaginous properties. Leaves are often steeped in hot water to produce a gentle infusion. For more concentrated therapeutic applications, the bark is macerated in alcohol to create a tincture. Additionally, a concentrated extract can be derived from the bark to provide a potent dose of its active compounds.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions in people sensitive to tree pollen, specifically manifesting as hay fever, sneezing, and itchy eyes. The fine pollen released from its flowers during the spring season is a common trigger for respiratory irritation. Some individuals may also experience skin irritation or contact dermatitis if they come into direct contact with certain parts of the tree. While it is not considered highly toxic to humans, the seasonal allergen load can be significant in urban environments. Monitoring your personal sensitivity to wind-borne allergens is recommended when near these trees.
