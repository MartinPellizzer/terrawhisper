Actinidia valvata

What's the taxonomical classification of Actinidia valvata?

Actinidia valvata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of botanical classification, it falls under the order Ericales and is a member of the family Actinidiaceae. Specifically, the plant is situated within the genus Actinidia, with its unique designation being the species valvata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Actinidia valvata has woody, climbing vines characterized by large, cordate to broadly ovate leaves with entire or slightly wavy margins and a distinctively thick, leathery texture. The stems are flexible and utilize tendrils to ascend through forest canopies. Its flowers are typically solitary and emerge from the leaf axils, displaying white petals and numerous prominent stamens. The fruit develops into a globose berry that transitions from green to a dark, purplish color when fully ripe. These morphological traits allow the plant to thrive in shaded, moist environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Actinidia valvata?

This plant has significant anti-proliferation, anti-tumor, and apoptosis induction properties that make it a subject of interest in cancer research. Various bioactive compounds extracted from Actinidia valvata work to inhibit the rapid multiplication of malignant cells. These substances effectively suppress tumor growth by disrupting the normal cell cycle of cancerous tissues. Furthermore, the plant components trigger programmed cell death, a process known as apoptosis induction, within targeted tumor cells. Through these combined mechanisms, the plant demonstrates potent therapeutic potential for managing various forms of cancer.

As discussed in a study, the plant Actinidia valvata Dunn provides the triterpenoid saponin 2a,-3a,-24-Trihydroxyurs-12-en-28-oic acid (TEO), which induces apoptosis in three human hepatocellular carcinoma cell lines. TEO exhibited inhibition effects on BEL-7402, BEL-7404, and SMMC-7721 cells treated for 24 h, with IC50 values of 34.6, 30.8, and 30.5 μg/mL, respectively. When treated with 40 μg/mL of TEO, the three cell lines increased by more than 21% in the G1 phase and presented DNA damage. The pharmacological activity involves declining mitochondrial membrane potential and activating TNF-α, caspase-3, caspase-8, and caspase-9. These effects are further characterized by a decrease in Bcl-2 protein expression and an increase in Bax and Bid protein expressions, thereby increasing the Bax/Bcl-2 ratio.
