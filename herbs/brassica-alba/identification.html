Botanical Identification of White Mustard (Brassica alba)

To identify White Mustard (Brassica alba) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

White Mustard (Brassica alba) Monograph

Scientific Classification

White Mustard, or Brassica alba, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of seed plants, class Caryophyllopsida, order Brassicales, family Brassicaceae, genus Brassica, and species Brassica alba. This classification places it within the mustard family, known for its edible and medicinal plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of White Mustard is Brassica alba, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Sinapis alba and Brassica hirta. Common names include White Mustard, Yellow Mustard, and Field Mustard, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

White Mustard (Brassica alba) is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, hairy stems that are branching at the base. Its leaves are alternate, lance-shaped, and have a rough texture. The plant produces a branched inflorescence with small yellow flowers. The fruit is a long, slender pod containing several small, round seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

White Mustard (Brassica alba) can be distinguished by its alternate, lance-shaped leaves with toothed margins, bright yellow flowers in clusters, and pods that are slender and slightly curved. It is often confused with other mustard species, but its leaf shape and flower structure are key distinguishing features.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

White Mustard (Brassica alba) is native to Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It commonly grows at altitudes between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

White Mustard blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and June. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering stage, which is critical for accurate botanical identification. Harvest timing for seeds is usually in late summer to early autumn.

Phylogenetic Relationships

White Mustard (Brassica alba) belongs to the Brassicaceae family, closely related to species like Arabidopsis thaliana and Capsella bursa-pastoris. It shares key family traits such as four-petaled flowers and siliques. Within the genus Brassica, it is closely related to other mustard species like B. napus and B. rapa. These relationships aid in understanding its ecological and medicinal roles.
