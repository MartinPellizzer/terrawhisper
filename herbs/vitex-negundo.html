Scientific and Botanical Profile

Negundo Vitex, with botanical name Vitex negundo, is a member of the Verbenaceae family, commonly known as the verbena or vervain family.

It is also referred to as Common Vitex, Vitex Tree, Chinese Vitex, Common Hackberry, Chaste Tree, and Negundo Vitex, and is native to Southern Asia, including China, India, Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Vietnam, Thailand, Myanmar, Bhutan, Nepal, Sri Lanka, and Bangladesh. Morphologically, it is a deciduous shrub or small tree that can grow up to 5 meters in height, featuring opposite, lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and clusters of small, tubular, fragrant flowers that range in color from white to violet.

The plant produces small, spherical berries that are typically green when immature and turn black when mature, containing several seeds.

History and Cultural Relevance

Negundo Vitex was used in traditional medicine systems across Asia, Africa, and the Americas for centuries, valued for its therapeutic properties and spiritual significance.

In Ayurveda and Traditional Chinese Medicine, its leaves, flowers, and roots were employed to treat menstrual disorders, anxiety, and skin conditions, reflecting its deep integration into holistic healing practices. The plant holds cultural importance in various rituals, such as being used in purification ceremonies or as a symbol of protection and prosperity in certain indigenous communities. Today, its extracts are still used in herbal remedies for hormonal balance and stress relief, highlighting its enduring relevance in modern wellness approaches.

Negundo Vitex continues to bridge ancient wisdom with contemporary health practices, underscoring its lasting cultural and medicinal legacy.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Vitex negundo has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, with various forms available including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

The plant can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the leaves for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion or applied topically for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried leaves in tea form, while children should only use it under medical supervision if safe.

Due to the lack of extensive research, it is advised to use Vitex negundo sparingly, with a short duration of use to minimize potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Vitex negundo can be used as a medicinal plant with caution due to its potential safety concerns and side effects.

While it is traditionally used for its purported therapeutic benefits, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, as well as skin irritation or allergic reactions in some individuals. There is limited clinical evidence on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with anticoagulants, sedatives, or other herbal supplements, as it may enhance their effects or lead to unintended consequences. Special populations, such as pregnant or breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to the risk of adverse effects on fetal development or infant health, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a qualified healthcare professional, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if any adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Vitex negundo grows best in full sun to partial shade, thriving in well-drained, loamy soil that is slightly acidic to neutral in pH.

It requires moderate watering, especially during dry periods, but it is drought-tolerant once established, making it suitable for a variety of climates. Regular pruning is recommended to encourage bushy growth and remove dead or diseased branches, while mulching helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Harvesting is most effective during the summer months when the leaves and flowers are at their peak potency, using clean, sharp scissors to cut the stems just above a node to preserve the plant's vigor.

To maintain potency, the harvested parts should be dried in a cool, dark, well-ventilated area, then stored in airtight containers away from light and moisture, or refrigerated for longer preservation.
