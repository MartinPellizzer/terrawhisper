Wormwood

Artemisia officinalis

Wormwood (Artemisia officinalis) is a perennial herb native to Europe, North Africa, and parts of Asia, known for its distinct bitter taste and a variety of traditional medicinal uses, including the treatment of digestive issues and parasitic infections.

Botanical Identification

Wormwood, scientifically known as Artemisia officinalis, belongs to the Asteraceae family. It is also called mugwort in some regions and is a perennial shrub native to temperate regions of Europe and Asia. The plant has serrated leaves and small yellow flowers, and it has been referred to by various names in traditional herbal texts.

Active Compounds

Wormwood contains essential oils, terpenoids, and flavonoids as its primary active compounds. These compounds contribute to its medicinal properties through anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and digestive effects. They often work synergistically to enhance therapeutic outcomes in herbal formulations.

Wormwood Actions.

Preparation Methods

Decoction: Used for respiratory infections and digestive issues.

Infusion: Used for cold symptoms and mild respiratory issues.

Tincture: Used for digestive disorders and as an antiseptic.

Safety Profile

Wormwood is contraindicated during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential risks to fetal and infant development. It should also be avoided by individuals with a history of seizures or epilepsy. Long-term use may lead to toxicity, particularly affecting the liver and nervous system.
