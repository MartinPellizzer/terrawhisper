Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name of Field Marigold is Calendula arvensis, first described by Carl von Linné. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Calendula officinalis var. arvensis and Helenium arvensis. Common names include Field Marigold, Common Marigold, and Wild Marigold, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Field Marigold is an annual herb with a taproot system. Its stems are erect and hairy, often branching at the base. Leaves are alternate, lance-shaped, and have a rough texture. The inflorescence consists of a single head of yellow-orange flowers. Each flower has ray florets surrounding a central disk, and the fruit is a capsule containing numerous seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Field Marigold can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow-orange petals with a darker central disk, hairy stems, and alternate leaves. It grows in disturbed soils and has a strong, spicy odor. It is often confused with other calendulas, but its hairy stems and leaf arrangement help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Field Marigold is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It is commonly found at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Field Marigold blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between May and September. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to October. For identification, the best time to harvest is during the flowering stage, when the petals are fully open and the plant is most identifiable.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) belongs to the Asteraceae family, sharing traits with other members like sunflowers and daisies. It is closely related to species within the Calendula genus, such as Calendula officinalis. Its phylogenetic position reflects its evolutionary ties to other flowering plants with similar structures and chemical compositions.
