Euphorbia micractina

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia micractina?

Euphorbia micractina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae. At the most specific levels of its biological classification, it is situated within the genus Euphorbia, ultimately making its scientific designation the species micractina.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia micractina has a succulent, caespitose growth habit characterized by short, cylindrical, or slightly clavate stems that are often covered in small, tuberculate bumps.

These stems are typically pale green to yellowish and can reach several centimeters in height when clustered in dense mounds. The plant features very small, inconspicuous leaves that are often deciduous or ephemeral, appearing only briefly during periods of moisture. Its reproductive structures consist of tiny cyathia that emerge from the stem axils, often accompanied by minute bracts.

The overall morphology is highly adapted to arid environments, prioritizing water storage within its thickened stem tissue.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia micractina contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its latex, which is the milky sap found throughout the stem and leaves. This substance is often harvested by making small incisions in the plant's tissues to collect the fluid. Local practitioners may apply this latex topically to treat skin conditions or minor irritations. Some traditional uses involve the controlled application of the sap to manage specific ailments, though caution is required due to its caustic nature. The chemical properties of the sap are thought to provide the plant's therapeutic effects.

        What are the herbal preparations of Euphorbia micractina?

        Euphorbia micractina is prepared as a topical application of the expressed latex or as a decoction made from the plant parts. The milky sap is often applied directly to the skin to treat specific dermatological conditions. Some traditional practices involve boiling the plant material in water to create a liquid extract for medicinal use. These preparations must be handled with extreme caution due to the potential toxicity of the caustic substances within the plant. Local knowledge dictates the specific concentration and method used to mitigate irritation during treatment.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Euphorbia micractina can cause severe skin irritation, eye damage, and gastrointestinal distress due to its toxic milky latex. Contact with the sap often results in redness, blistering, or chemical burns on the skin. If the fluid enters the eyes, it can lead to intense inflammation or temporary vision impairment. Ingesting even small amounts of the plant may trigger nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. These adverse reactions are typical of many species within the Euphorbia genus containing caustic substances.
