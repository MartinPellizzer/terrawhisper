Euphorbia micractina

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia micractina has a succulent, caespitose growth habit characterized by short, cylindrical, or slightly clavate stems that are often covered in small, tuberculate bumps. These stems are typically pale green to yellowish and can reach several centimeters in height when clustered in dense mounds. The plant features very small, inconspicuous leaves that are often deciduous or ephemeral, appearing only briefly during periods of moisture. Its reproductive structures consist of tiny cyathia that emerge from the stem axils, often accompanied by minute bracts. The overall morphology is highly adapted to arid environments, prioritizing water storage within its thickened stem tissue.

      What are the herbal preparations of Euphorbia micractina?

      Euphorbia micractina is prepared as a topical application of the expressed latex or as a decoction made from the plant parts. The milky sap is often applied directly to the skin to treat specific dermatological conditions. Some traditional practices involve boiling the plant material in water to create a liquid extract for medicinal use.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Euphorbia micractina can cause severe skin irritation, eye damage, and gastrointestinal distress due to its toxic milky latex. Contact with the sap often results in redness, blistering, or chemical burns on the skin. If the fluid enters the eyes, it can lead to intense inflammation or temporary vision impairment. Ingesting even small amounts of the plant may trigger nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. These adverse reactions are typical of many species within the Euphorbia genus containing caustic substances.
