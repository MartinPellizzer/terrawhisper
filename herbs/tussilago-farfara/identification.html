Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Coltsfoot is Tussilago farfara, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It has several synonyms, including Pulmonaria farfara and Anemone farfara. Common names include Coltsfoot, Lion's foot, and Woolly bell. It is also known by regional names such as Bitter root and Lungwort in various areas.

Morphological Characteristics

Coltsfoot is a perennial herb with a short, fibrous root system. It has a thick, fleshy rhizome that stores nutrients. The plant grows from the ground level, with a stout, hairy stem that becomes more robust as it matures.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Coltsfoot can be distinguished by its heart-shaped leaves with toothed edges and a hairy stem. It produces early spring flowers with a yellow center and white petals. It is often confused with other early bloomers, but its distinct leaf shape and timing set it apart. The leaves are also often covered in fine hairs, which is a key identifying feature.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Coltsfoot is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and is commonly found in meadows, roadsides, and riverbanks. It grows at elevations between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Coltsfoot blooms in early spring, typically between March and May, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested in early spring before the leaves fully develop.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Coltsfoot belongs to the genus Tussilago, which is closely related to other members of the Asteraceae family. It shares key family traits such as composite flowers and alternate leaves. Its closest species are other Tussilago species, which are distinguished by similar morphological and ecological characteristics.
