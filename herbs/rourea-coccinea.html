Rourea coccinea

What's the taxonomical classification of Rourea coccinea?

Rourea coccinea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls into the order Oxalidales. This plant is a member of the family Connaraceae, specifically situated within the genus Rourea, and is identified by its unique species name, coccinea.

Rourea coccinea has pharmacological properties that include anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant activities used primarily in traditional medicine. What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, wounds, stomach ailments, and various inflammatory conditions. Its medicinal properties are often utilized to soothe skin irritations and promote the healing of open cuts or sores. In traditional practices, decoctions made from the plant help alleviate digestive issues and gastrointestinal discomfort. Additionally, the plant is recognized for its ability to reduce swelling and manage pain in different parts of the body. These diverse therapeutic applications make it a significant component in regional herbal medicine.

What are the herbal preparations of Rourea coccinea?

Rourea coccinea is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using its dried leaves, stems, or roots. These liquid extracts are frequently consumed to treat internal ailments such as fever or digestive issues. The plant material can also be crushed into a fine paste for direct application to the skin to alleviate inflammation or wounds. Some traditional practices involve using the juice expressed from fresh leaves for its medicinal properties. This versatility allows practitioners to tailor the delivery method to the specific health concern being addressed.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Rourea coccinea can cause adverse reactions such as skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential allergic responses in sensitive individuals. While it is used in various traditional medicinal practices, the specific toxicological profile of the plant remains insufficiently documented in modern clinical studies. Some users may experience localized itching or redness if the plant material comes into direct contact with the skin. Ingesting preparations of the plant could lead to nausea or stomach discomfort if used in incorrect dosages. Because scientific research is limited, the safety and long-term effects of consuming this species are not yet fully understood.
