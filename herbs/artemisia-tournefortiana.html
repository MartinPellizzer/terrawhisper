Artemisia tournefortiana

What's the taxonomical classification of Artemisia tournefortiana?

Artemisia tournefortiana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its evolutionary lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, eventually reaching the order Asterales. As a member of the prominent family Asteraceae, this plant is situated within the genus Artemisia, and is specifically identified by the species name tournefortiana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artemisia tournefortiana has a woody, perennial shrub habit characterized by erect, branching stems that typically reach heights of 0.5 to 1 meter. The leaves are primarily basal or cauline, displaying a deeply lobed or pinnatifid structure with narrow, linear segments. These leaves often possess a silvery-grey or grayish-green hue due to a dense covering of fine, silky hairs on both surfaces. Small, inconspicuous flower heads are arranged in compact, terminal clusters or panicles. The plant's overall morphology is adapted to arid environments through its compact growth form and reduced leaf surface area.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and semi-arid regions of Southwest Asia and the Mediterranean Basin, specifically spanning from North Africa through the Middle East into parts of Central Asia. It thrives in dry, sandy, or rocky soils typical of steppe and desert ecosystems. Many populations are found within the Irano-Turanian phytogeographical region where seasonal droughts are common. Its distribution is often linked to open habitats that receive limited precipitation throughout the year. These environmental conditions allow it to spread across various mountainous and plateau landscapes within its natural range.

What are the pharmacological activities of Artemisia tournefortiana?

This plant has potent pharmacological activities including antibacterial, anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-vitiligo, and antimalarial properties. Its phytochemical constituents allow it to effectively combat various bacterial strains and inhibit the growth of malignant cells. The plant also demonstrates a significant ability to reduce inflammation and mitigate the effects of vitiligo in skin cells. Furthermore, its unique chemical profile makes it a valuable subject for developing new treatments against malaria. These diverse medicinal capabilities highlight its importance in traditional and modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, Artemisia tournefortiana Rchb ethanol extract was used for the green-synthesis of silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) which exhibited various biological properties. These synthesized AgNP particles were mostly spherical with an average diameter of 22.89±14.82nm. The resulting nanoparticles demonstrated antibacterial activity and showed dose-dependent cytotoxicity in HT29 cancer cells by increasing cell apoptosis. Furthermore, the photocatalytic activity of the nanoparticles was demonstrated through the degradation of Coomassie Brilliant Blue G-250 within 60min under UV light exposure. These results suggest that the AgNPs fabricated from the plant possess potent anti-pathogenic bacteria and anti-colon cancer activities.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds artemisinin, artetourneforin F-K, monoterpene, and sesquiterpene. These specific chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the therapeutic properties observed in the species. The artemisinin component is widely recognized for its significant antimalarial activities. Furthermore, the presence of various monoterpene and sesquiterpene groups contributes to the overall complex chemical profile. These diverse organic compounds work together to provide a broad range of biological effects.

As discussed in a study, Artemisia tournefortiana serves as an alternative source of the medicinal compound artemisinin. The maximum artemisinin content was detected in the flowers at 1.43%, while the roots contained 0.33% and the seeds contained 0.32%. Flower content across different geographical sites varied from 0.05% to 1.43%. Quantification of these levels was carried out using RP-HPLC-PDA with a C18 column measuring 250 mm x 4.6 mm and a 5 μm particle size. This process utilized a mobile phase of phosphate buffer at pH 2.2 and acetonitrile in a 50:50 v/v ratio.
