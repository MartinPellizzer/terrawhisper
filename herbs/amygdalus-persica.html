Amygdalus persica

What's the taxonomical classification of Amygdalus persica?

Amygdalus persica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this classification, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the biological hierarchy further, it falls under the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Amygdalus, and its unique species designation, persica.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Amygdalus persica contains amygdalin, fatty acids, vitamins, and various polyphenolic compounds within its seeds and kernels. The cyanogenic glycoside amygdalin is the most prominent constituent, which can release hydrogen cyanide when metabolized. The oil extracted from the seeds is rich in oleic and linoleic acids, providing significant moisturizing properties. Additionally, the plant provides vitamin E and various antioxidants that help reduce oxidative stress in biological systems. These chemical components are frequently utilized in both traditional medicine and modern dermatological applications.

What are the therapeutic actions of Amygdalus persica?

Amygdalus persica has therapeutic properties that primarily involve the use of its seeds and kernels to treat respiratory ailments, skin inflammations, and digestive issues through their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory compounds. The plant contains various bioactive substances, including flavonoids and phenolic acids, which help reduce oxidative stress in the body. When applied topically, the oils derived from the seeds can soothe irritation and promote skin healing. In traditional medicine, extracts are sometimes utilized to alleviate symptoms of coughing and asthma. These medicinal actions are largely attributed to the complex chemical profile found within the fruit's pit.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin conditions such as eczema and psoriasis, respiratory ailments like coughs and asthma, and various gastrointestinal issues including constipation. The seeds contain amygdalin, which is often utilized in traditional medicine to soothe inflammation and reduce pain. Some practitioners also apply oil derived from the kernels to alleviate joint stiffness and muscular aches. Its diuretic and expectorant properties help clear mucus from the lungs and promote fluid balance. These therapeutic applications vary significantly depending on whether the fruit or the seed is being utilized.

What are the herbal preparations of Amygdalus persica?

Amygdalus persica is prepared as dried kernels, infused oils, topical ointments, or aqueous extracts for medicinal and cosmetic use. The kernels are frequently crushed or ground to produce a potent oil that serves as a base for various skin treatments. These oils are often blended into salves or creams to leverage their emollient properties for dermatological applications. Additionally, decoctions made from the plant components can be utilized in traditional liquid formulations. Such preparations are carefully controlled to manage the presence of amygdalin within the seeds.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Amygdalus persica can cause cyanide poisoning if the seeds or pits are consumed in significant quantities due to their high concentration of amygdalin. This toxic reaction typically manifests through symptoms such as headache, dizziness, nausea, and vomiting. In more severe cases of ingestion, individuals may experience rapid heart rates, seizures, or even respiratory failure. Because the toxicity is linked directly to the chemical breakdown of the pit, avoiding the kernel is essential for safety. Careful management of consumption is necessary to prevent these dangerous physiological responses.
