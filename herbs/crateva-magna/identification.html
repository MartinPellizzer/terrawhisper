Botanical Identification of Cape Gooseberry (Crateva magna)

To identify Cape Gooseberry (Crateva magna) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Cape Gooseberry, or Crateva magna, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the angiosperms, eudicots, and Sapindales order. It is part of the Sapindaceae family, genus Crateva, and species Crateva magna. This classification helps identify its botanical relationships and characteristics within the plant kingdom.

Morphological Characteristics

Cape Gooseberry (Crateva magna) is a shrub with a sprawling growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly. The stems are woody and branched, often covered in short hairs. Leaves are heart-shaped, with a rough texture and a waxy surface. The plant produces clusters of small, white flowers with five petals. The fruit is a yellow, fleshy berry with a single seed inside.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cape Gooseberry can be distinguished by its distinctive yellowish-orange, spherical berries with a leathery skin. It has heart-shaped leaves with prominent veins and a woody stem with rough texture. These features help differentiate it from similar species like Physalis and Capsicum.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cape Gooseberry (Crateva magna) is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Americas. It thrives in warm climates with high humidity and moderate rainfall. The plant prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cape Gooseberry (Crateva magna) blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn, around August to October. For identification purposes, the fruit is ready for harvest when it turns bright orange-red, usually in late autumn to early winter.
