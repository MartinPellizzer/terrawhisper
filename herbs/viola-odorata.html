Viola odorata

What's the taxonomical classification of Viola odorata?

Viola odorata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls under the subclass Magnoliidae and is organized into the order Malpighiales. The plant is further categorized into the family Violaceae, specifically within the genus Viola, and is identified by its unique species name, odorata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viola odorata has a creeping rhizomatous stem with heart-shaped, crenate-margined leaves that measure between 2 and 5 centimeters in length. The foliage is typically dark green and grows in basal clusters near the ground. During the flowering season, it produces small, five-petaled violet flowers that emerge on slender peduncles. Each flower features a distinct yellow center and possesses a strong, sweet fragrance. The plant also develops small, rounded seed capsules that split open to disperse seeds upon maturity.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to much of Europe and parts of western and central Asia, spanning from the British Isles through temperate regions of the continent and into the Caucasus. It thrives primarily in moist, deciduous woodlands and shaded meadows where soil remains consistently damp. While its natural range is centered in the Old World, the species has been widely introduced to other continents through intentional cultivation. In regions like North America, it is frequently found in gardens and urban parks as an ornamental groundcover. Its ability to spread via rhizomes allows it to colonize various temperate habitats across its expanded global range.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Viola odorata contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its leaves, flowers, and aerial stems. The fresh leaves and blossoms are harvested to create infusions or tinctures used to treat respiratory ailments and soothe coughs. These plant parts contain salicylic acid, which provides mild analgesic and antipyretic effects similar to aspirin. The flowers are also frequently used in herbal preparations to reduce inflammation and promote skin healing. Collectors typically gather these components during the spring when the essential oil concentration is at its peak.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viola odorata?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antitussive, and expectorant pharmacological activities. The presence of various phytochemicals allows it to reduce pain and swelling in biological tissues. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage. Furthermore, the plant acts as an antitussive agent to suppress coughing and functions as an expectorant to facilitate the removal of mucus from the respiratory tract. These diverse medicinal actions make it a significant subject in ethnopharmacology.

As discussed in a study, Viola odorata L. has shown various medical applications including the management of cough, fever, common cold, headache, insomnia, epilepsy, constipation, palpitation, dyspnea, dysuria, and skin diseases reported in traditional Persian medicine from the 10th to 18th century AD. In modern phytotherapy, this herb plant has exerted antiinflammatory, analgesic, antioxidant, diuretic, antihypertensive, and antibacterial activities. The pharmacological activities of the plant are attributed to flavonoids, saponins, and alkaloids. Therapeutic preparations include violet oil for nasal or topical application and preparations such as pill, decoction, sweet syrup, confection, or semisolid oral forms.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including coumarins, flavonoids, cyclotides, total phenolic compounds, and alkaloids. These chemical constituents are responsible for the various therapeutic properties associated with the species. Specifically, the alkaloids and flavonoids often contribute to significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. The presence of cyclotides provides unique structural stability that is highly valued in drug development research. Additionally, the total phenolic compounds and coumarins work together to offer potent antimicrobial benefits.

As discussed in a study, Viola odorata has several medicinal uses including its ability to fight cancer, bacteria, and inflammation, as well as antioxidant and antipyretic activity. Phytochemical studies of different parts of Viola odorata resulted in the isolation of chemical constituents such as Coumarins, caffeic acid, methyl salicylate, flavonoids (Quercetin, kaempferol), glycosides (Rutin), and terpenoids (stigma sterol). Coumarin derivatives within the plant possess anti-inflammatory and anti-carcinogenic properties. In this specific study, the coumarins umbelliferone and esculetin of Viola odorata were identified by TLC and HPTLC.

This plant can be processed into an infusion, tincture, essential oil, extract, syrup, and powder for various medicinal and aromatic applications. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach cramps, skin irritation, and dizziness if ingested or touched. Consuming parts of the violet may lead to significant gastrointestinal distress such as abdominal pain and upset stomach. Topical contact with the leaves or stems can result in localized skin irritation for some individuals. Systemic reactions may also manifest as dizziness or lightheadedness after exposure. Careful avoidance is recommended to prevent these adverse physical reactions.
