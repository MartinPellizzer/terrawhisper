Solanum mammosum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum mammosum?

Solanum mammosum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Solanales and is a member of the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Solanum and the species mammosum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum mammosum has a perennial herbaceous growth habit characterized by erect, branched stems that can reach heights of up to one meter. The foliage consists of large, ovate to oblong leaves with wavy margins and a distinctively pubescent texture. Its most striking morphological feature is the fruit, which are fleshy, yellow berries covered in numerous fleshy, nipple-like protrusions. These specialized berries grow in clusters and serve as a primary identifying characteristic of the species. Small, star-shaped flowers with bright yellow anthers typically emerge from the axils of the leaves.

What are the pharmacological activities of Solanum mammosum?

This plant has antiproliferative, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities driven by its unique bioactive secondary metabolites. Research indicates that its extracts can effectively inhibit the growth of various cancer cell lines through antiproliferative mechanisms. Additionally, the presence of steroidal alkaloids contributes to its potent ability to combat infectious pathogens. Some studies also suggest that the plant possesses antioxidant properties that help mitigate oxidative stress in biological systems. These diverse medicinal qualities make Solanum mammosum a significant subject for future drug development.

According to a study published by "Journal of proteome research", the plant Solanum mammosum provides indioside D, which is a furostanol glycoside. This compound was found to possess antiproliferative activity toward a panel of human cancer cell lines. In HeLa cells, indioside D caused a rapid dissipation of mitochondrial transmembrane potential (DeltaPsim) and the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS). The treatment triggered the activation of caspase-8 and the cleavage of Bid through the Fas death receptor pathway. Ultimately, indioside D induced apoptosis in HeLa cells via both intrinsic and extrinsic cell death pathways.
