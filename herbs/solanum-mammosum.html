Solanum mammosum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum mammosum?

Solanum mammosum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Solanales and is a member of the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Solanum and the species mammosum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum mammosum has a perennial herbaceous growth habit characterized by erect, branched stems that can reach heights of up to one meter. The foliage consists of large, ovate to oblong leaves with wavy margins and a distinctively pubescent texture. Its most striking morphological feature is the fruit, which are fleshy, yellow berries covered in numerous fleshy, nipple-like protrusions. These specialized berries grow in clusters and serve as a primary identifying characteristic of the species. Small, star-shaped flowers with bright yellow anthers typically emerge from the axils of the leaves.

What are the pharmacological activities of Solanum mammosum?

This plant has antiproliferative, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities derived from its bioactive secondary metabolites. The presence of alkaloids, steroids, and flavonoids within its various parts contributes significantly to these biological effects. Specifically, the antiproliferative properties suggest a potential role in inhibiting the growth of certain cancer cell lines. Studies also indicate that its antioxidant capacity helps mitigate oxidative stress within biological systems. These diverse medicinal properties make Solanum mammosum a subject of interest for further pharmaceutical development.

As discussed in a study, Solanum mammosum produces a furostanol glycoside called indioside D that possesses antiproliferative activity toward a panel of human cancer cell lines. In HeLa cells, indioside D caused a rapid dissipation of mitochondrial transmembrane potential (DeltaPsim) and the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS). This treatment triggered the activation of caspase-8 and the cleavage of Bid through the Fas death receptor pathway. Ultimately, indioside D induced apoptosis in HeLa cells via both intrinsic and extrinsic cell death pathways.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe illness due to the presence of toxic alkaloids known as solanine. Consuming any part of the plant may lead to significant gastrointestinal distress, including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. These symptoms often arise as the body reacts to the chemical toxicity within the tissues. In addition to digestive issues, individuals may also experience neurological effects such as dizziness. Careful avoidance is necessary because these toxic compounds can pose serious health risks if ingested.
