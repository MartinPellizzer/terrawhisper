Eurya alata

What's the taxonomical classification of Eurya alata?

Eurya alata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it resides in the order Ericales and is a member of the family Pentaphylacaceae. Specifically, this plant is identified by the genus Eurya and is distinguished as the species alata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eurya alata has small, evergreen leaves that are typically elliptic to lanceolate in shape, measuring between 2 and 5 centimeters in length, and feature distinctively wavy or undulate margins.

The leaf surfaces are often lustrous and dark green on the upper side, while the undersides may appear slightly paler. Its stems are woody and can develop a somewhat rough texture as the plant matures into a shrub or small tree. The flowers are minute and produced in small clusters, followed by small, fleshy berries that change color as they ripen.

These morphological traits collectively help distinguish the species within its natural habitat.

      What are the herbal preparations of Eurya alata?

      Eurya alata is prepared primarily as a decoction made by boiling the leaves in water to extract their bioactive compounds. This liquid infusion is commonly consumed as a traditional tea to address various ailments. Some practitioners also utilize the plant material to create topical poultices for skin applications.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Eurya alata can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested or applied incorrectly. While specific human clinical trials are limited, the plant contains various bioactive compounds that may trigger hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. Contact with the leaves or stems might result in redness, itching, or localized dermatitis upon skin exposure. If consumed, the chemical constituents could potentially lead to stomach upset or other digestive complications. Users should exercise caution and avoid direct contact until more comprehensive toxicological data becomes available.
