Populus alba

What's the taxonomical classification of Populus alba?

Populus alba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Salicaceae. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Populus and the species alba.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Populus alba contains salicin, a phenolic glycoside that serves as a precursor to salicylic acid, which provides analgesic and antipyretic properties. This compound is structurally similar to modern aspirin and has been historically utilized to reduce inflammation and alleviate pain. The bark of the white poplar is the primary source for extracting these therapeutic substances. Beyond salicin, the plant may possess various tannins that contribute to astringent effects when applied topically. These chemical constituents make the species a significant subject in the study of traditional botanical pharmacology.

What are the therapeutic actions of Populus alba?

Populus alba has therapeutic properties primarily derived from its high concentration of salicin, which functions as a natural precursor to salicylic acid to reduce inflammation and alleviate pain. This compound acts by inhibiting the production of prostaglandins, making the plant an effective traditional remedy for fevers and various types of aches. Beyond its analgesic capabilities, the bark and leaves are often utilized in topical applications to soothe skin irritations and swelling. The plant's chemical constituents also suggest mild antimicrobial benefits when applied to affected areas. Consequently, it serves as a botanical foundation for managing discomfort through its systemic and localized biological actions.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions, skin irritations, and digestive ailments through the medicinal application of its bark. The bark contains salicin, a compound chemically similar to aspirin, which helps reduce fever and alleviate pain. When applied topically, extracts can soothe minor burns or insect bites by decreasing localized swelling. Some traditional practices also involve using decoctions to calm stomach cramps or intestinal discomfort. Because of these properties, the species has a long history in folk medicine for managing systemic inflammation.
