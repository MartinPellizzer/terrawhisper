Botanical Identification of Cassava (Manihot esculenta)

To identify Cassava (Manihot esculenta) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Cassava, or Manihot esculenta, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Malpighiales, family Euphorbiaceae, genus Manihot, and species Manihot esculenta. These classifications help categorize its biological relationships and characteristics within the plant kingdom.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name of Cassava is Manihot esculenta, a species within the Euphorbiaceae family. It was first described by the French botanist Jean-Baptiste de Lamarck in 1783. Common names include cassava, manioc, and yuca, while regional and historical names vary widely across cultures and regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Cassava is an herbaceous perennial plant with a woody base. It has a fibrous root system that develops underground. The stem is thick, swollen, and cylindrical, often branching at the base. Leaves are alternate, heart-shaped, and have a rough texture. The inflorescence is a terminal cluster of small green flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing several yellowish seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cassava can be distinguished by its stout, branching stems with rough, waxy bark. Its alternate, ovate leaves have a pointed tip and a heart-shaped base. The plant produces clusters of small, greenish-yellow flowers, and its roots are typically swollen and starchy, with a whitish or yellowish interior.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cassava is native to South America, particularly the Amazon basin. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates with high humidity and warm temperatures. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically grown at low altitudes, though it can tolerate some variations in elevation.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cassava typically blooms 6-8 months after planting, with flowers appearing in clusters. Seed formation occurs 2-3 months after blooming. Harvesting begins 8-10 months post-planting, when leaves turn yellow and stems become fibrous, indicating maturity for root collection.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cassava, or Manihot esculenta, is closely related to species within the genus Manihot. It shares a close phylogenetic relationship with other plants in the Euphorbiaceae family. These relationships are defined by shared morphological and genetic traits, which help classify and distinguish Cassava from related species.
