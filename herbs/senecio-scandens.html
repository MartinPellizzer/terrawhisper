Senecio scandens

What's the taxonomical classification of Senecio scandens?

Senecio scandens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is further categorized into the family Asteraceae, which is a prominent group of flowering plants. Within this family, it is defined by the genus Senecio and is specifically identified by its species name, scandens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Senecio scandens has a climbing or trailing growth habit characterized by slender, herbaceous stems that often possess a slightly pubescent or glabrous texture.

The leaves are typically arranged in an alternate pattern along the stem and can vary from lanceolate to ovate in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. Small, tubular flowers are produced in terminal or axillary clusters, featuring characteristic rayless heads typical of many species in the Asteraceae family. The reproductive structures include numerous small florets that emerge from a common receptacle surrounded by involucral bracts.

These morphological traits collectively enable the plant to scramble over other vegetation to reach sunlight.

        What are the herbal preparations of Senecio scandens?

        Senecio scandens is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical ointment depending on the intended therapeutic application. When used for internal purposes, the plant parts are typically boiled in water to extract active alkaloids. Infusions are also common for milder preparations where the delicate compounds are steeped in hot water rather than boiled. For external ailments, the plant material is often processed into a salve or oil to soothe skin irritations. These different forms allow for the specific concentration of phytochemicals required for various traditional uses.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Senecio scandens can cause severe liver damage, kidney failure, and death if ingested due to the presence of toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids. These chemical compounds are processed by the body into highly reactive metabolites that attack hepatic cells. Chronic exposure often leads to hepatic veno-occlusive disease, a condition where the small veins in the liver become blocked. Symptoms of poisoning may include nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain, which can progress to jaundice as the organ fails. Because the toxins accumulate over time, even small amounts consumed repeatedly can lead to irreversible systemic damage.
