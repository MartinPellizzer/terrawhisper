Senecio scandens

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Senecio scandens has a climbing or trailing growth habit characterized by slender, herbaceous stems that often possess a slightly pubescent or glabrous texture. The leaves are typically arranged in an alternate pattern along the stem and can vary from lanceolate to ovate in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. Small, tubular flowers are produced in terminal or axillary clusters, featuring characteristic rayless heads typical of many species in the Asteraceae family. The reproductive structures include numerous small florets that emerge from a common receptacle surrounded by involucral bracts. These morphological traits collectively enable the plant to scramble over other vegetation to reach sunlight.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of the tropical Andes, specifically within the mountain ranges of Ecuador, Peru, and Colombia. It thrives primarily in montane forests and páramo ecosystems where moisture levels remain consistently high. These specific habitats are often characterized by cool temperatures and frequent cloud cover. The species is restricted to these narrow elevational bands rather than being widespread across entire continents.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Senecio scandens can cause severe liver damage, kidney failure, and death if ingested due to the presence of toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids. These chemical compounds are processed by the body into highly reactive metabolites that attack hepatic cells. Chronic exposure often leads to hepatic veno-occlusive disease, a condition where the small veins in the liver become blocked. Symptoms of poisoning may include nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain, which can progress to jaundice as the organ fails. Because the toxins accumulate over time, even small amounts consumed repeatedly can lead to irreversible systemic damage.
