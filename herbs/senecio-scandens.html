Senecio scandens

What's the taxonomical classification of Senecio scandens?

Senecio scandens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is further categorized into the family Asteraceae, which is a prominent group of flowering plants. Within this family, it is defined by the genus Senecio and is specifically identified by its species name, scandens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Senecio scandens has a climbing or trailing growth habit characterized by slender, herbaceous stems that often possess a slightly pubescent or glabrous texture. The leaves are typically arranged in an alternate pattern along the stem and can vary from lanceolate to ovate in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. Small, tubular flowers are produced in terminal or axillary clusters, featuring characteristic rayless heads typical of many species in the Asteraceae family. The reproductive structures include numerous small florets that emerge from a common receptacle surrounded by involucral bracts. These morphological traits collectively enable the plant to scramble over other vegetation to reach sunlight.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of the tropical Andes, specifically within the mountain ranges of Ecuador, Peru, and Colombia. It thrives primarily in montane forests and páramo ecosystems where moisture levels remain consistently high. These specific habitats are often characterized by cool temperatures and frequent cloud cover. The species is restricted to these narrow elevational bands rather than being widespread across entire continents. Consequently, its distribution is highly fragmented due to the isolated nature of Andean mountain peaks.

What are the pharmacological activities of Senecio scandens?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its analgesic, anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, antioxidant, and antimicrobial activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce pain and suppress inflammatory responses in biological systems. Research indicates that certain extracts can inhibit tumor cell growth and protect cells from oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals. Furthermore, the plant exhibits the ability to combat various pathogenic microorganisms through its antimicrobial properties. These diverse therapeutic effects suggest that Senecio scandens could serve as a valuable source for developing new medicinal treatments.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Senecio scandens Buch.-Ham. have a long history in traditional Chinese medicine for treating ailments like bacterial diarrhea, enteritis, conjunctivitis, and respiratory tract infections. Extracts and compounds isolated from Senecio scandens demonstrate a wide spectrum of pharmacological activities, including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, anti-leptospirosis, hepatoprotective, anti-infusional, antioxidant, antiviral, antitumoral, analgesic, mutagenic, and toxicological activities. These pharmacological effects are attributed to the presence of various flavonoids, alkaloids, and phenolic acids, with adonifoline serving as one of the index ingredients. Due to the toxicity of the phenolic acids present in this medicinal plant, regulations vary, and the Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2010 does not have well-defined ranking criteria for its toxicity. It is proposed that the use of Senecio scandens be reevaluated using risk-benefit analysis and clinical data to ensure safe use.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal pyrrolizidine alkaloids jacozine N-oxide, senkirkine, jacobine, emiline, and senecionine N-oxide. These specific chemical compounds are secondary metabolites produced by the plant for various biological functions. Researchers study these alkaloids because they often exhibit complex pharmacological activities in living organisms. The presence of such diverse compounds contributes to the overall chemical profile and therapeutic potential of the species. Understanding the concentrations of these substances is essential for determining the plant's medicinal properties and toxicity levels.

As discussed in a study, the aqueous extract of Senecio scandens contains a total of nine toxic and two non-toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids. Among these, six alkaloids were unequivocally characterized, including neoplatyphylline, senecionine, senecionine N-oxide, seneciphylline, seneciphylliine N-oxide, and senkirkine. Other alkaloids were tentatively assigned as jacobine, jacozine N-oxide (or erucifoline N-oxide), 7-tigloylplatynecine, usaramine, and an isomer of yamataimine. The estimated total content of toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids in Senecio scandens is 10.82 microg/g herb. This level is significantly higher than the limit of >= 1 microg/g herb recommended by Belgium and Germany for clinical use.
