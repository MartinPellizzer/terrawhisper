Botanical Identification of Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis)

To identify Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Elderberry, or Sambucus canadensis, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Dipsacales, family Caprifoliaceae, genus Sambucus, and species Sambucus canadensis. These classifications help identify its botanical relationships and place it within the broader plant kingdom.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Sambucus canadensis is the scientific name for Elderberry. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Common synonyms include Sambucus nigra var. canadensis and Sambucus americana. It is also known by regional names like American Elder and Black Elderberry. Historically, it has been referred to as Elder, Black Elder, and Wild Elder.

Morphological Characteristics

Sambucus canadensis is a deciduous shrub or small tree with a spreading growth habit. It has a deep, fibrous root system that helps stabilize soil. Its stems are stout, branching, and covered with rough, grayish bark. Leaves are compound, odd-pinnate, with 5-7 oval leaflets. The inflorescence is a flat, clustered umbel of small white flowers. The fruit is a dark purple to black drupe containing 1-3 seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Elderberry can be distinguished by its compound leaves with 5-7 leaflets, white clustered flowers, and purple-black berries. It is often confused with other Sambucus species, but its leaf shape, flower structure, and berry color are key identifiers. Always confirm with other features like stem texture and habitat.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Sambucus canadensis is native to eastern North America, particularly the United States and Canada. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and is commonly found in woodlands, streambanks, and thickets. It grows at elevations ranging from lowlands to around 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Elderberry blooms in early to mid-summer, typically between June and July, producing clusters of white to pale yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, with berries ripening from August to October. For identification, harvest timing is crucial, as berries are most identifiable and useful in late summer to early fall.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Sambucus canadensis is closely related to other species in the Sambucus genus. It shares key characteristics with Sambucus nigra and Sambucus racemosa. Within the Caprifoliaceae family, it exhibits traits like opposite leaves and umbellate flowers, which help distinguish it from other genera. Its phylogenetic placement reflects its evolutionary ties to these related species.
