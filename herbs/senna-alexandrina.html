What to know about Senna alexandrina (cassia) before using it medicinally

Cassia, scientifically known as Senna alexandrina, is a herb that has been revered for its impressive health benefits, which include improving digestion, reducing inflammation, and boosting immunity.

In addition to its medicinal properties, cassia is also a popular spice in many cuisines around the world, particularly in Asian and Middle Eastern cooking, where it adds depth and warmth to curries, stews, and braises. As a versatile herb, cassia is also grown for ornamental purposes, with its delicate yellow flowers and feathery foliage adding a touch of elegance to gardens and floral arrangements. From a botanical standpoint, cassia is a member of the legume family and is characterized by its small, hairy leaves and stems that can grow up to 3 feet tall.

With its rich history dating back thousands of years, cassia has been mentioned in ancient texts such as the Egyptian Ebers Papyrus and the Indian Ayurvedic treatise, Charaka Samhita, making it a herb with both modern and timeless appeal.

What are the medicinal properties of Senna alexandrina?

Senna alexandrina has several medicinal properties, such as its stimulant laxative effect, which is used to treat constipation and other bowel disorders.

The plant's sennosides A and B are responsible for this property, as they increase the movement of water into the intestine and stimulate the muscles in the intestinal wall to contract, leading to a bowel movement. This property has been widely used to treat chronic constipation, especially in individuals with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), diverticulitis, and hemorrhoids. Additionally, Senna alexandrina has anti-inflammatory properties, which may contribute to its effectiveness in reducing inflammation and pain associated with digestive disorders.

Its antimicrobial properties have also been reported, showing activity against various bacterial, fungal, and protozoan species, making it a potential treatment option for infections such as urinary tract infections, diarrhea, and dysentery.
