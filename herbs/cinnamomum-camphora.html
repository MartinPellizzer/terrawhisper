Cinnamomum camphora

What's the taxonomical classification of Cinnamomum camphora?

Cinnamomum camphorum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls under the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Laurales. This plant is a member of the family Lauraceae, situated within the genus Cinnamomum, and is specifically identified by its species name, camphorum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cinnamomum camphora has evergreen leaves that are opposite, elliptical to lanceolate in shape, and possess a distinctively glossy, dark green upper surface with a paler underside. The leaf margins are typically entire or slightly undulate, and the texture feels somewhat leathery to the touch. Its bark is characteristically smooth and greyish when young, but it becomes deeply fissured and scaly as the tree matures. The plant produces small, inconspicuous yellowish-green flowers arranged in axillary panicles. Following pollination, it develops small, globose drupes that transition from green to a bright red color when ripe.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning from southern China and Taiwan through Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia. It flourishes in the humid climates of the Indochina peninsula and the various archipelagos of the Malay Archipelago. Its natural range is primarily concentrated within low-altitude rainforests and moist evergreen forests. Due to its historical use in traditional medicine and fragrance, it has been introduced to other tropical territories around the world. This widespread distribution is supported by the species' ability to thrive in high humidity and warm temperatures.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds borneol, cineole, limonene, linalool, and safrole. These chemical constituents contribute to the plant's distinct aromatic profile and therapeutic potential. Borneol and cineole are frequently recognized for their ability to act as decongestants and anti-inflammatory agents. The presence of limonene and linalool provides additional antioxidant and sedative properties that may help soothe the nervous system. Finally, safrole plays a specific role in the plant's chemical composition, though its concentration is carefully regulated due to its potency.

What health problems is this plant used for?

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an essential oil, tincture, infusion, or decoction for various medicinal uses. The essential oil is extracted through steam distillation of the leaves to capture its intense aromatic properties. A tincture made from alcohol extraction provides a concentrated way to preserve the plant's active compounds for long-term use. For milder applications, an infusion involves steeping the leaves in hot water to release delicate constituents. A decoction is used when boiling the tougher plant parts in water to ensure a more robust extraction of its medicinal strength.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a wide range of adverse reactions including skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, allergic reaction, dizziness, confusion, seizures, stomach pain, and headache. Topical application of its oils may lead to significant skin irritation or an intense allergic reaction upon contact. Ingesting components of the plant often triggers gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, and severe stomach pain. Neurological complications can also arise, manifesting as a headache, dizziness, or mental confusion. In extreme cases of toxicity, the central nervous system may be affected enough to cause seizures.
