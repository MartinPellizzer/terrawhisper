Tetrapleura tetraptera

What's the taxonomical classification of Tetrapleura tetraptera?

Tetrapleura tetraptera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is organized under the order Fabales and falls within the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Tetrapleura, with its specific designation being the species tetraptera.

These winged fruits are often hard and woody in texture when fully mature. What are the pharmacological activities of Tetrapleura tetraptera?

This plant has demonstrated diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, anti-diabetic, anti-oxidant, and anti-cancer properties. Its phytochemical constituents work to neutralize free radicals, which provides significant anti-oxidant support to cellular structures. These bioactive compounds also exhibit the ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms through anti-microbial action. Furthermore, research indicates that extracts can help regulate blood glucose levels and reduce systemic inflammation. Recent studies have even explored the potential of its chemical profile to disrupt the proliferation of malignant cells for anti-cancer applications.

As discussed in a study, the hydro-alcoholic extracts of Tetrapleura tetraptera were among 6 extracts selected from 11 Cameroonian spices for further investigation. These extracts exhibited antioxidant properties in a cell-free system and reduced H2O2-induced ROS generation in gastric epithelial cells. The study focused on the anti-inflammatory activity of the plant following preliminary screening on TNFα-induced NF-κB driven transcription. Tetrapleura tetraptera extracts reduced the expression of NF-κB-dependent pro-inflammatory mediators including IL-8 and IL-6. Additionally, the plant extracts reduced the expression of enzymes such as PTGS2 (COX-2) without affecting PTGS1 (COX-1).

Quercetin and rutin act as powerful flavonoids that can help reduce inflammation and support cellular health. Additionally, betulinic acid provides unique medicinal qualities that are frequently studied for their biological activity.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic seed extract of Tetrapleura tetraptera, known as TTMSE, was evaluated for various medicinal properties. This extract exhibited concentration-dependent AChE inhibitory activity with a p-value of < 0.05 to 0.001. At a concentration of 200 µg/mL, the AChE inhibitory activity for TTMSE was measured at approximately 26%. Furthermore, the IC50 for this extract was determined to be 495.4 µg/mL.
