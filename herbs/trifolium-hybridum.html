Trifolium hybridum

What's the taxonomical classification of Trifolium hybridum?

Trifolium hybridum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent, it falls into the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by its placement in the genus Trifolium, with the specific epithet being hybridum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Trifolium hybridum has trifoliate leaves consisting of three elliptical to obovate leaflets that are typically bright green with indistinct or pale markings. The stems are slender and often procumbent or ascending, frequently displaying a fine pubescence along their length. Its inflorescences are compact, globular heads containing numerous small, pinkish to purplish pea-like flowers. Each individual flower is attached to the head by a short pedicel and features a standard, wing, and keel structure characteristic of the Fabaceae family. The plant also produces small, legume-style pods that develop from the fertilized ovaries after pollination.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, primarily spanning across southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of Western Asia. Its natural range includes coastal areas and dry, sunny habitats throughout the Iberian Peninsula and the Italian coast. Many populations thrive in disturbed soils and meadows where the climate remains temperate and warm. Some specimens have also been introduced to other subtropical climates due to human activity. The species tends to avoid high-altitude alpine zones or extremely cold northern latitudes.

What are the pharmacological activities of Trifolium hybridum?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anthelmintic, antibiotic, and antioxidant properties. Its anthelmintic potential allows it to effectively combat various parasitic worms within a host organism. The plant also exhibits antibiotic capabilities that can help inhibit the growth of certain pathogenic bacteria. Furthermore, its antioxidant actions assist in neutralizing harmful free radicals to prevent cellular damage. These diverse therapeutic qualities make it a subject of interest for medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, Trifolium hybridum L. is a forage legume that contains high levels of bioactive compounds. These compounds include benzoic acid, myricetin, p-coumaric acid, rhamnetin, and rosmarinic acid. Among the 32 compounds identified with known antimicrobial/anthelmintiic properties, these substances can target multiple aspects of pathogen physiology and metabolism. Specifically, these identified compounds have been shown to inhibit quorum sensing in bacteria and egg viability in nematodes. The analysis was part of a larger study involving 17 plant species and 45 biomass samples.

As discussed in a study, the chemical profiles of Trifolium hybridum extracts contain three or four groups of (poly)phenolic compounds including phenolic acids, clovamides, isoflavones, and other flavonoids. These plant extracts were evaluated at concentrations ranging from 1.5-50.0 μg/ml. The antioxidant properties were assessed against 100 μM peroxynitrite-induced oxidative stress in vitro. The study noted that total phenolic concentrations in Trifolium extracts varied between 11.30 and 52.55 mg/g of dry mass. The research focused on biomarkers such as thiol groups, 3-nitrotyrosine, lipid hydroperoxides, and thiobarbituric acid-reactive substances (TBARS).
