Erigeron multiradiatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Erigeron multiradiatus?

Erigeron multiradiatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic descent, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the hierarchy, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Within this family, it is categorized under the genus Erigeron, ultimately reaching its specific identification as the species multiradiatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Erigeron multiradiatus has a perennial growth habit characterized by a woody caudex, basal leaves that are typically spatulate or oblanceolate with entire margins, and numerous narrow, linear leaves distributed along its stems.

The inflorescence consists of small heads arranged in corymbiform clusters, each featuring numerous white to pale lavender ray florets surrounding a central disc of yellow tubular florets. The involucral bracts are arranged in several overlapping series and are often tipped with distinct, darkened points. Stems are generally erect and can vary in pubescence, ranging from glabrous to being covered in fine, spreading hairs.

The overall structure is compact, reflecting its adaptation to specific montane or rocky environments.

        Erigeron multiradiatus is prepared as an infusion, a decoction, or a topical tincture for various medicinal applications.         What side-effects this plant can have?

        Erigeron multiradiatus can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested by humans or animals. While specific clinical studies on this particular species are limited, many plants in the Asteraceae family contain sesquiterpene lactones that act as contact allergens. Individuals with sensitive skin may develop redness, itching, or a rash upon direct contact with the foliage. If consumed accidentally, it might trigger nausea or stomach upset in sensitive organisms. Monitoring for these localized or systemic reactions is recommended when handling the plant.
