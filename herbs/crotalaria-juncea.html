Crotalaria juncea

What's the taxonomical classification of Crotalaria juncea?

Crotalaria juncea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Fabales. This plant is a member of the family Fabaceae, which places it within the genus Crotalaria, specifically identified by the species name juncea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crotalaria juncea has an erect, herbaceous stem that grows up to 3 to 4 meters tall, characterized by its cylindrical shape and a dense covering of fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are alternate and pinnately compound, typically featuring 4 to 8 pairs of oblong leaflets with a distinctively hairy texture. Its inflorescence consists of small, bright yellow flowers that are borne in axillary racemes. The plant produces distinctive legume pods that are inflated, bladder-like, and contain several hard, dark seeds. These structures allow the plant to function as a robust, fast-growing cover crop in various soil types.

What are the pharmacological activities of Crotalaria juncea?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities, specifically functioning as an effective antidyslipidemic and antioxidant agent. Its antioxidant properties allow it to combat oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals within biological systems. Additionally, the antidyslipidemic effects suggest a capacity to regulate lipid profiles and manage cholesterol levels in the body. These combined mechanisms contribute to the overall therapeutic potential of the species in managing metabolic health. Research into these specific biochemical pathways continues to highlight its medicinal importance.

As discussed in a study, an unusual amino acid known as 2-amino-5-hydroxyhexanoic acid (1) was isolated from the seeds of Crotalaria juncea. This 2-amino-5-hydroxyhexanoic acid (1) demonstrated dose dependent lipid lowering activity in in vivo experiments and also exhibited good in vitro antioxidant activity. Furthermore, a cyclized compound called 3-amino-6-methyltetrahydro-2H-pyran-2-one (2) was identified. This compound (2) showed a better lipid lowering and antioxidant profile than the parent compound 1.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe organ damage and systemic illness due to the presence of toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids in its tissues. Ingestion of these compounds often leads to acute gastrointestinal distress, including symptoms such as nausea and vomiting. Some individuals may also experience pyretic effects characterized by a sudden fever. Prolonged exposure to these alkaloids is particularly dangerous because they can lead to irreversible liver damage. Therefore, caution must be exercised to avoid accidental consumption of any part of the plant.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Toxicity from pyrrolizidine alkaloids
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Pyretic effects (fever)
