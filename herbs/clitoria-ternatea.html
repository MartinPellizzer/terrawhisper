Butterfly Pea (Clitoria ternatea): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Butterfly Pea (Clitoria Ternatea)?

Butterfly Pea (Clitoria ternatea) is a flowering plant native to Southeast Asia, widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its vibrant blue flowers and various therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Butterfly pea is Clitoria ternatea. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Clitoria, and species Clitoria ternatea. Common names for this plant include Butterfly pea in English, while in Thai it is known as Kham puay. In Vietnamese, it is referred to as Bướm tím. Synonyms for this plant include Clitoria major as a botanical synonym and various regional names such as Blue pea in some parts of Southeast Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Butterfly pea is an erect annual herb that typically grows to a height of 30-100 cm with a fibrous root system.

Its leaves are trifoliate, consisting of three oval to elliptic leaflets with serrated edges. The flowers are showy, butterfly-like, with a distinctive blue or purple hue, often with a yellow throat, and emit a mild, sweet fragrance. The petals are velvety in texture and have a slightly waxy surface.

The plant has a short, branching stem covered in fine hairs.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Butterfly pea is native to Southeast Asia, particularly regions such as India, Indonesia, Malaysia, and the Philippines.

It is commonly found in tropical and subtropical areas, extending into parts of Africa and the Pacific Islands. The plant thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil and full sunlight. It prefers areas with high rainfall and is often found in open fields, along roadsides, and in disturbed habitats.

Its natural range includes regions with moderate to high altitudes, typically between sea level and 1,500 meters.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in butterfly pea are the flowers, leaves, and seeds.

The flowers are most commonly utilized for their vibrant color and medicinal properties. They are harvested when fully open to ensure maximum potency. Leaves are often used in traditional remedies for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects.

Seeds are occasionally employed for their nutritional content and potential therapeutic benefits.

Phytochemical Composition

Butterfly Pea (Clitoria ternatea) contains a variety of active compounds, including flavonoids, alkaloids, and saponins, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Secondary metabolites such as kaempferol, quercetin, and isorhamnetin are prominent in the plant and are responsible for its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. These compounds are often identified through chemical markers like total polyphenol content and antioxidant activity measurements. The presence of these bioactive substances supports its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Chemical markers also help in standardizing the quality and potency of Butterfly Pea-based formulations.

These properties make it a subject of interest in herbal medicine for various therapeutic applications.

Therapeutic Uses

The therapeutic uses of Butterfly Pea (Clitoria ternatea) are well-documented in traditional herbal medicine, particularly for its potential to support cognitive function and alleviate symptoms of anxiety and depression.

It is commonly used to treat conditions such as memory loss, insomnia, and mood disorders due to its rich content of bioactive compounds like flavonoids and antioxidants. The plant is also employed in the management of inflammation and digestive issues, often prepared as a tea or tincture. Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Butterfly Pea involve various forms in which the plant is utilized.

It is commonly used as a powder, where the dried leaves are ground into a fine powder for internal consumption. The leaves can also be brewed as a tea by steeping them in hot water, providing a natural source of antioxidants. Additionally, the flowers are sometimes used to make infusions or extracts, which are applied topically or ingested.

The plant is occasionally processed into capsules or tablets for convenience in herbal supplementation.

It is generally recommended to take the herb once or twice daily, with a gap of several hours between doses. The duration of use should not exceed 8 weeks unless under the supervision of a qualified healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Butterfly Pea is generally considered safe for most adults, but it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Side effects are typically mild and may include upset stomach, diarrhea, or skin irritation. People with known allergies to plants in the legume family should avoid Butterfly Pea, as it may trigger an allergic response. It is contraindicated for individuals with hormone-sensitive conditions due to its potential estrogenic properties.

Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid Butterfly Pea due to insufficient safety data and potential risks to fetal or infant development.

Drug interactions with Butterfly Pea (Clitoria ternatea) can affect the efficacy and safety of both herbal and pharmaceutical treatments.

It may interact with medications such as anticoagulants, antidepressants, and antihypertensives by altering their metabolic pathways in the liver. These interactions can either enhance or reduce the intended effects of the drugs, potentially leading to adverse outcomes. While limited research exists on interactions with supplements, it is advisable to avoid combining Butterfly Pea with high-dose vitamin B12 or iron supplements due to potential absorption interference.

Always consult a healthcare provider before using Butterfly Pea alongside any medication or supplement.

Toxicity and Precautions

When used in recommended doses, Butterfly Pea is generally considered safe, but toxicity can occur with excessive intake.

The toxic dose varies depending on individual factors, but consuming large quantities of the plant or its extracts may lead to adverse effects. Long-term safety data is limited, and prolonged use may pose unknown risks, particularly for individuals with pre-existing health conditions. Poisoning risks are higher in children and pets, who may accidentally ingest the plant in large amounts.

It is important to consult a healthcare provider before using Butterfly Pea, especially in therapeutic doses or for extended periods.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Butterfly Pea (Clitoria ternatea) is typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions where it thrives in warm, humid climates.

It requires well-drained, loamy soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH for optimal growth. Full sunlight is essential for vigorous growth and flowering, though some afternoon shade may be beneficial in extremely hot climates. Regular watering is necessary, especially during dry periods, but the plant should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. Propagation is most commonly done through seeds or stem cuttings, with seeds requiring stratification to break dormancy. The plant reaches maturity in about 6 to 8 months and is best harvested when the pods are dry and the seeds inside are fully developed.

Harvesting involves cutting the mature pods and allowing them to dry further before collecting the seeds.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and Extraction of Butterfly Pea involves several key steps to obtain its active compounds.

The first step is drying the harvested flowers to reduce moisture content and prevent spoilage. Once dried, the flowers are ground into a fine powder to increase surface area for efficient extraction. Extraction methods typically involve using solvents like ethanol or water to dissolve bioactive components such as anthocyanins and flavonoids.

Distillation may be employed to concentrate the extracted compounds and remove residual solvents, ensuring a pure final product.

Storage and Shelf Life

The storage and shelf life of Butterfly Pea (Clitoria ternatea) depend on proper handling and environmental conditions.

The shelf life of dried Butterfly Pea flowers is typically 6 to 12 months when stored correctly. To maximize longevity, keep the product in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. Exposure to light can degrade the color and potency of the flowers.

Moisture control is essential to prevent mold growth and maintain quality.
