Botanical Identification of Salvia (Salvia officinalis)

To identify Salvia (Salvia officinalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Salvia (Salvia officinalis) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Salvia officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Salvia, and species Salvia officinalis. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the mint family, highlighting its botanical significance in herbal medicine.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Salvia is Salvia officinalis, first described by the botanist Carl von Linne in 1753. This name reflects its historical use in medicine. Common names include common sage, garden sage, and culinary sage, while regional names vary across cultures. It is also known by historical names such as Salvia viridis and Salvia herba.

Morphological Characteristics

Salvia officinalis is a perennial herb with a woody base and upright, branched stems. Its leaves are lance-shaped, gray-green, and aromatic. The plant produces upright flower clusters with violet to purple flowers, and it forms small, dry, three-lobed fruits containing tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Salvia officinalis can be distinguished by its opposite, ovate leaves with serrated edges and a grayish-white underside. It has square, purple flower clusters and a strong, herbal scent. It is often confused with other sage species, but its leaf shape and flower structure are key differentiators.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Salvia officinalis is native to the Mediterranean region, including southern Europe and parts of North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with mild, wet winters and warm, dry summers. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and typically grows at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Salvia officinalis blooms in late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to September. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest the leaves is during early summer when the plant is in active growth and the leaves are young and aromatic.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Salvia officinalis belongs to the Lamiaceae family and is closely related to other members of the Salvia genus. It shares key traits such as square stems and opposite leaves with its closest relatives. These relationships help in distinguishing it from similar species within the same family. Its phylogenetic position highlights its evolutionary ties to other medicinal plants.
