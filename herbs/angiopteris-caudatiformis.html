What's the taxonomical classification of Angiopteris caudatiformis?

Angiopteris caudatiformis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida, it is situated under the subclass Marattiidae and falls within the order Marattiales. The plant is further categorized into the family Marattiaceae, which contains the genus Angiopteris, ultimately leading to its specific identification as the species caudatiformis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angiopteris caudatiformis has a creeping rhizome structure with slender, dichotomously branching stems that terminate in specialized, tail-like fertile sporangiophores. These fertile branches are characterized by a distinctively tapered or caudate shape, which distinguishes them from the more broadly expanded fertile structures of related species. The sterile vegetative branches are typically more delicate and provide the photosynthetic surface for the plant. Spores are produced within the sporangia located along these specialized stalks to facilitate reproduction. This unique morphology reflects a highly specialized reproductive strategy within the Ophioglossaceae family.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Angiopterlactones A, Angiopterlactones B, osmundalactone, and osmundalin. These specific lactones and glycosides are characteristic secondary metabolites found within the genus. Researchers study these molecules to understand their potential bioactive properties and chemical signaling roles. The presence of these compounds often defines the unique phytochemical profile of the species. Detailed analysis of these substances provides insight into the plant's overall therapeutic potential.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", the rhizome of Angiopteris caudatiformis contains two unique lactones, Angiopterlactones A (1) and B (2), as well as three known lactones, osmundalactone (3), osmundalin (4), and 3,5-dihydroxy-gamma-caprolactone (5). The structure of compound 2 was confirmed by X-ray crystallography, while the structures of 1 and 2 were determined using NMR and MS methods. Compound 1 demonstrated slight cytotoxicity against HeLa cells with an IC50 value of 68.8 microM. Additionally, compounds 3 and 4 exhibited moderate insect antifeeding activity against Plutella xylostella and Heliothis virescens.
