Salvia rosmarinus

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia rosmarinus?

Salvia rosmarininus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of botanical classification, it falls under the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Salvia, and its specific species designation, rosmarininus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia rosmarinus has woody, perennial stems that support narrow, lanceolate leaves which are dark green on the upper surface and pale or pubescent underneath. The leaves are arranged oppositely along the branches and possess revolute margins that curve slightly downward. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves, typically displaying a shade of blue or violet. These inflorescences are often organized into verticillasters, which are false whorls positioned along the flowering stalks. The plant's overall structure is bushy and compact, reflecting its adaptation to Mediterranean environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, specifically spanning across parts of southern Europe, North Africa, and Western Asia. It thrives in coastal areas where the climate remains relatively mild and stable throughout the year. The species is most frequently found in rocky terrains and scrublands known as garrigue or maquis. Because it prefers well-drained soils and sunny exposures, its natural range is restricted to specific microclimates within these Mediterranean territories. It has also been introduced to other temperate regions globally for ornamental and medicinal purposes.

Salvie rosmarinus contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its aromatic aerial parts, specifically the leaves and the flowering stems. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains rosmarinic acid, carnosic acid, carnosol, apigenin- and luteolin-derivatives, and camphor as its primary medicinal compounds. Rosmarinic acid and carnosic acid serve as potent antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress. The diterpenes carnosol and camphor contribute to the plant's antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the presence of apigenin- and luteolin-derivatives supports neurological health and provides further bioactive benefits. These diverse chemical constituents work together to create a complex therapeutic profile.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Salvía rosmarinus targets various enzymes and receptors including cyclooxygenase-1 and -2, lipoxygenase, acetylcholinesterase, and various muscarinic acetylcholine receptors. Its primary bioactive compounds, such as rosmarinic acid and carnosic acid, inhibit the cyclooxygenase pathway to reduce pro-inflammatory prostaglandin synthesis. These phytochemicals also act as potent antioxidants by scavenging free radicals and modulating Nrf2 signaling pathways to enhance cellular defense. Furthermore, the inhibition of acetylcholinesterase by its diterpenes provides a mechanism for neuroprotective and cognitive-enhancing effects. Through these diverse protein interactions, the plant exerts significant anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, and antimicrobial properties.

Salvies rosmarinus has therapeutic actions that include being a digestive stimulant, carminative, cephalic, nervine, antiseptic, and astringent. What health problems are treated using this plant?

This plant is used for treating various gastric disorders including gastritis, peptic ulcer, and general stomach troubles. Its medicinal properties help soothe the lining of the digestive tract to alleviate discomfort. Some traditional applications also focus on managing the symptoms associated with gastric cancer. By addressing these specific stomach troubles, the plant serves as a natural remedy for internal inflammation. This herbal approach provides a way to manage a wide range of digestive ailments.

As discussed in a study, Salvia rosmarinus was among fifteen edible and medicinal herbs screened for anti-ureaase activity to combat gastric diseases. The screening process involved testing the plant at three different concentrations, specifically 25μg/ml, 50μg/ml, and 75μg/ml. The research results indicated significant outcomes for ureaase inhibitory activity for all tested medicinal plants, including Salvia rosmarinus. This study aimed to explore natural flora to decrease the prevalence of gastric diseases caused by H. pylori bacteria.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be processed into an infusion for mild tea, a concentrated tincture for medicinal use, an essential oil for aromatherapy, a decoction for tougher parts, and steam inhalation for respiratory relief. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause seizures if consumed in high doses due to its significant camphor content. Other potential side effects include nausea, dizziness, and vomiting. Individuals may also experience gastric irritation following ingestion. Furthermore, some people might suffer from an allergic reaction to the plant.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Seizures (in high doses/camphor content)
  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Vomiting
  • Gastric irritation
  • Allergic reaction
