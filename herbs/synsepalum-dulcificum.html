Synsepalum dulcificum

What's the taxonomical classification of Synsepalum dulcificum?

Synsepalum dulcificum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Ericales and is a member of the family Sapotaeceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Synsepalum, with its specific designation being the species dulcificum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Synsepalum dulcificum has woody stems, simple leaves with entire margins, and distinctive fruit structures that define its physical appearance. The leaves are typically arranged in an opposite pattern and possess a coriaceous texture. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils to eventually develop into large, fleshy fruits. These fruits are characterized by a thick pericarp and contain numerous seeds embedded within a sweet pulp. The overall growth habit is that of a climbing shrub or woody vine within the Sapindaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of West Africa, specifically ranging across countries such as Nigeria, Cameroon, and Gabon. It thrives primarily in lowland equatorial forests where moisture and shade are abundant. Within these habitats, it is often found growing in the understory layer of dense vegetation. The species follows a distribution pattern typical of many members of the Sapindaceae family in the Guineo-Congolian forest block. Localized populations are concentrated in areas with high annual rainfall and stable humid conditions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Synsepalum dulcificum?

This plant has demonstrated potent pharmacological activities including anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and α-glucosidase inhibitory properties. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize harmful free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative stress and damage. The plant also exhibits significant anti-inflammatory effects that can mitigate various inflammatory responses in the body. Furthermore, its antimicrobial activity allows it to combat diverse pathogenic microorganisms effectively. Through its α-glucosidase inhibitory activity, the plant aids in managing blood glucose levels by slowing down carbohydrate digestion.

According to a study published by "Plants (Basel, Switzerland)", Synsepalum dulcificum has a longstanding history as a folk remedy for diabetes. The plant contains a total of 2544 secondary metabolites, which are primarily composed of flavonoids and triterpenes. These metabolites are categorized into thirteen distinct compound classes. Research focused on investigating the anti-diabetic mechanisms and potential pharmacological components of the plant. Additionally, the α-glucosidase inhibitory activity of potential active components was evaluated through in vitro experiments.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds miraculin, syringaresinol, N-cis-caffeoyltyramine, N-cis-feruloyl-tyramine, and dihydro-feruloyl-5-methoxytyramine. Miraculin is a unique protein known for its ability to temporarily alter taste perceptions by making sour foods taste sweet. The presence of lignans like syringaresinol suggests potential antioxidant and health-promoting properties. Additionally, the plant features various phenylpropanoid amides such as N-cis-caffeoyltyramine and N-cis-feruloyl-tyramine. The compound dihydro-feruloyl-5-methoxytyramine further contributes to the complex chemical profile of this species. These diverse secondary metabolites highlight the pharmacological importance of Synsepalum dulcificum.

According to a study published by "Heliyon", Synsepalum dulcificum contains the glycoprotein miraculin, which is responsible for its unique taste-modifying property. The plant also includes alkaloids such as dihydro-feruloyl-5-methoxytyramine, N-cis-caffeoyltyramine, and N-cis-feruloyl-tyramine. Other identified pharmacological compounds include lignins like +-syringaresinol and +-epi-syringaresinol, as well as phytosterols, triterpenoids, phenolic acids, flavonoids, and amino acids. Scientific investigations have revealed properties such as antidiaabetic, blood cholesterol-lowering, anti-hyperuricaemia, antioxidant, anticonvulsant, and anticancer effects. Furthermore, subacute administration of the plant extract up to 200 mg/kg was not found to be toxic in rats.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing cancer, colorectal cancer, diabetes, inflammation, and insulin resistance. Its bioactive compounds work to suppress the growth of malignant cells within the body. Research suggests it may also help mitigate inflammation by regulating various immune responses. Additionally, the plant shows promise in improving metabolic health by addressing insulin resistance. These properties collectively assist in the stabilization of blood glucose levels for individuals with diabetes.

According to a study published by "International journal of molecular sciences", Synsepalum dulcificum has been shown to ameliorate insulin resistance in a preclinical model of diabetes induced by a chow diet enriched in fructose. Different polar extracts of the fruit have been described to inhibit cell proliferation and transformation of cancer cell lines in vitro. The non-polar supercritical extracts from the seed exhibit the highest effects on the inhibition of bioenergetics of colorectal (CRC) cancer cells. At the molecular level, these effects relate to the inhibition of main drivers of de novo lipogenesis, such as the sterol regulatory element binding transcription factor (SREBF1) and downstream molecular targets fatty acid synthase (FASN) and stearoyl coenzyme desaturase 1 (SCD1). Due to these findings, supercritical extracts from the seed are being researched to be proposed as co-adjuvants in the treatment of cancer.
