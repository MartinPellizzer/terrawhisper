Synsepalum dulcificum

What's the taxonomical classification of Synsepalum dulcificum?

Synsepalum dulcificum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Ericales and is a member of the family Sapotaeceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Synsepalum, with its specific designation being the species dulcificum.

What are the therapeutic actions of Synsepalum dulcificum?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat intestinal parasites, stomach pains, malaria, and various skin infections. Local healers often prepare decoctions from the bark or leaves to combat gastrointestinal distress and worms. The plant's properties are also applied topically to soothe inflammation and promote healing in wounded skin. Some traditional practices involve using the botanical extracts to manage symptoms associated with febrile illnesses. These medicinal applications reflect the long-standing ethnobotanical importance of the species in its native regions.

What are the herbal preparations of Synsepalum dulcificum?

Synsepalum dulcificum is prepared as a decoction or infusion using the dried leaves and stems of the plant. These aqueous extracts are commonly consumed to harness the medicinal properties stored within the botanical material. The process involves boiling the plant parts in water to ensure the thorough extraction of active compounds. Some traditional practitioners may also incorporate the plant into topical poultices for external applications. This versatility allows the herb to be utilized for both internal consumption and localized skin treatments.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Synsepalum dulcificum can cause potential adverse reactions such as gastrointestinal distress, allergic skin irritation, or unexpected metabolic changes if consumed in improper quantities. While much of its traditional use focuses on its medicinal properties, the specific toxicity levels for humans remain insufficiently documented in modern clinical studies. Some users may experience nausea or stomach cramps after ingestion due to the plant's unique chemical constituents. It is also possible for individuals with sensitive constitutions to develop localized rashes upon direct contact with the foliage. Because scientific data is limited, caution should be exercised to avoid unpredictable systemic responses.
