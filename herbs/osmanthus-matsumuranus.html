Osmanthus matsumuranus

What's the taxonomical classification of Osmanthus matsumuranus?

Osmanthus matsumuranus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Oleaceae, falling specifically into the genus Osmanthus, and is identified by the species name matsumuranus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Osmanthus matsumuranus has small, leathery, evergreen leaves that are elliptical or lanceolate in shape with finely serrated margins and a glossy dark green upper surface.

The plant typically grows as a dense shrub or small tree reaching heights of several meters. Its flowers are tiny, white, and highly fragrant, blooming in clusters from the leaf axils. These blossoms are often followed by small, fleshy drupes that turn dark when ripe.

The overall structure is characterized by a compact, spreading growth habit with woody stems.

    What are the herbal preparations of Osmanthus matsumuranus?

    Osmanthus matsumuranus is prepared as a dried flower infusion, a sweetened syrup, or a fragrant culinary extract. The dried blossoms are frequently steeped in hot water to create a restorative tea that captures the plant's distinct aroma. In traditional applications, the petals may be macerated in alcohol to produce a tincture or infused into oils for topical use. These preparations often serve as natural flavorings for desserts and beverages due to their intense floral scent. The concentrated extracts are also used to create aromatic syrups that can be added to various medicinal or culinary mixtures.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Osmanthus matsumuranus can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested in large quantities. While the plant is often used for its fragrant flowers, direct contact with the leaves or skin may trigger redness or itching in sensitive individuals. Some people may experience hypersensitivity reactions characterized by swelling or rashes upon exposure to its pollen or oils. There is limited clinical data regarding long-term toxicity, so caution is advised when handling the plant. Consumption of non-food grade parts should be avoided to prevent potential nausea or stomach discomfort.
