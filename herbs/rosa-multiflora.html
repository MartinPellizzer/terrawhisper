Rosa multiflora

What's the taxonomical classification of Rosa multiflora?

Rosa multiflora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls into the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized under the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Rosa and the species multiflora.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rosa multiflora has a sprawling, arching growth habit characterized by long, slender canes that can reach lengths of up to several meters. These stems are heavily armed with sharp, curved prickles that aid in its climbing and scrambling movement through vegetation. The foliage consists of pinnately compound leaves typically featuring five to nine small, ovate leaflets with serrated margins. During its blooming period, the plant produces large, flat-topped clusters known as corymbs containing numerous small, white, five-petaled flowers. Following flowering, it develops small, fleshy red hips that serve as its primary fruit structure.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across China, Japan, and Korea. Since its introduction to North America in the late 19th century, it has spread aggressively throughout much of the United States and Canada. It thrives in diverse environments ranging from coastal regions to inland woodlands and disturbed sites. The species has also established significant populations in various parts of Europe and other temperate zones globally. Its ability to colonize various soil types and climates allows it to occupy a vast geographical range.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Rosa multiflora contains medicinal compounds primarily found in its rose hips, flowers, and leaves. The bright red rose hips are highly valued for their exceptional concentration of vitamin C and antioxidants. These fruits are often processed into teas, syrups, or jellies to support immune function and skin health. Additionally, the fragrant flowers can be used in herbal infusions to soothe nerves and alleviate mild anxiety. The leaves are sometimes incorporated into topical preparations or decoctions to assist with digestive or inflammatory issues.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rosa multiflora?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and purgative properties. The presence of bioactive compounds allows it to alleviate pain and reduce swelling in various biological models. Its antioxidant capacity helps mitigate oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals within the body. Additionally, the plant exhibits antimicrobial effects that can inhibit the growth of certain pathogens. Finally, it possesses purgative qualities that can be utilized to stimulate bowel movements.

As discussed in a study, Rosae Multiflorae fructus possesses potent antioxidative, analgesic, and anti-inflamatory properties. The extract, known as RMFE, contains 4 active components including ellagic acid, hyperoside, isoquercitrin, and miquelianiin. When administered orally, RMFE inhibited the accumulation of eosinophils, goblet cells, and mast cells while ameliorating the swelling of the nasal epithelium, NALT, and lung tissue. The treatment suppressed Th2-related cytokines such as IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13 in NALF, NALT, and splenocytes, while the Th1-associated cytokine IL-12 was up-regulated. These findings suggest RMFE has therapeutic potential for treating AR by modulating Th1/Th2 responses.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 3'-methoxy-isoquercitrin, hyperin, isoquercitrin, multiflorin A, and quercitrin. These specific flavonoids are known for their significant biological activities and antioxidant properties. Research suggests that these molecules contribute to the plant's therapeutic potential in various health applications. The presence of these glycosides helps define the chemical profile used in pharmacological studies. Scientists study these individual constituents to understand how they interact with human cellular processes.

As discussed in a study, Rosa multiflora contains medicinal compounds within its dried achene or anthocarpous accessory fruits used to improve constipation. The plant is categorized into two chemotypes based on the presence or absence of the purgative component multiflorin A. Type I plants contain quercitrin, multinoside A, multiflorin B, and multinoside A acetate as major index compounds. Conversely, Type II contains hyperin, isoquercitrin, quercetin 3-O-glucuronide, and 3'-methoxy-isoquercitrin. While plants in Japan excluding Tsushima Island were all classified as Type I, both Type I and Type II were detected within Rosae fructus obtained from Rosa multiflora collected in South Korea and Tsushima Island, Japan.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, chronic pain, arthritis, osteoarthritis, and rheumatoid arthritis. Its medicinal properties are primarily harnessed to reduce swelling in the joints and soothe long-term discomfort. By addressing systemic inflammation, it can help alleviate the persistent ache associated with rheumatoid arthritis. It is also utilized to manage the degenerative effects of osteoarthritis to improve mobility. These therapeutic applications make it a relevant subject in the study of herbal remedies for joint-related ailments.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause physical injury through skin abrasions and scratches due to its dense, thorny stems. While these thorns can break the skin, there are none documented regarding systemic toxicity or poisonous ingestion. Individuals should exercise caution when handling the shrub to avoid painful punctures or minor lacerations. The primary risk involves mechanical irritation rather than chemical harm to the body. Careful management of the vegetation is recommended to prevent accidental contact.
