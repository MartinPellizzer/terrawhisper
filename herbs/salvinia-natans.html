Salvinia natans

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvinia natans has small, floating leaves that are divided into three distinct lobes, with the two lateral lobes being large and hairy and the central lobe being significantly smaller. The enlarged lateral lobes are covered in dense, water-repellent hairs that trap air to keep the plant buoyant. These specialized hairs create a hydrophobic surface that prevents the leaves from becoming waterlogged. The plant lacks true roots and instead relies on a submerged, highly branched rhizome system for stability and nutrient uptake. This morphology allows the plant to spread rapidly across the surface of still or slow-moving water.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, Asia, and Australia. It primarily inhabits freshwater environments such as ponds, lakes, and slow-moving rivers within these warm climates. While it originated in these specific zones, human activity has facilitated its spread to other parts of the world. It is now considered an invasive species in many regions, including parts of the Americas and Europe. Its ability to thrive in various aquatic settings allows it to colonize new territories rapidly.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Salvinia natans can cause significant ecological damage by forming thick floating mats that block sunlight and deplete dissolved oxygen in aquatic environments. These dense layers prevent light from reaching submerged plants, which disrupts photosynthesis and leads to the death of native vegetation. As the plant matter decomposes, it further lowers oxygen levels, potentially resulting in massive fish kills and the loss of aquatic biodiversity. This rapid growth also obstructs water flow in canals and drainage systems, increasing the risk of flooding in surrounding areas. Such environmental shifts can fundamentally alter the entire local ecosystem and make water management much more difficult.
