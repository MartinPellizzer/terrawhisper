Prunella vulgaris

What's the taxonomical classification of Prunella vulgaris?

Prunella vulgaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic lineage further, it falls under the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Prunella, and its species name, vulgaris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Prunella vulgaris has square, creeping stems that produce opposite, ovate-shaped leaves with serrated margins and a hairy texture. These leaves are typically dark green on top and lighter on the underside, often growing in whorls along the stem. The plant features a terminal spike of small, hooded, purple or bluish flowers that emerge from a protective bract. Each flower is supported by a calyx that remains persistent after blooming. The root system is rhizomatous, allowing the plant to spread horizontally across the ground.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, though it has become widely naturalized across much of North America. It thrives in a variety of temperate climates ranging from moist meadows to disturbed woodland edges. In its expanded range, it can often be found in gardens, roadsides, and damp pastures. The species is particularly successful in regions with consistent moisture and moderate temperatures. Its ability to spread via runners allows it to colonize new areas across many different continents.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as self-heal, thrives in moist, well-drained soils and requires full sun to partial shade to grow effectively.

It spreads readily through underground rhizomes to form dense, low-growing mats across a garden area. Regular moisture is essential during the establishment phase, though the species is relatively resilient once its root system is mature. While it can be invasive in certain environments, it can be managed by limiting its spread through strategic planting or frequent pruning.

Once established, it produces small purple flowers that attract pollinators throughout the summer months.

What are the pharmacological activities of Prunella vulgaris?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antipyretic, and wound healing pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce swelling and inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. The presence of specific phytochemicals also helps neutralize free radicals to prevent oxidative stress in cells. Furthermore, the plant can effectively lower body temperatures during fever and accelerate the regeneration of skin tissues. These diverse medicinal properties make Prunnella vulgaris a significant subject for natural therapeutic research.

According to a study published by "Chemistry & biodiversity", Prunella vulgaris is a medicinal and edible plant traditionally recognized for its ability to clear heat and relieve liver fire. The study investigated the metabolic composition, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory activities of the plant across four distinct regions. Multivariate analysis revealed a significant difference in the metabolic profile among samples, with the Xingren sample displaying preferred antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Network pharmacology and molecular docking confirmed the high binding affinity of the main active ingredient, isothankunic acid, and its targets. Additionally, naringenin 7-O-(2'',6''-di-O-α-rhamnopyranosyl)-β-glucopyranoside, okanin 4'-gentiobioside, 2''-(6-acetylglucosyl)astragalin, and dihydroscoparin may serve as active markers of antioxidant activity. These results validate the traditional anti-inflammatory use of P. vulgaris and support its potential as a source of natural food antioxidants.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds rosmarinic acid, prunellate A, vulgarisin A, abietoquinone A, and abietoquinone B. These diverse chemical constituents contribute to the various therapeutic properties attributed to the species. Rosmarinic acid is a well-known polyphenol that provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. The diterpenoid quinones, specifically abietoquinone A and abietoquinone B, are often studied for their biological activities. Additionally, unique compounds like prunellate A and vulgarisin A help define the specific phytochemical profile of the plant.

According to a study published by "Current drug discovery technologies", Prunella vulgaris contains varied and copious metabolites including triterpenoids, phenolic acid, sterols, carbohydrates, coumarins, fatty acids, and volatile oils. Possible leads for further development include specific compounds such as betulinic acid, oleanolic acid, ursolic acid, umbelliferone, scopoletin, esculetin, luteolin, homoorientin, Rosmarinic acid, and cinaroside. These constituents provide a wide range of biological activities such as anti-microbial, immunosuppressive, anti-cancer, cardio-protective, anti-allergic, and anti-inflammatory activities. The plant is considered a medicinal plant of immense importance due to this diversity in its pharmacological spectrum.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, skin urticaria, and various types of infections including viral infections and bacterial infections. Its natural properties help to reduce swelling and soothe irritated skin surfaces. People often apply the plant to calm the itching and redness associated with urticaria. It also serves as a traditional remedy to combat pathogens responsible for different bacterial and viral infections. By addressing these diverse conditions, the plant acts as a versatile medicinal resource for both internal and external ailments.

According to a study published by "Phytotherapy research : PTR", the organic fraction of Prunellla vulgaris, which contains 25.7% w/w of rosmarinic acid, exhibits various biological activities. This extract shows scavenging activity on the diphenylpicrylhydrazyl radical and inhibits in vitro human LDL Cu(II)-mediated oxidation. It provides protection for rat mitochondria and rat hepatocytes when they are exposed to tert-butyl hydroperoxide or to Cu(II) and Fe(III) ions. Furthermore, the organic fraction shows potential to inhibit rat erythrocyte haemolysis and reduces LTB(4) production in bovine PMNL via the 5-lipoxygenase pathway. The plant extract also demonstrates antiproliferative effects against HaCaT cells and mouse epidermal fibroblasts along with moderate antimicrobial activity on gram-positive bacteria.
