Ailanthus vilmoriniana

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ailantus vilmoriniana has a deciduous growth habit characterized by pinnately compound leaves that can reach up to 60 centimeters in length. Each leaf consists of numerous pairs of leaflets arranged oppositely along a central rachis. The leaflets themselves are typically elliptical or lanceolate in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. Small, yellowish-green flowers emerge in terminal panicles during the flowering season. These blossoms eventually develop into winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal of the seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South America, specifically within the borders of Brazil, Paraguay, and northern Argentina. It thrives primarily in diverse habitats ranging from humid rainforest margins to more open woodland areas. Its presence is most concentrated in the Atlantic Forest biome, where high humidity and specific soil compositions support its growth. Local climate patterns dictate that it prefers warm temperatures and seasonal rainfall typical of these latitudes.       What are the herbal preparations of Ailanthus vilmoriniana?

      Ailantus vilmoriniana is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or topical poultices depending on the intended therapeutic application. The bark and leaves are most frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal consumption. For external skin conditions, the plant material is often crushed into a paste to be applied directly to the affected area. Some traditional practices also involve using the extracts in alcohol-based tinctures to ensure long-term preservation. These various methods allow practitioners to extract the specific bioactive compounds necessary for treatment.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Ailantus vilmoriniana can cause severe skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and respiratory distress if its juices or dust are inhaled. The plant contains various quassinoids and secondary metabolites that act as potent irritants to human tissue upon contact. Individuals may experience redness, itching, or blistering on the skin after touching the leaves or bark. If the sap enters the eyes, it can lead to intense stinging and inflammation of the mucous membranes. Some people might also experience systemic reactions or breathing difficulties if they are exposed to the airborne particles of the plant.
