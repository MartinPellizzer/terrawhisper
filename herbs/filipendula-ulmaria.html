Filipendula ulmaria

What's the taxonomical classification of Filipendula ulmaria?

Filipendula ulmaria belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Rosales. It is further categorized under the family Rosaceae, which places it among a diverse group of flowering plants. Within this family, it is specifically identified under the genus Filipendula, with the formal species designation being ulmaria.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Filipendula ulmaria has erect, rhizomatous stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 150 centimeters and are clothed in deeply pinnate, serrated leaves. The foliage consists of leaflets that are alternately arranged and often feature a distinctively textured, rugose surface. At the top of the stems, the plant produces dense, creamy-white flower clusters known as panicles. These blossoms are composed of numerous small, five-petaled flowers that emit a potent, sweet fragrance. The root system is characterized by creeping rhizomes that allow the plant to spread effectively in moist environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to much of Europe, temperate Asia, and parts of North America. It thrives across a broad range of latitudes, extending from the British Isles through central Europe to Siberia and parts of East Asia. In North America, it has become naturalized in many wetland environments due to its hardy nature. The species is typically found in moist habitats such as riverbanks, marshes, and damp meadows across these continents. Its ability to colonize riparian zones allows it to spread effectively through various temperate climates.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Filipendula ulmaria contains salicylic acid and other therapeutic compounds primarily within its leaves, flowers, and roots. The aerial parts of the plant are most commonly harvested to create infusions or tinctures used to treat pain and fever. These components act as natural precursors to modern aspirin due to their high concentration of salicylates. Roots are also utilized in traditional medicine for their astringent and anti-inflammatory properties. By processing these specific botanical structures, practitioners can derive various remedies for inflammatory conditions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Filipendula ulmaria?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-ulceraction, antioxidant, antipyrretic, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities. These compounds also demonstrate the ability to reduce fever and suppress inflammatory responses in various models. Furthermore, the plant exhibits protective effects against gastric lesions and inhibits the growth of several pathogenic microorganisms. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds catechin, salicylic acid, quercetin, rosmarinic acid, and gallic acid. These specific polyphenols and organic acids contribute to the plant's potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Catechin and quercetin are known for their ability to neutralize free radicals within cellular structures. The presence of salicylic acid provides natural analgesic effects, while rosmarinic acid supports immune system regulation. Additionally, gallic acid assists in reducing oxidative stress throughout the body.

As discussed in a study, Filipendula ulmaria contains various phenolic compounds such as total phenolics which reach up to 65 mg g-1 in the leaves, flowers, fruits, and roots. The upper leaves and flowers contain a high flavonoid content ranging from 117-167 mg g-1, while hydroxycinnamic acids in the upper leaves, flowers, and fruits measure 6.4-7.8 mg g-1. In the roots, high contents of catechins and proanthocyanidins were found at 45.1 and 3.4 mg g-1, respectively. The fruits are characterized by a high tannin content of 38.3 mg g-1. Identified flavonoids include quercetin derivatives such as quercetin 3-O-rutinoside, quercetin 3-beta-d-glucoside, and quercetin 4'-O-glucoside. Individual phenolic acids like chlorogenic acid, ellagic acid, gallic, caffeic, and salicylic acids are also distributed throughout the different plant organs.
