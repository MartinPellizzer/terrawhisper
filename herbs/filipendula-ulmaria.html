Queen Anne's lace

Filipendula ulmaria

Queen Anne's Lace (Filipendula ulmaria) is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, known for its delicate, lace-like white flowers and its historical use in traditional medicine to treat conditions such as urinary tract infections and kidney stones.

Taxonomy

Queen Anne's Lace, or Filipendula ulmaria, is a flowering plant in the Rosaceae family, belonging to the genus Filipendula within the eudicot class of the Rosales order.

Morphology

Queen Anne's Lace (Filipendula ulmaria) is a biennial herb with a taproot, slender stems that branch near the top, and a compound umbel of small white flowers surrounded by a ring of pale pink bracts.

Distribution

Queen Anne's Lace (Filipendula ulmaria) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, and is commonly found in moist meadows, stream banks, and disturbed soils across these regions.

Traditional Uses

Queen Anne's Lace has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat respiratory issues, skin conditions, and as a mild diuretic due to its anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic properties.

Medicinal Systems

In traditional medicinal systems such as Native American herbalism, European herbal medicine, and traditional Chinese medicine, Queen Anne's Lace has been historically used for its diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties.

Phytochemistry

The phytochemistry of Queen Anne's Lace (Filipendula ulmaria) includes compounds such as coumarins, flavonoids, triterpenoids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and potential therapeutic applications.

Pharmacology

Queen Anne's Lace exhibits diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and mild sedative effects, supporting its traditional use in addressing urinary tract issues, reducing inflammation, and promoting relaxation.

Botanical Identification

Queen Anne's Lace, scientifically known as Filipendula ulmaria, belongs to the Rosaceae family. It is also called Dutch White Clover and is a perennial herb native to Europe and Asia. The plant has feathery leaves and white, lace-like flowers, with a taproot system. It is sometimes referred to as Queen Anne's lace in herbal texts, though this name is also used for another plant, Anthemis artemisiifolia.

Safety Profile

Queen Anne's Lace is generally safe when used in recommended amounts. However, it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals, especially those allergic to related plants. It should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential risks to the fetus or infant.
