Cercis chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Cercis chinensis?

Cercis chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is further categorized under the order Fabales. As a member of the family Fabaceae, this plant is situated within the genus Cercis, and its specific biological identity is defined by the species name chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cercis chinensis has heart-shaped leaves that are typically 5 to 12 centimeters long, pinnately veined, and arranged alternately along its woody stems. The plant produces clusters of small, pea-like flowers that emerge in shades of pink or purple during the spring. Its bark is generally smooth and gray on young branches but becomes deeply furrowed and dark as the tree matures. The fruit develops into flat, elongated brown pods that hang from the branches after flowering. This deciduous shrub or small tree can reach heights of several meters depending on its environmental conditions.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across central and southern China, Korea, and Japan. Its natural range encompasses diverse temperate and subtropical forest environments within these regions. It is frequently found growing in hilly areas and mountain slopes where soil drainage is adequate. Because of its ornamental appeal, the species has been widely introduced to gardens and parks in temperate zones throughout Europe and North America. This cultivation has expanded its presence far beyond its original historical boundaries.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cercis chinensis?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, and hypoglycemic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce pain sensations and suppress inflammatory responses in biological tissues. The plant also demonstrates significant antibacterial properties that help inhibit the growth of various pathogens. Furthermore, its antioxidant capacity assists in neutralizing free radicals to prevent cellular damage. Finally, the specimen exhibits hypoglycemic effects that contribute to the management of elevated blood glucose levels.

As discussed in a study, Cercis chinensis contains 78 characteristic chemical components, which include 32 flavonoids (1-32), 11 phenylpropanoids (33-43), four lignans (44-47), nine stilbenes (48-55), 10 polyketide derivatives (56-65), and 13 other components (66-78). The pharmacological activities of its extracts and monomer components cover antioxidant, hypoglycemic, antibacterial, anti-Alzheimer's disease, and immune regulation. Through network pharmacology, researchers screened 13 core active compounds and 46 key targets related to anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects. These anti-inflammatory and analgesic mechanisms primarily involve the regulation of interleukin-17 and tumor necrosis factor signaling pathways. Furthermore, PTGS2 was confirmed as a core target for these activities.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (2R)-naringenin, [+]-taxifolin, kaempferol, quercetin, and rutin. These specific flavonoids are known for their diverse biological activities including antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of rutin and quercetin often contributes to the strengthening of capillary walls and improved vascular health. Furthermore, the combination of kaempferol and (2R)-naringenin may assist in modulating various cellular signaling pathways. Such a complex profile of phytochemicals makes the plant a significant subject for pharmacological research.

As discussed in a study, nine compounds were isolated from the aerial part of Cercis chinensis. These medicinal compounds were identified as 3-O-methylquercetin (1), quercetin (2), (2R,3R)-3, 5, 7, 3', 5'-pentahydroxyflavan (3), 3', 5, 5', 7-tetrahydroxyflavanone (4), [+]-taxifolin (5), (2R)-naringenin (6), friedelin (7), beta-sitosterol (8), and daucosterin (9). Compounds 1 and 3-7 were isolated from the genus Cercis for the first time. The chemical constituents were separated using various chromatographic techniques and their structures were elucidated through spectral analysis and physico-chemical properties.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the bark or roots in water to extract its medicinal constituents, an infusion by steeping the flowers or leaves in hot water, or a tincture by soaking the plant parts in alcohol. These different methods allow practitioners to target specific therapeutic properties depending on the intended use. A decoction is typically reserved for tougher materials like the woody stem or roots to ensure a strong concentration of active compounds. Infusions are gentler and commonly used for the delicate floral elements to preserve volatile components. Tinctures provide a shelf-stable way to concentrate the plant's essence for long-term medicinal application.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive distress if consumed or handled improperly. While not widely documented as highly toxic, some individuals may experience contact dermatitis after touching the foliage or bark. Ingestion of various parts of the plant could lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain in sensitive subjects. There is also limited clinical research regarding long-term systemic effects or reproductive toxicity in humans. Consequently, cautious handling and avoiding any internal use are recommended to prevent adverse reactions.
