Garcinia hanburyi

What's the taxonomical classification of Garcinia hanburyi?

Garcinia hanburyi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Malpighiales. As a member of the family Clusiaceae, it is further categorized into the genus Garcinia, ultimately reaching its specific identification as the species hanburyi.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Garcinia hanburyi contains high concentrations of xanthones, specifically alpha-mangostin and gamma-mangostin, which provide its primary pharmacological properties. These bioactive polyphenolic compounds exhibit significant antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antibacterial activities within biological systems. Research suggests these substances may also possess cytotoxic effects against certain cancer cell lines. Additionally, the plant extracts have been studied for their potential to regulate metabolic processes and reduce oxidative stress. The complex chemical profile of the fruit and bark makes it a subject of interest for developing new therapeutic agents.

What are the therapeutic actions of Garcinia hanburyi?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various infections, including bacterial, fungal, and parasitic diseases. Its medicinal properties are primarily attributed to the presence of bioactive compounds like xanthones and tannins found in its extracts. These chemical constituents exhibit significant antimicrobial activity that can help combat harmful pathogens. Researchers have explored its potential in managing skin ailments and certain gastrointestinal issues caused by microbes. Consequently, it holds promise as a natural source for developing new therapeutic agents against resistant strains.

What are the herbal preparations of Garcinia hanburyi?

Garcinia hanburyi is prepared as a decoction, a concentrated infusion, or a topical paste derived from its bark, leaves, and fruit. Traditional medicinal practitioners often boil the woody bark in water to create a bitter liquid used for internal consumption. The juice extracted from the ripe fruit can also be used as a digestive aid or a flavoring agent in local culinary practices. In certain regional traditions, crushed leaves are applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat external ailments. These diverse preparations allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be utilized for both systemic and localized therapeutic purposes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Garcinia hanburyi can cause gastrointestinal distress, allergic skin reactions, and potential interference with metabolic enzymes if consumed in high quantities. While specific clinical trials on humans are limited, the high concentration of xanthones and tannins in the plant may irritate the digestive lining. Some individuals might experience nausea or abdominal discomfort after ingestion. There is also a risk of contact dermatitis if the sap touches sensitive skin. Because of these unknown long-term effects, caution is advised when using the plant for medicinal purposes.
