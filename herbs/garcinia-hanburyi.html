Garcinia hanburyi

What's the taxonomical classification of Garcinia hanburyi?

Garcinia hanburyi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Malpighiales. As a member of the family Clusiaceae, it is further categorized into the genus Garcinia, ultimately reaching its specific identification as the species hanburyi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Garcinia hanburyi has a woody, evergreen habit characterized by spreading branches and elliptical, leathery leaves that are typically dark green on the upper surface and paler underneath. The foliage is arranged in an opposite pattern along the stems, providing a dense canopy. Its inflorescences consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the axils of the leaves. These flowers eventually give rise to fleshy, globose or ovoid fruits that transition in color as they mature. The tree's structure is robust, supporting the weight of its heavy, fruit-bearing limbs.

What are the pharmacological activities of Garcinia hanburyi?

This plant has potent anti-cancer, anti-tumour, antioxidative, antiproliferative, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities derived from its unique phytochemical profile. Its bioactive compounds function by inhibiting the uncontrolled growth of malignant cells through targeted antiproliferative mechanisms. The observed cytotoxic effects demonstrate the plant's ability to induce cell death in various cancer cell lines. Additionally, its anti-tumour properties help suppress the progression and spread of abnormal growths within the body. These therapeutic benefits are further supported by strong antioxidative actions that neutralize harmful free radicals.

As discussed in a study, isogambogenic acid (iso-GNA) is an herbal medicine extracted from Garcinia hanburyi that demonstrates potent anti-angiogenic activity and low toxicity in zebrafish embryos at appropriate concentrations. In a xenograft nude mouse model of lung tumour, iso-GNA effectively inhibited tumour growth and tumour angiogenesis. A methyl thiazolyl tetrazolium assay showed that iso-GNA was more effective in inhibiting the proliferation of human umbilical vascular endothelial cells than A549 cancer cells. The substance suppressed neovascularization of implanted matrigel plugs in vivo and inhibited vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)-induced microvessel sprouting from mouse aortic rings ex vivo. Furthermore, iso-GNA inhibited VEGF-induced migration, invasion, and tube formation in vitro while targeting VEGFR2, Akt, mitoGen-activated protein kinase, Rho GTPase, vascular endothelium-cadherin, and focal adhesion kinase signalling pathways.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (-)-gambogic acid, gambogenin, gambogin, morellic acid, and gambogenic acid. These specific diterpene derivatives are primarily extracted from the resin of the plant. Scientists study these substances for their significant biological activities, particularly regarding their potential anti-inflammatory and anti-cancer properties. The complex chemical structure of these compounds allows them to interact with various cellular pathways in the human body. Ongoing research continues to explore how these individual molecules might be used in targeted therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, the dried resin of Garcinia hanburyi contains the biologically active caged xanthone known as (-)-gambogic acid (1). This compound is of interest as a potential anticancer agent. The absolute configuration of (-)-gambogic acid was determined for the first time using (-)-morellic acid (2) as a model compound. Through the comparison of physical and spectroscopic data, the absolute configuration was identified as 5R, 7S, 10aS, 13R, 27S. This determination included the deduction of the absolute configuration at C-13 as R.
