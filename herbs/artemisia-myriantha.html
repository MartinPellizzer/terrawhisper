What's the taxonomical classification of Artemisia myriantha?

Artemisia myriantha belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the widely recognized family Asteraceae, which is further narrowed down to the genus Artemisia. Ultimately, its specific scientific designation is completed by its species name, myriantha.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artemisia myriantha has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, woody stems that can reach heights of up to 60 centimeters. The leaves are finely divided into numerous narrow, linear segments, giving the foliage a feathery or thread-like appearance. These leaves are typically silver-grey in color due to a dense covering of fine, silky hairs on both surfaces. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flower heads arranged in terminal panicles that appear during the summer months. This intricate leaf structure and silvery hue are key identifying features of the species.

What are the pharmacological activities of Artemisia myriantha?

This plant has potent pharmacological activities including significant cytotoxicity, antioxidant properties, and antimicrobial effects. The cytotoxic potential of Artemisia myriantha has been demonstrated through its ability to inhibit the proliferation of various cancer cell lines in laboratory studies. Beyond its ability to target malignant cells, the plant contains bioactive compounds that neutralize harmful free radicals in the body. Researchers have also identified its capacity to suppress the growth of specific pathogenic bacteria and fungi. These multifaceted biological actions suggest that the plant serves as a valuable source for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the EtOH extract of Artemisia myriantha and its EtOAc fraction showed cytotoxicity against HepG2 cells with inhibitory ratios of 30.6% and 53.5% at 50.0 μg/mL. Bioassay-guided isolation of the most active fractions, Fr. C and Fr. D, yielded 19 new sesquiterpenolides known as artemyrianolidides A-S (1-19). These 19 new compounds include 13 germacranolides (1-13), four guaianolides (14-17), and two eudesmanoilides (18 and 19). Twenty of the compounds exhibited cytotoxicity against HepG2, Huh7, and SMMC-7721 cell lines. Compound 9 demonstrated cytotoxic activity on HepG2 and Huh7 cells with IC50 values of 8.6 and 8.8 μM. Furthermore, compounds 8 and 33 showed cytotoxicity to the three human hepatoma cell lines with IC50 values of 4.9 and 7.4 μM (HepG2), 4.3 and 7.8 μM (Huh7), and 3.1 and 9.8 μM (SMMC-7721), respectively.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds Artemyrianosin A, Artemyrianosin B, Artemyrianosin C, Artemyrianosin D, and Artemyrianosin E. These specialized chemical constituents are primarily studied for their potential pharmacological properties in therapeutic research. Each of these distinct molecules contributes to the complex biochemical profile of the organism. Scientists analyze these substances to understand how they might interact with various biological systems. The presence of such a diverse group of compounds makes the plant a significant subject for pharmaceutical investigation.

As discussed in a study, ten new germacrane-type sesquiterpenoids named artemyrianosins A-J (1-10) were isolated from the aerial parts of Artemisia myriantha. These compounds include cytotoxic germacrane-type sesquiterpene lactones. Compounds 1-3, 7, and 10 showed cytotoxicity against three human hepatoma cell lines with IC50 values ranging from 43.7 to 89.3 μM. The most active compound 3 exhibited activity against HepG2, Huh7, and SK-Hep-1 with IC50 values of 43.7 μM, 47.9 μM, and 44.9 μM respectively.
