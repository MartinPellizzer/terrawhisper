Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and Asia, known for its medicinal properties and traditional use in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing benefits.

What is Field Marigold (Calendula Arvensis)?

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and Asia, characterized by its bright yellow-orange petals and is commonly used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Field Marigold is Calendula arvensis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Asteridae, order Asterales, family Compositae, genus Calendula, and species Calendula arvensis. It is commonly known as Field Marigold in Europe and North America, and as Wild Marigold in parts of Asia and Africa. The synonyms for this plant include Calendula officinalis var. arvensis and Calendula vulgaris.

Plant Description and Morphology

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a short-lived perennial herb characterized by its erect, branching stems, broad lance-shaped leaves with serrated edges, and bright yellow flowers that typically bloom in clusters at the ends of the stems.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is native to temperate regions of Europe and western Asia, commonly found in disturbed soils, roadsides, and dry grasslands.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The entire flowering head of the Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is used medicinally, including the petals, receptacle, and surrounding bracts, which are typically harvested when the flowers are fully open and the color is at its peak.

Phytochemical Composition

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) contains a diverse array of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, triterpenoids, carotenoids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic potential.

Pharmacological Properties

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing effects, primarily due to its high content of flavonoids, triterpenoids, and essential oils.

Mechanisms of Action

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) exerts its therapeutic effects through the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, triterpenes, and carotenoids, which possess anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support skin health, reduce inflammation, and promote wound healing due to its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically prepared as a tincture, infused oil, or dried herb for use in topical applications and internal remedies.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) for herbal use ranges from 200 to 400 mg of dried herb per day, divided into two to three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the preparation method and intended use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is generally safe for most people, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, or skin irritation in some individuals, and it should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and by those with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family.

Drug Interactions

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) may interact with certain medications, including blood thinners, antidiabetic drugs, and chemotherapy agents, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding, lowering blood sugar levels, or enhancing the toxicity of these treatments.

Toxicity and Precautions

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and long-term or high-dose use should be avoided without medical supervision.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically cultivated in well-drained soil with full sun exposure, requiring moderate watering and minimal fertilization, and is harvested when the flowers are fully open, usually in late summer to early autumn.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) typically involve harvesting the flowers when fully bloomed, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain active compounds like flavonoids and triterpenoids.

Storage and Shelf Life

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a shelf life of up to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) has primarily focused on its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties, with some studies suggesting it may support skin health and immune function.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and wound-healing properties, with historical applications ranging from treating skin irritations to aiding in the management of digestive disorders.

Regulatory Status

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is not currently approved as a pharmaceutical drug in the United States or the European Union, but it is permitted for use in herbal remedies and dietary supplements under specific regulations governing natural products.
