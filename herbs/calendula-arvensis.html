What to know about Calendula arvensis (pot marigold) before using it medicinally

Pot marigold, scientifically known as Calendula arvensis, is a herb that boasts impressive health properties, including anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects, which can significantly improve overall well-being.

In the culinary world, pot marigold is often used to add vibrant color and subtle flavor to dishes such as soups, salads, and sauces. As a hardy annual, pot marigold requires minimal maintenance and can thrive in poor soil conditions, making it a popular choice among gardeners. Botanically speaking, pot marigold belongs to the Asteraceae family and is characterized by its bright yellow-orange flowers and hairy leaves.

With references dating back to ancient Greek and Roman medicine, pot marigold has been a valuable component of traditional remedies for centuries.

What are the side effects of Calendula arvensis when used improperly?

When used improperly, Calendula arvensis increases the chances of experiencing side effects, such as skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal disturbances.

If not prepared correctly or in excessive amounts, this herb can cause skin redness, itching, and even blistering due to its potential allergenic properties. Moreover, consuming large doses of Calendula arvensis tea or supplementing with high concentrations of the herb may lead to stomach upset, nausea, and diarrhea as a result of its stimulatory effects on the digestive system.

Furthermore, using Calendula arvensis during pregnancy or breastfeeding without proper guidance from a healthcare professional may pose risks to both mother and child, potentially inducing labor or affecting fetal development. Additionally, combining Calendula arvensis with certain medications, such as blood thinners or immunosuppressants, can lead to adverse interactions that compromise overall health.

As with any herbal remedy, it is essential to follow proper preparation methods, use recommended dosages, and consult with a healthcare professional before utilizing Calendula arvensis for medicinal purposes to minimize the risk of undesirable side effects.

What are the harvesting tips of Calendula arvensis?

Calendula arvensis, a perennial daisy-like flower, is often harvested for its petals and leaves, which are used in herbal remedies, cosmetics, and food.

To ensure optimal harvesting, it's essential to understand the plant's growth cycle. The best time to harvest calendula is when the flowers are fully opened and bright yellow in color, typically around mid-summer. For leaf harvesting, wait until the plant has at least 4-6 sets of leaves, as this indicates a more robust flavor and aroma.

Use scissors or pinch off the top sets of leaves, taking care not to damage the stems or nearby flowers. When collecting petals, gently pluck them from the center of the flower head, taking only what is necessary for your intended use. Harvesting in the morning, after dew has dried but before mid-day heat, can help preserve the plant's essential oils and prevent wilting.

Proper harvesting techniques will promote healthy regrowth and encourage the plant to continue producing flowers throughout the growing season.
