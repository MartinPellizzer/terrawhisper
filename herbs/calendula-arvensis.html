What to know about Calendula arvensis (pot marigold) before using it medicinally

Calendula arvensis, commonly known as pot marigold, is a herbaceous plant that boasts a range of health benefits, from soothing skin irritations to reducing inflammation and promoting wound healing.

A low-maintenance and easy-to-grow plant, Calendula arvensis is often cultivated in gardens and used in ornamental arrangements due to its vibrant yellow and orange flowers. Botanically speaking, Calendula arvensis belongs to the daisy family (Asteraceae) and is characterized by its distinctive, daisy-like flowers and fern-like leaves.

Historically, this versatile herb has been used in traditional medicine for centuries, with ancient Greeks and Romans using its petals to make a tea that treated a variety of ailments.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Calendula arvensis.

What are the medicinal properties of Calendula arvensis?

Calendula arvensis helps with wound healing, inflammation, and skin irritations. Its antimicrobial and antifungal properties make it effective against bacterial and fungal infections. It is also used to treat digestive issues and menstrual problems.

The active constituents of Calendula arvensis include calendulin, calendulic acid, and triterpenoid saponins, which give it its medicinal properties. These constituents have anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant effects. They also have immunomodulatory effects, which make the plant effective against various health conditions.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are the flowers and leaves. The flowers are used to make infusions, tinctures, and ointments, while the leaves are used to make teas and infusions. The flowers are rich in bioactive compounds, which give them their medicinal properties.

When used improperly, Calendula arvensis can cause allergic reactions, such as skin irritation and anaphylaxis. It can also interact with other medications, such as blood thinners and diabetes medications. Prolonged use can also lead to gastrointestinal upset and kidney damage.

Precautions when using Calendula arvensis medicinally include using it in moderation, as excessive use can cause side effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using it, as its effects on the fetus and baby are unknown. It is also recommended to consult a healthcare professional before using it, especially if taking other medications or have underlying health conditions.

What are the horticulural aspects of Calendula arvensis?

Calendula arvensis grow well in full sun and well-drained soil with a pH range of 6.0-7.0. It thrives in temperatures between 15-25°C (59-77°F) and requires minimal watering, making it suitable for drought-tolerant gardens. The plant's growth habits are erect, reaching a height of 60-90 cm (24-35 in).

Plant Calendula arvensis in spring or early summer, spacing plants 20-30 cm (8-12 in) apart. Sow seeds directly into the ground or start them indoors 4-6 weeks before the last frost date. Transplant seedlings when they have 2-3 sets of leaves, and thin to 15-20 cm (6-8 in) apart. Water gently, as excessive moisture can lead to root rot.

Harvest Calendula arvensis flowers when they are fully open and display their brightest colors, typically in mid to late summer. Pinch off spent flowers to encourage continuous blooming. Cut stems for use in floral arrangements or dry them for later use in potpourri, wreaths, or as a natural dye.

Common pests of Calendula arvensis include aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites, which can be controlled using neem oil, insecticidal soap, or diatomaceous earth. Fungal diseases such as powdery mildew and leaf spot can be managed with copper-based fungicides or sulfur. Regularly inspect plants and remove any infected or damaged leaves to prevent the spread of disease.

What are the botanical aspects of Calendula arvensis?

Calendula arvensis is an annual or biennial herb with a taproot and a stem up to 1 meter tall, covered with short hairs and bearing yellow or orange flowers in a conical inflorescence.

Calendula arvensis belongs to the family Asteraceae, tribe Calenduleae, and is classified as Calendula arvensis L. (1753). The genus Calendula comprises about 15 species, and the species C. arvensis is often considered a subspecies of C. officinalis.

The plant has several variants, including Calendula arvensis var. alba and Calendula arvensis var. citrina, which differ in flower color and petal shape. These variants are often considered to be part of the same species.

Calendula arvensis is native to Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa, but has been naturalized in many parts of the world. It can be found in fields, meadows, and along roadsides, often in disturbed or cultivated areas.

The life cycle of Calendula arvensis is annual or biennial, with a growth period of about 80 days. The plant germinates in the spring, forms a rosette of leaves, and then produces a flowering stem. The flowers are self-pollinated, and the seeds mature in the autumn.
