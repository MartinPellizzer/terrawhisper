Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Field Marigold (Calendula Arvensis)?

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and Asia, characterized by its bright yellow-orange petals and is commonly used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Field Marigold is Calendula arvensis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Asteridae, order Asterales, family Compositae, genus Calendula, and species Calendula arvensis. It is commonly known as Field Marigold in Europe and North America, and as Wild Marigold in parts of Asia and Africa. The synonyms for this plant include Calendula officinalis var. arvensis and Calendula vulgaris.

Plant Description and Morphology

Field Marigold is a low-growing annual herb that typically reaches heights of 10 to 30 centimeters, with a branching structure and a fibrous root system.

Its leaves are lance-shaped, rough to the touch, and have a serrated edge, arranged alternately along the stems. The flowers are bright yellow, with a radiate pattern of petals surrounding a central disk, and bloom from early summer to late autumn. The roots are slender and fibrous, anchoring the plant and absorbing nutrients from the soil.

The plant has a distinct, slightly bitter smell, with a rough, hairy texture on its stems and leaves.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Field Marigold is native to temperate regions of Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, with its natural range extending into the Mediterranean basin.

It is commonly found in disturbed habitats such as roadsides, fields, and open woodlands, where it thrives in well-drained soils. The plant prefers mild to cool climates with moderate rainfall, typically growing in regions with a temperate continental or oceanic climate. It is well adapted to full sun exposure and can tolerate light shade, making it a common weed in agricultural areas.

Ecological conditions favoring its growth include nutrient-poor soils and areas with low to moderate competition from other plant species.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in Field Marigold are the flowers, leaves, and roots.

The flowers are most commonly harvested for their medicinal properties, typically when they are fully open. The leaves are often used in infusions or decoctions to address various ailments. Roots are less frequently utilized but can be prepared as tinctures or powders.

Each part contains different concentrations of bioactive compounds that contribute to its therapeutic applications.

Therapeutic Uses

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is widely used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties.

It is particularly effective in treating skin conditions such as eczema, psoriasis, and minor burns. The plant's anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial compounds help reduce redness, swelling, and infection in wounds. It is also used to alleviate symptoms of digestive issues like gastritis and colitis.

Additionally, it supports immune health and is applied topically for its soothing and healing effects.

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is commonly used in various forms for its medicinal properties.

The plant is typically prepared as a dried herb, which can be used to make teas, tinctures, or oils. It is also available as powdered form for incorporation into capsules or tablets. Additionally, the flowers can be infused into creams or ointments for topical applications.

The typical dosage of Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) for herbal use ranges from 200 to 400 mg of dried herb per day, divided into two to three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the preparation method and intended use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is generally safe for most people, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, or skin irritation in some individuals, and it should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and by those with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family.

Drug Interactions

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) may interact with certain medications, including blood thinners, antidiabetic drugs, and chemotherapy agents, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding, lowering blood sugar levels, or enhancing the toxicity of these treatments.

Toxicity and Precautions

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and long-term or high-dose use should be avoided without medical supervision.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically cultivated in well-drained soil with full sun exposure, requiring moderate watering and minimal fertilization, and is harvested when the flowers are fully open, usually in late summer to early autumn.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) typically involve harvesting the flowers when fully bloomed, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain active compounds like flavonoids and triterpenoids.

Storage and Shelf Life

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a shelf life of up to 2 years when properly preserved.
