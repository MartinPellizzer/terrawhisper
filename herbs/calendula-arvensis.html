Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, known for its bright yellow-orange blooms and traditional use in herbal medicine to treat skin conditions and inflammation.

What is Field Marigold (Calendula Arvensis)?

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly found in meadows, roadsides, and disturbed soils, and is known for its yellow-orange flowers and historical use in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory and skin-soothing properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and western Asia, belonging to the Asteraceae family, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

Common Names and Synonyms

Field Marigold, also known as Calendula arvensis, is a flowering plant in the sunflower family (Asteraceae) commonly referred to by other names such as Common Marigold, Garden Marigold, and Wild Marigold, depending on regional usage and historical nomenclature.

Plant Description and Morphology

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a herbaceous annual plant with slender, branching stems that grow up to 60 cm tall, bearing opposite, lanceolate to ovate leaves with rough, hairy surfaces and yellow-orange composite flowers consisting of a central disc surrounded by ray florets.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is native to temperate regions of Europe and western Asia, commonly found in disturbed soils, grasslands, and along roadsides.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The above-ground parts, including leaves, flowers, and stems, are primarily used medicinally in traditional herbal medicine.

Phytochemical Composition

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) contains a variety of bioactive compounds including flavonoids, triterpenoids, carotenoids, essential oils, and polysaccharides, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic potential.

Pharmacological Properties

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing effects, primarily attributed to its flavonoid and triterpenoid compounds.

Mechanisms of Action

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties, which are mediated by compounds such as flavonoids, triterpenoids, and essential oils that modulate cellular responses and promote tissue repair.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Field Marigold is used to treat skin conditions such as eczema, psoriasis, and wounds, as well as to alleviate symptoms of digestive disorders like indigestion and inflammation.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically prepared as a tincture, infused oil, or dried herb for use in teas, poultices, or topical applications.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) for medicinal use ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried herb per day, often prepared as a tea, tincture, or topical application, with specific formulations varying based on the intended therapeutic effect.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly those with sensitivities to plants in the Asteraceae family.

Drug Interactions

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the gastrointestinal tract or blood coagulation, and it is advisable to consult a healthcare professional before use if taking other medications.

Toxicity and Precautions

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort or allergic reactions in some individuals, and pregnant women should avoid long-term use due to potential risks.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically cultivated in well-drained soils with full sun exposure, often in temperate climates, and requires regular watering during dry periods to ensure optimal growth and flower production.

Processing and Extraction

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically processed by drying the flowering tops and then grinding them into a fine powder or using them as a tincture, infusion, or topical salve to extract its therapeutic compounds.

Storage and Shelf Life

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) should be stored in a cool, dry, and dark place in a sealed container to maintain its potency and prevent moisture damage, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) has primarily focused on its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties, with studies indicating its active compounds may support skin health and immune response.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties, with historical applications ranging from topical treatments for skin conditions to internal use in digestive remedies.
