Field Marigold

Calendula arvensis

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, known for its bright yellow-orange blooms and traditional use in herbal medicine to treat skin conditions and inflammation.

Morphology

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a herbaceous annual plant with slender, branching stems that grow up to 60 cm tall, bearing opposite, lanceolate to ovate leaves with rough, hairy surfaces and yellow-orange composite flowers consisting of a central disc surrounded by ray florets.

Distribution

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is native to Europe and western Asia, with a broad distribution across temperate regions including parts of southern Russia, the Balkans, Turkey, and the Middle East.

Traditional Uses

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and wound-healing properties, often applied topically to treat skin conditions, minor cuts, and burns.

Medicinal Systems

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) has been utilized in traditional medicinal systems such as Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, and European herbalism for its anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and wound-healing properties.

Phytochemistry

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) contains bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, triterpenoids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

Pharmacology

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing effects, which support its traditional use in herbal medicine for skin conditions and inflammatory disorders.

Medicinal Uses

Field Marigold is used to treat skin conditions such as eczema, psoriasis, and wounds, as well as to alleviate symptoms of digestive disorders like indigestion and inflammation.

Preparations

Field Marigold is used to prepare tinctures, infusions, and ointments that are applied topically for skin conditions and internally for digestive support.

Safety

While Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly those with sensitivities to plants in the Asteraceae family.

Evidence

Clinical evidence for Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) primarily supports its use in topical applications for skin conditions, with studies indicating anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties, though limited human trials exist.

Cultivation

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically cultivated in well-drained soils with full sun exposure, often in temperate climates, and requires regular watering during dry periods to ensure optimal growth and flower production.

Processing

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is typically processed by drying the flowering tops and then grinding them into a fine powder or using them as a tincture, infusion, or topical salve to extract its therapeutic compounds.

Identification

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, characterized by its bright yellow to orange petals, heart-shaped leaves, and a distinctive, slightly aromatic scent.

Sustainability

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is a sustainable choice for herbal medicine due to its hardy nature, minimal water requirements, and ability to thrive in various soil conditions, reducing the need for chemical inputs and supporting biodiversity.

Related

Field Marigold (Calendula arvensis) is closely related to other species within the Asteraceae family, including Calendula officinalis, which is also known as pot marigold and is widely used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory and antiseptic properties.
