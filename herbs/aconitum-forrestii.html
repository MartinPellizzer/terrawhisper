Aconitum forrestii

What's the taxonomical classification of Aconitum forrestii?

Aconitum forrestii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Ranunculales. This plant is a representative of the family Ranunculaceae, specifically situated within the genus Aconitum, and is identified by its unique species designation, forrestii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aconium forrestii has erect, herbaceous stems that can reach heights of up to one meter, supporting deeply lobed, palmately divided leaves with serrated margins. The inflorescence consists of dense racemes bearing large, zygomorphic flowers characterized by a prominent, hood-like uppermost sepal. These petals are typically a deep shade of blue or violet, often featuring white markings or pale highlights near the center. The plant also produces a robust root system, often developing tuberous structures that allow it to thrive in moist, montane environments. Small, capsule-like fruits develop following pollination to facilitate seed dispersal.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Aconium forrestii contains toxic alkaloids primarily concentrated in its tuberous roots which are the parts traditionally used in medicinal preparations.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe poisoning characterized by nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dizziness, arrhythmia, bradycardia, hypotension, paralysis, numbness, tingling, respiratory failure, convulsions, and death. Ingestion of the toxic alkaloids leads to rapid neurological and cardiovascular distress. Patients often experience sensory disturbances like tingling and numbness alongside muscle weakness or total paralysis. The heart rate may slow dangerously while blood pressure drops, potentially triggering life-threatening arrhythmias. Ultimately, the toxins can cause convulsions and respiratory failure, which frequently lead to death.
