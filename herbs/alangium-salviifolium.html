Alangium salviifolium

What's the taxonomical classification of Alangium salviifolium?

Aliangium salviifolium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Cornales. This plant is further categorized into the family Cornaceae, placing it within the genus Aliangium, and is specifically identified by its species name, salviifolium.

Small, tubular flowers emerge from the axillary clusters, often displaying subtle variations in color. The root system is typically fibrous and well-adapted to the specific soil conditions of its natural habitat. What are the pharmacological activities of Alangium salviifolium?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and antidiabetic properties. Its anti-inflammatory effects are often attributed to the presence of specific bioactive compounds that inhibit inflammatory mediators in the body. The antibacterial capacity of the plant allows it to effectively combat various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, its antidiabetic potential is shown through its ability to regulate blood glucose levels and improve insulin sensitivity. These diverse biological actions make the plant a subject of interest for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, three new phenolic glycosides named salviifosides A-C (13) were isolated from the leaves of Alangium salviifolium (L.f.) Wangerin. The leaves also contained three known compounds, salicin (4), kaempferol (5), and kaempferol 3-O-beta-d-glucopyranoside (6). The anti-inflammatory activities of the new compounds (1-3) were investigated using the lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced murine macrophage cells line, RAW 264.7. Salviifoside B (2) potentially inhibits the production of nitric oxide (NO), prostaglandin E(2) (PGE(2)), and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha).

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds iphecac alkaloids, kaempferol, kaempferol 3-O-beta-d-glucopyranoside, salicin, and salviifosides A-C. These chemical constituents contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of the species. The presence of kaempferol and its glycoside form provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the salicin content offers analgesic effects similar to those found in willow bark. The specific combination of iphecac alkaloids and salviifosides A-C further enhances its potential therapeutic utility in traditional medicine.

As discussed in a study, Alangium salviifolium belongs to the order Cornales and contains medicinal ipecac alkaloids. These medicinal compounds are monoterpenoid-derived tetrahydroisoquinoline alkaloids. The biosynthesis of these alkaloids in the plant is initiated through a nonenzymatic Pictet-Spengler reaction. This nonstereoselective reaction produces both 1R and 1S stereoisomers. Each of these isomers follows a specific biosynthetic fate within the plant.
