Mimosa pudica

What's the taxonomical classification of Mimosa pudica?

Mimosa pudica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Fabales. As a member of the family Fabaceae, it is categorized into the genus Mimosa, and its specific designation is the species pudica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mimosa pudiica has bipinnately compound leaves consisting of numerous small, oblong leaflets arranged along a central axis, a woody stem, and a taproot system. The foliage displays a sensitive movement known as seismonasty, where the leaflets fold inward and the petioles droop when touched or exposed to heat. Small, pinkish-purple globular flower heads emerge from the leaf axils, creating a puffball appearance. The plant often features sharp prickles along its stems to serve as a defense mechanism against herbivores. This intricate structure allows the plant to respond rapidly to its surrounding environment.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Central and South America, stretching from Mexico through the Caribbean and down to Brazil. Because of its ability to thrive in warm climates, it has successfully naturalized across various parts of the world. Its presence is now widely recorded throughout much of Africa, Southeast Asia, and parts of Australia. It frequently colonizes disturbed habitats, such as roadsides and waste areas, in these diverse geographical zones. This wide expansion is largely attributed to its resilient nature and ability to spread via seeds.

What are the therapeutic actions of Mimosa pudica?

Mimosa pudica has diverse therapeutic actions including being an astringent, sedative, nervine, anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and emmenagogue. Its sedative and nervine properties help to calm the nervous system and reduce anxiety or insomnia. The plant acts as an effective anti-inflammatory to reduce swelling and as an antispasmodic to relieve muscle contractions or cramps. As an astringent, it helps to constrict tissues and tone skin or mucous membranes. Additionally, its role as an emmenagogue can assist in regulating menstrual cycles and promoting blood flow.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, glycosides, myricetin, saponins, and tannins which serve as its primary medicinal compounds. These diverse chemical constituents contribute to the plant's varied biological activities and therapeutic potential. The presence of glycosides and saponins often plays a significant role in its pharmacological properties. Additionally, the flavonoids and the specific compound myricetin provide potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Tannins further enhance the plant's medicinal profile through their astringent and antimicrobial characteristics.

As discussed in a study, Mimosa pudica contains a wide range of advantageous phytochemicals such as alkaloids, flavonoids, tannins, and saponins. The methanolic extract of this plant was evaluated at doses of 200 mg/kg and 400 mg/kg body weight. These doses showed neurobehavioral effects similar to conventional medications Diazepam and Escitalopram at 1 mg/kg body weight. Furthermore, molecular docking studies were performed on 11 compounds that were identified using GC-MS analysis. These findings regarding the chemical properties of Mimosa pudica highlight its potential to reduce anxiety and depression.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause significant toxicity due to the presence of the amino acid mimosine in its tissues. If ingested, the chemical compounds may lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. Some individuals may also experience systemic neurological symptoms like dizziness after consumption. Because of these potential risks, careful handling is necessary to avoid accidental poisoning.
