Aletris spicata

What's the taxonomical classification of Aletris spicata?

Aletris spicata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Dioscoreales, the plant is placed into the family Narcheciaeae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its assignment to the genus Aletris, resulting in its scientific name, Aletris spicata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aletris spicata has a basal rosette of long, lanceolate leaves that are dark green, smooth, and can reach up to 30 centimeters in length. A single, erect flowering stem rises from the center of this foliage, often growing significantly taller than the leaves themselves. This spike-like inflorescence carries numerous small, white, star-shaped flowers that are arranged densely along the upper portion of the stalk. Each individual blossom features six tepals and delicate pinkish filaments that provide a subtle contrast to the white petals. The plant's overall structure is slender and upright, making it easily distinguishable in its wetland habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Aletris spicata?

This plant has anti-asthma, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant pharmacological activities that stem from its diverse chemical constituents. These bioactive properties suggest that the plant can help mitigate respiratory distress by reducing airway constriction. Its anti-inflammatory effects work to suppress the production of mediators responsible for swelling and irritation in bodily tissues. Additionally, the antioxidant capacity of the plant helps neutralize harmful free radicals that cause cellular damage. Through these combined actions, Aletris spicata serves as a significant subject for studying natural therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, Aletris spicata (AS) was analyzed using LC-HRMS/MS to identify 33 compounds. In asthma models, AS significantly reduced inflammatory cells like neutrophils, lymphocytes, and eosinophils in BALF while decreasing IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, TNF-α, and serum IgE. The plant also reduced ROS and MDA levels, enhanced SOD activity, upregulated Nrf2/HO-1 expression, and inhibited NF-κB p65 nuclear translocation. Furthermore, AS restored intestinal flora homeostasis by modulating the richness, diversity, and composition of the gut microbiota. These multi-target mechanisms demonstrate the therapeutic potential of AS for asthma management through antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and microbiota modulation actions.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds amentoflavone, apiigenin, β-sitosterol-3-O-β-d-glucoside, 3-methoxy-4-hydroxy-cinnamic aldehyde, and 5-hydroxymethyl furfural. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the diverse pharmacological activities observed in the plant's extracts. Amentoflavone and apiigenin are flavones often studied for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The sterol derivative β-sitosterol-3-O-β-d-glucoside may support various physiological functions within the body. Additionally, the presence of 3-methoxy-4-hydroxy-cinnamic aldehyde and 5-hydroxymethyl furfural provides a complex chemical profile relevant to therapeutic research.

As discussed in a study, eleven compounds were isolated and identified from Aletris spicata. These constituents include 4 flavonoids, which are (2R,3R)-2,3-dihydro-3,5-dihydroxy-7,4'-dimethoxyflavone (1), 5-hydroxy-7,8,4'-trimethoxy-flavone (2), amentoflavone (10), and apiagenin (11). The plant also contains 2 phenylpropanoids, specifically sinapaldehde (3) and 3-methoxy-4-hydroxy-cinnamic aldehyde (4). Additionally, there are 3 phenolic acids identified as 4-hydroxyl-3,5-dimethoxy-benzaldehyde (5), 4-hydroxyacetophen-one (6), and p-hydroxybenzaldehyde (7). The remaining compounds consist of 1 furan derivative, 5-hydroxymethyl furfural (8), and 1 steroid saponin, β-sitosterol-3-O-β-d-glucoside (9). Compounds 1-7, 9, and 10 were reported from the genus Aletris for the first time.
