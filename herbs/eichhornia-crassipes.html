Eichhornia crassipes

What's the taxonomical classification of Eichhornia crassipes?

Eichhornia crassipes belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Commelinales and is a member of the family Pontederiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Eichhornia, and its species name, crassipes.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eichhornia crassipes has thick, bulbous, spongy petioles that are inflated with air to provide buoyancy for its floating lifestyle. These green, succulent leaf stalks are connected to long, bright green leaves that are elliptical or lanceolate in shape. The plant produces clusters of lavender or light purple flowers with six petals that emerge from the center of the rosette. Beneath the water surface, it develops a dense network of feathery, fibrous roots that aid in nutrient absorption and stability. This complex structure allows the plant to form massive, interconnected mats that can rapidly cover the entire surface of a water body.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South America, specifically spanning across Brazil, Paraguay, Uruguay, and Argentina. While its origins are confined to these specific river basins, it has been introduced to various aquatic ecosystems worldwide. It now thrives in many warm climates across Africa, Asia, and parts of North America. The species colonizes freshwater bodies like lakes, ponds, and slow-moving rivers where temperatures remain high. This widespread expansion has transformed it into one of the most invasive aquatic species globally.

What are the pharmacological activities of Eichhornia crassipes?

This plant has anti-arthritic, anti-inflammatory, anti-tubercular, anti-cancer, and antioxidant pharmacological activities. Its phytochemical constituents work to neutralize free radicals, which contributes significantly to its potent antioxidant properties. These bioactive compounds also inhibit inflammatory pathways, making the species effective for treating arthritis and other inflammatory conditions. Furthermore, research indicates that certain extracts possess the ability to combat tuberculosis bacteria and inhibit the proliferation of malignant cells. These diverse medicinal qualities highlight the potential of the species in developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, Eichhornia crassipes demonstrated anti-arthritic characteristics by inhibiting arthritic progress and ameliorating paw edema in a Sprague-Dawley rat model. The treatment inhibited the expression levels of inflammatory markers including tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), interleukin-1β (IL-1β), interleukin-6 (IL-6), nuclear factor KappaB (NF-κB), matrix metalloproteinase (MMP-2 and MMP-3), and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). Additionally, serum prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) levels were reduced in the treatment groups. Biochemical investigations showed improvements in hepatic markers, indicating the plant has no hepatotoxic or nephrotoxic effects at the studied dose. GC-MS analysis further displayed phytochemicals with known anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

As discussed in a study, Eichhornia crassipes contains different medicinal compounds such as alkaloids and terpenoids. A crude extract was processed to separate 9 fractions, labeled A through I, using a mobile phase of hexane/ethyl acetate at an 8.5: 1.5 v/v ratio. These identified compounds were tested for antioxidant and anticancer activities against 4 cell lines, including MCF-7, HeLa, Hep.G2, and EACC. Among the isolated substances, compound I exhibited the most potent anticancer activity against the HepG2 cell line, while compound D showed high anticancer activity against HeLa cells and EACC. The antioxidant activity was highest in the crude extract, followed in descending order by compounds D, E, G, and H.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating rheumatoid arthritis, cancer, and tuberculosis. Studies also explore its potential efficacy in inhibiting the growth of various cancer cell lines. Furthermore, the plant shows promise in addressing infectious diseases like tuberculosis through its medicinal properties. It is also studied for its ability to help mitigate the harmful effects of microcystis aeruginosa in aquatic environments.

As discussed in a study, Eichhornia crassipes flowers inhibited arthritic progress and ameliorated paw edema in a Sprague-Dawley rat model. The treatment with Eichhornia crassipes inhibited the expression levels of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, NF-κB, MMP-2, MMP-3, and VEGF. Additionally, serum PGE2 levels were found to be reduced in the treatment groups. Histopathological analysis of ankle joints and hematological analysis confirmed the diminution of rheumatoid arthritis in the plant-treated groups. Biochemical investigations showed improvements in hepatic markers, indicating that Eichhornia crassipes has no hepatotoxic or nephrotoxic effects at the studied dose.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause physical injury through skin irritation, respiratory distress from pollen, and the displacement of aquatic life due to its invasive growth. Contact with the plant's surface may lead to dermatological reactions or allergic dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Inhaling its pollen or particles can trigger asthma attacks or other allergic respiratory responses. Because it forms dense mats on water surfaces, it poses a significant risk to swimmers by creating entanglement hazards. Furthermore, its rapid spread can deplete oxygen levels in ecosystems, indirectly affecting the health of local communities that rely on those waters.
