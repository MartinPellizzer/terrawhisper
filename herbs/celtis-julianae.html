Celtis julianae

What's the taxonomical classification of Celtis julianae?

Celtis julianae belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Rosales and falls within the family Cannabaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Celtis and the species julianae.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Celtis julianae targets specific enzymes and inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. The bioactive secondary metabolites within the plant, including various alkaloids and flavonoids, act as ligands that bind to these protein structures. By inhibiting the cyclooxygenase pathway, these compounds effectively reduce the production of prostaglandins, which are responsible for pain and swelling. Furthermore, the interaction with cytokine signaling pathways helps to modulate the overall immune response in the body. These molecular interactions collectively contribute to the plant's observed antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

    What are the therapeutic actions of Celtis julianae?

    Celtis julianae has medicinal properties that primarily involve its ability to act as an anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing agent through the application of its bark and leaves. Traditional practitioners often utilize decoctions derived from the plant to treat various skin ailments and infections. The bioactive compounds within the foliage assist in reducing swelling and soothing irritated tissues when applied topically. Furthermore, certain preparations are used to address internal digestive issues or to alleviate systemic discomfort. These therapeutic actions make the species a significant component in regional ethnobotanical practices.

    What diseases this plant treats?

    This plant is used to treat skin infections, intestinal parasites, and various digestive ailments. The bark is frequently prepared as a decoction to alleviate symptoms of stomach pain and dysentery. Additionally, topical applications of its extracts are utilized to manage localized skin irritations and fungal issues. Traditional practitioners also employ the plant to address systemic inflammatory conditions. These medicinal properties are deeply rooted in indigenous ethnobotanical practices.

    What are the herbal preparations of Celtis julianae?

    Celtis julianae is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a medicinal infusion of the leaves, or a topical poultice made from crushed plant material. The bark is typically boiled in water for an extended period to extract its active tannins for internal use. Leaf infusions are often used to create soothing teas that address various inflammatory conditions. For external skin ailments, the fresh leaves can be macerated into a paste to be applied directly to the affected area. These diverse preparation methods allow practitioners to target different physiological needs using the specific parts of the tree.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Celtis julianae can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis if the sap or bark comes into direct contact with sensitive human tissue. Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or localized swelling upon touching the plant. There is also a potential for gastrointestinal distress if any part of the tree is accidentally ingested. While specific toxicological studies on this species are limited, caution is advised when handling the plant to prevent unexpected reactions. Always monitor for any signs of a rash or discomfort following exposure.
