Celtis julianae

What's the taxonomical classification of Celtis julianae?

Celtis julianae belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Rosales and falls within the family Cannabaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Celtis and the species julianae.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Celtis julianae has simple, alternate leaves with ovate to elliptical shapes, serrated margins, and prominent pinnate venation.

The foliage typically exhibits a glossy texture on the upper surface and a paler green color underneath. Its woody stems feature characteristic lenticels and a bark texture that may become somewhat furrowed with age. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into fleshy drupes used for dispersal.

These morphological traits are essential for distinguishing this specific species within the Celtis genus.

        What are the herbal preparations of Celtis julianae?

        Celtis julianae is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a medicinal infusion of the leaves, or a topical poultice made from crushed plant material. The bark is typically boiled in water for an extended period to extract its active tannins for internal use. Leaf infusions are often used to create soothing teas that address various inflammatory conditions. For external skin ailments, the fresh leaves can be macerated into a paste to be applied directly to the affected area. These diverse preparation methods allow practitioners to target different physiological needs using the specific parts of the tree.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Celtis julianae can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis if the sap or bark comes into direct contact with sensitive human tissue. Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or localized swelling upon touching the plant. There is also a potential for gastrointestinal distress if any part of the tree is accidentally ingested. While specific toxicological studies on this species are limited, caution is advised when handling the plant to prevent unexpected reactions. Always monitor for any signs of a rash or discomfort following exposure.
