Combretum indicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Combretum indicum?

Combretum indicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, falling under the order Myrtales. The plant is a member of the family Combretaceae and is further categorized into the genus Combretum, with its specific designation being the species indicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Combretum indicum has a woody, climbing shrub morphology characterized by slender, twining stems that can reach significant lengths through liana-like growth. The leaves are typically arranged in an opposite or sub-opposite pattern and feature an elliptical or oblong shape with entire margins. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in clusters, often displaying a tubular structure adapted for specific pollinators. The plant may develop specialized structures like aerial roots or tendril-like branches to assist in its climbing habit. Its overall architecture allows it to navigate through dense forest canopies to reach sunlight.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, specifically ranging from West Africa through Central Africa to East Africa and parts of Southern Africa. It thrives primarily in savanna ecosystems and open woodland habitats across the continent. The species is often found in areas with seasonal rainfall patterns that support its woody climbing habit. Its presence is most notable in countries such as South Africa, Zimbabwe, and various nations within the Congo Basin. Environmental factors like soil composition and temperature in these specific latitudes dictate its local density and growth patterns.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as Ketumpang or Indian Almond, thrives in tropical climates with well-drained soil and requires full sunlight to reach its maximum growth potential.

It is typically propagated through seeds or cuttings and benefits from regular watering during its early establishment phase. Once mature, the tree can tolerate varying soil types but performs best in nutrient-rich, moist environments. Growers must monitor for pests and ensure the tree has sufficient space due to its large canopy and spreading root system.

Proper pruning helps maintain its shape and encourages healthy foliage development.

What traditional systems uses this plant?

This plant, known as Black Catechu, has been utilized for centuries in Ayurvedic and Unani medicine to treat gastrointestinal issues, skin inflammation, and wounds.

Historically, the extract from its heartwood was processed into a substance called catechu for use in various traditional remedies. It was frequently applied topically to soothe ulcers and promote the healing of damaged tissues. In many cultures, the powder was also consumed as an astringent to manage diarrhea and bleeding gums.

These longstanding practices highlight the plant's central role in ancient pharmacological traditions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Combretum indicum?

This plant has anthelmintic and anti-infectious pharmacological activities that allow it to combat various parasitic and microbial threats. The extract demonstrates a significant ability to expel intestinal worms, making it a valuable resource for managing helminth infections. Its anti-infectious properties further extend its utility by inhibiting the growth of harmful bacteria and other pathogens. Researchers have identified specific bioactive compounds within the leaves and bark that drive these therapeutic effects. These diverse medicinal capabilities highlight the importance of the species in traditional and modern pharmacology.

According to a study published by "Antibiotics (Basel, Switzerland)", Combretum indicum is one of the most commonly used species in treatments of infectious diseases. This plant was identified through a meta-analysis of data from 16 ethnobotanical studies of 25 Karen villages. Among the 127 plant species from 59 plant families used by the Karen, Combretum indicum stands out as a priority for pharmacological testing. The researchers suggest that this specific species should be among the first to undergo testing for the possible development of medicines.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, dizziness, and headache in individuals who consume it. The potential for low blood sugar is a significant concern, especially for those already managing glucose levels. Gastrointestinal distress often manifests as painful cramping accompanied by upset stomach or frequent bowel movements. Some users may also experience neurological symptoms such as lightheadedness or persistent head pain. Monitoring these adverse reactions is essential when interacting with this botanical species.
