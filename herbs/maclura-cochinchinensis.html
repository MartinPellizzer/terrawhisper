Maclura cochinchinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Maclura cochinchinensis?

Maclurura cochinchinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, the plant is part of the order Rosales and is placed within the family Moraeaee. Finally, it is identified by the genus Maclurura and the specific species epithet cochinchinensis.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Maclurura cochinchinenesis contains bioactive compounds in its bark, leaves, and fruit that are utilized for various traditional medicinal purposes. The bark is frequently harvested to prepare decoctions used in treating inflammatory conditions and skin ailments. Extracts from the leaves are often applied topically or ingested to address digestive issues and fever. Additionally, the fruit is sometimes used in folk remedies to alleviate specific internal discomforts. These diverse plant parts provide a range of therapeutic applications depending on the specific ailment being treated.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin infections, intestinal parasites, and inflammatory conditions through its medicinal properties. The bark and sap contain compounds that act as antimicrobial agents against specific bacteria and fungi. Local traditional practices often involve applying extracts to wounds or skin lesions to promote healing. Additionally, certain preparations are ingested to combat internal ailments such as stomach discomfort or parasitic infestations. These medicinal applications highlight the plant's importance in ethnobotanical studies regarding tropical flora.

What are the herbal preparations of Maclura cochinchinensis?

Maclurura cochinchiensis is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a topical paste from the fruit, or an infusion of the leaves for various medicinal applications. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a strong liquid used for treating skin ailments or internal inflammation. When the fruit is used, it is often mashed into a poultice and applied directly to wounds to promote healing. Leaf infusions are sometimes consumed as a tea to address digestive discomfort or fever. These diverse preparations allow practitioners to target different physiological symptoms using specific parts of the plant.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Maclurura cochinchiensis can cause severe skin irritation, swelling, and blistering due to the presence of toxic milky sap in its stems and leaves. Contact with this latex often results in contact dermatitis, characterized by redness and intense itching. If the sap enters the eyes, it may trigger significant inflammation and potential vision damage. Ingestion of the plant parts is considered dangerous and can lead to gastrointestinal distress or systemic toxicity. Because of these potent chemical properties, handling the plant requires protective gear to avoid accidental exposure.
