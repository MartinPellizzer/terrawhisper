Maclura cochinchinensis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Maclurura cochinchinenesis has a woody structure characterized by large, spreading crowns, pinnately compound leaves with multiple leaflets, and small, yellowish-green flowers. The bark of the tree is typically smooth and greyish, providing a stark contrast to the dense foliage. Each leaf consists of several pairs of leaflets that exhibit an ovate to elliptical shape with entire margins. The plant produces small, fleshy drupes that serve as its primary reproductive structures. These fruits eventually mature into a color that attracts various seed dispersers within its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically ranging from southern China and Vietnam through Thailand, Malaysia, and into Indonesia. It thrives primarily within the humid, lowland rainforest ecosystems of these subtropical and tropical latitudes. The species is most frequently documented in dense, evergreen forests where moisture levels remain consistently high. Its distribution follows the moisture-rich corridors of the Indochinese Peninsula and the Malay Archipelago. Due to these specific environmental requirements, it is rarely found outside of these warm, wet maritime and continental zones.

What are the herbal preparations of Maclura cochinchinensis?

Maclurura cochinchiensis is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a topical paste from the fruit, or an infusion of the leaves for various medicinal applications. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a strong liquid used for treating skin ailments or internal inflammation. When the fruit is used, it is often mashed into a poultice and applied directly to wounds to promote healing. Leaf infusions are sometimes consumed as a tea to address digestive discomfort or fever. These diverse preparations allow practitioners to target different physiological symptoms using specific parts of the plant.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Maclurura cochinchiensis can cause severe skin irritation, swelling, and blistering due to the presence of toxic milky sap in its stems and leaves. Contact with this latex often results in contact dermatitis, characterized by redness and intense itching. If the sap enters the eyes, it may trigger significant inflammation and potential vision damage. Ingestion of the plant parts is considered dangerous and can lead to gastrointestinal distress or systemic toxicity. Because of these potent chemical properties, handling the plant requires protective gear to avoid accidental exposure.
