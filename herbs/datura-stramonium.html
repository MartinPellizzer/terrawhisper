Datura stramonium

What's the taxonomical classification of Datura stramonium?

Datura stramonium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its hierarchical descent, the plant is placed in the order Solanales and belongs to the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by its placement in the genus Datura, with the unique species designation stramonium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Datura stramonium has an erect, herbaceous growth habit characterized by hollow, branched stems that are often covered in fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are large, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and possess irregular, coarsely toothed margins. Its prominent white or pale purple flowers are trumpet-shaped and bloom from a central axis. Once pollination occurs, the plant produces distinctive, egg-shaped capsules that are heavily covered in sharp spines. These seed-bearing fruits eventually split open to release numerous small, dark seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to North and South America but has become widely naturalized across much of the globe, including Europe, Asia, Africa, and Australia. It typically thrives in disturbed habitats such as roadsides, fields, and waste areas where sunlight is abundant. Because of its ability to colonize varied environments, it is often classified as an invasive species in many non-native regions. Its distribution is heavily influenced by human activity and the movement of soil or seeds across international borders. The species successfully inhabits a wide range of climates, from temperate zones to tropical regions.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Datura stramonium contains tropane alkaloids such as atropine, hyoscyamine, and scopolamine located primarily within its seeds, leaves, and roots. These toxic compounds are most concentrated in the mature seeds, which are often used in highly controlled traditional remedies. The leaves are sometimes applied topically or used in infusions to treat respiratory ailments or skin irritations. Because the entire plant is highly poisonous, the dosage must be extremely precise to avoid severe hallucinations or death. Historically, practitioners have utilized the roots for their potent sedative and anticholinergic properties.

What are the pharmacological activities of Datura stramonium?

This plant has anticholinergic, hallucinogenic, sedative, anticonvulsant, and bronchodilator pharmacological activities driven primarily by its tropane alkaloid content. The presence of atropine, scopolamine, and hyoscyamine allows the plant to block acetylcholine receptors in the nervous system. These chemical compounds induce significant hallucinogenic effects and can act as potent sedatives in specific doses. Medicinally, the bronchodilator properties are utilized to treat respiratory conditions by relaxing airway muscles. Additionally, certain extracts have demonstrated anticonvulsant potential in various pharmacological studies.

As discussed in a study, Datura stramonium contains alkaloids, most of which are atropine. The consumption of any part of the plant causes atropine intoxication. This specific intoxication results in anticholinergic delirium. Because of its components, Datura stramonium is seemingly becoming popular as a hallucinogenic drug that can result in hallucinations.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains tropane alkaloids, specifically belladonna alkaloids such as atropine, hyoscyamine, and scopolamine. These medicinal compounds act as potent anticholinergic agents within the human body. Atropine is frequently utilized to increase heart rate and counteract certain types of poisoning. Hyoscyamine serves as a key component in treating gastrointestinal spasms and cramping. Scopolamine is widely recognized for its effectiveness in preventing motion sickness and nausea.

As discussed in a study, Datura stramonium contains the medicinal alkaloids scopoalamine and atropine which possess anticholinergic properties. The accumulation of these compounds can be influenced by methyl jasmonate, a plant growth regulator. Applying methyl jasmonate at 150 µM increased the production of scopoalamine and atropine in both leaves and roots. However, using methyl jasmonate at 300 µM had an adverse effect on the production of these alkaloids. The study also analyzed three critical enzymes in the biosynthetic pathway, including PMT, TR1, and h6h.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment of respiratory ailments including asthma, bronchial asthma, chronic bronchitis, and various chest complaints, as well as for managing pain. Its alkaloids act as antispasmodics to relax the smooth muscles in the airways. This relaxation helps to alleviate the constriction associated with chronic bronchitis and bronchial asthma. Additionally, the plant has been applied topically or internally to mitigate different types of physical pain. Careful dosage is required because its medicinal effects are closely linked to its toxicity.

As discussed in a study, the smoke of one Datura stramonium cigarette was measured in 12 asthmatic patients with mild airway obstruction to determine its effect on specific airway resistance (sRaw). In 11 patients, sRaw decreased substantially, with a mean maximal decrease of 40% at the 30th minute. Seven patients experienced no further decrease in sRaw after the subsequent inhalation of 200 micrograms of salbutamol, while the remaining four patients saw a larger decrease in sRaw from salbutamol than from the cigarette smoke. In one patient, the cigarette smoke produced only a negligible amount of bronchodilatation. Minor side effects were observed in 6 patients after the cigarette.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion, tincture, decoction, extract, or poultice for various topical or internal applications. Practitioners sometimes create a decoction by boiling the tough roots to pull out concentrated alkaloids. An infusion of the leaves is often used for localized treatments, while a tincture provides a shelf-stable liquid dose. Concentrated extracts offer a potent method for measuring specific chemical components. A topical poultice of crushed plant material may also be applied directly to the skin for certain medicinal purposes.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe anticholinergic toxicity characterized by nausea, vomiting, dizziness, tachycardia, hallucinations, confusion, delirium, dry mouth, blurred vision, mydriasis, urinary retention, constipation, seizures, hyperthermia, ataxia, paranoia, coma, and death. Consuming its alkaloids disrupts the central nervous system and autonomic functions, leading to profound mental disturbances and physical impairment. The physiological strain often manifests as a rapid heart rate and an inability to urinate or pass stool. In extreme cases, the combination of neurological chaos and metabolic dysfunction can trigger life-threatening seizures or a complete loss of consciousness. Such extreme toxicity makes the ingestion of this species a fatal medical emergency.
