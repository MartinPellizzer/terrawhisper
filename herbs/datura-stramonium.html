Datura stramonium

What's the taxonomical classification of Datura stramonium?

Datura stramonium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its hierarchical descent, the plant is placed in the order Solanales and belongs to the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by its placement in the genus Datura, with the unique species designation stramonium.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Datura stramonium contains high concentrations of tropane alkaloids, specifically atropine, hyoscyamine, and scopolamine. These specific compounds act as competitive antagonists at muscarinic acetylcholine receptors within the nervous system. In controlled clinical settings, these substances can be utilized to treat certain types of bradycardia or to induce mydriasis during ophthalmic examinations. However, the therapeutic window for these chemicals is exceptionally narrow and difficult to manage. Because the alkaloid content varies significantly between individual plants, even small doses can quickly lead to severe anticholinergic toxicity.

What are the therapeutic actions of Datura stramonium?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat asthma, bronchitis, and various gastrointestinal spasms through its alkaloid properties. The plant contains tropane alkaloids such as atropine and scopolamine which act as antispasmodics. These compounds help to relax smooth muscles in the airways and digestive tract. Historically, it has also been applied topically to alleviate certain skin irritations. However, extreme caution is required because the therapeutic window is very narrow. Precise dosing is critical to avoid severe toxicity or hallucinations.

What are the herbal preparations of Datura stramonium?

Datura stramonium is prepared as aqueous infusions, decoctions, topical tinctures, or concentrated extracts derived from its leaves, seeds, and roots. These preparations utilize the plant's tropane alkaloids to create potent medicinal or ritualistic substances. Because the concentration of alkaloids varies significantly between individual plants, dosage is notoriously difficult to standardize. Historical uses include applying crushed leaves as a poultice for skin ailments or ingesting diluted teas for sedative effects. Due to its high toxicity and unpredictable potency, modern herbal practice strictly avoids most internal preparations of this species.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Datura stramonium can cause severe anticholinergic toxicity characterized by intense hallucinations, extreme tachycardia, hyperthermia, urinary retention, and profound cognitive disorientation. The alkaloids present in the plant disrupt the central nervous system, often leading to terrifying visual and auditory distortions that can last for several days. Physical symptoms frequently include dilated pupils, a completely dry mouth, and dangerously high body temperatures. In many cases, the consumption of this plant results in a state of delirium that prevents the individual from distinguishing reality from illusion. This toxic reaction can escalate into seizures, coma, or even death if medical intervention is not provided immediately.
