Sonchus oleraceus

What's the taxonomical classification of Sonchus oleraceus?

Sonchus oleraceus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Asterales. As a member of the family Asteraceae, it falls under the genus Sonchus, and its specific scientific designation is the species oleraceus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sonchus oleaceus has erect, branched stems that reach up to 1 meter in height and are characterized by a hollow structure with milky white latex throughout the tissues. The leaves are primarily basal and lyrate, featuring large terminal lobes and smaller, pinnatifid lobes along the margins. Higher up the stem, the leaves become smaller, sessile, and more deeply lobed or lanceolate in shape. The inflorescence consists of bright yellow composite flower heads arranged in terminal clusters. Each flower head contains numerous tubular florets surrounded by a distinct involucre of linear bracts.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean Basin, including parts of Southern Europe, North Africa, and Western Asia. Its range extends across coastal and inland regions where it thrives in varied terrains. Many populations are found in disturbed habitats, such as roadsides and waste areas, throughout these warm climates. It is frequently documented in countries like Spain, Italy, Greece, and Morocco. The species favors temperate zones that provide seasonal moisture and significant sunlight.

What are the pharmacological activities of Sonchus oleraceus?

This plant has anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, gastroprotective, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within cellular structures. The anti-inflammatory components work to mitigate swelling and pain by modulating various biochemical pathways. Furthermore, the plant exhibits protective effects on the stomach lining and the liver to prevent tissue damage. These diverse therapeutic actions suggest that Sonchus oleraceus holds significant potential for medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, Sonchus oleraceus Linn is a medicinal plant that possesses bioactivities such as anti-diabetic and anti-inflammatory activity. To address the poor absorption of its polyphenols, a self-emulsifying delivery system containing Sonchus oleraceus Linn extracts was investigated for its anti-inflammatory effect. In diabetic rats, daily feeding of 100, 200, and 400 mg/kg/day of SSEDS for 4 weeks significantly restored NF-κB, TNF-α, and IL-6 levels in the liver to near normal levels (p < 0.05). This effect was comparable to the results seen with 1 mg/kg/day of metformin (p < 0.05). Immuno-histochemical studies further confirmed these anti-inflammatory effects.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains ferulic acid, flavonoids, phenolic acids, rutin, and ursolic acid, which serve as its primary medicinal compounds. These bioactive substances work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. The presence of various phenolic acids and flavonoids helps neutralize harmful free radicals in cells. Specifically, rutin acts as a potent antioxidant that supports vascular health and circulation. Furthermore, the inclusion of ursolic acid contributes to the plant's potential roles in regulating metabolism and protecting against cellular damage.

As discussed in a study, Sonchus oleraceus contains thirty-two bioactive metabolites including flavonoids, phenolic acids, and terpenes. The extract SOEtOH was administered at doses of 50, 100, and 200 mg/kg to study its effects. These compounds can replenish depleted hepatic glutathione up to 3.9-fold and superoxide dismutase up to 4.7-fold. Specific compounds such as luteolin-O-dihexoside, isorhamnetin-O-hexoside, di-O-caffeoylquinic, and kaempferol-O-hexoside showed the strongest binding affinities. These metabolites contribute to the catalytic binding sites of JNk1 and JNk2.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, gastric ailments, infection, diabetes mellitus, and wounds. Its medicinal properties help soothe internal digestive issues and manage various gastric ailments effectively. The plant also possesses antibacterial qualities that are applied to fight infection and promote the healing of open wounds. Additionally, certain extracts are utilized in traditional practices to assist in the management of diabetes mellitus. These diverse therapeutic applications demonstrate the plant's significant role in folk medicine.

As discussed in a study, Sonchus oleraceus Linn is an edible medicinal plant possessing anti-inflammatory activity. To address the poor absorption and rapid metabolism of its polyphenols, a self-emulsifying delivery system containing Sonchus oleraceus Linn extracts (SSEDDS) was introduced. When diabetic rats were fed daily doses of 100, 200, and 400 mg/kg/day of SSEDDS for 4 weeks, the levels of NF-κB, TNF-α, and IL-6 in the liver were significantly improved (p < 0.05). These parameters were restored to near normal levels in comparison to the 1 mg/kg/day metformin group (p < 0.05). Finally, immuno-histochemical studies confirmed the anti-inflammatory effects of SSEDDS.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Contact with the milky sap may lead to redness, itching, or contact dermatitis on the skin. If accidentally ingested, it might result in nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramping. Some people may also experience more severe allergic symptoms like swelling or rashes. Monitoring any physical response after handling the plant is advisable to avoid complications.
