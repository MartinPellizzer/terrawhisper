Aesculus turbinata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aesculus turbinata has large, palmately compound leaves typically consisting of five to seven leaflets that are lanceolate to ovate in shape with serrated margins. The tree produces upright, conical panicles of creamy white flowers that emerge in late spring. Its bark becomes deeply furrowed and textured as the specimen reaches maturity. The fruit is a large, globose nut contained within a thick, greenish-brown husk that splits open to reveal a dark seed. This species is distinguished from other buckeyes by its specific leaf architecture and the characteristic shape of its terminal flower clusters.

What are the pharmacological activities of Aesculus turbinata?

Its bioactive compounds demonstrate a capacity to reduce fat accumulation and manage weight in various biological models. The plant also exhibits significant antiviral properties that can combat different viral pathogens. Furthermore, its cytotoxic activities suggest potential for targeted cell death in specialized therapeutic contexts. Specifically, research has highlighted its ability to interfere with the replication process of Porcine Epidemic Diarrhea Virus.

As discussed in a study, total escins at 1 mg/ml and two isolated compounds, escin Ib and IIa, demonstrated inhibitory effects on pancreatic lipase activity in vitro. In vivo, total escins suppressed increases in body weight, parametrial adipose tissue weight, and hepatic triacylglycerol and total cholesterol contents in mice. Plasma triacylglycerol levels in rats were also reduced at 1, 2, and 3 h after the oral administration of lipid emulsions combined with different concentrations of escins. Furthermore, mice fed a high fat diet supplemented with 2% total escins for 3 d showed increased triacylglycerol levels in the feces. These results suggest that the plant's escins may delay intestinal absorption of dietary fat by inhibiting pancreatic lipase activity.

As discussed in a study, Aesculus turbinata contains many saponin mixtures called escins. Escin derivatives 1-7, which were developed without angeloyl or tigloyl groups and with modified glycosidic linkages by hydrolysis, showed strong inhibitory activities on PEDV replication. These specific compounds 1-7 had no cytotoxicity at 20μM on VERO cells. In contrast, compounds 8-10 showed strong cytotoxicity at similar concentrations on PEDV.
