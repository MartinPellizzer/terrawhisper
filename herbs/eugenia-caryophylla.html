Cloves (Eugenia caryophylla): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Cloves, derived from the dried flower buds of the Eugenia caryophylla tree, are a key spice and medicinal herb used in traditional herbal medicine for their antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Cloves (Eugenia Caryophylla)?

Cloves (Eugenia caryophylla) are the dried flower buds of a tropical evergreen tree native to the Maluku Islands of Indonesia, widely used in herbal medicine for their potent aromatic properties and medicinal benefits.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cloves is Eugenia caryophylla. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Myrtaceae, genus Eugenia, and species Eugenia caryophylla. Common names for Cloves include "cloves" in the Indian subcontinent and "kālā kāmān" in parts of Southeast Asia. It is also known as "syzygium aromaticum" in botanical contexts and "laksa" in some regional markets. Synonyms for Cloves include Syzygium aromaticum, which is its former botanical name, and regional names such as "clove stick" and "clove bud."

Plant Description and Morphology

Cloves is a small evergreen tree or shrub that typically grows to a height of 8 to 12 meters, with a dense, rounded canopy and a thick, woody trunk.

Its leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptical in shape, with a smooth margin and a glossy, dark green upper surface. The flowers are small, fragrant, and clustered in terminal spikes, with pale yellow petals and a distinctive, aromatic scent. The root system is taprooted, with a strong, central root that anchors the plant deeply into the soil.

The dried flower buds, known as cloves, are dark brown, have a rough, irregular surface, and emit a strong, pungent aroma.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cloves is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, particularly the islands of Indonesia, Madagascar, and the Comoros.

It thrives in humid, subtropical climates with high rainfall and warm temperatures year-round. The plant prefers well-drained, fertile soils and requires ample sunlight for optimal growth. It is commonly found in coastal areas and lowland forests where the climate remains consistently warm and moist.

Its natural distribution has been expanded through cultivation in other tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in cloves are the dried flower buds, which contain the essential oil and active compounds.

The buds are harvested when they are just beginning to open, ensuring maximum potency. The oil is extracted through steam distillation and is the main source of eugenol, a key therapeutic component. In traditional medicine, the whole bud is often used, either whole or ground into a powder.

The leaves and stems are occasionally used, though less frequently than the buds.

Phytochemical Composition

Cloves contain a variety of active compounds, including eugenol, which is the primary aromatic component responsible for its medicinal properties.

Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids and tannins contribute to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects. Chemical markers like caryophyllene and alpha-humulene are also present and play roles in its therapeutic applications. These compounds work synergistically to enhance the overall efficacy of clove in herbal medicine.

The specific composition can vary depending on the plant's origin and processing methods.

Pharmacological Properties

Cloves (Eugenia caryophylla) exhibit a range of pharmacological properties that make them valuable in herbal medicine.

Their therapeutic actions include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects. The biological activity of clove is largely attributed to its essential oil content, particularly eugenol. Pharmacodynamically, eugenol interacts with various receptors and enzymes, contributing to its therapeutic effects.

System-level effects of clove include gastrointestinal protection and potential anti-cancer properties.

Mechanisms of Action

Cloves exert their therapeutic effects through multiple mechanisms that involve interactions at the cellular, biochemical, and systemic levels.

At the cellular level, clove compounds such as eugenol and caryophyllin exhibit antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties by modulating oxidative stress and inflammatory cytokine production. Biochemically, these compounds inhibit key enzymes like cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase, which are central to the inflammatory response. They also interfere with signaling pathways such as NF-κB and MAPK, which regulate gene expression related to inflammation and cell survival.

Target systems include the gastrointestinal tract, where clove reduces inflammation and protects against ulcers, and the respiratory system, where it exhibits antimicrobial and antiviral activities.

Therapeutic Uses

Cloves are widely used in herbal medicine for their therapeutic properties, particularly due to their antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects.

They are commonly employed to treat digestive issues such as indigestion, gas, and bloating. Cloves are also used to alleviate toothache and oral infections because of their high eugenol content, which has numbing and antiseptic properties. Additionally, they may help with respiratory conditions like coughs and colds by acting as a decongestant.

Cloves are often incorporated into traditional remedies for their ability to improve circulation and reduce pain.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods for cloves involve various forms in which the plant is utilized, primarily as whole buds, ground powder, or essential oil.

Whole clove buds are commonly used in culinary and medicinal applications, often added whole to teas or decoctions. Ground clove powder is frequently incorporated into spice blends, herbal formulations, and topical applications. Essential oil extracted from clove buds is used in aromatherapy and as a concentrated medicinal extract.

These forms allow for different modes of consumption and therapeutic applications depending on the desired effect.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of cloves for most herbal uses is typically 0.3 to 1 gram per day, depending on the intended effect and individual tolerance.

Extracts should be diluted in a carrier oil or liquid to ensure safe consumption, as concentrated forms can be irritating. Cloves should be taken no more than three times daily to avoid gastrointestinal discomfort. The recommended duration of use is generally up to four weeks, with a break recommended before prolonged use.

Always consult a healthcare provider before starting clove supplementation, especially for individuals with pre-existing medical conditions.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in appropriate amounts, cloves are generally safe for most people, but they can cause side effects such as stomach upset, heartburn, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

High doses may lead to gastrointestinal irritation, nausea, or vomiting, especially when consumed in large quantities or for prolonged periods. Cloves should be avoided by individuals with known allergies to the plant family Myrtaceae, which includes eucalyptus and other related species. During pregnancy, cloves are considered possibly unsafe due to limited research on their effects, and it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before use.

Cloves may interact with certain medications, so it is important to discuss their use with a qualified medical professional.

Drug Interactions

Cloves may interact with medications that affect blood clotting, blood pressure, or blood sugar levels, and may also enhance the effects of certain antidepressants and anticoagulants.

Toxicity and Precautions

Cloves (Eugenia caryophylla) are generally safe when used in recommended doses, but may cause allergic reactions in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with bleeding disorders due to potential anticoagulant effects.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cloves are cultivated primarily in tropical climates, with the majority of commercial production occurring in countries like Indonesia, India, and Sri Lanka, where they are harvested by hand from the flower buds before they fully open.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of clove essential oil typically involve steam distillation of the dried flower buds, resulting in a concentrated oil rich in eugenol, which is the primary active compound responsible for its medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cloves should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain their potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly stored.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has demonstrated that cloves contain potent bioactive compounds such as eugenol, which exhibit antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties, supporting their traditional use in herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Cloves have been traditionally used in herbal medicine for their antiseptic, analgesic, and carminative properties, with historical applications dating back to ancient civilizations such as Egypt, Greece, and India for treating dental pain, digestive issues, and respiratory infections.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, cloves are recognized as a food spice and are generally considered safe for consumption, but their use in medicinal applications may require regulatory approval or oversight depending on the jurisdiction.
