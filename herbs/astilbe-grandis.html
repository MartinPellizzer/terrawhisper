What's the taxonomical classification of Astilbe grandis?

Astilbe grandis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its lineage through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is placed under the order Saxifragales. It is a member of the family Saxifragaceae, which is further refined into the genus Astilbe, ultimately identifying the specific species as grandis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Astilbe grandis has large, compound, serrated leaves and upright, pyramidal panicles of small, densely clustered flowers. The flowering spikes rise prominently above the greenery, showcasing a profusion of tiny blossoms. As the season progresses, the structural stems maintain their shape even as the flowers fade.

What are the pharmacological activities of Astilbe grandis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-tumor cytotoxicity, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties. Researchers have identified specific bioactive compounds within the plant that can inhibit the proliferation of various cancer cell lines. These chemical constituents work by inducing programmed cell death and disrupting the metabolic processes of malignant cells. Furthermore, the presence of polyphenols contributes to its ability to reduce oxidative stress and suppress inflammatory responses in biological systems. Such multifaceted biological effects suggest that the species holds considerable potential for future pharmaceutical development.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Astilbe grandis Stapf ex Wils contain two unprecedented oleane-type triterpene compounds 5 and 6, as well as four known triterpene compounds 1-4. These six compounds were evaluated for in vitro anti-tumor activities against A549, Caco-2, H460, and Skov-3 cell lines. All compounds exhibited obvious anti-Caco-2 activity with IC50 values ranging from 1.86 to 4.94 μM. Compound 6 also showed an apparent effect on A549 cells. Furthermore, compounds 1, 4, and 5 demonstrated relatively strong inhibitory activity against H460 cells' growth with IC50 values of 5.13 μM, 5.65 μM, and 8.85 μM, respectively.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and localized redness if the foliage or stems come into direct contact with sensitive skin. While it is primarily grown as an ornamental species, individuals with known allergies to members of the Saxifragaceae family may experience itching or swelling upon touch. Some people might also notice a mild rash if they handle the plant without wearing protective gloves. There is currently limited research regarding the systemic toxicity of consuming this plant, but it should not be ingested. Always exercise caution and consult a medical professional if an adverse reaction occurs.
