Ligularia lapathifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Ligularia lapathifolia?

Ligularia lapathifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae, placing it among the composite flowers, and is specifically identified by its genus, Ligularia, and its species designation, lapathifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ligularia lapathifolia has large, ovate to lanceolate leaves that are typically 10 to 30 centimeters long, featuring coarsely serrated margins and distinct, prominent venation. These leaves are arranged oppositely along thick, fleshy stems that can reach significant heights in wetland habitats. The foliage often exhibits a deep green color, sometimes showing reddish tints on the undersides or along the edges. During the flowering period, the plant produces large, terminal clusters of bright yellow, daisy-like flower heads. Each head consists of numerous ray florets surrounding a central disk, creating a striking visual display.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate regions of Northern and Central Europe, extending through parts of Siberia and into parts of East Asia. It thrives primarily in moist habitats such as riverbanks, marshes, and wet meadows. The species is frequently found in riparian zones where constant moisture is available. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of damp, alluvial soils found in these wetland ecosystems. Because it prefers these specific conditions, it remains localized to areas with consistent water access.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ligularia lapathifolia?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including cytotoxicity against various cancer cell lines. Further investigations are necessary to fully map the complex chemical interactions driving these biological effects.

As discussed in a study, six new highly oxygenated eremophilane-type sesquiterpene derivatives, labeled 1-6, including a norbisesquiterpene, were isolated from an extract of the roots of Ligularia lapathifolia. The structure of compound 1 was confirmed by single-crystal X-ray crystallography. The cytotoxicity of compounds 1, 2, 3, 5, and 6 was evaluated against selected cancer cell lines. These specific cell lines included human stomach carcinoma (MGC-803), human hepatoma (HEP-G2), and murine sarcoma (S-180) cell lines.
