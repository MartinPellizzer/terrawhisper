Oxytropis oxyphylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Oxytropis oxyphylla?

Oxytropis oxyphylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized into the order Fabales. It is a member of the family Fabaceae and falls under the genus Oxytropis, specifically identified as the species oxyphyllas.

These alkaloids primarily interact with nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in the central nervous system, which can modulate neurotransmission. They also influence acetylcholinesterase enzymes, potentially altering the breakdown of neurotransmitters within synaptic clefts. Some studies suggest these compounds may engage with ion channels to affect cellular membrane potential. Through these diverse protein interactions, the plant demonstrates complex pharmacological activity.

      What are the herbal preparations of Oxytropis oxyphylla?

      Oxytropis oxyphylla is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a dried powdered extract for medicinal use. These liquid preparations are often derived by boiling the plant parts in water to extract active alkaloids and glycosides. The dried plant material can also be ground into a fine powder to be encapsulated or mixed into topical salves. In some traditional practices, the plant is macerated in alcohol to create a concentrated tincture. These various forms allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Oxytropis oxyphylla can cause severe poisoning characterized by gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential death if ingested by livestock or humans. The plant contains toxic alkaloids that disrupt the central nervous system upon consumption. Animals that eat the plant often exhibit symptoms such as muscle tremors, incoordination, and extreme weakness. In many cases, high doses lead to sudden collapse or respiratory failure. Because the toxicity can be fatal, careful management of grazing areas is necessary to prevent accidental ingestion.
