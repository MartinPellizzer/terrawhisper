Anagallis arvensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Anagallis arvensis?

Anagallis arvensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Ericales and the family Primulaceae. The plant is specifically identified by its genus, Anagallis, and its species, arvensis.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Anagallis arvensis targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, lipoxygenase proteins, and various inflammatory cytokines to mediate its therapeutic properties. The bioactive compounds within the plant act as inhibitors to prevent the conversion of arachidonic acid into pro-inflammatory mediators like prostaglandins and leukotrienes. By modulating these specific protein pathways, the plant demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory and analgesic potential in biological systems. Research also suggests that its phytochemical constituents may interact with antioxidant enzymes to reduce oxidative stress at a cellular level. These multifaceted molecular interactions allow the plant to influence complex signaling cascades involved in pain and swelling.

    What are the therapeutic actions of Anagallis arvensis?

    Anagallis arvensis has antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties derived from its diverse concentration of secondary metabolites like flavonoids and polyphenols.       What are the herbal preparations of Anagallis arvensis?

      Anagallis arvensis is prepared as an infusion, decoction, or tincture to utilize its medicinal properties. The dried aerial parts are most commonly steeped in hot water to create a gentle tea for internal consumption. For more concentrated therapeutic effects, the plant material is macerated in alcohol to produce a potent tincture. These liquid extracts allow for precise dosing and easier absorption of the plant's active compounds. Some traditional practices also involve using the plant in topical applications such as poultices or washes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Anagallis arvensis can cause localized skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and mild gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Individuals with sensitive skin may experience redness, itching, or a burning sensation upon direct contact with the plant's foliage. Some people might develop small, raised bumps or hives as an inflammatory response to the plant's chemical compounds. Ingestion of the plant is generally discouraged because it may lead to nausea, abdominal cramping, or vomiting. It is essential to handle this species with care to avoid these adverse physical reactions.
