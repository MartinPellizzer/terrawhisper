Botanical Identification of Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

To identify Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Valeriana officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Dipsacales, family Caprifoliaceae, genus Valeriana, and species Valeriana officinalis. These classifications help identify its place in the plant kingdom and its relationships with other plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Valeriana officinalis is the scientific name for valerian. It was first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. The species has several synonyms, including Valerianella officinalis and Valeriana acuta. Common names include valerian, garden valerian, and all-heal. Regional and historical names vary, with some ancient references using terms like "valerian" derived from the Latin "valere," meaning to be strong or healthy.

Morphological Characteristics

Valeriana officinalis is a perennial herb with a rhizomatous root system. It grows up to 1 meter tall, featuring erect, branched stems. The leaves are opposite, ovate to elliptic, and have a serrated margin. The inflorescence is a dense, spike-like cluster of small, greenish-yellow flowers. The fruit is a dry, three-lobed capsule containing small, dark brown seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Valerian can be distinguished by its square, hairy stems, opposite leaves with three to five lobes, and small, pinkish-white flowers in dense spikes. It has a strong, earthy scent and produces dark brown, elongated seeds. These features help differentiate it from similar species like V. officinalis.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found in meadows, woodlands, and along streambanks. It grows best at elevations between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Valerian flowers from June to August, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested in late summer when the flowers are in full bloom and the leaves are mature.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Valeriana officinalis belongs to the Valerianaceae family and is closely related to species like Valeriana edulis and Valeriana sylvatica. It shares key characteristics with other members of the genus, such as opposite leaves and floral structures, but differs in specific morphological and chemical traits. Its phylogenetic position highlights its evolutionary ties to other Valeriana species within the family.
