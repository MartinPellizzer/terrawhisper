Mentha spicata

What's the taxonomical classification of Mentha spicata?

Mentha spicata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is classified under the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Mentha and the specific species epithet spicata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mentha spicata has square stems, opposite lanceolate leaves with serrated margins, and terminal spikes of small, bilabiate purple or white flowers. The leaves are typically arranged in pairs along the stem and possess a textured, rugose surface with prominent venation. Its stem is distinctly quadrangular, which is a characteristic trait of the Lamiaceae family. The inflorescence consists of dense, elongated clusters of flowers that emerge from the upper nodes. Below the flower spikes, the plant often features a creeping rhizomatous root system that allows for vegetative spreading.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to much of Europe, western Asia, and north-western Africa, where it grows naturally in damp environments. Its range extends through temperate regions that provide the moisture necessary for its survival. While widespread across the Eurasian landmass, it can also be found in parts of North America due to human introduction. It thrives specifically in marshy areas, riverbanks, and wet meadows across these diverse continents. This broad distribution allows it to colonize many different types of wetland habitats.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Mentha spicata contains therapeutic compounds primarily located within its aerial parts, which consist of the leaves, stems, and flowering tops. The essential oils concentrated in these green tissues are the main source of medicinal properties used in herbal remedies. Practitioners often harvest the leaves to prepare infusions, tinctures, or steam inhalations for digestive and respiratory support. These specific parts are rich in menthol and carvone, which provide the plant's characteristic aroma and calming effects. Using the dried or fresh foliage allows for the extraction of bioactive molecules that assist in soothing gastrointestinal discomfort.

What are the pharmacological activities of Mentha spicata?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, antioxidant, carminative, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Its potent antimicrobial and antibacterial properties allow it to effectively inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. The presence of bioactive compounds also provides significant antioxidant effects by neutralizing free radicals in the body. Furthermore, its carminative action helps alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort by reducing gas and bloating. Finally, the plant demonstrates anti-inflammatory capabilities that assist in reducing swelling and systemic irritation.

According to a study published by "Foods (Basel, Switzerland)", Mentha spicata L. extracts contain bioactive phytochemicals that may have medicinal properties. The total phenolic content of the extracts ranged from 38.79 mg/g extract to 84.51 mg/g extract. Rosmarinic acid was the main phenolic compound detected in these cases. These extracts may have the ability to prevent food spoilage through antibacterial and antifungal effects. They also demonstrate antioxidant and anti-inflammatory capacities while not displaying toxicity against healthy cells.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds menthol, menthone, isomenthone, carvone, and limonene. Menthol serves as the primary constituent responsible for the cooling sensation and analgesic properties often used in topical treatments. Menthone and isomenthone contribute to the complex aromatic profile and potential antimicrobial effects of the essential oil. Carvone provides distinct olfactory characteristics that can influence various biological responses. Additionally, limonene acts as a secondary component known for its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory capabilities.

According to a study published by "Journal of chromatography. B, Analytical technologies in the biomedical and life sciences", the essential oil of Mentha spicata contains several chiral terpenoids. These medicinal compounds include beta-pinene (Es 1.038), sabiene (Es 1.051), limonene (Es 1.045), isomenthone (Es 1.029), and alpha-terpineol (Es 1.014). Specific constituents such as (+)-isomenthone, (-)-menthone, (1R,2S,5R)-(-)-menthol, and (4S)-(+)-piperitone exist as a single enantiomer with >99% excess. The study also proved the existence of (R)-(+)-limonene and (S)-(+)-carvone enantiomers in M. spicata essential oils. Carvone enantiomers were resolved with an enantioseparation (Es) of 1.006.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing Irritable Bowel Syndrome along with bloating, constipation, diarrhea, and indigestion. The essential oils found in Mentha spicata help relax the smooth muscles of the gastrointestinal tract to alleviate discomfort. By reducing muscle spasms, it provides significant relief from the painful cramping often associated with digestive irregularities. It also helps regulate bowel movements to address both constipation and diarrhea effectively. This natural remedy serves as a versatile tool for soothing various upper and lower digestive upsets.

According to a study published by "Journal of traditional and complementary medicine", Mentha spicata is recommended for the treatment of flatulence. This plant is used to treat flatulence related to indigestion, cesarean section, and dysmenorrhea. It can also reduce the pain severity during colonoscopy or dysmenorrheal conditions. Mentha spicata essential oil may be applied for other intestinal complaints involving pain and flatulence, such as Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS), though this requires more deep clinical trials. No hazardous effects were reported for the essential oil if it is used in proper dosages.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a variety of adverse reactions including heartburn, acid reflux aggravation, nausea, abdominal pain, diarrhea, allergic reaction, and dizziness. Some individuals may experience significant gastrointestinal discomfort such as stomach aches or loose stools after consumption. Those prone to digestive issues might notice that the mint increases the frequency of acid reflux episodes. Sensitivities to the plant can also manifest as skin rashes or other forms of an allergic reaction. Additionally, some users report feeling lightheaded or experiencing dizziness following ingestion.
