Cotoneaster multiflorus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cotoneaster multiflorus?

Cotoneaster multiflorus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further categorized under the order Rosales. It is a member of the family Rosaceae, specifically falling within the genus Cotoneaster, with its scientific designation completed by the species epithet multiflorus.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Cotoneaster multiflorus contains various secondary metabolites including flavonoids, tannins, and organic acids that contribute to its potential therapeutic properties. These chemical constituents are primarily concentrated within the berries and the foliage of the shrub. The presence of polyphenolic compounds suggests that the plant may possess antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Research into the specific bioactive profiles of this species is limited compared to other medicinal plants. However, the general chemical makeup of the Cotoneaster genus often points toward astringent and antimicrobial qualities.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cotoneaster multiflorus?

Cotoneaster multiflorus has minimal documented therapeutic actions in modern clinical medicine, though it is traditionally utilized in certain herbal practices for its mild astringent and anti-inflammatory properties. The plant contains various secondary metabolites, such as flavonoids and tannins, which may contribute to its potential ability to soothe irritated tissues. Some historical applications suggest its use in topical preparations to manage minor skin irritations or digestive upsets. However, most of these uses remain rooted in folk medicine rather than standardized pharmacological treatment. Caution is necessary as the berries of many Cotoneaster species can be toxic if ingested in large quantities.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat respiratory ailments, digestive issues, and various inflammatory conditions in traditional medicinal practices. It contains bioactive compounds that may help soothe coughs and alleviate symptoms of bronchitis. Some historical applications involve using its extracts to manage stomach discomfort and intestinal irregularities. Additionally, the plant is believed to possess properties that assist in reducing localized swelling and pain. While used in folk medicine, modern scientific validation for these specific therapeutic uses remains limited.
