Cotoneaster multiflorus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cotoneaster multiflorus?

Cotoneaster multiflorus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further categorized under the order Rosales. It is a member of the family Rosaceae, specifically falling within the genus Cotoneaster, with its scientific designation completed by the species epithet multiflorus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cotoneaster multiflorus has woody, deciduous shrubs characterized by spreading branches and small, ovate to elliptical leaves that measure approximately 1 to 2 centimeters in length.

The leaf margins are finely serrated, and the foliage typically exhibits a dark green color on the upper surface. During the flowering season, the plant produces dense, terminal clusters of small, white, five-petaled flowers. These blossoms give way to small, globose pomes that turn bright red as they mature in the autumn.

The overall growth habit is compact, often forming a low, bushy mound in its natural habitat.

    What are the herbal preparations of Cotoneaster multiflorus?

    Cotoneaster multiflorus is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or tinctures derived from the dried berries, leaves, or bark of the plant. The berries are most frequently used in aqueous extractions to capture their specific phytochemical properties. Practitioners often create concentrated alcoholic tinctures to ensure a stable shelf life and high potency of the active constituents. Additionally, topical applications such as poultices or washes can be fashioned from crushed plant parts for external use. These various methods allow for different absorption rates and therapeutic applications depending on the intended goal.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Cotoneaster multiflorus can cause gastrointestinal distress, including nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain, if any part of the plant is ingested. The berries contain cyanogenic glycosides which may release toxic cyanide upon consumption. While skin contact is generally not considered highly toxic, some individuals might experience mild irritation or allergic reactions. It is important to keep this plant away from children and pets to prevent accidental poisoning. Always consult a medical professional if you suspect someone has eaten any part of the shrub.
