Botanical Identification of Star Anise (Illicium verum)

To identify Star Anise (Illicium verum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Star Anise (Illicium verum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Star Anise (Illicium verum) belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of flowering plants, class of eudicots, and order Schisandraceae. It is part of the same family, genus Illicium, and species Illicium verum, which is native to Southeast Asia and used in herbal medicine for its aromatic seeds.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Star Anise is Illicium verum, first described by the botanist Jussieu in 1789. It is also known by several synonyms, including Illicium anisatum and Illicium chunii. Common names such as Star Anise, Chinese Star Anise, and Bigarade are widely used. Regional and historical names reflect its use in traditional medicine across Asia.

Morphological Characteristics

Star Anise is a deciduous shrub or small tree with a taproot system. Its stems are upright and branching, bearing alternate, lanceolate leaves. The inflorescence is a spicate cluster, with small greenish-white flowers. The fruit is a star-shaped, capsule-like pod containing 8-10 oblong seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Star Anise can be distinguished by its distinctive star-shaped, dark brown fruit with 8-10 points. The fruit has a strong licorice scent and contains small, pale seeds. It is often confused with other Illicium species, but its unique fruit shape and aroma are key identifiers. The leaves are broadly ovate with a rough texture and a faintly aromatic scent.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Star Anise is native to southern China, northern Vietnam, and eastern India. It thrives in warm, humid climates with mild winters. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Star Anise (Illicium verum) blooms in late summer to early autumn, typically between July and September in its native climate. Seed formation occurs in the following spring, around April to June. Harvesting is done when the fruit turns from green to dark brown, usually in late autumn to early winter, ensuring the seeds are fully mature and aromatic.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Star Anise (Illicium verum) belongs to the family Schisandraceae and is closely related to other Illicium species. It shares morphological and genetic traits with its genus, such as aromatic berries and glandular leaves. Its phylogenetic position highlights its close ties to other species within the same family and genus.
