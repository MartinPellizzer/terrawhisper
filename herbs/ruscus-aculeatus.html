Scientific and Botanical Profile

Ruscus, with botanical name Ruscus aculeatus, is a member of the family Asparagaceae, which includes a variety of flowering plants.

Native to Europe, North Africa, and parts of western Asia, this plant is commonly known as Ruscus, Buttercup Plant, Common Spurge, Butcher's Broom, Candelabra Plant, and Buttercup Plant. It is a low-growing, evergreen shrub characterized by its dense, spiky leaves and small, inconspicuous flowers that develop into red berries.

Morphologically, Ruscus aculeatus features prostrate or upright stems covered in sharp, acicular leaves, and its compact growth habit makes it a popular ornamental plant in gardens.

History and Cultural Relevance

Ruscus was used in ancient times by various cultures for its medicinal and symbolic properties, with evidence of its use dating back to the Greeks and Romans, who valued it for its healing qualities.

In traditional medicine systems such as those of ancient Egypt, Greece, and medieval Europe, Ruscus was employed to treat ailments like hemorrhoids, varicose veins, and digestive issues, often in the form of tinctures or poultices. Its cultural significance extends beyond medicine, as it was associated with fertility and protection in certain rituals, and its evergreen appearance made it a symbol of resilience and renewal in various folk traditions. Today, Ruscus continues to be used in herbal remedies, particularly for circulatory health, with modern formulations incorporating its active compounds to support vein health.

Its enduring presence in both historical and contemporary contexts highlights its deep-rooted importance in human culture and natural healing practices.

Among these, the alkaloids, such as ruscine and neo-ruscine, are particularly notable for their cardiovascular effects. Nutritional-wise, Ruscus provides trace amounts of vitamins A and C, along with minerals such as potassium and magnesium, supporting overall cellular function.

Its mechanism of action involves improving blood circulation by dilating blood vessels and reducing venous pressure, making it beneficial for conditions like varicose veins and hemorrhoids.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Ruscus aculeatus has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping the dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by simmering the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used as an infusion or applied topically in the form of a poultice or ointment. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried leaves per cup of tea, while children should only use it if deemed safe by a healthcare professional.

Due to its potency, it is advised to use ruscus aculeatus sparingly, with a frequency of no more than 2-3 times per day and a duration not exceeding two weeks unless under medical supervision.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Ruscus aculeatus can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential cardiovascular benefits, such as improving circulation and reducing symptoms of varicose veins.

However, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, dizziness, and allergic reactions in some individuals. It should not be used by pregnant or breastfeeding women due to potential risks to the fetus or infant, and its safety in chronic illnesses like heart disease requires careful monitoring. Ruscus aculeatus may interact with medications such as anticoagulants or diuretics, so it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, always follow recommended dosages, avoid long-term use without medical supervision, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Ruscus aculeatus grows as a low-growing evergreen shrub, typically in woodland or shaded areas, preferring well-drained, slightly acidic to neutral soil that is rich in organic matter.

It thrives in partial to full shade, requiring minimal direct sunlight, and benefits from consistent moisture without being waterlogged. Regular watering during dry periods is essential, though it is somewhat drought-tolerant once established. To ensure optimal growth, mulching around the base helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds, while pruning dead or damaged foliage promotes healthy development. Harvesting ruscus aculeatus is best done in late spring to early summer when the leaves are fully mature and contain the highest concentration of active compounds. The leaves should be carefully removed by hand or with a pair of scissors to avoid damaging the plant, and only the upper portions of the stems should be collected. After harvesting, the leaves are typically dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area to preserve their potency and prevent mold. For long-term storage, dried ruscus leaves should be kept in airtight containers, such as glass jars or sealed plastic bags, in a cool, dry, and dark location to maintain their medicinal properties.

Properly stored, the leaves can retain their potency for several years, making them a valuable resource for herbal remedies and traditional medicine.
