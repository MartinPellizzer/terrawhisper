What to know about Rosa centifolia (damask rose) before using it medicinally

Rosa centifolia, commonly known as damask rose, is a herb renowned for its numerous health benefits, which include reducing inflammation, promoting relaxation, and exhibiting antimicrobial properties that enhance overall well-being.

As a flowering herb, Rosa centifolia is prized for its ornamental value, requiring well-drained soil and a cool, temperate climate to thrive. Botanically, the herb is identified as a deciduous shrub belonging to the Rosaceae family, characterized by its distinctive five-petaled flowers and thorny stems.

Historically, Rosa centifolia has been referenced in traditional medicine for centuries, with ancient civilizations such as the Greeks and Romans utilizing its extracts for various therapeutic purposes.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Rosa centifolia.

What are the medicinal properties of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia helps with anxiety, insomnia, and restlessness due to its sedative properties. Its essential oil and extracts are used in aromatherapy and herbal medicine to calm the mind and body. It also treats digestive issues and menstrual disorders.

The active constituents of Rosa centifolia include flavonoids, phenolic acids, and volatile oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties. The essential oil contains geraniol, linalool, and beta-phenylethyl alcohol, which have anti-inflammatory and antibacterial effects.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are the petals and essential oil. The petals are used to make tea, and the essential oil is obtained through steam distillation. The oil is also extracted from the rose hips, which are rich in vitamins and antioxidants.

Improper use of Rosa centifolia can lead to side effects such as allergic reactions, drowsiness, and digestive issues. Ingestion of large amounts can cause nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Patients with allergies or sensitive skin should exercise caution when using this plant.

Precautions when using Rosa centifolia medicinally include consulting with a healthcare professional, especially for pregnant or breastfeeding women, and children under 12 years old. It is also essential to use high-quality products and follow recommended dosages to avoid adverse effects.

What are the horticulural aspects of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia grow in USDA zones 5-9, preferring full sun (6+ hours direct sunlight) and well-drained soil with a pH range of 6.0-6.5. They thrive in areas with high humidity and moderate temperatures. Water requirement is moderate to high.

Planting tips include digging a hole 2-3 times the size of the root ball, adding compost or fertilizer, and planting the bush 18-24 inches deep, with the graft union (if applicable) 2-3 inches above the soil surface. Mulch around the plant to retain moisture. Prune the stems to encourage bushy growth.

Harvesting tips include cutting the stems in the morning, when the dew is still present, and selecting flowers with good color and fragrance. Remove the leaves from the lower part of the stem to promote water uptake and prevent bacterial growth. Tie the stems in bunches to prevent damage during drying.

Common pests affecting Rosa centifolia include aphids, spider mites, and scale. Diseases include black spot, powdery mildew, and root rot, often caused by overwatering. Regular monitoring and pruning can help prevent infestations and infections. Fungicides can be applied as needed to control diseases.

What are the botanical characteristics of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia, also known as the cabbage rose or Provence rose, is a species of flowering plant in the family Rosaceae.

Botanically, it is characterized by its deciduous shrub habit, growing up to 2 meters tall and wide. Its stems are thorny, with pubescent (hairy) undersides and a distinctive red or purple color. The leaves are pinnately compound, consisting of five leaflets that are elliptical in shape and measure around 5-10 cm long.

Each leaflet has a pointed tip and a serrated margin, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter green lower surface. The most striking feature of Rosa centifolia is its fragrant, showy flowers, which are produced in large clusters (up to 20cm in diameter) and typically have 30-40 petals that are pink or white in color. Each flower measures around 4-5 cm in diameter and has a sweet, clove-like fragrance. The plant produces red hips, or seed pods, after the flowers have faded, which contain numerous small seeds.

Overall, Rosa centifolia is a popular ornamental plant due to its attractive foliage and stunning flowers, which are often used in floral arrangements and perfumes.

What is the taxonomy of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae, phylum Angiospermophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Rosales, family Rosaceae, and genus Rosa.

Specifically, it is classified as a species within the section Caninae of the genus Rosa, which includes other species of rose with similar characteristics.

What are the variants of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia has variants, such as 'Céleste', 'Double Yellow', and 'Sultan's Seedling', which exhibit different characteristics.

'Céleste' is a white rose with delicate petals, while 'Double Yellow' is a bright yellow rose with double the number of petals found in the species. 'Sultan's Seedling' is a pink-tinged rose with a strong fragrance and large, cupped blooms.

These variants demonstrate the diversity within the Rosa centifolia species and offer growers and gardeners a range of choices to suit their preferences.

What is the geographic distribution of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia, also known as the hundred-petaled rose, is native to southern Europe and North Africa.

Its natural range extends from France, Spain, and Portugal in the west, to Italy, Greece, Turkey, and parts of the Middle East and North Africa. It thrives in dry, rocky areas with full sun, often found in mountainous regions, coastal hills, and scrublands.

This Old World species has been widely cultivated and is now grown in many parts of the world, but its geographic distribution remains centered around its native Mediterranean region.

What is the life cycle of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia, also known as the centifolia rose or Provence rose, is an evergreen shrub that has a life cycle consisting of three distinct stages.

In its first year, the plant produces a few leaves and develops its root system. During the second year, it grows rapidly, producing long stems covered with fragrant, pink to red flowers in clusters.

These flowers are followed by hips, which contain seeds that can be used for propagation. The third stage is the mature phase, where the shrub reaches its full height (up to 2-3 meters) and continues to produce new shoots and flowers annually.

With proper care, Rosa centifolia can live for many years, providing beauty and fragrance to gardens and landscapes.

What are the historical uses of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia, also known as cabbage rose, has been used in various ways throughout history.

In ancient Rome and Greece, it was used to create perfumes and fragrances. During the Middle Ages, its petals were used to make a type of potpourri called "millefleurs". In France, Rosa centifolia was a popular ingredient in traditional cosmetics and was used to create rosewater and other beauty products.

Its fragrance was also used in traditional medicine to treat various ailments.

What are the symbolic meanings of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia, also known as the cabbage rose, has been imbued with various symbolic meanings throughout history.

In ancient Greece and Rome, it was a symbol of love and passion, while in medieval Europe, it represented devotion and loyalty. The fragrant blooms are often associated with the Virgin Mary, representing purity and innocence.

Additionally, the rose's thorns symbolize the sacrifices made for love, making Rosa centifolia a powerful emblem of romance and selfless dedication.

What are the historical texts of Rosa centifolia?

Rosa centifolia, also known as Damask rose, has been mentioned in various historical texts throughout its cultivation history.

In ancient Greek and Roman texts, such as Dioscorides' De Materia Medica (60 AD) and Pliny the Elder's Naturalis Historia (77-79 AD), Rosa centifolia was described for its medicinal properties and use in perfumery.

Similarly, medieval Arab and Islamic texts, including Ibn Sina's Canon of Medicine (1025 AD) and Avicenna's The Book of Simple Remedies (1030s AD), also referenced the rose's virtues and uses.
