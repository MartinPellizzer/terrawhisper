Scientific and Botanical Profile

Centifolia Rose, with botanical name Rosa centifolia, is a highly fragrant and historically significant species belonging to the Rosaceae family.

Native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, it has been cultivated for centuries for its ornamental and aromatic qualities. This rose is known for its large, full blooms with numerous petals, often reaching up to 100 or more, hence its common name "Hundred Leaf Rose. " It is also referred to by various other names such as French Rose, Hybrid Tea Rose, and Madame Languedoc, among others, reflecting its widespread cultivation and historical importance.

The plant features upright stems, dark green foliage, and flowers that range in color from pale pink to deep red, making it a popular choice in gardens and perfumery.

History and Cultural Relevance

Centifolia Rose was used by ancient civilizations such as the Greeks, Romans, and Persians for its aromatic beauty and medicinal properties, often cultivated in gardens and perfumeries.

In traditional medicine systems like Ayurveda and Chinese herbalism, its petals were valued for their antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, and calming effects, with applications ranging from skin treatments to digestive aids. The flower holds deep cultural significance in various rituals, including weddings, festivals, and religious ceremonies, symbolizing love, purity, and divine favor in many regions of the Middle East and Europe. Folk traditions often involved the use of Centifolia Rose in bath preparations, teas, and as a natural dye, reflecting its enduring role in daily life and spiritual practices.

Today, its essential oils and dried petals continue to be used in aromatherapy, skincare, and culinary applications, preserving its legacy as a timeless symbol of beauty and healing.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Centifolia Rose contains a diverse array of bioactive compounds, including essential oils, flavonoids, terpenes, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its aromatic and therapeutic properties.

The essential oils, primarily composed of compounds like citronellol, geraniol, and farnesol, are responsible for its distinctive fragrance and have antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects. Nutritional-wise, Centifolia Rose is rich in antioxidants such as quercetin and rutin, along with trace amounts of vitamins C and E, and minerals like potassium and magnesium, which support immune function and cellular health. These compounds work synergistically in the body by neutralizing free radicals, modulating inflammatory responses, and enhancing skin and mucous membrane integrity.

Overall, its chemical composition and nutritional profile make it a valuable ingredient in both culinary and medicinal applications.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Rosa centifolia has been traditionally used for its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, making it beneficial for the skin, respiratory, and digestive systems.

Its flowers contain high levels of essential oils, flavonoids, and phenolic compounds that help reduce inflammation and support immune function. Compared to other roses like Rosa damascena, Rosa centifolia offers a more pronounced aroma and higher concentrations of certain bioactive compounds, enhancing its potency in aromatherapy and topical applications. It is particularly effective in treating skin conditions such as eczema and acne due to its soothing and regenerative effects.

Additionally, its calming properties make it a valuable herb for stress relief and emotional well-being, offering a unique combination of physical and mental health benefits.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Rosa centifolia has a variety of forms available, including fresh flowers, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules, each offering different methods of application.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. For topical use, a diluted essential oil or a powdered form mixed with a carrier can be applied directly to the skin. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision, with a much lower dosage.

Due to its potency, it is advised to use rosa centifolia sparingly, with a duration of no more than a few weeks unless under professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Rosa centifolia can be used in traditional medicine for its potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, but it is important to be aware of its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, Rosa centifolia may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals. It may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the liver or blood clotting, and should be used cautiously with other herbs like garlic or ginkgo biloba. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid Rosa centifolia due to limited safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with small doses, monitor for adverse reactions, and seek professional guidance when combining with other treatments.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Rosa centifolia grows best in well-drained, loamy soil that is rich in organic matter, with a pH range of 6.

0 to 7. 0, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal growth and blooming. It thrives in temperate climates with consistent moisture, needing regular watering during dry periods but avoiding waterlogging to prevent root rot. To ensure healthy growth, regular pruning of dead or diseased branches and fertilization with a balanced fertilizer in early spring are essential care practices. Harvesting should occur just as the flowers begin to open, typically in late spring to early summer, using clean shears to cut the stems at a 45-degree angle to preserve the floral integrity.

For storage, the harvested flowers should be spread out in a cool, shaded area to dry slowly, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain their potency and aromatic properties.
