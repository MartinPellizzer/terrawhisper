Centifolia Rose, scientifically known as Rosa centifolia, is a fragrant, ornamental rose prized for its medicinal properties and historical significance.

This flower is commonly regarded as a medicinal herb and a natural adaptogen, valued for its ability to support emotional balance and physical resilience. Its primary benefits include promoting relaxation, reducing stress, and enhancing mood due to its high concentration of essential oils and antioxidants. Traditionally used in ancient Persian, Indian, and Chinese medicine, Centifolia Rose was employed to treat anxiety, digestive issues, and skin conditions.

In modern wellness practices, it is used in aromatherapy, herbal teas, and skincare products, and its unique feature is the presence of a rare compound called damascenone, which contributes to its distinctive floral aroma and therapeutic effects.

Scientific and Botanical Profile

Centifolia Rose, with botanical name Rosa centifolia, is a species belonging to the Rosaceae family, native to regions such as the Middle East, Southwest Asia, North Africa, Southern Europe, Turkey, the Persian Gulf, Lebanon, Syria, Iran, and Iraq.

It is commonly known by various names including Centifolia Rose, French Rose, Madame Lemoine, A Hundred Petals Rose, Madonna Rose, Madame Lilienfeld, A Thousand Petals Rose, Hundred-Petal Rose, French Centifolia, French White Rose, and Hybrid Tea Rose. Morphologically, it is a shrub characterized by its multiple stems, thorny branches, and large, fragrant flowers with numerous petals, often appearing as a full, rounded bloom. The flowers are typically white or pink, with a strong, sweet aroma, and the leaves are pinnately compound, consisting of several oval-shaped leaflets.

This rose is widely cultivated for its ornamental value and is also valued in perfumery and traditional medicine for its essential oils.

History and Cultural Relevance

Centifolia Rose was used extensively in ancient civilizations such as Egypt, Greece, and Rome for its fragrance, beauty, and medicinal properties.

Its petals were valued in traditional medicine systems like Ayurveda and Chinese herbalism for their antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, and skin-rejuvenating qualities. In many cultures, the rose was also central to religious and ceremonial practices, often used in weddings, festivals, and purification rituals to symbolize love, purity, and spiritual awakening. Today, Centifolia Rose remains a key ingredient in natural remedies such as rose water, essential oils, and skincare products, continuing its legacy in holistic health and aromatherapy.

Its enduring presence in both historical and modern contexts highlights its deep cultural significance and timeless relevance.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Centifolia Rose contains a diverse array of bioactive compounds, including essential oils, flavonoids, terpenes, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Its essential oil, primarily composed of compounds like citronellol, geraniol, and farnesol, is known for its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects. The plant is also rich in antioxidants, such as quercetin and kaempferol, as well as vitamins like vitamin C and several B-complex vitamins, along with minerals like calcium, magnesium, and potassium. These nutrients and compounds work synergistically to support immune function, reduce oxidative stress, and promote skin health by enhancing cellular repair and reducing inflammation.

The mechanism of action involves the modulation of inflammatory pathways and the scavenging of free radicals, making Centifolia Rose a valuable component in both culinary and medicinal applications.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Rosa centifolia has been widely recognized for its medicinal properties and numerous health benefits, particularly due to its rich content of antioxidants, anti-inflammatory compounds, and essential oils.

It is known to support the cardiovascular system by helping to lower blood pressure and improve circulation, while also benefiting the respiratory system by reducing inflammation and easing symptoms of asthma and bronchitis. Compared to similar herbs like rosemary or lavender, Rosa centifolia offers a more balanced profile of phytochemicals, with higher concentrations of certain flavonoids that enhance its antioxidant potency. Its soothing properties also make it beneficial for the digestive system, helping to alleviate stomach discomfort and promote healthy digestion.

Additionally, Rosa centifolia is often used in aromatherapy for its calming effects on the nervous system, making it a versatile and potent alternative to other traditional herbs.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Rosa centifolia has been traditionally used for its therapeutic properties, with various forms available including fresh petals, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried petals in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the petals for a longer period, while an infusion involves soaking the petals in cold water. For topical use, the essential oil or dried petals can be applied directly to the skin or mixed with a carrier oil. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children may use a smaller amount if deemed safe by a healthcare professional.

Use should be conservative, with a duration of no more than two weeks unless advised otherwise by a qualified practitioner.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Rosa centifolia can be used as a medicinal plant for its calming and antispasmodic properties, but its safety depends on proper usage and individual health conditions.

While generally considered safe when used in moderation, it may cause mild side effects such as nausea, stomach upset, or allergic reactions in some individuals. It should be avoided by those with known allergies to roses or who experience gastrointestinal sensitivity. Rosa centifolia may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system or blood pressure, so it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use. Special precautions are necessary for pregnant or breastfeeding women, as well as individuals with chronic illnesses, due to limited research on long-term effects.

To ensure safe use, always start with a low dose, consult a healthcare professional, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Rosa centifolia grows best in well-drained, fertile soil that is rich in organic matter, with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal blooming.

It thrives in temperate climates and needs regular watering, especially during dry periods, but should not be overwatered to prevent root rot. Regular pruning after flowering encourages new growth and maintains the plant's shape, while mulching helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. The flowers are best harvested in the early morning when they are fully open but still firm, using sharp scissors to cut the stems just below the bloom to avoid damaging the plant.

To preserve its potency, the harvested flowers should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain their aromatic and medicinal properties.
