Tylophora ovata

What's the taxonomical classification of Tylophora ovata?

Tylophora ovata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this botanical hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic ranks, it is ordered under Gentianales and placed within the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Tylophora, and its species designation, ovata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tylophora ovata has climbing stems that are slender and twining, with leaves that are opposite, elliptical to ovate in shape, and possess entire margins. The foliage is typically glossy and arranged in pairs along the length of the vine. Its flowers are small, tubular, and creamy white, often featuring delicate longitudinal ridges. These blossoms are produced in axillary umbels or small clusters. The plant also produces small, oblong fruit capsules that contain numerous tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

These diverse medicinal qualities make Tylophora ovata a subject of intense interest for pharmaceutical research and drug development.

As discussed in a study, the leaves and stems of Tylophora ovata contain eleven alkaloids, including three new ones identified as tylophovatines A, B, and C. These alkaloids demonstrate in vitro anti-inflammatory activities through the suppression of nitric oxide production in RAW264.7 cells, with IC50 values ranging from 84 nM to 20.6 μM. They also exhibit growth inhibition across six cancer cell lines, specifically HONE-1, NUGC-3, HepG2, SF-268, MCF-7, and NCI-H460, with GI50 values between 4 nM and 24.2 μM. Furthermore, tylophovatine C (5) and 13a(S)-(+)-tylophorine (7) show potent in vivo anti-inflammation activities in a rat paw edema model. The phenanthroindoizidines from the plant are categorized into cytotoxic agents like 10 and 11, or anti-inflammation based anticancer agents such as 5 through 9.

As discussed in a study, the toxic medicinal plant Tylophora ovata provided the endophytic fungus Penicillium commune from its fresh leaves. This fungus yielded six undescribed meleagrin analogues, including isomeleagrin, meleagrin F, meleagrin G, methylmeleagrin G, isomethylmeleagrin G, and meleagrin H. Meleagrin F contains an undescribed skeleton with an aniline moiety linked to meleagrin via a C-C bond at C8-C26. Furthermore, the connection through the C-N bond in N19-C3' was observed in meleagrin G, methylmeleagrin G, isomethylmeleagrin G, and meleagrin H. Among these compounds, isomeleagrin showed selective cytotoxicity against HGC27 cells with an IC50 value of 2.01 μM.
