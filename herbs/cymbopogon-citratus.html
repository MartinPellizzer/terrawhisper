Lemon Grass (Cymbopogon citratus): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Lemon grass, scientifically known as Cymbopogon citratus, is a versatile herb widely used in traditional medicine for its aromatic properties and various therapeutic benefits.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Lemon Grass (Cymbopogon Citratus)?

Lemon grass, or Cymbopogon citratus, is a perennial herb native to tropical regions, characterized by its aromatic, lemon-scented leaves and widely used in traditional medicine and culinary practices.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Lemon Grass is Cymbopogon citratus. This plant belongs to the family Poaceae, order Poales, class Liliopsida, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. It is known as lemon grass in North America and citronella grass in Southeast Asia. Common names also include java grass in India and lemongrass in Australia. Synonyms include Cymbopogon flexuosus and Cymbopogon nardus, with regional names such as citronella in Africa and lemongrass in Europe.

Plant Description and Morphology

Lemon Grass is a tall, perennial herb that typically grows between 1 to 3 meters in height, with a clump-forming structure and upright, cylindrical stalks.

Its leaves are long, narrow, and sword-shaped, with a smooth, waxy surface and a distinctively strong citrus scent. The small, inconspicuous flowers are borne in dense, spike-like clusters at the top of the plant, and they are usually pale green to brown in color. The root system consists of a short, fibrous rhizome that spreads horizontally beneath the soil, helping the plant to anchor and absorb nutrients.

The leaves are usually bright green, have a crisp texture, and emit a sharp, aromatic lemony fragrance when crushed.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Lemon Grass is native to tropical regions of Southern Asia, particularly India, Sri Lanka, and parts of Southeast Asia.

It thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure. Its natural distribution extends across Africa, the Caribbean, and parts of South America, where it is often cultivated for its aromatic properties. The plant prefers temperatures between 20°C and 30°C and requires consistent moisture without waterlogging.

It typically grows in open fields, forest edges, and disturbed soils, making it adaptable to a variety of ecological conditions.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves and stems of Lemon Grass (Cymbopogon citratus) are primarily used medicinally, often prepared as infusions, tinctures, or essential oils to harness their aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Lemon grass (Cymbopogon citratus) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including essential oils rich in citral, myrcene, limonene, and geraniol, along with flavonoids, phenolic acids, and volatile terpenes that contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Lemon grass (Cymbopogon citratus) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and antispasmodic effects, supported by both traditional use and scientific research.

Mechanisms of Action

Lemon grass exerts its effects through the presence of essential oils such as citral, myrcene, and geraniol, which possess antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties that contribute to its therapeutic uses in herbal medicine.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Lemon Grass (Cymbopogon citratus) is used therapeutically for its antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties, commonly indicated for treating digestive disorders, reducing menstrual cramps, and alleviating symptoms of anxiety and stress.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Lemon grass is typically prepared as a tea by steeping dried or fresh leaves in hot water, or it can be used in culinary applications such as soups, curries, and rice dishes, and also in essential oil form for aromatherapy and topical use.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of lemon grass for herbal use ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried herb per cup of boiling water, with 2 to 4 cups consumed daily, though specific dosages may vary based on individual health conditions and intended use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in appropriate doses, lemon grass is generally considered safe, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system or blood pressure.

Drug Interactions

Lemon grass may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the liver or blood pressure, and it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use if taking other medications.

Toxicity and Precautions

Lemon grass is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a culinary herb or in traditional herbal medicine, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications when consumed in large quantities or over prolonged periods.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Lemon grass is typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical climates, requiring well-drained soil, full sun exposure, and consistent moisture, with harvesting occurring 6 to 12 months after planting, primarily during the dry season when the stalks are most aromatic and flavorful.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of lemon grass involve harvesting the fresh or dried aerial parts of the plant, followed by methods such as steam distillation, cold pressing, or solvent extraction to obtain its essential oil, which contains compounds like citral, myrcene, and limonene.

Storage and Shelf Life

Lemon grass should be stored in a cool, dry place in an airtight container to maintain its potency and prevent moisture damage, with a typical shelf life of up to 12 months when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has shown that lemon grass contains compounds such as citral, myrcene, and limonene, which contribute to its antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties, supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Lemon grass has been traditionally used for centuries in various cultures, particularly in Ayurvedic and traditional Chinese medicine, to treat digestive issues, reduce fever, and promote respiratory health.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, lemon grass is classified as a food additive or dietary supplement rather than a pharmaceutical drug, and its use in herbal medicine is generally regulated under food safety laws rather than specific herbal medicine regulations.
