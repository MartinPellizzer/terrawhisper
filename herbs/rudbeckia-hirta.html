Scientific and Botanical Profile

Black-Eyed Susan, with botanical name Rudbeckia hirta, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the sunflower family.

It is also referred to as Brown-Eyed Susan or Rudbeckia, and is native to North America, particularly the eastern and central United States and parts of Canada. Morphologically, it is a herbaceous perennial with upright stems, heart-shaped to lance-shaped leaves, and bright yellow ray florets surrounding a dark brown central disk that resembles eyes.

The plant typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 feet and produces showy blooms from late spring to early fall, making it a popular choice for gardens and natural habitats.

History and Cultural Relevance

Black-Eyed Susan was used by Indigenous peoples of North America for centuries for its medicinal properties, with the plant known as Rudbeckia hirta being valued for its anti-inflammatory and antiseptic qualities.

In traditional medicine systems, it was employed to treat ailments such as fever, wounds, and digestive issues, with its leaves and flowers often prepared as poultices or teas. The plant also held cultural significance in various Native American tribes, where it was used in ceremonies and rituals to symbolize strength, resilience, and the sun's life-giving energy. Today, Black-Eyed Susan continues to be used in herbal remedies, with its extracts still valued for their potential to soothe skin irritations and support immune health.

Its enduring presence in both historical and contemporary contexts highlights its deep-rooted cultural and medicinal legacy.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Rudbeckia hirta has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory and digestive properties, with various forms available including fresh plant, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare rudbeckia hirta, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves and flowers in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the tougher parts of the plant. It can also be used topically as an infusion or oil for skin applications, and the powder form is often taken internally in capsule form. For adults, a typical dosage is 1 to 2 grams of powder or 1 to 2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under professional guidance due to limited safety data.

Due to the lack of extensive research, it is recommended to use rudbeckia hirta sparingly, with a maximum duration of use not exceeding two weeks unless advised otherwise by a healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Rudbeckia hirta can be used as a medicinal plant with potential therapeutic benefits, but it is important to be aware of its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, or skin irritation in some individuals. There are limited studies on drug or herb interactions, but caution is advised when using it alongside medications that affect the liver or blood clotting. Special populations, including pregnant or breastfeeding women and individuals with chronic illnesses, should avoid rudbeckia hirta due to insufficient safety data.

To ensure safe use, consult a healthcare provider before use, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if any adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Rudbeckia hirta grows best in full sun to partial shade in well-drained, fertile soil that is rich in organic matter, with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5. It requires regular watering, especially during dry periods, but should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. To ensure healthy growth, it benefits from mulching to retain moisture and suppress weeds, as well as occasional fertilization during the growing season. The best time to harvest the flowers is in late summer to early fall when the petals are fully open and the color is at its peak, using clean pruning shears to cut the stems just below the flower heads.

After harvesting, the flowers should be dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area to preserve their potency, and stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties.
