Black-Eyed Susan

Rudbeckia hirta

Black-Eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta) is a member of the Asteraceae family, commonly found in North America. Traditionally, various parts of the plant, including the flowers and leaves, have been used for digestive issues, as a diuretic, and for skin conditions. It has been utilized in Native American and European traditional medicine practices.

Botanical Identification

Black-Eyed Susan, scientifically known as Rudbeckia hirta, belongs to the Asteraceae family. It is a hardy perennial native to North America, commonly found in prairies and open woodlands. The plant features hairy stems, dark green leaves, and bright yellow petals surrounding a dark brown center. It is also known as Coneflower or Brown-eyed Susan in some regions.

Active Compounds

Black-Eyed Susan contains flavonoids and phenolic acids as its main bioactive compounds. These compounds are believed to exert their effects through antioxidant and anti-inflammatory mechanisms. They may work synergistically with other plant compounds to enhance therapeutic potential.

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for digestive issues and respiratory conditions.

Decoction: Used for pain relief and inflammation.

Poultice: Used for skin irritations and wounds.

Safety Profile

Black-Eyed Susan, or Rudbeckia hirta, is generally considered safe when used in culinary or herbal contexts. However, it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals. It is contraindicated for pregnant or breastfeeding women due to limited safety data. Always consult a healthcare provider before use.
