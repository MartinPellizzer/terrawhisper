Gardneria multiflora

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gardneria multiflora has a woody, perennial growth habit characterized by erect, unbranched stems that reach heights of up to two meters. The leaves are lanceolate to narrowly elliptical, featuring entire margins and an alternate arrangement along the slender branches. Small, white, tubular flowers emerge in dense, terminal clusters or panicles, which give the species its distinctive name. Each flower possesses a long corolla tube that transitions into five spreading lobes. The plant also produces small, globose fruits that contain numerous tiny seeds for dispersal.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical and tropical regions of Florida, specifically concentrated within the coastal hammocks and pine rocklands of the southeastern part of the state. It thrives in sandy soils and limestone substrates common to these specific ecosystems. While its primary range is restricted to South Florida, small populations may also be found in the nearby Bahamas. The species prefers habitats that receive moderate moisture and consistent sunlight. This localized distribution makes it a key component of the unique Floridian landscape.

      What are the herbal preparations of Gardneria multiflora?

      Gardneria multiflora is prepared as a decoction of the roots, an infusion of the leaves, or as a topical paste made from crushed plant material. The root decoction is commonly utilized in traditional medicine to address specific internal ailments. Leaf infusions are frequently consumed as a tea to harness the plant's botanical properties.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Gardneria multiflora can cause skin irritation, digestive distress, and potential toxicity if ingested by humans or animals. Contact with the plant's sap may lead to localized dermatitis or allergic reactions on sensitive skin. If swallowed, the botanical compounds may trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. There is limited clinical data regarding long-term systemic effects, so caution is advised when handling the specimen. Always use protective gloves to prevent direct contact with the foliage or stems.
