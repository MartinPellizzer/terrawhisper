Botanical Identification of Safflower (Carthamus tinctorius)

To identify Safflower (Carthamus tinctorius) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Safflower, or Carthamus tinctorius, belongs to the kingdom Plantae. Its classification includes the family Compositae, genus Carthamus, and species Carthamus tinctorius. The exact divisions and classes are not specified in the provided data.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of safflower is Carthamus tinctorius, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Carthamus sativus and Carthamus leucocanthus. Common names include safflower, red safflower, and winter-flower, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Safflower is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and hollow. Its alternate leaves are ovate with rough margins and a hairy underside. The plant produces a composite inflorescence with yellow flowers that open sequentially. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous small, dark brown seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Safflower can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow-orange flowers, hairy stems, and lance-shaped leaves with serrated edges. It differs from similar species like Calendula and Tagetes by its narrower leaves and the presence of a hairy stem. The seeds are also unique in shape and color, aiding in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Safflower is native to the Mediterranean region and parts of Asia, including Turkey, Iran, and China. It thrives in temperate climates with well-drained soils. It prefers loamy or sandy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 500 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Safflower blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in mid to late summer, around July to August. Harvest timing for identification purposes is usually in late summer to early autumn, when the seeds are fully mature and the plant has begun to dry.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Safflower, Carthamus tinctorius, belongs to the Asteraceae family. Its closest relatives are other Carthamus species, such as C. balearicus and C. oxyacanthus. Within the genus, it shares morphological and genetic traits with these species, including ray florets and lignified stems. These relationships help in understanding its ecological and medicinal significance.
