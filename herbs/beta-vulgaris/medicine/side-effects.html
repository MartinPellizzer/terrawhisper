1. Decreases blood glucose levels

Beta vulgaris causes stomach upset in some individuals due to its high content of raffinose, a complex sugar that can be difficult for the body to digest.

The fiber and pectin present in beets can also irritate the stomach lining, leading to discomfort and indigestion.

Additionally, the natural compounds found in beets, such as isothiocyanates and betalains, may cause inflammation and irritation in the digestive tract, contributing to stomach upset.

2. Decreases blood glucose levels

Beta vulgaris induces kidney stones due to its high oxalate content.

Oxalates are naturally occurring compounds that can increase urinary oxalate levels, potentially leading to an increased risk of developing kidney stones. This is because excess oxalates in the urine can combine with calcium ions and form crystals that eventually harden into stones.

As a result, individuals who consume high amounts of beet products may be more susceptible to developing kidney stones over time.

3. Decreases blood glucose levels

Beta vulgaris increases blood pressure due to its high content of nitrates, which are converted into nitric oxide in the body.

Nitric oxide causes blood vessels to dilate, leading to increased blood flow and elevated blood pressure.

Additionally, the natural sugars present in beets can also contribute to an increase in blood sugar levels, further exacerbating blood pressure elevations.

Beta vulgaris triggers allergic reactions in some individuals due to its unique protein structure.

The allergenic properties are primarily attributed to the presence of beta-amylase, a enzyme found in the plant's leaves and stems.

When ingested or come into contact with skin, this protein can cause an immune response, leading to symptoms such as hives, itching, and swelling.

Beta vulgaris interferes with iron absorption by containing a high amount of oxalates, which can bind to iron in the gut and reduce its availability for absorption.

Additionally, the betalains present in Beta vulgaris have been shown to inhibit the expression of genes involved in iron metabolism, further exacerbating the interference with iron uptake.

This may lead to iron deficiency or impaired iron function in individuals consuming high amounts of beet products regularly.
