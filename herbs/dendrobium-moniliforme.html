Dendrobium moniliforme

What's the taxonomical classification of Dendrobium moniliforme?

Dendrobium moniliforme belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is placed in the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Asparagales. As a member of the diverse family Orchidaceae, it is further categorized into the genus Dendrobium, with its specific designation being the species moniliforme.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dendrobium moniliiforme has sympodial pseudobulbs that are cylindrical, segmented, and resemble a string of beads. These fleshy stems are relatively short and grow in clusters from a creeping rhizome. Each segment is typically green and contains a small, fleshy leaf at its nodes. The plant produces small, white to pale lilac flowers that emerge from the upper nodes of the stems. The roots are thick and adventitious, allowing the orchid to cling to its natural substrates in the wild.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires bright, filtered sunlight, high humidity levels between 60% and 80%, and a specialized growing medium like bark or charcoal to ensure excellent aeration for its roots.

It thrives in warm to hot temperatures, making it ideal for tropical or subtropical greenhouse environments. Watering should be frequent enough to keep the medium moist but must allow for complete drainage to prevent root rot. During the active growing season, providing a balanced orchid fertilizer will support vigorous shoot development.

To trigger flowering, the plant often benefits from a cooler, slightly drier period during the winter months.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several medicinal compounds including denbinobin, dendrobium alkaloids, dendrobium moniliside E, and dendrobium monilisides A-D. These bioactive secondary metabolites are primarily responsible for the biological activities observed in the species. Specifically, the dendrobium alkaloids and denbinobin have been studied for their potential pharmacological properties. Additionally, the unique structure of dendrobium monilisides A-D contributes to the plant's complex chemical profile. These various constituents work together to define the therapeutic potential of the organism.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", two phenanthraquinones were isolated from the stems of Dendrobium moniliforme. These compounds were identified as 2,6-dimethoxy-1,4,5,8-phenanthradiquinone, named moniliformin (1), and 5-hydroxy-3,7-dimethoxy-1,4-phenanthraquinone, named denbinobin (2). The first compound, moniliformin (1), is notable for possessing a novel 1,4,5,8-diquinone moiety in its phenanthraquinone skeleton. Additionally, the second compound, denbinobin (2), demonstrated potent antiinflammatory effects in vitro.
