What's the taxonomical classification of Brugmansia suaveolens?

Brugmansia suaveolens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is a member of the order Solanales and falls under the family Solanaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Brugmansia, and its specific species, suaveolens.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South America, specifically ranging from Panama through Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Bolivia. While its origins are strictly Neotropical, humans have introduced it to many other warm climates around the globe for ornamental purposes. What are the therapeutic actions of Brugmansia suaveolens?

Brugmansia suaveolenz has therapeutic properties derived from its potent anticholinergic alkaloids which act as a powerful deliriant and hallucinogen. These chemical compounds interfere with acetylcholine receptors in the nervous system to produce profound alterations in perception. In controlled medicinal contexts, the plant can serve as a sedative to calm agitation or as an antispasmodic to relieve muscle spasms. However, the narrow margin between a therapeutic dose and toxicity makes its application extremely hazardous. Careful administration is required to manage its intense physiological and psychological effects.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe anticholinergic toxicity leading to nausea, vomiting, dizziness, hallucinations, confusion, tachycardia, dry mouth, dilated pupils, blurred vision, urinary retention, delirium, seizures, hyperthermia, and coma. Ingesting its alkaloids triggers intense neurological disturbances that often manifest as frightening visual and auditory hallucinations. Physical symptoms like a racing heart and extreme body temperature can quickly escalate into life-threatening medical emergencies. The toxic effects frequently progress from simple disorientation to profound delirium and uncontrollable seizures. If the poisoning is sufficiently intense, the victim may descend into a deep coma.
