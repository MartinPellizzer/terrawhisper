Artemisia atrovirens

What's the taxonomical classification of Artemisia atrovirens?

Artemisia atrovirens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, following the taxonomic order Asterales. As a member of the prominent family Asteraceae, this plant is situated within the genus Artemisia, ultimately reaching its specific identification as the species atrovirens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artemisia atrovirens has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, woody stems and deeply pinnatifid leaves that exhibit a distinct dark green to grayish-green coloration. The foliage is highly dissected, often featuring narrow, linear lobes that give the plant a feathery appearance. Its leaves are typically covered in fine hairs, which contributes to a subtle pubescence across the surface. During the flowering season, the plant produces small, inconspicuous flower heads arranged in dense, terminal clusters. These inflorescences are characteristic of the genus and are often surrounded by specialized bracts.

What are the pharmacological activities of Artemisia atrovirens?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-cervical cancer, antihepatoma activity, and potent cytotoxic effects. Research indicates that its chemical constituents exert strong cytotoxicity against various malignant cell lines. Specifically, the extracts show a promising ability to inhibit the growth of cervical cancer cells in laboratory settings. Furthermore, the plant displays notable antihepatoma activity by targeting liver cancer cell proliferation. These diverse biological properties highlight its potential as a source for developing new anticancer therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the guaianolide dimer artematrolide A isolated from Artemisia atrovirens demonstrates significant inhibitory effects on 3 human hepatoma cell lines including HepG2, Huh7 and SMMC7721. When HeLa S3 and SiHa cells are treated with artematrolide A at various concentrations, it inhibits cell viability, proliferation, migration and invasion in a dose-dependent manner. The compound causes cell cycle arrest in the G2/M phase and induces cell apoptosis via the Bcl-2/PARP-1 pathway. Furthermore, artematrolide A suppresses proliferation by activating the ROS/ERK/mTOR signaling pathway. It also promotes a metabolic shift from aerobic glycolysis to mitochondrial respiration by activating the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDC) and oxoglutarate dehydrogenase complex (OGDC) through the inhibition of alkaline phosphatases (ALP) activity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains 10-methanoyloxy-seco-atrovirenoic acid A, Artatrovirenol A, Artatrovirenol B, Artematrolide A, and the series of Artematrovirenins A-P. These specific medicinal compounds are secondary metabolites that contribute to the unique chemical profile of the species. Researchers study these molecules to understand their potential therapeutic applications and biological activities. The structural complexity of these compounds makes them significant subjects for phytochemical investigation. Detailed analysis of these substances helps determine how the plant interacts with various biological systems.

As discussed in a study, a phytochemical investigation of Artemisia atrovirens resulted in the characterization of a novel bis-nor seco-guaianolide, seco-atrovirenolide A (1), and a new 1,10-seco-guaianolide derivative, seco-atrovirenoic acid A (2). The investigation also identified a new artifact 10-methanoyloxy-seco-atrovirenoic acid A (3) along with eight known guaianolide and seco-guaianolide derivatives (4-11). These compounds were tested in vitro for cytotoxicity against HL-60 and A549 cell lines. Certain compounds demonstrated moderate inhibitory activity against HL-60 cell lines with IC50 values ranging from 5.99 to 11.74 μM.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause adverse reactions such as skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled improperly.
