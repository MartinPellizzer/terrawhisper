Viola yedoensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Viola yedoensis?

Viola yedoensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical classification, it falls under the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Violaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Viola, specifically identified by the species epithet yedoensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viola yedoensis has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by long-petioled leaves and small, five-petaled violet flowers. The leaves are typically ovate to cordate in shape with crenate or serrate margins and are arranged alternately along the stems. Its flowering structures feature a zygomorphic symmetry with a prominent spur at the base of the lower petals. The plant often produces small, capsule-like fruits that contain numerous tiny seeds for dispersal. These morphological traits help distinguish it from other members of the violet genus found in similar habitats.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", Viola yedoensis is a Chinese herb from the Violaceae family reported to contain potential anti-HIV agents. From this plant, 5 new and 3 known cyclotides were identified and shown to have anti-HIV activity. The most active of these is cycloviolacin Y5, which is one of the most potent of all cyclotides tested so far using in vitro XTt-based anti-HIV assays. Cycloviolacin Y5 is also the most hydrophobic of the cyclotides from V. yedoensis. There is a positive correlation between the hydrophobicity and the anti-HIV activity of the new cyclotides. This trend tracks with their ability to disrupt membranes as judged from hemolytic assays on human erythrocytes.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Viola yedoensis Makiho is a medicinal herb traditionally used to treat inflammation-related disorders, including allergic skin reactions. The plant is proposed as a possible remedy for atopic dermatitis, which is a chronic inflammatory skin disease. In studies involving ICR mice, treatment with Viola yedoensis Makiho ethanol extract for 3 weeks helped alleviate symptoms induced by 2,4-dinitrochlorobenzene. This extract suppressed serum levels of IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α while reducing the ratio of CD4+/CD8+ T lymphocyte in the spleen. Furthermore, the administration of the extract down-regulated the expression of iNOS and COX-2 in the dorsal skin.
