Euphorbia sororia

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia sororia?

Euphorbia sororia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Malpighiales and falls under the family Euphorbiaceae. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Euphorbia, and its specific designation is identified by the species name sororia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia sororia has a perennial life cycle characterized by erect, milky-sap-producing stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 50 centimeters. The leaves are primarily basal and lanceolate in shape, though smaller, alternate leaves may appear along the stem. Its inflorescence consists of terminal cyathia, which are specialized structures that mimic flowers to attract pollinators. These small, yellowish bracts surround the reproductive organs and are clustered in a way that appears as a single unit. The plant often exhibits a spreading habit due to its rhizomatous root system.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the central and eastern United States, ranging from the Great Plains through the Midwest to the Atlantic coast. Its primary habitat spans across states such as Missouri, Illinois, Indiana, and parts of the southeastern region. It is frequently found in open woodlands, prairies, and rocky slopes within these territories. The species prefers well-drained soils and sun-dappled environments characteristic of these diverse landscapes. This distribution allows it to thrive in various temperate ecosystems across the midsection of the continent.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia sororia contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its milky white latex and its entire vegetative structure. This specialized sap is produced throughout the plant's stems and leaves when the tissue is damaged. Historically, various parts of the plant have been utilized in folk medicine to treat skin conditions and other topical ailments. The chemical constituents found in the latex are responsible for the plant's potent biological activity. However, users must exercise extreme caution because the sap is highly caustic and can cause severe irritation to the skin and eyes.

What are the pharmacological activities of Euphorbia sororia?

This plant has pharmacological activities that include MDR reversal, anti-multidrug resistance, chemoreversal, multidrug resistance modulator, and multidrug resistance reversal. These specific properties allow the plant to counteract the mechanisms that cancer cells use to evade treatment. By acting as a multidrug resistance modulator, the plant can enhance the efficacy of existing chemotherapeutic agents. This chemoreversal process helps to restore the sensitivity of resistant cells to drugs that were previously ineffective. Ultimately, the plant's ability to facilitate multidrug resistance reversal presents significant potential for advanced cancer therapies.

As discussed in a study, jatrophane diterpenes obtained from the fructus of Euphorbia Sororia demonstrate MDR reversal abilities with low toxicity. Component I, which primarily consists of eight jatrophane diterpenes with a total content reaching up to 92.3% as quantified by HPLC analysis, exhibited superior efficacy and lower toxicity compared to component G and E. This component may act as a P-gp substrate to directly inhibit P-gp-mediated efflux and stimulate P-gp ATPase activity, increasing intracellular drug accumulation in both MCF-7/ADR and HCT-8/T cell lines. Component I inhibited the PI3K/NF-κB pathway in MCF-7/ADR cells to reduce P-gp and enhanced ROS levels in DOX-resistant MCF-7/ADR cells. These pharmacological effects ultimately resulted in increased cell apoptosis and restored sensitivity to P-gp substrate chemoterapeutic agents in both MDR cell lines.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds E-p-hydroxycinnamic acid, kaempferol, quercetin, scopeletin, and protocatechuic acid. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile and biological activities. The presence of flavonoids like kaempferol and quercetin suggests significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory potential. Additionally, scopeletin and protocatechuic acid are known for their unique chemical properties within various biological systems. Together, these constituents form a complex chemical matrix that defines the plant's medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, ten compounds were isolated from the aerial parts of Euphorbia sororia. These compounds were identified through silica gel and Sephadex LH-20 column chromatographies. The isolated structures include kaempferol (1), scopoletin (2), kaempferol 3-O-glucopyranoside (3), quercetin (4), vanillic acid (5), E-p-hydroxycinnamic acid (6), protocatechuic acid (7), 6, 7-dihydroxycoumarin (8), beta-sitosterol (9), and daucosterol (10). All 10 of these compounds were isolated from the plant for the first time.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a topical application of its milky white latex to treat skin conditions like warts or calluses. The sap contains diterpene esters that act as a potent irritant when applied to the skin. Because this substance is highly caustic, it is primarily used externally rather than being ingested. Some historical folk practices involve using diluted infusions for minor topical ailments, though caution is necessary due to potential toxicity. Practitioners must handle the milky fluids with extreme care to avoid chemical burns or eye damage.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, eye irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain upon contact or ingestion. The milky sap contains caustic compounds that lead to redness or burning sensations on the skin. If the sap enters the eyes, it may result in severe irritation or temporary vision impairment. Ingesting any part of the plant often triggers gastrointestinal distress, including nausea and vomiting. Furthermore, consumers may suffer from diarrhea and intense abdominal pain following accidental consumption.
