Mammea africana

What's the taxonomical classification of Mammea africana?

Mammea africana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Calophyllaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Mammea, and its species designation, africana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mammea africana has a woody, perennial structure characterized by a thick, succulent stem and large, leathery leaves that typically emerge in a spiral pattern. The leaves are broadly ovate to elliptical in shape, possessing smooth margins and a deep green coloration on the upper surface. Small, inconspicuous flowers often appear in clusters, eventually giving rise to fleshy fruits that serve as a primary feature of the plant. The root system is robust and adapted to store moisture, allowing the plant to survive in varied environmental conditions. This combination of thickened tissues and specialized leaf structure defines its unique morphological profile.

What are the pharmacological activities of Mammea africana?

This plant has potent antimicrobial and anti-beta-lactamase activities that allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms. Its phytochemical constituents work to inhibit the growth and reproduction of bacteria, making it a significant subject for natural medicine research. Beyond direct killing of microbes, the plant exhibits the ability to inhibit beta-lactamase enzymes, which are proteins produced by bacteria to resist antibiotics. By neutralizing these enzymes, the plant can help restore the efficacy of traditional beta-lactam antibiotics against resistant strains. These combined properties suggest that the species holds great promise for developing new therapeutic agents to treat infectious diseases.

According to a study published by "Annals of tropical medicine and parasitology", the in-vitro antimicrobial activities of Mammea africana were investigated using a methanol extract. This plant was one of 10 species tested against 37 reference or laboratory strains of seven species of microorganism. In disc-diffusion assays, the methanol extract of Mammea africana displayed some degree of antimicrobial activity against at least one species of microorganism. However, no activity against the Gram-negative bacteria, specifically Es. coli, K. pneumoniae and Ps. aeruginosa, was observed. For more susceptible microorganisms that produced an inhibition zone measuring at least 9 mm in diameter, the minimal inhibitory concentrations were estimated by agar dilution.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating bacterial infections, combating cancer cells, and inhibiting beta-lactamase enzymes to overcome antibiotic resistance. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate significant antimicrobial properties that can target various pathogenic bacteria. Research indicates that specific extracts from the plant possess cytotoxic effects that may help suppress tumor growth in cancer studies. Furthermore, the plant contains substances that can neutralize beta-lactamase, an enzyme produced by bacteria to resist common antibiotics. These diverse medicinal applications highlight the therapeutic potential of its unique chemical profile.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", the stem bark of Mammea africana is commonly used in traditional medicine. The purified compound mammea A/AA isolated from the plant was found to be highly active against 7 common pathogenic bacteria. Specifically, it showed inhibitory activity against Campylobacter jejuni with an MIC of 0.5 μg/ml, Streptococcus pneumoniae with an MIC of 0.25 μg/ml, and Clostridium difficile with an MIC of 0.25 μg/ml. Furthermore, the compound exhibited significant antiproliferative activities and cytotoxicity against both the NIH 3T3 mouse embryonic fibroblast cell line and the A549 non-small cell lung cancer cell line.
