Pentadesma butyracea

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pentadesma butyracea has woody shrubs or small trees characterized by simple, opposite, elliptical leaves with entire margins and a coriaceous texture. The foliage is typically dark green and possesses a prominent midvein. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary clusters, which eventually develop into small, fleshy drupes. These fruits are often yellowish or greenish when mature and contain a single seed. The overall architecture of the plant is compact, often forming dense thickets in its natural habitat.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Pentadesma butyracea targets inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and various pro-inflammatory cytokines including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). These interactions specifically modulate the NF-kappaB signaling pathway, which is a central regulator of the immune response. This biochemical mechanism explains the traditional use of the plant in treating skin inflammations and gastrointestinal distress.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Pentadesma butyracea can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested improperly. While traditionally used in medicinal preparations, the plant contains bioactive compounds that may trigger localized dermatitis upon direct contact with the skin. Some users have reported redness or itching in areas where the plant extracts were applied. Internal consumption carries risks of nausea or stomach discomfort if the dosage is not strictly controlled. Because clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the full spectrum of systemic side effects remains largely undocumented.
