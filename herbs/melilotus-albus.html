Melilotus albus

What's the taxonomical classification of Melilotus albus?

Melilotus albus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Fabales. It is a member of the family Fabaceae, which places it among the legumes, and is further categorized into the genus Melilotus, with the specific designation of the species albus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Melilotus albus has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 100 centimeters and features trifoliate leaves composed of three oval to lanceolate leaflets. The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and possess finely serrated margins with a slightly pubescent texture. Its inflorescence consists of long, dense racemes containing numerous small, white, pea-like flowers. Each flower possesses a characteristic papilionaceous structure typical of the Fabaceae family. The plant also produces small, flattened, oblong pods that turn brown as they mature and contain several seeds.

The species prefers well-drained soils and can tolerate a variety of environmental conditions across different continents. Its ability to spread via seeds allows it to establish itself in many different climates globally. Because of its hardy nature, it is frequently found in many non-native habitats far from its original range.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Melilotus albus contains medicinal coumarins primarily located in the seeds, leaves, and stems of the plant. These chemical compounds are extracted from the various parts to serve as precursors for anticoagulant medications. Historically, the dried flowering tops and foliage have been utilized in traditional herbal preparations to support circulatory health. While the seeds are the most potent source of coumarin, careful processing is required to manage potential toxicity. Scientific interest remains focused on how these plant components can be harnessed for therapeutic purposes in modern medicine.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds coumarin, dicoumarol, melilotoside I, melilotoside II, and melilotoside III. The presence of coumarin serves as a chemical precursor to various anticoagulant substances found within the species. Specifically, the derivative dicoumarol is known for its potent ability to inhibit blood clotting processes. Additionally, the plant features several unique glycosides identified as melilotoside I, melilotoside II, and melilotoside III. These specific compounds contribute to the complex chemical profile used in pharmacological studies.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Melilotus albus targets enzymes such as cyclooxygenase, lipoxygenase, and various inflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. The plant contains coumarins and flavonoids that interact with these specific protein pathways to inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory mediators. By modulating the expression of nitric oxide synthase, its bioactive compounds help regulate vascular tone and reduce oxidative stress. These interactions with signaling proteins allow the plant to demonstrate significant anti-inflammatory and anticoagulant activities in biological systems. Such molecular engagement provides a mechanistic basis for its traditional use in treating swelling and circulatory issues.

What are the therapeutic actions of Melilotus albus?

Melilotus albus has therapeutic actions as a bitter, diuretic, lymphatic alterative, and venous tonic. Its bitter properties stimulate digestive secretions and improve appetite through the stimulation of taste buds. As a lymphatic alterative, it helps to purify the blood by supporting the efficient movement of lymph through the body. The plant also functions as a diuretic to assist in the elimination of metabolic waste through the kidneys. Finally, its role as a venous tonic helps to improve circulation and reduce the swelling associated with vascular congestion.

What health problems are treated using this plant?

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe coumarin poisoning, which may lead to life-threatening internal bleeding and significant liver damage. Consuming the plant often triggers gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Individuals may also experience neurological symptoms like dizziness as the toxicity progresses. These serious complications arise because the plant contains high levels of natural anticoagulants. Monitoring for these specific signs is critical to avoid permanent organ injury.

  • Internal bleeding
  • Liver damage

  • Abdominal pain
  • Dizziness
