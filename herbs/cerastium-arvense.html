Cerastium arvense

What's the taxonomical classification of Cerastium arvense?

Cerastium arvense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized into the order Caryophyllales. It is a member of the family Caryophyllaceae, falling under the genus Cerastium, and is specifically identified by its species name, arvense.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Cerastium arvense contains various secondary metabolites including flavonoids, phenolic acids, and certain alkaloids that contribute to its biological activity. These chemical constituents are primarily concentrated within the plant's leaves and stems. Research into its phytochemical profile suggests that these compounds may possess antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. While the plant is not widely used in mainstream pharmacology, its chemical makeup is of interest for studying plant-based bioactive molecules. Detailed isolation of these specific compounds remains a subject for specialized botanical studies.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cerastium arvense?

Cerastium arvense has limited documented therapeutic actions in modern pharmacology, though it is historically studied for its potential diuretic and anti-inflammatory properties. Some traditional herbal practices suggest the plant may assist in managing fluid retention due to its mild diuretic effects. Research into its chemical constituents often focuses on secondary metabolites like flavonoids which may contribute to its antioxidant activity. While these biological activities are noted in botanical studies, clinical evidence for human medicinal use remains sparse. Consequently, it is primarily viewed as a subject of phytochemical interest rather than a standardized medical treatment.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin conditions and inflammatory ailments such as wounds, sores, and eczema. Historically, herbalists have applied the crushed foliage or extracts to soothe skin irritations and promote healing in damaged tissues. The plant contains specific bioactive compounds that may provide mild antiseptic properties during topical application. Some traditional practices also suggest using it to manage minor internal inflammations through controlled preparations. However, modern clinical evidence regarding its efficacy remains limited in medical literature.
