Sambucus chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Sambucus chinensis?

Sambucus chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant is placed in the order Dipsacales and belongs to the family Viburnaceae. Finally, it is identified under the genus Sambucus, with its specific designation being the species chinensis.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Sambucus chinensis contains medicinal properties primarily derived from its flowers, fruits, and stems. The dried flowers are frequently used in traditional remedies to reduce inflammation and treat respiratory ailments. Its dark berries are harvested to create syrups or decoctions that help boost the immune system and alleviate fever. Additionally, the bark and stems are sometimes utilized in specific herbal preparations for their astringent qualities. These various components work together to provide a diverse range of therapeutic applications in folk medicine.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, phenolic acids, anthocyanins, terpenoids, alkaloids, and tannins.

These chemical constituents work synergistically to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The high concentration of anthocyanins and flavonoids contributes to the plant's ability to scavenge free radicals. Additionally, the presence of phenolic acids and terpenoids supports various therapeutic applications in traditional medicine.

Alkaloids and tannins further enhance its medicinal complexity by offering potential antimicrobial and astringent effects.

  • Flavonoids
  • Phenolic acids
  • Anthocyanins
  • Terpenoids
  • Alkaloids
  • Tannins

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Sambucus chinensis targets specific inflammatory mediators such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha, interleukin-6, and cyclooxygenase-2 enzymes to exert its therapeutic effects. The bioactive polyphenols and flavonoids within the plant interact with these proteins to inhibit the signaling pathways that drive chronic inflammation. By modulating these molecular targets, the plant extracts can effectively reduce the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines in various cellular environments. Additionally, certain compounds may interact with viral proteins to inhibit replication processes, providing potential antiviral properties. These protein-ligand interactions collectively contribute to the plant's medicinal profile in managing oxidative stress and immune responses.

What are the therapeutic actions of Sambucus chinensis?

Sambucus chinensis has pharmacological properties that include anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial activities used primarily to treat respiratory ailments and skin inflammations. The plant contains high concentrations of flavonoids and phenolic compounds that help neutralize free radicals in the body. These bioactive constituents work to reduce oxidative stress and support the immune system during infection. Additionally, its extracts are often applied topically to soothe irritated tissues and promote wound healing. Researchers continue to study its specific chemical profile to better understand its potential in managing systemic inflammation.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various respiratory ailments, inflammatory conditions, and skin disorders. It is commonly employed in traditional medicine to alleviate symptoms of cough, asthma, and bronchitis. The plant possesses properties that help reduce swelling and soothe skin irritations such as eczema or sores. Some practitioners also utilize its extracts to address digestive issues and fever. Its medicinal applications focus on leveraging its natural anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial qualities.

What are the herbal preparations of Sambucus chinensis?

Sambucus chinensis is prepared as decoctions, powders, pills, or topical washes depending on the specific therapeutic goal. The dried flowers are most commonly boiled in water to create a fragrant tea used for treating respiratory issues or skin inflammations. In traditional formulations, the plant material may be ground into a fine powder and mixed with honey to form medicinal pills. Some preparations involve soaking the flowers in alcohol to create tinctures that concentrate the plant's active compounds. Additionally, concentrated extracts are sometimes applied directly to the skin in liquid form to soothe local irritation.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Sambucus chinensis can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain if the raw berries, leaves, bark, or roots are ingested. These specific plant parts contain cyanogenic glycosides, which release toxic cyanide into the body upon consumption. Consuming improperly prepared parts of the plant may also lead to dizziness or more severe systemic poisoning. Most adverse reactions are mitigated by cooking the berries thoroughly to neutralize these harmful compounds. Individuals with sensitivities should exercise caution when using any derivatives of this species.
