Hibiscus cannabinus

What's the taxonomical classification of Hibiscus cannabinus?

Hibiscus cannabinus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Malvales and the family Malvaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Hibiscus, with its specific designation being the species cannabinus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hibiscus cannabinus has an erect, herbaceous growth habit characterized by tall, slender stems that can reach heights of up to three meters and feature serrated, palmately lobed leaves. The foliage consists of deeply divided leaflets that provide a lush, bushy appearance to the plant. Its flowers are typically solitary and funnel-shaped, displaying vibrant colors like yellow or cream with a prominent central stamen column. Small, rounded capsules develop from the flowers to house numerous tiny seeds. The overall structure is highly fibrous, making the stalks useful for textile production.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, specifically spreading through areas such as West Africa and parts of East Africa. It has been introduced to various other tropical climates across the globe due to its utility in fiber production. Currently, it can be found cultivated in parts of Asia and certain regions of the Americas where warm temperatures prevail. The species thrives in diverse environments provided they offer sufficient moisture and sunlight. Its distribution is largely shaped by human agricultural practices aimed at harvesting its stalks.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Hibiscus cannabinus contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its leaves, flowers, and stems. The leaves are frequently utilized in traditional practices to treat inflammation and skin irritations due to their cooling properties. Floral extracts are often processed to create infusions that aid in digestion or serve as mild sedatives. Additionally, the fibrous stems are sometimes processed for their mucilaginous content, which can act as a soothing agent for internal ailments. These various plant parts work together to provide a diverse range of therapeutic applications.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including flavonoids, kaempferitrin, p-coumarate, phenolics, and sinapyl acetate. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's overall therapeutic profile and biological activity. The presence of various phenolics and flavonoids provides significant antioxidant properties to the organism. Additionally, molecules like kaempferitrin and p-coumarate are recognized for their potential roles in supporting cellular health. Sinapyl acetate further adds to the complex chemical makeup that defines its medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, Hibiscus cannabinus responds to six different sodium chloride concentrations of 0, 50, 100, 150, 200, and 250 mM by altering its bioactive constituents. Salt stress leads to an accumulation of hydrogen peroxide, superoxide anion, malondialdehyde, proline, total soluble sugar, and total soluble protein. The levels of antioxidants, including phenolics and flavonoids, also increase under these conditions. Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry results identified induced volatile compounds such as heptacosane, 1-octadecanesulphonyl chloride, and tetratetracontane. These changes significantly improve the antioxidant capacity of the leaf extracts.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Alzheimer's disease, Escherichia coli IR-6 infections, HepG-2 cancer cells, diabetes mellitus, and malaria. Researchers have investigated its bioactive compounds to determine their ability to inhibit the growth of HepG-2 cells. The extract also shows significant potential in managing blood glucose levels associated with diabetes mellitus. Furthermore, studies suggest its efficacy in combating the Escherichia coli IR-6 strain and the malaria parasite. These medicinal properties provide a foundation for developing new therapies against these diverse health conditions.

As discussed in a study, the polyphenolic-rich extract of Hibiscus cannabinus seed (PEHc) contains 12 chemical compounds identified via HPLC. This extract showed a total phenolic content of 243.5 ± 0.71 mg GAE/g and a total flavonoid content of 54.06 ± 0.09 mg QE/g. Regarding potential for Alzheimer's disease, PEHc demonstrated significant inhibitory activity against acetylcholinesterase with an IC50 of 262.95 ± 1.47 µg/ml and butyrylcholinesterase with an IC50 of 189.97 ± 0.82 µg/ml. The extract also exhibited antioxidant activity with an ABTS IC50 of 218.30 ± 0.87 µg/ml and a 1, 1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl free radical IC50 of 227.79 ± 0.74 µg/ml. These findings suggest that the drug-like compounds identified in PEHc could be useful for the management of Alzheimer's disease.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion by steeping the delicate flowers in hot water, a decoction by simmering the tougher stems or roots, a tincture using alcohol extraction, or a powder made from dried material. An infusion of the blossoms often yields a tart, flavorful tea rich in antioxidants. For more fibrous parts, a long-simmering decoction is necessary to extract the medicinal compounds effectively. Tinctures allow for a concentrated dosage that can be easily stored and added to other liquids. Finally, the dried plant can be ground into a fine powder to be incorporated into capsules or topical applications.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions including low blood pressure, low blood sugar, estrogen reduction, nausea, dizziness, and digestive upset. Consuming hibiscus cannabinus may lead to a drop in glucose levels or a decrease in blood pressure, which can be dangerous for certain individuals. Some users might experience hormonal shifts due to estrogen reduction. Gastrointestinal issues such as nausea and general digestive upset are also reported side effects. Additionally, the physiological changes can trigger feelings of dizziness in some people.
