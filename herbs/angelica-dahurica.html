Angelica dahurica

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica dahurica?

Angelica dahurica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Apiales. This plant is a member of the family Apiaceae, which places it within the genus Angelica, and it is specifically identified by the species name dahurica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica dahurica has an erect, perennial herbaceous structure characterized by hollow stems that can reach heights of up to 1.5 meters and a thick, fleshy taproot. The leaves are large, compound, and pinnate, featuring serrated margins and a deep green coloration. Small, white flowers are arranged in dense, flat-topped umbels that emerge from the upper leaf axils. These floral clusters are supported by peduncles that vary in length depending on the plant's maturity. The overall morphology allows the plant to thrive in moist, shaded environments.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds coumarin, psoralen, imperatoriin, osthole, and bergaptol. These specific phytochemicals are known for their diverse pharmacological activities, including anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Psoralen and bergaptol are particularly noted for their roles in photosensitizing treatments for certain skin conditions. Additionally, compounds such as imperatorin and osthole contribute to the plant's complex chemical profile used in traditional medicine. Together, these substances provide the biochemical basis for the plant's various therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, the aboveground part of Angelica dahurica, which accounted for over 70% of the total plant, contains various medicinal compounds. GC-MS identified 85 volatile components from the leaves of Angelica dahurica. Additionally, 39 non-volatile components, including sugars, amino acids, and organic acids, were identified via pre-column derivatization GC-MS analysis. The study also qualitatively and quantitatively analyzed 7 coumarins using HPLC. Among the potential arginine kinase inhibitors, l-aspartic acid exhibited the strongest effect with an IC50 value of 0.558 mM, which was much lower than the 6.644 mM value of chlorpheniramine.
