Angelica dahurica

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica dahurica?

Angelica dahurica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Apiales. This plant is a member of the family Apiaceae, which places it within the genus Angelica, and it is specifically identified by the species name dahurica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica dahurica has an erect, perennial herbaceous structure characterized by hollow stems that can reach heights of up to 1.5 meters and a thick, fleshy taproot. The leaves are large, compound, and pinnate, featuring serrated margins and a deep green coloration. Small, white flowers are arranged in dense, flat-topped umbels that emerge from the upper leaf axils. These floral clusters are supported by peduncles that vary in length depending on the plant's maturity. The overall morphology allows the plant to thrive in moist, shaded environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Angelica dahurica contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its dried rhizomes and roots. These underground parts are harvested and processed to extract essential oils and coumarins used in traditional therapies. The rhizomes are frequently utilized to treat skin conditions, alleviate pain, and reduce inflammation. Practitioners also employ the roots to address respiratory issues and regulate blood circulation. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds coumarin, imperatorin, psoralen, osthole, and bergaptol. These specific phytochemicals are often studied for their diverse pharmacological activities in human health. For instance, psoralen and bergaptol are well-known furanocoumarins that can influence skin sensitivity to light. Compounds such as osthole and imperatorin contribute to the complex chemical profile that defines the plant's therapeutic potential. Together, these constituents demonstrate why the species is frequently utilized in traditional medicinal practices.

As discussed in a study, the aboveground part of Angelica dahurica, which accounts for over 70% of the total plant, contains various medicinal compounds. Through GC-MS, 85 volatile components were identified from the leaves of Angelica dahurica. Additionally, 39 non-volatile components, including sugars, amino acids, and organic acids, were identified via pre-column derivatization GC-MS analysis. The study also qualitatively and quantitatively analyzed 7 coumarins using HPLC. Among the screened potential inhibitors, l-aspartic acid showed the strongest inhibitory effect on arginine kinase with an IC50 value of 0.558 mM, which was significantly lower than the 6.644 mM value of chlorpheniramine.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction, a powder, a tincture, an essential oil, or an infusion to harness its medicinal properties. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions such as nausea, diarrhea, abdominal pain, dizziness, photosensitivity, and allergic reactions.
