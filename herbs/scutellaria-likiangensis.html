Scutellaria likiangensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Scutellaria likiangensis?

Scutelariia likiangensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant follows the taxonomic order Lamiales and is a member of the family Lamiaceae. At the most specific levels of its classification, it is identified by the genus Scutellaria and the species likiangensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Scutellaria likiangensis has a woody, perennial growth habit characterized by erect stems and small, lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage typically presents a dark green color and is arranged oppositely along the branches. Its flowers are zygomorphic and emerge from the axils, often displaying a tubular structure typical of the Lamiaceae family. The inflorescence structure and the specific shape of the calyx serve as key identifying features for this species. Small, nutlet-like fruits are produced following successful pollination.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds baicalin, baicalein, wogonin, wogonoside, and scutellarin. These specific flavonoids are primarily responsible for the plant's significant therapeutic properties. Baicalin and baicalein are often studied for their potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects on human cells. Wogonin and its glycoside form, wogonoside, contribute to the plant's ability to interact with various neurological and biological pathways. The presence of scutellarin further enhances the complex chemical profile used in traditional herbal medicine. Together, these bioactive substances work synergistically to provide diverse pharmacological benefits.

As discussed in a study, Scutelariia likiangensis is one of the four substitute medicinal species used in traditional medicines. Metabolomics and content determination analyses were used to reveal the interspecific chemical diversity of these species. The research showed that the four species exhibit a metabolism similar to that of S. baicalensis in different parts. While all parts of the substitute species showed high contents of baicalin, this was an exception for the roots of S. likiangensis. These genetic and chemical analyses of the four substitute medicinal species were performed for the first time.
