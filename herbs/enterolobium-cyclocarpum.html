Enterolobium cyclocarpum

What's the taxonomical classification of Enterolobium cyclocarpum?

Enterolobium cyclocarpum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Fabales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Fabaceae, which characterizes it as a legume. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Enterolobium, and its species name, cyclocarpum.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat gastrointestinal issues such as diarrhea, stomach aches, and intestinal worms. Its medicinal properties are often utilized in traditional practices to soothe digestive discomfort and promote bowel health. Some local healers also apply extracts from the plant to address skin irritations or minor wounds. The bark and roots are frequently prepared in decoctions to manage these various ailments. Research into its bioactive compounds continues to explore its potential for broader therapeutic applications.

What are the herbal preparations of Enterolobium cyclocarpum?

Enterolobium cyclocarpum is prepared primarily as a decoction of the bark or a decoction of the leaves to treat various ailments. The bark is frequently boiled in water to extract active compounds used for medicinal purposes. Some traditional practices also involve using the plant in the form of a tea or an infusion. These liquid preparations are often consumed to address digestive issues or other systemic conditions. The choice of form typically depends on which specific part of the plant is being harvested for treatment.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Enterolobium cyclocarpum can cause gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and potential toxicity if consumed in improper doses or preparations. While some traditional uses exist, the plant contains chemical compounds that may trigger allergic reactions upon direct contact with the skin. Ingesting parts of the plant without correct processing can lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping. There is also limited scientific data regarding long-term systemic effects on human organ function. Consequently, caution is advised due to the unpredictable nature of its bioactive constituents.
