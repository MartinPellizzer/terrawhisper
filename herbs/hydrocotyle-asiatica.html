Scientific and Botanical Profile

Water Pennywort, with botanical name Hydrocotyle asiatica, is a flowering plant belonging to the Araliaceae family, commonly known as the ginseng or ivy family.

Native to Asia, including regions such as China, Japan, and Southeast Asia, it is also found in parts of Africa and the Americas. Morphologically, it is a herbaceous perennial with floating or submerged leaves that are typically heart-shaped or rounded, often with a waxy surface, and small white or yellowish flowers that appear in clusters. It is commonly referred to by various common names such as Dollarweed, Asian Dollarplant, Water Shield, Asian Pennywort, Dove's Foot, and Asiatic Water Shield, reflecting its widespread use and appearance.

This versatile plant is also known as Spurge Laurel, Copperleaf, Asian Shield-Leaf, and Asian Marsh Pennywort, and is valued for its medicinal and ecological properties in wetland environments.

Its enduring presence in both historical and contemporary practices highlights its deep-rooted cultural relevance and continued relevance in natural healthcare.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Hydrocotyle asiatica has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported healing properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

The preparation methods vary, with common approaches including brewing a tea by steeping dried leaves, making a decoction by boiling them, creating an infusion for topical application, or using essential oil in diluted form for massage. For adults, a typical dosage might involve 1-2 teaspoons of the dried herb in tea form, taken up to three times daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision due to limited safety data. It is recommended to use hydrocotyle asiatica for short durations, typically no longer than two weeks, and to consult a healthcare professional before prolonged use.

Conservative use is advised to minimize potential side effects and ensure safe integration into one's health regimen.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Hydrocotyle asiatica can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential therapeutic effects, but it is important to consider its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals. There is limited research on drug or herb interactions, but caution is advised when combining it with medications that affect the liver or blood clotting. Special populations, including pregnant or breastfeeding women and individuals with chronic illnesses, should avoid its use due to insufficient evidence on safety and potential risks.

To ensure safe use, consult a healthcare provider before starting hydrocotyle asiatica, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Hydrocotyle asiatica grows best in moist, well-drained soils with a pH range of 6.

0 to 7. 5, thriving in full sun to partial shade and requiring consistent moisture without waterlogging. It prefers warm, humid climates and can be planted in early spring after the last frost, with spacing of about 30 cm between plants to allow for proper growth. Regular watering is necessary, especially during dry periods, and mulching helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. For harvesting, the leaves and stems are typically collected during the early morning or late afternoon when the plant is most hydrated, using clean shears to cut the top 10-15 cm of the plant to encourage regrowth.

To preserve its potency, the harvested material should be air-dried in a cool, shaded area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain its medicinal properties.
