Roselle

Hibiscus sabdariffa

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is a member of the Malvaceae family, native to tropical regions of Africa and widely cultivated in South and Southeast Asia. Traditionally, its calyces have been used as a diuretic, to treat hypertension, and as a laxative. The plant is also valued for its culinary and medicinal applications in various cultural contexts.

Botanical Identification

Roselle, scientifically known as Hibiscus sabdariffa, belongs to the Malvaceae family. It is also called roselle, Jamaica flower, and sour sorrel, commonly grown as an annual in tropical regions. The plant features vibrant red calyces, green leaves, and yellow flowers, with a history of use in herbal medicine for its medicinal properties.

Active Compounds

Roselle contains key bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, and anthocyanins. These compounds work synergistically to provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Their combined action enhances the herb's therapeutic potential in herbal medicine.

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for digestive issues and as a mild diuretic.

Decoction: Used for respiratory conditions and inflammation.

Extract: Used for cardiovascular support and antioxidant benefits.

Safety Profile

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts. However, it may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting blood pressure. It is contraindicated for pregnant women and those with hypertension due to its potential to lower blood pressure. Always consult a healthcare provider before use.
