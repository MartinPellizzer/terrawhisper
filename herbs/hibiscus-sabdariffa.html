Hibiscus sabdariffa

What's the taxonomical classification of Hibiscus sabdariffa?

Hibiscus sabdariffa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Malvales and is a member of the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Hibiscus and is specifically designated as the species sabdariffa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hibiscus sabdariffa has an erect, perennial shrubby structure characterized by woody stems, ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins, and prominent, fleshy red calyces. The leaves are typically arranged alternately along the branches and feature a deep green color with distinct venation. Small, pale yellow flowers emerge from the axils, displaying five delicate petals that contrast sharply with the developing fruit. Once pollination occurs, the most notable morphological feature is the enlargement of the calyx into a thick, succulent, and deeply pigmented red structure. This thickened calyx surrounds the seed capsule and serves as the primary edible portion of the plant.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to tropical regions of Africa and is widely cultivated throughout the humid, tropical, and subtropical zones of the world. It thrives in many parts of Asia, including India, China, and Southeast Asian nations, where it is often grown for its medicinal and culinary uses. In the Americas, it is frequently found in the Caribbean, Central America, and parts of South America due to its preference for warm climates. Because it requires consistent moisture and high temperatures, it is rarely seen in temperate or arid environments. Its distribution remains heavily concentrated in areas where frost is uncommon and sunlight is abundant.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Hibiscus sabdariffa contains medicinal properties primarily found in its fleshy red calyces, though its leaves and roots are also utilized in traditional medicine. The dried calyces are most commonly brewed into teas to help lower blood pressure and manage cholesterol levels. These floral parts are rich in organic acids and anthocyanins that provide potent antioxidant benefits. Some traditional practices also employ the leaves for their anti-inflammatory properties and the roots for treating various ailments. What are the pharmacological activities of Hibiscus sabdariffa?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including antioxidant, antihypertensive, ACE inhibitor, anti-hyperglycemia, and anti-hyperlipidemia properties. The high concentration of anthocyanins and polyphenols provides significant antioxidant effects that combat oxidative stress in cells. Its ability to function as an ACE inhibitor helps regulate blood pressure by relaxing blood vessels, thereby acting as an effective antihypertensive agent. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates anti-hyperglycemia capabilities by helping to manage blood glucose levels. It also exhibits anti-hyperlipidemia activity, which assists in reducing harmful lipid profiles in the bloodstream.

As discussed in a study, Hibiscus sabdariffa possesses antihypertensive potentials originating from vasodilator activity, diuretic efficacy, ACE inhibitor functionality, adipocyte differentiation inhibition, heart rate reduction ability, and anti-inflammatory mechanisms. Its dose-dependent antihyperlipidemic effect results from the activation of AMPK and the inhibition of regulatory adipogenic transcription factors PPAR-γ, C/EBP-α, and SREBP-1c. As an antihyperglycemic, the plant serves as anti-insulin resistance by inhibiting the phosphorylation of IRS-1, demonstrating a similar effect to gliptins. Hibiscus sabdariffa protects against neuroinflammation in microglial cell culture exposed to LPS by decreasing IL-1, IL-6, and TNF-α expression. Additionally, it offers protective effects against glucotoxicity and improves memory function by inhibiting the formation of hyperphosphorylated tau proteins in the mouse brain.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypotension, nausea, electrolyte imbalance, dizziness, gastric irritation, and amenorrhea. Because it has the potential to lower blood pressure, individuals may experience bouts of dizziness. Some people might also suffer from nausea or gastric irritation after consumption. Changes in hormone levels could potentially lead to amenorrhea or other menstrual irregularities. Furthermore, the diuretic effects of the plant can contribute to an electrolyte imbalance in the body.
