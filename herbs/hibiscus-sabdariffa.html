Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Roselle, also known as Hibiscus sabdariffa, is a flowering plant native to Africa and widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its medicinal properties and various health benefits.

What is Roselle (Hibiscus Sabdariffa)?

Roselle, also known as Hibiscus sabdariffa, is a flowering plant native to Africa, Asia, and the Americas, commonly used in herbal medicine for its tart, cranberry-like fruit and its various medicinal properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Roselle is Hibiscus sabdariffa. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Malvales, family Malvaceae, genus Hibiscus, and species Hibiscus sabdariffa. It is commonly known as Roselle in Africa and the Middle East, and as Jamaica flower or calabash in the Caribbean and South America. Synonyms for Roselle include Hibiscus roselle (botanical) and Bissap, Karkade, and Roselle (regional).

Plant Description and Morphology

Roselle is an erect, herbaceous annual plant that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters, with a branching structure that supports its large, showy flowers.

Its leaves are ovate to elliptical in shape, with a smooth margin and a slightly hairy surface. The flowers are bright red or orange, with a five-lobed corolla and a prominent, rounded calyx that becomes fleshy and edible as the fruit develops. The root system is fibrous and shallow, adapting to nutrient-poor soils by spreading horizontally near the surface.

The plant has a distinct reddish color, a mild acidic scent, and a slightly rough, hairy texture on its stems and leaves.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, particularly Nigeria and Sudan, and has been widely cultivated in Southeast Asia, the Caribbean, and parts of the Americas for centuries.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, flowers, and calyces of Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) are used medicinally, with the calyces being the most commonly utilized part for their high content of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids and anthocyanins.

Phytochemical Composition

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) contains a variety of phytochemicals including anthocyanins, flavonoids, polysaccharides, and organic acids such as citric and malic acid, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) exhibits pharmacological properties including antihypertensive, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and diuretic effects, supported by both traditional use and modern scientific studies.

Mechanisms of Action

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) exerts its pharmacological effects primarily through the activation of adrenergic receptors, modulation of the renin-angiotensin system, and the presence of bioactive compounds such as anthocyanins, flavonoids, and polysaccharides that contribute to its hypotensive, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support cardiovascular health, manage hypertension, aid digestion, and alleviate symptoms of menopause due to its high content of antioxidants, anthocyanins, and flavonoids.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is commonly prepared as a tea by steeping the dried calyces in hot water, or used in the form of powder, capsules, or syrup for various medicinal purposes.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) for herbal use ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried calyces per day, usually prepared as a tea by steeping in hot water, though specific dosages may vary based on the intended use and individual health conditions.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is generally considered safe when consumed in moderate amounts as a herbal remedy, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea or diarrhea, in some individuals, and should be avoided by those with hypertension or during pregnancy without medical supervision.

Drug Interactions

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) may interact with medications that affect blood pressure, blood sugar, or diuretics, as it possesses hypotensive, hypoglycemic, and diuretic properties that could enhance or counteract the effects of these drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is generally considered safe when used in typical dietary or medicinal amounts, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to limited safety data.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions, typically in well-drained soil with adequate sunlight, and harvested primarily from the calyces of the flower when they turn a deep red color, usually 60 to 90 days after planting.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) typically involve drying the calyces, grinding them into a powder, and then using methods such as water extraction, ethanol extraction, or supercritical fluid extraction to obtain bioactive compounds like anthocyanins, flavonoids, and mucilage.

Storage and Shelf Life

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and extend its shelf life, which is typically up to 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) has demonstrated its potential therapeutic effects, including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and hypoglycemic properties, with studies supporting its use in managing conditions such as hypertension and diabetes.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its cooling properties to treat fevers, digestive issues, and as a diuretic, with historical records tracing its use back to ancient Egypt and traditional Chinese medicine.

Regulatory Status

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) when used as a food ingredient, but its use as a herbal medicine is not specifically regulated under the same framework as pharmaceuticals.
