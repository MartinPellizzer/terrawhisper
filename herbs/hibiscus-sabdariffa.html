Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is a flowering plant native to Africa and widely cultivated for its calyx, which is used in traditional medicine and as a food ingredient, known for its potential to improve cardiovascular health and lower blood pressure.

What is Roselle (Hibiscus Sabdariffa)?

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is a flowering plant native to tropical regions, commonly cultivated for its edible calyces which are used in herbal medicine, culinary applications, and traditional remedies.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Roselle, scientifically known as Hibiscus sabdariffa, is a flowering plant species belonging to the Malvaceae family, characterized by its distinctive calyces that are commonly used in traditional herbal medicine.

Common Names and Synonyms

Roselle, also known as Hibiscus sabdariffa, is commonly referred to by various names such as Roselle flower, Red sorrel, and Karkalla, depending on regional usage and botanical classification.

Plant Description and Morphology

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is a flowering plant characterized by its distinctive red calyces, elongated green leaves, and showy yellow or orange flowers that bloom at the top of the plant.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, particularly the savannas of West Africa, and has been cultivated for centuries in countries such as Nigeria, Senegal, and Sudan, with its cultivation also spreading to parts of Asia and the Americas.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, calyces, and roots of Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) are used medicinally, with the calyces being the most commonly utilized part for traditional and modern herbal applications.

Phytochemical Composition

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) contains a diverse range of phytochemicals including anthocyanins, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and alkaloids, which contribute to its medicinal properties and potential health benefits.

Pharmacological Properties

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) possesses pharmacological properties that include antihypertensive, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and diuretic effects, supported by both traditional use and modern scientific research.

Mechanisms of Action

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its bioactive compounds, such as flavonoids, anthocyanins, and phenolic acids, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and hypoglycemic properties by modulating cellular signaling pathways and enzyme activities.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Roselle is used to treat hypertension, diabetes, digestive issues, urinary tract infections, inflammation, menstrual cramps, and skin conditions such as eczema and acne.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is commonly prepared as a tea by steeping the dried calyces in hot water, or used in the form of powder, capsules, or syrup for various medicinal applications.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) for herbal use typically ranges from 2 to 4 grams of dried calyx per cup of water, prepared as a tea, with a maximum of 3 cups per day, though specific dosages may vary depending on the form of the herb and individual health conditions.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Roselle is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, excessive consumption may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, including nausea, diarrhea, and stomach cramps, and in rare cases, it may interact with certain medications or exacerbate existing health conditions.

Drug Interactions

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) may interact with certain medications, including antihypertensive drugs, as its hypotensive effects could enhance the blood pressure-lowering actions of these medications, potentially leading to excessive lowering of blood pressure.

Toxicity and Precautions

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is generally considered safe when consumed in typical food amounts, but excessive use may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by individuals with hypertension due to its potential hypotensive effects.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions with well-drained soil, warm temperatures, and ample sunlight, often grown as an annual crop with a short growing season.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) typically involves harvesting the calyces, drying them in the sun or using mechanical methods, and then grinding them into a powder or using them whole in various formulations for medicinal and culinary purposes.

Storage and Shelf Life

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain its potency and extend its shelf life, which is typically up to 12 months when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) has demonstrated its potential in managing hypertension, reducing oxidative stress, and supporting metabolic health, with studies indicating its bioactive compounds such as anthocyanins and flavonoids contribute to these therapeutic effects.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, has been traditionally used in various cultures for its medicinal properties, including as a diuretic, to reduce fever, and to treat hypertension, with historical records tracing its use back to ancient Egypt and China.

Regulatory Status

Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) is classified as a dietary supplement in the United States and is regulated by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994.
