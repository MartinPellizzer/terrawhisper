Pinus kwangtungensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Pinus kwangtungensis?

Pinus kwangtungensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Pinidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Pinales and the family Pinaceae, eventually reaching the genus Pinus. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by its designation as the species kwangtungensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pinus kwangtungensis has needles arranged in bundles of two, scale-like leaves on the branches, and woody cones that are ovoid in shape. The needles are typically long, slender, and possess a distinct bluish-green or grayish-green coloration. Its bark is thick and develops deep fissures as the tree matures, providing a rugged texture to the trunk. The cones feature stiff, thickened scales that often end in small spines to protect the developing seeds. These morphological characteristics allow the species to thrive in its specific mountainous habitats.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate mountainous regions of Northeast China, the Korean Peninsula, and the Russian Far East. Its range extends specifically through provinces such as Heilongjiang and Jilin in China. It also inhabits the rugged terrains of North Korea and the Primorsky Krai region of Russia. These populations are typically found growing in high-altitude coniferous forests on rocky slopes. The species thrives in these cool, subalpine environments across its fragmented East Asian distribution.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including abietaan and isopimarane type acids, cis-abienol, labdane type acids, serrataneoids, and sterols. These diterpenoids serve as significant bioactive constituents that contribute to the plant's unique chemical profile. The specific presence of cis-abienol is often used as a marker for certain botanical species. Furthermore, the labdane type acids and serrataneoids play critical roles in the biological activities associated with the plant. Finally, the inclusion of various sterols supports the overall complexity of its medicinal properties.

As discussed in a study, Pinus kwangtungensis needles contain more trinorlabdane (14,15,16-trinor-8(17)-labdene-13,19-dioic acid) and other labdane type acids, nonacosan-10-ol, and its saponifiable esters. The defoliated twig extract of Pinus kwangtungensis contains larger quantities of fatty acids, caryophyllene oxide, serratanoids, sterols, saponifiable sterol esters, and acylglycerols. This twig extract contains lesser amounts of abietane and isopimarane type acids, cis-abienol, and lacks cembranoids. Both needle and twig extracts of Pinus kwangtungensis significantly inhibited the growth of the Gram-negative bacteria Serrautia marcescens with an MIC of 0.1 mg ml-1. These lipophilic extractive compounds are prospective for the development of antiseptics against Gram-negative bacteria.
