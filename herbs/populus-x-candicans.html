What to know about Populus x candicans (bastard cottonwood) before using it medicinally

Populus x candicans, commonly known as bastard cottonwood, is a valuable herb that has been used for centuries to support respiratory health, improve symptoms of allergies, and reduce inflammation in the body.

This deciduous tree is widely cultivated in gardens and parks due to its stunning white flowers and ability to thrive in a variety of environments. Botanically, Populus x candicans is a hybrid cross between Populus deltoides and Populus trichocarpa, and its leaves are characterized by their broad, rounded shape and serrated edges.

With a history dating back to the 18th century, this herb has been mentioned in various botanical texts, including the works of prominent naturalist and horticulturist, Sir Joseph Banks.

What are the medicinal properties of Populus x candicans?

Populus x candicans helps with wound healing, inflammation, and infections. Its extracts have been used to treat skin conditions, such as eczema and acne, and to reduce fever and pain.

The active constituents of Populus x candicans include salicin, salicylic acid, and flavonoids, which have anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and antioxidant properties. These compounds are responsible for its medicinal effects, including pain relief and reduced inflammation.

The most commonly used parts of Populus x candicans for medicinal purposes are the bark, leaves, and flowers. The bark is used to make salicylic acid, while the leaves and flowers are used to make infusions and tinctures.

Ingestion of large amounts of Populus x candicans can lead to side effects such as stomach upset, nausea, and diarrhea. Additionally, individuals with salicylate sensitivity should avoid using this plant, as it can cause allergic reactions.

Precautions when using Populus x candicans medicinally include avoiding use during pregnancy and breastfeeding, as well as monitoring blood sugar levels in individuals with diabetes. It is also essential to consult a healthcare professional before using this plant to treat underlying health conditions.

To plant hybrid black cottonwood, choose a location with full sun and adequate moisture. Plant in early spring or fall, spacing trees 20 to 30 feet apart. Water thoroughly after planting and keep the soil consistently moist during the first growing season.

Harvesting hybrid black cottonwood is best done in early winter, after the foliage has fallen. Cut the trunks at a 45-degree angle, 6 to 12 inches above the ground. Harvest the wood in the winter or early spring, when it is dry and free of sap.

Hybrid black cottonwood is susceptible to pests like aphids, caterpillars, and root weevils. Common diseases include powdery mildew, leaf spot, and root rot. Regular monitoring and sanitation practices can help prevent infestations and infections.
