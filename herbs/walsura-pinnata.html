Walsura pinnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Walsura pinnata?

Walsura pinnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Sapindales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Meliaceae, falling into the genus Walsura, and is specifically identified by its species name, pinnata.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Walsura pinnata contains bioactive compounds in its bark, leaves, and roots that are utilized for various therapeutic purposes. The bark is frequently processed to create decoctions used to treat skin ailments and internal inflammations. Additionally, the roots are sometimes employed in traditional medicine to address specific neurological or systemic conditions. These various components work together to provide a diverse range of medicinal applications in folk healing practices.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Walsura pinnata targets several key enzymes and inflammatory mediators, most notably cyclooxygenase (COX), lipoxygenase (LOX), and various pro-inflammatory cytokines. Its bioactive phytochemicals, including terpenoids and polyphenols, inhibit the COX-2 enzyme to reduce prostaglandin synthesis, which alleviates pain and swelling. These compounds also interact with nitric oxide synthase to modulate oxidative stress levels within cellular environments. Furthermore, the plant's constituents can influence signaling pathways related to apoptosis and cell proliferation in diseased tissues. This multi-target mechanism explains its diverse pharmacological applications in treating inflammation and infectious conditions.

Walsura pinnata has anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties that are utilized in traditional medicine to treat skin infections, wounds, and gastrointestinal issues. What diseases this plant treats?

What are the herbal preparations of Walsura pinnata?

Walsura pinnata is prepared as decoctions, infusions, and paste-like applications derived from its bark, leaves, and seeds to treat various ailments. The bark is often boiled in water to create a potent decoction used for systemic internal conditions. Leaf extracts are frequently processed into infusions or topical applications to soothe skin irritations. Some traditional practices involve grinding the seeds into a fine powder to be mixed with honey or oils. These diverse preparation methods ensure that the plant's bioactive compounds are effectively delivered for therapeutic use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Walsura pinnata can cause adverse physiological reactions such as skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, or allergic dermatitis if handled or ingested without proper preparation. While many parts of the plant are used in traditional medicine, the specific concentration of bioactive compounds may trigger unexpected sensitivities in certain individuals. Contact with the sap or raw plant matter might result in localized redness or itching on the skin. Furthermore, consuming unrefined extracts could lead to nausea or stomach cramping due to the presence of complex alkaloids. It is essential to consult a medical professional before using any part of this species for therapeutic purposes.
