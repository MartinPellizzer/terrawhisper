Thalictrum squamiferum

What's the taxonomical classification of Thalictrum squamiferum?

Thalictrum squamiferum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Ranunculales. This plant is a specific member of the family Ranunculaceae, situated within the genus Thalictrum, and is identified by the species name squamiferum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Thalictrum squamiferum has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by compound leaves with small, scale-like stipules and small, yellowish-green flowers.

The leaves are typically divided into several leaflets that exhibit a delicate, dissected structure. Its stems are erect and support the foliage throughout the growing season. The inflorescence consists of multiple small flowers held in loose clusters.

These floral structures are relatively inconspicuous compared to other members of the Ranunculaceae family.

        What are the herbal preparations of Thalictrum squamiferum?

        Thalictrum squamiferum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice using its dried aerial parts. These traditional methods allow the plant's active compounds to be extracted into liquid forms for internal or external use. Decoctions are typically made by boiling the plant material in water to release denser constituents. Infusions involve steeping the herb in hot water for a gentler extraction process. When applied as a poultice, the crushed plant material is placed directly onto the skin to treat localized ailments.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Thalictrum squamiferum can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap may lead to redness, itching, or localized swelling on the skin. Some individuals might experience more severe dermatological reactions if they have a known sensitivity to the Thalictrum genus. Ingestion of the plant is generally discouraged due to the potential for stomach upset or nausea. Further clinical research is required to fully categorize the specific toxicity levels of this particular species.
