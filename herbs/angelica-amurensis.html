Angelica amurensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica amurensis?

Angelica amurensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is situated in the order Apiales. It is a member of the family Apiaceae and is categorized under the genus Angelica, specifically identified by the species name amurensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica amurensis has an erect, perennial herbaceous habit characterized by hollow, furrowed stems that can reach heights of up to 2 meters.

The leaves are large, pinnately compound, and feature serrated margins with long petioles. Its inflorescence consists of large, compound umbels composed of numerous small, white flowers. The plant develops a thick, fleshy taproot that serves as a primary storage organ.

These structural features allow the species to thrive in damp, mountainous environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Angelica amurensis contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are harvested and processed to extract bioactive substances used in traditional East Asian medicine. The roots are frequently utilized to treat various ailments such as menstrual irregularities, abdominal pain, and blood circulation issues. Beyond the roots, the plant's overall chemical profile contributes to its reputation for warming the body and dispelling coldness. Practitioners often decoct these parts to create potent herbal remedies for systemic health support.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse range of bioactive constituents including coumarins, flavonoids, triterpenoids, and phenolic acids that contribute to its therapeutic potential.

The presence of coumarins is often linked to significant anti-inflammatory and anticoagulant activities within the medicinal profile. Flavonoids and phenolic acids act as potent antioxidant agents that help protect cells from oxidative stress and damage. Additionally, the triterpenoids found in the species contribute to various biological functions such as antimicrobial and immunomodulatory effects.

Together, these chemical components form the basis for the plant's traditional and pharmacological applications in treating various ailments.

  • Coumarins
  • Flavonoids
  • Triterpenoids
  • Phenolic acids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Angelica amurensis targets various inflammatory mediators including cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6. These interactions occur primarily through bioactive compounds like coumarins and organic acids that modulate intracellular signaling pathways. By inhibiting the cyclooxygenase-2 enzyme, the plant reduces the production of prostaglandins which are key drivers of pain and swelling. Furthermore, its ability to suppress nitric oxide synthase helps mitigate oxidative stress and tissue damage in inflammatory models. These multi-target mechanisms collectively contribute to the plant's documented analgesic and anti-inflammatory properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Angelica amurensis?

Angelica amurensis has therapeutic properties that include acting as a bitter, carminative, stomachic, diaphoretic, antispasmodic, and expectorant agent.

Its bitter components stimulate the appetite and improve overall digestion by functioning as a potent stomachic. The plant also serves as a carminative to relieve intestinal gas and an antispasmodic to reduce muscle contractions. To address respiratory issues, it acts as an expectorant to help clear mucus from the airways.

Additionally, its diaphoretic action helps promote sweating to assist in regulating body temperature.

What are the herbal preparations of Angelica amurensis?

Angelica amurensis is prepared primarily as a decoction made by boiling the dried roots in water, though it can also be processed into powders, tinctures, or topical pastes. The roots are typically sliced and dried before being simmered to extract their active medicinal compounds. In traditional practices, these concentrated liquid extracts are often consumed to support blood circulation and menstrual health. Some preparations involve grinding the dried material into a fine powder that can be mixed into warm water or food. These various forms allow for different rates of absorption and methods of administration depending on the specific therapeutic goal.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Angelica amurensis can cause skin irritation, photosensitivity, and gastrointestinal distress if consumed improperly. The plant contains furanocoumarins, which are compounds that increase the skin's sensitivity to ultraviolet light and may lead to burns or rashes upon exposure. When ingested, some individuals may experience stomach upset, nausea, or diarrhea. While often used in traditional medicine, excessive consumption can potentially disrupt internal bodily functions. It is important to exercise caution as individual reactions to the plant's active components can vary significantly.
