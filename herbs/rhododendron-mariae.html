Rhododendron mariae

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rhododendron mariae has elliptic to oblong leaves that are coriaceous, measuring approximately 3 to 7 centimeters in length, and characterized by a revolute margin. The foliage typically exhibits a dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes indumentum-covered underside. This species produces small, tubular flowers that are usually pink or white in color, growing in terminal clusters. The stems are woody and branched, forming a compact shrubby habit. These morphological traits distinguish it from other members of its genus within its specific high-altitude habitat.

This plant is endemic to the high-altitude montane forests of the island of Luzon in the Philippines. It is specifically found within the mossy forests of the Cordillera Central mountain range. These populations thrive in cool, moist environments characterized by consistent cloud cover and high humidity. The species is restricted to very specific niches where the soil and elevation meet its unique physiological requirements.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Rhododendron mariae can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potentially fatal cardiac arrhythmia if any part of the plant is ingested due to its high concentration of grayanotoxins. These toxins act by binding to sodium channels in cell membranes, which prevents them to close properly and disrupts normal electrical signaling. Symptoms often begin with intense nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain shortly after consumption. As the toxins affect the nervous system, individuals may experience dizziness, confusion, or muscle weakness. In more extreme cases, the disruption of cardiac electrical activity can lead to dangerous drops in blood pressure and irregular heartbeats.
