Senecio vulgaris

What's the taxonomical classification of Senecio vulgaris?

Senecio vulgaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is further organized under the order Asterales. As a member of the family Asteraceae, this plant is characterized by its specific placement within the genus Senecio, eventually reaching its unique identification at the species level as vulgaris.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Senecio vulgaris contains various pyrrolizidine alkaloids, specifically senecionine, seneciphylline, and seneciphylline, which serve as its primary chemical constituents. These specific compounds are known for their potent biological activity and are frequently studied in pharmacological research. While some alkaloids exhibit potential antimicrobial or anti-inflammatory properties, they are also highly toxic to the liver. This toxicity can lead to serious hepatic damage or even death if the plant is ingested in significant quantities. Consequently, the medicinal application of this species is strictly limited due to the narrow margin between potential benefit and lethal harm.

What are the therapeutic actions of Senecio vulgaris?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin conditions such as eczema, psoriasis, and various types of dermatitis. Its leaves contain bioactive compounds that can help soothe inflammatory skin reactions when applied topically. Some traditional practices also suggest using it for minor wounds or insect bites to reduce swelling. However, extreme caution must be exercised because the plant contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids that are toxic to the liver. It should never be ingested, as internal consumption can lead to severe organ damage or death.

What are the herbal preparations of Senecio vulgaris?

Senecio vulgaris is prepared primarily as an aqueous infusion, a decoction, or a topical tincture for external applications. Because the plant contains toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids, oral consumption is generally discouraged in many traditional practices. When used externally, a concentrated tincture can be applied to the skin to treat minor irritations or localized inflammation. Infusions made from the dried aerial parts are sometimes used in very dilute quantities for specific traditional remedies. Practitioners must exercise extreme caution regarding dosage due to the potential for liver toxicity.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Senecio vulgaris can cause severe liver damage and potentially fatal toxicity in humans and animals if ingested due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids. These toxic compounds are processed by the liver into reactive metabolites that destroy liver cells and lead to progressive hepatic fibrosis. Long-term exposure to these alkaloids can result in hepatic veno-occlusive disease, which obstructs the small veins within the liver. In addition to liver issues, ingestion may cause gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Because the concentration of these toxins varies by plant age and environment, even small amounts can pose a significant health risk.
