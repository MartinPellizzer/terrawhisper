Coula eduledis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Santalales and is a member of the family Olacaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Coulia and the species edulis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Coula edulis has a woody, evergreen shrub or small tree structure characterized by oppositely arranged, elliptical leaves with entire margins and a glossy texture. The plant typically reaches heights of up to five meters and features a spreading crown of dense foliage. Its reproductive structures consist of small, fragrant white flowers that emerge from the axils of the leaves. These flowers eventually develop into fleshy, bright red or orange drupes containing several seeds. The bark is generally smooth and brownish, providing a sturdy framework for the plant's rapid growth in tropical environments.

Within these zones, the species is most commonly found in shaded, understory environments of dense jungle habitats. Its distribution is closely tied to the availability of moist, fertile soils characteristic of these lowland forest ecosystems. How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, known as kola nut, thrives in the humid, tropical climates of West Africa when grown in well-drained, fertile soils under partial shade.

Growers typically plant seeds in nursery beds before transplanting young seedlings into permanent locations where they can reach heights of up to 10 meters. To ensure optimal fruit production, the trees require consistent moisture and protection from intense, direct sunlight during their early development. Regular fertilization and careful weeding are essential practices to maintain the health of the grove.

What traditional systems uses this plant?

This plant, known as kola nut, has been used by West African ethnic groups for centuries as a sacred ceremonial offering, a social lubricant for hospitality, and a traditional stimulant.

In many cultures, presenting the nut is a required gesture of respect during marriage negotiations, funerals, and visits to elders. People historically chewed the seeds to alleviate hunger and fatigue due to their high caffeine and theobromine content. It also served as a vital component in traditional medicine to treat various ailments and as a symbolic medium for divination.

Through these diverse applications, the plant became deeply embedded in the social and spiritual fabric of many African societies.
