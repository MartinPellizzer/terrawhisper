Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Wild Angelica, or Pimpinella major, is a medicinal plant native to Europe and Asia, known for its historical use in traditional herbal medicine for digestive, respiratory, and skin-related ailments.

What is Wild Angelica (Pimpinella Major)?

Wild Angelica, or Pimpinella major, is a biennial herb native to Europe and parts of Asia, known for its aromatic leaves and clusters of small white flowers, and has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported digestive, respiratory, and circulatory benefits.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Wild Angelica is Pimpinella major. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Pimpinella, and species Pimpinella major. It is commonly known as Wild Angelica in North America and as Angelica major in Europe. The synonyms for this plant include Pimpinella major var. major and Pimpinella major subsp. major.

Plant Description and Morphology

Wild Angelica is a biennial herb that grows up to 1.5 meters tall, with a slender, branching stem that is hollow and covered in fine hairs.

Its leaves are pinnately compound, with each leaflet being ovate to lanceolate in shape and deeply lobed, giving the plant a feathery appearance. The flowers are small, white, and clustered in umbrella-like umbels, with each umbel consisting of numerous tiny florets that bloom in early summer. The root system consists of a thick, fleshy taproot that is white and aromatic, often used in traditional medicine.

The plant has a distinctive spicy, sweet aroma, with leaves and stems that are rough to the touch and have a slightly bitter taste.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) is native to temperate regions of Europe and western Asia, particularly found in grasslands, woodland edges, and disturbed soils across countries such as Germany, France, Italy, and Turkey.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, and seeds of Wild Angelica are primarily used medicinally, with the roots being most commonly harvested in early spring and the seeds collected in late summer.

Phytochemical Composition

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, alkaloids, essential oils, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic potential.

Pharmacological Properties

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and antioxidant effects, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as sesquiterpenes, flavonoids, and essential oils.

Mechanisms of Action

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) exerts its pharmacological effects primarily through the presence of bioactive compounds such as volatile oils, alkaloids, and flavonoids, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and analgesic properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support respiratory health, alleviate digestive discomfort, and promote urinary function due to its expectorant, carminative, and diuretic properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or powder, with the most common method involving harvesting the roots and leaves during the early flowering stage, drying them thoroughly, and then grinding into a fine powder or using them to make a decoction by simmering in water.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) for medicinal use ranges from 200 to 400 mg per day, usually in the form of a standardized extract, with the exact dose depending on the specific preparation and the intended therapeutic effect.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts for short periods, it may cause gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and photosensitivity in some individuals, and it is contraindicated during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential harm to the fetus or infant.

Drug Interactions

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, blood pressure medications, and drugs that affect the central nervous system, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects or reducing the effectiveness of these medications.

Toxicity and Precautions

Cultivation and Harvesting

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) is typically cultivated in shaded, moist environments with well-draining soil, and is harvested in late summer to early autumn when the leaves and seeds are at their peak potency.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) typically involve harvesting the roots and seeds during their peak maturity, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as solvent extraction, steam distillation, or cold pressing to isolate active compounds like alkaloids and flavonoids.

Storage and Shelf Life

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dark, and dry place, away from moisture and direct sunlight, and it maintains its potency for up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) has shown promising results in its traditional uses, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, with some studies suggesting potential applications in treating digestive disorders and respiratory conditions.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) has been historically used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to support liver function, alleviate digestive issues, and treat skin conditions, with traditional applications dating back to ancient times in European and Asian medicinal practices.
