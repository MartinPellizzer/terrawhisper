Pleioblastus amarus

What's the taxonomical classification of Pleioblastus amarus?

Pleioblastus amarus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant falls under the order Poales. It is further categorized into the family Poaceae, which is the characteristic family for grasses. Within this family, the plant is identified by the genus Pleioblastus, and its specific biological designation is the species amarus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pleioblastus amarus has climbing stems that are woody at the base and herbaceous toward the tips, featuring opposite, ovate to elliptic leaves with entire margins and acute apices. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary clusters, which eventually develop into fleshy, indehiscent fruits. These fruits are often used as a primary food source for various forest fauna. The overall growth habit allows the plant to weave through the canopy of tropical forests using its flexible stems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Pleioblastus amarus?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, antispasmodic, and antipyretic pharmacological activities. These properties make it a valuable resource in traditional medicine for treating various ailments. Its antioxidant capabilities help protect cells from oxidative stress and damage. Additionally, the plant demonstrates significant potential in combating microbial infections and reducing fever. The antispasmodic effects further allow it to assist in relieving muscle contractions and spasms.

As discussed in a study, the total alkaloids from the bamboo shoots (ABSP) and bamboo shoot shells (ABSSP) of Pleioblastus amarus exhibit anti-inflammatory effects. Both ABSP and ABSSP decreased nitric oxide production induced by 0.5 μg/mL of LPS in RAW264.7 murine macrophages, with IC50 values of 78 μg/mL and 55 μg/mL, respectively. At concentrations of 100 μg/mL, ABSP and ABSSP decreased the expression of iNOS and COX-2 at both mRNA and protein levels. Furthermore, 100 μg/mL of ABSP and ABSSP significantly inhibited the LPS-induced mRNA expression of IL-1β and TNF-α. These extracts also decreased the phosphorylation of ERK in LPS-stimulated RAW264.7 cells through the inhibition of ERK signaling.

Together, these chemical components form a complex profile used in traditional and modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, a total of 32 alkaloids were extracted from the bamboo shoots and bamboo shoot shells of Pleioblastus amarus. There were 15 alkaloids common to both the bamboo shoots and shells, while 10 and 7 alkaloids were distinct to the shoots and shells, respectively. The bamboo shoot alkaloids (ABSP) and shell alkaloids (ABSSP) decreased nitric oxide production induced by 0.5 μg/mL of LPS with IC50 values of 78 and 55 μg/mL. At concentrations of 100 μg/mL, both ABSP and ABSSP decreased the expression of iNOS and COX-2 as well as the mRNA expression of IL-1β and TNF-α. Additionally, 100 μg/mL of these alkaloids decreased the phosphorylation of ERK in LPS-stimulated RAW264.7 cells.
