Botanical Identification of Bell Pepper (Capsicum annuum)

To identify Bell Pepper (Capsicum annuum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Bell Pepper, or Capsicum annuum, belongs to the plant kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Solanales, family Solanaceae, genus Capsicum, and species Capsicum annuum. These classifications reflect its botanical relationship to other flowering plants and its place within the nightshade family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Bell Pepper is Capsicum annuum, a member of the Solanaceae family. It was first described by Carl Linnaeus in 1753. Common synonyms include Capsicum frutescens and Capsicum dulce. It is also known by regional names such as "pimento" in Spanish and "pepper" in many languages.

Morphological Characteristics

Bell Pepper (Capsicum annuum) is an annual herb with a bushy growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors the plant and absorbs nutrients. The stem is upright, branching, and covered in fine hairs. Leaves are ovate to lanceolate, serrated, and arranged alternately along the stem. The inflorescence is axillary, with solitary flowers that are yellow and bilaterally symmetrical. The fruit is a fleshy berry, typically red when mature, containing numerous seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Bell Pepper (Capsicum annuum) can be distinguished by its thick, fleshy fruit with a smooth, waxy surface and a range of colors from green to red. It has simple, alternate leaves that are ovate to lanceolate with a slightly serrated margin. The plant typically grows to about 1-2 meters tall, with hairy stems and yellow flowers that develop into the characteristic pepper fruit.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Bell Pepper, or Capsicum annuum, is native to the Americas, particularly Central and South America. It thrives in warm climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Bell Pepper (Capsicum annuum) typically blooms 60-90 days after planting, depending on climate and variety. Seed formation occurs 2-3 weeks after flowering. For identification purposes, harvest timing is usually 60-90 days post-transplanting, when the fruit is fully colored and firm.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Bell Pepper (Capsicum annuum) is closely related to other Capsicum species within the Solanaceae family. It shares genus relations with Capsicum baccatum and Capsicum chinense. Family traits such as hairy stems and yellow flowers are common among these related species.
