What's the taxonomical classification of Lysimachia candida?

Lysimachia candida belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Ericales and into the family Primulaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Lysimachia, specifically identified as the species Lysimachia candida.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lysimachia candida has upright, herbaceous stems that reach heights of approximately 20 to 50 centimeters and feature opposite, lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically bright green and possesses a smooth, glabrous texture along both the upper and lower surfaces. Small, white, star-shaped flowers emerge from the leaf axils, characterized by five distinct petals that form a central corolla. These blossoms are often accompanied by inconspicuous greenish sepals and are borne on slender pedicels. The plant's overall structure is compact and spreading, making it well-adapted to moist, shaded environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Lysimachia candida?

This plant has anti-obesity, lipid metabolism regulation, fatty liver prevention, metabolic syndrome reversal, and anti-adipogenic pharmacological activities. These therapeutic properties allow the extract to effectively manage weight by inhibiting the formation of new fat cells. It plays a crucial role in improving lipid profiles and preventing the accumulation of lipids in the liver. Furthermore, the plant helps to reverse the complex components of metabolic syndrome through its diverse biological actions. These combined mechanisms offer a comprehensive approach to addressing various metabolic disorders.

As discussed in a study, Lysimachia candida (LCM) shows potential anti-obesity effects through the administration of 200 mg/kg/body weight daily for 140 days. This dosage significantly decreased body weight gain by 15.66% in high-fat-diet-fed male Wistar rats. The treatment also significantly decreased blood triglyceride and free fatty acid levels without causing substantial changes in leptin and adiponecetin levels. In 3T3-L1 cell-based assays, the butanol fraction of LCM exhibited antiadipogenic activity by downregulating adipogenic transcription factors and regulatory proteins. These pharmacological effects are mediated by the phytochemical astragalin, which works by downregulating the PPARG and FKBP51 signaling cascade.
