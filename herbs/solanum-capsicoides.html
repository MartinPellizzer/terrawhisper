Solanum capsicoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum capsicoides?

Solanum capsicoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Solanales. It is a member of the family Solanaceae, which features a wide array of diverse species. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Solanum, and its unique biological identity is defined by its species name, capsicoide.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum capsicoides has an erect, perennial growth habit characterized by woody stems, ovate to lanceolate leaves with wavy margins, and small, upright clusters of white, star-shaped flowers. The foliage is often covered in fine, bristly hairs that provide a textured surface to the leaf blades. Its inflorescence consists of several small blossoms that emerge from the leaf axils. Following pollination, the plant produces small, globose berries that transition from green to a bright, striking red color when mature. These berries are typically surrounded by a persistent green calyx that stays attached to the fruit.

What are the pharmacological activities of Solanum capsicoides?

This plant has antiproliferative pharmacological activity that can inhibit the growth and reproduction of certain cancer cells. Researchers study these properties to determine how specific compounds within the plant might arrest the cell cycle. By targeting rapid cellular division, these bioactive molecules demonstrate potential for therapeutic development in oncology. Further investigations are necessary to isolate the exact chemical constituents responsible for this effect. Understanding these mechanisms helps clarify the plant's role in medicinal chemistry.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic extract from Solanum capsicoides seeds was evaluated for its antiproliferative activity and toxicological effects. When mice were orally treated with the extract at 2000 mg/kg, no signs of toxicity were observed during behavioral observations or autopsies, and no mutagenicity or hemolytic activity was found. The S. capsicoides extract exhibited antiproliferative activity mainly in the K562 leukemia cell lineage. A pure compound from the plant, carpesterol, showed antiproliferative activity in glioma (U251), breast (MCF-7), kidney (786-0), ovary (OVCAR-03), and K562 cell lineages. While carpesterol showed no mutagenic or hemolytic activity, the TEST software estimated its developmental toxicology and an LD50 of 410 mg/kg.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compound carpesterol, along with various other bioactive phytochemicals that serve specific biological functions. Carpesterol is a known steroid-like substance that has been studied for its potential effects on hormone regulation and metabolic processes. In addition to this specific compound, the plant may harbor alkaloids or flavonoids that contribute to its overall therapeutic profile. These chemical constituents work in combination to interact with different cellular pathways in the body. Understanding the concentration of these substances is essential for determining the plant's medicinal efficacy.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic extract of Solanum capsicoides seeds was evaluated alongside the pure compound carpesterol. The extract was orally treated in mice at 2000 mg/kg and showed no signs of toxicity, mutagenicity, or hemolytic activity. While the extract exhibited antiproliferative activity mainly in the K562 leukemia cell lineage, carpesterol showed activity in glioma (U251), breast (MCF-7), kidney (786-0), ovary (OVCAR-03), and K562 lineages. According to the Toxicity Estimation Software Tool (TEST), carpesterol presented developmental toxicology and a lethal dose (LD50) of 410 mg/kg. These findings establish the antiproliferative and toxicological parameters for both the Solanum capsicoides extract and carpesterol.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe physiological distress including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, dizziness, hallucinations, and tachycardia. These toxic reactions occur because the plant contains solanine, a glycoalkaloid that acts as a potent neurotoxin. Ingesting even small amounts may disrupt the gastrointestinal system and lead to intense stomach cramping. The chemical compounds can also affect the central nervous system, triggering altered mental states and disorientation. Furthermore, the body may experience a rapid increase in heart rate as a systemic response to the poisoning.
