Gynura japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Gynura japonica?

Gyunura japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Gynura, and its specific species name, japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gyunura japonica has perennial, creeping rhizomes that produce basal clusters of long-petioled, cordate to reniform leaves with crenate margins and prominent venation. The foliage typically exhibits a soft, textured surface and can vary in size depending on the environmental conditions. During the flowering season, the plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers arranged in terminal panicles. These inflorescences emerge on slender stalks that rise above the primary leaf canopy. The overall structure allows the plant to spread effectively through its vegetative growth pattern.

Within these territories, it is often found growing in forest understories or along the edges of woodland streams. What are the pharmacological activities of Gynura japonica?

This plant has pharmacological activities that include treating blood stasis and treating traumatic injury. Its chemical constituents help promote blood circulation to resolve internal blockages and congestion. These properties also make it effective for reducing swelling and pain associated with physical wounds. By stimulating the healing process, it assists in the recovery of damaged tissues after an impact. This combination of effects makes it a valuable resource in traditional medicinal practices.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Gynura japonica are used as traditional medicine for treating blood stasis or traumatic injury. A total of 35 batches of the roots and aerial parts were collected and analyzed, revealing that 21 HPAs were identified. The roots contained 4.90 mg/g of HPAs, which was a higher level than the 2.21 mg/g found in the aerial parts. Male C57BL mice were orally administered extracts at dosages equivalent to 50 mg HPAs/kg body weight to evaluate hepatotoxicity. These HPAs, including senecionine, were responsible for the hepatotoxicity of the plant and caused significantly impaired bile acid homeostasis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal and toxic compounds including pyrrolizidine alkaloids, pyrrolizidine alkaloids (HPAs), and seneconine. These chemical constituents are often studied for their complex biological activities in various organic systems. While some alkaloids possess potential therapeutic properties, many pyrrolizidine alkaloids (HPAs) are known for their significant toxicity to the liver. Seneconine is a specific type of alkaloid within this group that can trigger various physiological responses. Understanding the concentration and structure of these substances is essential for evaluating the plant's overall pharmacological profile.

As discussed in a study, Gynura japonica contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids. These specific compounds are noted for causing over 50% of cases of hepatic sinus obstruction syndrome. The presence of these alkaloids in the plant induces hepatotoxicity in rats and Buffalo rat liver cells. Research into the decoction of the plant shows that it triggers mid-to late-stage apoptosis and necrosis in BRL cells. These medicinal compounds lead to increased expression of cytochrome C and pro-apoptotic proteins while elevating caspase-3 activity.
