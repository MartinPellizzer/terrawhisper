Buddleja madagascariensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Buddleja madagascariensis?

Buddleja madagascariensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized into the order Lamiales. It is a member of the family Scrophulariaceae, which places it within the genus Buddleja, ultimately identifying it by its specific species name, madagascariensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Buddlejia madagascariensis has lanceolate to narrowly elliptical leaves that are typically 5 to 15 centimeters long, featuring finely serrated margins and a textured, rugose upper surface. The plant exhibits a woody, shrubby growth habit with stems that can become quite robust over time. Its inflorescences consist of dense, terminal panicles bearing small, tubular flowers that vary in color from white to pale lilac. These flowers are characterized by four spreading lobes and are often accompanied by small, glandular hairs on the calyx and stems. The fruit develops into small, ovoid capsules containing numerous tiny, winged seeds.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds scutellarin 7-glucoside, hesperetin, and mimengoide B. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the overall therapeutic potential of the botanical species. Scutellarin 7-glucoside is often studied for its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the presence of hesperetin provides flavanone-based benefits to the plant's chemical profile. Mimengoide B serves as another significant component within its complex medicinal makeup.

As discussed in a study, two new flavonoid triglycosides have been isolated from the leaves of Buddleja madagascariensis. These specific compounds were identified as hesperetin and diosmetin 7-O (2",6"- di-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosyl)-beta-D-glucopyranosides through the use of mass and NMR spectroscopy. Additionally, scutellarien 7-glucoside is reported from this plant for the first time.
