Cayratia ciliifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Cayratia ciliifera?

Cayratiia ciliifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Vitales and the family Vitaceae, eventually reaching its specific classification within the genus Cayratiia. Finally, the plant is identified at the species level as ciliifera.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Cayratia ciliifera contains a diverse profile of bioactive secondary metabolites including alkaloids, flavonoids, saponins, tannins, and terpenoids. These chemical constituents contribute to the plant's significant pharmacological activities, such as antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Specifically, the presence of phenolic compounds provides antioxidant capabilities that help mitigate oxidative stress in biological systems. Researchers have also identified various glycosides within the plant that may play a role in its traditional medicinal uses. These combined compounds make the species a subject of interest for developing new therapeutic agents.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cayratia ciliifera?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat gastrointestinal disorders such as diarrhea, stomach aches, and digestive inflammation. Its medicinal properties are frequently applied to soothe intestinal discomfort and regulate bowel movements. Local practitioners also utilize the plant to address urinary tract issues and certain skin irritations. The phytochemical composition of the leaves contributes to its effectiveness in managing these various ailments. Traditional healing practices often rely on decoctions made from this species to alleviate internal pain.

What are the herbal preparations of Cayratia ciliifera?

Cayratia ciliifera is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice made from its leaves and roots. The leaves are often boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal consumption. When used externally, the plant material can be crushed into a paste to be applied directly to the skin. Some traditional practices also involve using the plant in concentrated extracts or macerated oils. These various forms allow the bioactive compounds to be administered for different therapeutic purposes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cayratia ciliifera can cause gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and potential toxicity if ingested in uncontrolled amounts due to the presence of unidentified bioactive compounds. While some traditional uses suggest medicinal benefits, the lack of standardized clinical data means that side effects are not fully documented for human consumption. Individuals may experience nausea, abdominal pain, or vomiting after consuming preparations of the plant. Topical application might lead to localized allergic reactions or contact dermatitis in sensitive users. Because its chemical composition is not entirely understood, accidental overdose could lead to more severe systemic complications.
