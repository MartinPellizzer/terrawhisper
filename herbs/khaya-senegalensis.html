Khaya (Khaya senegalensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Khaya senegalensis, commonly known as African shea, is a valuable tree species widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties and diverse applications in treating various health conditions.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Khaya (Khaya Senegalensis)?

Khaya senegalensis, also known as African shea tree or Khaya, is a tropical tree native to Africa, valued for its medicinal properties and the use of its seeds in traditional herbal medicine.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Khaya is Khaya senegalensis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Meliaceae, genus Khaya, and species senegalensis. It is commonly known as African shea in West Africa and Indian satinwood in South Asia. The plant is also referred to as Khaya in some regions and has synonyms such as K. indica and K. anthoteca in botanical literature.

Plant Description and Morphology

Khaya is a large, deciduous tree that can grow up to 30 meters tall with a broad, spreading crown and a straight trunk.

Its leaves are alternate, simple, and pinnately compound, typically consisting of 10-20 elliptic or ovate leaflets arranged in pairs. The flowers are small, fragrant, and white or pale yellow, arranged in panicles, with prominent stamens and a superior ovary. The root system is deep and extensive, providing stability and access to groundwater.

The bark is rough and fissured, the leaves are smooth and leathery, and the wood has a pale yellow color with a sweet, woody aroma.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Khaya is native to tropical regions of Africa, particularly the savannas and dry forests of West and Central Africa.

Its geographic distribution spans countries such as Senegal, Mali, Guinea, and Nigeria, extending into parts of Sudan and the Democratic Republic of Congo. The species thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soils, typically found in regions with a distinct dry season. It prefers full sun exposure and is commonly found in open woodlands and secondary forests.

Ecological conditions favoring its growth include moderate rainfall and minimal frost, making it adaptable to a range of tropical environments.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of Khaya senegalensis primarily involves the bark, leaves, and roots of the tree.

The bark is the most commonly utilized part, often harvested and prepared as a decoction or powder. Leaves are also used, typically in the form of a poultice or infusion to treat skin conditions. Roots are sometimes dried and ground into a powder for internal use.

Each part is processed differently depending on the traditional application and regional practices.

Phytochemical Composition

Khaya senegalensis contains a variety of active compounds that contribute to its medicinal properties, including alkaloids, flavonoids, and terpenoids.

Secondary metabolites such as saponins, tannins, and phenolic acids are also present and play a significant role in its therapeutic effects. These compounds are known for their antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities. Chemical markers like quercetin and kaempferol have been identified in the plant and are associated with its pharmacological benefits.

The combination of these phytochemical components makes Khaya senegalensis a valuable resource in traditional and modern herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Khaya senegalensis exhibits a range of pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, antimicrobial, and analgesic effects, supported by both traditional use and scientific studies.

Mechanisms of Action

Khaya senegalensis exerts its therapeutic effects through the bioactive compounds such as alkaloids, flavonoids, and tannins, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antispasmodic properties by interacting with various cellular targets and physiological pathways.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Khaya senegalensis is traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat a variety of conditions including pain, inflammation, respiratory disorders, and skin ailments due to its rich content of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and alkaloids.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Khaya senegalensis is typically prepared as a decoction, infusion, or powder, with the bark, leaves, and seeds being the most commonly used parts, depending on the traditional remedy being sought.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Khaya senegalensis for medicinal use typically ranges from 10 to 30 grams of the dried bark, which is decocted in water and consumed as a tea, with specific amounts and duration depending on the condition being treated.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Khaya senegalensis is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interactions with certain medications, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and lactation.

Drug Interactions

Khaya senegalensis may interact with medications that affect the liver or blood clotting, and it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use if you are taking any regular medications.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Khaya senegalensis is generally considered safe when used in traditional medicinal practices, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals and should be used with caution, especially in high doses or during pregnancy.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Khaya senegalensis is typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions with well-drained soil and ample sunlight, with harvesting of its timber and leaves occurring several years after planting, often between 5 to 10 years, depending on growth conditions.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Khaya senegalensis involve methods such as decoction, maceration, and cold pressing to obtain active compounds like limonoids, alkaloids, and flavonoids, which are then used in traditional herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Khaya senegalensis should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and prevent spoilage, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Khaya senegalensis has shown its potential in traditional medicine, with studies indicating its use in treating ailments such as inflammation, malaria, and skin conditions due to the presence of compounds like limonoids and flavonoids.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Khaya senegalensis has been traditionally used in various African countries for its medicinal properties, including treating ailments such as fever, inflammation, and digestive issues, with its bark, leaves, and seeds being the primary parts utilized.

Regulatory Status

Khaya senegalensis is not currently regulated as a medicinal herb in most countries, but its use in traditional medicine may be subject to local laws governing the sale and distribution of herbal products.
