Chloranthus multistachys

What's the taxonomical classification of Chloranthus multistachys?

Chloranthus multistachys belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Chloranthales and the family Chloranthaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is completed by its placement within the genus Chloranthus, with the specific designation of the species multistachys.

These spikes are characterized by numerous small, inconspicuous flowers that lack true petals. The floral bracts are often prominent and serve to protect the developing reproductive organs. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds beta-sitosterol, stigmasterol, lupeol, umbelliferone, and daphnin. These bioactive molecules contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of the species. The sterols beta-sitosterol and stigmasterol are known for their potential anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, lupeol provides significant therapeutic benefits through its various biological activities. The presence of umbelliferone and daphnin further enhances the chemical complexity and medicinal utility of the plant.

As discussed in a study, nine compounds were isolated and identified from the root of Chloranthus multistachys Pei. These constituents include lupeol (I), cycloeucalenol (II), isofragidin (III), daphnin (IV), umbelliferone (V), palmitic acid (VI), stigmasterol (VII), beta-sitosterol (VIII), and beta-daucosterol (IX). Aside from compound VI, all of these substances were isolated from this plant for the first time. The chemical isolation process involved techniques such as column chromatography and semi-prepariative thin layer chromatography. Spectroscopic analyses were utilized to elucidate the structures of the 9 identified compounds.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating various conditions including inflammation, fever, and certain types of cancer. Research indicates that extracts from the plant exhibit significant potential in inhibiting the growth of specific leukemia cells. Laboratory studies have demonstrated its ability to induce apoptosis in several human tumor cell lines. These biological activities are primarily attributed to the presence of bioactive compounds like chloranthic acid. Consequently, the plant is being investigated as a possible source for developing new anti-tumor therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the compound SGP-17-S was extracted from Chloranthus multistachys to evaluate its anti-leukemic effects. This compound inhibited the growth of leukemia cells in a time- and dose-dependent manner. SGP-17-S blocked HEL cells in the G2 phase and induced apoptosis by decreasing Bcl-2 and caspase-8 protein expression while increasing Bax and caspase-3 expression. Through CETSA, PARP1 was identified as an important target gene for the inhibition of HEL cell growth. Ultimately, SGP-17-S exerted its action on leukemia cells by targeting PARP1.
