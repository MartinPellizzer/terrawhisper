Artemisia minor

What's the taxonomical classification of Artemisia minor?

Artemisia minor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Asterales, this plant is a member of the family Asteraceae. Its precise taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Artemisia and the specific species minor.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artemisia minor has aromatic, silver-grey foliage composed of deeply pinnatifid leaves that measure between 2 and 5 centimeters in width. The leaf segments are narrow and lobed, creating a delicate, feathery texture across the plant surface. These leaves are densely covered in fine, silky hairs that provide their characteristic metallic sheen and protective coating. The plant grows in a low-mounding habit with stems that can become woody at the base over time. Small, inconspicuous flower heads appear on stalks during the summer months, typically appearing in yellowish-green clusters.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, sandy or loamy soil and full sun to maximum growth potential.

You should plant it in a location that avoids excessive moisture to prevent root rot. Once established, it is highly drought tolerant and performs well in rocky environments. Regular pruning during the growing season helps maintain its compact shape and encourages fresh foliage.

It is a hardy perennial that thrives in USDA zones 4 through 8.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Artemisia minor contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its aromatic leaves and flowering tops. The leaves are often dried to create infusions or tinctures aimed at aiding digestion and stimulating appetite. Because the plant's potency is concentrated in the foliage, practitioners avoid using the roots for most common applications. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds arteminorin C, arteminorin D, arteminorins A-C, ethyl caffeate, and caffeic acid. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse therapeutic profile found within the species. The various forms of arteminorin are particularly notable for their unique structural properties. Additionally, the presence of ethyl caffeate and caffeic acid provides important antioxidant potential. Together, these substances define the complex phytochemical makeup of the plant.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", 4 new compounds were isolated from the aerial parts of Artemisia minor, which include 3 bicoumarins named arteminorins A-C (1-3) and 1 neolignan named arteminorin D (4). These 4 new compounds were identified alongside 31 known ones. Among the constituents, ethyl caffeate (27) demonstrated in vitro cytotoxicity against the HepG2 cancer cell line. Additionally, arteminorin C (3) and luteolin (19) showed inhibitory activity on xanthine oxidase (XOX). Finally, caffeic acid (28) exhibited inhibitory activity on protein tyrosine phosphatase 1B (PTP1B).

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, stomach pain, allergic reactions, dizziness, skin irritation, neurotoxicity, and seizures. Consuming large amounts may lead to significant gastrointestinal distress or neurological damage. Some individuals might experience localized skin irritation upon direct contact with the foliage. High doses are particularly concerning because they can trigger severe neurotoxicity and potentially life-threatening seizures. Allergic reactions can also manifest as various bodily sensitivities in certain people.
