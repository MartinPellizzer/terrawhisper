Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Cotula is Anthemis cotula. It was first described by Carl von Linne in 1753. Synonyms include Cotula cotula and Anthemis cotula. Common names include common cudweed and mouse-ear. It is also known as chamomile weed in some regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is an annual herb with a fibrous root system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and often reddish. The leaves are alternate, lanceolate, and covered in fine hairs. The inflorescence is a flat-topped cluster of small yellow flowers. Each flower has four petals and a central disk, with yellow stamens and a single pistil. The fruit is a small achene with a pappus for wind dispersal.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) can be distinguished by its small, daisy-like flowers with yellow centers and white petals, and its finely divided, grass-like leaves. It has a distinct musty scent and grows in dry, open habitats. It is often confused with Anthemis species but can be differentiated by its leaf shape and flower structure.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cotula, or Anthemis cotula, is native to southern Europe and parts of Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained soils and is commonly found at low to mid-altitude regions.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) blooms from early spring to late summer, typically between April and September. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn, around August to October. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during its flowering stage, usually between June and August, when the characteristic small yellow flowers are most prominent.

Phylogenetic Relationships
