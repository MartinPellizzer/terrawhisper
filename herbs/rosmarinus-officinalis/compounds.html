Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis) Compounds

The primary compounds of Rosemary include essential oil, which contains terpenoids like cineole and camphor. Flavonoids such as apigenin and luteolin contribute to its antioxidant properties. Phenolic acids and tannins provide anti-inflammatory effects, while coumarin and glycosides support its medicinal uses.

Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis) Monograph

1. Essential oil

The essential oil of Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis) is a complex mixture of volatile compounds, primarily comprising 1,8-cineole and camphor. It is widely used in herbal medicine for its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective properties, supporting cognitive function and skin health.

2. Terpenoid

Terpenoids are a class of organic compounds found in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis). They contribute to the plant's aromatic properties and have antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective effects. Terpenoids like carnosic acid and rosmarinic acid are key to Rosemary's medicinal uses. These compounds support cognitive function and may help in treating oxidative stress-related conditions.

3. Flavonoid

Flavonoids are a group of bioactive compounds found in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis). They contribute to the plant's antioxidant properties, helping to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress. These compounds may support cognitive function and have anti-inflammatory effects, making them valuable in traditional herbal medicine.

4. Phenolic acid

Phenolic acid is a key active compound in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis). It contributes to the plant's antioxidant properties and is believed to support cognitive function and reduce oxidative stress. This compound is widely studied for its potential therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

5. Coumarin

Coumarin is a bioactive compound found in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis). It contributes to the plant's aromatic profile and may have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Coumarin is often studied for its potential therapeutic effects in herbal medicine.

6. Tannin

Tannin is a compound found in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis). It contributes to the plant's astringent properties and may support digestive health. Tannins also have antioxidant effects, helping to protect cells from oxidative damage. They are part of the complex bioactive profile of Rosemary used in herbal medicine.

7. Glycoside

Glycoside is a compound found in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis). It contributes to the plant's aromatic properties and has been studied for its potential anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. Glycosides in Rosemary may support digestive health and may help in reducing oxidative stress in the body.

8. Saponin

Saponin is a compound found in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis). It contributes to the plant's antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Saponins may support digestive health and help in the absorption of certain nutrients. They are also believed to have potential in reducing oxidative stress.

9. Anthocyanin

Anthocyanin is a pigment found in Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis) that contributes to its color and has antioxidant properties. It helps protect cells from oxidative stress and may support cardiovascular and cognitive health. While not the primary active compound in rosemary, anthocyanin enhances its overall therapeutic profile.

10. Alkaloid

Rosemary contains the alkaloid rosmarinine. This compound is found in the essential oils and foliage of Rosmarinus officinalis. It contributes to the plant's aromatic profile and has been studied for its potential anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Rosmarine is also known to support cognitive function and may aid in memory retention.
