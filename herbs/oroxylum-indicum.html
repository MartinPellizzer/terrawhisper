Oroxylum indicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Oroxylum indicum?

Oroxylum indicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Bignoniaceae. At the most specific levels of its biological classification, it is identified by the genus Oroxylum and the species indicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oroxylum indicum has a medium-sized tree habit characterized by pinnately compound leaves, small yellowish flowers, and small, fleshy, ellipsoidal fruits. The leaves are composed of several pairs of leaflets that are typically elliptical or oblong in shape with entire margins. Its flowers are arranged in small panicles and possess five petals that contribute to a delicate appearance. The fruit develops into a small drupe that contains a single seed, aiding in its dispersal within its native habitat. The bark and woody stems exhibit a sturdy structure typical of many members of the Rutaceae family.

It thrives primarily in moist, deciduous forests and evergreen habitats located within these warm climates. The species is often distributed along river valleys and hilly terrains where soil moisture is consistently available. Its presence is typically limited to specific ecological niches that provide the necessary humidity and temperature profiles for its growth. What are the pharmacological activities of Oroxylum indicum?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-cancer, anti-tumor, anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidant, and anti-microbial properties. Its chemical constituents work to inhibit the growth of malignant cells and prevent the progression of various types of tumors. Additionally, the plant provides significant protection against oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals in the body. It also exhibits the ability to reduce inflammation and combat various pathogenic microorganisms. These combined biological effects make the species a subject of interest for therapeutic research.

As discussed in a study, Oroxylum indicum is a versatile plant where almost every part is reported to possess a wide range of pharmacological activities. Many of the important bioactivities of this medicinal plant are related to its most abundant bioactive constituent, baicalein. A systematic review of 20 articles searched from databases up to 31 March 2020 reported the biological activities and therapeutic potential of baicalein originating from Oroxylum indicum. These activities include anti-cancer, antibacterial, anti-hyperglycemia, neurogenesis, cardioprotective, anti-adipogenesis, anti-inflammatory, and wound healing effects. Despite these findings, there remains a scarcity of evidence regarding the efficacy of this natural active compound in human clinical studies.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal flavones baiyeclein, baicalin, scutellarein, oroxin A, and apiin. These bioactive compounds are primarily concentrated within the roots of the species and contribute to its significant therapeutic potential. Baiyeclein and baicalin are frequently studied for their potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Scutellarein and oroxin A further support these biological activities by modulating various cellular signaling pathways. Additionally, the presence of apiin adds to the complex chemical profile used in traditional medicine. Together, these specific constituents provide the foundation for the plant's diverse pharmacological effects.

As discussed in a study, Oroxylum indicum is a very important medicinal plant with several therapeutic applications. The isolated compound baicalein (3) from this plant acts as a potent µ- and κ-opioid receptor antagonist agent via the reversal of agonist-mediated cAMP inhibition. In in vivo experiments, the compound 3 showed the most potent and competitive antagonistic activity at a 20 mg/kg dose. When an acute dose of 3 (20 mg/kg) and the pan opioid receptor antagonist naloxone (20 mg/kg) were used, the morphine-induced antinociception was blocked. This caused the paw withdrawal latency to decrease up to 8.3 s and 9.6 s, respectively. In silico studies further support that compound 3 binds with MOR and KOR.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cancer, inflammation, bacterial infections, diabetes, and arthritis. Its bioactive compounds possess potent antioxidant properties that help mitigate systemic inflammation throughout the body. Researchers have explored its potential to combat various bacterial infections by inhibiting microbial growth. Additionally, studies suggest its extracts may assist in managing blood sugar levels related to diabetes. These therapeutic qualities also make it a candidate for reducing the joint pain and swelling associated with arthritis.

As discussed in a study, Oroxylum indicum is one of the most widely used medicinal plants in India, China, and Southeast Asian countries. A new co-crystal consisting of two active pharmaceutical ingredients, orooxylin A and chrysin, was isolated from the chloroform extract of the leaves of Oroxylum indicum. This novel co-crystal was found to exist in a 1:1 ratio of the two components. When studied in HeLa cervical cancer cells, the isolated co-crystal induced cell death with a very low IC50 value of 8.49 μM. The compound induced caspase-dependent apoptosis through the activation of Caspase-3 via the inhibition of ERKs and activation of p38 of the MAPK cell signaling pathway.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or touched without protection. Some individuals may experience redness or itching upon direct contact with the plant's sap or foliage. Internal consumption of concentrated extracts might lead to nausea or stomach cramps in sensitive users. There is also a potential for systemic allergic reactions if the bioactive compounds enter the bloodstream through broken skin. Careful handling is recommended to avoid these adverse physiological responses.
