Nasturtium officinale

What's the taxonomical classification of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Brassicales and is a member of the family Brassicaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Nasturtium and the species officinale.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nasturtium officinale has a creeping, hollow, cylindrical stem that can grow up to several meters long and produces small, white to pale blue flowers.

The leaves are alternate and cordate, featuring long petioles and slightly wavy or crenate margins. Its submerged and emergent stems develop adventitious roots at the nodes to facilitate vegetative spreading through aquatic environments. The plant's root system is relatively shallow, consisting of rhizomes that anchor the stems to the muddy substrate.

Small, ovate leaves may also appear on the submerged portions of the plant.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Nasturtium officinale contains medicinal compounds in its entire plant body, specifically within the leaves, stems, and roots. The leaves are frequently harvested to create infusions or topical applications due to their high concentration of vitamin C and glucosinolates. These chemical constituents provide potent antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties that support the immune system. Some traditional practices also utilize the roots for their unique biochemical profile, though the foliage remains the most common medicinal component. Consuming the plant in various forms can help alleviate symptoms of respiratory infections and digestive issues.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive phytochemicals including glucosinolates such as sinigrin, flavonoids like quercetin and kaempferol, phenolic acids including ferulic and caffeic acid, terpenoids, and alkaloids.

The presence of glucosinolates is particularly significant because their breakdown products often contribute to the plant's potent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activities. Flavonoids and phenolic acids work synergistically to provide strong antioxidant properties that help neutralize free radicals in the body. Additionally, the complex mixture of terpenoids and alkaloids contributes to the unique pharmacological profile used in traditional medicine.

These combined chemical constituents make Nasturtium officinale a significant subject of study for its therapeutic potential.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Glucosinolates (e.g., sinigrin)
  • Flavonoids (e.g., quercetin, kaempferol)
  • Phenolic acids (e.g., ferulic acid, caffeic acid)
  • Terpenoids
  • Alkaloids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Nasturtium officinale targets cyclooxygenase-1 and cyclooxygenase-2 enzymes, nuclear factor kappa B, and various microbial enzymes to exert its pharmacological effects. The plant contains glucosinolates like sinigrin, which break down into isothiocyanates that inhibit the NF-kappaB signaling pathway to reduce inflammation. These bioactive compounds also interact with bacterial proteins to disrupt cell wall integrity and metabolic processes in pathogens. Additionally, polyphenolic constituents in the plant may influence antioxidant enzymes and mitigate oxidative stress within cellular environments. Such diverse interactions with protein targets underpin its documented antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale has therapeutic actions including bitter, alterative, diaphoretic, diuretic, expectorant, astringent, and antiseptic properties.

Its bitter profile stimulates digestion, while its role as an alterative helps to gradually restore systemic health and purify the blood. The plant acts as a diuretic to increase urine output and a diaphoretic to induce sweating for detoxification. As an expectorant, it assists in clearing mucus from the respiratory tract.

Finally, its astringent and antiseptic qualities help to tighten tissues and inhibit the growth of harmful microorganisms.

The primary therapeutic actions of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Bitter
  • Alterative
  • Diaphoretic
  • Diuretic
  • Expectorant
  • Astringent
  • Antiseptic

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat respiratory infections, digestive issues, urinary tract infections, and inflammation.

Its antimicrobial properties help the body fight off various bacterial and viral pathogens. For those suffering from digestive issues, it can assist in regulating gut health and easing discomfort. The plant also acts as a natural diuretic to help clear urinary tract infections.

Furthermore, its bioactive compounds are effective at reducing systemic inflammation throughout the body.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Respiratory infections
  • Digestive issues
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Inflammation

What are the herbal preparations of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale is prepared as fresh juices, decoctions, infusions, tinctures, dried herb powders, or culinary garnishes. The leaves and flowers are frequently consumed raw in salads to preserve their high vitamin C and sulfur content. For medicinal purposes, a liquid infusion or decoction can be brewed from the fresh or dried plant material to support liver and kidney function. Tinctures made from the plant parts provide a concentrated way to administer its bioactive compounds over a longer period. Additionally, the dried herb can be ground into a fine powder for use in capsules or blended into food preparations.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Nasturtium officinale can cause gastrointestinal distress, allergic reactions, and potential interference with blood clotting. Consuming large amounts of the plant may lead to stomach aches, nausea, or diarrhea in sensitive individuals. Some people may experience skin irritation or rashes if they come into direct contact with the plant's juices. Because the plant contains compounds that may affect blood viscosity, those on anticoagulant medications should exercise caution. It is also important to note that pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid significant consumption due to a lack of extensive safety data.
