What to know about Nasturtium officinale (watercress) before using it medicinally

Nasturtium officinale, commonly known as watercress, is a nutrient-rich herb that has been valued for centuries for its ability to improve overall health, boost the immune system, and combat various diseases due to its high levels of vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants.

From a horticultural perspective, Nasturtium officinale is a fast-growing, cold-hardy annual that thrives in moist environments and can be harvested continuously, making it an ideal crop for home gardens and commercial agriculture. From a botanical standpoint, this herb belongs to the Brassicaceae family and is characterized by its pungent, peppery flavor and its delicate, round leaves.

Historically, Nasturtium officinale has been used for centuries in traditional medicine, with references to its use dating back to ancient Greece and Rome, where it was prized for its medicinal and culinary properties.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Nasturtium officinale.

What are the medicinal properties of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale helps with various health issues, including inflammation, antimicrobial properties, and antioxidant activity. It also supports cardiovascular health by reducing blood pressure and cholesterol levels. Additionally, it may help in reducing cancer risk and improving cognitive function.

The active constituents of Nasturtium officinale include flavonoids, alkaloids, and glucosinolates, which are responsible for its medicinal properties. These compounds have been shown to exhibit anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant activities, making the plant a potential therapeutic agent for various health conditions.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are its leaves, stems, and roots. The leaves are rich in vitamins and minerals, while the stems and roots contain a higher concentration of flavonoids and alkaloids. These plant parts have been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat various health conditions, including digestive issues and skin problems.

Improper use of Nasturtium officinale may lead to side effects such as allergic reactions, digestive issues, and interactions with certain medications. Excessive consumption of the plant may cause nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, while long-term use may lead to kidney damage and bleeding disorders.

To use Nasturtium officinale medicinally, it is essential to exercise caution and follow proper guidelines. Pregnant and breastfeeding women, as well as individuals with kidney disease, should consult a healthcare professional before using the plant. Additionally, individuals with allergies or sensitivities to other plants in the Brassicaceae family should avoid using Nasturtium officinale.

What are the horticulural aspects of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale grow best in moist, fertile soils with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. They require partial shade to full sun and a consistent water supply. The optimal temperature for growth is between 15°C and 20°C. Watercress is a fast-growing crop, requiring frequent watering.

Planting tips for Nasturtium officinale include selecting a location with consistent moisture and providing a nutrient-rich soil mix. Sow seeds thinly and evenly, 1-2 cm deep, in late spring or early summer. Water gently but thoroughly after sowing. Thin seedlings to 5-10 cm apart to prevent overcrowding.

Harvesting watercress typically begins 20-30 days after sowing, when leaves are 5-7 cm long. Pinch or cut off individual leaves or harvest the entire plant by cutting the stem near the base. Harvesting watercress in the morning, when the leaves are at their highest water content, is ideal. Harvesting continuously encourages the plant to produce new leaves.

Common pests affecting Nasturtium officinale include aphids, slugs, and snails, which damage leaves and stems. Fungal diseases like downy mildew and root rot can also affect watercress, often caused by overwatering or poor soil drainage. Regularly inspecting the crop and controlling pest populations can prevent damage to the plant.

What are the botanical characteristics of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale, commonly known as watercress, is a fast-growing, annual or biennial herb that exhibits several distinctive botanical characteristics.

Its leaves are alternate, simple, and deeply lobed or pinnatifid, typically having 2-4 pairs of leaflets with a rounded base and an acute tip. The leaves are also slightly succulent and have a waxy texture, allowing them to withstand periodic flooding in aquatic environments.

The stems of Nasturtium officinale are slender, erect, and branching, growing up to 1-3 feet tall and spreading outwards as they mature. Small, white or yellowish flowers are borne on long, thin stalks, typically clustered at the stem tips, and consist of four petals and numerous stamens. The flowers are self-pollinating and produce small, nut-like seed pods that contain several seeds each.

Overall, the botanical characteristics of Nasturtium officinale enable it to thrive in a variety of aquatic and terrestrial environments, making it a popular crop for salads, soups, and other culinary applications.

What are the variants of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale has variants, such as N. microphyllum, which is commonly known as "small-leafed nasturtium" or "narrow-leaved peppermint", characterized by its smaller leaves and more delicate appearance.

Another variant is N. speciesa, also referred to as "Indian cress", featuring a stronger peppery flavor than the original species.

Additionally, there are several cultivars, such as 'Alaska' and 'Empress of India', which offer various leaf shapes, sizes, and colors, making them popular for ornamental purposes in gardens and landscapes.
