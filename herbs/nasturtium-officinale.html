Nasturtium officinale

What's the taxonomical classification of Nasturtium officinale?

Nasturtium officinale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Brassicales and is a member of the family Brassicaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Nasturtium and the species officinale.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nasturtium officinale has a creeping, hollow, cylindrical stem that can grow up to several meters long and produces small, white to pale blue flowers. The leaves are alternate and cordate, featuring long petioles and slightly wavy or crenate margins. Its submerged and emergent stems develop adventitious roots at the nodes to facilitate vegetative spreading through aquatic environments. The plant's root system is relatively shallow, consisting of rhizomes that anchor the stems to the muddy substrate. Small, ovate leaves may also appear on the submerged portions of the plant.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, specifically spanning across much of Europe, Northern Asia, and North America. It thrives in wetland environments such as marshes, stream banks, and damp ditches where moisture is abundant. While its primary natural range is centered in these northern latitudes, it has been introduced to various other parts of the world through human activity. The species prefers cool, moist climates and is rarely found in arid or extremely tropical zones. Because it relies on specific hydrological conditions, its distribution is often patchy and tied to local water sources.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Nasturtium officinale contains medicinal compounds in its entire plant body, specifically within the leaves, stems, and roots. The leaves are frequently harvested to create infusions or topical applications due to their high concentration of vitamin C and glucosinolates. These chemical constituents provide potent antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties that support the immune system. Some traditional practices also utilize the roots for their unique biochemical profile, though the foliage remains the most common medicinal component. Consuming the plant in various forms can help alleviate symptoms of respiratory infections and digestive issues.

What are the pharmacological activities of Nasturtium officinale?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and antiviral pharmacological activities that stem from its rich concentration of glucosinolates and secondary metabolites. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within cellular structures. The presence of bioactive compounds also provides significant antibacterial and antimicrobial effects against various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, the plant demonstrates antiviral potential by inhibiting the replication of certain viruses. These diverse therapeutic actions make Nasturtium officinale a valuable subject for medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, Nasturtium officinale possesses a wide range of pharmacological properties, particularly anti-inflammation and antioxidant potentials. In a study involving 44 male Wistar rats, the hydroalcoholic extract of Nasturtium officinale (NOE) was administered at a dose of 500 mg/kg. Treatment with NOE at 500 mg/kg remarkably reduced SMA-α gene expression compared to the asthma group (p < 0.05). The administration of NOE also significantly increased the level of glutathione peroxidase (GPX) compared to the asthma group (p < 0.05). Furthermore, NOE decreased the expression of IL-1B and TNF-α genes, although this result was not statistically significant. Overall, NOE is able to reduce airway inflammation by reducing inflammatory markers and increasing GPX activity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including glucobrassicin, glucunasurtiin, gluconasturtiin, glucosinolates, and isotisocyanates. The presence of various glucosinolates serves as a chemical foundation for the plant's specialized defense mechanisms. When the plant tissue is damaged, these precursors undergo enzymatic breakdown to produce bioactive isotisocyanates. Specific derivatives such as glucobrassicin and glucunasurtiin contribute to the unique pharmacological profile of the species. Research suggests that both glucunasurtiin and gluconasturtiin play significant roles in the plant's biological activity.

As discussed in a study, Nasturtium officinale contains various medicinal compounds including glucobrassicin at 493.00 mg/100 g DW after 10 days and gluconasturtiin at 268.04 mg/100 g DW after 20 days. The highest total polyphenol content reaches 2690 mg GAE/100 g DW after a 20-day growth period. Dominant polyphenols include sinapinic acid at 114.83 mg/100 g DW at 10 days and ferulic acid at 87.78 mg/100 g DW at 20 days. Extracts from microshoot cultures show an inhibition of hyperpigmentation by 18.12% and anti-elastase activity of 79.78%. Additionally, these extracts can inhibit the growth of Cutibacterium acnes with a MIC of 0.625 mg/mL.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating respiratory infections, scurvy, inflammation, oral microbial infections, and liver diseases. Its high vitamin C content makes it an effective remedy for preventing and managing scurvy. The plant contains bioactive compounds that help reduce inflammation throughout the body. It also demonstrates antimicrobial properties that can combat various oral microbial infections. Additionally, its medicinal properties are utilized to support liver health and address certain liver diseases.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause an allergic reaction, nausea, stomach upset, and diarrhea in some individuals. Sensitive people may experience skin rashes or respiratory issues after coming into contact with the leaves or flowers. Ingesting the plant in large quantities often leads to significant gastrointestinal distress. These digestive side effects typically manifest as cramping or loose stools shortly after consumption. It is important to monitor how your body reacts to any part of this herb.
