Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

The Garden Nasturtium, scientifically known as Nasturtium officinale, is a vibrant and versatile plant widely used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties and medicinal benefits.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium Officinale)?

Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale) is a flowering plant native to Europe and Asia, commonly used in herbal medicine for its peppery leaves, vibrant flowers, and medicinal properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Garden Nasturtium is Nasturtium officinale. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Equisetopsida, order Brassicales, family Tropiceae, genus Nasturtium, and species Nasturtium officinale. It is commonly known as Garden Nasturtium in Europe and North America, and as Indian Bittercress in South Asia. Synonyms for this plant include Nasturtium hederifolium (botanical) and various regional names such as Tropaeolum hederifolium and Tropaeolum officinale.

Plant Description and Morphology

Garden Nasturtium is an annual herbaceous plant that grows up to 60 cm tall, with a bushy, branching structure and upright stems covered in fine hairs.

Its leaves are heart-shaped, with a waxy surface and serrated edges, typically 3-8 cm in length. The flowers are brightly colored, with a spurred petal at the base and showy, rounded petals in shades of yellow, orange, and red. The root system consists of fibrous roots that are shallow and spread across the soil surface.

The plant has a distinctive peppery scent, with leaves and flowers that are slightly rough to the touch and have a peppery taste.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Garden Nasturtium is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa.

It thrives in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure. The plant is commonly found in meadows, along roadsides, and in disturbed soils. It prefers moderate temperatures and can tolerate light frost.

Its natural range extends from southern England to the Mediterranean region.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in Garden Nasturtium are the leaves, flowers, and seeds.

The leaves are commonly harvested for their peppery taste and medicinal properties. Flowers are also used, often dried and made into infusions or tinctures. Seeds are collected and used in various formulations, though they are less frequently utilized compared to the leaves and flowers.

Each part contains compounds that contribute to the plant's therapeutic uses in herbal medicine.

These compounds collectively support the use of Nasturtium officinale in herbal medicine for various health applications.

Therapeutic Uses

The Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale) is widely used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties.

It is commonly employed to treat respiratory conditions such as coughs, bronchitis, and sinusitis due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects. The plant is also used to support digestive health by alleviating symptoms of indigestion and bloating. Its active compounds help reduce inflammation and fight bacterial infections.

Additionally, it is applied externally to treat skin infections and wounds due to its antiseptic properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The Garden Nasturtium is commonly prepared in various forms for use in herbal medicine, including tinctures, teas, salves, and topical ointments.

Tinctures are made by soaking the aerial parts of the plant in alcohol to extract its active compounds. Teas are prepared by steeping dried leaves and flowers in hot water, often used for respiratory and digestive support. Salves and ointments are made by combining the plant's oils with a base such as beeswax or petroleum jelly, applied directly to the skin for its anti-inflammatory properties.

These forms allow for targeted application depending on the intended therapeutic use.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale) for herbal use is typically 200 to 400 mg of dried herb per day, divided into two or three doses.

Extracts should be prepared at a concentration of no more than 1:5 (1 gram of herb to 5 milliliters of liquid) to ensure safe usage. It is recommended to take the herb once or twice daily, depending on the condition being addressed. The duration of use should not exceed four weeks without consulting a healthcare professional.

Prolonged or excessive use may lead to gastrointestinal upset or other adverse effects.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Garden Nasturtium is generally safe for most adults, but it can cause mild side effects such as stomach upset, nausea, or skin irritation in some individuals.

Side effects are more likely with excessive consumption or topical application, especially in sensitive skin. People with allergies to plants in the Brassicaceae family, such as mustard or radish, may experience allergic reactions to Garden Nasturtium. It is contraindicated for individuals with kidney disease due to its potential to affect renal function.

During pregnancy, Garden Nasturtium should be used with caution, as its safety has not been fully established, and it is recommended to consult a healthcare provider before use.

Drug Interactions

When used alongside pharmaceutical medications, Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale) may interact with certain drugs, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.

It can affect the metabolism of some medications in the liver, particularly those processed by the CYP450 enzyme system. This interaction may lead to higher concentrations of these drugs in the bloodstream, increasing the likelihood of adverse reactions. Additionally, Garden Nasturtium may interact with supplements such as vitamin C, iron, and others, potentially enhancing or reducing their absorption.

These interactions should be monitored closely, especially when combining with prescription medications or high-dose supplements.

Toxicity and Precautions

The Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it can be toxic if consumed in excessive amounts.

The toxic dose varies depending on individual factors, but ingestion of more than 10 grams of the plant per day may lead to adverse effects. Long-term safety data is limited, and prolonged use may cause gastrointestinal irritation or other systemic effects. While poisoning is rare, it can occur in cases of accidental ingestion by children or pets.

Symptoms of poisoning may include nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale) is a hardy annual that thrives in well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5.

It prefers full sun but can tolerate partial shade, though reduced flowering may occur. Regular watering is necessary during dry periods, but overwatering should be avoided to prevent root rot. Propagation is typically done from seeds, sown directly in the garden after the last frost. The plant reaches maturity in about 60 to 80 days, with flowers and leaves ready for harvest in early summer.

Harvesting is best done in the morning when essential oils are most concentrated, using scissors to cut stems just above a leaf node.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and Extraction of Garden Nasturtium involves several methods to obtain its active compounds for use in herbal medicine.

Drying is a common initial step where the harvested plant material is spread in a well-ventilated area or using low-temperature drying to preserve its medicinal properties. Extraction methods include using solvents like water or alcohol to draw out the essential oils and alkaloids. Distillation is employed to concentrate volatile compounds, particularly the essential oils, which are highly valued in herbal formulations.

Grinding the dried plant material enhances surface area, making it more effective for further processing or direct use in herbal remedies.

Storage and Shelf Life

The shelf life of Garden Nasturtium (Nasturtium officinale) depends on storage conditions and intended use.

When properly stored, dried leaves and flowers can last up to one year, while fresh plant material should be used within a few weeks. It is light sensitive, so it should be kept in a cool, dark place to prevent degradation of active compounds. Moisture control is essential to avoid mold and spoilage; store in a dry environment with low humidity.

Use airtight containers to maintain potency and extend shelf life.

The plant's leaves and flowers were traditionally brewed into teas to aid digestion and reduce inflammation. In ancient times, it was also applied topically to soothe skin irritations and as a natural remedy for wounds.

Its historical significance is further noted in traditional European and Asian herbal practices.

Cultivation alternatives are essential to reduce pressure on wild sources and ensure long-term availability.
