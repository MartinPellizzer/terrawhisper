Haleinia elliptica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Gentianales and falls under the family Gentianaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Halenia, and its species name, elliptica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Halenia elliptica has a compact, leafless, and branched morphology characterized by small, yellowish-green, cylindrical stems that terminate in tiny, inconspicuous flowers. The plant body consists of a highly branched, somewhat dichotomous structure that lacks true leaves or roots. Each stem segment is typically very short and slender, giving the organism a delicate, moss-like appearance. The reproductive structures are minute and lack petals, which is a common trait among these parasitic plants. This specific morphology allows the plant to function as a mycoheterotroph, drawing nutrients directly from fungal networks in the soil.

These diverse properties make Halenia elliptica a subject of interest for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the polysaccharide HEPN-1 from Halenia ellipticca has a molecular weight of 10.80 kDa and a branching degree of 0.52. Within the dose range of 75-300 μg/mL, HEPN-1 increases the enzyme activities of SOD, CAT, and GSH-Px while decreasing MDA levels in H2O2-induced RAW 264.7 cells. After 6 weeks of intragastric administration at 300 mg/kg, HEPN-1 improved the antioxidant enzyme system and learning and memory deficits in mice. The substance also inhibited MDA formation in D-gal-induced aging mice through the activation of PI3K/Akt and Nrf2/HO-1 signalling pathways. These findings suggest that HEPN-1 can serve as a potential natural antioxidant to prevent aging.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract of Halenia elliptica contains several medicinal components including xanthone glycosides and xanthone aglycons. Researchers utilized HPLC with a diode array detector (DAD) and HPLC-MS to detect these constituents within animal samples. After rats were treated with different extracts of Halenia elliptica, 6 main components were isolated from the animal samples. Low polar xanthone aglycons were clearly observed in the liver, lipid, blood, kidney, heart, and brain tissue of the rats. Conversely, the polar xanthone glycosides were detected in very small amounts in the animal samples because they can be decomposed into xanthone aglycons during digestion, absorption, and metabolism.
