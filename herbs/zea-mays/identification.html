Botanical Identification of Indian Corn (Zea mays)

To identify Indian Corn (Zea mays) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Indian Corn (Zea mays) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Indian Corn, or Zea mays, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Liliopsida, order Poales, family Poaceae, genus Zea, and species Zea mays. These classifications reflect its role as a monocot grass plant within the larger group of flowering plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Zea mays is the scientific name for Indian Corn, also known as maize. It was first described by Carl von Linne in 1753. The plant has several synonyms, including Zea mays L. and Zea mays subsp. mays. Common names vary widely, including corn, sweet corn, and Indian corn, reflecting its cultural and regional significance.

Morphological Characteristics

Indian Corn, or Zea mays, is an annual plant with a robust root system consisting of a primary taproot and fibrous roots. Its stem is cylindrical, hollow, and ridged, growing upright. The leaves are broad, parallel-veined, and taper to a point, with a waxy surface. The inflorescence is a tassel at the top, with male flowers, and female ears of corn below. Each ear bears rows of kernels enclosed in a protective husk, with each kernel being a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Indian Corn (Zea mays) can be distinguished by its large, tasseled male flowers at the top of the plant and the presence of multiple rows of kernels on the female ears. The leaves are broad and flat, with a distinctive blue-green color. It is typically taller than other grasses and has a robust stem. The husk of the ear is tightly wrapped around the kernels, which are often yellow or white in color.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Indian Corn, or Zea mays, is native to Central and South America. It thrives in warm climates with ample sunlight and well-drained soils. It is commonly found at low to moderate altitudes, typically between 0 and 2,000 meters. Its adaptability allows it to grow in various regions with suitable conditions.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Indian Corn (Zea mays) blooms during late summer, typically between July and August, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in the fall, from September to October. Harvest is usually done when the kernels are fully mature, indicated by a hard, dry stalk and a milky appearance inside the ears.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Indian Corn, Zea mays, is closely related to other grasses in the Poaceae family. Its closest relatives include species like Zea luxurians and Zea diploperma. These species share similar genetic and morphological traits, reflecting their evolutionary ties within the genus Zea. The family traits of Poaceae, such as hollow stems and parallel leaf veins, are also evident in Indian Corn.
