Engelhardia roxburghiana

What's the taxonomical classification of Engelhardia roxburghiana?

Engelhardia roxburghiana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae, which places it in the order Fagales. The plant is a member of the family Juglandaceae and is categorized under the genus Engelhardia, eventually reaching its specific identification as the species roxburghiana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Engelhardia roxburghiana has simple, alternate leaves that are typically ovate or elliptical in shape with serrated margins and acuminate tips. The leaf texture is often coriaceous, providing a sturdy structure to the canopy. Its bark is generally smooth or slightly fissured, serving as a protective outer layer for the woody stem. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are arranged in catkin-like structures. These inflorescences eventually give rise to winged samaras, which facilitate seed dispersal through the wind.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds astilbin, engeletin, isoengeletin, kaempferol, and naringenin. These specific flavonoids are responsible for many of the plant's therapeutic properties. Astilbin and its derivatives, engeletin and isoengeletin, are often studied for their ability to improve circulation and reduce inflammation. Additionally, the presence of kaempferol and naringenin contributes significant antioxidant and protective effects to the organism. Together, these chemical constituents form a complex profile of bioactive substances.

As discussed in a study, Engelhardia roxburghiana leaves are used to treat diabetes mellitus and hyperlipidemia. Chemical analysis of the total flavonoids of E. roxburghiana leaves (TFER) revealed 10 components, with 7 acting as active ingredients. These 7 active ingredients include naringenin, kaempferol, quercetin, isoengeletin, engeletin, astilbin, and quercitrin. The TFER demonstrated regulatory effects on blood glucose and lipids by down-regulating the mRNA level of the NF-κB p65 subunit, TNF-α, VCAM-1, and IL-1β expressions in the thoracic aorta. These components also target inflammatory signaling pathways such as NF-κB, Toll-like receptor, and TNF signaling.
