Eleutherococcus sessiliflorus

What's the taxonomical classification of Eleutherococcus sessiliflorus?

Eleutherococcus sessiliflorus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the taxonomic lineage of the order Apiales. It is further categorized under the family Araliaceae, placing it within the genus Eleutherococcus, and is specifically identified by its species name, sessiliflorus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eleutherococcus sessiliflorus has woody, deciduous stems characterized by a prickly surface and leaves that are pinnately compound with several pairs of leaflets. The leaflets are typically elliptical or lanceolate in shape, featuring serrated margins and a smooth or slightly textured upper surface. Flowers are small and arranged in sessile umbels, meaning they lack stalks and sit directly upon the stem. These inflorescences produce small fruits that transition in color as they mature. The overall architecture of the plant is a spreading shrub that relies on these distinct leaf and stem structures for identification.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and eastern China, specifically within provinces such as Hubei, Hunan, Jiangxi, and Zhejiang. It typically thrives in temperate forest ecosystems located at moderate elevations. These populations are often found in shaded areas under the canopy of deciduous or mixed woodlands. The distribution is somewhat localized, following the specific climatic and soil requirements of these East Asian mountain ranges. What are the pharmacological activities of Eleutherococcus sessiliflorus?

This plant has anti-tumor, antineoplastic, and cytotoxic activities that demonstrate its potential in medicinal research. These pharmacological properties suggest that the plant can effectively inhibit the growth and spread of malignant cells. Specifically, its antineoplastic effects target the development of cancer within biological systems. The observed cytotoxic activity indicates that certain compounds within the plant can induce cell death in targeted diseased tissues. Consequently, these diverse anti-tumor characteristics make it a subject of significant interest for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the ethanol extract of the leaves of Eleutherococcus sessiliflorus contains a rich amount of 3,4-seco-lupane tritrepeneoids (3,4-SLT), specifically chiisanoside (CSS), divarosiide (DVS), sessiloside-A1 (SSA), chiisanogenin (CSG), and sessiligenin (SSG). These 5 isolated 3,4-SLT compounds can inhibit the growth of HepG2 cells as demonstrated by a CCK-8 assay. Among these, SSG showed the most significant cytotoxicity and can effectively induce HepG2 cell apoptosis. Furthermore, results from western blot and qRT-PCR indicated that SSG can inhibit the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway through controlling multi-targets. This research suggests that 3,4-SLT from the plant might become a new research hotspot for antineoplastic drugs.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds chiisanogenin, chiisanoside, divaroside, eleutheroside E, and sessiloside-A. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the complex therapeutic profile of the species. Researchers study these substances to understand their potential roles in enhancing physiological resilience and immune function. The presence of eleutheroside E, in particular, is often highlighted for its biological activity. Detailed biochemical analysis reveals how these varied compounds interact within different organic systems.

As discussed in a study, the ethanol extract of the leaves of Eleutherococcus sessiliflorus contains a new 3,4-seco-lupane triterpene named sessiligenin (1). This plant also yields four known 3,4-seco-lupane triterpene derivatives identified as chiisanogenin (2), chiisanoside (3), divaroside (4), and sessiliside-A1 (5). These compounds were tested for cytotoxicities against cancer cell lines HepG2, B16-F10, Lewis, and YAC-1, as well as normal cell lines NCTC1469 and HL-7702. Significant cytotoxicities were found for sessiligenin (1), which exhibited much lower cytotoxicities against the 2 normal cell lines NCTC1469 and HL-7702. The study noted that cytotoxicity increased significantly with the complete removal of the glycosyl group and the formation of the 11-hydroxyl and 3-carboxyl by the opening of the lactone ring.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse physiological reactions including nausea, dizziness, insomnia, headache, tachycardia, hypertension, and agitation. Individuals consuming this species may experience an increased heart rate or elevated blood pressure due to its stimulating properties. Some users might also report neurological symptoms such as persistent headaches or a sense of agitation. Sleep disturbances like insomnia are common if the plant is taken too late in the day. Gastrointestinal discomfort and lightheadedness can further complicate the experience for those sensitive to its effects.
