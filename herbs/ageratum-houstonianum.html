Ageratum houstonianum

What's the taxonomical classification of Ageratum houstonianum?

Agapanthus houstonianum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Asterales, the plant is placed in the family Asteraceae, which is a large group of flowering plants. Finally, its specific taxonomic position is defined by the genus Ageratum and the species houstonianum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ageratum houstonianum has herbaceous, perennial stems that produce opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and a slightly hairy texture. The foliage is typically a vibrant green and serves as a dense base for the flowering structures. Its inflorescence consists of dense, discoid flower heads that lack ray florets, giving them a fuzzy or button-like appearance. These clusters of tiny tubular florets are most commonly found in shades of blue, lavender, or white. The plant overall maintains a mounded growth habit with a compact, bushy structure.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of the Himalayas, specifically spanning across parts of India, Nepal, Bhutan, and southern China. It thrives primarily in montane environments where it can access the cooler temperatures and specific soil conditions found in mountain ranges. Its distribution is often concentrated in subalpine zones and rocky slopes at elevations ranging from several thousand feet up to much higher peaks. Because it relies on these specific ecological niches, the species is geographically restricted to these mountainous corridors. This localized habitat preference limits its occurrence to certain parts of the Asian continent.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ageratum houstonianum?

This plant has antioxidant, cytotoxic, insecticidal, larvicidal, and antifeedant pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, while its cytotoxic effects demonstrate potential for inhibiting various cancer cell lines. The botanical extracts function as potent insecticidal agents that can effectively control pest populations. Specifically, the plant exhibits strong larvicidal activity against mosquito larvae and other harmful insects. Furthermore, it acts as an effective antifeedant by reducing the feeding rates of targeted insect species.

As discussed in a study, Ageratum houstonianum was evaluated for its antioxidant and cytotoxic activities. The plant collected from Murlen National Park showed a TPC of 453.80 ± 0.95 µg gallic acid equivalents/mg of plant extract dry weight (µg GAE/mg) and a TFC of 120.66 ± 1.93 µg quercetin equivalents/mg of plant extract dry weight (µg GE/mg). The extract of Ageratum houstonianum from Murlen National Park exhibited an antioxidant activity against ABTS radicals with an IC50 of 241.6 µg/mL. In comparison, the Ageratum houstonianum extract from Dampa Tiger Reserve had an IC50 of 371.2 µg/mL. Analysis using UPLC-ESI MS/MS and GC/MS detected five compounds in the Ageratum houstonianum extracts.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds rosmarinic acid, chlorogenic acid, β-caryophyllene, caryophyllene oxide, and linoleic acid. Rosmarinic acid and chlorogenic acid serve as potent antioxidants that help reduce inflammation within the body. The sesquiterpene β-caryophyllene is known for its ability to interact with cannabinoid receptors to provide therapeutic effects. Additionally, caryophyllene oxide contributes to the plant's complex chemical profile and potential antimicrobial properties. Linoleic acid acts as an essential fatty acid that supports overall cellular health and skin integrity.

As discussed in a study, HPLC analysis of Ageratum houstonianum aerial parts revealed chlorogenic acid as a major phenolic acid in the leaf and rosmarinic acid as a major phenolic acid in the flower. GC-MS analysis identified phytol as the major makeup of the plant. The flower extract contained Precocene I and II, while linoleic, linolenic, and oleic acid were detected in comparable amounts across the studied organs. These extracts influenced mortality with LC50 values of 259.79 ppm for the flower, 266.85 ppm for the leaf, and 306.86 ppm for the stem after 24 h. Additionally, the flower extract achieved a repellency percentage of 89.10% at a dose of 3.60 mg/cm2 and caused a 36.60% decrease in cytochrome P-450 monooxygenase activity.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating dermatological problems, tonsillitis, pancreatitis, and hair darkening, while also acting as a control against Tribolium castaneum. Its medicinal properties are applied topically to soothe various skin conditions and infections. When consumed or prepared in specific ways, it helps alleviate inflammation in the throat and digestive organs. Additionally, certain extracts are utilized in traditional practices to promote hair darkening. The plant also shows effectiveness in managing pests like the red flour beetle in stored products.

As discussed in a study, the dermatological category showed high agreement among informants in the municipality of Tlanchinol Hidalgo, Mexico. Ageratum houstonianum was identified as one of the most important species for this specific category based on its fidelity level. This plant was selected through an ethnopharmacological field survey that utilized two different quantitative tools to analyze medicinal uses. The research involved direct interviews with people in a region surrounded by a rain forest to determine the most important species. The study suggests that Ageratum houstonianum should be included for further ethnopharmacological studies.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, nausea, vomiting, dizziness, and gastrointestinal distress upon contact or ingestion. Direct physical contact with the foliage or stems may trigger localized redness or painful rashes. Individuals with sensitive systems might experience more severe allergic dermatitis following exposure. If the plant is accidentally eaten, it can lead to nausea, vomiting, and general gastrointestinal distress. Some cases of toxicity have also been associated with feelings of dizziness in affected individuals.
