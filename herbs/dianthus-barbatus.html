Dianthus barbatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Dianthus barbatus?

Dianthus barbatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of biological classification, it falls under the order Caryophyllales and the family Caryophyllaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Dianthus, with its specific identification being the species barbatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dianthus barbaetus has erect, branched stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and are covered in fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are narrow, linear, and arranged in opposite pairs along the stem, often appearing grayish-green in color. At the apex of the stems, the plant produces clusters of fragrant, five-petaled flowers that can vary in color from deep pink to white. Each flower features a distinct calyx composed of fused sepals that protect the developing bud. The base of the plant consists of a basal rosette of foliage that provides a dense foundation for the upward growth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dianthus barbatus?

This plant has demonstrated significant analgesic and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities through various biochemical pathways. Researchers have identified that its extracts can effectively reduce pain perception and mitigate swelling in biological models. These therapeutic effects are often attributed to the presence of specific secondary metabolites like saponins and flavonoids. Such compounds work by inhibiting inflammatory mediators that trigger discomfort and tissue damage. Consequently, Dianthus barbatus serves as a promising subject for developing new natural medicinal agents.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Dianthus barbatus cv. "China Doll" yielded two saponins known as barbatoside A and barbatoside B. These two isolated saponins demonstrated both analgesic and anti-inflammatory activities. The aglycone for each of these saponins was identified as quillaic acid. The glycone of barbatoside A was composed of rhamnose, arabinose, fucose, xylose, galactose, glucose, and 1 unidentified sugar. In contrast, the glycone of barbatoside B contained arabinose, fucose, xylose, mannose, galactose, glucose, and 3 unidentified sugars. Other compounds isolated from the plant included astragalin, kaempferol-3-O-beta-D-sophoroside, D-pinitol, and L-leucine.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals due to the presence of saponins. While most people tolerate the plant without issue, direct contact with the sap may lead to redness, itching, or a mild rash. There is limited toxicological research available regarding the internal consumption of the flowers or leaves. Because of this lack of data, ingestion should be avoided to prevent potential gastrointestinal distress or unknown systemic reactions. Always exercise caution when handling the foliage if you have a history of plant allergies.
