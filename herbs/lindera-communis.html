Lindera communis

What's the taxonomical classification of Lindera communis?

Lindera communis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Laurales. The plant is further categorized into the family Lauraceae, and its specific taxonomic position is defined by the genus Lindera and the species communis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lindera communis has simple, alternate, ovate to lanceolate leaves that are typically three to seven inches long and possess entire or slightly serrated margins. The foliage is characterized by a smooth texture and a bright green color that may turn brilliant red or orange during the autumn season. Its stems are woody and can grow into a multi-stemmed shrub or a small tree reaching heights of up to fifteen feet. Small, inconspicuous yellow flowers emerge in early spring from the axils of the leaves. These flowers eventually give way to small, fleshy red drupes that serve as a food source for various bird species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southwestern region of Australia, specifically ranging from the Shark Bay area in Western Australia across to the southern coast of South Australia. It primarily inhabits various coastal and near-coastal environments where sandy soils are prevalent. The species is frequently found within heathlands and mallee shrublands that characterize these Mediterranean-style climates. Its distribution is closely linked to the presence of well-drained, nutrient-poor sandy substrates. This specific range ensures the plant thrives in the unique ecological niches of the Australian coastline.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant Lindera communis is cultivated in well-draining, acidic soils with partial shade to protect its delicate foliage from intense sunlight.

Pruning should be done sparingly during the growing season to maintain a compact shape without stressing the plant.
