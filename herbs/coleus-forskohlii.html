Scientific and Botanical Profile

Indian Coleus, with botanical name Coleus forskohlii, is a member of the Lamiaceae family, commonly known as the mint family, and is also referred to as Forskohlii Plant, Plectranthus Forskohlii, Coleus Plant, Coleus Blumei, Forskolea, Forskolin Plant, and Kushmanda.

Native to regions across South and Southeast Asia, including India, Nepal, Bhutan, Bangladesh, Myanmar, Sri Lanka, Cambodia, Laos, Vietnam, China, Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia, this plant is widely cultivated for its ornamental value and medicinal properties. Morphologically, it is a herbaceous perennial with square stems, opposite leaves that are often brightly colored in shades of red, orange, and purple, and small, tubular flowers clustered in whorls at the ends of branches.

The plant is characterized by its high concentration of forskolin, a diterpene compound responsible for many of its pharmacological effects.

History and Cultural Relevance

Indian Coleus was used for centuries in traditional Ayurvedic and Siddha medicine systems across South and Southeast Asia, where it was valued for its medicinal properties and spiritual significance.

The plant, scientifically known as Coleus forskohlii, has a long history of being employed to treat various ailments, including respiratory conditions, inflammation, and digestive disorders, due to its active compound forskolin. In many indigenous cultures, Indian Coleus was incorporated into rituals and ceremonies, often symbolizing protection, healing, and spiritual connection to the divine. Its leaves were also used in folk traditions to prepare herbal remedies for stress relief and to enhance vitality.

Today, Indian Coleus remains relevant in modern herbal medicine, with forskolin being studied for its potential in managing hypertension and improving athletic performance.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Indian Coleus contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including forskolin, a diterpene that is the primary alkaloid responsible for its medicinal properties, along with flavonoids, terpenoids, and essential oils that contribute to its therapeutic effects.

It is also rich in nutrients such as vitamins A, C, and E, as well as minerals like calcium, magnesium, and potassium, which support overall health and immune function. The plant is known for its high antioxidant content, which helps neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress in the body. Forskolin works by activating adenylate cyclase, leading to increased cyclic AMP (cAMP) levels, which can influence various physiological processes such as smooth muscle relaxation and hormone regulation.

These combined chemical and nutritional properties make Indian Coleus a valuable herb in traditional and modern herbal medicine.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Coleus forskohlii has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms such as fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare it, one can make a tea by steeping the dried leaves in hot water, or create a decoction by simmering them for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion or applied topically as an oil for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 100-200 mg of standardized extract per day, while children should only use it if deemed safe by a healthcare professional.

Due to limited scientific evidence, it is advised to use it cautiously, with a short duration and frequency, to minimize potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Coleus forskohlii can be a promising medicinal plant used for its potential therapeutic effects, particularly in supporting respiratory and cardiovascular health.

However, it is important to note that while generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, and headaches in some individuals. There are also potential interactions with certain medications, including anticoagulants and blood pressure drugs, so caution is advised when combining it with other herbs or prescription medications. Special populations, such as pregnant or breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to limited safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to follow dosage guidelines, consult with a qualified healthcare professional, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Coleus forskohlii grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, in a location that receives full sun to partial shade. It requires consistent moisture, especially during the growing season, but should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. Regular watering and mulching help retain soil moisture and suppress weeds, while periodic fertilization with a balanced nutrient mix supports healthy growth. The plant is typically harvested when the leaves are fully developed, usually in late summer to early fall, by cutting the stems just above the soil line to encourage regrowth.

For optimal potency, the harvested leaves should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to preserve their active compounds.
