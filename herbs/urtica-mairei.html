Urtica mairei

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Urtica mairei has ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and a dense covering of stinging trichomes along its stems and foliage. The plant typically exhibits a perennial growth habit with upright, herbaceous stems that can reach various heights depending on its environment. Its leaf arrangement is opposite, featuring prominent venation that extends from the central midrib to the edges. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in clusters, characteristic of the Urticaceae family. The overall structure is defined by its bristly texture which serves as a primary defensive mechanism.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the Canary Islands, specifically found on the island of Tenerife. It grows primarily within the high-altitude zones of the island's volcanic terrain. These populations are often restricted to specific microclimates such as humid ravines or laurel forests. Because of its limited range, the species is highly localized to these unique Macaronesian habitats. Its survival depends on the stable environmental conditions provided by these specific island ecosystems.

      What are the herbal preparations of Urtica mairei?

      Urtica mairei is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice to harness its medicinal properties. When used as a tea, the dried leaves are simmered in water to extract bioactive compounds for internal consumption. Infusions involve steeping the fresh plant in hot water to create a milder liquid remedy. For external applications, the crushed leaves can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to soothe irritation. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the concentration and delivery of the plant's nutrients.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Urtica mairei can cause skin irritation, localized stinging, itching, and redness through the injection of irritating chemicals upon contact. These effects occur because the plant's trichomes act like tiny needles that penetrate the skin to release bioactive substances. Once the skin is breached, the immediate sensation is often described as a sharp, burning sting. Some individuals may experience swelling or small white bumps in the affected area as an inflammatory response. While most reactions are temporary, sensitive people might encounter more prolonged discomfort or heightened dermatological sensitivity.
