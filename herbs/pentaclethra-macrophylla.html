Pentaclethra macrophylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Pentaclethra macrophylla?

Pentaclethra macrophylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized under the order Fabales. It is a representative of the family Fabaceae, situated within the genus Pentaclethra, and is specifically identified by its species name, macrophylla.

What are the therapeutic actions of Pentaclethra macrophylla?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, stomach ailments, and various inflammatory conditions. The leaves and bark are often prepared as decoctions or poultices to accelerate the healing of physical injuries. In traditional medicine, it is frequently applied to manage symptoms related to digestive issues and internal discomfort. Additionally, its antimicrobial properties help in addressing superficial dermatological problems. These applications highlight the plant's diverse role in managing both external and internal health concerns.

What are the herbal preparations of Pentaclethra macrophylla?

Pentaclethrha macrophylla is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion by boiling or soaking the leaves and roots in water to extract medicinal properties. This liquid extract is frequently used to treat various ailments such as stomach issues or skin inflammations. In some traditional practices, the plant material is also processed into a poultice for topical application on wounds. Some practitioners may also create concentrated extracts or tinctures to ensure a longer shelf life and more potent dosage. These diverse preparations allow for both internal consumption and external therapeutic use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Pentaclethra macrophylla can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if its chemical compounds are handled improperly or ingested. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the presence of secondary metabolites suggests potential reactivity with sensitive tissues. Direct contact with the sap or foliage may trigger redness or itching in individuals with hypersensitivity. Internal consumption of unprocessed parts could lead to nausea or abdominal discomfort due to unverified alkaloid concentrations. Therefore, caution is necessary when interacting with this species to avoid unexpected adverse reactions.
