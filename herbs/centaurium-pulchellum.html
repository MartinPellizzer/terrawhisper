Centaurium pulchellum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Centaurium pulchellum has an erect, branched herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 10 to 40 centimeters and features opposite, lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is generally smooth and possesses a bright green coloration that provides a backdrop for its small, four-petaled flowers. These blossoms are predominantly pink or pale rose in hue and emerge from the leaf axils. Small, yellowish centers distinguish the floral structure, while the plant often produces small, capsule-like fruits upon maturity. The root system consists of a slender taproot that supports the upright growth habit of the species.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Centaurium pulchellum contains secoiridoid glycosides, specifically xanthoside, along with various alkaloids and phenolic compounds. These chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's bitter taste and its potential pharmacological activity. The presence of these glycosides suggests the plant may possess anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Researchers often study these specific secondary metabolites to understand their impact on digestive health and metabolic processes. Such biochemical profiles are characteristic of the Gentianaceae family, which is known for producing potent medicinal substances.

Centaurium pulchellum targets secoiridoid glycosides such as gentiopicroside and xanthone derivatives to modulate inflammatory pathways and gastrointestinal enzyme activities. These bioactive secondary metabolites interact with specific signaling proteins to inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines and suppress cyclooxygenase enzymes. Centaurium pulchellum has therapeutic properties that function as a bitter stomachic, an aperient, and a febrifuge to support digestive and systemic health.
