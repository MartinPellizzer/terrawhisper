What to know about Capsicum frutescens (cayenne) before using it medicinally

Cayenne, scientifically known as Capsicum frutescens, is a spice that has been touted for its numerous health benefits, boasting anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties that have been shown to alleviate pain, improve circulation, and boost the immune system.

In addition to its medicinal uses, cayenne is also a staple in many cuisines around the world, adding a spicy kick to dishes like stir-fries, soups, and sauces. As an ornamental plant, cayenne is often grown for its vibrant red flowers and attractive foliage, making it a popular choice for gardens and indoor containers. Botanically speaking, cayenne belongs to the Solanaceae family, along with other peppers and tomatoes, and is characterized by its thick, waxy leaves and pungent flavor.

With ancient roots tracing back to Central and South America, where it was used by indigenous cultures as a symbol of fertility and prosperity, cayenne has a rich history that spans thousands of years, earning it a revered place in the culinary and cultural traditions of many nations around the world.

What are the side effects of Capsicum frutescens when used improperly?

Furthermore, improper preparation methods or dosages can cause the capsaicin contained within Capsicum frutescens to irritate the mucous membranes, resulting in symptoms such as sore throat, hoarseness, and even respiratory issues like bronchitis. In extreme cases, high doses of capsaicin have been linked to an increased heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac arrhythmias, particularly in individuals with pre-existing cardiovascular conditions.

Additionally, the capsaicin present in Capsicum frutescens can also interact with certain medications, such as antacids, blood thinners, and diabetes medications, which may exacerbate existing health issues or lead to adverse reactions.
