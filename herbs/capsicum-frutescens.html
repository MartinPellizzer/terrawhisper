What to know about Capsicum frutescens (cayenne) before using it medicinally

Capsicum frutescens, commonly known as cayenne, is a herb rich in health benefits, including anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties that help to reduce pain, improve digestion, and boost the immune system.

Grown as a perennial or annual in tropical climates, cayenne is a versatile herb that thrives in well-drained soil and can be cultivated in containers or directly in the ground. As a member of the nightshade family, Capsicum frutescens contains high concentrations of capsaicinoids, alkaloids responsible for its distinctive flavor and aroma.

With origins dating back to the indigenous cultures of Central and South America, cayenne has been used for centuries in traditional medicine and as a food spice, earning its place as one of the most recognizable and widely used herbs globally.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Capsicum frutescens.

What are the medicinal properties of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens helps with pain relief, inflammation, and digestive issues due to its bioactive compounds. It has anti-inflammatory properties, reducing swelling and pain in joints and muscles. It also aids in digestion, alleviating symptoms of irritable bowel syndrome.

Capsicum frutescens contains capsaicin, a polyisoprene-derived chemical compound responsible for its medicinal properties. Capsaicin is a potent analgesic and anti-inflammatory agent, capable of inhibiting pain and inflammation by blocking the production of prostaglandins and other inflammatory mediators.

The most used parts of Capsicum frutescens for medicinal purposes are the dried and powdered seeds, as well as the fruit's pulp and oil. These parts are rich in capsaicin and other bioactive compounds, making them effective for relieving pain and inflammation.

Improper use of Capsicum frutescens can lead to side effects such as skin and mucous membrane irritation, heartburn, and gastrointestinal upset. Prolonged use can also cause an increased heart rate, sweating, and flushing.

When using Capsicum frutescens medicinally, individuals should exercise caution, especially those with sensitive skin or allergies to spicy foods. They should also consult a healthcare professional before using it, especially if they are taking medications or have underlying health conditions.

What are the horticulural aspects of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens grow best in full sun to partial shade and well-draining soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. It requires a warm and humid climate, tolerating temperatures up to 90°F (32°C). Soil fertility is crucial, with high potassium levels.

Planting tips for Capsicum frutescens include sowing seeds ¼ inch deep and 1-2 inches apart in late spring to early summer. Transplant seedlings 12-18 inches apart when 6-8 inches tall. Water consistently, but avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot. Mulch around the plants to retain moisture.

Harvest Capsicum frutescens 70-80 days after sowing, when the peppers reach their full color and are firm. Cut the fruit from the plant, leaving a small stem attached. Avoid pulling the peppers, which can damage the plant. Harvest in the morning, when the peppers are at their highest moisture content.

Pests and diseases that commonly affect Capsicum frutescens include aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites. Root rot, caused by overwatering, is also a common issue. Fungal diseases like powdery mildew and leaf spot can occur when the plant is under stress. Regularly inspect the plant for signs of pest or disease infestation.

What are the botanical characteristics of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens, a species of pepper plant, is characterized by its bushy or sprawling habit, growing up to 1-2 meters in height and width.

The stems are erect to spreading, often woody at the base, with a smooth, glossy surface and a reddish-brown color. Leaves are elliptical to oval-shaped, 5-10 cm long, and have a pointed tip, with three to five lobes on each side of the midrib.

They are dark green in color with lighter veins, and possess small hairs along the edges and underside. The flowers, which appear in clusters at the leaf axils, are white or yellowish-white, tubular in shape, and around 1-2 cm long. Fruit is a capsule-like berry, typically conical or ellipsoidal in shape, with four chambers and numerous seeds.

It can range in color from green to yellow, orange, red, brown, or even purple, depending on the maturity stage, and may have a glossy or matte surface.

What is the taxonomy of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens belongs to the kingdom Plantae, class Magnoliopsida, order Solanales, family Solanaceae, and genus Capsicum.

Specifically, it is a species of pepper that is closely related to other varieties of hot peppers, such as cayenne and tabasco.

As a member of the nightshade family (Solanaceae), Capsicum frutescens shares characteristics with other plants like tomatoes, potatoes, and eggplants.

What are the variants of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens has variants, such as C. f. conoides, which is commonly known as Malagueta pepper, and C. f. longum, also known as Piri-piri or African bird's eye chili.

Another variant is C. f. rubrum, a sweet pepper with a reddish-orange color, while C. f. typicum is the typical form of this species, characterized by its small size and bright red color.

These variants can be found in different regions around the world, each with their unique flavor profiles and uses in cuisine.

What is the geographic distribution of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens, also known as malagueta pepper, has a widespread geographic distribution across various tropical and subtropical regions.

It is native to Central and South America, specifically in countries such as Brazil, Costa Rica, Ecuador, French Guiana, Guyana, Suriname, Trinidad and Tobago, and Venezuela. Additionally, it can be found in parts of the Caribbean, including Jamaica, Haiti, and Dominican Republic.

This pepper species has also been naturalized in various other regions, including Africa, Asia, and Oceania, often introduced by human activity or trade.

What is the life cycle of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens, also known as Malagueta pepper, is an annual plant that completes its life cycle within a year.

The process begins with seed germination, which typically occurs within 7-14 days after sowing. The seedling emerges and develops into a small plant, eventually producing its first set of true leaves.

As the plant matures, it produces flowers and fruit, with each pepper taking around 60-90 days to fully ripen. After harvesting the peppers, the plant will continue to produce new flowers and fruits until it reaches maturity, typically around 120-150 days after sowing.

At this point, the plant will begin to decline and eventually die off, marking the end of its life cycle.

What are the historical uses of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens, also known as malabar pepper or bird's eye chili, has been used for centuries in various cultures.

In traditional medicine, it was employed to treat a range of ailments, including fever, rheumatism, and respiratory issues.

Its peppers were also used as a natural dye, insect repellent, and food seasoning, adding flavor and heat to dishes.

What are the mythological referencec of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens, a type of hot pepper, has mythological references in various cultures.

In Mexican mythology, it is associated with the god Xochipilli, who is said to have created the pepper as a symbol of love and fertility.

Similarly, in African and Afro-Caribbean traditions, Capsicum frutescens is linked to the Orisha Yemaya, goddess of fertility and childbirth, who is often depicted holding a burning hot pepper.

What are the historical texts of Capsicum frutescens?

Capsicum frutescens, also known as tabasco pepper, has a rich history of use in traditional medicine and cuisine.

Historical texts dating back to the 16th century describe its use by indigenous peoples in Mexico and Central America to treat various ailments, including arthritis and fever. The Aztecs and Mayans believed it had spiritual powers and used it in rituals.

Early European explorers also documented its use as a condiment and remedy.
