Dendrobium aduncum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dendrobium aduncum has pendulous, cane-like pseudobulbs that are distinctly curved or hooked at the apex, ranging from cylindrical to slightly compressed in shape. These elongated stems are typically several decimeters long and possess a somewhat wrinkled or ridged surface texture. The leaves are arranged distichously along the upper portion of the pseudobulb, appearing leathery, lanceolate, and often somewhat fleshy. At the terminal ends of the canes, the plant produces inflorescences that bear small, fragrant flowers with a characteristic lip structure. The overall growth habit is epiphytic, allowing the plant to hang from branches in its natural forest habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically distributed across the Malay Peninsula, Sumatra, Borneo, and the Philippines. It thrives primarily in humid, lowland rainforest environments where moisture levels remain consistently high. Within these habitats, it is commonly found growing as an epiphyte on the branches of trees. The species occupies a wide range of elevations but is most frequently documented in low-to-mid altitude forest canopies. Its presence is often tied to specific microclimates that provide dappled sunlight and adequate air circulation.
