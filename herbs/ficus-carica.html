Ficus carica

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus carica?

Ficus carica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is organized under the order Rosales and placed in the family Moraceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by the genus Ficus and is specifically designated by the species name carica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus carica has large, deeply lobed leaves with three to five distinct lobes and a rough, sandpaper-like texture on the upper surface. The plant grows as a deciduous shrub or small tree characterized by a spreading canopy and grayish, fissured bark. Its reproductive structures are unique syconia, which are fleshy, hollow receptacles containing numerous tiny flowers inside. The milky white latex flows from the stems and leaves whenever the plant tissue is damaged. This species typically develops a robust root system that can expand extensively in search of moisture.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, encompassing parts of Southern Europe, North Africa, and Western Asia. It thrives in various climates ranging from subtropical to temperate zones where well-drained soil is available. Due to human cultivation, it has been introduced to many other parts of the world, including the Americas and parts of Australia. The species can be found growing in diverse habitats such as rocky hillsides, coastal areas, and cultivated orchards. Its ability to adapt to different environments has allowed it to spread far beyond its original geographical boundaries.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ficus carica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its ripe fruit, latex, and leaves. The edible fruit is frequently used to treat constipation and digestive irregularities due to its high fiber content. The milky white latex extracted from the stems and leaves is applied topically to manage skin conditions like warts or fungal infections. Additionally, leaf extracts are often studied for their potential to help regulate blood sugar levels in diabetic patients. These various components provide a diverse range of therapeutic applications in traditional medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ficus carica?

Ficus carica has laxative, emollients, mucilaginous, demulcent, and astringent properties that support digestive and integumentary health. The plant contains high levels of fiber and specific enzymes that act as a natural laxative to stimulate bowel movements. Its mucilaginous components function as a demulcent, coating irritated mucous membranes to provide soothing relief. Additionally, the plant serves as an emollient to soften skin and tissues through its hydrating qualities. The presence of tannins also provides an astringent effect that helps to tone and tighten various bodily tissues.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, phenolic acids, catechin, kaempferol, and rutin. These bioactive molecules contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of phenolic acids helps protect cells from oxidative stress caused by free radicals. Furthermore, specific flavonoids like kaempferol and rutin provide additional therapeutic benefits through their unique chemical structures. Catechin also plays a vital role in supporting overall metabolic health within the body.

As discussed in a study, the aqueous leaf extract of Ficus carica contains flavonoids, cardiac glycosides, anthocyanosides, saponin, and reducing sugar. The dry aqueous leaf extract of this plant has a dark brown colour and a pH ranging from 6.1 to 7.2. During the investigation, doses of 1, 2, 4, and 8 g/kg body weight were administered to the FCG group. The LD50 determination results for Ficus carica using Thompson and Finney methods were 3.36 +/- 0.26 and 4.00 +/- 0.04 g/kg body weight.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating constipation, diarrhea, gastric ulcers, peptic ulcer disease, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. The high fiber content found in the fruit serves as an effective natural remedy to regulate bowel movements and alleviate constipation. Conversely, its astringent properties can be utilized to manage cases of diarrhea. Compounds within the plant also demonstrate potential in healing gastric ulcers and general peptic ulcer disease by protecting the stomach lining. What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a medicinal infusion of the leaves, a concentrated tincture of the fruit, a potent extract, a fine powder of the dried parts, a topical latex, or a soothing syrup. An infusion made from the dried leaves is often used to help manage blood pressure and blood sugar levels. The milky white latex tapped from the stems is primarily applied externally to treat warts and skin lesions. For easier consumption, the dried fruit or leaves can be processed into a fine powder to be added to foods. Herbalists also create a syrup using the fruit to soothe coughs or improve digestion. Finally, an alcohol-based tincture provides a shelf-stable way to administer the plant's bioactive compounds.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause diarrhea, skin irritation, nausea, abdominal cramps, and photosensitivity in some individuals. The milky sap found within the stems and leaves is known to trigger contact dermatitis or painful skin irritation upon contact with the flesh. If the sap is applied to the skin before sun exposure, it may lead to photosensitivity and severe blistering. Ingesting large amounts or consuming unripe fruit can also result in gastrointestinal distress such as nausea and abdominal cramps. These digestive issues often manifest as diarrhea following consumption.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Diarrhea
  • Skin irritation
  • Nausea
  • Abdominal cramps
