Cornus alba

What's the taxonomical classification of Cornus alba?

Cornus alba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Cornales and is a member of the family Cornaceae. More specifically, it is categorized under the genus Cornus, with the specific epithet alba identifying its unique species.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cornus alba has opposite, ovate leaves with prominent veins and bright red stems that become more vibrant in winter. The foliage typically reaches a height of six to nine feet and develops a dense, shrubby habit. During the spring, small, white, four-petaled flowers appear in flat clusters atop the branches. The plant eventually produces small, dark blue berries that provide visual interest against the colorful bark. As the seasons change, the woody stems transition from green to intense shades of crimson or orange.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across regions of China, Japan, and Korea. It grows naturally in diverse habitats ranging from temperate forests to moist riverbanks and marshy areas. While its primary origin is concentrated in these eastern territories, it has been widely introduced to other parts of the world for ornamental use. Many gardeners in North America and Europe cultivate it due to its hardiness and attractive winter stems. Its ability to adapt to various climates allows it to thrive in many temperate zones globally.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires full sun to partial shade and well-draining, moist, acidic to neutral soil to thrive.

You should plant it in a location where it will not face extreme drought or waterlogged conditions. To maintain its vibrant red winter stems, prune the shrub heavily every year during the late winter or early spring. Regular mulching helps retain essential soil moisture and suppresses competing weeds around the base.

Once established, this hardy shrub is relatively low-maintenance and performs well in most temperate climates.
