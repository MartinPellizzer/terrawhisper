Cardamom

Elettaria cardamomum

Cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum) is a spice and medicinal plant native to the tropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, known for its aromatic seeds and various therapeutic properties.

Botanical Identification

Cardamom, scientifically known as Elettaria cardamomum, is a member of the ginger family, Zingiberaceae. Native to the wet tropics of India, it is a perennial herb with aromatic leaves and small white flowers. Commonly referred to as "kari" in India and "badian" in Indonesia, it has been widely used in herbal medicine for its digestive and aromatic properties.

The following list summarize the taxonomy of plant:

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Liliopsida
  • Order: Zingiberales
  • Family: Zingiberaceae
  • Genus: Elettaria
  • Species: Elettaria cardamomum

Active Compounds

Cardamom contains essential oils, terpenoids, and flavonoids as its primary bioactive compounds. These compounds work synergistically to exert anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. The essential oils, in particular, contribute to its aromatic and therapeutic properties.

  • Essential oil
  • Terpenoid
  • Flavonoid

Cardamom Uses.

Preparation Methods

Culinary use: Used to flavor food and beverages, aiding digestion and providing a soothing effect.

Essential oil: Used for respiratory issues and to relieve stress and anxiety.

Powder: Used to treat digestive issues and as a breath freshener.

Safety Profile

Cardamom is generally safe when used in typical culinary amounts. However, high doses may cause gastrointestinal discomfort. It should be avoided by individuals with diabetes or during pregnancy due to potential adverse effects.
