Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) is a hardy, coastal shrub native to eastern Asia that is widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties and abundant floral and fruit components.

What is Rugosa Rose (Rosa Rugosa)?

Rugosa Rose, or Rosa rugosa, is a shrub native to eastern Asia that is widely used in herbal medicine for its flowers, leaves, and fruits, which contain compounds with antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Rugosa Rose is Rosa rugosa. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rosales, family Rosaceae, genus Rosa, and species Rosa rugosa. It is commonly known as Rugosa Rose in North America and Hōzō in Japan. It is also known as Coastal Rose in the United States and Sea Rose in the United Kingdom. Synonyms for this plant include Rosa rugosa subsp. rugosa, Rosa rugosa var. rugosa, and Rosa rugosa var. alpina.

Plant Description and Morphology

Rugosa Rose is a deciduous shrub that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters with a spreading, multi-stemmed structure.

Its leaves are elliptic to ovate, with serrated edges and a rough, hairy texture on the underside. The flowers are large, fragrant, and typically pink to red, with five to seven petals and a hairy, yellowish center. It has a robust, fibrous root system that spreads widely and is highly adaptive to various soil types.

The plant produces a pungent, slightly bitter aroma, particularly in its leaves and stems.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) is native to the coastal regions of eastern Asia, particularly China, Korea, and Japan, where it thrives in sandy or salt-laden soils along shorelines and dunes.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, flowers, and fruits of Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) are used medicinally, with the roots and fruits being the most commonly utilized parts for their therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) contains a variety of bioactive compounds including flavonoids, triterpenoids, phenolic acids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic applications.

Pharmacological Properties

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties including antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antitumor activities, supported by both traditional use and modern scientific research.

Mechanisms of Action

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, and essential oils, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties by interacting with cellular pathways involved in oxidative stress, immune response, and microbial growth.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to support digestive health, alleviate symptoms of menopause, and address skin conditions due to its high content of antioxidants, flavonoids, and essential oils.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) is typically prepared as a dried herb, used in tinctures, teas, and topical salves, with the flowers, leaves, and stems harvested at specific times to maximize potency and therapeutic effects.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) for herbal use is 2-4 grams of dried flowers per day, usually prepared as a tea by steeping in hot water for 10-15 minutes, though specific formulations may vary based on intended therapeutic purpose.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions in sensitive individuals, and should be avoided by those with known allergies to plants in the Rosaceae family.

Drug Interactions

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) may interact with medications that affect the liver or kidneys, as its compounds can influence metabolic processes and may potentiate or reduce the effects of certain drugs when taken together.

Toxicity and Precautions

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and excessive consumption could lead to allergic reactions in those sensitive to plants in the Rosaceae family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, and is harvested primarily during late summer to early autumn when the flowers are fully open and mature.

Processing and Extraction

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) is processed and extracted through methods such as steam distillation, cold pressing, or maceration to obtain its essential oil, which contains compounds like geraniol, citral, and other volatile aromatic substances.

Storage and Shelf Life

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a shelf life of up to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) has shown promising evidence for its potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, with studies suggesting its use in supporting digestive health and immune function.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Rugosa Rose (Rosa rugosa) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, and astringent properties, with applications ranging from treating skin conditions and digestive issues to supporting respiratory health.

Regulatory Status
