Osmanthus armatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Osmanthus armatus?

Osmanthus armatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Oleaceae, which places it within the genus Osmanthus, ultimately identifying it as the specific species Osmanthus armatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Osmanthus armatus has evergreen, leathery leaves that are characterized by sharp, prickly teeth along the margins and an elliptical shape. The foliage typically displays a dark green color on the upper surface while being slightly paler underneath. Small, tubular white flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often producing a highly fragrant scent during the blooming season. These blossoms are typically accompanied by small, fleshy drupes that transition from green to a dark color as they ripen. The overall structure of the shrub is dense, providing a compact and textured appearance to the plant.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the potent antimalarial compound artemisinic acid and its derivative artemisia artemisinin, which are crucial for treating malaria infections. Among its most significant medicinal components is artemisitide, a compound studied for its specific biological activities. These chemical constituents work by generating free radicals that target parasites within the bloodstream. Beyond its antimalarial properties, the plant is researched for its potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Scientists continue to isolate these various compounds to improve the efficacy of modern pharmaceutical treatments.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract of the heartwoods of Osmanthus armatus yielded a new ester called armatuside (1). Along with this new ester, 13 known compounds (2-14) were obtained from the plant. The structures for all of these compounds were deduced using 1D and 2D NMR spectroscopic methods. Additionally, compounds 2-14 were isolated from the plant for the first time.
