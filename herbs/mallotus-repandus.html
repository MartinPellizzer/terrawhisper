Mallotus repandus

What's the taxonomical classification of Mallotus repandus?

Mallotus repandus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Malpighiales. The plant is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae, falling under the genus Mallotus, and is specifically identified by its species name, repandus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mallotus repandus has prostrate or climbing stems with simple or branched leaves that are typically ovate to elliptic in shape and possess entire or slightly wavy margins. The leaves are characterized by an acute or acuminate apex and a cuneate base, often displaying a somewhat leathery texture. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary clusters, leading to the development of small, globose capsules. These capsules eventually release tiny seeds that are characteristic of the Euphorbiaceae family. The overall growth habit allows the plant to spread across the forest floor or climb over surrounding vegetation.

What are the pharmacological activities of Mallotus repandus?

This plant has antibacterial, antihepatotoxic, hydroxyl radical scavenging, superoxide radical scavenging, and pain relief pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate a strong ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria. The extract also provides significant protection against liver damage through its antihepatotoxic properties. Furthermore, it functions as a potent antioxidant by utilizing its hydroxyl radical scavenging activity and superoxide radical scavenging activity to neutralize harmful oxidative species. Finally, the plant serves as an effective agent for pain relief in biological models.

According to a study published by "TheScientificWorldJournal", Mallotus repandus (Willd.) Müll.Arg. seeds exhibited prominent antibacterial effects. The ethyl acetate fraction of the plant exhibited the highest efficacy, producing a 29.00 mm inhibition zone against S. typhi. This specific fraction was most significant, showing the lowest MIC of 0.25 mg/mL and the lowest MBC of 0.53 mg/mL against S. typhi. The study suggested that the ethyl acetate extract of M. repandus seeds could serve as a useful source to inhibit Gram-negative bacteria.

The study suggests that the ethyl acetate extract of these seeds could serve as a useful source to inhibit Gram-negative bacteria.
