Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Olibanum is Boswellia frereana, a species within the Burseraceae family. It was first described by the French botanist Jean-Baptiste Joseph Pierre Frere in the 19th century. This name is widely accepted in modern botanical taxonomy and is used to distinguish it from other frankincense species.

Morphological Characteristics

Olibanum, or Boswellia frereana, is a small to medium-sized tree with a compact growth habit. It has a fibrous root system adapted to arid conditions. The stem is straight and branching, with a rough, fissured bark. Leaves are alternate, trifoliate, and have a leathery texture. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small, greenish-white flowers. The fruit is a capsule that releases small, winged seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Olibanum (Boswellia frereana) can be distinguished by its pale yellow resin, which hardens into a brittle, flake-like substance. It has small, dark green leaves with a slightly hairy underside. The plant typically grows in dry, rocky areas and is native to regions like Somalia and Ethiopia. It is often confused with Boswellia sacra, but its resin has a more distinct, sharper aroma.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Olibanum, or Boswellia frereana, is native to regions of eastern Africa, including Somalia, Ethiopia, and Eritrea. It thrives in arid and semi-arid climates with well-drained, sandy or gravelly soils. This species typically grows at elevations between 300 and 1,500 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Olibanum (Boswellia frereana) blooms from October to December, producing small yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs from January to March. Harvesting of the resin typically takes place between March and June, depending on the region and climate conditions.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Olibanum, or Boswellia frereana, is closely related to other frankincense resins within the Burseraceae family. It shares genetic and morphological traits with species like Boswellia sacra and Boswellia carterii. These relationships highlight its role in the broader genus and family classification, aiding in accurate identification and understanding of its medicinal properties.
