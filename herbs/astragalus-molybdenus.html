What is Astragalus molybdenus?

Astragalus molybdenus is a perennial legume native to China and Mongolia, belonging to the family Fabaceae.

It grows up to 50 cm tall, with a branching stem and a leafy foliage consisting of 2-5 leaflets. The plant produces small, yellow flowers in a raceme, which are followed by pods containing 2-4 seeds. Astragalus molybdenus is characterized by its taproot system and its ability to fix nitrogen from the air through symbiotic bacteria in its root nodules.

The plant's growth occurs from May to August in the northern hemisphere.

What are the main herbal preparations this herb?

The main herbal preparations of Astragalus molybdenus are astragalus tea, astragalus tincture, astragalus infusion, astragalus decoction, and astragalus extract.

Astragalus tea is made by steeping dried Astragalus molybdenus roots in hot water, typically used for its anti-inflammatory and immune-boosting properties. Astragalus tincture is a concentrated liquid extract of the roots, often used in traditional Chinese medicine to tonify the qi and prevent illness. Astragalus infusion is similar to tea but is typically made with a larger quantity of herbs and left to steep for a longer period, yielding a more potent brew.

Astragalus decoction is a method of extracting the roots' active compounds by boiling them in water, and astragalus extract is a highly concentrated powder or liquid obtained through a solvent-based extraction process, often used in modern supplements.

What are the possible side effects of using this herb improperly?

The possible side effects of using Astragalus molybdenus are numerous and can be attributed to its bioactive compounds and complex interactions with the body.

Astragalus molybdenus may interact with blood thinners, such as warfarin, and increase the risk of bleeding, a condition known as hemorrhage, due to its ability to inhibit platelet aggregation and enhance the effect of anticoagulant medications. Prolonged consumption of Astragalus molybdenus has also been linked to nephrotoxicity, a condition in which the kidneys are damaged, as its glycosides and saponins can cause oxidative stress and renal injury. Furthermore, Astragalus molybdenus may induce liver damage, particularly in individuals with pre-existing liver conditions, due to its hepatotoxic compounds and the ability to disrupt normal liver function.

Additionally, Astragalus molybdenus can trigger allergic reactions, including skin rashes, itching, and anaphylaxis, in sensitive individuals, highlighting the need for caution and proper dosing guidelines.
