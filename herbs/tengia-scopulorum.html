Tengia scopulorum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tengia scopulorum has a slender, erect stem that typically reaches heights of thirty to fifty centimeters and is characterized by a finely textured, woody surface. The leaves are arranged in an alternate pattern along the stem, displaying a narrow, linear shape with smooth margins. Each leaf tapers to a fine point and possesses a deep green coloration that provides a stark contrast to the stem. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils in clusters during the peak growing season. The root system consists of a shallow but spreading network designed to anchor the plant in loose, sandy soils.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and semi-arid regions of the southwestern United States and northern Mexico. Its range extends primarily through the desert landscapes of Arizona, New Mexico, and parts of Texas. It is commonly found in rocky soils and sandy washes where drainage is sufficient for its root systems. Populations are often scattered across diverse elevations within these subtropical zones.       What are the herbal preparations of Tengia scopulorum?

      Tengia scopulorum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the intended therapeutic use. The dried stems and leaves are most commonly boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid for internal consumption. For milder applications, a hot infusion is made by steeping the plant material in water without prolonged boiling. The crushed fresh plant material can also be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat localized inflammation. These various forms allow the active compounds to be delivered effectively through different medicinal routes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Tengia scopulorum can cause severe skin irritation, localized swelling, and intense itching upon direct contact with the flesh. These dermatological reactions often manifest as red, inflamed patches that may blister if the sap remains on the skin for an extended period. If the plant is accidentally ingested, it may trigger gastrointestinal distress including nausea, cramping, and vomiting. Some individuals might also experience an allergic reaction characterized by respiratory discomfort or hives. Constant exposure to the plant's toxins can lead to long-term sensitivity in the affected areas.
