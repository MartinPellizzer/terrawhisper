Tylophora koi

What's the taxonomical classification of Tylophora koi?

Tylophora koi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Gentianales and belongs to the family Apocynaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Tylophora, with its unique designation being the species koi.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, wounds, swellings, and respiratory issues. In traditional medicinal practices, it is often applied topically to soothe inflammatory conditions and promote healing of the skin. It is also utilized in decoctions to address internal issues such as digestive discomfort or fever. Some practitioners use specific preparations of the plant to alleviate symptoms related to coughs and congestion. The medicinal properties are primarily derived from its unique chemical constituents which possess bioactive effects.

What are the herbal preparations of Tylophora koi?

Tylophora koi is prepared as a decoction, a topical paste, or an infusion to utilize its bioactive compounds. The dried leaves and stems are most commonly boiled in water to create a concentrated medicinal tea. For external applications, the plant material is often crushed into a fine powder and mixed with a carrier oil or water to form a soothing paste. Some traditional methods involve steeping the aerial parts in alcohol to produce a tincture for long-term storage. These various preparations allow for both internal consumption and localized skin treatment.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Tylophora koi can cause adverse reactions such as skin irritation, digestive upset, and potential toxicity if ingested in uncontrolled amounts. Some individuals may experience localized dermatitis or allergic rashes upon direct contact with the plant's sap. Internal consumption might lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain due to its complex chemical composition. Because specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the long-term physiological impacts remain largely unknown. It is essential to exercise extreme caution and avoid self-medicating with this species.
