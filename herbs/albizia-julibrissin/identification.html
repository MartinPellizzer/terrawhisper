Botanical Identification of Chinese Silk Tree (Albizia julibrissin)

To identify Chinese Silk Tree (Albizia julibrissin) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

The Chinese Silk Tree, or Albizia julibrissin, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Albizia, and species Albizia julibrissin. This classification places it within the legume family, reflecting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the Chinese Silk Tree is Albizia julibrissin, first described by the French botanist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck in 1785. It is also known by several synonyms, including Robinia pseudacacia and Baptisia juncea, though these are now considered outdated. Common names such as silk tree, Chinese silk tree, and crown flower reflect its ornamental appearance and cultural significance.

Morphological Characteristics

The Chinese Silk Tree is a deciduous shrub or small tree with a shallow, fibrous root system. It has a slender, often multi-branched stem. Its bipinnate leaves are light green, with 30-60 pairs of leaflets. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing showy, pink to crimson flowers. The fruit is a flat, dehiscent pod containing elongated, brown seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

The Chinese Silk Tree can be distinguished by its compound, pinnate leaves with 10-20 pairs of oval leaflets, its fluffy, pinkish-white flower clusters, and its flat, dark brown seed pods. It is often confused with Albizia chinensis, but it has more rounded leaflets and longer, more delicate flowers.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

The Chinese Silk Tree is native to China, Korea, and Japan. It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters and warm summers. It prefers well-drained soil and can grow at altitudes between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Chinese Silk Tree blooms in late spring to early summer, typically between May and June. Seed pods mature from late summer to early autumn, around August to September. For identification purposes, seeds are best harvested when the pods turn brown and begin to split open.

Phylogenetic Relationships

The Chinese Silk Tree, Albizia julibrissin, is closely related to species within the genus Albizia and the family Fabaceae. It shares key traits with other legumes, such as compound leaves and pod fruits, but is distinguished by its feathery leaves and fluffy seed pods. Its phylogenetic placement highlights its evolutionary ties to other leguminous plants.
