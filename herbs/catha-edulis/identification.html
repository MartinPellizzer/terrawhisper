Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Khat is Catha edulis, first described by the botanist John Sims in 1794. It belongs to the family Celastraceae and is also known by various synonyms such as Catha sanguinea and Catha arabica. Common names include khat, qat, and chat, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Khat (Catha edulis) is an evergreen shrub or small tree with a compact growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly. The stem is straight and cylindrical, with a rough, bark-like surface. Leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptical, and have a leathery texture with prominent veins. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small, greenish-white flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing small, white, winged seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Khat (Catha edulis) can be distinguished by its unique leaf shape, with elliptical leaves that have a prominent midrib and a slightly serrated margin. The plant also has a distinctive red or purple stem and produces small, yellowish flowers. These features help differentiate it from other Catha species and similar-looking plants.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Khat (Catha edulis) is native to East Africa, particularly Somalia, Ethiopia, and Kenya, and parts of the Arabian Peninsula. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates with high humidity. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils and grows at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Khat (Catha edulis) blooms during the rainy season, typically between April and July, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn, around August to October. Harvest timing for identification purposes is usually when the leaves are mature and the plant is in its vegetative stage, typically between March and June.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Khat (Catha edulis) is closely related to species within the same genus, Catha. It shares characteristics with other plants in the Cucurbitaceae family, such as leaf structure and growth habits. Its phylogenetic position reflects its evolutionary ties to these related species.
