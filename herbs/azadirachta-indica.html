Azadirachta indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Azadirachta indica?

Azadirachta indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Sapindales and the family Meliaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Azadirachta, and its species name, indica.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Azadirachta indica contains a diverse range of bioactive limonoids, including azadirachtin, nimbin, salannin, and gedunin, which provide its primary therapeutic properties. These complex triterpenoids are responsible for the plant's well-documented insecticidal, antifungal, and antibacterial activities. Additionally, the plant is rich in various polyphenols, flavonoids, and glycosides that contribute to its potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. These chemical constituents work synergistically to support skin health and immune function in traditional medicinal applications. The presence of these specific alkaloids and terpenoids makes the neem tree a significant resource in pharmacological research.

What are the therapeutic actions of Azadirachta indica?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections such as eczema, psoriasis, and acne, as well as various parasitic ailments like intestinal worms and scabies. Its antimicrobial properties allow it to combat fungal, bacterial, and viral infections throughout the body. Many traditional practitioners also utilize its extracts to manage inflammatory conditions and promote wound healing. Additionally, it is frequently employed to alleviate symptoms of malaria and other fever-inducing diseases. Through these diverse applications, the plant serves as a versatile medicinal resource in many cultures.

What are the herbal preparations of Azadirachta indica?

Azadirachta indica is prepared as oils, powders, decoctions, pastes, leaf extracts, and infused teas for various medicinal uses. The seeds are most commonly pressed to produce neem oil, which serves as a potent base for topical treatments and skin ointments. Leaf powders and crushed pastes are frequently applied directly to the skin to treat fungal infections or inflammatory conditions. Aqueous extracts and decoctions made from the bark or leaves are often consumed to support internal detoxification and blood purification. These diverse forms allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be utilized for both systemic wellness and localized dermatological care.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Azadirachta indica can cause skin irritation, stomach upset, nausea, dizziness, and potential reproductive toxicity if consumed in high doses. Topical application of neem oil or extracts may lead to contact dermatitis or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. When ingested orally, excessive amounts can result in vomiting, diarrhea, or even more severe symptoms like seizures. There is also significant concern regarding its potential to interfere with fertility and fetal development in humans. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should strictly avoid consuming parts of this plant due to these risks.
