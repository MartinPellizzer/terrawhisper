Clerodendrum bracteatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Clerodendrum bracteatum?

Clerodendrum bracteatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Lamiales. This species is a member of the family Lamiaceae, falling under the genus Clerodendrum, with the specific designation of species bracteatum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clerodendrum bracteatum has large, spreading, ovate leaves with entire margins and prominent venation, alongside showy, bright red, petal-like bracts that surround small, tubular, white flowers.

These conspicuous bracts are arranged in terminal clusters and serve to attract pollinators to the plant. The true flowers emerge from the center of these colorful structures, typically exhibiting a long corolla tube. The plant develops into a woody shrub characterized by an upright growth habit and branching stems.

Its overall morphology is defined by the striking contrast between the dark green foliage and the vibrant floral displays.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Clerodendrum bracteatum targets cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. The bioactive compounds within the plant, such as flavonoids and terpenoids, inhibit these specific enzymes to reduce systemic inflammation. By suppressing nitric oxide synthase, the plant helps regulate oxidative stress and prevents tissue damage in inflammatory models. These interactions also extend to the modulation of signaling pathways that control the production of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6. Consequently, these molecular interactions provide the pharmacological basis for the plant's traditional use in treating pain and swelling.

      What are the herbal preparations of Clerodendrum bracteatum?

      Clerodendrum bracteatum is prepared as decoctions, infusions, poultices, and topical pastes to utilize its medicinal properties. The leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a tea used for internal consumption. For external ailments, the crushed fresh leaves are applied directly to the skin as a cooling poultice. Some traditional practices involve using the plant extracts in liquid washes for dermatological issues. These diverse preparation methods allow for the effective delivery of the plant's bioactive compounds.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Clerodendrum bracteatum can cause potential allergic reactions such as skin rashes, itching, or localized swelling if the plant comes into direct contact with sensitive tissue. While scientific studies on human toxicity are limited, consuming unknown plant parts may lead to gastrointestinal distress including nausea or stomach cramps. Individuals with known sensitivities to the Verbenaceae family should exercise extreme caution when handling this species. There is currently insufficient clinical data to definitively outline long-term systemic side effects or reproductive toxicity in humans. Therefore, medical professionals generally advise avoiding ingestion unless specifically supervised by a qualified practitioner.
