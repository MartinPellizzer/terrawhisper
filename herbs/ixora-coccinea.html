Ixora coccinea

What's the taxonomical classification of Ixora coccinea?

Ixora coccinea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Gentianaales. This plant is a member of the family Rubiaceae, which is further categorized into the genus Ixora, ultimately defining its specific classification as the species coccinea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ixora coccinea has an evergreen, woody shrub structure characterized by dense, spreading branches and leathery, elliptical leaves that measure approximately 5 to 15 centimeters in length.

The foliage typically presents a deep glossy green color with smooth margins and an entire shape. Large, terminal corymbs emerge from the branch tips, consisting of numerous small, tubular flowers packed closely together. These individual blossoms feature four spreading lobes and open into a vibrant scarlet or bright red color.

The plant develops a compact, bushy habit that makes it ideal for ornamental hedging and tropical landscaping.

      What are the herbal preparations of Ixora coccinea?

      Ixora coccinea is prepared as decoctions, infusions, pastes, and topical poultices using its various plant parts including the flowers, leaves, and roots. The flowers are often steeped in hot water to create a mild tea used for internal consumption. Leaves can be crushed into a fine paste to be applied directly to the skin for treating localized ailments. Root extracts are frequently boiled down into concentrated decoctions for more potent medicinal applications. These diverse preparation methods allow the plant's active compounds to be utilized for both systemic and external therapeutic purposes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Ixora coccinea can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or a localized rash upon direct contact with the plant's sap or leaves. If the plant material is consumed, it may lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain depending on the quantity and the person's sensitivity. While the plant is widely used for ornamental purposes, careful handling is recommended to avoid these adverse reactions. Always consult a professional if symptoms persist after exposure.
