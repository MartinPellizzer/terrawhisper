Stauntonia hexaphylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Stauntonia hexaphylla?

Stauntonia hexaphyllia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Ranunculales. The plant is a member of the family Lardizabalaceae, falling under the genus Stauntonia, and is specifically identified by its species name, hexaphyllia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Stauntonia hexaphyllia has woody, climbing vines that produce compound leaves typically composed of six to seven serrated leaflets arranged in a whorl. These leaves emerge from a central point on the stem, giving the foliage a distinct star-like appearance. The plant features small, yellowish-green flowers that grow in clusters along the stems. After flowering, it develops small, fleshy berries that transition from green to a bright red or orange color when mature. The stems are often twining and can become quite thick and woody as the plant ages.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", Stauntonia hexaphyllia has been used as a traditional herbal medicine in Korea and China for its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. A phytochemical study of the leaves of this plant led to the isolation of 2 oleanane-type triterpene saponins. These compounds are identified as 3-O-[beta-d-glucopyranosyl (1->2)-alpha-l-arabinopyranosyl] oleanolic acid-28-O-[beta-d-glucopyranosyl (1->6)-beta-d-glucopyranosyl] ester (1) and 3-O-alpha-l-arabinopyranosyl oleanolic acid-28-O-[beta-d-glucopyranosyl (1->6)-beta-d-glucopyranosyl] ester (2). When orally administered, these saponin constituents exerted much more potent anti-inflammatory activities in vivo than their sapogenin core. Both the 2 saponins and the sapogenin molecule inhibited the RAW 264.7 cell activation comparably well in vitro.
