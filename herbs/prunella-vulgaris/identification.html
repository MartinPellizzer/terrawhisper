Botanical Identification of Selfheal (Prunella vulgaris)

To identify Selfheal (Prunella vulgaris) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Selfheal (Prunella vulgaris) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Selfheal, or Prunella vulgaris, belongs to the plant kingdom, classified under Magnoliophyta, Lamiales class, Lamiales order, Lamiaceae family, genus Prunella, and species Prunella vulgaris. This classification places it within the mint family, known for its aromatic properties and medicinal uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Prunella vulgaris is the scientific name for Selfheal. It was first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. Common synonyms include Prunella lapidea and Prunella grandiflora. It is also known by regional names such as Heal-all and Woolly Betony.

Morphological Characteristics

Selfheal is a perennial herb with a creeping rhizome and fibrous roots. It has square, ascending stems that are hairy and can grow up to 30 cm tall. Its opposite, lance-shaped leaves are hairy and have toothed margins. The plant produces spike-like inflorescences with purple flowers, and it forms dry, three-lobed fruits containing three seeds each.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Selfheal can be distinguished by its square, hairy stems, opposite leaves with scalloped edges, and spikes of blue to purple flowers. It differs from related species like deadnettle by its hairy stems and the absence of a basal leaf rosette. The presence of a distinctive violet-blue flower spike also helps in identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Selfheal is native to Europe, western Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with mild summers and cool winters. It prefers well-drained soils, often found in meadows, woodlands, and disturbed areas. It grows at elevations between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Selfheal blooms from late spring to early summer, typically June to July. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August. For identification, the best time to harvest is during flowering, when the leaves and stems are most distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Prunella vulgaris belongs to the Lamiaceae family, sharing traits like square stems and opposite leaves with other members. It is closely related to species like Prunella granatensis and Prunella laciniata. These plants are part of the genus Prunella, which includes several similar, small flowering herbs.
