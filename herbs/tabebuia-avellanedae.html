Scientific and Botanical Profile

Native to Central America, the Caribbean, Mexico, and parts of South America, including Brazil, Colombia, Venezuela, Peru, Ecuador, Guyana, Suriname, and French Guiana, it thrives in tropical and subtropical climates. Morphologically, it is a deciduous tree or shrub characterized by its smooth, reddish-bark trunk and lance-shaped, alternate leaves that turn red in autumn. The tree produces showy, trumpet-shaped flowers in clusters, typically in shades of red, pink, or yellow, depending on the variety, which bloom in spring and attract pollinators such as bees and birds.

Its ornamental value and adaptability make it a popular choice for landscaping in warm regions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Tabebuia avellanedae grows in tropical and subtropical regions, preferring well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in warm climates with moderate rainfall, though it can tolerate short drought periods once established. Regular watering during the growing season is essential, but overwatering should be avoided to prevent root rot. For care and maintenance, pruning dead or diseased branches encourages new growth and improves air circulation, while mulching helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Harvesting is best done during the dry season when the bark contains the highest concentration of active compounds; the bark is carefully stripped from the trunk or branches using a sharp knife, ensuring minimal damage to the plant.

After harvesting, the bark should be cleaned, cut into small pieces, and dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area to preserve its potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place to maintain its medicinal properties.
