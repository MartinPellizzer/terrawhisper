Tephrosia vogelii

What's the taxonomical classification of Tephrosia vogelii?

Tephrosia vogelii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Fabales. As a member of the family Fabaceae, this plant is further categorized under the genus Tephrosia, with its specific designation being the species vogelii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tephrosia vogelii has woody stems, pinnate leaves with several pairs of leaflets, and pea-like flowers that typically feature a yellow or pale coloration. The foliage consists of small, elliptical leaflets arranged symmetrically along a central rachis. Its inflorescence is organized into clusters that emerge from the leaf axils. The plant also develops characteristic legume pods that contain multiple seeds once the flowering cycle is complete. These structural features are typical of members within the Fabaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the state of Bahia in northeastern Brazil. It is specifically located within the Caatinga biome, which is a unique semi-arid scrubland region. Populations of this species are restricted to very localized areas within this dry vegetation zone. The distribution is highly constrained by the specific environmental conditions found in this part of the Brazilian interior. Consequently, its geographical range is quite narrow compared to more widespread legumes.

What are the pharmacological activities of Tephrosia vogelii?

This plant has anti-leishmanial, antibacterial, antioxidant, ichthyotoxic, and mosquitoicidal pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents demonstrate the ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and protozoan parasites. Research indicates that its extracts can effectively neutralize free radicals, providing significant antioxidant protection. The plant also exhibits toxicity toward fish, which categorizes its ichthyotoxic properties. Additionally, the bioactive compounds serve as an effective mosquitoicidal agent against various insect species.

As discussed in a study, Tephrosia vogelii extracts exhibit anti-leishmanial activity against L. major infection. In vitro assays revealed that Tephrosia vogelii had a 50% inhibitory concentration (IC50) of 12 μg/ml and an IC90 of 68.5 μg/ml. When 200 µg/ml of the extract was used in the amastigote assay, it showed a multiplication index of 20.57. In BALB/c mice, intraperitoneal administration of the extract resulted in a significantly (p < 0.05) larger reduction in lesion size and parasite burden control compared to oral administration. The study also noted significant differences (p < 0.05) in the levels of Nitric oxide produced in macrophages following treatment.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds cadinol, cis-nerolidol, maackiain, obovatiin, and tephrosin. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the biological activities and therapeutic potential of the species. Research into these molecules helps scientists understand how the plant interacts with various physiological systems. Each compound possesses unique structural properties that may offer distinct pharmacological benefits. Studying these substances is essential for developing new herbal remedies and understanding natural chemistry.

As discussed in a study, Tephrosia vogelii is a traditional medicinal plant used to treat hypertension, diarrhea and urinary disorders. Silica gel chromatographic separation of the CH2Cl2/MeOH (1:1) roots extract afforded seven compounds: beta-sitosterol (1a), stigmasterol (1b), 6a, 12a-dehydro-deguelin (2), tephrosin (3), maackiain (4), obovatin (5) and 6-oxo, 6a, 12a-dehydro-deguelin (6). GC-MS analysis of the roots' essential oils displayed 17 compounds, where cis-nerolidol (41.7%) and cadinol (19.7%) were major constituents. The CH2Cl2/MeOH (1:1) extract and maackiain (4) showed inhibitory activity against Pseudomonas aeruginosa with MIC values of 0.5 and 0.83 mg/mL, respectively. Additionally, tephrosin (3), obovatin (5) and 6-oxo, 6a, 12a-dehydro-deguelin (6) showed potent DPPH scavenging activity with IC50 values of 10.97, 10.43 and 10.73 μg/mL. Compounds 2-6 obeyed Lipinski's rule of five with 0 violations and were not found to be hepatotoxic, mutagenic, or cytotoxic.
