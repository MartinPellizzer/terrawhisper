Drosera peltata

What's the taxonomical classification of Drosera peltata?

Drosera peltata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Caryophyllales and the family Droseraceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Drosera, with its specific identification being the species peltata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Drosera peltata has a rosette of perennial, spoon-shaped leaves that measure approximately 10 to 30 millimeters in diameter and are arranged in a flat, circular pattern. Each leaf is covered in numerous glandular trichomes that secrete a sticky, glistening mucilage to trap insects. The margins of these leaves are typically entire or slightly wavy, lacking the deep serrations seen in some other sundew species. Long, slender petioles connect the leaf blades to a central basal rosette, allowing the plant to spread across the substrate. The plant also produces upright flowering stalks that rise well above the foliage to display small, delicate blossoms.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South America, specifically found within Brazil, Paraguay, Uruguay, and northern Argentina. It typically grows in moist, sandy soils found in open wetlands or marshy areas across these territories. The species thrives in sunny habitats where water remains consistent throughout the growing season. Its distribution is primarily concentrated in the southern half of the continent where suitable bog-like conditions exist. These specific environmental requirements limit its presence to certain latitudes within its range.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating respiratory ailments such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma, as well as digestive issues like gastritis. The active compounds in the plant act as an expectorant to help clear mucus from the lungs. It is also frequently employed to soothe inflamed mucous membranes in the throat. Some traditional practices utilize its extracts to regulate irregular menstrual cycles. These medicinal applications rely on the plant's natural astringent and anti-inflammatory properties.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or localized dermatitis if the mucilage or digestive enzymes come into contact with sensitive areas of the body. While primarily known as a carnivorous species, the sticky secretions used to trap insects may trigger redness or itching in individuals with hypersensitivity. Direct contact with the eyes should be avoided to prevent potential chemical irritation from its enzymatic components. There is limited scientific research regarding the toxicity of internal consumption, so ingestion should be strictly avoided to prevent unknown gastrointestinal distress. Always wash hands thoroughly after handling the leaves to mitigate these risks.
