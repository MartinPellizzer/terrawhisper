Oxytropis kansuensis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxytropis kansuenensis has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect to ascending stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters. The leaves are pinnately compound, consisting of multiple narrow, linear leaflets that are arranged oppositely along the rachis. Its inflorescence features small, papilionaceous flowers that are often tinged with purple or pink hues. The calyx is notably hairy, which is a common trait among members of this genus. Additionally, the plant develops a woody taproot that allows it to thrive in rocky or alpine environments.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Oxytropis kansuensis can cause severe poisoning characterized by gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential death due to the presence of toxic alkaloids. Ingestion of the plant typically leads to immediate symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. As toxins enter the bloodstream, individuals may experience dizziness, muscle tremors, and loss of coordination. In high doses, these alkaloids can cause respiratory failure or cardiac complications. Careful avoidance is necessary because even small amounts can be fatal to livestock and humans.
