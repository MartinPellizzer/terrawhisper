Alstonia scholaris

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Alstonia scholaris has pinnate leaves composed of several pairs of oblong to lanceolate leaflets that are arranged oppositely along a central rachis. These leaflets typically feature entire margins and a smooth, slightly leathery texture that distinguishes them from many other members of the Sapindaceae family. The plant develops a woody structure with a trunk and branches that can reach significant heights in its natural habitat. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in panicles, which eventually give way to fleshy, orange or red fruits. These fruits are often capsule-like and contain seeds that facilitate the plant's dispersal.

    What are the herbal preparations of Alstonia scholaris?

    Alstonia scholaris is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, or topical pastes derived from its leaves, bark, and roots. The leaves are most commonly steeped in hot water to create medicinal teas used for various systemic ailments. For more concentrated therapeutic effects, the woody bark is boiled for extended periods to extract its active alkaloids. Some traditional practices involve grinding the dried plant material into a fine powder to be mixed with honey or water. Additionally, the crushed foliage can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat localized inflammation or wounds.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Alstonia scholaris can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. The plant contains various bioactive compounds that may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals through direct touch. If any part of the plant is consumed accidentally, it may lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain. Some studies suggest that its chemical constituents could interfere with metabolic processes if taken in large quantities. It is important to handle this species with caution to avoid unexpected physiological responses.
