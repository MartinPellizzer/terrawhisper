Alstonia scholaris

What's the taxonomical classification of Alstonia scholaris?

Alstonia scholaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Gentianales, it is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Alstonia, with its specific identification being the species scholaris.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Alstonia scholaris contains medicinal compounds found primarily in its bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is frequently utilized in traditional medicine to treat various inflammatory conditions and skin ailments. Extracts from the leaves are often employed to address digestive issues and feverish symptoms. Additionally, the roots are processed to create decoctions used for their antimicrobial and analgesic properties. These various components work together to provide a broad range of therapeutic applications in herbal remedies.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Alstonia scholaris targets various enzymes and receptors including cyclooxygenase-2, alpha-glucosidase, and acetylcholinesterase to produce its pharmacological effects. The presence of bioactive alkaloids and flavonoids allows these compounds to inhibit cyclooxygenase-2, which directly mediates the plant's potent anti-inflammatory properties. Furthermore, the inhibition of alpha-glucosidase by its phytochemical constituents suggests a mechanism for managing postprandial blood glucose levels in diabetic models. The plant also interacts with acetylcholinesterase, providing a potential pathway for enhancing cholinergic neurotransmission in neuroprotective applications. These diverse protein interactions collectively underpin the medicinal utility of the species in treating inflammation, diabetes, and neurological dysfunction.

What are the therapeutic actions of Alstonia scholaris?

Alstonia scholaris has antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties that are utilized in traditional medicine to treat skin infections and wound healing. The plant contains various bioactive phytochemicals such as alkaloids and flavonoids that help combat oxidative stress in cells. These chemical compounds work together to reduce swelling and soothe irritated tissues when applied topically. Some studies suggest that extracts from the leaves can inhibit the growth of certain pathogenic bacteria. Consequently, it serves as a natural resource for managing minor dermatological ailments and promoting tissue repair.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, inflammatory conditions, wounds, and certain types of stomach issues. The leaves and bark contain bioactive compounds that exhibit significant antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. These medicinal qualities allow the plant to help heal minor cuts and soothe irritated skin surfaces. What are the herbal preparations of Alstonia scholaris?

Alstonia scholaris is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, or topical pastes derived from its leaves, bark, and roots. The leaves are most commonly steeped in hot water to create medicinal teas used for various systemic ailments. For more concentrated therapeutic effects, the woody bark is boiled for extended periods to extract its active alkaloids. Some traditional practices involve grinding the dried plant material into a fine powder to be mixed with honey or water. Additionally, the crushed foliage can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat localized inflammation or wounds.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Alstonia scholaris can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. The plant contains various bioactive compounds that may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals through direct touch. If any part of the plant is consumed accidentally, it may lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain. Some studies suggest that its chemical constituents could interfere with metabolic processes if taken in large quantities. It is important to handle this species with caution to avoid unexpected physiological responses.
