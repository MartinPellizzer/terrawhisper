Alstonia scholaris

What's the taxonomical classification of Alstonia scholaris?

Alstonia scholaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Gentianales, it is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Alstonia, with its specific identification being the species scholaris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Alstonia scholaris has pinnate leaves composed of several pairs of oblong to lanceolate leaflets that are arranged oppositely along a central rachis. These leaflets typically feature entire margins and a smooth, slightly leathery texture that distinguishes them from many other members of the Sapindaceae family. The plant develops a woody structure with a trunk and branches that can reach significant heights in its natural habitat. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in panicles, which eventually give way to fleshy, orange or red fruits. These fruits are often capsule-like and contain seeds that facilitate the plant's dispersal.

What are the pharmacological activities of Alstonia scholaris?

This plant has analgesic, anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidative, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities that stem from its rich concentration of bioactive phytochemicals. Its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties allow it to effectively manage pain and reduce swelling in various biological models. The presence of potent antioxidants helps neutralize free radicals, providing a strong anti-oxidative defense against cellular damage. Additionally, the plant exhibits significant antimicrobial capabilities by inhibiting the growth of diverse pathogenic microorganisms. Research also indicates that its unique chemical constituents contribute to its promising anti-cancer potential in targeting malignant cells.

As discussed in a study, Alstonia scholaris is an Indian traditional medicinal plant used in Ayurveda to treat disorders like asthma, bronchitis, diarrhea, dysentery and malaria. In folklore medicine, the milky juice of the plant is applied to wounds and ulcers to treat pain, ear ache and rheumatic pains. When administered orally for 14 consecutive days, the chloroform and methanol extract of Alstonia scholaris leaves at doses of 100 and 200 mg/kg was tested against neuropathic pain. The methanol extract at 200 mg/kg significantly attenuated changes in nociceptive threshold and biochemical changes, a result comparable to pregabalin at 10 mg/kg. HPLC of the bioactive methanol extract revealed flavonoids such as gallic acid, catechin, epicatechin, ellagic acid and kaempferol, with kaempferol being present in higher concentration. The ameliorative effect of the 200 mg/kg methanol extract on CCI induced neuropathic pain may be due to the anti-oxidative and anti-inflammatory properties of kaempferol.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds betulinic acid, ursolic acid, oleanolic acid, kaempferol, and gallic acid. These specific pentacyclic triterpenoids and polyphenols contribute to a diverse range of therapeutic properties. Betulinic and ursolic acids are frequently studied for their potential anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor activities. Additionally, oleanolic acid and kaempferol provide significant antioxidant benefits to the biological systems they interact with. Gallic acid further enhances these effects by offering strong antimicrobial and radical scavenging capabilities.

As discussed in a study, eight triterpenoids and five sterols were isolated from the hexane portion of Alstonia scholaris. The identified compounds include ursolic acid, oleanolic acid, betulinic acid, betulin, 2β,3β,28-lup-20(29)-ene-triol, lupeol, β-amyrin, α-amyrin, poriferasterol, epicampesterol, β-sitosterol, 6β-hydroxy-4-stigmasten-3-one, and ergosta-7,22-diene-3β,5α,6β-triol. Compounds 5, 9, 10, 12, and 13 were isolated from Alstonia scholaris for the first time. Among these, ursolic acid, betulinic acid, betulin, and 2β,3β,28-lup-20(29)-ene-triol showed anti-proliferative activity against NSCLC. The IC50 values for these four compounds were 39.8, 40.1, 240.5, and 172.6 μM, respectively. These pentacyclic triterpenoids may constitute a valuable group of therapeutic agents in the future.
