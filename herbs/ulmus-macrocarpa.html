What's the taxonomical classification of Ulmus macrocarpa?

Ulmus macrocarpa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Rosales, it is placed in the family Ulmaceae, which contains the genus Ulmus. Finally, the specific biological identity of this plant is defined by its species name, macrocarpa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ulmus macrocarpa has an upright, spreading crown with bark that transitions from smooth gray to deeply furrowed ridges as the tree matures. The leaves are typically large, ovate to elliptical, and feature doubly serrated margins with an asymmetrical base. A distinguishing morphological trait is the presence of fine, soft hairs on the underside of the leaf surface. The fruit consists of small, circular samaras that enclose a single seed and are produced in clusters. During the spring, the tree produces inconspicuous flowers that precede the development of the foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the eastern and central United States, ranging from the Atlantic coast to the Great Plains and from southern Canada to the Gulf of Mexico. Their range extends significantly from the humid subtropical environments of the South up into more temperate northern climates. What are the pharmacological activities of Ulmus macrocarpa?

This plant has pharmacological activities including anti-hyperlipidemic and antihypertensive effects, as well as the ability to enhance bone health by inhibiting osteoclast differentiation and preventing osteoporosis. These bioactive properties suggest that extracts from Ulmus macrocarpa can effectively regulate lipid levels and blood pressure in the body. Furthermore, the plant plays a critical role in skeletal maintenance through its specific mechanism of action on bone cells. By suppressing the differentiation of osteoclasts, it helps maintain bone density and structural integrity. This multi-faceted medicinal potential makes the species a subject of significant interest for treating metabolic and skeletal disorders.

As discussed in a study, Ulmus macrocarpa water extract (UME) shows potential for treating metabolic disorders such as hyperlipidemia. In experimental rats fed a high-cholesterol diet, UME was orally administered at concentrations of 25, 50, and 100 mg/kg for 6 weeks. This treatment significantly improved biochemical parameters by increasing triglyceride, total cholesterol, and low-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels while reducing high-density lipoprotein cholesterol. UME also prevented lipid accumulation by regulating AMPK activity and lipid metabolism proteins including ACC, SREBP1, and HMGCR. Similar patterns of gene expression levels were confirmed in oleic acid-treated HepG2 cells. These findings indicate that UME prevents hyperlipidemia via activating the AMPK pathway.
