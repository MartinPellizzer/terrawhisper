Ulmus macrocarpa

What's the taxonomical classification of Ulmus macrocarpa?

Ulmus macrocarpa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Rosales, it is placed in the family Ulmaceae, which contains the genus Ulmus. Finally, the specific biological identity of this plant is defined by its species name, macrocarpa.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ulmus macrocarpa contains medicinal properties primarily found in its inner bark and leaves. The inner bark is often harvested to prepare decoctions or poultices used for treating skin inflammations and digestive issues. Its leaves are also utilized in traditional herbal remedies to help manage fever and soothe respiratory discomfort. These plant parts contain mucilage, which provides a soothing effect on mucous membranes when ingested or applied topically. Practitioners of folk medicine have historically valued these components for their astringent and anti-inflammatory qualities.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Ulmus macrocarpa contains bioactive polysaccharides, tannins, and various glycosides within its inner bark and roots. These specific chemical constituents provide the plant with significant anti-inflammatory and astringent properties. The presence of mucilage helps to soothe irritated mucous membranes when used in traditional herbal preparations. Researchers often study these compounds for their potential to aid in digestive health and wound healing. Such medicinal qualities stem from the complex structural makeup of the plant's secondary metabolites.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Ulmus macrocarpra targets various inflammatory mediators and enzymes, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) and lipoxygenase (LOX) to reduce prostaglandin and leukotriene synthesis. The plant's bioactive compounds, such as polyphenols and tannins, interact with protein kinases to modulate cellular signaling pathways related to oxidative stress. These phytochemicals also demonstrate an ability to bind with certain bacterial enzymes, which contributes to its antimicrobial properties. Additionally, the mucilage and polysaccharides found in the bark can interact with mucosal proteins to provide a protective coating against irritation. Through these diverse protein interactions, the species exerts significant anti-inflammatory and cytoprotective effects.

    What are the therapeutic actions of Ulmus macrocarpa?

    Ulmus macrocarpa has therapeutic properties derived from its inner bark, which contains mucilage that acts as a demulcent to soothe irritated mucous membranes. This viscous substance provides a protective coating for the digestive tract, helping to alleviate symptoms of gastritis and intestinal inflammation. The plant also exhibits mild astringent qualities that can assist in reducing swelling and calming minor skin irritations. Additionally, traditional applications suggest its use in treating respiratory discomfort by calming coughs through its soothing effect on the throat. These phytochemical components work collectively to provide relief from various inflammatory conditions.

    What diseases this plant treats?

    This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin inflammations, digestive issues, and respiratory congestion. The inner bark contains mucilage which provides a soothing effect on irritated mucous membranes. Historically, decoctions made from the bark have been applied topically to soothe skin irritations and swellings. When ingested, the slippery texture of the plant material can help calm stomach upset and inflammation in the intestinal tract. These traditional medicinal uses rely on the plant's natural ability to coat and protect sensitive tissues.

    What are the herbal preparations of Ulmus macrocarpa?

    Ulmus macrocarpa is prepared as decoctions from the inner bark, infusions from the leaves, or poultices using the crushed bark and roots. The inner bark is frequently boiled in water to create a mucilaginous liquid used for soothing internal inflammation. Leaf infusions are often brewed to serve as a gentle tonic or topical wash. For external skin irritations, the bark is ground into a paste and applied directly to the affected area. These various methods allow practitioners to extract the specific soothing properties required for different medicinal applications.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Ulmus macrocarpa can cause allergic skin reactions, respiratory irritation, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the bark or leaves may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals, resulting in redness or itching. Inhaling fine dust from the wood or pollen can exacerbate asthma or cause sneezing and congestion. While not considered highly toxic, consuming any part of the tree without proper preparation may lead to stomach upset. These reactions are generally mild but can be significant for those with known plant allergies.
