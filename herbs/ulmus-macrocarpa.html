Ulmus macrocarpa

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ulmus macrocarpa has an upright, spreading crown with bark that transitions from smooth gray to deeply furrowed ridges as the tree matures. The leaves are typically large, ovate to elliptical, and feature doubly serrated margins with an asymmetrical base. A distinguishing morphological trait is the presence of fine, soft hairs on the underside of the leaf surface. The fruit consists of small, circular samaras that enclose a single seed and are produced in clusters. During the spring, the tree produces inconspicuous flowers that precede the development of the foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the eastern and central United States, ranging from the Atlantic coast to the Great Plains and from southern Canada to the Gulf of Mexico. It is commonly found throughout the Midwest and the Appalachian region where moist soils are abundant. These trees frequently inhabit river valleys, floodplains, and bottomland forests near water sources. Their range extends significantly from the humid subtropical environments of the South up into more temperate northern climates. Because they thrive in riparian zones, they are often distributed along major river systems across much of the country.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ulmus macrocarpa contains medicinal properties primarily found in its inner bark and leaves. The inner bark is often harvested to prepare decoctions or poultices used for treating skin inflammations and digestive issues. Its leaves are also utilized in traditional herbal remedies to help manage fever and soothe respiratory discomfort. These plant parts contain mucilage, which provides a soothing effect on mucous membranes when ingested or applied topically. Practitioners of folk medicine have historically valued these components for their astringent and anti-inflammatory qualities.
