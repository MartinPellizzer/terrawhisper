Litsea glutinosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Litsea glutinosa?

Litsea glutinosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Laurales and the family Lauraceae, eventually reaching its specific classification within the genus Litsea. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its unique species name, glutinosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Litsea glutinosa has an evergreen tree habit characterized by a spreading crown, simple alternate leaves with entire margins, and small yellowish-green flowers arranged in axillary panicles. The leaves are typically elliptic to oblong in shape and feature a distinctively glossy, coriaceous texture on the upper surface. Young twigs and petioles are often covered in a fine, sticky pubescence that gives the plant its characteristic name. The inflorescences consist of numerous small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into small, globose drupes. These fruits transition from green to a dark color upon maturity and are a key identifying feature of the species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, spanning from India and Sri Lanka through Myanmar, Thailand, and Malaysia to Indonesia and the Philippines. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland evergreen forests and hilly terrain throughout these humid zones. Within these habitats, the species is often found in well-drained soils along riverbanks or in dense jungle understories. Its presence is most concentrated in the Indo-Malayan botanical realm where seasonal rainfall is consistent. This widespread distribution reflects its adaptation to warm, moisture-rich ecosystems across the continent.

What are the pharmacological activities of Litsea glutinosa?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including analgesic, antibacterial, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and antipyretic properties. Its bioactive compounds function to alleviate pain and reduce fever in biological systems. The plant also exhibits a strong capacity to inhibit the growth of various harmful microorganisms through its antimicrobial and antibacterial mechanisms. Furthermore, its antioxidant capabilities help protect cells from oxidative stress and damage caused by free radicals. These diverse therapeutic effects suggest that Litsea glutinosa holds substantial potential for medicinal applications.

According to a study published by "PloS one", Litsea glutinosa leaves demonstrate significant analgesic, antipyretic, and antibacterial activities. In the hot plate method, a 500 mg/kg dose of the crude extract produced a pain inhibitory activity of 3.37 ± 0.31 sec, which differed significantly (P < 0.01 and P < 0.001) from morphine's 6.47 ± 0.23 sec. The 500 mg/kg dose also achieved a 70% suppression of writhing in mice (p < 0.001) compared to the 77.5% inhibition from morphine. Regarding antipyretic activity, the crude extract at 300 mg/kg-body weight reduced body temperature to 36.17 ± 0.32 °C after 3 h, while the standard at 100 mg/kg-body weight resulted in 36.32 ± 0.67 °C. Furthermore, a 400 μg dose of each extract inhibited bacterial growth within zones of 11 to 22 mm.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the alkaloid laurolitsine alongside a diverse range of bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, saponins, tannins, and phenols. These chemical constituents work together to provide the species with significant pharmacological potential. The presence of flavonoids and phenols contributes to the plant's antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the saponins and tannins play crucial roles in its biological activities and defense mechanisms. Together, these medicinal compounds justify the traditional use of Litsea glutinosa in various therapeutic applications.

According to a study published by "Phytomedicine : international journal of phytotherapy and phytopharmacology", the bark of Litsea glutinosa contains an alkaloid-rich extract. Laurolitsiine is identified as the richest alkaloid found in this extract. In studies using the db/db mouse model, this compound demonstrated potent antidiabetic effects and hypoglycemic activity. The substance stimulated glucose consumption and activated the AMP-activated protein kinase without inducing lactic acid production or cytotoxicity in HL-7702 hepatocytes. Additionally, the compound modulated the gut microbial community by decreasing the abundances of Mucispirillum schaedleri and Anaerotruncus_sp_G3_2012.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cancer, digestive system disorders, fever, microbial infections, and pain. Its medicinal properties are often utilized to soothe various ailments affecting the gastrointestinal tract. The plant also acts as an effective agent to reduce high body temperatures and combat harmful microbial infections. Furthermore, its natural compounds help in alleviating physical pain throughout the body. These diverse applications highlight the significant therapeutic potential of Litsea glutinosa in traditional medicine.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", Litsea glutinosa is considered an evidence-based medicinal plant for the treatment of cancer. The methanolic extract of the plant, known as LGBME, demonstrated antioxidant properties with an IC50 value of 5.51 µg/mL. In cytotoxicity studies, the LD50 of the LGBME was recorded at 24.93 µg/mL. When used in vivo for Ehrlich's ascites carcinoma-induced Swiss albino mice, the LGBME at a dose of 150 mg/kg/bw showed significant cell-growth inhibition. Specifically, the highest cell-growth inhibition observed at the 150 mg/kg/bw dose was 85.76%. This dose also resulted in decreased tumor weight and an increased mean survival rate.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested in improper quantities. While it is often used in traditional medicine, the essential oils extracted from its leaves may act as a topical irritant for individuals with sensitive skin. Some people might experience redness or itching upon contact with the plant's concentrated extracts. There is also limited clinical data regarding the long-term systemic effects of internal consumption. Consequently, caution is advised when using this species for medicinal purposes.
