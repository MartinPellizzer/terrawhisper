Polygonum chinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Polygonum chinense?

Polygonum chinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized under the order Caryophyllales and is a member of the family Polygonaceae. Finally, it is defined by its specific genus, Polygonum, and its unique species designation, chinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Polygonum chinense has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 20 to 60 centimeters and features distinct swollen nodes. The leaves are simple, lanceolate to ovate in shape, and possess an entire margin with an alternating arrangement along the stem. Small, pinkish to white flowers emerge from the leaf axils in clusters, often appearing in the summer months. The plant is characterized by its smooth, somewhat succulent texture and a root system that is slender and fibrous. Its overall structure is slender and delicate, reflecting its growth habit in shaded or moist environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across China, Japan, Korea, and parts of Russia. It is commonly found in temperate regions where it thrives in various habitats such as roadsides, fields, and forest edges. Within these areas, the species prefers moist soil and can often be found in sunny or partially shaded environments. Its distribution is largely determined by these specific climatic and soil conditions across the northern hemisphere. What are the therapeutic actions of Polygonum chinense?

Polygonum chinense has therapeutic actions that include serving as a bitter tonic, a diuretic, an anti-inflammatory agent, an antipyretic, and a hemostatic. The plant's bitter properties help stimulate digestion and improve metabolic functions within the body. Its diuretic effects promote the elimination of excess fluids through increased urine production. To combat fever, the plant utilizes its antipyretic qualities to help regulate body temperature. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory properties reduce swelling and redness in various tissues. Finally, the hemostatic action assists in controlling bleeding and promoting blood clotting when necessary.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds N-E-feruloyl tyramine, caffeic acid, coumarins, ellagic acid, and methyl gallate. These bioactive molecules work together to provide various therapeutic benefits to the human body. Caffeic acid and ellagic acid are well known for their potent antioxidant properties that protect cells from damage. The presence of coumarins can contribute to anti-inflammatory and anticoagulant effects. Additionally, methyl gallate and N-E-feruloyl tyramine add to the complex chemical profile used in pharmacological research.

As discussed in a study, five new compounds including two phenyldilactones (1, 2), two coumarins (3, 4) and a dimer of N-E-feruloyl tyramine (5) were isolated from Polygonum chinense. These were found alongside twenty-three known compounds (6-28) from the medicinal plant. All of these compounds showed potent anticomplement activity with CH50 values ranging from 0.18 to 1.45 mM. Additionally, the AP50 values for these compounds ranged from 0.26 to 2.80 mM. The study also identified the targets of compounds 1, 3, 5 and 10 in the complement activation cascade.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating respiratory infections such as the influenza A virus and the influenza B virus through its bioactive compounds. It possesses significant antiviral properties that help inhibit the replication of these specific pathogens within the body. Researchers have studied its ability to modulate immune responses to combat the symptoms associated with seasonal flu. The plant is also frequently utilized in traditional medicine to address inflammation and fever resulting from viral outbreaks. These medicinal characteristics make it a subject of interest for developing natural treatments for various viral illnesses.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extract of Polygonum chinense showed anti-influenza virus activity with EC50 values ranging from 38.4 to 55.5 μg/mL in a CPE inhibition assay. Eight pure metabolites were isolated from the ethyl acetate layer of Polygonum chinense through chromatography. Among these, ellaic acid (PC5), methyl gallate (PC7), and caffeic acid (PC8) significantly inhibited viral replication with EC50 values ranging from 14.7 to 81.1 μg/mL. These three metabolites also exhibited CC50 values higher than 300 μg/mL. The results demonstrate that Polygonum chinense and its metabolites possess effective anti-influenza virus activities against strains like A/Puerto Rico/8/34 (H1N1, PR8) and A/Hong Kong/8/68 (H3N2, HK).

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause serious liver toxicity and a range of gastrointestinal issues including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. Individuals consuming it may also experience dizziness as a neurological side effect. There is a significant risk of hypoglycemia, which refers to dangerously low blood sugar levels. These adverse reactions necessitate caution and medical supervision when using the herb.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Liver toxicity
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Abdominal pain
  • Dizziness
  • Hypoglycemia
