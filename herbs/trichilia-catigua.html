Scientific and Botanical Profile

Catigua, with botanical name Trichilia catigua, is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree native to Central and South America, including Brazil, Guyana, Suriname, French Guiana, Colombia, Venezuela, Peru, Ecuador, and parts of the Caribbean.

It belongs to the Meliaceae family, commonly known as the mahogany family, and is also referred to as Brazilian Catigua, Red Catigua, Yellow Catigua, and Catigua Tree. Morphologically, it features a straight, cylindrical trunk with smooth, reddish-brown bark, and lanceolate, opposite leaves that are glossy and dark green on top and pale beneath.

The tree produces small, greenish-yellow flowers followed by yellow or orange fruits that split open to reveal edible seeds, often used in traditional medicine and as a source of natural oils.

History and Cultural Relevance

Catigua was used by indigenous communities in South America, particularly in the Amazon Basin, for centuries as a sacred plant with profound medicinal and spiritual significance.

The plant, known scientifically as Trichilia catigua, was traditionally valued for its heart-shaped leaves and its ability to promote emotional healing, making it a key component in rituals aimed at balancing the mind and spirit. In various cultures, Catigua was often incorporated into ceremonies for emotional release, grief processing, and spiritual cleansing, with shamans and healers using it to facilitate deep introspection and emotional catharsis. The plant's enduring presence in both cultural and medicinal contexts underscores its role as a bridge between ancestral wisdom and contemporary well-being.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Trichilia catigua has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Trichilia catigua can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential effects on mood and cognitive function, but it is important to consider its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, trichilia catigua may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, headache, and dizziness in some individuals. It may interact with medications such as antidepressants, anticoagulants, and other herbal supplements, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Special populations, including pregnant or breastfeeding women and individuals with chronic illnesses, should avoid its use due to insufficient evidence on safety and potential risks.

For safe use, it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before starting trichilia catigua, follow recommended dosages, and monitor for any unusual symptoms.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Trichilia catigua grows best in tropical and subtropical regions with well-drained, fertile soil rich in organic matter, and it thrives in full sun to partial shade.

It requires consistent moisture, especially during the growing season, but it should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. Regular weeding and pruning are essential for maintaining plant health, while mulching helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Harvesting is best done when the leaves and fruits are fully mature, typically during the dry season, using clean knives to cut the branches or fruits without damaging the plant.

To preserve its potency, the harvested parts should be dried in a cool, shaded area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties.
