Sterculia lanceolata

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sterculia lanceolata has large, imparipinnate leaves consisting of several pairs of lanceolate leaflets that are typically 10 to 20 centimeters long with acute tips and entire margins. The tree reaches significant heights and is characterized by a sturdy trunk covered in bark that becomes fissured with age. Its inflorescences are small, yellowish-green clusters that emerge from the axils of the leaves or the woody branches. The flowers are relatively inconspicuous and lack petals, instead featuring a prominent calyx. Following pollination, the plant produces distinctive woody follicles that split open to reveal bright red seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning from India and Sri Lanka through Myanmar, Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia. The species is frequently found in low-to-medium altitude habitats where humidity levels remain consistently high. Its presence is often concentrated in coastal areas and riverine landscapes throughout the Malay Archipelago. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Sterculia lanceolata targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various cytokines to exert its potent anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects. The plant's bioactive secondary metabolites, including terpenoids and flavonoids, interfere with the signaling pathways of nuclear factor kappa B to reduce systemic inflammation. These compounds also interact with specific proteases and oxidative stress markers to mitigate tissue damage during injury. By modulating these protein interactions, the plant's extracts can inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory mediators like nitric oxide and prostaglandin E2. Such multi-target mechanisms allow the plant to provide broad therapeutic relief for skin conditions and febrile illnesses.

What are the herbal preparations of Sterculia lanceolata?

Sterculia lanceolata is prepared as decoctions from its bark, infusions from its leaves, and poultices from its crushed fruits or roots. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for treating various internal ailments. Leaves are often steeped in hot water to produce a liquid remedy for skin conditions or respiratory issues. What side-effects this plant can have?

Sterculia lanceolata can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested in improper quantities due to the presence of various alkaloids and tannins. While some cultures use parts of the plant for medicinal purposes, unregulated consumption may lead to nausea or stomach cramps. The chemical composition of the bark and leaves can trigger allergic reactions or contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. It is crucial to exercise caution because the specific concentrations of bioactive compounds can vary significantly between different plant parts. Proper identification and dosage are essential to avoid adverse physiological responses.
