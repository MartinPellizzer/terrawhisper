Chenopodium album

What's the taxonomical classification of Chenopodium album?

Chenopodium album belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Caryophyllales and falls under the family Amaranthaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identification is defined by its placement within the genus Chenopodium and the species album.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Chenopodium album has an erect, branched stem that reaches heights of up to 150 centimeters and features alternate, ovate to lanceolate leaves with wavy margins and a mealy, whitish coating on the undersides. The leaf texture is often fleshy, and the surface may appear glaucous or dusty due to minute granules. Small, green, inconspicuous flowers are arranged in dense, terminal or axillary panicles. These flowers are primarily wind-pollinated and lack showy petals. The plant develops a robust taproot system that aids in its survival in disturbed soils.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Eurasia and North Africa but has become widely naturalized across much of the globe, including North America, Australia, and parts of South America. It thrives in diverse climates ranging from temperate to subtropical regions where disturbed soils are present. You can frequently find it growing in agricultural fields, roadsides, waste ground, and riverbanks. Because it is highly adaptable, it has successfully colonized many different continents through human activity and wind dispersal. Its ability to tolerate various soil types and moisture levels allows it to spread across vast geographical ranges.

What are the pharmacological activities of Chenopodium album?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its documented analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anti-diabetic activities. Its phytochemical constituents work to reduce pain and swelling by inhibiting specific inflammatory pathways within the body. The presence of bioactive compounds also helps neutralize free radicals, providing a strong antioxidant defense against oxidative stress. Furthermore, the plant exhibits inhibitory effects against various bacterial strains, showcasing its antimicrobial properties. Research also suggests that its extracts can assist in regulating blood glucose levels, supporting its classification as an anti-diabetic agent.

As discussed in a study, Chenopodium album was screened to corroborate its traditional medicinal use for pain through the determination of its extract and fractions for in-vivo analgesic activity. The crude extract of Chenopodium album, known as ChAl-Crd, was tested at doses of 100 and 200 mg/kg. Additionally, the chloroform and ethylacetate fractions of Chenopodium album, referred to as ChAl-Chf and ChAl-Et, were administered at 75 mg/kg. These applications caused significant inhibition of the analgesic response induced by the acetic acid, formalin, and hotplate methods with significance levels of P<0.05, P<0.01, and P<0.001 where n=8.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds carvacrol, kaempferol, quercetin, rutin, and various flavonoids. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile and biological activities. The presence of carvacrol often provides significant antimicrobial and antioxidant properties. Additionally, the flavonoids and polyphenolic compounds like quercetin and rutin assist in reducing inflammation within the body. Kaempferol further enhances these health benefits by supporting cellular protection. Together, these substances form a complex chemical synergy that defines the plant's therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal compounds in Chenopodium album include coumaric acid and ferulic acid with concentrations of 4.74 and 5.72 μg ml-1 respectively. The essential oils from the shoot parts of Chenopodium album contain carvacrol at a concentration of 8.43%. These bioactive metabolites allow the species to exert significant inhibitory effects. The plant shows potential for future use in herbicide treatments.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause kidney stones due to oxalate toxicity, gastrointestinal distress, nausea, and diarrhea. The high concentration of oxalic acid in the leaves can lead to the formation of calcium oxalate crystals in the renal system. Consuming large quantities may also irritate the digestive tract, resulting in significant abdominal discomfort. This irritation frequently manifests as bouts of nausea and persistent diarrhea. Individuals should exercise caution when incorporating this species into their diet to avoid these complications.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Kidney stones (oxalate toxicity)
  • Gastrointestinal distress
  • Nausea
  • Diarrhea
