Scientific and Botanical Profile

Parietary, with botanical name Parietaria officinalis, is a common flowering plant belonging to the family Urticaceae, known for its stinging hairs and medicinal uses.

It is also commonly referred to by numerous names such as Lungwort, Parietaria, Pellitory, Parrowgrass, Pennyroyal, Wallwort, Parnassian, Piert, Wall Pennywort, Mouse-Ear, Walls Of London, Wall Pennyroyal, Stinging Nettle, Ground Ivy, and Stickyweed. Native to Europe, western Asia, and North Africa, this plant thrives in moist, shaded environments such as walls, fences, and rocky areas. Morphologically, Parietaria officinalis is a low-growing perennial with heart-shaped leaves covered in fine, stinging hairs, and small, greenish-white flowers arranged in umbrella-like clusters.

Its widespread common names reflect its historical use in traditional medicine and its prevalence in various habitats across its native range.

History and Cultural Relevance

Parietary was used in ancient civilizations such as Egypt, Greece, and Rome for its medicinal properties, where it was valued for its anti-inflammatory and skin-soothing effects.

In traditional European medicine, it was often employed to treat skin conditions, respiratory ailments, and as a remedy for insect bites, with its leaves and flowers being used to make poultices and tinctures. The plant also held cultural significance in various folk traditions, where it was sometimes incorporated into rituals for protection or to ward off evil spirits, particularly in medieval European superstitions. Today, parietaria officinalis is still used in some herbal remedies for conditions like eczema and allergies, reflecting its enduring relevance in alternative medicine.

Its historical and cultural legacy continues to influence modern herbal practices and botanical research.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Parietaria officinalis has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, with various forms available including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, one can make a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the leaves for a longer period, while an infusion involves soaking the leaves in cold water. Topical applications include using the fresh leaves or a diluted tincture to treat skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried herb per cup of tea, taken up to three times daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision due to potential safety concerns.

It is advisable to use parietaria officinalis sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to avoid adverse effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Parietaria officinalis can be used in medicinal preparations for its anti-inflammatory and antihistaminic properties, but it must be used with caution due to potential side effects and contraindications.

Common side effects may include gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and skin irritation, particularly in individuals sensitive to related plants in the Urticaceae family. It may interact with antihistamines, anti-inflammatory drugs, and certain herbs, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects or reducing the efficacy of these substances. Special populations such as pregnant or breastfeeding women, as well as individuals with chronic illnesses like asthma or autoimmune disorders, should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data and potential risks.

To ensure safe use, consult a healthcare professional before use, start with low doses, and discontinue if adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Parietaria officinalis grows in temperate climates, thriving in well-drained soil that is slightly acidic to neutral, with a pH range of 5.

5 to 7. 0. It prefers full sun to partial shade and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without becoming waterlogged. To ensure healthy growth, it benefits from annual fertilization with a balanced organic fertilizer in early spring, and frequent weeding to reduce competition for nutrients. Harvesting should occur in late summer to early autumn when the leaves are fully developed and the plant is at its most potent, using sharp scissors to cut the leaves just above the stem to avoid damaging the plant.

For storage, the harvested leaves should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area or using a dehydrator at a low temperature to preserve their medicinal compounds, then stored in airtight glass containers in a cool, dark place to maintain potency and extend shelf life.
