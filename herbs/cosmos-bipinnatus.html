Cosmos bipinnatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cosmos bipinnatus?

Cosmos bipinnatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its progression through the order Asterales, the plant is situated within the family Asteraceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identification is defined by the genus Cosmos and the species bipinnatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cosmos bipinnatus has pinnately dissected, feathery leaves that are deeply divided into narrow, linear segments. These leaves are typically arranged oppositely or alternately along slender, herbaceous stems that can reach several feet in height. The plant produces large, showy flower heads consisting of a central disc of tubular florets surrounded by long, spreading ray florets. These blooms come in various colors, including pink, white, and deep magenta, often featuring a contrasting central eye. The overall structure of the plant is airy and delicate due to the fine texture of its foliage.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cosmos bipinnatus?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including potent PTP-1B inhibitory activity and strong α-glucosidase inhibitory activity. These specific biochemical properties suggest that extracts from Cosmos bipinnatus may play a role in managing metabolic disorders like diabetes and obesity. By inhibiting PTP-1B, the plant can potentially modulate insulin signaling pathways within the body. Additionally, its ability to inhibit α-glucosidase helps slow the digestion of carbohydrates, which prevents rapid spikes in blood glucose levels after eating. These combined effects highlight the medicinal potential of the species in treating glucose metabolism issues.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", Cosmos bipinnatus is one of three ornamental plant species identified that could inhibit enzymes related to diabetes. Specifically, the study investigated the inhibitory activity of enzymes such as α-glucosidase and PTP-1B within a collection of 45 Latin-American plant species. While only 30 % of plants used in traditional medicine to treat diabetes showed greater than 60 % inhibitory activity against these enzymes, Cosmos bipinnatus was among six species previously undescribed for antidiaabetic use that showed potential. These findings suggest that such plants can inhibit these specific enzymes even without previous evidence of use in traditional medicine.
