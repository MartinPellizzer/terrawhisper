Cosmos bipinnatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cosmos bipinnatus?

Cosmos bipinnatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its progression through the order Asterales, the plant is situated within the family Asteraceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identification is defined by the genus Cosmos and the species bipinnatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cosmos bipinnatus has pinnately dissected, feathery leaves that are deeply divided into narrow, linear segments. These leaves are typically arranged oppositely or alternately along slender, herbaceous stems that can reach several feet in height. The plant produces large, showy flower heads consisting of a central disc of tubular florets surrounded by long, spreading ray florets. These blooms come in various colors, including pink, white, and deep magenta, often featuring a contrasting central eye. The overall structure of the plant is airy and delicate due to the fine texture of its foliage.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cosmos bipinnatus?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its potent PTP-1B inhibitory activity and α-glucosidase inhibitory activity. These specific biochemical properties suggest that extracts from Cosmos bipinnatus could play a role in managing metabolic disorders such as diabetes and obesity. By inhibiting PTP-1B, the plant may help regulate insulin signaling pathways within the body. Furthermore, its ability to inhibit α-glucosidase helps control postprandial blood glucose levels by slowing down carbohydrate digestion. Such multi-target mechanisms highlight the plant's promise for developing new therapeutic agents for glycemic control.

As discussed in a study, Cosmos bipinnatus is one of three ornamental plant species previously undescribed for antidiabetic use that could inhibit PTB-1B or alpha-glucosidase enzymes. This plant is part of a diverse botanical resource consisting of 45 Latin-American species. The research involved testing a plant-extract collection for inhibitory activity against human neutrophil elastase, alpha-glucosidase, and PTB-1B. It was found that only 30 % of plants mentioned in traditional medicine to treat diabetes had greater than 60 % inhibitory activity against PTB-1B or alpha-glucosidase. Cosmos bipinnatus belongs to a group of six plant species that demonstrated the ability to inhibit these enzymes.
