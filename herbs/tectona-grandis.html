Tectona grandis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tectona grandis has a massive, woody trunk characterized by deeply fissured, dark grey bark and a spreading crown of large, pinnately compound leaves. Each leaf consists of multiple pairs of oblong leaflets that possess smooth margins and a leathery texture. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into woody, capsule-like fruits. The root system is extensive and fibrous, providing strong structural stability for its heavy limbs. These morphological traits allow the tree to thrive in diverse forest environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of the Caribbean, specifically found throughout the islands of the West Indies including Cuba, Hispaniola, and Jamaica. It thrives in diverse coastal and inland habitats ranging from lowland forests to rocky maritime cliffs. The species is often distributed in areas with high humidity and seasonal rainfall patterns characteristic of the Caribbean basin. Its presence is most notable in limestone-rich soils where it can establish stable populations. These specific environmental requirements limit its natural range primarily to these island territories.

    What are the herbal preparations of Tectona grandis?

    Tectona grandis is prepared as decoctions, infusions, and topical washes using the bark, leaves, or wood of the plant. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction used for internal medicinal purposes. Leaves are often steeped in hot water to form a mild infusion suitable for drinking. Additionally, liquid extracts or washes derived from these parts can be applied directly to the skin for topical relief. These various methods allow for the extraction of different bioactive compounds depending on the desired therapeutic effect.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Tectona grandis can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and potential respiratory distress if its dust or oils are inhaled. The plant contains specific chemical compounds that may trigger redness, itching, or swelling upon direct contact with human skin. Individuals with known sensitivities to similar woody species might experience more severe inflammatory responses. Inhaling fine particles from the wood during processing can lead to coughing or irritation of the mucous membranes. Precautions such as wearing gloves and protective eyewear are recommended when handling the raw material.
