Tectona grandis

What's the taxonomical classification of Tectona grandis?

Tectona grandis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows a specialized evolutionary lineage that places it under the order Lamiales. Within this order, it is categorized into the family Lamiaceae, which is further refined into the genus Tectona. Ultimately, the specific identification of this plant is defined by its species name, grandi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tectona grandis has a massive, woody trunk characterized by deeply fissured, dark grey bark and a spreading crown of large, pinnately compound leaves.

Each leaf consists of multiple pairs of oblong leaflets that possess smooth margins and a leathery texture. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into woody, capsule-like fruits. The root system is extensive and fibrous, providing strong structural stability for its heavy limbs.

These morphological traits allow the tree to thrive in diverse forest environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Tectona grandis contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its heartwood, bark, and leaves. The dense heartwood is frequently utilized for its potent antiseptic and anti-inflammatory properties in various traditional remedies. Extracts from the bark are often employed to treat skin ailments and digestive issues due to their high concentration of bioactive phytochemicals. Additionally, the leaves are sometimes prepared as decoctions to help manage fever or promote wound healing. These various components work together to provide a diverse range of therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

    What are the herbal preparations of Tectona grandis?

    Tectona grandis is prepared as decoctions, infusions, and topical washes using the bark, leaves, or wood of the plant. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction used for internal medicinal purposes. Leaves are often steeped in hot water to form a mild infusion suitable for drinking. Additionally, liquid extracts or washes derived from these parts can be applied directly to the skin for topical relief. These various methods allow for the extraction of different bioactive compounds depending on the desired therapeutic effect.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Tectona grandis can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and potential respiratory distress if its dust or oils are inhaled. The plant contains specific chemical compounds that may trigger redness, itching, or swelling upon direct contact with human skin. Individuals with known sensitivities to similar woody species might experience more severe inflammatory responses. Inhaling fine particles from the wood during processing can lead to coughing or irritation of the mucous membranes. Precautions such as wearing gloves and protective eyewear are recommended when handling the raw material.
