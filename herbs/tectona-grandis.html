Tectona grandis

What's the taxonomical classification of Tectona grandis?

Tectona grandis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows a specialized evolutionary lineage that places it under the order Lamiales. Within this order, it is categorized into the family Lamiaceae, which is further refined into the genus Tectona. Ultimately, the specific identification of this plant is defined by its species name, grandi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tectona grandis has a massive, woody trunk characterized by deeply fissured, dark grey bark and a spreading crown of large, pinnately compound leaves. Each leaf consists of multiple pairs of oblong leaflets that possess smooth margins and a leathery texture. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into woody, capsule-like fruits. The root system is extensive and fibrous, providing strong structural stability for its heavy limbs. These morphological traits allow the tree to thrive in diverse forest environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of the Caribbean, specifically found throughout the islands of the West Indies including Cuba, Hispaniola, and Jamaica. It thrives in diverse coastal and inland habitats ranging from lowland forests to rocky maritime cliffs. The species is often distributed in areas with high humidity and seasonal rainfall patterns characteristic of the Caribbean basin. Its presence is most notable in limestone-rich soils where it can establish stable populations. These specific environmental requirements limit its natural range primarily to these island territories.

What are the pharmacological activities of Tectona grandis?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anti-diabetic properties. These biological actions are primarily attributed to the presence of various bioactive secondary metabolites found within its tissues. The antioxidant capacity helps protect cells from oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals. Furthermore, its antimicrobial and antibacterial effects provide a defense against a wide range of pathogenic microorganisms. Studies also indicate that certain extracts can assist in managing blood glucose levels, demonstrating significant anti-diabetic potential.

As discussed in a study, carbonized Tectona grandis showed antimicrobial activity against Streptococcus pyogenes, Staphylococcus aureus, Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), and Pseudomonas aeruginosa. The ethanol extracts from carbonized T. grandis showed good antimicrobial activity, especially against S. pyogenes. Additionally, the ethanol extracts from carbonized T. grandis demonstrated good anti-inflammatory activity. These specific pharmacological activities are relevant to the pathogenesis of pharyngitis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 2β-hydroxyursolic acid, caffeic acid, flavonoids, gallic acid, and myricetin. These bioactive substances work together to provide various therapeutic benefits to the human body. The presence of flavonoids and myricetin contributes to significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Additionally, gallic acid and caffeic acid are known for their ability to protect cells from oxidative stress. The specific inclusion of 2β-hydroxyursolic acid further enhances the plant's complex chemical profile.

As discussed in a study, Tectona grandis fruits contain three isolated compounds consisting of two quinones and one triterpene. These identified metabolites include tectograndone (1), 6-methyl-1,4-dihydroxyanthraquinone (2), and 2β-hydroxyursolic acid (3). The crude ethanol extract demonstrated antibacterial activity against four Gram negative bacteria strains with a MIC of 64-256 μg/mL. Specifically, 6-methyl-1,4-dihydroxyanthraquinone exhibited strong activity against Escherichia aerogenes with a MIC of 16 μg/mL. Additionally, tectograndone showed moderate activity against Escherichia coli with a MIC of 32 μg/mL.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating biliousness, cholelithiasis, dysentery, jaundice, and liver disease. Its medicinal properties are primarily applied to manage various digestive and hepatic ailments. It helps alleviate the discomfort associated with bile issues and gallbladder stones. Additionally, the plant serves as a remedy for intestinal infections like dysentery. It is also utilized to improve liver function and reduce the symptoms of yellowing skin or eyes.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion by steeping the leaves in hot water, a decoction by boiling the woody bark to extract its constituents, a tincture using alcohol to preserve its properties, or a concentrated extract for potent dosing. These various methods allow for different levels of strength and absorption depending on the desired therapeutic effect. Herbalists often prefer a decoction for the tougher stem tissues to ensure all active compounds are released. Conversely, a delicate infusion is better suited for the softer foliage to avoid bitterness. Tinctures and extracts provide long-lasting shelf stability and high concentrations of the plant's bioactive elements.
