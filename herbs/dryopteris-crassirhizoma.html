Dryopteris crassirhizoma

What's the taxonomical classification of Dryopteris crassirhizoma?

Dryopteris crassirhizoma belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is categorized under the subclass Polypodiinae and falls into the order Polypodiales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Polypodiaceae, placing it within the genus Dryopteris, and it is specifically identified as the species crassirhizoma.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dryopteris crassirhizoma has thick, woody rhizomes that are densely covered with brownish scales and bear pinnate fronds that can reach up to 60 centimeters in length. The stipes are stout and often feature a dark brown or reddish pigmentation near the base. Each leaf is bipinnate to tripinnate, displaying a delicate, feathery texture with lanceolate pinnae. The pinnules possess serrated margins and a slightly leathery consistency. Small sori are arranged in rows on the undersides of the fertile segments, typically protected by kidney-shaped indusia.

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anthelmintic, anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, and anti-oxidant properties. Additionally, the plant exhibits potent anti-inflammatory effects that help reduce swelling and pain in biological systems. It also serves as an effective anthelmintic agent by helping to eliminate parasitic worms from host organisms.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic extract of Dryopteris crassirhizoma was observed to be the most effective against Dactylogyrus intermedius. This extract demonstrated an EC50 value of 22.97 mg L-1 after 48 h of exposure. It exhibited a 100% efficacy against D. intermedius at 60.00 mg L-1. Additionally, the acute toxicity assay indicated that the 48-h LC50 value of the methanolic extract of Dryopteris crassirhizoma was 4.10-fold higher than its corresponding EC50.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Dryocrassin ABBA, Kaempferol acetylrhamnosides, crassirhizo-mosides A, crassirhizo-mosides B, and crassirhizo-mosides C. These specific chemical constituents are primary bioactive substances found within the plant's biological structure. Research suggests that these molecules contribute to the unique pharmacological profile of the species. The presence of these various glycosides and flavonoids defines its therapeutic potential. Scientists continue to study how these individual components interact within medicinal formulations.

As discussed in a study, the stems of Dryopteris crassirhizoma contain the key antiviral component dryocrassin ABBA, which is characterized by polyphenol groups and low contents. The total synthesis of dryocrassin ABBA was achieved in nine steps with an over-all yield of 4.6%. This compound and its analogues, specifically b5 and e2, showed better neuraminiadases inhibitory activity against Anhui H7N9 compared to the positive control OSV at 9.6 μM. Specifically, the IC50 values for these substances were 3.6 μM, 2.5 μM, and 1.6 μM. These findings suggest that dryocrassin ABBA and its analogues may have beneficial effects on treating the avian influenza H7N9 virus.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe health complications because its rhizomes contain ptaquiloside, a known carcinogen that may lead to cancer development. Ingestion of the plant typically results in immediate gastrointestinal distress, including nausea and vomiting. Many individuals who consume it also experience intense abdominal pain. Diarrhea is another frequent symptom associated with the toxic effects of this species. These reactions serve as warning signs of the plant's chemical toxicity.
