Botanical Identification of Little Lily (Lilium pumilum)

To identify Little Lily (Lilium pumilum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Little Lily, Lilium pumilum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Liliopsida, order Liliales, family Liliaceae, genus Lilium, and species Lilium pumilum. These classifications reflect its role as a flowering plant within the lily family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Lilium pumilum is the scientific name for Little Lily. It was first described by the botanist Carl von Linné, hence the author citation is Linnaeus. Synonyms include Lilium parvum and Lilium tenuifolium. It is also known by common names such as dwarf lily and small lily.

Morphological Characteristics

Little Lily (Lilium pumilum) is a small, herbaceous perennial with a rhizomatous root system. It has erect, slender stems that grow up to 30 cm tall. Its basal leaves are lanceolate, with a hairy surface, and it produces a single, upright inflorescence. The flowers are white with a yellow center, and the fruit is a capsule containing small, black seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Little Lily (Lilium pumilum) can be distinguished by its small, nodding white flowers with a greenish throat, narrow linear leaves, and a slender, hairy stem. It lacks the showy, trumpet-shaped blooms of larger lilies and has smaller, more clustered flowers. It is also shorter in stature compared to other lily species, often growing under trees or in shaded areas.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Little Lily (Lilium pumilum) is native to the mountainous regions of western North America, particularly in the Pacific Northwest. It thrives in cool, moist climates with moderate temperatures. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at elevations between 1,500 and 3,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Little Lily (Lilium pumilum) blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and June. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the best time to observe key features is during the blooming period, when leaves and flower structures are most distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Lilium pumilum is closely related to other lily species within the genus Lilium. It shares key characteristics with its closest relatives, such as bulb structure and floral morphology. Its phylogenetic placement reflects its position within the Liliaceae family, sharing traits like leaf arrangement and reproductive features with related genera.
