Scientific and Botanical Profile

Yanhusuo Corydalis, with botanical name Corydalis yanhusuo, is a member of the family Fumariaceae, commonly known as the fumitory family.

It is also referred to by several common names, including Chinese Corydalis, Yanhu Suo, Corydalis Yanhusuo, Corydalis Sativa, and Yanshu. Native to regions of East Asia, particularly China, Korea, and Japan, this plant thrives in temperate climates and is often found in forested areas or along rocky slopes. Morphologically, it is a perennial herb with slender, hairy stems and opposite, ovate leaves that are often hairy on the underside.

Its most distinctive feature is its clusters of delicate, pink to purple flowers, which bloom in early summer, followed by seed pods that mature into distinctive, curved capsules.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Corydalis yanhusuo can be used as a medicinal plant for its analgesic and sedative properties, but it must be used with caution due to its potential safety concerns.

It may cause side effects such as dizziness, nausea, gastrointestinal discomfort, and in some cases, liver toxicity, especially with prolonged use. It can interact with certain drugs, including anticoagulants and antidepressants, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Special populations, such as pregnant or breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to limited safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to follow a healthcare professional's guidance, start with a low dose, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Corydalis yanhusuo grows in well-drained, loamy soil that is slightly acidic to neutral, in a location that receives partial to full sun.

It thrives in cool to temperate climates and requires consistent moisture without waterlogging, making it suitable for regions with moderate rainfall or regular irrigation. Regular weeding and mulching help control weeds and retain soil moisture, while periodic fertilization with a balanced organic fertilizer supports healthy growth. Harvesting is best done in late spring to early summer when the roots are most mature, using a spade or trowel to carefully dig up the plants to avoid damaging the roots.

After harvesting, the roots should be washed, cut into pieces, and dried in a cool, well-ventilated area or using a dehydrator to preserve their potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to maintain their medicinal properties.
