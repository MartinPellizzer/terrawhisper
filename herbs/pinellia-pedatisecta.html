Pinellia pedatisecta

What's the taxonomical classification of Pinellia pedatisecta?

Pinellia pedatisecta belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Alismatales. This species is a member of the family Araceae and falls under the genus Pinellia, specifically identified by its species name, pedatisecta.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pinellia pedatisecta has deeply lobed leaves that are typically divided into five to seven distinct segments with serrated margins. The leaf arrangement is alternate along a slender, upright stem that can reach varying heights depending on the environment. Each lobe is lanceolate in shape and tapers to a sharp point at the apex. The texture of the foliage is somewhat leathery, providing a durable surface for the plant. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves during the blooming season.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds citrinin, pedaetisectine F, pedaetisectine G, terreic acid, and terremutin. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their unique biological properties and potential pharmacological applications. The presence of these various metabolites suggests a complex chemical profile that distinguishes the species. Researchers analyze these substances to better understand their interactions with organic systems. Detailed investigation into these compounds can reveal new insights into plant-based medicine.

As discussed in a study, the endophytic fungus Aspergillus floccosus PP39 isolated from Pinellia pedatisecta yielded three specific medicinal compounds. These isolated compounds include terreic acid (5), terremutin (6), and citrinin (7). Compound 5 demonstrated strong antibacterial activities against Escherichia coli, Micrococcus tetragenus, Staphylococcus aureus, and Pseudomonas syringae pv. actinidiae with inhibition zone diameters of 36.0, 31.0, 33.7, and 40.2 mm. The minimum inhibitory concentration values for these bacteria were 1.56, 3.13, 1.56, and 1.56 μg/mL respectively. Additionally, compound 7 exhibited strong antibacterial activity against P. syringae pv. actinidiae with an inhibition zone diameter of 26.0 mm and a minimum inhibitory concentration value of 6.25 μg/mL. Compound 7 also showed a phytotoxic activity against E. crusgalli with an inhibition rate of 73.4% at a concentration of 100 μg/mL.
