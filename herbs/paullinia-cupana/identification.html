Botanical Identification of Guaraná (Paullinia cupana)

To identify Guaraná (Paullinia cupana) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Guaraná, or Paullinia cupana, belongs to the plant kingdom, classified under Magnoliophyta, Eudicots, Sapindales, Sapindaceae family, genus Paullinia, and species Paullinia cupana. This classification places it within the broader group of flowering plants and highlights its evolutionary relationships.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Paullinia cupana is the scientific name for Guaraná, first described by the botanist Jean-Baptiste Champain in 1780. It belongs to the Sapindaceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Strychnos cupana and Paullinia cupana var. sanguinea. Common names include Guaraná, Brazilian pepper, and coffee bean, while regional and historical names reflect its traditional use by indigenous communities.

Morphological Characteristics

Guaraná is an evergreen shrub that grows up to 3 meters tall. It has a fibrous root system that helps anchor the plant and absorb nutrients. The stems are woody and branched, with a rough texture. The leaves are alternate, simple, and elliptic, with a glossy green upper surface and a lighter underside. The inflorescence is a short, erect spike, with small, greenish-white flowers. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, hard seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Guaraná can be distinguished by its elliptical, glossy leaves with prominent veins and its unique yellowish-green flowers. The fruit is a red, shiny drupe with a single large seed. These features help differentiate it from other Paullinia species, which may have different leaf shapes or fruit characteristics. The presence of a single large seed within a red drupe is a key identifying trait.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Guaraná is native to the tropical regions of Brazil, particularly the states of Pará, Maranhão, and Tocantins. It thrives in a warm, humid climate with consistent rainfall. It prefers well-drained, nutrient-rich soils and is typically found at altitudes between 200 and 800 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Guaraná blooms between April and June, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs from September to November. Harvesting is typically done when the fruits turn red and the seeds are fully developed, usually in late autumn.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Guaraná, Paullinia cupana, is closely related to species within the genus Paullinia, which belongs to the family Sapindaceae. Its closest relatives include Paullinia montana and Paullinia gaucho, sharing similar morphological and chemical characteristics. The genus Paullinia is part of the subfamily Paullinioideae, distinguished by its unique leaf and fruit structures. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary history and medicinal potential.
