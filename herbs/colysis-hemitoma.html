Colysis hemitoma

What's the taxonomical classification of Colysis hemitoma?

Colysis hemitoma belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida, it is further organized into the subclass Polypodiinae and the order Polypodiales. Taxonomically, this plant is situated within the family Polypodiaceae, falling under the genus Colysis, and is specifically identified by its species name, hemitoma.

What are the therapeutic actions of Colysis hemitoma?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, and inflammatory conditions such as eczema or dermatitis. The extracts from the plant possess antimicrobial properties that help combat bacterial pathogens on the skin's surface. It is also frequently applied topically to accelerate the healing process of minor cuts and abrasions. Some traditional practices utilize the plant to soothe itching and reduce localized swelling. These medicinal applications rely on the specific bioactive compounds found within its tissues.

What are the herbal preparations of Colysis hemitoma?

Colysis hemitoma is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste for medicinal use. The dried roots and rhizomes are often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid extract used for internal consumption. In some traditional practices, the plant material is crushed and mixed with a carrier to form a poultice for skin applications. These preparations are specifically designed to utilize the plant's active phytochemical constituents for their purported therapeutic effects. The choice of preparation method depends heavily on whether the intended treatment is systemic or localized to a specific area of the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Colysis hemitoma can cause skin irritation, localized redness, and allergic contact dermatitis upon direct contact with the plant's tissues. Some individuals may experience a burning sensation or itching if the sap touches sensitive areas of the body. If ingested, the plant might trigger gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or stomach cramping. Careful handling is necessary to avoid these adverse physical reactions during any interaction with the specimen. Always monitor for signs of swelling or dermatological changes after exposure to the plant.
