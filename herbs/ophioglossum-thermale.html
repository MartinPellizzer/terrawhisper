Ophioglossum thermale

What's the taxonomical classification of Ophioglossum thermale?

Ophioglossum thermale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Ophioglossidae. It follows the order Ophioglossales and is a member of the family Ophioglossaceae. Finally, its taxonomic position is completed by its placement within the genus Ophioglossum, specifically identifying it as the species thermale.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ophioglossum thermale has a solitary, erect, lanceolate fertile spike that emerges from a basal rosette of fleshy, simple, linear-to-oblong leaves. The vegetative portion consists of a single, undivided leaf blade that lacks the complex dissection found in many other ophioglossoid ferns. Its reproductive structure is a dense, cylindrical spike composed of numerous small, closely packed sporangia. This spike typically develops a distinct reddish or brownish hue as the spores mature. The overall plant architecture is relatively small and adapted to the specific geothermal soil conditions of its habitat.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ophioglossum thermale?

This plant has antibacterial properties that allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Research indicates that the chemical constituents within Ophioglossum thermale contribute to its ability to combat bacterial infections. These bioactive compounds work by disrupting the cellular integrity or metabolic processes of the targeted bacteria. Beyond its antibacterial effects, the plant is studied for other potential medicinal applications in pharmacology. Understanding these specific mechanisms is essential for developing new therapeutic agents from natural sources.

As discussed in a study, two new peroxy fatty acids, thermalic acids A (1) and B (2), were isolated from the herba of Ophioglossum thermale Kom. These 2 new acids exhibited potential antibacterial activities against Staphylococcus aureus, Bacillus subtilis, and Escherichia coli. Along with these 2 acids, 8 known compounds were also isolated from the plant. These 8 compounds include (3β)-methyl-3-hydroxy-urs-11-en-28 oate (3), luteolin (4), quercetin (5), 3-methoxyquercetin (6), ophioglonol (7), ophioglonol 4'-O-α-D-glucopyranoside (8), pedunculosumoside B (9), and syringol (10). This study represents the first report of peroxy fatty acids isolated from herbaceous plants of Ophioglossaceae.
