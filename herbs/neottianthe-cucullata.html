Neottianthe cucullata

What's the taxonomical classification of Neottianthe cucullata?

Neottianthe cucullata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As part of the order Asparagales, it falls under the family Orchidaceae, which is a diverse group of flowering plants. Specifically, this plant is identified by the genus Neottianthe and is further distinguished at the species level as Neottianthe cucullaata.

What are the therapeutic actions of Neottianthe cucullata?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, intestinal parasites, and various inflammatory conditions. The leaves and roots are often prepared as decoctions or poultices to address topical ailments. In traditional medicine, it serves as a remedy for digestive issues and stomach pains. Some practitioners also utilize it to manage symptoms associated with fever or systemic inflammation. These medicinal applications are rooted in the plant's bioactive properties used by specific indigenous groups.

What are the herbal preparations of Neottianthe cucullata?

Neottianthe cucullata is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the intended medicinal application. The dried roots and aerial parts are often boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction for internal consumption. For milder therapeutic effects, the plant material is steeped in hot water to produce a gentle infusion. When treating skin conditions or localized inflammation, the crushed fresh leaves are applied directly to the affected area as a poultice. These various preparations allow practitioners to extract specific alkaloids and bioactive compounds for different health needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Neottianthe cucullaata can cause localized skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap or foliage may trigger redness, itching, or swelling on sensitive skin areas. Some individuals might experience more severe reactions such as hives or a rash upon exposure to its chemical compounds. If the plant is consumed accidentally, it can lead to stomach pain, nausea, or vomiting. Careful handling and avoidance of ingestion are necessary to prevent these adverse physiological responses.
