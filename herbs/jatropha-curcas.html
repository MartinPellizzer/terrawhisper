Jatropha curcas

What's the taxonomical classification of Jatropha curcas?

Jatrophia curcas belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malpighiales and into the family Euphorbiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Jatrophia, and its species designation, curcas.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Jatropha curcas has a woody, perennial shrub structure characterized by erect, branching stems that typically reach heights of two to five meters and possess a succulent, latex-producing bark. The leaves are large, simple, and palmate, often featuring three to five distinct lobes with serrated margins. Small, yellowish-green flowers emerge from terminal or axillary clusters, functioning as monoecious structures with separate male and female components. Following pollination, the plant produces small, globose, three-lobed capsules that contain oily seeds. These morphological traits allow the plant to adapt to diverse tropical and subtropical environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Caribbean, where it thrives in warm, subtropical climates. It has since been widely naturalized across many other tropical and subtropical areas of the world, including parts of the Americas and Oceania. Because it is highly drought-tolerant, it frequently populates arid and semi-arid landscapes that other vegetation cannot inhabit. The species is often found growing along roadsides, in disturbed soils, and in wasteland areas. Its ability to adapt to diverse soil types has allowed it to spread effectively across various continents.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Jatropha curcas contains medicinal compounds found in its seeds, leaves, bark, and latex that are utilized for various therapeutic purposes. The seeds are frequently processed to extract oil, which is applied topically to treat skin infections and inflammatory conditions. The leaves are often crushed or decocted into a liquid to serve as a remedy for intestinal parasites and swelling. Additionally, the bark and the milky latex are used in traditional medicine to address wounds and certain types of pain. What are the pharmacological activities of Jatropha curcas?

This plant has antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer, anti-diabetic, and anti-oxidant pharmacological activities. Its diverse chemical profile allows it to combat various bacterial strains and reduce systemic inflammation in biological tissues. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can inhibit tumor cell growth and help regulate blood glucose levels in diabetic models. Furthermore, the plant contains potent molecules that neutralize free radicals to prevent oxidative stress. These multifaceted properties make Jatropha curcas a significant subject for modern drug development and natural therapy.

As discussed in a study, the 20% ethanol stem extract of J. curcas showed a maximum antibacterial activity of 40 ± 0.0 mm against Klebsiella pneumonia. The water extract of the root of J. curcas also inhibited E. coli by 35.25 ± 0.35 mm. Regarding antifungal activity, the ethanol extract of the flower showed 38.5 ± 0.7 mm against Aspergillus niger and Pencilium notatum, while the methanol extract of the root showed 27.25 ± 0.35 mm. J. curcas extracts contained phytochemicals and secondary metabolites including alkaloids, steroids, tannins, glycosides, flavonoids, saponins, courmeriin, and phenolic compounds. The plant also showed the highest density of color and maximum amounts of biochemical primary metabolites in different parts.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several medicinal compounds including apigenin, isoamericanin, isoprincepin, naringenin, and quercetiin. These specific flavonoids and glycosides are known to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. The presence of apigenin and naringenin contributes to potential neuroprotective and cardiovascular health advantages. Furthermore, the inclusion of isoamericanin and isoprincepin may assist in regulating various biological processes. By working together, these phytochemicals offer a complex profile of therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, the leaf extracts of J. curcas contain higher total phenolic content and total flavonoid content than the stem bark extracts, with ranges of 5.79-48.95 mg GAE/g and 1.64-13.99 mg RE/g, respectively. The leaf extract obtained by homogenizer assisted extraction showed a potent antioxidant capacity related to these higher levels. These extracts can reduce the gene expression of TNFα and BDNF, a process that may be partially mediated by phenolic compounds such as naringenin, apiigenin, and quercetin. The plant also displays various medicinal properties including anti-neurodegenerative, anti-hyperpigmentation, and antidiabetic effects.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating constipation, stomach problems, skin diseases, and various types of wounds. Its seeds and oils are frequently utilized to address a strong laxative need when digestive issues arise. When applied topically, the sap or oil can help soothe various skin diseases. The plant also possesses properties that aid in the healing process of an open wound. These medicinal applications make it a significant resource in many traditional healing practices.

As discussed in a study, Brown Hisex chicks were fed diets containing 0.5% Jatropha curcas seed. The investigation looked at symptoms, lesions, and changes in growth, haematology, and clinical chemistry. Results showed that high mortality and more severe changes occurred in chicks on a different diet than the Jatrophas feed. The study notes that caution should be observed in tropical countries where people are accustomed to using a laxative.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and intestinal inflammation if ingested. These intense digestive symptoms often lead to significant dehydration and a dangerous electrolyte imbalance in the body. Contact with the plant's sap or tissues may also result in localized skin irritation for some individuals. Furthermore, those affected by its toxins may experience dizziness as their physical condition declines.
