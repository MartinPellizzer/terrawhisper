Salvia japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia japonica?

Salvia japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic progression, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Lamiales and belongs to the family Lamiaceae. Within this family, it is identified under the genus Salvia, with its specific classification being the species japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia japonica has square stems, opposite lanceolate leaves with serrated margins, and terminal spikes of bilabiate flowers that transition from purple to blue. The foliage is typically hairy and provides a textured green base for the upright flowering stalks. Each flower spike consists of numerous individual blossoms arranged in whorls along the stem. The petals are distinctly two-lipped, a characteristic feature of the Lamiaceae family. These structures allow the plant to attract various pollinators during its blooming period.

What are the pharmacological activities of Salvia japonica?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-arthritic, antioxidant, antitumor, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals that cause cellular damage within the body. The anti-inflammatory and anti-arthritic effects suggest its potential for managing joint pain and systemic swelling. Additionally, the plant demonstrates antitumor capabilities by inhibiting the growth of various cancer cells. These anticancer processes are closely linked to the observed cytotoxicity against malignant cell lines.

As discussed in a study, the essential oil from Salvia japonica (SJEO) contains 47 identified components. This essential oil exhibits promising anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting the expression of interleukin-1, interleukin-6, tumor necrosis factor-α, and cyclooxygenase-2 in rats with adjuvant arthritis. It also displays significant cytotoxicity on B16 cell lines with an IC50 of 86.91-228.91 μg/mL and on LNCaP cell lines with an IC50 of 116.4-189.63 μg/mL. Furthermore, the SJEO presents satisfactory antioxidant activity with an IC50 of 7.93 μg/mL in the DPPH assay. Due to this performance, SJEO served as an optimal candidate natural antioxidant compared to Trolox C which had an IC50 of 13.83 μg/mL.
