Celtis soyauxii

What's the taxonomical classification of Celtis soyauxii?

Celtis soyauxii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Rosales, the plant is a member of the family Cannabaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its membership in the genus Celtis, with its unique designation being the species soyauxii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Celtis soyauxii has alternate, simple leaves that are typically ovate to elliptic in shape with serrated margins and prominent pinnate venation.

Its branches are characterized by slender, woody stems that may exhibit lenticels on the bark surface. The reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into small, fleshy drupes.

These fruits serve as a primary morphological identifier for the species during its fruiting season.

    What are the herbal preparations of Celtis soyauxii?

    Celtis soyauxii is prepared primarily as a decoction made by boiling the bark, leaves, or roots in water to extract medicinal compounds. This aqueous extraction is the most traditional method used in folk medicine to treat various ailments. In some regional practices, the plant materials may also be processed into a topical paste by grinding the fresh leaves. This paste is then applied directly to the skin to address local inflammation or irritation. Some practitioners also utilize the dried plant components to create infusions, which are similar to teas.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Celtis soyauxii can cause adverse physiological reactions such as skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or digestive distress if consumed or applied improperly. While many species in the Celtis genus are used in traditional medicine, the specific chemical constituents of this plant may trigger hypersensitivity in certain individuals. Contact with the sap or bark can lead to redness, itching, or localized swelling on the skin. Ingesting parts of the plant without expert guidance poses a risk of nausea or stomach cramping. Always consult a medical professional or toxicologist before using any wild plant for medicinal purposes.
