What's the taxonomical classification of Artabotrys hexapetalus?

Artabotrys hexapetalus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Magnoliales and into the family Annonaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by the genus Artabotrys and is specifically designated as the species hexapetalus.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, wounds, stomach aches, and various forms of fever. The leaves and stems are often prepared into topical applications to soothe skin irritations and promote the healing of open cuts. When consumed as a decoction, the plant components are believed to alleviate internal digestive distress and abdominal pain. Traditional practitioners also utilize the botanical extracts to help regulate body temperature during febrile episodes. These medicinal applications rely on the specific phytochemical properties found within the plant's various parts.

What are the herbal preparations of Artabotrys hexapetalus?

Artabotrys hexapetalus is prepared as a decoction of the roots, a juice expressed from the leaves, or as a topical paste made from crushed plant parts. These different methods allow for the extraction of specific medicinal compounds depending on the desired therapeutic effect. For internal ailments, the boiled root decoction is commonly consumed to treat various systemic issues. The fresh leaf juice is often utilized in liquid form for its specific bioactive properties. Additionally, applying a prepared paste directly to the skin serves as a traditional method for treating localized surface conditions.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Artabotrys hexapetalus can cause severe skin irritation, contact dermatitis, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of unidentified bioactive compounds. The plant contains various phytochemicals that may trigger allergic reactions when the sap or foliage touches sensitive skin. Contact with the plant often results in redness, itching, or localized swelling in susceptible individuals. If the plant is swallowed, it may lead to gastrointestinal distress or more serious internal complications. Caution is necessary when handling this species to avoid these adverse physiological responses.
