Prunus cerasoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Prunus cerasoides?

Prunus cerasoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Prunus and is specifically designated as the species cerasoides.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Prunus cerasoides has woody, deciduous stems that produce simple, alternate leaves with serrated margins and elliptical to ovate shapes. The foliage often exhibits a glossy texture and a deep green color on the upper surface. Small, white or pale pink flowers emerge from terminal or axillary clusters during the spring flowering season. These blossoms are characterized by five petals and numerous stamens typical of the Rosaceae family. The plant eventually produces small, fleshy drupes that transition in color as they ripen.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the central and southern regions of Chile, ranging from the Mediterranean-type climates of the Coquimbo region down to the more temperate zones in the Los Rios region. It primarily inhabits various habitats including sclerophyllous forests, coastal scrublands, and mountain slopes. Within these areas, it thrives in well-drained soils and can often be found in disturbed sites or forest edges. Its distribution is closely tied to the specific moisture and temperature patterns found along the Chilean coastal range and the Andes foothills. This localized range makes it a distinct component of the Chilean matorral ecosystem.

What are the pharmacological activities of Prunus cerasoides?

This plant has antimicrobial, astringent, wound healing, skin disease curing, and lipid lowering pharmacological activities. Its astringent properties help to contract tissues and reduce inflammation effectively. These characteristics support the plant's ability to accelerate wound healing and cure various skin diseases. Additionally, its chemical constituents demonstrate significant antimicrobial action against harmful pathogens. The plant also functions in a lipid lowering capacity to help manage cholesterol levels.

As discussed in a study, Prunus cerasoides bark is reported to possess therapeutic applications in wound healing, foot and mouth disease, and indigestion. The ethyl acetate extract demonstrated antimicrobial potential where Klebsiella pneumoniae 1 showed a zone of 39.5 mm and Staphylococcus aureus showed 22.5 mm. Major phytoconstituent antimicrobial activities included flavonoids ranging from 14.5-33.5 mm, diterpenes from 14-28.7 mm, and cardiac glycosides from 11.5-20.5 mm. The ethyl acetate extract showed a minimum inhibitory concentration of 0.1-10 mg/ml, while partially purified phytoconstituent concentrations were 0.5-10 mg/ml. Total activity potency for the ethyl acetate extract ranged from 26.66-2666 ml/g and for flavonoids was 41-410 ml/g.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause cyanide poisoning if any part of it is ingested by a person or animal. Consuming the toxic components typically leads to immediate symptoms such as nausea and vomiting. As the poison affects the body, individuals may experience dizziness, headache, and mental confusion. The cardiovascular system is also impacted, which can result in a rapid heartbeat. These serious side effects necessitate immediate medical attention following accidental ingestion.
