Scientific and Botanical Profile

Madagascar Periwinkle, with botanical name Catharanthus roseus, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Apocynaceae, commonly known as the dogbane family.

Native to regions such as Madagascar, Seychelles, Comoros, Mascarene Islands, Sri Lanka, India, and Indonesia, this plant is widely cultivated for its ornamental value and medicinal properties. Morphologically, it is a low-growing shrub with opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves and clusters of tubular, fragrant flowers that range from pink to white or red, depending on the variety. Commonly referred to by names such as Rose Periwinkle, Rose Carnation, Carnation, Rose Geranium, Rose Bush, Madagascar Rose, Common Madagascar Periwinkle, and Madagascar Rose, it has become a popular garden plant worldwide.

Its scientific and botanical significance is further enhanced by its role in the production of alkaloids used in modern medicine.

History and Cultural Relevance

Madagascar Periwinkle was used for centuries by indigenous communities in Madagascar for its medicinal properties, particularly in treating ailments such as diabetes and infections.

The plant has a rich history in traditional medicine systems, where its leaves and flowers were often brewed into teas or applied topically to soothe skin irritations and reduce inflammation. In local cultures, the plant holds symbolic significance, often featured in rituals and ceremonies that honor its healing powers and connection to nature. Today, its alkaloids, such as vincristine and vinblastine, are still used in modern cancer treatments, demonstrating the enduring relevance of traditional remedies.

This blend of historical use and contemporary application highlights the plant's profound cultural and medical legacy.

Catharanthus roseus has been recognized for its potent medicinal properties, particularly due to its alkaloid content, which includes vincristine and vinblastine, both of which are used in cancer chemotherapy.

These compounds exhibit strong antimitotic effects, inhibiting cell division and making them valuable in treating various types of cancer, such as leukemia and lymphoma. In addition to its anti-cancer properties, Catharanthus roseus also supports cardiovascular health by improving circulation and reducing inflammation, which can benefit the cardiovascular system. Compared to similar herbs like Vinca minor or other traditional anti-cancer plants, Catharanthus roseus offers higher potency and more targeted therapeutic effects, making it a preferred choice in modern pharmacology.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Catharanthus roseus has been traditionally used in medicinal practices for its potential therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms such as fresh plant, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, one can make a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or create a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion for topical applications, such as applying the liquid to the skin for certain ailments. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 capsules per day, while for children, it should only be used under medical supervision if deemed safe.

Due to the lack of standardized guidelines, it is advisable to use catharanthus roseus sparingly and for short durations, avoiding prolonged or frequent use without professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Catharanthus roseus can be used in medicinal formulations for its potential anti-cancer properties, but it must be handled with caution due to its toxic nature.

The plant contains alkaloids such as vincristine and vinblastine, which can cause severe side effects including gastrointestinal distress, neurological symptoms like numbness and tingling, and even organ failure if ingested in large amounts. It may interact with other medications, particularly chemotherapy drugs, anticoagulants, and immunosuppressants, increasing the risk of adverse effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid catharanthus roseus due to the risk of harm to the fetus or infant, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, always use catharanthus roseus under medical supervision, follow prescribed dosages, and avoid self-medication to prevent poisoning or drug interactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Catharanthus roseus grows best in well-drained, fertile soil with a pH between 5.

5 and 6. 5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without becoming waterlogged. It thrives in warm climates and benefits from occasional fertilization during the growing season to promote vigorous growth and flower production. Regular pruning and deadheading are essential for maintaining plant health and encouraging continuous blooming throughout the growing season. The best time to harvest Catharanthus roseus is in the early morning when the plant's alkaloid content is highest, using clean, sharp scissors to cut the flower buds just below the node to preserve potency.

After harvesting, the flowers should be dried in a cool, dark, well-ventilated area or in a dehydrator to retain their medicinal properties, and stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to extend shelf life.
