Syzygium jambos

What's the taxonomical classification of Syzygium jambos?

Syzygium jambos belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Myrtales and the family Myrtaceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Syzygium, specifically identified as the species jambos.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Syzygium jambos has an evergreen growth habit characterized by elliptical, opposite leaves that measure up to 15 centimeters in length and possess a glossy, dark green upper surface with prominent pinnate venation. The tree develops a spreading crown with smooth, greyish-brown bark that often becomes mottled as the plant matures. Its inflorescences consist of numerous small, white flowers featuring long, protruding stamens that create a delicate, brush-like appearance. These flowers eventually give way to fleshy, globose fruits that transition from green to a vibrant rose-pink or white color upon ripening. The internal structure of the fruit contains a single seed surrounded by a sweet, aromatic pulp.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains anthraquinones, flavonoids, phenolic acids, tannins, and triterpenes which serve as its primary medicinal compounds. These anthraquinones are often utilized for their potent laxative properties in traditional medicine. The presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the body. Tannins within the plant contribute astringent qualities that can assist in wound healing and digestive health. Finally, the triterpenes offer additional therapeutic potential through their diverse biological activities.

As discussed in a study, Syzygium jambos fruits are rich in polyphenolic compounds, with an optimized total polyphenol content of 31.24 mg/g. The ethyl acetate phase achieved a TPC of 96.25 mg/g, which is threefold higher than the crude extracts. A total of 41 polyphenolic compounds were identified in this phase, including flavonoids like quercetin, isorhamnetin, and rutin, phenolic acids such as caffeic acid and ferulic acid, and the tannin corilagin. These extracts showed significant antibacterial activity at 100 mg/mL, with inhibition zones of 16.2 ± 0.5 mm against Escherichia coli and 14.3 ± 0.6 mm against Staphylococcus aureus.
