Annual St. John's Wort

Hypericum annulatum

Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, known for its bright yellow flowers and historical use in traditional medicine to support mood and mental well-being.

Taxonomy

Annual St. John's Wort, scientifically known as Hypericum annulatum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Garryales, family Clusiaceae, genus Hypericum, and species Hypericum annulatum.

Morphology

Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is a low-growing herb with opposite, oval to elliptic leaves that are typically reddish or purple underneath, and yellow flowers with five petals and prominent yellow stamens.

Distribution

Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, and is typically found in dry, sunny habitats such as rocky soils, grasslands, and coastal areas.

Traditional Uses

Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to support mood regulation, alleviate mild depressive symptoms, and promote skin healing through its anti-inflammatory and antiseptic properties.

Medicinal Systems

In traditional medicinal systems such as Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, and Native American herbal practices, Annual St. John's Wort has been historically used for its purported calming and healing properties.

Phytochemistry

The phytochemistry of Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) includes bioactive compounds such as hypericin, hyperforin, flavonoids, and essential oils, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine for mood regulation and anti-inflammatory effects.

Pharmacology

Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) exhibits pharmacological properties characterized by antidepressant, anti-inflammatory, and mild analgesic effects, primarily through its influence on neurotransmitter activity and modulation of inflammatory pathways.

Medicinal Uses

Annual St. John's Wort is used to treat mild to moderate depression, seasonal affective disorder, anxiety, sleep disorders, and symptoms of menopause such as hot flashes and mood swings.

Preparations

Annual St. John's Wort is used to prepare tinctures, teas, and topical salves from its dried flowers and leaves, which are harvested during the summer months.

Safety

While St. John's Wort is generally considered safe for short-term use, it can cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, dry mouth, dizziness, and increased sensitivity to sunlight, and may interact with certain medications.

Evidence

Clinical evidence for Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is limited compared to its more commonly studied relative, Hypericum perforatum, with most research focusing on its potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties rather than specific therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

Cultivation

Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is typically cultivated in well-drained, sunny soils with moderate moisture, often in temperate climates, and requires minimal maintenance once established.

Processing

Annual St. John's Wort is typically processed by drying the above-ground parts of the plant, including the stems, leaves, and flowers, which are then used to make teas, tinctures, or capsules for medicinal purposes.

Identification

Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is a low-growing perennial shrub characterized by its yellow flowers with orange centers, opposite leaves that are oval to ovate with toothed margins, and red-tinged stems that often bear small glands.

Sustainability

The sustainability of Annual St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is a critical consideration due to its limited geographic distribution and the potential for overharvesting, which can threaten its natural populations and ecological balance.

Related

St. John's Wort (Hypericum annulatum) is closely related to other species within the Hypericaceae family, including Hypericum perforatum (common St. John's Wort), Hypericum phillyreifolium, and Hypericum douglasii, which share similar chemical compositions and medicinal properties.
