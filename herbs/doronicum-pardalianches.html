Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum pardalianches): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum pardalianches) is a flowering plant native to the alpine regions of Europe, known for its bright yellow flowers and historical use in traditional medicine to support respiratory health and alleviate symptoms of respiratory conditions.

What is Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum Pardalianches)?

The Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum pardalianches) is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of Europe, characterized by its upright stems, lance-shaped leaves, and bright yellow flowers that bloom in early spring.

Botanical Identity and Classification

The Alpine Buttercup, or Doronicum pardalianches, is a perennial herbaceous plant belonging to the Ranunculaceae family, characterized by its distinctive yellow flowers and subalpine habitat.

Common Names and Synonyms

The Alpine Buttercup, scientifically known as Doronicum pardalianches, is also referred to by several common names including Alpine Buttercup, Mountain Buttercup, and White Buttercup, reflecting its geographic distribution and floral characteristics.

Plant Description and Morphology

The Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum pardalianches) is a perennial herb with erect, branching stems that are typically hairy, bearing alternate, doubly pinnate leaves with finely divided segments and small, yellowish-white flowers arranged in racemes at the tips of the stems.

Native Habitat and Distribution

The Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum pardalianches) is native to the mountainous regions of southern Europe, including the Pyrenees, Alps, and Carpathians, where it thrives in rocky, well-drained soils at elevations ranging from 1,000 to 2,500 meters.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, and flowers of the Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum pardalianches) are used medicinally, with the roots being the most commonly utilized part for their purported therapeutic properties.

Pharmacological Properties

The Alpine Buttercup (Doronicum pardalianches) contains various bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, alkaloids, and mucilage, which contribute to its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties.
