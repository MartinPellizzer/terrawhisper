Passiflora caerulea

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora caerulea?

Passiflora caerulea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Passifloraceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Passiflora, with its specific identification being the species caerulea.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Passiflora caerulea contains various bioactive alkaloids, flavonoids, and glycosides that serve as its primary medicinal compounds. Key alkaloids such as harmine and harman provide significant sedative and neuroprotective effects by interacting with the central nervous system. Flavonoids like vitexin and apigenin contribute to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties within the body. These chemical constituents work synergistically to reduce anxiety and promote sleep by modulating GABA receptors. The presence of these specific phytochemicals makes the plant a common natural remedy for insomnia and restlessness.

What are the therapeutic actions of Passiflora caerulea?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat anxiety, insomnia, restlessness, and various forms of nervous tension. Its sedative properties help calm the central nervous system to improve sleep quality and reduce stress levels. It is also frequently employed to manage mild symptoms of irritability and excitability in patients. Some traditional practices utilize its extracts to soothe digestive upsets caused by emotional distress. These therapeutic effects are largely attributed to the presence of alkaloids and flavonoids within the plant.
