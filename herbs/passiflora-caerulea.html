Passiflora caerulea

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora caerulea?

Passiflora caerulea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Passifloraceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Passiflora, with its specific identification being the species caerulea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Passiflora caerulea has a climbing, perennial vine structure characterized by three-lobed leaves and specialized tendrils that facilitate upward growth. The foliage consists of bright green, deeply lobed leaves that provide a dense canopy for the plant. Its most distinctive feature is the large, complex flower, which displays blue or violet petals surrounded by a prominent corona of fine filaments. The reproductive structures include a central ovary and several stamens that are arranged symmetrically around the floral center. As the plant matures, it produces edible, round fruits that transition from green to a deep purple hue.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical and tropical regions of South America, specifically spanning across Brazil, Paraguay, Uruguay, and Argentina. While its origins are rooted in these southern latitudes, it has successfully naturalized in many other parts of the world due to its hardy nature. It is frequently found growing wild in Mediterranean climates and parts of North America where temperatures remain relatively mild. In many regions, it is often categorized as an invasive species because it spreads rapidly through local ecosystems. The species thrives in various environments ranging from forest edges to disturbed garden areas.

What are the pharmacological activities of Passiflora caerulea?

This plant has anti-diarrhoeal, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, neuroprotective, and spasmolytic pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help mitigate oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals in the body. The presence of specific flavonoids and alkaloids contributes to its ability to reduce inflammation and relieve muscle spasms. Furthermore, its spasmolytic effects can soothe gastrointestinal distress, which supports its use as an anti-diarrhoeal agent. Research also suggests that its neuroprotective qualities may offer potential benefits for maintaining brain health and protecting neurons from damage.

As discussed in a study, Passiflora caerulea exhibits anti-inflammatory, anti-diarrhoeal, and spasmolytic activities. An extract dose of 250mg/kg, p.o. induced a significant reduction in the weight/length ratio, macroscopic lesion score, TBARS levels, and microscopic tissue damage. At a dose of 125mg/kg, p.o., the plant significantly decreased the amount of watery stools in a castor oil-induced-diarrhoea model. The extract also antagonized jejunum contractions induced by Ach with Emax values of 76.25% for 0.3mg/mL, 63.47% for 1mg/mL, and 42.01% for 3mg/mL. Furthermore, it antagonized contractions induced by CaCl2 with Emax values of 75.69% for 0.3mg/mL, 56.1% for 1mg/mL, and 53.4% for 3mg/mL.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the flavonoid compounds isoorientin, isovitexin, vicenin-2, and vitexin. These specific chemical constituents belong to a broader class of bioactive molecules known as flavonoids. Each of these compounds contributes to the unique medicinal profile and therapeutic potential of the species. Researchers study these substances to understand their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties in human health. The presence of these distinct glycosides characterizes the chemical makeup of the plant's extracts.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract of Passiflora caerulea contains identified compounds including isoorientin, vitexin, isovitexin, and vicenin-2. When administered at 250mg/kg, p.o., the extract induced a significant reduction in the weight/length ratio, macroscopic lesion score, TBARS levels, and microscopic tissue damage. The plant also demonstrated anti-diarrhoeaal properties at a dose of 125mg/kg, p.o., which significantly decreased the amount of watery stools. Furthermore, the extract showed spasmolytic activity by antagonizing jejunum contractions induced by Ach with Emax values of 76.25% for 0.3mg/mL, 63.47% for 1mg/mL, and 42.01% for 3mg/mL. It also antagonized CaCl2 induced contractions with Emax values of 75.69% for 0.3mg/mL, 56.1% for 1mg/mL, and 53.4% for 3mg/mL.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing epilepsy, colitis, diarrhoea, inflammatory bowel disease, and Acanthamoebiasis. Its sedative properties are frequently applied to help stabilize neurological conditions like epilepsy. The plant also contains compounds that target gastrointestinal distress, offering relief for colitis and diarrhoea. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions including drowsiness, sedation, dizziness, confusion, disorientation, and nausea. Individuals consuming Passiflora caerulea may experience a significant calming effect that leads to heavy sleepiness or a sense of sedation. Some users might also report feeling lightheaded or experiencing dizziness shortly after ingestion. Cognitive symptoms such as confusion and disorientation can occur, potentially impairing mental clarity. Additionally, gastrointestinal discomfort like nausea is a possible side effect for some people.
