Passiflora caerulea

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora caerulea?

Passiflora caerulea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Passifloraceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Passiflora, with its specific identification being the species caerulea.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Passiflora caerulea contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its leaves, stems, and flowers. The aerial parts of the plant are most frequently harvested to prepare herbal teas, tinctures, or extracts. These components are rich in alkaloids, flavonoids, and cyanogenic glycosides that contribute to its sedative properties. Many traditional remedies utilize the dried leaves to help alleviate anxiety and promote better sleep patterns. Consistent use of these specific plant parts is often studied for their ability to calm the central nervous system.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of medicinal compounds including flavonoids such as vitexin, isovitexin, and apiigenin, alkaloids like harmine, harmaline, and harmol, cyanogenic glycosides such as passiflorin, as well as various terpenoids and phenolic acids.

These bioactive constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's documented sedative and anxiolytic properties. The specific combination of beta-carboline alkaloids plays a significant role in its psychoactive effects. Additionally, the presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids contributes to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory capacities.

Together, these chemical classes form a complex therapeutic matrix used in traditional herbal medicine.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Flavonoids (e.g., vitexin, isovitexin, apigenin)
  • Alkaloids (e.g., harmine, harmaline, harmol)
  • Cyanogenic glycosides (e.g., passiflorin)
  • Terpenoids
  • Phenolic acids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Passiflora caerulea targets gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) receptors, specifically modulating GABA-A receptor subtypes to exert its sedative and anxiolytic effects. The plant contains various flavonoids and alkaloids that act as ligands to enhance inhibitory neurotransmission within the central nervous system. These bioactive compounds also interact with adenosine receptors, which contributes to the regulation of sleep cycles and relaxation. Additionally, certain constituents may influence serotonin pathways by interacting with specific neuroreceptors to alleviate anxiety. This multi-target mechanism allows the plant to influence complex neurological processes rather than acting on a single protein alone.

What are the therapeutic actions of Passiflora caerulea?

Passiflora caerulea has sedative, anxiolytic, and hypnotic properties used primarily to treat insomnia and nervous tension. The plant contains various alkaloids and flavonoids that interact with the central nervous system to promote relaxation. These compounds are believed to increase levels of gamma-aminobutyric acid, a neurotransmitter that reduces brain activity to facilitate sleep. It is frequently utilized in herbal remedies to manage mild anxiety and restlessness without causing heavy dependency. Regular consumption in controlled doses may also help soothe digestive issues related to stress.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat insomnia, anxiety, nervousness, and restlessness.

Its sedative properties help calm the central nervous system to promote better sleep patterns. People often consume extracts or teas to manage symptoms of emotional tension and agitation. The botanical compounds work to soothe an overactive mind during periods of high stress.

By reducing physiological arousal, it assists in stabilizing a person's overall mood.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Insomnia
  • Anxiety
  • Nervousness
  • Restlessness

What are the herbal preparations of Passiflora caerulea?

Passiflora caerulea is prepared as liquid tinctures, dried herb capsules, standardized extracts, and infused teas for therapeutic use. These preparations typically utilize the dried aerial parts of the plant to capture its sedative properties. Tinctures are particularly common because they provide a concentrated way to administer the alkaloids found in the leaves and flowers. Many commercial dietary supplements also come in tablet or capsule form for convenient daily dosing. Tea infusions offer a gentler method for relaxation and are often used as a bedtime beverage.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Passiflora caerulea can cause side effects such as dizziness, confusion, drowsiness, and gastrointestinal issues like nausea or stomach cramps. These adverse reactions often stem from its sedative properties which may impair mental alertness and motor coordination. Some individuals might also experience mild hypotension due to its potential to lower blood pressure. Excessive consumption can lead to increased sedation, making tasks like driving dangerous. It is important to consult a medical professional before use to avoid unexpected interactions with other medications.
