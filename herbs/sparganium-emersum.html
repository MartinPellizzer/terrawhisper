Sparganium emersum

What's the taxonomical classification of Sparganium emersum?

Sparganium emersum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Poales and is a member of the family Typhaceae. Within this family, the plant is categorized under the genus Sparganium, with its specific designation being the species emersum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sparganium emersum has a morphology characterized by a small, globose to subglobose spore sac that typically measures between 150 and 300 micrometers in diameter and is supported by a short, slender stalk. The peridium, or outer skin of the spore sac, is often thin and can be smooth, finely granular, or slightly textured. Inside the sac, the spores are contained within a capillitium that consists of branched, septate threads. These spores are generally small, ellipsoidal, and possess a distinct ornamentation that is visible under magnification. The overall structure is designed to release spores as the outer casing degrades or is disturbed.

What are the pharmacological activities of Sparganium emersum?

This plant has antimicrobial activities that allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Scientific investigations into Sparganium emersum have demonstrated its potential to combat infectious agents through these bioactive properties. Researchers often focus on its phytochemical constituents to understand how these compounds disrupt microbial cell membranes or metabolic processes. Such biological actions suggest that the plant could serve as a natural source for developing new therapeutic agents. These findings contribute to the broader understanding of the medicinal value found within the Sparganium genus.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", the antimicrobial activity of methanol and acetone extracts of Sparganium emersum was assessed using the disk diffusion method. The methanol extracts of this plant did not exhibit any inhibitory activity against the 3 Gram-positive, 5 Gram-negative bacteria, or 1 fungus tested. However, the acetone extracts showed significant activity specifically against Bacillus subtilis. For this activity, Sparganium emersum produced inhibition zones in the 7-16 mm range. Additionally, the minimal inhibitory concentration values for these extracts were within the 0.49-12.50 mg/mL range.
