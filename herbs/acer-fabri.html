Acer fabri

What's the taxonomical classification of Acer fabri?

Acer fabri belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Sapindales. Within this order, the plant is a member of the family Sapindaceae, which contains the genus Acer. Ultimately, its specific scientific designation is identified at the species level as fabri.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer fabri has opposite, palmately lobed leaves with three to five distinct lobes and serrated margins.

The foliage typically exhibits a deep green color during the growing season and can turn vibrant shades in autumn. Its woody stems are characterized by a greyish bark that becomes more textured as the tree matures. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into winged samaras used for seed dispersal.

These specialized fruit structures allow the seeds to spin through the air to reach new locations.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Acer fabri contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its bark and leaves. The bark is often harvested to extract tannins and other phytochemicals used in traditional applications. Leaf extracts are also studied for their potential bioactive properties in various therapeutic preparations.     What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Acer fabri targets cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its primary medicinal effects. The bioactive compounds within the plant, such as specific flavonoids and phenolic acids, act by inhibiting the enzyme cyclooxygenase-2 to reduce prostaglandin synthesis. By suppressing inducible nitric oxide synthase, these molecules effectively decrease the production of nitric oxide, a key mediator in inflammatory signaling pathways. Furthermore, the phytochemicals interfere with the expression of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6 to modulate the systemic immune response. This multi-target interaction profile suggests that the plant functions primarily through the regulation of key protein mediators involved in inflammation and oxidative stress.

    What are the therapeutic actions of Acer fabri?

    Acer fabri has therapeutic properties characterized by its astringent, bitter, and tonic actions.

    The presence of tannins provides an astringent effect that helps to tighten tissues and reduce inflammation. Its bitter components stimulate the digestive system by promoting the secretion of bile and improving appetite. As a tonic, the plant helps to restore overall vigor and strengthen bodily functions through consistent use.

      What are the herbal preparations of Acer fabri?

      Acer fabri is prepared as a decoction, infusion, or tincture to utilize its medicinal properties. The bark and leaves are most commonly boiled in water to create a potent decoction for systemic use. For more concentrated extracts, the plant material is macerated in alcohol to produce a tincture. These liquid forms allow for easier absorption of the active compounds within the body. Practitioners may also incorporate the plant into topical washes or poultices for external applications.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Acer fabri can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive distress if ingested or handled improperly. While specific clinical data on human toxicity is limited, many members of the Acer genus contain compounds that may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Ingesting parts of the plant could potentially lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal discomfort. It is important to avoid direct contact with the sap if you have a history of plant allergies. Always consult a medical professional if you suspect a reaction has occurred after exposure.
