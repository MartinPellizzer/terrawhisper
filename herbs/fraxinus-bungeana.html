Fraxinus bungeana

What's the taxonomical classification of Fraxinus bungeana?

Fraxinus bungeanana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Lamiales. This species is a member of the family Oleaceae, falling under the genus Fraxinus, and is specifically identified by the species name bungeanana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Fraxinus bungeanay has pinnately compound leaves composed of seven to thirteen lanceolate leaflets with serrated margins and a glossy green upper surface.

The tree features a spreading canopy and smooth, greyish bark that becomes fissured as the specimen matures. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge in clusters from the axils of the leaves during the spring season. These blossoms eventually develop into winged samaras that are held upright on the branches to facilitate wind dispersal.

The overall structure of the plant is characterized by its upright growth habit and spreading branch architecture.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Fraxinus bungeana targets specific inflammatory enzymes and signaling pathways, most notably inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX) enzymes to reduce prostaglandin and leukotriene synthesis. These phytochemical constituents interact with the NF-kappaB signaling pathway to suppress the transcription of various pro-inflammatory cytokines. Additionally, certain flavonoids within the plant demonstrate an ability to modulate nitric oxide synthase (NOS) activity, which helps regulate vascular tone and oxidative stress. By binding to these protein targets, the plant exhibits significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory pharmacological profiles. These molecular interactions collectively contribute to its potential therapeutic applications in managing inflammatory conditions.

      Fraxinus bungeanla is prepared primarily as a decoction, tincture, or infused tea using its bark, leaves, or flowers.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Fraxinus bungeanana can cause allergic reactions such as skin irritation, respiratory distress, or contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. While the plant is primarily known for its ornamental value, exposure to its pollen or sap may trigger hay fever symptoms. Some people might experience itching or redness if they come into direct contact with the bark or leaves. There is limited clinical data regarding the ingestion of its parts, but caution is advised to avoid potential gastrointestinal upset. Always consult a medical professional if you suspect an adverse reaction to this species.
