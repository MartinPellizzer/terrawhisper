Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is a medicinal plant native to North America, valued for its bark, fruit, and resin in traditional herbal medicine for their antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties.

What is Yellow Wood (Maclura Pomifera)?

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is a deciduous shrub or small tree native to the United States, known for its distinctive yellow heartwood, rough bark, and the fruit it produces, which has been used traditionally in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Yellow wood is Maclura pomifera. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Urticales, family Moraceae, genus Maclura, and species Maclura pomifera. It is commonly known as yellow wood in North America and as horse lung in Europe. The synonyms for this plant include botanical names such as Maclura pomifera and regional names like yellow wood and horse lung.

Plant Description and Morphology

Yellow wood is a deciduous shrub or small tree that typically grows to a height of 6 to 12 feet, with a dense, bushy structure and a thick, gnarled trunk.

Its leaves are large, heart-shaped, and deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, hairy underside. The flowers are small, yellowish-green, and clustered in dense, rounded inflorescences, blooming in late spring to early summer. The root system is extensive and fibrous, often forming a dense network that helps stabilize the plant in its native habitat.

The fruit is a large, woody, spiny capsule that turns yellow to orange with age, emitting a strong, unpleasant odor and having a rough, leathery texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Yellow wood is native to the southern United States, specifically found in regions from Texas to Florida and as far north as Kansas and Missouri.

It thrives in dry, sandy soils and is commonly found in open woodlands, prairies, and along streambanks. The plant prefers a warm climate with mild winters and hot, dry summers, making it well adapted to the southeastern and southwestern United States. It grows best in areas with full sun and low moisture levels, often colonizing disturbed or degraded habitats.

Its ecological success is linked to its ability to tolerate drought and poor soil conditions, allowing it to spread across a wide range of environments.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, bark, leaves, and fruits of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) are used medicinally, with the fruit being the most commonly utilized part for its purported health benefits.

Pharmacological Properties

The mechanisms of action of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) involve its rich content of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and iridoids, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties, contributing to its traditional use in herbal medicine.
