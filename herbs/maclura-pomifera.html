Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Yellow Wood (Maclura Pomifera)?

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is a deciduous shrub or small tree native to the central and eastern United States, known for its toxic, amber-colored fruits and historical use in herbal medicine for its purported tonic and circulatory benefits.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Yellow Wood, or Maclura pomifera, is a deciduous shrub or small tree native to North America, belonging to the family Moraceae, which includes figs and mulberries.

Common Names and Synonyms

Common names for Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) include Yellow Wood, Green Ash, and Bitterberry, with synonyms such as Common Maclura, Dogwood, and Bitter Mulberry.

Plant Description and Morphology

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is a deciduous shrub or small tree characterized by its thick, gnarled branches, rough gray bark, and compound leaves with 9 to 17 pairs of oval-shaped leaflets.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is native to the central and eastern regions of North America, primarily found in the United States and parts of Canada, growing in dry, rocky soils along riverbanks and in open woodlands.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, bark, leaves, and fruits of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) are used medicinally, with the fruit being the most commonly utilized part for its therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including flavonoids, tannins, iridoid glycosides, and triterpenoids, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic potential.

Pharmacological Properties

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antitumor, and immunomodulatory effects, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as maclurin and pomiferin.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) involve its ability to modulate inflammatory responses through the inhibition of pro-inflammatory cytokines and the activation of anti-inflammatory pathways, along with its antioxidant properties that neutralize free radicals and protect cellular structures.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Yellow wood is used to treat digestive issues, skin conditions, respiratory infections, joint pain, and as a diuretic and laxative.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) in herbal medicine typically involve drying the fruit, which is then used to make decoctions, tinctures, or powders, with the dried fruit being the most common form for internal use.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) for herbal use typically ranges from 200 to 600 mg of standardized extract per day, divided into two to three doses, with specific guidelines depending on the intended therapeutic application and individual health status.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or skin irritation in some individuals, particularly with prolonged use or high concentrations.

Drug Interactions

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) may interact with medications that affect the liver or blood clotting due to its potential hepatotoxic and anticoagulant properties.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is generally considered safe when used in appropriate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal upset in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and lactation due to limited safety data.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) is typically cultivated in well-drained soils with full sun exposure, requiring minimal water and thriving in temperate climates.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) typically involves drying the fruit, which is then ground into a powder or used to make tinctures, decoctions, or capsules for medicinal purposes.

Storage and Shelf Life

Proper storage of Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight is essential to maintain its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when stored correctly.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) has primarily focused on its potential medicinal properties, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects, with some studies suggesting its use in traditional herbal medicine for treating conditions such as arthritis and skin disorders.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Yellow Wood (Maclura pomifera) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and wound-healing properties, with historical applications ranging from treating arthritis and skin conditions to serving as a natural dye and adhesive.
