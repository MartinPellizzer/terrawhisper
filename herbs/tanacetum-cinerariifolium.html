Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Cineraria, also known as Tanacetum cinerariifolium, is a flowering plant native to the Mediterranean region and has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and digestive properties.

What is Cineraria (Tanacetum Cinerariifolium)?

Cineraria, or Tanacetum cinerariifolium, is a perennial herb native to southern Europe and western Asia, known for its aromatic leaves and flowers used in traditional herbal medicine for its antispasmodic, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cineraria is Tanacetum cinerariifolium. This plant belongs to the family Compositae, genus Tanacetum, and is classified under the kingdom Plantae. It is commonly known as Cineraria in Europe and as Tanacetum in North America. The synonyms for this plant include Tanacetum leucophaeum and regional names such as White Tanacetum.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cineraria, or Tanacetum cinerariifolium, is a perennial herbaceous plant characterized by its silver-gray, fuzzy leaves and clusters of small, yellowish-white flower heads that bloom in late summer to early autumn.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is native to the mountainous regions of southern Europe, particularly the Pyrenees, Alps, and Carpathian Mountains, where it thrives in rocky, well-drained soils and high-altitude environments.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves and flowers of Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) are the primary plant parts used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, and volatile oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) exhibits pharmacological properties including antispasmodic, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory effects, primarily attributed to its volatile oils and sesquiterpene lactones.

Mechanisms of Action

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) exerts its effects through the presence of bioactive compounds such as sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat respiratory conditions such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma due to its expectorant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or topical application, with the most common method involving the use of its dried leaves and flowers in alcohol-based tinctures or as a steam-distilled essential oil.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) for herbal use is 1 to 2 grams of dried herb per day, usually prepared as a tincture or infusion, with specific formulations varying based on traditional practices and intended therapeutic effects.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and for children has not been fully established.

Drug Interactions

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) may interact with medications that affect the central nervous system or blood clotting due to its potential sedative and anticoagulant properties.

Toxicity and Precautions

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause skin irritation or allergic reactions in some individuals, and long-term use should be avoided due to potential cumulative effects.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, requiring regular watering during dry periods and careful harvesting of the leaves and flowers when the plant is in full bloom to ensure optimal medicinal potency.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) typically involve harvesting the aerial parts of the plant, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain active compounds like essential oils and flavonoids.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) has primarily focused on its traditional uses in herbal medicine, with limited studies examining its pharmacological properties, potential therapeutic applications, and safety profile.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Cineraria, or Tanacetum cinerariifolium, has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its antispasmodic, anti-inflammatory, and carminative properties, often employed to treat digestive disorders, respiratory ailments, and skin conditions.
