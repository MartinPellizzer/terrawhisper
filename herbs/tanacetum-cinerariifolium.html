Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Cineraria (Tanacetum Cinerariifolium)?

Cineraria, or Tanacetum cinerariifolium, is a flowering plant native to southern Europe and parts of Asia, known for its medicinal properties and use in traditional herbal remedies.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Cineraria, scientifically known as Tanacetum cinerariifolium, is a flowering plant species belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly referred to as the daisy or sunflower family.

Common Names and Synonyms

Cineraria, also known as Tanacetum cinerariifolium, is commonly referred to by several regional names including burnet, cineraria, and false chamomile, and is widely recognized in herbal medicine for its medicinal properties.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cineraria, or Tanacetum cinerariifolium, is a herbaceous plant with upright, branching stems that are covered in fine hairs, bearing opposite, lance-shaped leaves with serrated edges and a distinctive grayish-white underside.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is native to southern Europe, particularly the Mediterranean region, and is commonly found in rocky soils and dry grasslands across countries such as Spain, Italy, and Greece.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves and flowers of Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) are primarily used medicinally, with the leaves being most commonly employed for their therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and volatile oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) exhibits pharmacological properties primarily attributed to its high content of sesquiterpene lactones, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antispasmodic effects.

Mechanisms of Action

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) exerts its pharmacological effects primarily through the presence of sesquiterpene lactones, which interact with inflammatory pathways and modulate immune responses, while also exhibiting antioxidant and antispasmodic properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Cineraria is used to treat respiratory infections, skin conditions, digestive issues, menstrual cramps, and inflammation.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or decoction, with the most common method involving harvesting the dried aerial parts and soaking them in alcohol or water to extract the active compounds.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) for herbal use is 2-4 grams of the dried herb per day, usually prepared as a tea by steeping in hot water, and it is recommended to consult with a qualified healthcare practitioner for personalized guidance.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly with prolonged or excessive use.

Drug Interactions

Toxicity and Precautions

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a herbal remedy, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal irritation in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) is typically cultivated in well-drained soils in full sun to partial shade, requiring regular watering and moderate temperatures for optimal growth.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) typically involves drying the harvested leaves and flowers to preserve their active compounds, followed by grinding into a fine powder or extracting with alcohol to create tinctures or essential oils.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cineraria (Tanacetum cinerariifolium) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence
