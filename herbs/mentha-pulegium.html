What to know about Mentha pulegium (pennyroyal) before using it medicinally

Mentha pulegium, commonly known as pennyroyal, is a highly valued herb for its numerous health properties, including its ability to relieve digestive issues, reduce stress and anxiety, and act as a natural insect repellent.

As a low-maintenance and adaptable plant, Mentha pulegium is a favorite among gardeners and herbalists alike, making it a popular choice for home cultivation. From a botanical standpoint, Mentha pulegium is a perennial herb belonging to the mint family, characterized by its small, purple flowers and square stems.

Historically, pennyroyal has been used by ancient cultures, including the Greeks and Romans, who valued it for its culinary and medicinal properties.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Mentha pulegium.

What are the medicinal properties of Mentha pulegium?

Mentha pulegium helps with digestive issues, relieving symptoms of indigestion, nausea, and bloating. It also acts as an antispasmodic, calming muscle cramps and spasms. Additionally, it exhibits antiseptic properties, treating wounds and minor cuts.

The active constituents of pennyroyal, including pulegone and menthone, contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds exhibit analgesic, anti-inflammatory, and antifungal activities, making it a versatile natural remedy.

Leaves and essential oil of the plant are the most commonly used parts for medicinal purposes. Infused oils, tinctures, and teas can be made from the leaves to extract their medicinal properties.

Improper use of pennyroyal can cause liver damage, particularly when consumed in large quantities or for extended periods. It can also interact with certain medications, such as anticoagulants and antacids, leading to adverse effects.

Precautions include using pennyroyal in moderation, avoiding its use during pregnancy and lactation, and consulting a healthcare professional before using it, especially with underlying medical conditions or taking medications.

What are the horticulural aspects of Mentha pulegium?

Mentha pulegium grow best in well-draining acidic soil with a pH range of 5.5 to 6.5. It requires full sun to partial shade and consistent moisture levels, but can tolerate some drought.

Plants should be spaced 12 to 18 inches apart, and can be grown in containers or directly in the ground. Water regularly, but avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot.

Harvesting begins when the stems are at least 6 inches tall, usually around 60 to 90 days after planting. Pinch off flower buds to encourage leaf growth and prevent the plant from flowering. Regular pruning also helps control the plant's spread.

Common pests affecting pennyroyal include aphids, spider mites, and root-knot nematodes. Leaf spot diseases such as Alternaria and Cercospora can also occur, especially in humid environments, while root rot is typically caused by overwatering or waterlogged soil.

What are the botanical aspects of Mentha pulegium?

Mentha pulegium is an annual or perennial herbaceous plant with square stems, 10-30 cm tall, and 4-8 cm wide, with 1-2 cm long, ovate, green leaves and small, purple flowers in axillary whorls.

Mentha pulegium belongs to the family Lamiaceae, subfamily Nepetoideae, tribe Mentheae, and is a member of the genus Mentha, section Pulegium, and is sometimes considered a subspecies of Mentha viridis. Its name is derived from Greek mythology.

Variants of Mentha pulegium include Mentha pulegium var. pulegium and Mentha pulegium var. micrantha, which differ in leaf and flower size, as well as Mentha pulegium f. microphylla and f. angustifolia, which have smaller leaves.

Mentha pulegium is native to the Mediterranean region, but has been naturalized in many parts of the world, including North Africa, Europe, and the Americas. It grows in moist environments, typically along streams, rivers, and coastal areas.

The life cycle of Mentha pulegium involves germination in spring, vegetative growth during the summer, flowering in late summer, and seed production in autumn. Seeds are dispersed by wind, water, and animals, and the plant can regrow from stem fragments.
