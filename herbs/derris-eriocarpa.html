Derris eriocarpa

What's the taxonomical classification of Derris eriocarpa?

Derris eriocarpa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic lineage of the order Fabales and is a member of the family Fabaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Derris, and its unique species designation, eriocarpa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Derris eriocarpa has a woody climbing habit characterized by pinnate leaves with several pairs of leaflets and small, densely hairy flowers.

The leaves are typically composed of an odd number of leaflets that exhibit a soft, pubescent texture on both surfaces. Its stems act as twining vines that allow the plant to scramble through forest undergrowth to reach sunlight. The inflorescence consists of small, clustered blossoms that are often white or pale in color.

These flowers are distinguished by their fine coating of hairs, which is a key identifying feature of the species.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains isoflavonoids, flavonoids, rotenoids, and terpenoids which function as its primary bioactive medicinal compounds.

The presence of rotenoids is particularly significant due to their historical use as natural insecticides and potential pharmacological activities. Flavonoids and isoflavonoids contribute to the plant's profile by providing antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Derris eriocarpa targets various protein structures including cyclooxygenase enzymes, specific neuroreceptors, and inflammatory signaling kinases to exert its bioactive effects. The plant contains rotenoid derivatives that act as potent inhibitors of mitochondrial complex I, disrupting the electron transport chain in targeted cells. These compounds also interact with various enzymes involved in the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway to reduce prostaglandin synthesis. Additionally, certain phytochemicals found in the species show an affinity for modulating cytokine production by interfering with intracellular signaling proteins. This multi-target interaction profile allows the plant to demonstrate significant insecticidal, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Derris eriocarpa?

Derris eriocarpa has insecticidal, acaricide, and vermicide properties due to the presence of rotenone within its roots.

This plant functions as a potent natural pesticide that targets a wide range of harmful organisms through respiratory inhibition. Its chemical compounds effectively eliminate mites and ticks by acting as a specialized acaricide. Additionally, the plant serves as a vermicide to clear internal parasitic worms in various biological subjects.

These combined actions make the species a significant botanical resource for controlling diverse pests and parasites.

What are the herbal preparations of Derris eriocarpa?

Derris eriocarpa is prepared primarily as a liquid extract or a concentrated decoction derived from its roots to utilize its insecticidal properties. The crushed roots are often boiled in water to create a potent solution used for controlling pests in agricultural settings. In some traditional applications, the plant material is processed into a powder that can be applied directly to surfaces or mixed into topical ointments. These different formulations allow for various methods of application, ranging from environmental sprays to direct contact treatments. The choice of preparation form depends entirely on whether the goal is large-scale pest management or specific localized use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Derris eriocarpa can cause severe skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and potential neurological toxicity if absorbed in significant quantities through the skin or ingested. The plant contains rotenone, a potent natural insecticide that can interfere with cellular respiration in humans. Exposure to its concentrated extracts may lead to symptoms such as dizziness, nausea, or headaches. Prolonged contact with the crushed roots can result in localized redness, itching, or blistering on the skin. Because of these toxic properties, careful handling is necessary to prevent accidental poisoning or systemic effects.
