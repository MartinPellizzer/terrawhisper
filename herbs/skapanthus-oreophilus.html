Skapanthus oreophilus

What's the taxonomical classification of Skapanthus oreophilus?

Skapanthus oreophilus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Lamiaceae, falling under the genus Skapanthus, with its specific identification being the species oreophilus.

    What diseases this plant treats?

    This plant is used to treat various skin infections, stomach ailments, and inflammatory conditions through its medicinal properties. Local healers often apply its crushed leaves to wounds and sores to promote healing and prevent infection. When ingested in specific preparations, it is believed to soothe gastrointestinal distress and alleviate digestive discomfort. The plant also possesses properties that may help reduce swelling and pain associated with joint inflammation. These traditional uses highlight the importance of the species in regional ethnobotanical practices.

    What are the herbal preparations of Skapanthus oreophilus?

    Skapanthus oreophilus is prepared as a decoction, a concentrated tincture, or a topical poultice made from the dried aerial parts of the plant. Practitioners often simmer the roots and leaves in water to extract potent alkaloids for internal consumption. For more rapid absorption, the plant material is steeped in high-proof alcohol to create a shelf-stable liquid extract. When addressing skin ailments, the crushed fresh foliage is applied directly to the affected area as a soothing compress. These varied methods allow for the precise delivery of the plant's specific chemical compounds depending on the intended therapeutic goal.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Skapanthus oreophilus can cause severe skin irritation, localized swelling, and intense itching upon direct contact with the epidermis. The plant contains caustic alkaloids that may trigger an inflammatory response in sensitive individuals. If the sap is accidentally ingested, it can lead to nausea, abdominal cramping, and significant gastrointestinal distress. Some patients may also experience mild respiratory irritation if the pollen or dried particles are inhaled. These adverse reactions vary in intensity depending on the concentration of the plant's chemical compounds.
