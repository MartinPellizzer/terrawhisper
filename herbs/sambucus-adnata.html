Sambucus adnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Sambucus adnata?

Sambucus adnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Dipsacales and is a member of the family Viburnaceae. Finally, it is identified under the genus Sambucus, with the specific species designation being adnata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sambucus adnata has opposite, pinnately compound leaves typically featuring five to seven elliptical leaflets with serrated margins and an acute apex.

The plant exhibits a woody, shrubby growth habit characterized by slender, branching stems that may display lenticels. Small, cream-colored flowers are arranged in dense, flat-topped cymes at the terminal ends of the branches. These blossoms transition into dark, fleshy drupes that serve as a primary morphological feature during the fruiting season.

The overall structure is defined by a spreading canopy and a fine texture provided by the delicate leaf arrangement.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Sambucus adnata contains bioactive compounds primarily located within its leaves, flowers, and bark that are utilized for various medicinal purposes. The flowers are often harvested for their aromatic properties and used in traditional preparations to treat respiratory ailments. Extracts from the leaves may be applied topically to address skin irritations or inflammatory conditions. Some regional folk medicines also utilize the bark in decoctions to manage fever or digestive issues. These different components provide a diverse range of phytochemicals that serve as the basis for its therapeutic applications.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Sambucus adnata targets specific inflammatory mediators and enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) and lipoxygenase (LOX) to modulate the immune response. These phytochemical constituents primarily inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). By interacting with these signaling proteins, the plant's bioactive compounds help regulate the NF-kappaB pathway to reduce systemic inflammation. Additionally, antioxidant polyphenols within the plant can neutralize reactive oxygen species by interacting with various cellular redox-sensitive proteins. This multi-targeted approach allows the plant to exert significant pharmacological effects on both the immune system and oxidative stress mechanisms.

        What are the herbal preparations of Sambucus adnata?

        Sambucus adnata is prepared as a decoction from the dried berries, a concentrated infusion of the flowers, or a topical tincture derived from the bark. The berries are frequently simmered into a thick syrup to preserve their bioactive compounds for long-term storage. Floral infusions are often consumed as a gentle tea to utilize the aromatic properties of the blossoms. For more localized applications, alcoholic tinctures allow for the extraction of specific phytochemicals used in skin treatments. These diverse methods ensure that different parts of the plant can be utilized for their unique medicinal profiles.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Sambucus adnata can cause nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and diarrhea if the raw berries, leaves, or bark are ingested. These adverse reactions primarily occur because the plant contains cyanogenic glycosides which release toxic hydrogen cyanide upon consumption. While many elderberry species are safe when properly cooked, the unripened parts of this specific plant pose a significant risk of poisoning. Symptoms of toxicity typically manifest shortly after ingestion and may include dizziness or weakness. It is essential to avoid consuming any part of the plant unless it has been professionally identified and processed to remove these harmful compounds.
