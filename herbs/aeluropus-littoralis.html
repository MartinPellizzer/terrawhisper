Aeluropus littoralis

What's the taxonomical classification of Aeluropus littoralis?

Aleuropus littoralis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is classified under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Poales. It is a member of the family Poaceae, which places it within the genus Aeulorpus, ultimately defining its specific identity as the species littoralis.

Aeluropus littoralis contains bioactive secondary metabolites including alkaloids, flavonoids, saponins, and tannins that contribute to its therapeutic potential. What are the therapeutic actions of Aeluropus littoralis?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, digestive disorders, and respiratory issues. Its bioactive compounds possess antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties that help combat bacterial and fungal pathogens. Some traditional medicinal practices also utilize the plant to alleviate localized pain and reduce swelling in affected tissues. When applied topically, it can assist in the healing of minor wounds and dermatological conditions. Researchers continue to investigate its chemical constituents to better understand their therapeutic potential in modern medicine.

What are the herbal preparations of Aeluropus littoralis?

Aeluropus littoralis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste depending on the specific medicinal application required. The dried plant material is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid extract used for internal consumption. For external ailments, the leaves are crushed into a thick pulp to be applied directly to the skin. Some traditional methods also involve grinding the dried parts into a fine powder that can be mixed with honey or oils. These various preparations allow the plant's active compounds to be utilized effectively for different health concerns.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Aeluropus littoralis can cause skin irritation, localized dermatitis, and allergic reactions if the plant's physical components come into contact with sensitive tissue. Contact with the leaves or stems may lead to redness, itching, or a burning sensation on the skin. Some individuals might experience swelling or minor inflammation in areas where the plant has touched them. While systemic toxicity is not widely documented, caution is advised when handling the species to prevent dermatological discomfort. It is best to avoid direct skin contact to minimize these potential adverse effects.
