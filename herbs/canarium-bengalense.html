What's the taxonomical classification of Canarium bengalense?

Canarium bengalense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Sapindales and the family Burseraceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Canarium, and its unique species designation, bengalense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canarium bengalense has an erect tree habit characterized by a straight trunk, grayish bark, and pinnate leaves that typically consist of five to nine leaflets. The leaves are arranged alternately and feature elliptical or oblong leaflets with entire margins and acute tips. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in axillary panicles, often appearing in yellowish or greenish hues. The plant produces fleshy, drupe-like fruits that are somewhat ovoid or ellipsoidal in shape. These fruits contain a single hard seed which is encased within a fibrous or leathery endocarp.

What are the pharmacological activities of Canarium bengalense?

This plant has cytoprotective pharmacological activity that helps shield cells from damage and injury. This specific protective effect works by maintaining cellular integrity and preventing the breakdown of vital biological structures. Such activity is often crucial in defending tissues against harmful oxidative stress or toxic substances. By stabilizing cell membranes, the plant helps preserve normal physiological functions within the body. This defensive mechanism makes it a significant subject for research into various therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical investigation of the stem bark of Canarium bengalense resulted in the isolation of a new flavone glycoside (5) alongside six known compounds (1-4, 6, and 7). The new compound was identified as 3'-hydroxy-7,4'-dimethoxyflavone-5-O-α-L-arabinofuranosyl-(1→6)-β-D-glucopyranoside using 1D and 2D NMR and MS analyses. To evaluate cytoprotective effects, the 7 isolates (1-7) were tested against hydrogen peroxide-induced damage in primary cultured hepatocytes. Treatment with each compound increased hepatocyte viability, with the exception of compound 1. Specifically, compounds 3, 4, and 7 exerted cytoprotective effects comparable to the positive control, curcumin. These constituents may contribute to the traditional use of Canarium bengalense in treating liver damage and tumors.
