Canarium bengalense

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canarium bengalense has an erect tree habit characterized by a straight trunk, grayish bark, and pinnate leaves that typically consist of five to nine leaflets. The leaves are arranged alternately and feature elliptical or oblong leaflets with entire margins and acute tips. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in axillary panicles, often appearing in yellowish or greenish hues. The plant produces fleshy, drupe-like fruits that are somewhat ovoid or ellipsoidal in shape. These fruits contain a single hard seed which is encased within a fibrous or leathery endocarp.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, including parts of India, Bangladesh, Myanmar, and Thailand. It typically thrives in lowland areas and along the edges of deciduous forests. The species is often found in moist environments such as riverbanks and seasonally flooded plains. Its distribution is closely tied to the availability of warm, humid climates characteristic of these latitudes. Local ecosystems within these regions rely on its presence for various ecological functions.

    What are the herbal preparations of Canarium bengalense?

    Canarium bengalense is prepared as decoctions, infusions, pastes, oils, and powders derived from its various parts like the leaves, bark, and resin. The resin, often called dammar, is frequently extracted to create medicinal oils or used in topical applications for skin ailments. Leaves are commonly boiled in water to produce a decoction used for treating internal inflammatory conditions. The bark can be processed into a fine powder that is mixed with honey or water for therapeutic ingestion. These different forms allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be utilized for both systemic and localized treatments.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Canarium bengalense can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if handled or ingested improperly. The plant contains various phytochemicals and resins that may trigger contact dermatitis upon direct skin exposure. Some individuals might experience redness, itching, or swelling if the sap touches sensitive areas. If consumed in large quantities or in an unregulated manner, it could lead to stomach upset or nausea. Always exercise caution and consult a professional before attempting to use any part of this species for medicinal purposes.
