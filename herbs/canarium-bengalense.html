Canarium bengalense

What's the taxonomical classification of Canarium bengalense?

Canarium bengalense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Sapindales and the family Burseraceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Canarium, and its unique species designation, bengalense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canarium bengalense has an erect tree habit characterized by a straight trunk, grayish bark, and pinnate leaves that typically consist of five to nine leaflets. The leaves are arranged alternately and feature elliptical or oblong leaflets with entire margins and acute tips. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in axillary panicles, often appearing in yellowish or greenish hues. The plant produces fleshy, drupe-like fruits that are somewhat ovoid or ellipsoidal in shape. These fruits contain a single hard seed which is encased within a fibrous or leathery endocarp.

What are the pharmacological activities of Canarium bengalense?

This plant has cytoprotective pharmacological activity. This specific action means that the plant contains compounds capable of protecting cells from damage caused by various external stressors. Such biological properties are often studied for their potential to maintain cellular integrity and prevent cell death. By shielding cells, the plant may offer therapeutic benefits in contexts where cellular injury is a primary concern. Therefore, the cytoprotective nature of Canarium bengalense makes it a subject of interest in pharmacological research.

According to a study published by "Archives of pharmacal research", phytochemical investigation of the stem bark of Canarium bengalense resulted in the isolation of 1 new flavone glycoside (5) and 6 known compounds (1-4, 6, and 7). These 7 isolates were tested against hydrogen peroxide-induced damage in primary cultured hepatocytes to evaluate in vitro cytoprotective effects. Treatment with each compound increased hepatocyte viability, with the exception of compound 1. Specifically, compounds 3, 4, and 7 exerted cytoprotective effects comparable to the positive control, curcumin. These findings suggest that the cytoprotective constituents of Canarium bengalense may contribute to its traditional use in treating tumor and liver damage.
