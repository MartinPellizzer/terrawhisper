Alchemilla japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Alchemilla japonica?

Alchemilla japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant is placed in the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Ultimately, it is defined by its genus, Alchemilla, and its specific species designation, japonica.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Alchemilla japonica contains high concentrations of tannins, flavonoids, and triterpenoid saponins that provide its therapeutic properties. These chemical constituents work together to exhibit significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities in biological systems. The presence of phenolic compounds specifically aids in neutralizing free radicals and protecting cells from oxidative stress. Additionally, the tannins contribute to astringent effects that can be useful in treating various skin irritations. These diverse medicinal compounds make the plant a subject of interest for pharmacological research.

What are the therapeutic actions of Alchemilla japonica?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various gastrointestinal ailments such as diarrhea, dysentery, and stomach pains, as well as urinary tract infections and inflammatory conditions. Its high concentration of tannins provides potent astringent properties that help tighten tissues and reduce fluid loss. These medicinal qualities make it effective for managing excessive bleeding or bleeding gums. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory effects can assist in soothing minor skin irritations or internal swellings. Traditional practitioners often rely on its ability to calm digestive distress through these chemical compounds.

What are the herbal preparations of Alchemilla japonica?

Alchemilla japonica is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a concentrated tincture to extract its active phytochemical components. The dried aerial parts of the plant are often boiled in water to create a potent decoction used for internal therapeutic purposes. Alternatively, steeping the herb in hot water for a shorter period produces a milder infusion suitable for daily consumption. For more concentrated medicinal applications, the plant material is soaked in alcohol to create a tincture that provides a long shelf life. These various forms allow practitioners to tailor the dosage and strength based on the specific health objective being addressed.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Alchemilla japonica can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested or applied incorrectly. While the plant is often used in traditional practices, individual sensitivity to its chemical compounds remains a primary concern for users. Contact with the foliage may lead to localized redness or itching for those with sensitive skin. There is limited clinical data regarding the long-term toxicity of this species in humans. Therefore, caution is advised when using any part of the plant for topical or internal purposes.
