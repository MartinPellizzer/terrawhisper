Scientific and Botanical Profile

Cochlearia, with botanical name Cochlearia officinalis, is a member of the Brassicaceae family, commonly known as the mustard or crucifer family.

Native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, this plant is widely distributed across temperate regions. It is also referred to as Common Mouse-Ear, Mouse-Ear Chickweed, Yellow Bittercress, Yellow Sandwort, Common Pennywort, Common Groundsel, Common Sandparsley, Yellow Buttercup, Common Bittercress, Common Sandwort, Common Cochlearia, Officinal Pennywort, and Sandworts. Morphologically, Cochlearia officinalis is a low-growing perennial herb with small, fleshy, spoon-shaped leaves and yellow flowers arranged in clusters, often with a distinctive heart-shaped cotyledon.

It typically grows in moist, calcareous soils and is often found in grasslands, woodland edges, and disturbed habitats.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Cochlearia officinalis has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant for a longer period. It can also be used topically as a poultice or diluted essential oil for skin application. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

Due to limited scientific evidence, it is advisable to use cochlearia officinalis sparingly and for short durations, avoiding prolonged or frequent use without professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Cochlearia officinalis can be used as a medicinal plant with potential benefits, but it must be approached with caution due to its possible side effects and interactions.

While generally considered safe in moderate doses, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea or vomiting, in some individuals. It is important to note that cochlearia officinalis may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the nervous system or blood pressure, and should be used cautiously with other herbs like valerian or passionflower. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid its use due to limited safety data, and it is contraindicated in individuals with chronic illnesses such as diabetes or hypertension without medical supervision.

To ensure safe use, consult a healthcare professional before incorporating cochlearia officinalis into your regimen, and follow recommended dosages to minimize risks.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Cochlearia officinalis grows in cool, temperate climates, preferring well-drained, moist soil rich in organic matter, and partial to full sun exposure.

It thrives in slightly acidic to neutral pH levels and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without becoming waterlogged. To ensure healthy growth, it should be planted in early spring, with spacing to allow for air circulation and reduce the risk of disease. Harvesting is best done in late spring to early summer when the leaves are fully developed and the plant is at its peak potency, using clean, sharp tools to cut the stems just above the soil line.

After harvesting, the plant should be dried in a cool, dark, well-ventilated area to preserve its medicinal compounds, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place to maintain potency and prevent degradation.
