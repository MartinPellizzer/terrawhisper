Schima wallichii

What's the taxonomical classification of Schima wallichii?

Schima wallichii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Ericales and the family Theaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by the genus Schima and is specifically designated as the species wallichii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Schima wallichii has simple, alternate, elliptical to oblong leaves that are leathery in texture and possess serrated margins with a lustrous dark green upper surface. The foliage often displays a distinctively pale underside, which provides a subtle color contrast to the dense canopy. Its woody structure typically forms a medium to large tree characterized by a straight trunk and spreading branches. During the flowering season, the plant produces clusters of white, five-petaled flowers that emerge from the axils of the leaves. These blossoms are accompanied by small, globose capsules that eventually split open to reveal seeds.

What are the pharmacological activities of Schima wallichii?

This plant has anti-proliferative, anti-diabetic, anti-hypertensive, cytotoxic, and osteoregenerative pharmacological activities. Its cytotoxic and anti-proliferative properties suggest potential applications in inhibiting the growth of various cancer cell lines. The extract also demonstrates significant anti-diabetic effects by helping to regulate blood glucose levels. Additionally, its anti-hypertensive qualities contribute to the management of high blood pressure in medicinal contexts. Finally, the plant exhibits osteoregenerative capabilities that may assist in the repair and formation of bone tissue.

As discussed in a study, Schima wallichii leaves possess a crude extract and solvent fractions that exhibit dose-dependent inhibitory effects on the proliferation of HepG2 hepatocellular carcinoma cells. A major flavonoid isolated from the most active fraction, kaempferol-3-O-rhamnoside, effectively suppresses HepG2 cell growth and induces apoptosis by activating caspase-3. In silico analyses showed this compound binds to apoptosis-related targets including caspase-3, caspase-8, caspase-9, and PARP-1. Molecular dynamics simulations confirmed the structural stability of the kaempferol-3-O-rhamnoside-caspase 3 complex throughout a 100-ns trajectory. Density functional theory calculations indicated a moderate HOMO-LUMO energy gap of 6.48 eV and a global electrophilicity index of ω = 3.18 eV.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including alkaloids, kaempferol-3-O-rhamnoside, saponins, tannin, and glycosides. These bioactive molecules work together to provide various therapeutic benefits to the human body. The presence of alkaloids and saponins often contributes to the plant's potent biological activities and defense mechanisms. Additionally, the glycosides and kaempferol-3-O-rhamnoside play significant roles in modulating cellular responses. Tannin further enhances the chemical complexity by providing astringent and antioxidant properties.

As discussed in a study, Schima wallichii is used in traditional Mizo medicine for human helmintiasis and as a balm against ectoparasites in animals. Chemical analysis of bark extracts using petroleum ether, chloroform, and methanol showed that sterols were the most abundant compounds in all extracts. The methanol extract yielded the highest concentrations of alkaloids, carbohydrates, glycosides, sterols, saponins, and tannins. Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry identified cholest-22-ene-21-ol as the major steroid component. Phytosterols in the bark are responsible for the plant's anthelmintic properties.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating malaria caused by Plasmodium falciparum, diabetes, hypertension, and liver cancer. Traditional practitioners utilize its various extracts to regulate blood glucose levels in patients suffering from diabetes. The medicinal properties of the plant also assist in managing high blood pressure to combat hypertension. Additionally, research explores its potential bioactive compounds in inhibiting the progression of liver cancer cells. These diverse applications demonstrate the plant's significant role in managing several chronic and infectious diseases.

As discussed in a study, the ethanol extract of Schima wallichii was fractionated based on polarity using n-hexane, ethyl acetate and water. The antiplasmodial activity of Schima wallichii was tested in vitro against chloroquine-resistant Plasmodium falciparum at 100 μg/ml for 72 h. At 100 μg/ml, the ethanol extract of Schima wallichii exhibited a significant parasite shrinkage after 24 h of treatment. The ethyl acetate fraction of the plant at 100 μg/ml was the most active fraction against chloroquine-resistant Plasmodium falciparum. The major compound isolated from this ethyl acetate fraction, kaempferol-3-O-rhamnoside, showed promising antiplasmodial activity against chloroquine-resistant Plasmodium falciparum with an IC50 of 106 μM after 24 h of treatment.
