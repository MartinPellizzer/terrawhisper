Triadica rotundifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. This plant is a member of the order Malpighiales and falls within the family Euphorbiaceae. At the most specific taxonomic levels, it is identified by the genus Triadica and the species name rotundifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Triadica rotundifolia has simple, alternate, ovate to elliptical leaves with entire margins and a lustrous, dark green upper surface. The foliage typically measures between two and six inches in length and features a prominent midrib. During the growing season, the plant develops small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually transition into fleshy, globose fruits. These berries are dark purple or black when ripe and serve as a primary characteristic of the species. The woody stems exhibit a spreading growth habit, often forming dense, shrubby thickets.

What are the pharmacological activities of Triadica rotundifolia?

This plant has significant pharmacological potential due to its potent anti-neuroinflammatory properties that help mitigate damage within the central nervous system. Researchers have identified specific bioactive compounds within its leaves and extracts that modulate inflammatory pathways in the brain. These mechanisms work by reducing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines that contribute to neurodegeneration. Additionally, the plant exhibits antioxidant effects that support overall neuronal health and protection. Such activities suggest that Triadica rotundifolia could serve as a valuable source for developing new treatments for cognitive disorders.

According to a study published by "Bioorganic chemistry", the phytochemical investigation of Triadica rotundifolia resulted in the discovery of eight new casbane diterpenoid derivatives. Compound 1 (6S,12R-dihydroxy-1S,2R-casba-3E,7E,10E-trien-5-one) exhibited potent inhibition of NO production in LPS-stimulated microglia. This specific compound markedly reduced pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion and induced microglial polarization from M1 to M2 states. Furthermore, compound 1 disrupted the NF-κB signaling cascade by blocking both IκBα phosphorylation and subsequent p65 nuclear translocation in LPS-stimulated cells. These findings demonstrate consistent neuroprotective efficacy across both cellular and animal models.
