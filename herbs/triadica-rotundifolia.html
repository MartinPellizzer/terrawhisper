Triadica rotundifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Triadica rotundifolia?

Triadica rotundifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the biological hierarchy, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Malpighiales and falls within the family Euphorbiaceae. At the most specific taxonomic levels, it is identified by the genus Triadica and the species name rotundifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Triadica rotundifolia has simple, alternate, ovate to elliptical leaves with entire margins and a lustrous, dark green upper surface. The foliage typically measures between two and six inches in length and features a prominent midrib. During the growing season, the plant develops small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually transition into fleshy, globose fruits. These berries are dark purple or black when ripe and serve as a primary characteristic of the species. The woody stems exhibit a spreading growth habit, often forming dense, shrubby thickets.

What are the pharmacological activities of Triadica rotundifolia?

This plant has potent anti-neuroinflammatory properties that serve to protect the central nervous system from oxidative damage and cellular dysfunction. These pharmacological activities are largely attributed to its high concentration of bioactive polyphenolic compounds and flavonoids. By modulating various signaling pathways, the plant helps reduce the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines in brain tissue. This specific action makes it a significant subject of interest for research into neurodegenerative diseases. Such therapeutic potential highlights its value in developing natural interventions for cognitive health.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical investigation of Triadica rotundifolia resulted in the discovery of eight new casbane diterpenoid derivatives. Specifically, compound 1 (6S,12R-dihydroxy-1S,2R-casba-3E,7E,10E-trien-5-one) exhibited potent inhibition of NO production in LPS-stimulated microglia. This compound markedly reduced pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion and induced microglial polarization from M1 to M2 states. Furthermore, compound 1 disrupted the NF-κB signaling cascade by blocking both IκBα phosphorylation and subsequent p65 nuclear translocation in LPS-stimulated cells. This research represents the first report of casbane diterpenoids from T. rotundifolia and the first example of anti-neuroinflammatory effects from these terrestrial plant sources.
