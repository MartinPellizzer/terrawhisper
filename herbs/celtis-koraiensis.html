Celtis koraiensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Celtis koraiensis?

Celtis koraiensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it falls into the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its higher taxonomic ranks, the plant is part of the order Rosales and the family Cannabaceae. Finally, it is categorized under the genus Celtis, with its specific designation being the species koraiensis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Celtis koraiensis contains a complex profile of bioactive secondary metabolites including alkaloids, flavonoids, tannins, and saponins. These chemical constituents contribute to the plant's documented pharmacological activities such as antioxidant and antimicrobial properties. Researchers focus on the specific phenolic compounds within the leaves and bark which may provide therapeutic benefits for inflammatory conditions. The presence of various terpenoids further suggests potential medicinal applications in traditional healing practices. Detailed phytochemical screening remains essential to fully map the synergy of these individual compounds.

What are the therapeutic actions of Celtis koraiensis?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, stomach ailments, respiratory issues, and inflammatory conditions. The bark and leaves are often prepared into decoctions or poultices to address various localized ailments. Traditional healers utilize these extracts to soothe gastrointestinal discomfort and alleviate symptoms of indigestion. Furthermore, the plant serves as a remedy for wound healing and the management of topical skin irritations. Its medicinal application relies on the bioactive compounds found within its botanical structures.

What are the herbal preparations of Celtis koraiensis?

Celtis koraiennis is prepared as a decoction from its bark, a leaf infusion, or a topical paste using the crushed foliage. The bark is typically boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for internal medicinal purposes. Leaf infusions are often brewed to soothe digestive issues or to alleviate minor inflammatory conditions. For external ailments, the fresh leaves are ground into a thick paste and applied directly to the skin as a poultice. These varied preparations allow different parts of the plant to target specific physiological needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Celtris koraiensis can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis or localized itching if the sap or leaves come into direct contact with sensitive skin. While comprehensive clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, potential irritants within the plant's chemical composition may trigger redness or swelling in some individuals. Some people might also experience gastrointestinal discomfort if any part of the plant is ingested accidentally. It is important to exercise caution and avoid prolonged exposure to the plant's tissues to prevent these adverse reactions. Always consult a medical professional if you suspect a reaction has occurred after contact.
