Paspalum distichum

What's the taxonomical classification of Paspalum distichum?

Paspalum distichum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Poales, it falls into the family Poaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Paspalum and the species distichum.

Paspalum distichum contains no widely documented or clinically proven medicinal compounds for human therapeutic use in modern pharmacology. What are the therapeutic actions of Paspalum distichum?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin infections, fungal ailments, and inflammatory conditions through its medicinal properties. Traditional healers often apply crushed leaves or infusions to soothe localized irritations and rashes. The plant contains bioactive compounds that help manage minor wounds and prevent the spread of certain microbes. It is frequently utilized in folk medicine to address digestive upsets and respiratory discomfort. These applications rely on the plant's natural ability to reduce swelling and combat pathogens.

What are the herbal preparations of Paspalum distichum?

Paspalum distichum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice for various medicinal applications. The dried leaves and stems are often boiled in water to extract soluble compounds for internal consumption. For external skin conditions, a concentrated liquid extract can be applied directly to affected areas. Some practitioners also crush the fresh plant material into a mash to create a moist poultice. These different methods allow for the targeted delivery of the plant's bioactive constituents depending on the specific ailment being treated.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Paspalum distichum can cause allergic contact dermatitis or skin irritation in individuals with hypersensitivity to specific pollen or plant proteins. While it is generally considered non-toxic to humans, direct contact with the foliage may trigger redness or itching on sensitive skin. People with seasonal allergies might experience respiratory irritation or sneezing if they inhale the pollen produced by the plant. There are no documented cases of systemic poisoning from ingestion, but any unknown plant material should be consumed with extreme caution. Monitoring for local skin reactions is the most practical way to manage potential sensitivities to this grass species.
