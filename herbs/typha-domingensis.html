Typha domingensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Typha domingensis?

Typha domingensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Poales. More specifically, this plant is a member of the family Typhaceae and falls under the genus Typha, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species domingensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Typha domingensis has cylindrical, brownish-to-tan flowering spikes that typically measure between 10 and 30 centimeters in length and are supported by upright, linear leaves. The plant features long, sword-shaped leaves that are often slightly channeled or folded along the midrib to facilitate water runoff. These leaves emerge from a creeping rhizomatous root system that allows the species to spread effectively through wetland substrates. The terminal spike consists of a dense cluster of small, densely packed flowers that eventually develop into a characteristic fuzzy texture. During the maturation process, the spike may exhibit a distinct separation between the female and male floral zones.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds kaempferol, formononetin 7-O-glucosid-6''-O-malonate, sitosterol, stigmasterol, and gallic acid. These specific bioactive molecules contribute to the diverse therapeutic profile of the species. The presence of sterols like sitosterol and stigmasterol often supports anti-inflammatory and membrane-stabilizing activities. Additionally, the flavonoid kaempferol and the organic acid gallic acid provide potent antioxidant properties. Together, these constituents offer a complex chemical foundation for potential pharmacological applications.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in chemistry", the methanolic extract derived from the entire plant, TDME, contains specific polyphenols including p-coumaric acid at 207.47 μg/g extract, gallic acid at 96.25 μg/g extract, and kaempferol at 95.78 μg/g extract. The enzyme inhibition assays revealed IC50 values for TDME of 44.75 ± 0.51, 52.71 ± 0.01, and 67.19 ± 0.68 µgmL-1 for lipoxygenase, α-glucosidase, and urease, respectively. In silico molecular docking revealed that 1,3,4,5-tetracaffeoylquinic acid and formononetin 7-O-glucoside-6″-O-malonate possibly contribute to enzyme inhibitory activities. Safety assessments showed an in vitro hemolysis of 4.25% ± 0.16% and were further confirmed in an in vivo model of rats up to 10 g/kg. These bioactive compounds allow TDME to demonstrate significant (p < 0.05) potential in analgesic and anti-inflammatory activities.
