Ficus auriculata

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus auriculata?

Ficus auriculata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant falls under the order Rosales and the family Moraceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Ficus, with the specific epithet auriculata, identifying it as the distinct species Ficus auriculata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus auriculata has large, broadly ovate leaves characterized by prominent auriculate bases, a woody stem, and a specialized reproductive structure known as a syconium. The foliage is typically dark green with distinct venation and can reach significant sizes depending on the environmental conditions. Its growth habit often resembles a small tree or large shrub with a spreading canopy. The fruit consists of a fleshy, hollow receptacle containing numerous tiny flowers enclosed within. Small, milky latex is exuded from the plant when the leaves or stems are broken.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, including India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and Malaysia. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland forests and along riverbanks where humidity levels remain consistently high. Within these habitats, the species can be found growing as an epiphyte or a hemi-epiphyte on larger host trees. Its range extends across diverse ecosystems ranging from coastal mangroves to dense inland rainforests. This broad distribution is supported by its ability to adapt to various shaded understory environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ficus auriculata?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-alpha-glucosidase, antibacterial, antioxidant, hepatoprotective, and hypolipidemic properties. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize harmful free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. The antibacterial effects allow the plant extracts to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, its hypolipidemic actions assist in managing cholesterol levels while the anti-alpha-glucosidase activity aids in regulating blood glucose. Finally, the plant demonstrates hepatoprotective qualities that help safeguard the liver from chemical or toxic injuries.

As discussed in a study, the 70% ethanolic extract of Ficus auriculata contributed to a total phenolic content ranging from 51 to 344 mg gallic acid equivalent (GAE)/g dry weight. This plant exhibited a high level of inhibition against the α-glucosidase enzyme activity. Specifically, the extract of Ficus auriculata showed an IC50 value of 0.36 μg/mL. These results demonstrate its potential as a natural source of α-glucosidase inhibitors.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several potent medicinal compounds including alpinumisoflavone (4), ficuisoflavone (3), ficusine D, ficusines A-C, and 6-oxolasiodiplodin. Research into these substances often focuses on their biological activities and interactions within cellular systems. The presence of these isoflavones and related structures suggests significant pharmacological interest. Understanding the concentration of these molecules helps scientists determine the plant's medicinal efficacy.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Ficus auriculata contain a new isoflavone named 5,7,4'-trihydroxy-3'-hydroxymethylisoflavone (1). This rare isoflavone (1) possesses a carbon skeleton consisting of 16 carbon atoms. Additionally, three known isoflavones were isolated, which are 3'-formyl-5,4'-dihydroxy-7-methoxyisoflavone (2), ficuisoflavone (3), and alpinumisoflavone (4). These four compounds (1-4) were evaluated for antibacterial activities against 5 terrestrial pathogenic bacteria in vitro. The compounds demonstrated significant antibacterial activities with MIC values ranging from 1.30 to 39.93 μM.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, redness, and swelling if its sap comes into contact with the body. The milky latex found within the stems and leaves is known to be a skin irritant for many people. If this sap is accidentally ingested, it may lead to gastrointestinal distress or other internal discomfort. Eye contact with the plant's fluids can also result in significant irritation or inflammation of the mucous membranes. It is important to use protective gear when handling any part of the specimen to prevent these adverse reactions.
