What's the taxonomical classification of Argyreia wallichii?

Argyreiia wallichii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized under the order Solanales. It falls within the family Convolvulaceae, specifically placed in the genus Argyreiia, with its unique biological identity defined by its species name, wallichii.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various types of skin conditions including eczema, psoriasis, and dermatitis. It also possesses antimicrobial properties that help combat localized bacterial and fungal infections. Many traditional practitioners apply the plant to soothe inflammation and reduce redness in irritated skin tissue. Additionally, its medicinal components are sometimes utilized to aid in the healing of minor wounds and superficial cuts. The plant's application is primarily focused on topical dermatological relief.

What are the herbal preparations of Argyreia wallichii?

Argyreia wallichii is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste depending on the specific therapeutic goal. The dried roots and leaves are commonly boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal consumption. In some traditional practices, the plant material is ground into a fine powder and mixed with liquids to form a concentrated solution. For external skin ailments, the fresh leaves are crushed into a poultice and applied directly to the affected area. These various methods allow the bioactive compounds of the plant to be delivered effectively through different administration routes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Argyreia wallichii can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled without protection. The plant contains various phytochemicals that may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals through direct skin contact. Ingesting any part of the vine may lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain due to its unknown toxicity levels in humans. While specific clinical studies on its systemic toxicity are limited, caution is advised when working with the foliage. Always use gloves and avoid accidental consumption to minimize these potential health risks.
