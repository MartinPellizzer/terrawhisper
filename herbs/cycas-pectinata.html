Cycas pectinata

What's the taxonomical classification of Cycas pectinata?

Cycas pectinata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Cycadidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Cycadales and is a member of the family Cycadaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Cycas and is specifically designated as the species pectinata.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cycas pectinata contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its seeds, roots, and leaf bases. The seeds are often processed to extract substances used in traditional remedies for treating various ailments. Root extracts are sometimes utilized in folk medicine to address digestive issues or skin conditions. Additionally, the starch derived from the trunk or base of the plant serves as a foundational material for certain herbal preparations. These various parts are carefully prepared to mitigate the natural toxicity inherent in the Cycas genus.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Cycas pectinata targets cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to mediate its potent anti-inflammatory and analgesic activities. These bioactive compounds interact with these specific enzymes to inhibit the synthesis of arachidonic acid derivatives like prostaglandins. By suppressing these inflammatory mediators, the plant's phytochemicals effectively reduce swelling and pain at the cellular level. Additionally, certain extracts may interact with oxidative stress-related proteins to provide antioxidant protection. This multi-target approach allows the plant to address complex pathological processes through diverse biochemical pathways.

    What are the herbal preparations of Cycas pectinata?

    Cycas pectinata is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a juice extracted from its various parts including the seeds, leaves, and roots. The seeds are often boiled in water to create a medicinal decoction used for specific therapeutic purposes. In many traditional practices, the plant material is dried and ground into a fine powder to be administered orally or applied topically. The fresh juice can be extracted directly from the fleshy parts of the plant for immediate use. These different preparation methods allow for the controlled administration of the plant's active compounds depending on the desired effect.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Cycas pectinata can cause severe liver damage, vomiting, abdominal pain, and death if any part of the plant is ingested. The plant contains potent neurotoxins and carcinogens known as cycasin and BMAA that target the central nervous system and metabolic organs. Ingestion of even small amounts can lead to acute hepatic necrosis or progressive neurological impairment. These toxins are most concentrated in the seeds and the fleshy outer coating of the fruit. Because the symptoms can be delayed, accidental poisoning often goes unnoticed until significant physiological damage has already occurred.
