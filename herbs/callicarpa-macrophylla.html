Callicarpa macrophylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Callicarpa macrophylla?

Callicarpa macrophylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Lamiales. It is a member of the family Lamiaceae, which contains the genus Callicarpa, and is specifically identified by the species name macrophylla.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Callicarpa macrophylla contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its leaves, stems, and fruits. The fruits can be processed to extract substances that may assist in regulating physiological functions.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Callicarpa macrophylla targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) and inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) through its bioactive terpenoids and polyphenolic compounds. The plant's secondary metabolites effectively inhibit these proteins to suppress the production of pro-inflammatory mediators like prostaglandin E2 and nitric oxide. Additionally, certain extracts interact with nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) signaling pathways to downregulate the expression of various cytokine-related proteins. These molecular interactions contribute to the plant's observed antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory medicinal properties. By modulating these specific enzymatic targets, the plant helps regulate cellular responses to oxidative stress and infection.

Callicarpa macrophylla has medicinal properties that include anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and analgesic effects primarily derived from its leaves and bark.     What are the herbal preparations of Callicarpa macrophylla?

    Callicarpa macrophylla is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or topical pastes to utilize its various medicinal properties. The leaves and stems are commonly boiled in water to create a liquid extract used for internal consumption. For external applications, the plant material is often crushed into a fine pulp to be applied directly to the skin as a poultice. Some traditional practitioners also use the dried plant parts to make concentrated tinctures through alcohol extraction. These different preparations allow for the targeted delivery of the plant's bioactive compounds depending on the ailment being treated.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Callicarpa macrophylla can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or touched directly. While many species in this genus are used in traditional medicine, the chemical constituents in this specific plant may trigger localized redness or itching upon contact with sensitive skin. Ingestion of the leaves or berries can lead to nausea, stomach cramps, or vomiting in susceptible individuals. Because scientific studies on human toxicity are limited, any potential reaction should be treated with caution. It is important to avoid direct contact with the sap and foliage to prevent unexpected physiological responses.
