Ficus beecheyana

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus beecheyana has a large, woody habit characterized by a spreading crown and a trunk often adorned with numerous adventitious aerial roots. The leaves are coriaceous, elliptical to oblong, and possess an acute apex with an entire margin. These foliage structures are arranged in an alternate pattern along the branches and feature a lustrous, dark green upper surface. The venation is prominent, with secondary veins extending toward the margins in a pinnate fashion. Small, inconspicuous figs serve as the reproductive structures, typically appearing in the leaf axils.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

Its range extends across various states in India, often clustering in areas with well-drained soils and moderate rainfall. The species thrives in diverse altitudes, ranging from lowland plains to mid-elevation hilly terrains. Within these habitats, it often grows near water bodies or in shaded forest understories.

Ficus beecheyana has medicinal properties that include astringent, antidiarrheal, antipyretic, anti-inflammatory, and haemostatic actions.     What are the herbal preparations of Ficus beecheyana?

    Ficus beecheyana is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a paste using various parts of the plant including the bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal decoction used for treating specific ailments. Ground leaves are often processed into a fine powder that can be mixed with honey or water for internal consumption. In many traditional applications, the roots are crushed into a thick paste and applied topically to the skin. These diverse preparations allow for different methods of absorption depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Ficus beecheyana can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and digestive distress if the sap or parts of the plant are ingested or touched. The milky latex produced by the plant contains irritants that may lead to redness, itching, or swelling upon contact with sensitive skin. If these fluids enter the eyes, they can cause significant stinging or inflammation. Ingestion of the plant material is generally discouraged as it may lead to nausea or abdominal discomfort. While specific toxicological studies on humans are limited, caution should be exercised when handling any member of the Ficus genus.
