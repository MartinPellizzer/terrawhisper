Botanical Identification of Dogwood (Cornus florida)

To identify Dogwood (Cornus florida) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Dogwood, or Cornus florida, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Saxifragales, family Cornaceae, genus Cornus, and species Cornus florida. This classification places it within the flowering plants, specifically in the family that includes other dogwoods and related species.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Dogwood is Cornus florida, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Cornus florida L. and Cornus americana. Common names include American Dogwood, Corn Dogwood, and White Dogwood. Regional and historical names vary, with some Native American tribes referring to it as "red root" or "white flower."

Morphological Characteristics

Dogwood (Cornus florida) is a deciduous shrub or small tree with a rounded growth habit. It has a shallow, fibrous root system that spreads horizontally. The stem is typically smooth and grayish-brown, with a tendency to branch near the base. Leaves are opposite, ovate, and have serrated edges, turning red in the fall. The inflorescence is a flat-topped cluster of white or pink bracts, which resemble flowers but are actually modified leaves. The true flowers are small and green, located at the base of the bracts. The fruit is a red drupe that matures in the fall, containing a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cornus florida can be distinguished by its opposite, oval leaves with serrated edges, white bract-like flowers that appear before leaves, and red, berry-like fruits. It differs from similar species like Cornus sericea by its larger leaves and more showy flowers. The bark is smooth and gray, and the plant typically grows in moist woodlands.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cornus florida is native to eastern North America, ranging from southern New England to Florida and westward to Texas and Missouri. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall and prefers moist, well-drained soils. It is commonly found at elevations between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Dogwood (Cornus florida) blooms in late spring to early summer, typically from May to June. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around July to August. For identification purposes, the best time to observe key features is during the blooming period, which aligns with its peak visual identification stage.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cornus florida belongs to the genus Cornus, which is part of the family Cornaceae. It is most closely related to species like Cornus sericea and Cornus sanguinea. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, such as opposite leaf arrangement and drupe fruit structure. The family Cornaceae includes only a few genera, making Cornus florida a distinctive yet closely related member.
