Kochia scoparia

What's the taxonomical classification of Kochia scoparia?

Kochia scoparia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is placed in the order Caryophyllales. It is a member of the family Amaranthaceae, falling under the genus Kochia, and is specifically identified by its species name, scoparia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Kochia scoparia has an erect, annual herbaceous habit characterized by slender, branching stems that can reach heights of up to two meters. The leaves are small, linear to lanceolate in shape, and arranged alternately along the stem. These leaves typically possess a smooth margin and a dull green color that may shift toward reddish hues as the plant matures. The inflorescence consists of small, inconspicuous flowers clustered in axillary panicles. As the plant reaches its final life stage, the foliage often develops a distinctive reddish or purplish tint.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

Some populations are also widely distributed throughout Southeast Asia and the Pacific islands. The species frequently colonizes disturbed areas, roadsides, and open grasslands within these warm climates. What are the pharmacological activities of Kochia scoparia?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-ulcer, anti-cancer, tumoricidal, and antiparasitic pharmacological activities that demonstrate its significant therapeutic potential. Its anti-inflammatory and anti-ulcer properties help mitigate tissue damage and gastric distress in various biological models. Research also indicates that the plant exhibits potent anti-cancer and tumoricidal effects by inhibiting the growth and proliferation of malignant cells. Furthermore, its antiparasitic capabilities provide a natural defense against various parasitic infections. These diverse medicinal properties suggest that the plant serves as a valuable source for developing new pharmaceutical agents.

As discussed in a study, the mature fruit of Kochia scoparia is widely administered in China and Korea for the treatment of skin diseases, diabetes mellitus, and rheumatoid arthritis. Methanol extracts of K. scoparia dried fruit (MEKS) were investigated for their effects on ear swelling, histopathological changes like epidermal acanthosis, spongiosis, and immune cell infiltration, and cytokine production in 1-fluoro-2,4-dinitrofluorobenzene (DNFB)-induced contact dermatitis mice. Topical application of MEKS inhibited DNFB-induced ear thickness and weight increases, as well as DNFB-induced epidermal acanthosis, spongiosis, and immune cell infiltration. Treatment with MEKS significantly decreased the levels of tumor necrosis factor-alpha, interferon-gamma, and monocyte chemotactic protein-1 in inflamed tissues. These data indicate that the mature fruit of K. scoparia has the potential for treating inflammatory skin diseases through the inhibition of type 1 T helper cell skewing reactions.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including momordin Ic, oleanolic acid glycosides, and various forms of saponin. These specific phytochemicals are significant due to their potential biological activities in pharmacological studies. The plant is characterized by a high concentration of total saponin within its tissues. Different types of saponins contribute to the overall medicinal profile of the species. Researchers often study these oleanolic acid glycosides to understand their therapeutic properties.

As discussed in a study, the content of momordin Ic and total saponin was determined in Kochia scoparia fruits from 11 producing areas. Researchers used HPLC-ELSD and a colorimetric method to measure these medicinal compounds. Among the 11 kinds of Kochia scoparia fruits tested, those from Bozhou, Baoding Anguo, and Heilongjiang contained more saponins. The study found that the content of momordin Ic in Kochia scoparia fruits was related to that of total saponin. Variations in saponin levels across these different areas suggest that the environment affects the quality of these herbs.
