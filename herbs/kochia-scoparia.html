Kochia scoparia

What's the taxonomical classification of Kochia scoparia?

Kochia scoparia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is placed in the order Caryophyllales. It is a member of the family Amaranthaceae, falling under the genus Kochia, and is specifically identified by its species name, scoparia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Kochia scoparia has an erect, annual herbaceous habit characterized by slender, branching stems that can reach heights of up to two meters. The leaves are small, linear to lanceolate in shape, and arranged alternately along the stem. These leaves typically possess a smooth margin and a dull green color that may shift toward reddish hues as the plant matures. The inflorescence consists of small, inconspicuous flowers clustered in axillary panicles. As the plant reaches its final life stage, the foliage often develops a distinctive reddish or purplish tint.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including momordin Ic, oleanolic acid glycosides, and various types of saponin. These specific saponins are known to contribute to the biological activity and therapeutic potential of the species. Researchers often measure the total saponin content to evaluate the overall medicinal potency of the extract. The presence of oleanolic acid glycosides further enhances the chemical profile of the plant. Such diverse phytochemicals make this plant a subject of significant interest in pharmacological studies.

As discussed in a study, the content of momordin Ic and total saponin in Kochia scoparia fruits varies across eleven different producing areas. Researchers used HPLC-ELSD and a colorimetric method to determine these specific medicinal compounds. Among the eleven kinds of Kochia scoparia fruits tested, the highest saponin contents were found in those produced in Bozhou, Baoding Anguo, and Heilongjiang. The study indicates that the content of momordin Ic in these fruits is directly related to the total saponin content. Because the amount of saponin in Kochia scoparia fruits from various areas is different, environmental effects must be considered regarding herb quality.
