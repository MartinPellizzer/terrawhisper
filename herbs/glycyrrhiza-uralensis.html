Licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Licorice, or Glycyrrhiza uralensis, is a widely used herb in traditional medicine known for its potent anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and expectorant properties.

What is Licorice (Glycyrrhiza Uralensis)?

Licorice, or Glycyrrhiza uralensis, is a flowering plant native to regions of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East, widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its soothing and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Licorice is Glycyrrhiza uralensis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Glycyrrhiza, and species Glycyrrhiza uralensis. It is commonly known as licorice in Europe and America, and as gansheng in China. The synonyms for this plant include Glycyrrhiza glabra (botanical) and gansheng, licorice, and sweet root (regional).

Plant Description and Morphology

Licorice is a perennial herb that typically grows to a height of 30-100 cm, with a straight, cylindrical stem covered in fine, white hairs.

Its leaves are opposite, ovate to elliptical in shape, with serrated edges and a hairy upper surface. The flowers are small, pink to purple, and clustered in loose inflorescences at the top of the stem, each flower having five petals and a prominent central stamen. The root system consists of a thick, fleshy main root that branches into smaller lateral roots, which are the primary source of the medicinal and aromatic compounds.

The root is pale yellow to reddish brown, has a fibrous texture, and emits a sweet, intense licorice-like aroma.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Licorice is native to temperate regions of Asia, particularly China, the Middle East, and parts of southern Europe.

It thrives in areas with well-drained soils and a semi-arid to continental climate. The plant prefers moderate temperatures and adequate sunlight, though it can tolerate some shade. It is commonly found in grasslands, rocky slopes, and along riverbanks.

Its natural range extends from the Caucasus to the steppes of Central Asia.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots and rhizomes of Glycyrrhiza uralensis are primarily used medicinally.

These parts contain the active compound glycyrrhizin, which is responsible for many of the plant's therapeutic effects. The roots are typically harvested in the fall or early spring when the concentration of active constituents is highest. The rhizomes are often used in combination with the roots to enhance potency.

These plant parts are dried and processed into powders, tinctures, or decoctions for medicinal use.

Phytochemical Composition

Licorice contains a variety of active compounds, including triterpene saponins, which are responsible for its characteristic sweet taste.

Among these, glycyrrhizin is the most well-known and has potent anti-inflammatory and antiviral properties. Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids and isoflavones contribute to its therapeutic effects and antioxidant activity. Chemical markers like liquiritigenin and isoliquiritin are used for quality control and authentication of licorice products.

These compounds collectively determine the medicinal value and pharmacological profile of Glycyrrhiza uralensis.

Pharmacological Properties

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that underpin its use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and expectorant effects, making it valuable in respiratory and immune support. Biologically, licorice contains glycyrrhizin, a triterpene glycoside responsible for many of its active effects. Pharmacodynamically, glycyrrhizin inhibits the enzyme 11β-hydroxylase, reducing cortisol synthesis and influencing adrenal function.

System-level effects include modulation of the immune system, enhancement of mucosal protection, and potential anti-cancer properties through antioxidant and anti-proliferative mechanisms.

Mechanisms of Action

Licorice root exerts its effects through multiple physiological and biochemical pathways, primarily by modulating cellular responses and influencing key biochemical processes.

At the cellular level, it interacts with various receptors and ion channels, such as the mineralocorticoid receptor, leading to anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory effects. Biochemically, licorice contains compounds like glycyrrhizin, which inhibit enzymes like phospholipase A2 and cyclooxygenase, reducing inflammation and pain. These actions are mediated through the modulation of the renin-angiotensin system, affecting blood pressure and fluid balance.

Target systems include the respiratory, digestive, and nervous systems, where licorice exhibits antiviral, antacid, and calming properties.

Therapeutic Uses

Licorice root has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat a variety of conditions due to its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and expectorant properties.

It is commonly used to alleviate symptoms of respiratory infections such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma by helping to loosen mucus and reduce airway inflammation. Licorice also supports digestive health by soothing stomach ulcers and reducing nausea. Additionally, it is used to manage skin conditions like eczema and psoriasis due to its soothing and anti-inflammatory effects.

Its adaptogenic qualities make it useful in supporting the body's response to stress and fatigue.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) vary depending on its intended use, with common forms including raw root, powdered root, and decoctions.

The plant is frequently used in its dried root form, which can be sliced, ground, or chewed directly. It is also commonly processed into powder for oral consumption or incorporated into herbal formulations. Another popular method involves making a decoction by simmering the root in water to extract its active compounds.

Additionally, licorice is sometimes prepared as a tincture or extract for enhanced potency and convenience.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of licorice root for general use is typically 0.3 to 1 gram per day, depending on the form and preparation.

Extracts should be used at a concentration of no more than 1:5 (w/v) to avoid excessive intake of glycyrrhizin. It is recommended to take licorice root no more than three times daily to minimize potential side effects. The duration of use should not exceed four weeks unless under the guidance of a healthcare professional.

Prolonged or excessive use can lead to electrolyte imbalances and hypertension, so it is important to follow these guidelines carefully.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in appropriate doses, licorice is generally safe for most people, but it can cause side effects such as hypertension, fluid retention, and electrolyte imbalances, especially with prolonged use.

Long-term or excessive consumption may lead to pseudoaldosteronism, a condition resembling adrenal insufficiency, due to its mineralocorticoid-like effects. Licorice should be avoided by individuals with hypertension, heart failure, or kidney disease because it can exacerbate these conditions. Allergic reactions, though rare, may include skin rashes, itching, or more severe systemic responses in sensitive individuals.

During pregnancy, licorice is considered possibly unsafe due to limited research, and it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before use.

Drug Interactions

Licorice can interact with various medications, including corticosteroids, antihypertensive drugs, and diuretics, potentially leading to increased blood pressure, electrolyte imbalances, or reduced effectiveness of the medication.

Toxicity and Precautions

While licorice is generally safe when used in moderate amounts, excessive consumption can lead to adverse effects such as hypertension, electrolyte imbalances, and potential adrenal gland suppression, particularly in individuals with pre-existing health conditions.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil, requiring a growing season of at least 120 days, and is harvested primarily from the roots after 2-3 years of growth.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of licorice involve methods such as decoction, maceration, and steam distillation to isolate active compounds like glycyrrhizin, which are then purified and standardized for use in herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, in an airtight container to maintain its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly stored.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) in herbal medicine includes studies demonstrating its anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and bronchodilatory effects, as well as its role in traditional formulations for respiratory and digestive health.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for over 2,000 years to treat respiratory conditions, digestive issues, and as an anti-inflammatory and expectorant.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) is regulated as a herbal medicine, with varying degrees of oversight depending on the region, including restrictions on its use in food and pharmaceutical products due to potential health risks.
