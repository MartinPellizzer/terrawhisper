Glycyrrhiza uralensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Glycyrrhiza uralensis?

Glycyrrhiza uralensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent, it falls under the order Fabales and is a member of the family Fabaceae. Finally, its specific biological identification is defined by the genus Glycyrrhiza and the species uralensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Glycyrrhiza uralensis has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by erect stems that reach heights of up to 1 meter and a robust, woody taproot system. The leaves are pinnately compound, consisting of multiple pairs of oval to oblong leaflets with finely serrated margins. Small, papilionaceous flowers emerge in clusters from the axils, typically displaying shades of light purple or white. The plant produces legume-style pods that contain numerous small, somewhat angular seeds. Its overall structure is defined by a dense, bushy growth pattern supported by a deep, fleshy rootstock rich in bioactive compounds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically distributed across northern and northwestern China, Mongolia, and parts of the Russian Far East. It thrives in temperate climates and is frequently found in sandy or loamy soils within grasslands and forest margins. The species occupies a wide latitudinal range that extends from the dry continental regions to more humid northern territories. Local variations in habitat allow it to persist in diverse ecological niches across these specific geographic zones. This distribution pattern makes it a key component of the regional flora in these Eurasian landscapes.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Glycyrrhiza uralensis contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its thick, woody roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are harvested and processed to extract glycyrrhizin, a sweet-tasting triterpenoid saponin used for its anti-inflammatory properties. The roots are frequently employed in traditional medicine to soothe gastrointestinal inflammation and alleviate respiratory coughs. Beyond the roots, certain studies suggest that the whole plant may possess bioactive components, though the root remains the principal therapeutic part. Careful preparation of these root parts is essential to maximize their soothing effects on the digestive tract and mucous membranes.

What are the pharmacological activities of Glycyrrhiza uralensis?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidative, anti-viral, antibacterial, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to suppress inflammatory cytokines and neutralize free radicals within cellular environments. The plant also demonstrates the ability to inhibit the replication of various viruses and disrupt the growth of pathogenic bacteria. Furthermore, its compounds provide significant protection to liver cells against chemical-induced toxicity. These diverse biological effects make it a subject of intense interest in modern drug development.

According to a study published by "Acta pharmaceutica Sinica. B", the main active flavonoids of Glycyrrhiza uralensis are liquiritigenin (LG), isoliquiritigenin (Iso-LG), liquiritin (LN), and isoliquiritiin (Iso-LN). Pharmacological studies have shown that these ingredients possess broad medicinal value, which includes anti-cancer and anti-inflammatory effects. The plant also contains a triterpenoid component known as glycyrrhizin. These flavonoids are used extensively in Chinese medicine prescriptions, preparations, health care products, and food.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several bioactive medicinal compounds including glycyrrhizin, liquiritigenin, isoliquiritigenin, glycyrrhiziic acid, and glycyrrhretinic acid. Glycyrrhizin acts as the primary saponin responsible for the distinct sweetness of the root. The flavonoids liquiritigenin and isoliquiritigenin are also present and contribute to the plant's anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Additionally, the organic acids glycyrrhiziic acid and glycyrrhretinic acid play significant roles in its chemical profile. These diverse constituents work together to provide various therapeutic benefits in traditional medicine.

According to a study published by "Yakugaku zasshi : Journal of the Pharmaceuitaal Society of Japan", HPLC profile analysis was performed to determine index compounds of MeOH extracts of aerial parts and EtOAc extracts of subterranean parts of Glycyrrhiza uralensis. The research compared glycyrrhizin contents and growth rates of the underground parts of some types of Glycyrrhiza uralensis. Investigation of alkaloidal constituents of Glycyrrhiza uralensis resulted in the obtaining of anabasine and a new tricyclic alkaloid. One excellent type of Glycyrrhiza uralensis was located in Kanzo-Yashiki, Enzan, Yamanashi prefecture, where it was cultivated in the Edo period.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating peptic ulcer, various gastric diseases, inflammation, asthma, and liver diseases. Its bioactive compounds work to soothe the lining of the digestive tract to alleviate stomach irritation. The anti-inflammatory properties of the plant help reduce swelling and discomfort in different parts of the body. When addressing respiratory issues, it can help ease the symptoms associated with asthma. Additionally, its medicinal qualities support hepatic function to help manage several liver diseases.

According to a study published by "Life sciences", the extract of Glycyrrhiza uralensis is used as a basis for anti-ulcer medicines for the treatment of peptic ulcer. Specifically, the flavonoids licoricidin and licoisoflavone B exhibited inhibitory activity against the growth of Helicobacter pylori in vitro, including against a clarithromycin (CLA) and amoxicillin (AMOX)-resistant strain. From the methanol extract of G. uralensis, 3 new isoflavonoids and 15 known flavonoids were isolated. Among these, vestitol, licoricone, 1-methoxyphaseollidin, and gancaonol C showed anti-H. pylori activity against 4 CLA (AMOX)-sensitive strains and resistant strains. Additionally, compounds such as glycyrin, formononetin, isolicoflavonol, glyasperin D, 6,8-diprenylorobol, gancaonin I, dihydrolicoisoflavone A, and gancaonol B may be useful chemopreventive agents for peptic ulcer or gastric cancer in H. pylori-infected individuals.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause serious physiological issues such as hypertension, hypokalemia, edema, muscle weakness, cardiac arrhythmia, nausea, dizziness, fatigue, and insomnia. Excessive consumption often leads to a dangerous drop in potassium levels, which directly triggers muscle weakness and life-threatening cardiac arrhythmia. This electrolyte imbalance frequently results in fluid retention known as edema and elevated blood pressure or hypertension. Patients may also experience gastrointestinal or neurological discomfort including nausea, dizziness, fatigue, and insomnia. Careful monitoring is necessary to avoid these systemic complications.
