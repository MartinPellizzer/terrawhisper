What to know about Glycyrrhiza uralensis (licorice) before using it medicinally

Glycyrrhiza uralensis, commonly known as licorice, is a herb that has been prized for its numerous health properties, which can help to boost the immune system, reduce inflammation, and even alleviate digestive issues.

As a versatile plant, Glycyrrhiza uralensis is a hardy perennial that thrives in a variety of environments and can be grown in temperate climates, making it an attractive option for gardeners and farmers. Botanically, the herb belongs to the legume family and is characterized by its large, branching roots and attractive foliage.

Historically, Glycyrrhiza uralensis has been used in traditional medicine for centuries, particularly in Asian cultures, where it is revered for its medicinal and spiritual properties.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Glycyrrhiza uralensis.

What are the medicinal properties of Glycyrrhiza uralensis?

Glycyrrhiza uralensis helps with digestive issues, such as ulcers and diarrhea, and has anti-inflammatory properties. It also has antiviral and antibacterial effects, making it useful for treating respiratory infections. Additionally, licorice is used for skin issues like eczema and acne. It is also used to treat hypertension and menopausal symptoms.

The active constituents of Glycyrrhiza uralensis include glycyrrhizin, a triterpenoid saponin, and flavonoids. These compounds are responsible for the plant's medicinal properties, including its anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and antibacterial effects. Glycyrrhizin, in particular, has been shown to inhibit the production of corticosteroids, which can be beneficial for treating conditions like hypertension and hypokalemia.

The root of Glycyrrhiza uralensis is the part of the plant most commonly used for medicinal purposes. The root contains a higher concentration of glycyrrhizin and other active constituents compared to the leaves and stems. The root is harvested in the summer months and then dried or processed into a powder or extract for use in traditional medicine.

When used improperly, Glycyrrhiza uralensis can cause side effects such as hypertension, hypokalemia, and edema. These effects are usually caused by the plant's ability to inhibit corticosteroid production, leading to an imbalance of electrolytes in the body. Prolonged use of the plant can also lead to symptoms like fatigue, headache, and muscle weakness.

Precautions should be taken when using Glycyrrhiza uralensis medicinally, especially for pregnant or breastfeeding women, children, and individuals with kidney disease. The plant should be used under the guidance of a healthcare professional and in moderation, as excessive use can lead to adverse effects. Additionally, patients with hypertension, hypokalemia, or heart conditions should monitor their electrolyte levels and blood pressure when using the plant.

What are the horticulural aspects of Glycyrrhiza uralensis?

Glycyrrhiza uralensis grow best in full sun to partial shade and well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. It requires a dry to moist climate, with an annual rainfall of at least 800 mm. Soil should be rich in organic matter and have a moderate level of fertility.

When planting Glycyrrhiza uralensis, choose a location with good air circulation to prevent fungal diseases. Plant rhizomes or seeds in the spring or fall, spacing them 1-2 meters apart. Water regularly, but avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot.

For optimal harvesting, cut the roots of Glycyrrhiza uralensis 2-3 years after planting, when the plant is in its fourth to sixth year of growth. Dig up the entire root system, using a spade or fork to minimize damage. Harvest the roots in the morning, after the plant has been watered.

Glycyrrhiza uralensis is susceptible to pests like aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites, which can be controlled using neem oil or insecticidal soap. Diseases like root rot, leaf spot, and powdery mildew can be managed by maintaining good air circulation, watering carefully, and treating with fungicides as needed.
