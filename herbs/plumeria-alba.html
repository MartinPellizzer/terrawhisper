Plumeria alba

What's the taxonomical classification of Plumeria alba?

Plumeria alba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it is placed in the order Gentianales and the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Plumeria, and its unique species designation, alba.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Plumeria alba has thick, succulent, grayish-green branches that are sparsely covered with small, elliptical leaves and terminate in clusters of five-petaled, waxy white flowers. The stems exhibit a characteristic fleshy texture designed for water storage, which allows the plant to thrive in arid environments. Its leaves are typically lanceolate or narrowly ovate, featuring prominent veins and a smooth margin. During the flowering stage, the fragrant blossoms emerge from terminal cymes, showcasing a delicate structure with a yellowish center. The overall growth habit is arborescent, forming a rounded canopy as the woody branches expand over time.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of the Caribbean, specifically encompassing islands such as Cuba, Jamaica, and Hispaniola. It thrives primarily in lowland coastal areas and dry forest ecosystems throughout these maritime territories. The species is also frequently found in various parts of Central and South America where warm, humid climates prevail. Because it is often cultivated for its ornamental value, it has been introduced to other tropical zones worldwide. Its natural range is strictly limited to environments that maintain consistently high temperatures and seasonal moisture.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

What are the pharmacological activities of Plumeria alba?

This plant has anti-DENV activity, anti-dengue properties, anti-cancerous potential, anti-proliferative effects, and antioxidant capabilities. These diverse pharmacological activities make it a subject of significant medicinal interest. Specifically, its ability to combat the dengue virus suggests a role in managing viral infections. The presence of anti-proliferative and anti-cancerous actions indicates its potential to inhibit the growth of malignant cells. Furthermore, its antioxidant properties help protect cells from oxidative stress and damage.

As discussed in a study, Plumeria alba was one of 25 extracts prepared for in vitro antiviral screening. The study assessed extracts from this plant for both anti-DENV and CHIKV activity using focus-forming unit assay, quantitative real-time RT-PCR, and immunofluorescence assays. Extracts from Plumeria alba showed activity against both the dengue virus and the chikungunya virus. The researchers utilized the maximum nontoxic concentrations of the chloroform, methanol, ethyl acetate, petroleum ether, dichloromethane, and hydroalcoholic extracts. These findings suggest that such extracts can be further characterized for finding effective phytopharmacuetical drugs.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compound plumercine, which belongs to a class of organic molecules known as coumarins. These chemical constituents are often studied for their potential biological activities, including anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. The presence of such compounds suggests that the plant may possess significant therapeutic value in pharmacological research. Scientists continue to investigate how these specific alkaloids interact with human cellular pathways to treat various ailments. Understanding the concentration of plumercine helps researchers determine the plant's overall medicinal potency.

As discussed in a study, Plumeria alba was selected for research due to its existing medicinal values. The investigation focused on three bioactive compounds unique to the plant, which are Plumericine, Isoplumericine, and 13-O-p-Coumaroylplumieride. These compounds were subjected to in silico docking against 5 different cyclin and Cdk proteins responsible for ALL. Among these, Plumercine was identified as the most competent compound and is considered a potential leukemic drug candidate. The study also evaluated the effects of methanolic and ethyl acetate extracts on several cell lines, including NALM 6, A549, and MOLT4.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness if the sap or parts of the plant are ingested or touched. Direct contact with the milky sap often results in redness, itching, or a painful rash on the skin. If the plant is swallowed, it can lead to severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. Systemic reactions may also include feelings of dizziness as the body reacts to the plant's toxins. It is important to handle this species with caution to avoid these adverse physical effects.
