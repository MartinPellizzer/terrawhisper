Tropaeolum majus

What's the taxonomical classification of Tropaeolum majus?

Tropaeolum majus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its lineage through the order Brassicales, it is placed within the family Tropaeolaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Tropaeolum, and its unique species designation, majus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tropaeoluum majus has long, slender, lanceolate leaves that are arranged alternately along trailing or climbing stems. These leaves often exhibit a characteristic tendency to divide into smaller, deeply lobed segments as the plant matures. The stems are herbaceous and can reach several meters in length, providing a flexible structure for climbing. Large, trumpet-shaped flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying vibrant colors like orange, red, or yellow. Each flower features five distinct petals and a prominent nectar spur extending from the base. Small, round seed pods follow the flowering stage, eventually developing into dark, hard seeds.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains sesquiterpene lactones, benzeneacetic acid, depsides, phylloxanthobilins, and terpene steroids. These specific medicinal compounds contribute to the complex chemical profile that defines the plant's biological activities. The presence of sesquiterpene lactones often provides significant anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Additionally, the depsides and phylloxanthobilins play critical roles in the plant's secondary metabolism. The terpene steroids further enhance its pharmacological potential through various physiological interactions.

As discussed in a study, the leaf and flower extracts derived from Tropaeolum majuus L. contain organic acids, proteins, amino acids, and terpene steroids. Specifically, sesquiterpene lactones and depside were detected within the leaf extracts. These extracts were evaluated using the 50% Inhibition Concentration (IC50) to perform cell cycle analysis. The higher concentration tested exhibited a marked phytotoxic effect. These compounds induced clastogenic, aneugenic cytotoxic, and potential mutagenic effects.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating gastritis, peptic ulcer, functional dyspepsia, infections of the draining urinary tract, and various bacterial infections. Its medicinal properties help soothe inflammation within the digestive tract to alleviate discomfort from stomach ailments. The plant also possesses antimicrobial characteristics that assist in combating harmful bacterial infections throughout the body. Additionally, its therapeutic effects are utilized to manage issues related to the urinary system. These diverse applications make it a significant resource for addressing both gastrointestinal and systemic health concerns.

As discussed in a study, Tropaeolum majus was screened for anti-ureaase activity to combat gastric diseases. The plant was tested at three different concentrations, which were 25μg/ml, 50μg/ml, and 75μg/ml. At the concentration of 75μg/ml, T. maju showed a significant ureaease inhibitory activity of 87.3±0.14 when compared to Thiourea. This medicinal plant is considered a good candidate for therapeutic purposes to decrease the prevalence of gastric diseases caused by H. pylori bacteria.
