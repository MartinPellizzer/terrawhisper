Scientific and Botanical Profile

Garden Nasturtium, with botanical name Tropaeolum majus, is a flowering plant belonging to the Tropaeolaceae family, commonly known as the nasturtium family.

Native to South America, particularly regions of Peru, Ecuador, and Chile, it has been widely cultivated in temperate regions for its ornamental and edible qualities. Morphologically, it is an annual herb with heart-shaped, lobed leaves that are often reddish or purple underneath, and bright, showy flowers in shades of orange, yellow, and red, typically with a spurred petal at the back. Common names for this plant include Indian Cress, Spicy Nasturtium, Spangle Flower, Spade Flower, Spatterflower, Spikerush, and Tropaeolum, reflecting its varied uses and appearance.

Its edible leaves and seeds are prized for their peppery flavor, while the flowers are popular in gardens for their vibrant color and ability to attract pollinators.

History and Cultural Relevance

Garden Nasturtium was used by ancient civilizations such as the Aztecs and Greeks for both culinary and medicinal purposes, with its vibrant flowers and peppery leaves being valued for their flavor and purported health benefits.

In traditional medicine systems, including Ayurveda and Native American healing practices, it was employed to treat infections, digestive issues, and skin conditions due to its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Culturally, nasturtiums have played a role in various rituals, such as being used in May Day celebrations and as symbols of protection and prosperity in some European traditions. Today, its edible leaves and flowers are still used in salads and herbal remedies, with studies supporting its potential to boost the immune system and aid in respiratory health.

The plant's enduring presence in gardens and folklore highlights its deep historical roots and continued relevance in both cultural and therapeutic contexts.

It also contains essential oils rich in terpenes and other volatile compounds that give it its characteristic peppery flavor and aromatic profile. Nutritional-wise, Garden Nasturtium is a good source of vitamins C and A, along with minerals such as iron and potassium, and it is high in antioxidants that help combat oxidative stress. The active compounds in Garden Nasturtium may support immune function and have anti-inflammatory effects by modulating cellular responses and reducing free radical damage.

Overall, its unique combination of chemical constituents and nutritional value makes it a valuable plant for both culinary and therapeutic use.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Tropaeolum majus has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms such as fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare it, one can make a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the dried plant material for a longer period. It can also be used topically as an infusion or applied directly as a poultice for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 teaspoons of tea twice daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision due to limited safety data.

Due to the lack of extensive research, it is advisable to use tropaeolum majus sparingly, with a duration of use not exceeding a few weeks unless guided by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Tropaeolum majus can be used as a medicinal plant with potential benefits, but it is important to be aware of its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, nausea, and allergic reactions in some individuals. There are limited studies on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised as it may interact with anticoagulants or medications affecting the liver. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid using tropaeolum majus due to insufficient evidence on its safety during these periods.

Individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use, and safe practices include starting with small doses, monitoring for adverse reactions, and avoiding long-term use without medical supervision.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Tropaeolum majus grows best in well-drained soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, requiring full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in temperate climates and prefers consistent moisture without waterlogging, so regular watering is essential during dry periods. To ensure healthy growth, deadheading spent flowers and providing support for climbing varieties can promote better foliage and flowering. The best time to harvest the leaves and flowers is during the early morning when the plant is most hydrated, using sharp scissors to cut stems just above a node to encourage regrowth.

For storage, dried leaves and flowers should be placed in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to preserve potency, while fresh specimens can be refrigerated in sealed bags for up to a week.
