Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

The Garden Nasturtium, or Tropaeolum majus, is a vibrant annual plant native to South America that has been widely used in herbal medicine for its medicinal properties, including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and digestive benefits.

What is Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum Majus)?

Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) is an annual flowering plant native to South America, widely cultivated for its edible leaves, flowers, and seeds, and traditionally used in herbal medicine for its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and digestive properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Garden nasturtium is Tropaeolum majus. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Tropaeolales, family Tropaeolaceae, genus Tropaeolum, and species Tropaeolum majus. It is commonly known as garden nasturtium in North America and nasturtium in Europe. The plant is also referred to as Indian cress in India and wild nasturtium in Australia. Synonyms for this plant include Tropaeolum speciosum and Tropaeolum fendleri.

Plant Description and Morphology

Garden nasturtium is an annual plant that typically grows to a height of 30 to 100 centimeters, with a bushy, branching structure.

Its leaves are heart-shaped, with a slightly wavy margin and a glossy, dark green surface. The flowers are bright and showy, with a spurred petal at the back and vibrant colors ranging from orange to red. The root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing the plant to anchor itself in loose soil.

The plant has a peppery, tangy scent, with leaves and flowers that are slightly rough to the touch.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Garden nasturtium is native to South America, specifically the Andean region of Peru and Ecuador, where it grows in moist, temperate climates.

It has been widely cultivated and naturalized in temperate regions across North America, Europe, and parts of Asia. The plant thrives in climates with moderate temperatures, preferring full sun to partial shade. It requires well-drained soil and moderate moisture levels to flourish.

Its ecological conditions favor areas with good air circulation and protection from strong winds.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) primarily involves its leaves, flowers, and seeds.

Leaves are commonly harvested for their peppery taste and potential health benefits. Flowers are also used, often dried and incorporated into herbal teas. Seeds are utilized both for culinary and medicinal purposes.

Each part contributes unique compounds that support various therapeutic applications.

Therapeutic Uses

The Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) is valued in herbal medicine for its potential therapeutic properties.

It is traditionally used to support respiratory health, particularly for conditions like coughs and bronchitis. The plant contains compounds that may help reduce inflammation and act as a mild expectorant. It is also applied topically for skin conditions such as eczema and insect bites.

Additionally, it is used to aid digestion and relieve symptoms of gastrointestinal discomfort.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) is commonly used in herbal medicine in various forms, including tinctures, teas, and topical salves.

It is often prepared as a leaf or seed infusion by steeping fresh or dried parts in hot water. The plant can also be made into a tincture by soaking the leaves or flowers in alcohol for several weeks. Topical applications include crushed leaves or seeds applied directly to the skin for their anti-inflammatory properties.

Dried flowers and leaves are also used to make poultices for skin conditions.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) for herbal use typically ranges from 500 to 1,000 milligrams per day, depending on the form and intended purpose.

For dried herb, a common preparation is 1 to 2 grams of dried leaves or flowers in capsule form. Extract concentration should not exceed 5% active compounds to ensure safety and efficacy. It is recommended to take the supplement once or twice daily, depending on individual response and health needs.

The duration of use should generally not exceed four weeks without a break, to minimize the risk of adverse effects.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Garden Nasturtium is generally considered safe for most people, but it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or skin irritation in some individuals.

Side effects are more likely with high doses or prolonged use, and may include digestive upset or allergic reactions. Contraindications include individuals with known allergies to plants in the Tropaeolaceae family, as well as those with gallbladder issues, as nasturtium may stimulate bile production. Allergic reactions can range from mild symptoms like itching to more severe responses such as swelling or respiratory difficulties.

During pregnancy, it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before using Garden Nasturtium, as its safety has not been fully established for expectant mothers.

Drug Interactions

When used alongside certain pharmaceuticals, Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) may interfere with drug metabolism due to its bioactive compounds, potentially altering the effectiveness or side effects of medications.

It may interact with anticoagulants like warfarin, increasing the risk of bleeding due to its mild blood-thinning properties. Interactions with supplements such as vitamin K or fish oil could also enhance anticoagulant effects, further elevating bleeding risks. The plant's alkaloids and flavonoids may affect liver enzymes responsible for metabolizing drugs, leading to either reduced efficacy or increased toxicity.

It is essential to consult a healthcare provider before combining Garden Nasturtium with any pharmaceuticals or supplements.

Toxicity and Precautions

The Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) is generally considered safe when used in typical culinary or medicinal amounts, but it may pose risks if consumed in excessive quantities.

The toxic dose varies depending on individual factors, but ingestion of large amounts—typically more than 10 grams of leaves or 5 grams of seeds—can lead to gastrointestinal distress, vomiting, or diarrhea. Long-term safety data is limited, and prolonged use may cause liver or kidney damage, especially in individuals with pre-existing conditions. Poisoning risks are higher in children and pets, who may chew on the leaves or seeds, leading to more severe symptoms.

It is important to consult a healthcare professional before using Garden Nasturtium for therapeutic purposes.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) is a hardy, fast-growing plant that thrives in well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5.

It prefers full sun to partial shade, though it performs best with at least 6 hours of direct sunlight daily. Watering should be moderate, keeping the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged. Propagation is easiest through seeds sown directly in the garden after the last frost, or started indoors 4-6 weeks before the last frost date. The plant reaches maturity in about 60 to 80 days, with leaves and flowers appearing within a few weeks of germination. Harvesting begins when the leaves are fully developed and the flowers are open, typically in mid to late summer.

To harvest, gently pluck the leaves and flowers by hand, ensuring not to damage the plant.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and Extraction of Garden Nasturtium involves methods to isolate its active compounds for use in herbal medicine.

Drying is the first step, typically done by spreading the harvested flowers and leaves in a well-ventilated area or using a dehydrator to remove moisture. Extraction methods include using solvents like ethanol or water to draw out alkaloids and flavonoids. Distillation can be used to produce essential oils, although it is less common due to the plant's low oil content.

Grinding the dried material into a powder enhances its surface area, making it more effective for tinctures and capsules.

Storage and Shelf Life

The shelf life of dried Garden Nasturtium (Tropaeolum majus) is typically 1 to 2 years when stored properly.

To maximize its shelf life, keep the dried plant material in a cool, dark, and dry place away from direct sunlight. Exposure to light can degrade the active compounds, reducing potency and effectiveness. Moisture control is critical; store in airtight containers to prevent humidity from causing mold or mildew.

Ideal storage conditions include a temperature range of 15-20°C with low humidity levels.
