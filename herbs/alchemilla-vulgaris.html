What to know about Alchemilla vulgaris (lady's mantle) before using it medicinally

Alchemilla vulgaris, commonly known as lady's mantle, is a herb that has been valued for its health-giving properties, particularly in supporting liver function and alleviating menstrual cramps.

From a horticultural perspective, this perennial plant thrives in moist soils and partial shade, making it a popular choice for woodland gardens and naturalized areas. From a botanical standpoint, Alchemilla vulgaris features clusters of small, star-shaped flowers in shades of yellow and green, which are highly prized by pollinators and gardeners alike.

In historical terms, the herb has been used for centuries in traditional medicine, with references to its use dating back to the Roman naturalist Pliny the Elder.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Alchemilla vulgaris.

What are the medicinal properties of Alchemilla vulgaris?

Alchemilla vulgaris helps with menstrual cramps, bloating, and other symptoms associated with premenstrual syndrome (PMS). It has anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic properties, making it a popular herbal remedy for women's health issues. The plant has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to treat various gynecological problems.

The active constituents of Alchemilla vulgaris, including flavonoids, tannins, and phenolic acids, contribute to its medicinal properties. Flavonoids, in particular, have been shown to have estrogen-like effects, which may help alleviate symptoms associated with PMS. These compounds also exhibit antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities.

The most commonly used parts of Alchemilla vulgaris for medicinal purposes are the leaves and flowers. The leaves are typically harvested in summer and dried for later use in teas, infusions, and tinctures. The flowers can also be used fresh or dried to make infusions and tinctures.

Improper use of Alchemilla vulgaris can cause gastrointestinal upset, including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. This is due to the plant's high content of tannins, which can irritate the stomach and intestines if ingested in excessive amounts.

Precautions when using Alchemilla vulgaris medicinally include using it in moderation, avoiding excessive doses, and consulting a healthcare professional before using the plant to treat any medical condition, particularly if you have underlying health issues or are pregnant or breastfeeding.

What are the horticulural aspects of Alchemilla vulgaris?

Alchemilla vulgaris grow best in moist, well-drained soils in partial shade. The plant prefers a pH range of 6.0 to 7.0 and tolerates a wide range of temperatures. It thrives in USDA zones 3-9.

Planting tips for Alchemilla vulgaris include selecting a location with good air circulation to prevent fungal diseases. Plant the rhizomes in the spring or fall, spacing them 12-18 inches apart. Water regularly during the first growing season. Mulch around the plants to retain moisture and suppress weeds.

Harvesting Alchemilla vulgaris requires snipping off the stems with seed heads attached, typically in late summer or early fall. Cut back the stems to encourage new growth. Remove any dead or damaged foliage to maintain plant health. Divide and replant the rhizomes every 3-4 years to maintain vigor.

Common pests and diseases affecting Alchemilla vulgaris include aphids, spider mites, and powdery mildew. Regularly inspect the plants for signs of infestation or infection. Use insecticidal soap or neem oil to control pests, and treat fungal diseases with a copper-based fungicide.

What are the botanical characteristics of Alchemilla vulgaris?

Alchemilla vulgaris, also known as Lady's Mantle, is a perennial herbaceous plant that belongs to the Rosaceae family.

Botanically speaking, it exhibits several characteristic features. The plant produces rosettes of toothed, palmate leaves that are typically 3-6 inches in diameter, with 9-17 leaflets that are serrated and have a distinctive "frilled" margin.

The leaves are often covered in fine hairs and have a leathery texture. In the summer, A. vulgaris produces yellow-green flowers in dense, flat-topped clusters on slender stalks, each flower consisting of five petals and numerous stamens. The plant's reproductive structures are characterized by a distinctive "stigmatic disc" that covers the receptacle of the fruiting head, which is an important diagnostic feature for identification purposes.

Additionally, A. vulgaris has a shallow taproot and spreading rhizomes that allow it to spread and form dense colonies over time.

What is the taxonomy of Alchemilla vulgaris?

Alchemilla vulgaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Rosopsida, order Rosales, family Rosaceae, and genus Alchemilla.

Specifically, it is classified as Alchemilla vulagris L., a species of the lady's mantle plant.

Alchemilla vulgaris has variants, such as A. v. subsp.

pyrenaica, which is found in the Pyrenees and is characterized by its smaller leaves and stems. Another variant is A. v. subsp. mollissima, which is found in Eastern Europe and Asia and has longer and softer hairs on its leaves and stems.

Additionally, there are several botanical varieties of A. vulgaris, including var. glabra, which lacks the characteristic sticky hairs on its leaves and stems, and var. sericata, which has a more silvery coloration on its leaves due to dense hairs.

These variants can be distinguished based on their morphological characteristics and geographic distribution.

What is the geographic distribution of Alchemilla vulgaris?

Alchemilla vulgaris, also known as common lady's mantle or marshmallow leaf, has a wide geographic distribution across Europe, Asia, and North Africa.

It is native to moist meadows, woodland edges, and mountain slopes, typically growing in areas with mild winters and cool summers. Its range spans from the British Isles and Scandinavia in the north to Spain, Italy, and Greece in the south, and as far east as Russia and China. In North Africa, it can be found in Morocco, Algeria, and Tunisia.

It is also naturalized in parts of North America, Australia, and New Zealand, likely due to human introduction through gardening or landscaping.

What is the life cycle of Alchemilla vulgaris?

Alchemilla vulgaris, also known as common lady's mantle, has a perennial life cycle.

In early spring, it produces small, delicate leaves that emerge from underground stems called rhizomes. As the weather warms up, the plant grows and produces white or yellowish flowers in late spring to early summer.

After pollination, the flowers give rise to fruiting capsules that contain numerous small seeds. The seeds are dispersed by wind or water, allowing the plant to spread and colonize new areas.

In the fall, the leaves turn yellow and die back, and the rhizomes prepare for dormancy until the next spring.

What are the historical uses of Alchemilla vulgaris?

Alchemilla vulgaris, also known as lady's mantle, has a rich history of traditional use dating back to ancient times.

In medieval Europe, it was used to treat wounds and burns due to its ability to speed up the healing process. The plant was also employed in folk medicine for its anti-inflammatory and antibacterial properties to treat various ailments such as digestive issues and urinary tract infections.

Its leaves were often made into a tea or poultice for topical application.
