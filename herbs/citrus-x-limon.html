Citrus × limon

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrus × limon?

Citrus × limon belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Sapindales and the family Rutaceae, placing it within the genus Citrus. Finally, the specific identity of this plant is defined by its species name, limon.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Citrus × limon has evergreen, woody stems characterized by small thorns, elliptical leaves with serrated margins, and yellow, globose fruits covered in a thick, oil-rich rind. The foliage is typically dark green and glossy, often displaying a winged petiole that is characteristic of the citrus genus. Its flowers are white, fragrant, and pentamerous, typically appearing in small clusters. The fruit, known as a lemon, develops from an inferior ovary and contains numerous juice-filled vesicles within its pulp. Mature specimens can grow into large shrubs or small trees depending on the environmental conditions and cultivation methods used.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to South Asia but is now cultivated extensively in tropical and subtropical regions across the entire globe. It thrives in warm climates with well-drained soil, such as those found in the Mediterranean basin, parts of Southeast Asia, and Florida. Farmers frequently grow these trees in areas that avoid prolonged freezing temperatures to ensure consistent fruit production. While it can be grown in various containers, its primary commercial distribution follows major citrus-producing belts in Brazil, Mexico, and China. The species has successfully naturalized in many coastal zones where humidity and sunlight are abundant.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as the lemon, requires well-draining acidic soil, consistent warmth between 75 and 85 degrees Fahrenheit, and at least six to eight hours of full sunlight daily for optimal fruit production.

Growers must provide regular deep watering to prevent soil from drying out completely while ensuring the roots do not sit in standing water. Nitrogen-rich fertilizers are essential during the growing season to support lush foliage and vigorous citrus development. Because the species is sensitive to frost, plants in colder climates often require protection or must be grown in containers to be moved indoors.

Managing pests like citrus leafminers and scale is also a vital part of maintaining a healthy harvest.

Essential oils extracted from the rind are used in aromatherapy and topical applications to reduce anxiety and inflammation. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause dental erosion, heartburn, acid reflux, nausea, stomach upset, and mouth irritation due to its high citric acid content. Consuming large amounts of the fruit may lead to the thinning of tooth enamel, which results in dental erosion. The acidic nature of the juice often triggers digestive issues such as heartburn and acid reflux in sensitive individuals. Some people might also experience unpleasant sensations like nausea or general stomach upset after ingestion. Additionally, the acidity can cause temporary mouth irritation on the tongue or gums.
