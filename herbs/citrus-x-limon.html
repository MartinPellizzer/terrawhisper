Citrus × limon

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrus × limon?

Citrus × limon belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Sapindales and the family Rutaceae, placing it within the genus Citrus. Finally, the specific identity of this plant is defined by its species name, limon.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Citrus × limon contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its juice, peel, leaves, and seeds. The juice is frequently consumed to provide a high concentration of vitamin C, which supports immune function and skin health. Essential oils extracted from the rind are used in aromatherapy and topical applications to reduce anxiety and inflammation. The leaves are often steeped into teas to promote relaxation and aid in digestive relief. Additionally, the seeds and pulp contain various flavonoids that possess antioxidant and antimicrobial properties.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive substances including flavonoids, citric acid, limonene, ascorbic acid, terpenoids, polymethoxylated flavones, hesperidin, and limonoids.

The presence of ascorbic acid provides essential antioxidant properties, while citric acid contributes to the fruit's characteristic acidity and metabolic roles. Flavonoids and polymethoxylated flavones such as hesperidin offer significant anti-inflammatory and cardioprotective benefits. Additionally, the terpenoids and limonene found in the peel contribute to its aromatic qualities and antimicrobial actions.

Limonoids further enhance the medicinal profile by providing various biological activities within the plant's chemical makeup.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Citrus × limon targets various enzymes and receptors including cyclooxygenase-2, alpha-glucosidase, acetylcholinesterase, and specific inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha. The bioactive limonoids and flavonoids found in the plant exert these effects by inhibiting the enzymatic activity responsible for glucose absorption and neurodegeneration. Polyphenolic compounds within the fruit further modulate the expression of pro-inflammatory genes to mitigate oxidative stress in cellular pathways. These interactions with protein targets facilitate the plant's recognized antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and hypoglycemic medicinal properties. Through these complex biochemical mechanisms, the plant components regulate systemic metabolic and immune responses.

What are the therapeutic actions of Citrus × limon?

Citrus × limon has antibacterial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties that arise from its high concentration of citric acid and vitamin C. These bioactive compounds work together to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within bodily tissues. The presence of limonene in the essential oil provides additional antimicrobial effects against various pathogens. Additionally, its acidic nature can aid in digestive processes and support immune system function. These combined therapeutic actions make the plant a versatile component in both dietary and topical applications.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat no human ailments because it is a fruit tree primarily used in culinary and aromatherapy contexts.

While it provides flavor and fragrance, it does not possess medicinal properties for healing illnesses. It is important to note that lemon balm (melissa officinalis) is often confused with citrus limon, but they are entirely different species. Unlike the medicinal herb, citrus limon is grown for its edible fruit and essential oils.

Therefore, it serves more as a food ingredient or scent than a therapeutic remedy.

What are the herbal preparations of Citrus × limon?

Citrus × limon is prepared as essential oils, infusions, decoctions, tinctures, and topical extracts using its peel, juice, or leaves. The essential oil is most commonly extracted through cold pressing the rind to utilize its high limonene content for aromatherapy or flavoring. Aqueous preparations like teas or infusions are made by steeping the fruit or leaves to capture vitamin C and flavonoids. Tinctures prepared with alcohol serve as concentrated extracts for long-term preservation and medicinal use. Additionally, the juice and peel are often utilized in topical applications for their antimicrobial and antioxidant properties.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Citrus × limon can cause skin irritation, photosensitivity, and gastrointestinal distress when consumed in excessive amounts. The citric acid in the fruit may erode tooth enamel if frequent contact occurs. Contact with the juice can lead to phytophotodermatitis, a painful skin reaction triggered by sunlight exposure. Some individuals might also experience acid reflux or heartburn due to the high acidity levels. In rare cases, allergic reactions such as swelling or rashes may occur in sensitive people.
