Salvia officinalis

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia officinalis?

Salvia officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Lamiales. It is a member of the family Lamiaceae, which falls under the genus Salvia, and is specifically identified by its species name, officinalis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia officinalis has a woody, perennial base with quadrangular stems that reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bear grayish-green, rugose leaves with a velvety texture. The foliage is characterized by oblong or lanceolate shapes and crenate margins, often displaying a dense covering of fine hairs on both surfaces. During the flowering season, the plant produces verticillasters arranged in terminal racemes. These inflorescences feature bilabiate flowers that typically range in color from pale blue to violet or lavender. The floral structure is specifically adapted for pollination, featuring a prominent upper lip and a lower lip that serves as a landing platform.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, specifically spanning across southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of western Asia. It thrives in various microclimates ranging from rocky hillsides to coastal scrublands within these territories. Due to its long history of medicinal use, it has been naturalized in many other temperate areas around the world. Cultivation is widespread in gardens throughout Europe and North America where the soil provides sufficient drainage. Its distribution is largely influenced by its preference for sunny, warm environments and well-drained terrain.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant grows best in well-drained, sandy or loamy soil within full sun exposure and a temperate climate.

It requires minimal watering once established, as it is highly drought-tolerant and prone to root rot in soggy conditions. For optimal development, gardeners should provide plenty of air circulation to prevent fungal diseases on its aromatic foliage. Pruning the stems in early spring helps stimulate vigorous new growth and prevents the shrub from becoming overly woody.

Maintaining a slightly alkaline to neutral soil pH will further ensure the plant thrives throughout the growing season.

Salvvia officiinalis contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated within its aromatic leaves and flowering stems. What traditional systems uses this plant?

This plant, commonly known as sage, has been utilized in Mediterranean traditional medicine for centuries to treat ailments ranging from sore throats to digestive issues.

Ancient Greek and Roman physicians frequently employed its leaves to promote healing and enhance cognitive functions. During the Middle Ages, herbalists integrated the species into various folk remedies to address inflammation and wound care. It was often considered a sacred herb in spiritual rituals due to its aromatic properties and perceived purifying qualities.

This long history of application laid the foundational knowledge for its modern use in herbal supplements and culinary traditions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Salvia officinalis?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antibacterial, antimicrobial, and antidepressant pharmacological activities. Its rich concentration of phenolic compounds and diterpenoids allows it to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress in the body. These chemical constituents also inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and microbes, making it effective for fighting infections. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates potential in modulating neurotransmitters to provide antidepressant effects. Research continues to explore how these diverse biological properties can be utilized in therapeutic applications.

According to a study published by "Global advances in integrative medicine and health", Salvia officinalis extracts have been investigated for their broad medicinal benefits in treating polycystic ovarian syndrome. The extracts were able to significantly minimize markers of the disorder by decreasing the number of cysts in the ovaries. Treatment resulted in a decrease in serum Testosterone and Estradiol levels of 50 and 55% respectively with P < 0.05. Furthermore, the extracts decreased MDA levels by approximately 90% with P < 0.05. They also helped normalize oxidative stress markers including CAT, SOD, and GSH while decreasing IL-1β levels by approximately 40% with P < 0.05.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains rosmarinic acid, carnosiic acid, salvianolic acid isomers, thucone, and carnosoal as its primary medicinal compounds. Rosmarinic acid and salvianolic acid isomers are prominent polyphenols known for their significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The diterpenes carnosiic acid and carnosoal contribute to the plant's ability to protect cells against oxidative stress. Additionally, the monoterpene thucone is present, though it must be managed carefully due to its potential neurotoxicity in high concentrations. Together, these diverse chemical constituents form the complex therapeutic profile of Salvia officinalis.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", Salvia officiinalis contains various medicinal compounds that demonstrate in vitro AChE inhibitory properties in a dose dependent manner. When tested at 1 mg/mL, the ethanolic extracts of Salvia officiinalis showed strong inhibitory activity against AChE. Rosmanic acid was found to be the predominant constituent in this plant as determined by RP-HPLC. This specific hydroxycinnamic acid has a substantial influence on the AChE inhibitory and antioxidant properties of the plant. These findings indicate that Salvia officiinalis is a rich source of various natural AChE inhibitors and antioxidants.

Regarding metabolic health, it may help regulate blood sugar levels in individuals suffering from diabetes mellitus. Additionally, its natural properties work to suppress systemic inflammation, providing broad therapeutic benefits.

According to a study published by "CNS & neurological disorders drug targets", Salvia has shown anti-Alzheimer's properties in various in vivo and in vitro studies. The diverse phytochemicals in the plant play a protective role against amyloid-beta-induced neurotoxicity and improve cognitive and learning impairments. These bioactive compounds help to lower the amyloid burden, improve memory dysfunction, and inhibit acetylcholinesterase activity. Furthermore, the plant can reduce apoptosis by the inactivation of Caspase 3 and 9. These medicinal plants help to reduce AD pathogenesis by controlling different pathways and could be used as a therapeutic agent against the symptoms.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, dizziness, vomiting, dry mouth, hallucinations, seizures, and confusion if consumed in excessive amounts. These adverse reactions typically occur when the active compounds in the leaves affect the central nervous system. Some individuals may experience mild discomfort like a parched throat or lightheadedness. More severe cases can lead to disorientation or intense visual disturbances. In extreme scenarios, the toxicity may trigger physical convulsions or significant mental impairment.
