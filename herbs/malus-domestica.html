Malus domestica

What's the taxonomical classification of Malus domestica?

Malus domestica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of biological classification, it falls under the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its genus, Malus, and its species designation, domestica.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Malus domestica contains high concentrations of polyphenolic compounds, specifically flavonoids like quercetin, catechin, and chlorogenic acid, which provide significant antioxidant properties. These phytochemicals work to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within human cells. Additionally, the plant's fruit contains pectin, a soluble fiber that aids in digestive health and helps regulate cholesterol levels. Certain phenolic acids found in the skin are also associated with anti-inflammatory and potential anti-carcinogenic effects. Regular consumption of these bioactive components contributes to overall cardiovascular health and immune system support.

What are the therapeutic actions of Malus domestica?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including respiratory infections, digestive issues, and certain inflammatory conditions. The fruit contains high levels of pectin and antioxidants that can help regulate blood sugar and improve cholesterol levels. Consuming the fruit may also alleviate symptoms of diarrhea and help soothe intestinal discomfort. Some studies suggest that its phytochemical properties offer protective effects against cardiovascular diseases and oxidative stress. Regular consumption can contribute to a stronger immune response against seasonal illnesses.

What are the herbal preparations of Malus domestica?

Malus domestica is prepared as culinary syrups, dried fruit slices, vinegars, tinctures, and infusions derived from the fruit, skin, or flowers. The fruit can be dried into slices to be steeped in hot water for a soothing tea. Pectin extracted from the flesh is frequently used in food preparations to create jams and jellies. For medicinal purposes, a vinegar made from fermented apple juice serves as a common tonic. Additionally, concentrated extracts or tinctures can be made from the plant components for more potent applications.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Malus domestica can cause gastrointestinal distress, allergic reactions, or choking hazards if consumed improperly. Excessive intake of the fruit may lead to bloating, gas, or diarrhea due to its high fiber and fructose content. Individuals with sensitivities to certain proteins might experience oral allergy syndrome, characterized by itching or swelling in the mouth. Additionally, the seeds contain amygdalin, which can release cyanide if swallowed in large quantities. Careful preparation and moderation are necessary to avoid these adverse reactions.
