Passiflora jugorum

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora jugorum?

Passiflora jugorum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a member of the family Passifloraceae, falling within the genus Passiflora, and is specifically identified as the species jugorum.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Passiflora jugorum contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its dried aerial parts, which include the leaves, stems, and flowers. These vegetative structures are harvested and processed to extract alkaloids and flavonoids used for their sedative properties. The flowers are particularly valued in traditional preparations due to their high concentration of active constituents. While the fruit pulp is sometimes used for nutrition, the therapeutic efficacy relies almost entirely on the plant's foliage. These various parts are commonly prepared as teas, tinctures, or extracts to help alleviate anxiety and promote sleep.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains alkaloids, flavonoids, cyanogenic glycosides, phenolic acids, and terpenoids that contribute to its diverse pharmacological profile.

The presence of alkaloids and flavonoids is often associated with its sedative and anxiolytic properties. Cyanogenic glycosides serve as a defensive mechanism within the plant tissue but play a role in its complex chemical identity. Phenolic acids provide significant antioxidant activity to protect cells from oxidative stress.

Finally, the terpenoids contribute to the plant's overall therapeutic potential through various biological pathways.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Alkaloids
  • Flavonoids
  • Cyanogenic glycosides
  • Phenolic acids
  • Terpenoids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Passiflora jugorum targets gamma-aminobutyric acid type A receptors, serotonin receptors, and various monoamine oxidase enzymes to exert its sedative and anxiolytic effects. The bioactive alkaloids and flavonoids within the plant act as modulators of the central nervous system by enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission. Specifically, certain compounds increase the binding affinity of GABA to its receptor sites, which helps reduce neuronal excitability. These interactions facilitate a calming effect on the brain by influencing the pathways responsible for mood regulation and sleep induction. Additionally, the inhibition of certain enzymes may prolong the presence of neurotransmitters in the synaptic cleft, further contributing to its medicinal properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Passiflora jugorum?

Passiflora jugorum has sedative, anxiolytic, and anticonvulsant properties that primarily function by increasing the availability of gamma-aminobutyric acid in the central nervous system. This plant acts as a natural tranquilizer by modulating GABA receptors to reduce neuronal excitability. These biochemical actions help alleviate symptoms of insomnia, nervousness, and acute anxiety. Additionally, its mild sedative effects support the relaxation of smooth muscles, which can assist in managing digestive spasms. Regular use may promote a calmer mental state and improve overall sleep quality through these calming mechanisms.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat anxiety, insomnia, restlessness, and various forms of nervous tension. Its sedative properties help calm the central nervous system to improve sleep quality. Patients often use it to manage mild palpitations and irritability caused by stress. The plant also acts as a gentle relaxant for tension-related headaches. It is frequently integrated into herbal remedies designed to stabilize mood and reduce emotional agitation.

What are the herbal preparations of Passiflora jugorum?

Passiflora jugorum is prepared as a dried liquid extract, a concentrated tincture, or a fine powder derived from the aerial parts of the plant. These various forms allow for flexible dosing depending on whether a patient prefers a fast-acting liquid or a stable capsule. Tinctures are often favored for their rapid absorption through the mucous membranes. Alternatively, the dried plant material can be steeped into herbal teas to create a mild infusion. Each preparation method preserves the essential alkaloids necessary for the plant's therapeutic effects.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Passiflora jugorum can cause side effects such as extreme drowsiness, dizziness, confusion, and gastrointestinal upset like nausea or stomach cramps. Because the plant contains sedative properties, it may significantly impair your ability to drive or operate heavy machinery. Some individuals might also experience feelings of lightheadedness or a drop in blood pressure. It is important to note that combining this plant with other sedative medications can lead to dangerous levels of lethargy. Consult a medical professional before use to avoid potential adverse reactions or interactions.
