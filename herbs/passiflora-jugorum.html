Passiflora jugorum

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora jugorum?

Passiflora jugorum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a member of the family Passifloraceae, falling within the genus Passiflora, and is specifically identified as the species jugorum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Passiflora jugorum has woody climbing vines characterized by trifoliate leaves, tendrils for attachment, and large, complex flowers with prominent corona filaments. The foliage typically consists of three distinct leaflets that exhibit a smooth or slightly serrated margin. Its climbing mechanism relies on specialized coiled tendrils that emerge from the nodes to secure the plant to surrounding vegetation. The inflorescence features a dramatic corona composed of multiple layers of colorful filaments surrounding a central structure of reproductive organs. These flowers are often accompanied by large, fleshy fruits that develop from the inferior ovary.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Passiflora jugorum contains high concentrations of alkaloids, flavonoids, and essential oils that serve as its primary medicinal agents. The presence of harmala alkaloids provides significant sedative and antispasmodic effects for the central nervous system. Flavonoids such as vitexin and apigenin contribute to the plant's ability to reduce anxiety and promote relaxation. Additionally, the essential oils within the leaves offer mild antimicrobial properties that support overall wellness. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Passiflora jugorum targets gamma-aminobutyric acid type A receptors, serotonin receptors, and various monoamine oxidase enzymes to exert its sedative and anxiolytic effects. The bioactive alkaloids and flavonoids within the plant act as modulators of the central nervous system by enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission. Specifically, certain compounds increase the binding affinity of GABA to its receptor sites, which helps reduce neuronal excitability. These interactions facilitate a calming effect on the brain by influencing the pathways responsible for mood regulation and sleep induction. Additionally, the inhibition of certain enzymes may prolong the presence of neurotransmitters in the synaptic cleft, further contributing to its medicinal properties.
