Sambucus javanica

What's the taxonomical classification of Sambucus javanica?

Sambucus javanica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is classified under the order Dipsacales. It is a member of the family Viburnaceae, specifically falling within the genus Sambucus, and is identified by its unique species name, javanica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sambucus javanica has decumbent to erect stems that reach heights of up to two meters, characterized by opposite, pinnately compound leaves with five to seven serrated leaflets. The plant features terminal cymes of creamy white, five-lobed flowers that emerge from clusters of small, inconspicuous buds. Its fruit consists of small, globose, dark purple to black berries that hang in dense bunches. The leaf petioles and stems often exhibit a reddish tint, providing a visual contrast to the green foliage. This shrubby species typically develops a woody base as it matures in its natural habitat.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains aloin, emodin, and various anthraquinones that function primarily as potent stimulant laxatives. These specific chemical compounds interact with the intestinal lining to increase water secretion and stimulate peristaltic contractions. In addition to these laxative agents, the plant possesses antioxidant properties due to the presence of polyphenols and flavonoids. Some studies also suggest that certain alkaloids within the leaves may provide mild anti-inflammatory effects.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and stomach cramps if the raw berries or stems are ingested. These gastrointestinal symptoms typically arise from the presence of cyanogenic glycosides found in the unprepared parts of the species. Consuming even small amounts of these toxic compounds can lead to significant digestive distress shortly after ingestion. Such adverse reactions often subside once the harmful substances are expelled from the body. It is essential to ensure all parts of the plant are properly processed to avoid these unpleasant side effects.
