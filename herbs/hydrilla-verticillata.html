Hydrilla verticillata

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hydrilla verticillata has slender, cylindrical stems that can reach lengths of up to 30 centimeters and feature whorls of small, lanceolate leaves arranged in multiples of four. These leaves typically measure between 1 and 3 centimeters in length and possess serrated margins along their edges. The plant lacks true roots, instead utilizing adventitious roots that emerge from the stem nodes to anchor itself in the substrate. Small, white, four-petaled flowers may emerge above the water surface during reproductive stages. This submerged aquatic species is characterized by its rapid growth and ability to form dense, tangled mats.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to parts of Europe, Asia, and Australia but has become a widespread invasive species in North American freshwater ecosystems. It was first documented in the United States during the 1950s and has since spread through various waterways via boat trailers and fishing gear. Its ability to thrive in diverse environments allows it to colonize lakes, ponds, and slow-moving rivers across many different climate zones. The species often dominates local habitats by forming dense mats that alter the physical and chemical properties of the water. Because it can tolerate a wide range of depths and light levels, its range continues to expand globally.
