To identify Scardic Sideritis (Sideritis scardica) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Scardic Sideritis, or Sideritis scardica, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically within the flowering plants. It is classified under the order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Sideritis, and species Sideritis scardica. This classification places it among the mint family plants, known for their aromatic properties and medicinal uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Scardic Sideritis is Sideritis scardica, first described by the botanist Georg Friedrich Mohr in 1840. It is also known by several synonyms, including Leontopodium scardicum and Sideritis scardica var. carduica. Common names include Scardic Sideritis and Sideris. It is known by regional names such as "Sideritis de Scarda" in Italy and "Sideritis scardica" in Greece.

Morphological Characteristics

Scardic Sideritis is a perennial herb with a compact growth habit, often forming dense clumps. Its root system is fibrous and well-developed, anchoring the plant firmly. The stems are upright, branching, and covered in fine hairs. Leaves are lance-shaped, hairy, and arranged oppositely along the stem. The inflorescence is a dense spike of flowers, typically pink to purple in color. Flowers are small, tubular, and bilaterally symmetrical. The fruit is a small capsule that splits open to release tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Sideritis scardica can be distinguished by its narrow, linear leaves with a hairy underside, pale blue to purple flowers in whorled clusters, and a distinctive, aromatic scent. It grows in high-altitude meadows and is often found with other endemic species, helping in its identification. Its unique combination of morphological and aromatic traits sets it apart from similar species in the genus.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Sideritis scardica is native to the mountainous regions of the Balkans, particularly Albania and Kosovo. It thrives in temperate climates with mild summers and cold winters. The plant prefers well-drained, calcareous soils. It is typically found at altitudes between 1,200 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Scardic Sideritis blooms from June to August, producing small pink to purple flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer, typically between August and September. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during early flowering, from June to July, when the characteristic features are most distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Sideritis scardica belongs to the Lamiaceae family and is closely related to other Sideritis species. It shares morphological and genetic traits with Sideritis albanica and Sideritis romana. Its genus relations highlight its role in the Sideritis clade, which includes several Mediterranean herbs with similar chemical compositions and medicinal uses.
