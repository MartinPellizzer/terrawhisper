Botanical Identification of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum)

To identify Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Jasmine, or Jasminum grandiflorum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Oleales, family Oleaceae, genus Jasminum, and species Jasminum grandiflorum. This classification places it within the flowering plants, specifically in the olive family, highlighting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Jasminum grandiflorum is the scientific name for Jasmine, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by synonyms such as Jasminum officinale and Jasminum sambac. Common names include Jasmine flower and night-blooming jasmine, while regional names vary across cultures. Historically, it has been referred to by names like "Arborvitae" in ancient texts.

Morphological Characteristics

Jasminum grandiflorum is a deciduous shrub with a compact growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly. The stem is woody and branched, with a smooth, greyish-brown bark. Leaves are opposite, ovate, and have a pointed tip, with a glossy dark green upper surface. The inflorescence consists of clusters of small, white, fragrant flowers. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, dry seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Jasminum grandiflorum can be distinguished by its white, star-shaped flowers with a strong fragrance, opposite leaf arrangement, and smooth, dark green leaves. It differs from similar species like Jasminum sambac by its larger flowers and more robust stems. The presence of a terminal umbel of flowers is another key identifying feature.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Jasminum grandiflorum is native to regions of southern Europe, the Middle East, and parts of Asia, including the Mediterranean and India. It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters and warm summers. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at elevations between 500 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Jasminum grandiflorum blooms primarily during the winter and early spring, producing fragrant white flowers. Seed formation occurs after flowering, typically in late spring to early summer. Harvesting of flowers is best done in the early morning when the blooms are fully open, ensuring maximum fragrance and potency for herbal use.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Jasminum grandiflorum is closely related to other jasmine species within the genus Jasminum. It shares key traits with its closest relatives, such as similar floral structures and aromatic compounds. Its placement within the Oleaceae family highlights its evolutionary ties to other members like Forsythia and Fraxinus. These relationships aid in understanding its ecological and medicinal significance.
