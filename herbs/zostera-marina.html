Scientific and Botanical Profile

Zostera Grass, with botanical name Zostera marina, is a species of seagrass belonging to the family Zosteraceae.

It is commonly known as Eelgrass, Marine Grass, Sea Grass, and Common Eelgrass, and is widely recognized as a vital component of marine ecosystems. Native to the coastal waters of the Northern Hemisphere, including the Atlantic and Pacific coasts, this plant thrives in shallow, brackish, and freshwater environments. Morphologically, Zostera marina features long, ribbon-like leaves that can grow up to 1 meter in length, with a dense network of roots that anchor it to the seabed.

It produces small, inconspicuous flowers that develop into cylindrical, dark brown fruits, contributing to its role as a foundational species in estuarine and coastal habitats.

History and Cultural Relevance

Zostera Grass continues to symbolize resilience and connection to the sea, reflecting its enduring cultural and ecological significance.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Zostera marina has been utilized in traditional medicine for its purported therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms such as fresh or dried plant material, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, it can be brewed into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion for topical applications, such as compresses or salves, to address skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 teaspoons of the dried herb steeped in hot water as a tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

Due to limited scientific evidence, it is advised to use zostera marina sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to minimize potential risks.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Zostera marina can be used as a medicinal plant with potential benefits, but it is important to consider its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals. There is limited research on drug or herb interactions, but caution is advised when combining it with medications that affect the nervous system or blood pressure. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient data on fetal or infant safety, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a qualified healthcare professional, start with small doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Zostera marina grows in shallow coastal waters, typically in areas with sandy or muddy substrates, where it thrives in brackish or saltwater environments.

It requires full sun to partial shade and consistent water flow to support its growth, though it is highly tolerant of varying salinity levels. For optimal growth, the plant should be planted in well-draining soil with a pH between 6. 5 and 8. 0, and regular fertilization with nitrogen and potassium can enhance its biomass. Care and maintenance involve monitoring for pests and diseases, ensuring water quality remains stable, and pruning dead or damaged parts to promote healthy growth.

Harvesting is best done during the spring and summer months when the plant is most nutrient-rich, using a sharp tool to cut the stems close to the base without damaging the rhizomes, and storage should involve drying the plant in a cool, shaded area followed by sealing in airtight containers to preserve its medicinal potency.
