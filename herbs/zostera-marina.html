Scientific and Botanical Profile

Marine Grass, with botanical name Zostera marina, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Zosteraceae and is commonly known as Zostera Grass, Eelgrass, Marine Eelgrass, Seagrass, Sea Grass, or Marine Grass.

It is a submerged aquatic plant found in various coastal regions, including the North Atlantic Ocean, Coastal Areas of Europe, North America, Japan, Korea, the Mediterranean Sea, the Black Sea, the Sea of Japan, the Baltic Sea, and the North Sea. Morphologically, Zostera marina is a perennial plant with long, ribbon-like leaves that are typically 10-30 cm in length, forming dense underwater meadows that provide critical habitat for marine life. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers in clusters, which are followed by elongated, cylindrical fruits that disperse via water currents.

As a key component of seagrass ecosystems, Zostera marina plays an essential role in coastal marine biodiversity and carbon sequestration.

History and Cultural Relevance

Marine Grass was used by ancient coastal civilizations for its medicinal and practical benefits, with evidence of its use dating back thousands of years in regions such as the Mediterranean, Scandinavia, and along the coasts of Japan and the Pacific Northwest.

In traditional medicine systems, it was valued for its ability to soothe respiratory issues, aid digestion, and promote wound healing, often prepared as decoctions or poultices. Culturally, Marine Grass played a role in various rituals, including those related to fertility, protection, and spiritual cleansing, with some coastal communities incorporating it into ceremonial offerings or as a symbol of connection to the sea. Its fibrous structure also made it an essential material for basket weaving, boat building, and as a natural binding agent in construction.

Today, some of these traditional remedies, such as its use in respiratory treatments and skin care, remain relevant in modern herbal practices, reflecting its enduring legacy in both ecological and cultural contexts.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Zostera marina has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms including fresh, dried, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare Zostera marina, it can be brewed into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or made into a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion or applied topically as a compress for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried plant material per cup of tea, while children should only use it if deemed safe by a healthcare professional.

Due to limited scientific evidence, it is advised to use Zostera marina sparingly, with a maximum duration of use not exceeding two weeks unless under medical supervision.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Zostera marina can be used as a medicinal plant with potential benefits for respiratory and inflammatory conditions, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when used in moderation, it may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals. There is limited research on its interactions with drugs or other herbs, but caution is advised when combined with anticoagulants or anti-inflammatory medications due to potential additive effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using Zostera marina due to insufficient evidence on its safety during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to follow recommended dosages, consult a qualified healthcare professional, and discontinue use if adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Zostera marina grows in brackish or saltwater environments, typically in shallow coastal areas with sandy or muddy substrates, where it forms dense underwater meadows.

It requires full sunlight penetration for optimal photosynthesis and thrives in water temperatures between 10°C and 25°C. Regular water circulation is essential to prevent stagnation, and the plant benefits from moderate salinity levels, although it can tolerate a range of salinities. For care and maintenance, periodic monitoring for pests and diseases is important, and removing dead or damaged foliage helps maintain plant health and promote new growth.

Harvesting is best done during the summer months when the plant is most nutritious, using a sharp tool to cut the stems close to the base without damaging the root system, and storage should involve drying the plant in a cool, shaded area followed by sealing in airtight containers to preserve its medicinal potency.
