What is Zostera Grass (Zostera Marina)?

Zostera marina, commonly known as Zostera Grass, is a submerged marine plant found in coastal areas, characterized by its ribbon-like leaves and ability to thrive in brackish and saltwater environments.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Zostera grass is Zostera marina. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Charophyta, class Zosteraceae, order Zosterales, family Zosteraceae, genus Zostera, and species Zostera marina. It is commonly known as eelgrass in the North Atlantic region and sea grass in the North Pacific region. The synonyms for this plant include Zostera noltii, Zostera caespitosa, and regional names such as "green grass" in parts of Europe.

Zostera grass is a submerged aquatic plant that typically grows in dense underwater meadows, reaching heights of up to 1 meter with a fibrous, ribbon-like structure.

Its leaves are long, narrow, and strap-shaped, often measuring up to 30 centimeters in length, with a smooth, waxy surface. The plant produces small, clustered flowers that are usually white or pale blue, arranged in spikelets along the stem. Its root system consists of a complex network of rhizomes and fibrous roots that anchor it to the seabed and facilitate nutrient absorption.

The leaves are generally green, with a slight bluish tint, and have a firm, leathery texture, while the plant has a mild, grassy scent.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Zostera grass is native to the coastal regions of the Northern Hemisphere, including the Atlantic and Pacific coasts of North America, the Mediterranean, and parts of Asia.
