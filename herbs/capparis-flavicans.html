Capparis flavicans

What's the taxonomical classification of Capparis flavicans?

Capparis flavicans belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized under the order Brassicales. The plant is a representative of the family Capparaceae, falling specifically within the genus Capparis, and is identified by its unique species name, flavicans.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Capparis flavicans has a woody, perennial shrub structure characterized by spreading branches and trifoliate leaves composed of three distinct oval-shaped leaflets. The foliage is typically leathery in texture and can exhibit a slightly hairy surface on the undersides. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the axils, featuring prominent long stamens that create a delicate appearance. Following pollination, the plant produces fleshy, globose berries that transition in color as they mature. These morphological traits allow the species to adapt effectively to its native arid and semi-arid environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Capparis flavicans?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including antiestrogenic, galactogogue, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties. Its antiestrogenic effects make it a subject of interest for research into hormonal regulation and breast cancer prevention. As a galactogogue, it is traditionally used to stimulate and increase milk production in lactating women. Additionally, the presence of bioactive compounds like flavonoids contributes to its ability to combat oxidative stress within cells. These combined medicinal attributes highlight its significant potential in both traditional medicine and modern drug development.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of the Thai medicinal plant Capparis flavicans yielded the new compound capparoside A (1) alongside 28 known compounds. These isolates were tested for biological activity using the estrogen-responsive human breast cancer cell lines MCF-7 and T47D. Among the compounds isolated from C. flavicans, syringaresinol (3) and principin (6) showed the most potent inhibitory effects on estrogen-enhanced cell proliferation. These results suggest that the lactation-promoting properties of C. flavicans might be related to its inhibitory effect on excess estrogen.
