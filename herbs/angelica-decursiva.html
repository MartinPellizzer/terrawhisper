Angelica decursiva

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica decursiva?

Angelica decursiva belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Apiales. As a member of the family Apiaceae, this plant is placed within the genus Angelica, ultimately identified by its specific species epithet, decursiva.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica decursiva has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 100 centimeters and features leaves with long, decurrent petioles that extend down the stem. The foliage is characterized by large, compound leaves that are divided into multiple leaflets with serrated margins. Small, white flowers are arranged in dense, terminal umbels consisting of numerous tiny individual florets. The plant produces small, oblong fruits that turn from green to brown upon maturity. Its root system is robust and fleshy, providing the structural foundation for its upright growth habit.

What are the pharmacological activities of Angelica decursiva?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antitussive, and expectorant pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to reduce pain sensations and mitigate swelling by inhibiting specific inflammatory mediators. The presence of bioactive compounds also allows the plant to neutralize free radicals, providing significant antioxidant protection to cells. Furthermore, it serves as a respiratory aid by suppressing cough reflexes and promoting the thinning of mucus. These diverse medicinal properties make it a valuable subject for therapeutic research in treating respiratory and inflammatory conditions.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Angelica decursiva are used in traditional medicine as anti-inflammatory, antitussive, analgesic, and expectorant agents for treating asthma, bronchitis, cough, and upper respiratory tract infections. Water and 70% ethanol extracts of the roots showed inhibitory activity against LPS-induced lung inflammation in mice when administered orally at a dose of 400 mg/kg. Five coumarin derivatives were isolated from the plant, including columbianadin, umbelliferone, umbelliferone 6-carboxyliic acid, nodakenin, and nodakenetin. The constituent columbianadin possesses strong inhibitory activity against the inflammatory response of IL-1β-treated A549 cells and LPS-treated MH-S cells. Furthermore, columbianadin shows significant inhibitory activity against LPS-induced lung inflammation following oral administration at a dose of 20-60 mg/kg.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds bergapten, coumarin, imperatoriin, scopoletin, and umbelliferone. These specific chemical constituents are categorized as furanocoumarins and related phenylpropanoids. Each compound contributes to the plant's overall therapeutic profile through distinct biological activities. For instance, certain derivatives are studied for their potential effects on the central nervous system and skin pigmentation. Research into these molecules continues to explore their diverse pharmacological applications in modern medicine.

As discussed in a study, Angelica decursiva is an important traditional medicinal plant possessing unique pharmacological activity. Its principal active ingredients are coumarins, which include scopoletin, bergapten, and imperatorin. Researchers screened 14 candidate O-methyltransferases through transcriptomics and metabolic determination to identify the enzymes responsible for these compounds. Two specific O-methyltransferases, AdOMT1 and AdOMT2, were identified to be responsible for the O-methylation of coumarins in the plant. Notably, AdOMT1 showed a higher catalytic efficiency for bergaptol with a Kcat/Km value of 3123.70. The study also identified key catalytic residues including His126/Asn132, Phe171/Phe177, Trp261/Trp267, and Asn312/Ile317 for the O-methylation of bergaptol and xanthotozol.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating upper respiratory tract infections, asthma, cough, fevers, and thick phlegm. It is frequently utilized in traditional medicine to help clear congestion from the lungs and throat. By acting as an expectorant, the herb assists in breaking down and expelling thick phlegm more easily. It also serves to soothe the respiratory system during episodes of asthma or persistent coughing. Additionally, its properties are often employed to help reduce fevers associated with various ailments.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions including nausea, diarrhea, stomach discomfort, dizziness, and increased photosensitivity. Individuals consuming it may experience significant gastrointestinal issues such as upset stomach or irregular bowel movements. Neurological side effects like dizziness can also occur during or after use. Furthermore, the plant contains compounds that may lead to photosensitivity, making the skin more susceptible to sunburn. Monitoring these symptoms is essential for anyone using this botanical substance.
