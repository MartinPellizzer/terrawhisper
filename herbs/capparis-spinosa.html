Capparis spinosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Capparis spinosa?

Capparis spinosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is situated in the order Brassicales. It is a member of the family Capparaceae, falling under the genus Capparis, and is specifically identified by its species name, spinosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Capparis spinosa has a woody, perennial shrub structure characterized by spreading branches that often terminate in sharp, formidable spines. The leaves are typically leathery, oval-shaped, and possess a glaucous or grayish-green hue on their surfaces. Large, showy white flowers emerge from the leaf axils, featuring numerous long, prominent stamens that create a brush-like appearance. The plant also produces fleshy, globose fruits that transition from green to a bright red color as they mature. These morphological adaptations allow the species to thrive in harsh, rocky, and arid Mediterranean environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean Basin, spanning across southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. It thrives primarily in coastal regions and arid environments where rocky terrain provides necessary drainage. Its range extends from the Atlantic coast of Morocco through the Levant and into parts of central Asia. Because it is highly adaptable to drought, it can be found in various semi-desert landscapes across these territories. The species is frequently encountered in sun-drenched, limestone-rich habitats along the shores of the Mediterranean Sea.

What are the pharmacological activities of Capparis spinosa?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anticancer, antibacterial, and antidiabetic properties. Its rich concentration of bioactive compounds like flavonoids and alkaloids allows it to effectively neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress in the body. These phytochemicals also demonstrate a capacity to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and suppress inflammatory pathways. Furthermore, research indicates that extracts from the plant can assist in regulating blood glucose levels and interfering with the proliferation of malignant cells. These multi-targeted therapeutic actions make it a significant subject in medicinal chemistry research.

As discussed in a study, Capparis spinosa has been used as a traditional medicine to relieve ailments including rheumatism, pain, and inflammatory diseases. In vivo experiments using 40 male rats showed that treatment with 100 and 300 mg/kg/d of the extract before LPS administration (1 mg/kg/d; i.p. for 4 weeks) resulted in lower escape latency in the Morris water maze compared to the LPS group (p < 0.001). In vitro studies using primary microglia pretreated with 10-300 μg/ml of the extract and stimulated with 1 μg/ml of LPS revealed that the extract significantly reduced levels of TNF-α, iNOS, COX-2, IL-1β, IL-6, NO, and PGE2 (p < 0.001). The extract also significantly lowered the levels of IL-1β, TNF-α, and the iNOS/Arg-1 ratio (p < 0.001), as well as the NO/urea ratio. These findings provide evidence that Capparis spinosa has a neuroprotective effect against neuroinflammation and cognitive impairment.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, flavanols, quercetin, rutin, and alkaloids. Within the flavonoid group, the plant specifically provides high concentrations of the flavanols quercetin and rutin. These specific polyphenols are known for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties in human health. Additionally, the presence of various alkaloids contributes to the complex pharmacological profile of the species. Together, these bioactive substances work synergistically to provide therapeutic benefits.

As discussed in a study, the phytochemical analysis of Capparis spinosa showed that total phenol, flavonoids, alkaloids, and vitamin C were significantly higher (P ≤ 0.05) in the methanolic extract than in other solvents. Tannin content was significantly high (P ≤ 0.05) in the water extract of the plant. GC-MS analysis identified isopropyl isothiocyanate as one dominant compound in Capparis spinosa. The methanolic extract exerted higher antioxidant activity in DPPH, ABTS, β-Carotene/linoleic acid assay, and reducing the power methods, though it was lower than the BHA standard. Additionally, the methanolic extract inhibited bacterial pathogens when a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) method was applied.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Type 2 diabetes, inflammation, liver damage, oxidative stress, and cancer. Its bioactive compounds work to regulate blood glucose levels and improve insulin sensitivity in diabetic patients. The plant contains potent antioxidant properties that neutralize free radicals to combat oxidative stress throughout the body. These medicinal qualities also help reduce systemic inflammation and protect hepatic cells from various forms of liver damage. Furthermore, research suggests that its phytochemicals may inhibit the growth and spread of certain cancer cells.

As discussed in a study, C. spinosa oxymel was administered at 10 mL/thrice daily for 3 months to 30 diabetic patients with metabolic syndrome. This preparation consisted of hydroalcoholic extract of C. spinosa fruit added to simple oxymel. Compared to baseline, C. spinosa oxymel significantly decreased body weight and body mass index. While the placebo and simple oxymel groups displayed further increases in FBG or PPBG, C. spinosa oxymel inhibited the progression of hyperglycemia. Furthermore, it inhibited the progression of hypertriglyceridemia, although it had no significant effect on serum cholesterol. The treatment also showed no significant changes in creatinine, microalbuminuria, AST, ALT, and ALP values.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain in some individuals. These gastrointestinal side effects may occur when the plant is consumed in large quantities or by sensitive users. Low blood sugar levels, known as hypoglycemia, are a specific concern for those monitoring their glucose. Managing these reactions often requires monitoring the intensity of the digestive distress. It is important to be cautious when using this species for medicinal purposes.
