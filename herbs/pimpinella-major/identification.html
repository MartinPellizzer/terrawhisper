Botanical Identification of Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major)

To identify Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Wild Angelica, or Pimpinella major, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the flowering plants. It is classified under the order Apiales, family Apiaceae, which includes aromatic herbs. The genus Pimpinella contains several species, with Pimpinella major being one of the more notable ones in herbal medicine.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Wild Angelica is Pimpinella major, first described by the Swedish botanist Carl von Linné in the 18th century. It is also known by several synonyms, including Pimpinella latifolia and Angelica major, though these are now considered outdated. Common names such as "Wild Angelica" and "Large-leafed Hogweed" reflect its appearance and historical use in herbal medicine. Regional and historical names vary, but the species has long been recognized for its medicinal properties.

Morphological Characteristics

Wild Angelica is a biennial plant with a taproot system that becomes thick and fleshy. It has a hollow, ridged stem that is often branched. The leaves are pinnately compound, with a large, heart-shaped leaflet at the base. The inflorescence is a complex umbel, with small white flowers arranged in a flat-topped cluster. The fruit is a dry, three-lobed schizocarp, containing two tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Wild Angelica can be distinguished by its large, compound leaves with serrated edges and a distinct, carroty scent. It has a tall, hollow stem with purple spots and small, yellowish flowers in umbrella-like clusters. These features help differentiate it from similar species like Common Angelica and Cow Parsnip.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) is native to temperate regions of Europe and parts of western Asia. It thrives in cool climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found at elevations between 500 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Wild Angelica blooms from June to August, producing white umbels with 20-30 flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer, with seeds maturing by early autumn. For identification, the best time to observe key features is during the flowering period, typically between June and August.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Wild Angelica (Pimpinella major) is closely related to other members of the Apiaceae family, particularly species like Pimpinella anisum and Pimpinella latifolia. It shares key characteristics with its genus, such as compound umbels and aromatic foliage. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary placement and medicinal properties.
