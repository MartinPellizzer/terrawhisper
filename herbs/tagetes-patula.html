Tagetes patula

What's the taxonomical classification of Tagetes patula?

Tagetes patula belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the evolutionary hierarchy further, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by its specific genus, Tagetes, and its unique species designation, patula.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tagetes patula has a spreading, herbaceous growth habit characterized by slender, branched stems and pinnately compound leaves that feature small, serrated leaflets. The foliage is typically dark green and possesses a distinct, pungent aroma when crushed. Its inflorescences consist of solitary, daisy-like flower heads that vary in color from bright yellow to deep orange and reddish tones. These composite flowers are composed of both ray florets and disc florets arranged in a dense central cluster. The plant often reaches a height of approximately 20 to 30 centimeters, forming a compact mound of textured greenery.

As discussed in a study, Tagetes patula flowers possess medicinal properties including antioxidant and analgesic activities. The synthetic methyl protocatechuate (1) isolated from the plant showed an IC50 value of 2.8 ± 0.2 µg/mL. Patuletin (2) demonstrated an IC50 value of 4.3 ± 0.25 µg/mL, which was significantly better than the IC50 of 10.17 ± 1.16 µg/mL found in patulitrin (3). Additionally, compound 2 demonstrated mild analgesic property when evaluated using the acetic acid-induced writhing test and hot-plate test in mice. Toxicity tests for the methanol extract and compound 2 did not elicit any behavioral changes or cause mortality in mice.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation or contact dermatitis, allergic reactions, nausea, vomiting, stomach upset, and dizziness if ingested or touched. Direct contact with the foliage or flowers may trigger redness, itching, or a rash on sensitive skin. Some individuals might experience systemic allergic reactions such as swelling or respiratory discomfort. If the plant is consumed, it frequently leads to gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, and general stomach upset. Additionally, certain toxic compounds within the plant may cause dizziness in those affected.
