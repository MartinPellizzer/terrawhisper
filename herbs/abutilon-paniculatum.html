Abutilon paniculatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malvales and the family Malvacaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Abutilon, and its unique species designation, paniculatum.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin conditions such as eczema, psoriasis, and various types of dermatitis. Its leaves are often crushed or prepared into poultices to soothe inflammation and reduce itching on the skin. The plant also possesses antimicrobial properties that help combat superficial infections and wounds. In traditional medicine, extracts may be applied to address localized swelling or minor abrasions. These medicinal applications rely on the bioactive compounds found within the plant's foliage.

What are the herbal preparations of Abutilon paniculatum?

Abutilon paniculatum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste for medicinal use. Practitioners often boil the plant parts in water to create a concentrated liquid for oral consumption. Infusions are also made by steeping the leaves in hot water to preserve more delicate components. For external applications, the plant material can be crushed into a paste to treat skin conditions directly. These various methods allow for the extraction of different therapeutic properties depending on the intended ailment.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Abutilon paniculatum can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested by humans or animals. While the plant is primarily grown for its ornamental value, direct contact with its foliage or flowers may trigger redness and itching in sensitive individuals. There is limited toxicological research available regarding its systemic effects, so caution is advised when handling the plant. Allergic reactions often manifest as a localized rash or swelling upon contact with the skin. It is best to avoid consuming any part of the plant to prevent potential stomach upset or more severe internal reactions.
