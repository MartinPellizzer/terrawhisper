The scientific name of the plant is Tagetes erecta, which was first described by the botanist Linnaeus in the 18th century. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is commonly known as the African marigold. This name is widely accepted in botanical literature and is used to distinguish it from other species within the Tagetes genus.

Morphological Characteristics

Tagetes erecta, commonly known as the African marigold, is an annual herb with a straight, erect stem that can grow up to 60 cm tall. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, adapting to well-drained soils. The leaves are lance-shaped, dark green, and covered in fine hairs. The inflorescence consists of a single terminal head of bright yellow to orange flowers, which are composite, with ray florets surrounding disk florets. The fruit is a dry, dehiscent capsule that releases numerous small, black seeds.

Tagetes erecta, or the African marigold, can be distinguished by its upright growth habit, bright yellow-orange flowers with a prominent central disk, and smooth, green, lance-shaped leaves. It differs from similar species like Tagetes patula by its taller stature and more robust flower structure.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Tagetes erecta, or African marigold, is native to tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas. It thrives in warm climates with full sun exposure. It prefers well-drained, loamy soil and is commonly found at low to moderate altitudes, typically between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Tagetes erecta, commonly known as the African marigold, blooms from late spring to early autumn. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, harvesting is best done during the flowering stage to ensure accurate botanical recognition.
