What to know about Nerium oleander (oleander) before using it medicinally

Nerium oleander, commonly known as oleander, is a herb that has been prized for its health-promoting properties, which have been shown to improve heart health, reduce inflammation, and alleviate symptoms of anxiety and depression.

This versatile herb is also a popular choice for gardeners, as it is relatively low-maintenance and can thrive in a variety of conditions. From a botanical perspective, Nerium oleander is a shrub-like plant that belongs to the Apocynaceae family and is characterized by its glossy leaves and showy flowers.

The use of oleander has been documented throughout history, with ancient civilizations such as the Egyptians and Greeks utilizing it for medicinal and ceremonial purposes.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Nerium oleander.

What are the medicinal properties of Nerium oleander?

Nerium oleander helps with treating various ailments, including cardiovascular conditions, such as high blood pressure, and gastrointestinal issues like diarrhea and dysentery. It is also used to treat fever, rheumatism, and skin conditions like eczema and acne.

The active constituents of Nerium oleander that give it its medicinal properties include cardiac glycosides, specifically oleandrin and neriine. These compounds have been shown to have cardiotonic and inotropic effects, meaning they can strengthen heart contractions and improve cardiac function.

The most commonly used parts of the Nerium oleander plant for medicinal purposes are the leaves and flowers. The leaves are rich in cardiac glycosides, while the flowers are used to treat skin conditions and fever. The plant's roots and bark are also used, but to a lesser extent.

Using Nerium oleander improperly can lead to side effects like nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. In severe cases, it can cause cardiac toxicity, resulting in irregular heartbeats, shortness of breath, and even cardiac arrest.

Precautions when using Nerium oleander medicinally include using it only under the guidance of a healthcare professional, as the plant's toxicity can be severe. Additionally, individuals with pre-existing heart conditions, pregnant or breastfeeding women, and children should avoid using the plant entirely.

What are the horticulural aspects of Nerium oleander?

Nerium oleander grow best in well-draining soil with full sun and temperatures between 40-100°F (-40-38°C). They can thrive in poor soil, but optimal growth is achieved with moderate to high water and nutrient levels. Average annual rainfall is sufficient.

Planting tips for Nerium oleander involve digging a hole slightly larger than the root ball, about 6-8 hours before planting to allow soil to settle. Water thoroughly and fertilize in early spring and summer months with a balanced, water-soluble fertilizer.

Harvesting tips for Nerium oleander flowers are typically done when blooms are fully open, usually in late spring to early summer. Cut branches, not the entire plant, to encourage new growth. Pinch off buds that are not yet open to redirect energy to fully blooming flowers.

Pests affecting Nerium oleander include scale insects and whiteflies, while diseases include root rot, leaf spot, and stem canker. Regular pruning and water management practices help prevent root rot. Leaf spot and stem canker are often caused by overwatering or poor air circulation.

What are the botanical characteristics of Nerium oleander?

Nerium oleander, also known as the Oleander or Rose Bay, is a deciduous shrub that exhibits several distinctive botanical characteristics.

It typically grows to be 6-9 meters tall, with a spread of around 3-4 meters, and has a rounded or oval-shaped crown. The leaves of Nerium oleander are simple, opposite, and elliptical in shape, measuring between 5-15 cm in length and 2-5 cm in width. They have a pointed apex, a wavy margin, and a short petiole that is often hairy.

The flowers of Nerium oleander are showy and fragrant, appearing in clusters at the ends of branches or along the stems. Each flower has five petals, which are white or pinkish-white with a yellow center, and are highly toxic due to the presence of cardiac glycosides. The fruit of Nerium oleander is a capsule that contains several seeds, each measuring about 1-2 mm in length.

Overall, the botanical characteristics of Nerium oleander reflect its ability to thrive in warm climates and attract pollinators, making it a popular ornamental plant in many parts of the world.

Nerium oleander belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Gentianales, family Apocynaceae, genus Nerium, and species oleander.

It is a flowering plant that is commonly known as the oleander or rose laurel.

What are the variants of Nerium oleander?

Nerium oleander has variants, such as N. o. album, which is an albino or white-flowered variety, and N. o. rubrum, also known as Scarlet Oleander, featuring deep red flowers.

Another variant is N. o. variegatum, with leaves having yellow or cream-colored stripes. Additionally, there are cultivars like 'Petra', a compact and dwarf variety, and 'Compactum', which has smaller leaves and stems.

These variants offer different aesthetic options for gardeners and landscapers, allowing them to choose the best fit for their specific needs and preferences.

What is the geographic distribution of Nerium oleander?

Nerium oleander is native to the Mediterranean region, specifically in southwestern Europe, North Africa, and Western Asia.

It has been widely cultivated and naturalized in many parts of the world, including tropical and subtropical regions such as Australia, Hawaii, and South America. The plant thrives in areas with mild winters and hot summers, often growing along coastlines, rivers, and roadsides.

In its native range, Nerium oleander can be found in countries such as Spain, France, Italy, Greece, Turkey, and Morocco, where it has been used for centuries in traditional medicine and ornamental horticulture.

What is the life cycle of Nerium oleander?

Nerium oleander, also known as Oleander, has a unique and complex life-cycle.

The plant reproduces primarily through cuttings, seed, and offsets. It begins its life as a seedling that germinates from seeds released by the parent plant.

The young seedling grows into a mature plant within 6-12 months, producing showy flowers in shades of pink, red, yellow, and white. As the plant ages, it produces offsets or suckers around its base, which can be separated and grown into new plants. Through this process, Oleander can spread rapidly and form dense thickets.

However, the plant's life-cycle is often short-lived due to its susceptibility to pests, diseases, and environmental factors.

What are the historical uses of Nerium oleander?

Nerium oleander has a rich history of use dating back thousands of years.

In ancient Greece, it was believed to have medicinal properties and was used to treat various ailments, including epilepsy and snakebites. The plant's wood was also used for making furniture, while its leaves were used as a natural dye.

In many Mediterranean cultures, oleander has been used in traditional medicine, beauty treatments, and even as an ingredient in perfumes.
