Botanical Identification of Guggul (Commiphora mukul)

To identify Guggul (Commiphora mukul) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Guggul, or Commiphora mukul, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Burserales, family Burseraceae, genus Commiphora, and species Commiphora mukul. This classification places it within the group of flowering plants known for their aromatic resins.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name of Guggul is Commiphora mukul, a member of the Burseraceae family. It was first described by the botanist A.R. Stewart in 1873. Synonyms include Commiphora wightii and Commiphora guggul. Common names include Indian bdellium and guggul resin, while regional names vary across different parts of India.

Morphological Characteristics

Guggul, or Commiphora mukul, is a small to medium-sized shrub or tree. It has a shallow root system that spreads horizontally. Its stem is straight, often branching near the base, and covered with rough, fissured bark. The leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptical, and have a leathery texture with a dark green upper surface. The inflorescence consists of small, clustered panicles. The flowers are unisexual, greenish-yellow, and lack showy petals. The fruit is a globose capsule that splits open to release small, black, aromatic seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Guggul, or Commiphora mukul, can be distinguished by its aromatic, yellowish resinous exudate, unique leaf morphology with opposite, ovate leaves, and its characteristic swollen, woody base. It differs from similar species like Commiphora wightii through its distinct leaf shape and the presence of a prominent, rounded rootstock.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Guggul, or Commiphora mukul, is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly regions like Rajasthan, Gujarat, and parts of Pakistan. It thrives in arid and semi-arid climates with well-drained sandy or loamy soils. It is commonly found at altitudes ranging from 300 to 1,500 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Guggul (Commiphora mukul) blooms from March to May, producing small greenish-yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to September. Harvesting of the resin is typically done during the dry season, from October to February, when the resin is most abundant and easily extracted.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Commiphora mukul, or Guggul, is closely related to species like Commiphora wightii and Commiphora gileadensis. It belongs to the genus Commiphora, which is part of the Burseraceae family. The family is characterized by aromatic resins and specific morphological traits, which are also evident in Guggul.
