Clerodendrum kwangtungense

What's the taxonomical classification of Clerodendrum kwangtungense?

Clerodendrum kwangtungense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Clerodendrum, with its specific designation being the species kwangtungense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clerodendrum kwangtungense has woody stems, opposite leaves with entire or slightly serrated margins, and small, tubular flowers arranged in dense terminal cymes.

The foliage consists of elliptical to lanceolate blades that exhibit a prominent pinnate venation pattern. These leaves are typically arranged in decussate pairs along the branches to maximize light interception. The inflorescence structure supports numerous individual florets that emerge from a central cluster. Each flower features a long, slender corolla tube characteristic of the Verbenaceae family.

Small, fleshy fruits develop following successful pollination of the floral structures.

        What are the herbal preparations of Clerodendrum kwangtungense?

        Clerodendrum kwangtungense is prepared primarily as a decoction through boiling the plant parts in water or as a topical paste applied directly to the skin. These liquid extracts are often consumed to address internal ailments such as digestive issues or inflammatory conditions. The powdered form of the dried leaves can also be integrated into capsules or mixed with honey for easier ingestion. In certain traditional practices, the fresh leaves are crushed to create a poultice for treating localized swelling or wounds. This versatility allows practitioners to tailor the administration method to the specific symptoms being treated.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Clerodendrum kwangtungense can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested without proper processing. While some traditional practices use the plant medicinally, its chemical constituents may trigger hypersensitivity reactions in sensitive individuals. Contact with the leaves or stems often leads to localized redness or itching on the skin. There is also a risk of stomach upset or nausea if the plant is consumed in incorrect dosages. Further clinical studies are necessary to fully document the long-term toxicological profile of this specific species.
