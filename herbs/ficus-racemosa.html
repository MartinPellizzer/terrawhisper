Ficus racemosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus racemosa?

Ficus racemosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Rosales, the plant falls into the family Moraceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Ficus and is specifically distinguished as the species racemosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus racemosa has a woody, evergreen growth habit characterized by spreading branches, elliptical leaves with acute tips, and small, pale yellow syconia produced in axillary clusters. The leaves are typically leathery in texture and feature prominent pinnate venation on the underside. Its stems are smooth and often exhibit lenticels which assist in gas exchange. The reproductive structures consist of specialized inflorescences called figs that enclose numerous tiny flowers within a fleshy receptacle. These plants can reach significant heights in tropical environments, forming dense canopies.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, including parts of India, China, Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia. It typically thrives in moist, lowland environments such as rainforest edges and riverbanks. Its presence is often concentrated in areas with high annual rainfall and warm temperatures. Within these habitats, it can be found growing both as an epiphyte and as a terrestrial tree. The distribution follows a pattern consistent with other tropical species that depend on humid forest ecosystems.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ficus racemosa contains medicinal properties primarily derived from its bark, leaves, and fruit. The bark is frequently utilized in traditional medicine to treat various ailments including inflammation and skin disorders. Extracts from the leaves are often applied or consumed to address issues related to diabetes and gastrointestinal health. Additionally, the fruit is used in some regional practices for its nutritional and therapeutic benefits. These diverse plant parts collectively contribute to the species' reputation as a valuable natural remedy.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ficus racemosa?

This plant has anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to stabilize blood glucose levels and reduce systemic inflammation throughout the body. The plant also exhibits significant antimicrobial properties that help combat various bacterial infections. Additionally, its potent antioxidant capacity protects cells from oxidative stress and damage. These combined effects contribute to its ability to shield the liver from toxic injuries and promote overall organ health.

As discussed in a study, Ficus racemosa fruit extract contains six compounds identified through GC-MS analysis. This extract demonstrated potential alpha-amylase and alpha-glucosidase inhibitory properties in biochemical enzyme assays. The plant extract showed better enzyme inhibitory property compared to the standard inhibitor, acarbose, with a P value of less than or equal to 0.05. In vitro studies showed minimal cytotoxicity of the extract, while it contained only trace levels of toxic elements. All 6 compounds showed favorable drug-likeness, with compound-3 displaying the strongest binding in docking studies and acting as a pivot molecule. These results suggest that Ficus racemosa fruits may be a potential source of antihyperglycemic agents.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds chlorogenic acid, alpinumisoflavone, bergenin, mucusisoflavone B, and β-sitosterol. These bioactive molecules contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile and therapeutic potential. Chlorogenic acid is recognized for its antioxidant and metabolic benefits, while bergenin often exhibits anti-inflammatory properties. The isoflavones, specifically alpinumisoflavone and mucusisoflavone B, provide unique phytochemical characteristics. Additionally, the presence of β-sitosterol supports various physiological functions within the body.

As discussed in a study, the aqueous extract of Ficus racemosa bark demonstrated 100 % neutralization of some enzymatic activities of Naja naja and Daboia russelii venom. HR-LCMS analysis tentatively identified 19 major phytocompounds in the bioactive fraction AEFRB-P3. Among these, Chlorogenic acid (CGA) was identified as the active constituent. CGA demonstrated in vitro neutralization of the enzymatic activity of Daboxin P with an IC50 of 28.248 ± 1.104 μM. Furthermore, CGA showed dose-dependent neutralization of PLA2, anti/pro-coagulant, proteolytic, and indirect haemolytic activities of Indian Big Four snake venoms.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating diabetes mellitus, diarrhea, inflammation, liver disorders, and urinary diseases. Its medicinal properties help in regulating blood sugar levels for individuals managing diabetes. The extracts can soothe gastrointestinal distress to alleviate symptoms of diarrhea. Additionally, the plant possesses anti-inflammatory qualities that assist in reducing swelling and pain throughout the body. It is also applied in traditional medicine to support liver function and address various urinary diseases.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction of the bark for treating digestive issues, an infusion of the leaves for cooling the body, or a fresh juice from the fruit for nutritional support. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if consumed or handled improperly. The milky white sap contains proteolytic enzymes that may trigger redness, itching, or swelling upon contact with the skin. Ingestion of the plant parts can lead to stomach upset or more severe digestive complications. Because its toxicity levels are not fully documented in human clinical studies, caution is necessary when using it. Always perform a patch test and consult a professional before any exposure.
