Botanical Identification of Feverfew (Harpagophytum zeyheri)

To identify Feverfew (Harpagophytum zeyheri) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Feverfew, or Harpagophytum zeyheri, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the flowering plants. It is classified under the order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Harpagophytum, and species Harpagophytum zeyheri. The scientific name of Feverfew is Harpagophytum zeyheri, first described by the botanist Friedrich Anton Wilhelm Miquel. Morphological Characteristics

Feverfew (Harpagophytum zeyheri) is a perennial shrub with a fibrous root system. It has upright, branched stems that are woody at the base. The leaves are alternate, pinnately compound with serrated leaflets. The inflorescence is a terminal raceme, bearing small, yellow flowers. The fruit is a dry capsule containing numerous small seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Feverfew (Harpagophytum zeyheri) can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow flowers with orange centers, opposite leaves with serrated edges, and the presence of a hooked fruit. It is often confused with other Harpagophytum species, but its unique floral structure and leaf morphology help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Feverfew (Harpagophytum zeyheri) is native to southern Africa, particularly South Africa. It thrives in warm, temperate climates with well-drained soils. It commonly grows at elevations between 600 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Feverfew (Harpagophytum zeyheri) blooms from September to November. Seed formation occurs between December and February. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during its flowering stage, typically in late summer to early autumn.
