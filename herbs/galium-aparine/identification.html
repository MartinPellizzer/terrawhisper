Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Cleavers is Galium aparine. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Common synonyms include Galium hirsutum and Galium verum. It is also known by regional names such as bedstraw and cleavers, with historical references to its use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Cleavers (Galium aparine) is a low-growing annual herb with a sprawling growth habit. It has a slender, branched root system that anchors it to the soil. The stems are thin, hairy, and often lie close to the ground, rooting at the nodes. Leaves are alternate, heart-shaped, and have fine, hooked hairs that aid in seed dispersal. The inflorescence consists of small, greenish-white flowers arranged in clusters. Each flower has five petals and is surrounded by bracts. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release tiny, hooked seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cleavers can be distinguished by its sticky, hooked hairs on the stems and leaves, which are absent in most similar species. It has opposite, heart-shaped leaves and small white flowers in clusters. The stems are square and often sprawling, setting it apart from other Galium species. Its distinctive appearance makes it relatively easy to identify in the field.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cleavers, or Galium aparine, is native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates and prefers moist, nutrient-rich soils. It commonly grows in low-lying areas, along stream banks, and in woodland edges, typically at altitudes below 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cleavers blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between May and September. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to October. For identification purposes, the most accurate harvest time is during the flowering period when the plant is most distinctive.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cleavers, or Galium aparine, is closely related to other species within the Galium genus. It shares key characteristics with other members of the Rubiaceae family, such as opposite leaves and small, clustered flowers. Its phylogenetic position highlights its evolutionary ties to other trailing herbs with similar growth habits.
