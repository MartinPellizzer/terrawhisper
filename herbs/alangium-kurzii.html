Alangium kurzii

What's the taxonomical classification of Alangium kurzii?

Aliangium kurzii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Cornales. It is further categorized under the family Cornaceae, specifically falling within the genus Aliangium, with the distinct species designation kurzii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Alangium kurzii has a woody, perennial structure characterized by aromatic, branched stems and elliptical to lanceolate leaves with prominent venation. The foliage typically displays a glossy texture and deep green pigmentation on the upper surface. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves in clusters. These floral structures eventually develop into fleshy, drupe-like fruits that contain several seeds. The overall vegetative form is adapted to the humid, shaded environments of its native tropical habitat.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as Kurz's ginger, requires well-draining, humus-rich soil and a warm, humid environment with dappled sunlight to thrive.

Growers should maintain consistent moisture levels without allowing the soil to become waterlogged, as this can lead to rhizome rot. Because it is a tropical species, it performs best in temperatures ranging from 65 to 85 degrees Fahrenheit. Providing organic mulch around the base helps stabilize soil temperature and retain necessary humidity.

Periodic fertilization with a balanced organic compound will encourage lush foliage and healthy root development.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds alangisiide, demethylalangisiide, flavonoid glycosides, iridoid glucoside, and tetrahydroisoquinoline-monoterpene glucoside. These specific chemical constituents are responsible for the diverse pharmacological activities observed in the species. The presence of various flavonoid glycosides contributes to the plant's antioxidant properties. Additionally, the iridoid glucoside and tetrahydroisoquinoline-monoterpene glucoside play significant roles in its biological profile. These complex molecules work together to define the therapeutic potential of Alangium kurzii.

According to a study published by "Chemical & pharmaceuitaal bulletin", the leaves of Alangium kurzii contain a new tetrahydroisoquinoline-monoterpene glucoside known as 6-O-methyl-N-deacetylipecosidic acid. The plant also yields a new iridoid glucoside identified as 10-O-benzoyladoxosidic acid. Researchers isolated alangiside, demethylalangiside, and 6''-O-beta-D-glucosylhenryoside from the species. Additionally, the leaves contain uridine and 4 known flavonoid glycosides.
