Cirsium griseum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cirsium griseum has erect stems that reach heights of up to 100 centimeters and are covered in dense, grayish-white tomentose hairs. The leaves are deeply lobed and lanceolate, featuring a distinct silvery appearance due to the thick layer of fine pubescence on both surfaces. These leaves are typically arranged alternately along the stem and possess serrated margins. The plant produces terminal flower heads that are composed of numerous small, tubular florets. These inflorescences are characterized by their pale lavender to pinkish color and are surrounded by involucral bracts.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-elevation montane and subalpine regions of the Rocky Mountains in the western United States. Its range primarily extends through Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, and parts of New Mexico. It typically thrives in specific habitats such as open coniferous forests, mountain meadows, and rocky slopes. These populations are often found at altitudes ranging from mid-mountain to near the timberline. Because it is adapted to these cool, montane environments, its distribution is strictly limited by specific climatic and elevational requirements.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Cirsium griseum can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis or localized itching if the plant comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, individuals with allergies to the Asteraceae family should exercise extreme caution. Ingestion of unknown wild plants may lead to gastrointestinal distress, including nausea or stomach cramping. Some people might also experience respiratory irritation if they inhale dust or pollen from the plant's flowering heads. Always consult a medical professional if an adverse reaction occurs after exposure.
