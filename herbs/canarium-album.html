Canarium album

What's the taxonomical classification of Canarium album?

Canarium album belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Sapindales. This plant is a representative of the family Burseraceae, falling under the genus Canarium, and is specifically identified by its species name, album.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canarium album has a medium-sized tree structure characterized by a straight trunk, smooth grayish bark, and a crown of pinnately compound leaves that can reach up to 30 centimeters in length. The foliage consists of several pairs of leaflets that are typically elliptical or lanceolate in shape with entire margins. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the axils of the branches, eventually developing into fleshy drupes. These fruits are often yellowish or greenish when ripe and contain a single large seed. The overall vegetative morphology supports a dense canopy suitable for tropical forest environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South America, specifically ranging from southern Mexico through Central America and into various parts of northern and eastern South America. It is most frequently encountered in lowland rainforests and seasonal dry forests within these territories. The species thrives in humid environments where high precipitation levels support its growth patterns. Its distribution is closely tied to specific climatic zones that provide consistent warmth throughout the year. Many populations are concentrated in countries such as Brazil, Colombia, and Venezuela.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds hydrolysable tannin, isocorilagin, xanthotoxol, canaroleosides A-C, and esculetin. These specific chemical constituents are responsible for many of the biological activities attributed to the species. Hydrolysable tannins and isocorilagin contribute to its complex phytochemical profile. The presence of xanthotoxol and esculetin provides additional therapeutic potential through their unique molecular structures. Furthermore, the canaroleosides A-C represent specialized compounds found within its chemical makeup.

As discussed in a study, the fruit of Canarium album L. is rich in hydrolysable tannins (HTs) which serve as the primary material basis for its pharmacological effects. These HTs are polyphenols related to the medicinal value of the Chinese olive fruit. A total of 83 differentially expressed Chinese olive MYB transcription factors (CaMYBs) were identified, including 54 1R-MYBs, 25 2R-MYBs, 3 3R-MYBs, and 1 4R-MYB. The regulator CaMYBR04, also known as Isoform0032534, was found to exhibit a significantly higher expression (FPKM) than the other CaMYBs. Research demonstrated that CaMYBR04 significantly enhanced the fruit's hydrolysable tannin content.

    What health problems is this plant used for?

    This plant is used for treating respiratory infections and systemic symptoms caused by the influenza virus, specifically targeting strains like influenza A. Researchers have investigated its potential efficacy against the H1N1 influenza virus to mitigate severe viral symptoms. By utilizing its bioactive compounds, it may help manage the complications associated with influenza A. This traditional remedy is often applied to ease the progression of the influenza virus in patients. Such applications aim to reduce the overall severity of the influenza infection.

    As discussed in a study, the leaves of Canarium album (Lour.) DC. exerted great anti-influenza activities in the MDCK cell model with an IC50 of 31.11-36.72 μg/mL. In IAV infected A549 cell and mouse models, the GLY aqueous extract reduced viral proteins, cytokines, and chemoalkine expressions. When administered at 2 g/kg/d, GLY could improve the mouse survival rate and elongate the average survival days. The study identified 20 active compounds, including xanthotoxol, esculetin, isorhoifolin, and nobiletin, as key contributors to the activity of GLY. These components work by inhibiting the phosphorylation of proteins in the PI3K-AKT signal pathway to protect infected cells and mice.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness if its parts are consumed. These gastrointestinal symptoms are common reactions following the accidental ingestion of the species. While small amounts may only cause discomfort, the plant exhibits toxicity if ingested in large amounts. Such severe reactions can lead to significant physical distress and systemic issues. Therefore, careful avoidance of all parts of the plant is necessary to prevent these adverse effects.
