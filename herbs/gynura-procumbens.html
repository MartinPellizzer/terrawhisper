Gynura procumbens

What's the taxonomical classification of Gynura procumbens?

Gyunura procumbens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae, which places it within the genus Gynura, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species procumbens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gynura procumbens has a prostrate growth habit characterized by creeping stems that root at the nodes and leaves that are fleshy, ovate, and covered in fine, soft hairs.

The leaves are typically bright green with slightly serrated margins and a succulent texture. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters. The plant's stems can grow quite long as they spread across the soil surface.

This creeping morphology allows the species to colonize ground space effectively.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Gynura procumbens targets various inflammatory mediators, oxidative stress enzymes, and glucose-regulating proteins to exert its therapeutic effects. Its bioactive compounds, such as flavonoids and polyphenols, inhibit the activity of cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX) enzymes to reduce systemic inflammation. The plant also influences the insulin signaling pathway by modulating the expression of glucose transporter 4 (GLUT4) and glucose-6-phosphatase. Additionally, its phytochemicals act on antioxidant enzymes like superoxide dismutase (SOD) and catalase to neutralize reactive oxygen species. Through these specific protein interactions, the plant provides significant anti-inflammatory, antidiabetic, and antioxidant benefits.

Gynura procumbens is prepared primarily as a liquid decoction, an infusion, or a concentrated tincture to extract its bioactive compounds. What side-effects this plant can have?

Gynura procumbens can cause gastrointestinal distress, allergic skin reactions, and potential interactions with blood-thinning medications. Some individuals may experience nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping after consuming the plant. Contact with the leaves might trigger dermatitis or redness in people with sensitive skin. There is also limited data regarding how the plant affects hormonal balance or long-term organ function. Consult a healthcare professional before use to avoid these adverse physiological responses.
