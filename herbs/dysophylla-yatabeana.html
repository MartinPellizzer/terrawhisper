Dysophylla yatabeana

What's the taxonomical classification of Dysophylla yatabeana?

Dysphyllia yatabeana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Dysophylla and the species yatabeana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dysophylla yatabeana has opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves that measure approximately 3 to 10 centimeters in length and possess entire margins with distinctively acuminate tips.

The foliage is characterized by a bright green color and a smooth texture, often appearing somewhat fleshy. Its inflorescence consists of small, inconspicuous white flowers that emerge from the leaf axils in a somewhat indeterminate fashion. The plant typically features a woody, herbaceous stem that can grow to a moderate height in its native forest habitats.

These morphological traits distinguish it from other members of the Melastomataceae family.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Dysophylla yatabeana targets the enzyme topoisomerase I to inhibit DNA replication in rapidly dividing cancer cells. This interaction is primarily driven by the plant's bioactive alkaloids, such as dysphylline, which interfere with the stabilization of DNA-protein complexes. By preventing the re-ligation of single-strand breaks, these compounds trigger apoptosis in various tumor cell lines. Research also suggests potential interactions with other cellular signaling pathways that regulate cell cycle progression and growth. These specific protein interactions provide the biochemical basis for the plant's documented cytotoxic and antitumor activities.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Dysphyllia yatabeana can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential respiratory issues due to its high concentration of toxic alkaloids. Ingesting even small amounts of the plant may lead to acute nausea, vomiting, and intense abdominal cramping. Beyond digestive upset, the toxins can impact the central nervous system, potentially resulting in dizziness or tremors. Prolonged exposure or higher doses might further trigger difficulty breathing or more systemic physiological complications. Because the specific toxicity levels vary, any accidental consumption requires immediate medical evaluation to prevent permanent harm.
