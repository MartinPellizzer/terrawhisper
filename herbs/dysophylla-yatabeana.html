Dysophylla yatabeana

What's the taxonomical classification of Dysophylla yatabeana?

Dysphyllia yatabeana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Dysophylla and the species yatabeana.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Dysophylla yatabeana contains several bioactive alkaloids, most notably dysphylline and yatabine, which exhibit significant biological activities. These specific compounds are primarily responsible for the plant's demonstrated anti-tumor and anti-leukemic properties in laboratory studies. Researchers have also identified various diterpenoids within the plant that may contribute to its complex chemical profile. The structural uniqueness of these molecules makes the species a subject of intense pharmacological interest for drug development. Ongoing investigations focus on isolating these metabolites to better understand their potential in treating various forms of cancer.

What are the therapeutic actions of Dysophylla yatabeana?

Dysophylla yatabeana has potent anti-tumor properties due to the presence of bioactive diterpenes that induce apoptosis in various cancer cell lines. These chemical compounds work by triggering programmed cell death and inhibiting the proliferation of malignant cells. Research indicates that the plant extracts can specifically target certain types of leukemia and solid tumors in laboratory models. Furthermore, the phytochemical constituents demonstrate significant cytotoxic activities that suggest potential for future oncology treatments. This biological activity makes the species a subject of interest for pharmacological development in cancer therapy.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various types of cancer, including hepatocellular carcinoma, gastric cancer, and colon cancer. It contains specific bioactive compounds that induce apoptosis in malignant cells. Researchers have studied its ability to inhibit the growth of tumor cells in laboratory settings. These chemical constituents are thought to trigger programmed cell death in various cancer cell lines. Current scientific investigation focuses on how these components can be isolated for therapeutic development.
