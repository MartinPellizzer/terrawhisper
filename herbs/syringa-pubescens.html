Syringa pubescens

What's the taxonomical classification of Syringa pubescens?

Syringa pubevescens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Lamiales and the family Oleaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Syringa, and its unique species designation, pubevescens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Syringa pubescens has opposite, ovate to elliptical leaves with pubescent undersides and terminal panicles of fragrant, tubular, pale lilac to white flowers. The foliage is typically soft to the touch due to a fine layer of hairs covering the leaf surfaces. Its stems transition from woody bases to flexible, green young shoots that can exhibit varying degrees of pubescence. The plant often develops a spreading or shrubby habit with multiple stems emerging from the ground. Small, woody capsules containing winged seeds are produced following a successful flowering period.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, oxidative stress, pain, tonsillitis, and hepatitis. Its bioactive compounds help reduce bodily swelling and neutralize harmful free radicals. The plant also serves as a natural remedy to alleviate physical discomfort and aches. Furthermore, it is frequently applied to soothe infections in the throat like tonsillitis. It may also be utilized in traditional practices to support liver health during hepatitis.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", the flowers of Syringa pubescens Turcz. are commonly used in the treatment of hepatitis and tonsillitis. The ethyl acetate fraction of these flowers, known as SPFEA, demonstrates significant antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects. In tests involving a 3000 mg/kg SPFEA group in mice, no obvious side effects were observed. The SPFEA group significantly reduced inflammation factors in foot tissue, including NO, TNF-α, IL-6, COX-2, and IL-1β. Furthermore, the SPFEA effectively decreased oxidative stress levels in liver tissue involving MDA, SOD, GSH-PX, and CAT.
