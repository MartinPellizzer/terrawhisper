Rosa bracteata

What's the taxonomical classification of Rosa bracteata?

Rosa bracteata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical progression continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Rosales. As a member of the family Rosaceae, it is further categorized into the genus Rosa, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species bracteata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rosa bracteata has a deciduous, shrubby growth habit characterized by arching stems that are armed with small, sharp prickles. The leaves are pinnately compound, typically featuring five to seven leaflets with serrated margins and an elliptic to ovate shape. A defining morphological feature is the presence of enlarged, leaf-like bracts that subtend the flowers, often giving the inflorescence a lush appearance. The flowers are usually solitary or in small clusters, displaying pale pink to white petals with numerous stamens. This species often grows in dense thickets, utilizing its woody structure to compete for light in its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

Within these mountainous zones, the species favors moist, well-drained soils often found on steep slopes or near mountain streams. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 3-hydroxy-3-methoxycarbonyl-pentanedioic acid, calcium citrate, dimethyl 3-carboxyl-3-hydroxypentanedioate, and trimethyl citrate. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the unique pharmacological profile of Rosa bracteata. Researchers study these substances to understand how they interact with biological systems. The presence of such organic acids and citrate derivatives suggests potential therapeutic applications. Analyzing these compounds helps scientists determine the plant's overall medicinal value.

According to a study published by "Zhong yao cai = Zhongyaocai = Journal of Chinese medicinal materials", five compounds were isolated from the fruits of Rosa bracteata Wendl. through repeated silica gel chromatography. Four of these specific constituents were identified as trimethyl citrate, dimethyl 3-carboxyl-3-hydroxypentanedioate, 3-hydroxy-3-methoxycarbonyl-pentanedioic acid, and calcium citrate. These compounds were determined using chemical reactions and spectroscopic evidence. These four substances were found from the Rose genus plant for the first time. The study aimed to find these active constituents to antagonize withdrawal syndrome in morphine-dependent mice.
