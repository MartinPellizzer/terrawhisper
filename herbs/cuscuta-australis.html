Cuscuta australis

What's the taxonomical classification of Cuscuta australis?

Cuscuta australis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized as Equisetopsida, which further descends into the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the order Solanales and the family Convolvulaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Cuscuta, and its specific species name, australis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cuscuta australis has thin, leafless, yellowish-to-orange wiry stems that lack chlorophyll and function as parasitic vines. These stems consist of tightly coiled, thread-like filaments that wrap around host plants to extract nutrients through specialized structures called haustoria. Instead of true leaves, the plant features minute, scale-like structures that perform little to no photosynthetic activity. The flowers are small and tubular, typically appearing in clusters at the ends of the vine segments. This specialized morphology allows the plant to focus its energy entirely on parasitic attachment and rapid spreading across its host.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cuscuta australis?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antihyperlipidemic, antioxidant, hepatoprotective, and infertility management pharmacological activities. The antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, which in turn supports its ability to mitigate oxidative stress within the body. By reducing inflammation and regulating lipid levels, the extracts can assist in managing metabolic health and cardiovascular risks. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates protective effects on liver cells to maintain organ function against toxins. Research also indicates that its bioactive compounds play a significant role in infertility management by supporting reproductive health.

As discussed in a study, Cuscuta australis exhibits hepatoprotective, antioxidant, antihyperlipidemic, and anti-inflammatory effects. When administered at 60 mg/kg, the plant reduced ALT, AST, ALP, and bilirubin by 32%, 33%, 63%, and 51% respectively. It improves lipid metabolism by decreasing TC, TG, and LDL-C while increasing HDL-C. The plant enhances antioxidant defense by elevating CAT, SOD, and GPx activities and reducing MDA levels. Additionally, it decreases TNF-α and IL-6 by 48% and 53% respectively. Molecular docking of its metabolites showed strong binding affinities ranging from -7.07 to -19.20 kcal/mol against targets like CYP450, IL-2, TNF-α, and IL-6.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds kaempferol, quercetin, caffeic acid, p-coumaric acid, and hyperoside. These specific polyphenols and flavonoids contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Kaempferol and quercetin are well-known for their ability to combat oxidative stress within biological systems. The presence of caffeic acid and p-coumaric acid further enhances the chemical complexity and therapeutic potential of the plant's profile. Additionally, the flavonoid hyperoside plays a specialized role in the overall medicinal efficacy of the species.

As discussed in a study, the flavonoids contained in 40 samples of Semen Cuscutae were investigated using a reversed phase liquid chromatograph system. Five principal flavonoids were analyzed, including quercetin 3-O-beta-D-galactoside-7-O-beta-D-glucoside, quercetin 3-O-beta-D-apiofuranosyl-(1-->2)-beta-D-galactoside, hyperoside, quercetin, and kaempferol. The method used 0.025 M phosphoric acid-methanol as a mobile phase and achieved a recovery of 97.0-102.9%. All flavonoids displayed good linearity with r >= 0.9990 across a wide concentration range. Specifically, Cuscuta australis was found to contain a much higher content of kaempferol compared to other species.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating infertility, skin diseases, and respiratory ailments. Traditional practitioners utilize its properties to assist with reproductive health and hormonal imbalances. It is also applied topically to soothe various skin irritations and infections. Additionally, certain preparations are used to alleviate symptoms associated with coughs and asthma. These diverse medicinal applications make it a significant component in various folk medicine practices.

As discussed in a study, Cuscuta australis seed and stem are historically used as a dietary supplement for the management of infertility. The study evaluated orally administered aqueous extracts of Cuscuta australis seed and stem at 300 mg/kg body weight/day for seven days. Relative weight of the testis was non-significantly increased (p>0.05) in the Low Protein-energy diet-Seed-treated (LPSE) and Normal Protein-energy diet-Seed-treated (NPSE) groups. The NPSE, LPSE, LPST, LPST, and LPWA showed significantly decreased (p<0.001) plasma levels of luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle stimulating hormone (FSH) relative to NPWA. Seed-diet interactions significantly affected the levels of FSH (p<0.001) and LH (p<0.05). Data suggest the aqueous extract of Cuscuta australis stem is more potent than the seed extract.
