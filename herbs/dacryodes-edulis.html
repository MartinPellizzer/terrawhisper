Dacryodes edulis

What's the taxonomical classification of Dacryodes edulis?

Dacryodes eduilis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Sapindales and is a member of the family Burseraceae. Specifically, it is identified by the genus Dacryodes and the species name edulis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dacryodes edulis has an evergreen tree structure characterized by an erect trunk that typically reaches heights of 15 to 25 meters and possesses a crown composed of dense, spreading branches. The leaves are simple, alternate, and coriaceous, often exhibiting a glossy texture with an elliptical to oblong shape. Its reproductive morphology includes small, inconspicuous flowers that give rise to fleshy, drupaceous fruits. These fruits are typically oval or ellipsoidal in shape and turn a dark purple or black color when fully mature. The bark is generally smooth or slightly fissured, providing a protective layer for the tree's internal vascular system.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of Central and West Africa, spanning from Guinea and Sierra Leone eastward to the Democratic Republic of the Congo and south toward Angola. It thrives primarily within the humid, lowland evergreen forests that characterize these equatorial zones. Its range is strictly limited to these high-moisture environments where dense canopy cover provides necessary shade. The species is most frequently documented in the Guineo-Congolian regional center of endemism. Localized populations can be found throughout various river basins across the African continent.

As discussed in a study, Dacryodes eduilis is an evergreen tree indigenous to western and eastern Africa used for nutritional and medicinal purposes. The butanol fraction of D. eduilis ethanol extract (BFDE) was studied in fructose-streptozotocin induced type 2 diabetic rats grouped into 4 categories. In the study, rats were administered BFDE at doses of 150 and 300 mg/kg bodyweight, while a metformin group received 200 mg/kg bodyweight. After 6 weeks of intervention, BFDE treatment significantly (p < 0.05) reduced blood glucose levels and increased serum insulin secretion. This treatment also caused significant (p < 0.05) elevation of reduced glutathione, superoxide dismutase, catalase, α-amylase, and ATPase activities. Additionally, BFDE significantly (p < 0.05) inhibited the activities of glycogen phosphorylase, fructose 1,6 biphosphaatas, glucose 6 phosphatase, and acetylcholinesterase.
