Valeriana tangutica

What's the taxonomical classification of Valeriana tangutica?

Valeriana tangutica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Dipsacales and belongs to the family Caprifoliaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Valeriana, and its unique species designation, tangutica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Valeriana tangutica has erect, perennial stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 80 centimeters and feature opposite, lanceolate to ovate leaves with serrated margins. The foliage is arranged in opposite pairs along the stem, often becoming smaller and more widely spaced toward the apex. During the flowering season, the plant produces dense, terminal cymes composed of small, five-petaled flowers that range in color from white to pale pink. These inflorescences are supported by branched peduncles that emerge from the upper leaf axils. The root system consists of a stout, woody rhizome which allows the plant to persist through various seasonal changes.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to high-altitude regions of central and western China, primarily occurring in provinces such as Qinghai, Gansu, Sichuan, and Tibet. It typically flourishes in montane environments, often found on rocky slopes or within alpine meadows. The distribution is closely tied to specific mountain ranges where the climate remains cool and moist. Because it prefers these specialized habitats, the species is not commonly found in lowland or tropical areas. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (+)-maaliol, ethoxyvalerianol, selinene, and valeranone. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of Valeriana tangutica. Research suggests that these substances may interact with the central nervous system to produce calming effects. Scientists study these molecules to understand how they function within the plant's complex biochemical structure. The presence of such specialized compounds makes the species a subject of interest for medicinal chemistry.

According to a study published by "Journal of Asian natural products research", the plant Valeriana tangutica contains the new guaiane-type sesquiterpenoid ethoxyvalerianol (1). The study also isolated the known sesquiterpenoid valeranone (2) from the plant. Additionally, selina-4,7(11)-diene (3) and selinene (4) were obtained from (+)-maaliol (5) through decyclization and dehydration. These structures were elucidated using 1D- and 2D-NMR spectroscopic data and HR-ESI-MS analysis.
