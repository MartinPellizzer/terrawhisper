What's the taxonomical classification of Anemone delavayi?

Anemone delavayi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Ranunculales. This plant is a distinguished representative of the family Ranunculaceae, falling under the genus Anemone, and is specifically identified by its species name, delavayi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anemone delavayi has perennial herbaceous stems that reach heights of approximately 10 to 30 centimeters, supporting compound leaves with deeply lobed or divided leaflets and solitary terminal flowers.

The foliage is characterized by a basal rosette of leaves that are typically dark green and covered in fine hairs. Each flower features several spreading petals that are usually white or pale pink in color, often accompanied by numerous central stamens. The plant produces small, aggregate achenes that form from the central carpels after successful pollination.

This species is structurally adapted to thrive in rocky or mountainous terrains where its low-growing habit provides stability.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Anemone delavayi can cause skin irritation, redness, itching, and blistering upon direct contact with its tissues. These dermatological reactions occur because the plant contains protoanemonin, a toxic substance released when the plant is crushed or damaged. If ingested, the plant may lead to severe gastrointestinal distress, including nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. In some cases, exposure to its sap can result in temporary swelling or more intense allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. It is essential to handle this species with caution to avoid these unpleasant physical symptoms.
