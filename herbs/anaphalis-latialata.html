Anaphalis latialata

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anaphalis latialatw has lanceolate to linear leaves that are typically hairy and a terminal flower head consisting of numerous small white disc florets surrounded by white involucral bracts. The foliage is often covered in fine, soft pubescence which gives the plant a silvery or grayish appearance. Its stems are sturdy and upright, supporting the compact, daisy-like inflorescences. The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and exhibit a distinct texture due to their trichomes. This morphological structure allows the plant to thrive in various African montane habitats.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude montane regions of East Africa, specifically within the mountain ranges of Kenya, Uganda, Ethiopia, and Tanzania. It thrives primarily in afro-alpine ecosystems and sub-alpine moorlands characterized by cool temperatures. Its distribution is often restricted to rocky outcrops and high-elevation grasslands where it can withstand intense solar radiation. These specific habitats are typically found at elevations ranging from 2,500 to over 4,000 meters above sea level. Consequently, the species is geographically fragmented due to its reliance on isolated mountain peaks.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Anaphalis latialata can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if handled improperly or ingested in large quantities. While often used in traditional medicine, direct contact with the plant's sap may lead to localized redness or itching in sensitive individuals. There is limited clinical data regarding the systemic toxicity of its various chemical compounds in humans. Therefore, individuals should exercise caution and avoid direct skin contact or consumption without professional guidance. Careful observation is necessary to identify any adverse reactions during its use.
