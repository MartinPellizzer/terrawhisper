Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium

What's the taxonomical classification of Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium?

Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a representative of the family Asteraceae, a diverse group of flowering plants, and is specifically categorized under the genus Chrysanthemum. Its unique scientific identity is finalized by its species designation, lavandulifolium.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium contains a complex profile of bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, terpenoids, phenolic acids, and essential oils. The presence of specific flavone glycosides contributes significantly to its potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Terpenoid compounds within the plant structure are often associated with antimicrobial and sedative effects. These phytochemicals work synergistically to provide therapeutic potential in traditional medicinal applications. Researchers study these specific chemical constituents to understand their role in cellular protection and metabolic regulation.

What are the therapeutic actions of Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat fever, headache, dizziness, and various inflammatory conditions. Its medicinal properties are often utilized to alleviate symptoms of the common cold and flu. Practitioners frequently apply its extracts to reduce swelling and soothe skin irritations. The botanical components are also believed to assist in regulating blood pressure and supporting cardiovascular health. These diverse therapeutic applications make it a significant element in traditional herbal medicine.

What are the herbal preparations of Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium?

Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium is prepared as a dried herbal infusion, a concentrated tincture, or a topical oil for medicinal use. The most common method involves steeping the dried flowers in hot water to create a fragrant tea. For a more potent dose, the plant material is macerated in high-proof alcohol to extract its active constituents over several weeks. These liquid extracts can then be diluted in water or applied directly to the skin. Some practitioners also incorporate the dried petals into salves to leverage their soothing properties.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Chrysanthemum lavandulifolium can cause allergic skin reactions, gastrointestinal distress, and respiratory irritation in sensitive individuals. Some users may experience contact dermatitis, which presents as redness, itching, or swelling upon touching the plant. If ingested, the botanical components might lead to nausea, abdominal cramping, or stomach upset. Rare cases of hypersensitivity could also trigger sneezing or mild asthma-like symptoms through inhalation of pollen or dust. Monitoring for these adverse reactions is essential for anyone handling or consuming the plant.
