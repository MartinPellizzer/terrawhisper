Cleavers

Galium aparine

Cleavers (Galium aparine) is a low-growing herb with sticky hairs that is commonly found in moist environments and has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to support the health of the urinary and lymphatic systems.

Botanical Identification

Galium aparine, commonly known as cleavers, is a member of the Rubiaceae family. It is a low-growing, annual herb native to Europe, Asia, and North America. The plant has tiny, hooked hairs on its stems and leaves, and small white flowers. It is also known by names such as bedstraw and sheep's bit.

Active Compounds

Cleavers contains key active compounds such as tannins, flavonoids, and coumarins, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds work together to support detoxification and have anti-inflammatory effects. They may also interact synergistically to enhance the herb's therapeutic potential in herbal medicine.

Preparation Methods

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and skin irritations.

Poultice: Applied for wounds and inflammatory skin conditions.

Infusion: Used for respiratory issues and as a general tonic.

Safety Profile

Cleavers is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses. However, it should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential hormonal effects. Individuals with kidney issues should also use it with caution.
