Dioscorea alata

What's the taxonomical classification of Dioscorea alata?

Dioscorea alata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant is a member of the order Dioscoreales and the family Dioscoreaceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Dioscorea, specifically identified as the species alata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dioscorea alata has a perennial climbing habit characterized by twining stems that emerge from thick, fleshy underground tubers. The leaves are typically cordate or sagittate in shape with prominent venation and smooth margins. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in clusters, often appearing in a greenish or yellowish hue. The plant develops elongated, cylindrical tubers that serve as its primary nutrient storage organs. These tubers can grow to significant sizes and possess a starchy interior suitable for consumption.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, spanning across countries such as Thailand, Vietnam, Indonesia, and parts of the Philippines. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland environments and forest edges where humidity remains high. Its distribution is heavily influenced by the availability of seasonal rainfall and warm temperatures characteristic of these latitudes. Cultivation has also extended to other tropical zones globally due to its importance as a food source. Consequently, it is found in both wild habitats and managed agricultural landscapes throughout much of the equatorial belt.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Dioscorea alatata contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its underground tuberous rhizomes. These starchy tubers are harvested and processed to extract diosgenin, a steroid sapogenin used in pharmaceutical manufacturing. This extracted component serves as a crucial precursor for synthesizing various hormonal medications, including corticosteroids and oral contraceptives. Beyond industrial applications, traditional practitioners often utilize the processed tuber for its potential anti-inflammatory and metabolic properties. The concentration of these active phytochemicals makes the root the most significant part of the plant for therapeutic use.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dioscorea alata?

This plant has antihypercholesterolemic, antihypertensive, antioxidant, estrogen-stimulating, and immunomodulatory pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce elevated cholesterol levels and help manage high blood pressure in the body. The presence of potent antioxidants protects cells from oxidative stress and prevents damage caused by free radicals. Additionally, the plant exhibits estrogen-stimulating properties that can support hormonal balance. Finally, its immunomodulatory effects assist in regulating and enhancing the overall functions of the immune system.

As discussed in a study, Dioscorea alata possesses antihypercholesterolemic, antiatherogenic, and antihypertensive bioactivity. The diethylyl ether extract DR2 of Dioscorea alata revealed potent antihypertensive activity with an ACE IC50 of 41.99 μg/mL. GC-MS analysis of DR2 indicated the presence of small organic compounds ranging from 95.1 g/mol to 200 g/mol, with 2-Phenyl-1,3-oxazol-2-ine being the most predominant. Molecular docking showed that 2PO forms two (2) hydrogen bonds with Tyr135 and Trp220 in the active site of the ACE enzyme. Using human umbilical vein endothelial cell lines, the DR2 extract showed significant dose-dependent ACE-Inhibitory activity and stimulated nitric oxide release with no toxicity towards the cells.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains gallic acid (GA), anthocyanin, flavonoids, myricetin (MY), and p-coumaric acid (PCA) which provide significant therapeutic benefits. These bioactive molecules work together to offer potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the human body. The presence of anthocyanins and flavonoids contributes to cellular protection and improved vascular health. Additionally, the specific inclusion of myricetin and gallic acid supports metabolic regulation and immune function. Finally, p-coumaric acid assists in reducing oxidative stress within various biological systems.

As discussed in a study, the hydro-methanol extract (70%) of the Dioscorea alata tuber contains various identified medicinal compounds. HPLC identified and quantified gallic acid (GA), 4-hydroxy benzoic acid (4HBA), syringic acid (SYA), p-coumaric acid (PCA), and myriceetin (MY). Furthermore, GC-MS analysis revealed the presence of 13 different phytocompounds, including hexadecanoic acid, methyl stearate, cinnamyl cinnamate, and squalenene. These compounds contribute to the inhibition of NO and TNF-α, which had IC50 values of 134.51 ± 6.75 and 113.30 ± 7.44 μg/mL, respectively. The extract also inhibits total COX, COX-1, COX-2, and PGE2 with IC50 values of 41.96 ± 3.07, 141.41 ± 8.99, 32.50 ± 1.69, and 186.34 ± 15.36 μg/mL.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating diabetes, menopause, inflammation, inflammatory diseases, and aging disorders. Its bioactive compounds help regulate blood sugar levels which provides significant relief for individuals managing diabetes. The plant contains steroidal saponins that can alleviate the physical and hormonal symptoms associated with menopause. Additionally, its natural properties work to reduce systemic inflammation and combat various inflammatory diseases. Finally, the antioxidant effects of Dioscorea alata assist in mitigating the physiological impact of aging disorders.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause no documented side effects in humans based on current scientific literature. Because Dioscorea alata is primarily consumed as a food source, research has focused more on its nutritional value than on potential toxicity. Some researchers suggest that individual sensitivities to specific starches might occur, though these instances are not widely reported. There is currently a lack of extensive clinical trials to confirm any long-term health risks associated with its consumption. Consequently, it is generally considered safe for dietary use when prepared correctly.
