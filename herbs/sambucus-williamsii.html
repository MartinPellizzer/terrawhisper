Sambucus williamsii

What's the taxonomical classification of Sambucus williamsii?

Sambucus williamsii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Dipsacales, it is placed in the family Viburnaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its membership in the genus Sambucus, with its unique designation being the species williamsii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sambucus williamsii has opposite, pinnately compound leaves consisting of five to seven elliptical leaflets with serrated margins and a blunt tip. The plant typically grows as a deciduous shrub characterized by slender, woody stems that can reach several feet in height. During its flowering period, it produces small, creamy white blossoms arranged in dense, flat-topped cymes. These flowers eventually give way to small, dark purple or black drupes that are clustered along the branches. The overall structure is compact, making it a common choice for ornamental hedging and garden borders.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and diarrhea if the raw berries, leaves, or stems are consumed due to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides. These toxic compounds release cyanide into the body when ingested, which may lead to more serious systemic poisoning. While cooked elderberries are generally considered safer, consuming underripe fruit or improperly prepared parts increases the risk of toxic reactions. Most adverse effects are gastrointestinal in nature but can escalate if large quantities are ingested. It is essential to avoid all raw parts of the plant to prevent these potentially dangerous health complications.
