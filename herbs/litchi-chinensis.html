Litchi chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Litchi chinensis?

Litchi chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Sapindales and the family Sapindaceae, eventually reaching its specific classification within the genus Litchi and the species chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Litchi chinensis has evergreen, lanceolate leaves that are dark green, leathery, and arranged in a spiral pattern on woody branches. These leaves typically measure between 5 and 15 centimeters in length and possess a glossy surface with slightly serrated margins. The plant develops dense, spreading canopies supported by sturdy, grayish-brown trunks. During the flowering stage, small, fragrant white flowers emerge in large terminal panicles. The resulting fruit is a globose drupe characterized by a thin, reddish, protuberant skin covering a translucent, succulent pulp.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical regions of southeastern China and parts of Southeast Asia, specifically spreading through areas like Vietnam and Thailand. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates characterized by warm temperatures and distinct seasonal variations. Cultivation has expanded globally to other warm regions, including India, South Africa, and parts of Australia. The species requires specific humidity levels and frost-free environments to produce high-quality fruit. Because of its niche environmental needs, its commercial distribution is largely confined to specific latitudinal belts around the world.

What are the pharmacological activities of Litchi chinensis?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, anti-oxidant, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its high concentration of polyphenols and flavonoids allows it to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress throughout the body. These bioactive compounds also help inhibit the growth of harmful bacteria and fungi while modulating immune responses to suppress inflammation. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant potential in protecting liver cells from chemical toxins and preventing cellular damage. Research suggests these combined properties make it a valuable subject for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, Litchi chinensis is used for the treatment of cough, flatulence, stomach ulcers, diabetes, obesity, testicular swelling, hernia-like conditions, and epigastric and neuralgic pains. The ethnopharmacological history indicates it possesses hypoglycemic, anticancer, antibacterial, anti-hyperlipidemic, anti-platelet, anti-tussive, analgesic, antipyretic, hemostatic, diuretic, and antiviral activities. Crude extracts and pure compounds from the plant exhibit significant antioxidant, anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, anti-viral, anti-diabetic, anti-obesity, hepatoprotective, and immunomodulatory activities. Phenolics serve as the major bioactive components of Litchi chinensis with potential pharmacological activities. From a toxicological perspective, the fruit juice and extracts have been proven to be safe at a dose of 1 g/kg.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (+)-catechin (CT), (-)-epicatechin (EC), kaempferol, rutin, and quercetin. These specific polyphenols function as potent antioxidants that help mitigate oxidative stress within human cells. The presence of flavonoids like kaempferol and quercetin provides significant anti-inflammatory and cardioprotective benefits. Additionally, the flavan-3-ols CT and EC contribute to improved vascular health and metabolic regulation. Together, these bioactive constituents work synergistically to support overall physiological wellness.

As discussed in a study, the Litchi chinensis genome contains five anthocyanidin reductase (LcANR) and two leucoanthocyanidin reductase (LcLAR) members. High expression of LcANR1a/2a and LcLAR1/2 is significantly positively correlated with the abundant proanthocyanidins and (-)-epicatechin (EC) found in the pericarp, leaf, and root. The recombinant LcANR1a/2a converts cyanidin to both EC and (+)-catechin (CT) at an EC:CT ratio of approximately 1:1. Furthermore, the recombinant LcLAR1/2 converts leucocyanidin to CT with Km values of 19 and 34 μM and Vmax values of 7 and 5 nmol min-1 mg-1. These enzymatic activities are linked to the distinct proanthocyanidin profiles characteristic of Litchi chinensis.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, allergic reactions, nausea, and diarrhea in some individuals. Consuming unripe fruit or eating it on an empty stomach may lead to a dangerous drop in blood sugar levels. Some people may also experience skin rashes or respiratory issues due to an allergic reaction. Digestive upset, specifically nausea and diarrhea, can occur after ingestion. These side effects are more common when the fruit is consumed in excessive amounts.
