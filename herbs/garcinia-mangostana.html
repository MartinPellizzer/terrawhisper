Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Mangosteen, scientifically known as Garcinia mangostana, is a tropical evergreen tree native to Southeast Asia, renowned for its dark purple rind and its traditional use in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits.

What is Mangosteen (Garcinia Mangostana)?

Mangosteen, or Garcinia mangostana, is a tropical evergreen tree native to Southeast Asia, known for its deep purple fruit and traditional use in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Mangosteen is Garcinia mangostana. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division unknown, class unknown, order unknown, family Guttiferae, genus Garcinia, and species Garcinia mangostana. Commonly known as mangosteen in Southeast Asia, it is also referred to as garcinia in parts of Africa and the Caribbean. Synonyms for this plant include botanical names such as Garcinia calyptrata and regional names like "nangka" in Indonesia and "mangostan" in India.

Plant Description and Morphology

Mangosteen is a small evergreen tree that typically grows to a height of 10-15 meters with a dense, rounded crown.

Its leaves are elliptical or ovate, measuring 5-15 cm in length, with a smooth, glossy upper surface and a slightly hairy underside. The flowers are small, white, and clustered in panicles, with five petals and numerous stamens, blooming in clusters along the branches. The root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing the plant to anchor itself in tropical soils.

The fruit is a deep purple or red when ripe, has a thin, segmented rind, and a white, segmented flesh with a subtle, tart aroma.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Mangosteen is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, particularly the Malay Archipelago, including countries such as Indonesia, Malaysia, and Thailand.

It thrives in humid, tropical climates with high rainfall and consistent temperatures throughout the year. The plant prefers well-drained, fertile soils and is commonly found in lowland areas near rivers or in secondary forests. Its natural range extends to parts of Africa and the Pacific Islands, where it has been introduced for cultivation.

Mangosteen trees require a warm, sheltered environment with minimal frost to grow and produce fruit effectively.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of mangosteen primarily involves its pericarp, commonly known as the rind or outer skin.

The pericarp is the most valued part due to its high concentration of bioactive compounds such as xanthones. Other parts, including the seeds and leaves, are also used in traditional medicine. The rind is typically dried, ground, or made into extracts for therapeutic purposes.

These parts are utilized in various formulations to address a range of health conditions.

Phytochemical Composition

Mangosteen contains a variety of active compounds, including xanthones, which are known for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Secondary metabolites such as polyphenols and tannins contribute to the plant's medicinal effects by modulating cellular processes and enzyme activities. Chemical markers like alpha-mangostin and gamma-mangostin are key components that have been extensively studied for their therapeutic potential. These compounds are primarily responsible for the pharmacological activities attributed to mangosteen in traditional and modern herbal medicine.

The unique combination of these phytochemicals makes mangosteen a valuable source of natural bioactive substances.

Pharmacological Properties

The pharmacological properties of Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) are well-documented and include a range of therapeutic actions such as anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant effects.

Its biological activity is largely attributed to the presence of xanthones, which are bioactive compounds known for their diverse pharmacological effects. Pharmacodynamically, these compounds interact with various cellular pathways, modulating enzyme activities and gene expressions to exert their therapeutic benefits. At the system level, Mangosteen exhibits effects on the immune system, digestive tract, and cardiovascular system, contributing to overall health maintenance.

These properties make it a valuable component in herbal medicine for treating a variety of ailments.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of mangosteen involve complex interactions at the cellular, biochemical, and systemic levels.

At the cellular level, the polyphenolic compounds in mangosteen, such as xanthones, exhibit antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties by neutralizing free radicals and modulating intracellular signaling pathways. Biochemically, these compounds inhibit enzymes like cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase, which are involved in the production of pro-inflammatory molecules. They also interfere with key metabolic pathways, including those related to glucose metabolism and lipid synthesis, contributing to their hypoglycemic and anti-obesity effects.

Target systems include the digestive, immune, and nervous systems, where mangosteen's bioactive components exert protective and regulatory influences.

Therapeutic Uses

The therapeutic uses of Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) are rooted in its traditional application as a herbal remedy for a variety of health conditions.

It is commonly used to treat inflammation, digestive issues, and skin disorders due to its rich content of xanthones and antioxidants. The plant's extracts are believed to support immune function and may help manage symptoms of oxidative stress-related diseases. Additionally, Mangosteen has been traditionally employed to address urinary tract infections and gastrointestinal discomfort.

Its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties make it a valued component in herbal medicine for promoting overall wellness.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of mangosteen for use in herbal medicine primarily involve its fruit, which is consumed either raw or processed.

The fruit is commonly used in the form of whole fruits, extracts, powders, and capsules. Extracts are made by concentrating the juice or using ethanol to isolate active compounds. Powders are derived from dried and ground rinds or pulp.

Capsules are a popular form for standardized dosing and convenience.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of mangosteen for general use is typically 200 to 400 milligrams of the extract, taken two to three times daily.

Most commercially available products contain a concentration of 20% to 40% active compounds, so it is important to check the label for specific concentrations. It is recommended to take mangosteen with meals to minimize gastrointestinal discomfort. The typical duration of use is 4 to 8 weeks, depending on the intended health goal.

Always consult a healthcare professional before starting any new supplement regimen.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, mangosteen is generally considered safe for most people, but it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, and allergic reactions in some individuals.

Common side effects include upset stomach, diarrhea, and skin rashes, which are usually mild and temporary. People with known allergies to other plants in the Guttiferae family should avoid mangosteen due to the risk of allergic reactions. It is not recommended for use during pregnancy, as there is insufficient evidence to confirm its safety for fetal development.

Additionally, individuals with certain medical conditions or those taking medications should consult a healthcare provider before using mangosteen.

Drug Interactions

Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, antiplatelet drugs, and medications metabolized by the liver, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or altering drug efficacy.

Toxicity and Precautions

While mangosteen is generally considered safe when consumed in typical food amounts, excessive ingestion of its rind or certain preparations may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interactions with medications, so it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use, especially during pregnancy or breastfeeding.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Mangosteen trees are typically cultivated in tropical climates with high humidity and well-drained soil, requiring careful management of temperature, irrigation, and pest control to ensure optimal fruit development and yield.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of mangosteen involve methods such as cold pressing, solvent extraction, and enzymatic hydrolysis to obtain bioactive compounds like xanthones and polyphenols, which are then purified and standardized for use in herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Mangosteen fruit should be stored at room temperature in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, and will typically remain fresh for about 5 to 7 days, though its shelf life can be extended by refrigeration at 4°C (39°F) for up to 2 weeks.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) has explored its potential health benefits, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, with some studies suggesting possible applications in managing conditions such as diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and cancer, though more rigorous clinical trials are needed to confirm these effects.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Mangosteen has been traditionally used in Southeast Asian medicine for centuries to treat digestive issues, inflammation, and skin conditions, with its rind and pulp valued for their medicinal properties.

Regulatory Status

The regulatory status of mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) varies by country, with some regions classifying it as a food supplement while others regulate it as a medicinal product, requiring specific approval for health claims.
