Eucalyptus tereticornis

What's the taxonomical classification of Eucalyptus tereticornis?

Eucalyptus tereticornis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Myrtales and is a member of the family Myrtaceae. Ultimately, it is identified by its genus, Eucalyptus, and its specific species name, tereticornis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eucalyptus tereticornis has narrow, lanceolate leaves that are typically bright green and pendulous, paired with smooth, greyish-brown bark that often peels in long strips. The tree reaches significant heights and features a spreading crown with branches that often droop downwards. Its flowers are characterized by numerous prominent stamens that form a creamy-white to pale yellow cluster. Small, woody capsules known as fruit develop from these flowers and contain numerous tiny seeds. The overall structure is defined by its cylindrical or slightly swollen branchlets and a robust trunk.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the eastern coastal regions of Australia, ranging from the Cape York Peninsula in Queensland down through New South Wales. It primarily thrives in subtropical and temperate climates within well-drained soils found along coastal lowlands and near riverbanks. The species is often distributed in moist environments where it can access consistent water supplies. In many areas, it occupies specific niches in sclerophyll forests and woodland habitats. Its range is closely tied to the humid corridors that follow the eastern seaboard.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Eucalyptus tereticornis contains bioactive medicinal compounds primarily located within its aromatic leaves and essential oils. The leaves are the most frequently harvested part due to their high concentration of eucalyptol, which provides antiseptic and anti-inflammatory properties. When processed, these leaves are often used in steam inhalations to relieve respiratory congestion or brewed into decoctions for topical applications. Some traditional practices also involve using the bark or oil extracts to treat skin infections and minor wounds. This plant serves as a valuable source of natural agents for managing coughs, fevers, and various bacterial ailments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Eucalyptus tereticornis?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and larvicidal pharmacological activities. The presence of bioactive compounds allows its extracts to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, the plant demonstrates significant antioxidant properties that help neutralize harmful free radicals in biological systems. Its anti-inflammatory effects contribute to reducing swelling and pain in inflammatory processes. Furthermore, the species exhibits larvicidal potential, making it a candidate for controlling insect populations.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extracts of Eucalyptus tereticornis leaves were prepared by mixing 20 g of powder with 200ml of ethanol and centrifuging at 4000 rpm for 10 min. The antimicrobial activity of these extracts was effective against test microorganisms, including Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. The diameters of zones of inhibition for the tested microorganisms included S. aureus PCSIR-83 in the range of 0-28mm, E. coli PCSIR-102 at 0-28mm, and B. subtilis PCSIR-05 at 15-26mm. A combination involving Eucalyptus tereticornis at 20mg/μl was found to be most effective at pH 9.0 and a temperature of 35°C.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains ursolic acid, a pentacyclic triterpenoid known for its potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. This specific medicinal compound is believed to help inhibit tumor growth and protect cellular structures from oxidative stress. In addition to ursolic acid, the plant possesses various other phytochemicals that contribute to its therapeutic profile. These substances work synergistically to support immune function and reduce systemic inflammation. Researchers continue to study how these unique chemical constituents can be utilized in modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, leaves of Eucalyptus tereticornis were chemically investigated and resulted in the isolation of ursolic acid (UA) as a major constituent. This anti-filarial agent was found to be lethal to microfilariae with an LC100 of 50 and an IC50 of 8.84 µM. For female adult worms, the UA showed an LC100 of 100 and an IC50 of 35.36 µM, while also exerting 86% inhibition in MTT reduction potential. Molecular docking studies showed a LibDock score of -8.6 for UA, compared to -8.4 for ivermectin and -4.6 for diethylcarbamazine. In vivo models demonstrated that UA provided 54% macrofilaricidal activity and 56% female worm sterility.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, hyperglycemia, and infections caused by Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Escherichia coli. Research indicates that extracts from the plant may also assist in managing elevated blood glucose levels associated with hyperglycemia. What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be processed into an essential oil, an infusion, a decoction, or a tincture to extract its medicinal volatile compounds and active constituents. The essential oil is typically obtained through steam distillation of the leaves and is used primarily for its potent antimicrobial properties. For stronger therapeutic effects, a decoction is made by boiling the tougher plant materials to release more concentrated compounds. Additionally, a tincture can be prepared by soaking the plant parts in alcohol to create a long-lasting and highly potent extract.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe physical reactions including nausea, vomiting, stomach pain, diarrhea, dizziness, confusion, seizures, respiratory irritation, and skin irritation. Ingesting the essential oils or leaves often leads to significant gastrointestinal distress such as nausea and vomiting. Contact with the foliage may trigger localized skin irritation or respiratory irritation if the vapors are inhaled deeply. Neurological symptoms are also a serious risk, potentially manifesting as dizziness, confusion, or even seizures. These varied side effects underscore the toxic potential of Eucalyptus terectornis when handled or consumed improperly.
