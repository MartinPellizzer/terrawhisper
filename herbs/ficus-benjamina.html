Ficus benjamina

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus benjamina?

Ficus benjamina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Moraceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic designation is defined by the genus Ficus and the species benjamina.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus benjamina has an upright growth habit characterized by slender, woody stems and elliptic to ovate leaves that measure between two and six inches in length. The foliage is typically dark green with a glossy texture and prominent pinnate venation on the underside. These leaves are arranged in an alternate pattern along the branches, creating a dense, weeping canopy. The bark is relatively smooth and greyish, becoming more textured as the plant matures into a larger tree. Small, inconspicuous reddish fruits may develop in the leaf axils during certain seasonal cycles.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, spanning from India and Sri Lanka through Malaysia and Indonesia to the Philippines. It thrives in humid environments where consistent warmth is available throughout the year. While its origins are strictly Asian, it has been widely introduced to other tropical climates across Africa and the Americas. In many parts of the world, it is cultivated extensively as an ornamental indoor plant due to its ability to tolerate various indoor conditions. Consequently, its current distribution is both natural and anthropogenic, stretching across most global tropical zones.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ficus benjamina?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anticancer, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities derived from its diverse phytochemical constituents. The presence of flavonoids and tannins allows it to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. Its antimicrobial properties enable it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Additionally, the plant exhibits significant anticancer potential by interfering with the proliferation of malignant cells. Research also indicates that its extracts provide hepatoprotective benefits by safeguarding liver tissues from chemical-induced damage.

As discussed in a study, Ficus benjamina demonstrates various pharmacological activities including antimicrobial, anticancer, pro-wound-healing, and anti-inflammatory properties. The extract and its 80% fraction were very potent (p < 0.001) at inducing the death of MCF7 and U87 cancer cell cultures, performing more effectively than the positive control doxorubicin. Antimicrobial activity was observed as the extract, the 80% fraction, and all identified flavonoids except quercetin 3-O-rutinoside inhibited certain drug-resistant microorganisms. Additionally, the extract and the 80% fraction exhibited pronounced pro-wound-healing properties. Finally, the extracts and the 80% fraction significantly inhibited the secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-6 and IL-8 (p < 0.001).

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains caffeic acid, several types of flavonoids, kaempferol 3-O-robinobioside, kaempferol 3-O-rutinoside, and quercetin 3-O-rutinoside. These diverse medicinal compounds contribute to the plant's complex chemical profile and biological activity. The presence of various flavonoids suggests that the plant may possess significant antioxidant properties. Specifically, the glycoside forms like kaempferol 3-O-rutinoside and quercetin 3-O-rutinoside are important components of its secondary metabolism. Such specialized molecules are often studied for their potential roles in human health and wellness.

As discussed in a study, Ficus benjamina contains various phytochemicals including flavonoids, caffeic acid, kaempferol 3-O-rutinoside, and kaempferol 3-O-robinobioside. The 80% fraction and the plant extract were very potent (p < 0.001) at inducing death of MCF7 and U87 cancer cell cultures. All identified flavonoids except quercetin 3-O-rutinoside inhibited certain drug-resistant microorganisms. Furthermore, the extract, the 80% fraction, caffeic acid, kaempferol 3-O-rutinoside, and kaempferol 3-O-robinobioside significantly inhibited the secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-6 and IL-8 (p < 0.001). These active components also demonstrated pronounced pro-wound-healing properties.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for combating bacterial infections caused by Acinetobacter bauumanii, Escherichia coli, Klebsiella pneumoniae, and Salmonella enteritidis, while also reducing inflammation. Research indicates that extracts from the plant possess significant antimicrobial properties against these specific pathogens. These bioactive compounds work to disrupt the cellular integrity of harmful bacteria. Additionally, the plant contains phytochemicals that help mitigate inflammatory responses within the body. Such therapeutic actions make it a subject of interest for managing both infectious diseases and swelling.

As discussed in a study, Ficus benjamina is a common park tree with medicinal properties. The extract and its 80% fraction were found to inhibit the drug-resistant microorganism Acinetobacter baumannii. All identified flavonoids from the plant inhibited Acinetobacter baumannii except for quercetin 3-O-rutinoside. The extract and this specific compound also inhibited the growth of biofilm-producing bacteria. This research revealed significant therapeutic potential for the Ficus benjamina extract and its active phytochemicals.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be used to create various herbal preparations including topical poultices and decoctions derived from its latex and leaves to treat skin irritations or gastrointestinal issues. In some traditional practices, the milky sap is applied directly to wounds to promote healing or act as a mild caustic agent. Leaf extracts are often boiled into a tea to address internal ailments, though precise dosages are critical due to potential toxicity. Practitioners must exercise caution because the sap contains irritants that can cause dermatological reactions in sensitive individuals.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, respiratory irritation, and dizziness if a person is exposed to its toxic sap or dust. Contact with the milky latex produced by the leaves and stems often leads to redness or itching on the skin. Ingesting any part of the plant can trigger gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Inhaling fine particles or dust from the plant may result in respiratory irritation for sensitive individuals. Additionally, some people might experience dizziness following accidental exposure to its chemical compounds.
