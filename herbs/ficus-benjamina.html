Ficus benjamina

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus benjamina?

Ficus benjamina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Moraceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic designation is defined by the genus Ficus and the species benjamina.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Ficus benjamina contains various bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, tannins, saponins, and triterpenoids that contribute to its pharmacological potential. These specific chemical constituents are believed to exhibit significant antioxidant and antimicrobial properties in laboratory studies. Some research suggests that the extracts may possess anti-inflammatory activities through the modulation of various biological pathways. Additionally, the presence of phenolic compounds provides the plant with the ability to neutralize free radicals. Scientists continue to investigate these isolated compounds to determine their precise therapeutic applications in human medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ficus benjamina?

Ficus benjamina has the ability to improve indoor air quality by removing common airborne toxins such as formaldehyde, benzene, and toluene through a process of phytoremediation. By absorbing these chemical pollutants through its leaves and roots, the plant helps mitigate the effects of sick building syndrome. The plant also contributes to a healthier living environment by increasing ambient humidity levels through transpiration. This natural moisture release can assist in soothing dry respiratory passages and skin. Consequently, keeping this plant in a workspace or bedroom can foster a more revitalizing atmosphere for occupants.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin conditions including eczema, psoriasis, and various types of dermatitis through the application of its latex. The milky sap contains bioactive compounds that possess significant antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Some traditional practices involve using these extracts to manage fungal infections and minor wounds on the skin surface. However, the sap can be highly irritating to the eyes and sensitive areas if not handled with extreme care. Medical professionals caution that direct contact should be managed carefully due to the potential for allergic reactions.

What are the herbal preparations of Ficus benjamina?

Ficus benjamina is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice using the leaves or bark of the plant. These liquid preparations are commonly derived by boiling the plant material in water to extract its bioactive compounds. Infusions are often used for milder extractions where the plant parts are steeped in hot water rather than boiled. Topical applications involve crushing the fresh leaves to create a paste that is applied directly to the skin. Each method is chosen based on whether the desired medicinal effect requires a concentrated extract or a gentle infusion.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Ficus benjamina can cause skin irritation, respiratory distress, and digestive upset if the plant's milky white sap comes into contact with the body. This sap contains proteolytic enzymes that may trigger allergic contact dermatitis or redness upon skin exposure. If the sap is accidentally ingested, it can lead to vomiting, abdominal pain, or irritation of the mouth and throat. Inhaling airborne particles or dust from the plant might also irritate the lungs of sensitive individuals. It is important to handle the plant with gloves and keep it out of reach of children and pets to avoid these reactions.
