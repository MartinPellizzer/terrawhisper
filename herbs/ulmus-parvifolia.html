Ulmus parvifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Ulmus parvifolia?

Ulmus parvifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Rosales. This plant is a member of the family Ulmaceae and falls within the genus Ulmus, eventually reaching its specific designation as the species parvifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ulmus parvifolia has small, elliptical leaves with serrated margins and a textured surface characterized by prominent veins and a scabrous feel.

The foliage typically displays a dark green upper surface and a paler underside, often featuring a distinctively rough texture. Its bark is highly distinctive, peeling away in thin, exfoliating flakes to reveal a mosaic of grey, orange, and brown patches. The tree produces small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge in early spring before the leaves fully expand.

Its overall structure is spreading and irregular, supporting a dense canopy of fine-textured branches.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ulmus parvifolia contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its inner bark, leaves, and roots. The inner bark is frequently harvested to extract mucilaginous substances that serve as soothing agents for gastrointestinal inflammation. Extracts from the leaves are often utilized in traditional practices to address skin irritations and feverish conditions. Additionally, the roots may be decocted to treat various ailments related to respiratory health or digestive upset. These different botanical components work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, phenolic acids, terpenoids, tannins, alkaloids, and sterols.

These secondary metabolites contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of phenolic acids and flavonoids specifically helps in neutralizing free radicals within biological systems. Furthermore, the tannins and alkaloids play crucial roles in the plant's defensive mechanisms and potential therapeutic effects.

Sterols and terpenoids also add to the complex chemical structure that defines its biological activity.

  • Flavonoids
  • Phenolic acids
  • Terpenoids
  • Tannins
  • Alkaloids
  • Sterols

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Ulmus parvifolia targets cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6 to exert its medicinal effects. The plant contains bioactive compounds like flavonoids and phenolic acids that inhibit these specific enzymes to reduce systemic inflammation. These secondary metabolites also interact with nitric oxide synthase to modulate vascular tone and oxidative stress levels within cells. By modulating these protein pathways, the extracts demonstrate potential antioxidant and neuroprotective properties in various biological models. This multifaceted interaction with cellular signaling proteins explains the plant's diverse pharmacological profile.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ulmus parvifolia?

Ulmus parvifolia has therapeutic properties derived primarily from its high mucilage content, which provides demulcent, astringent, anti-inflammatory, and hemostatic actions.

The presence of soothing mucilage allows the plant to act as a demulcent, coating and protecting irritated mucous membranes in the digestive and respiratory tracts. Its astringent qualities help to contract tissues and reduce excessive secretions or swelling in various bodily systems. Additionally, the plant functions as an anti-inflammatory agent by mitigating redness and irritation within inflamed areas.

The hemostatic effects of the species can also assist in slowing minor bleeding through its ability to constrict blood vessels and promote tissue cohesion.

The primary therapeutic actions of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Astringent
  • Hemostatic
  • Anti-inflammatory
  • Demulcent

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat various environmental stressors and biological threats including dutch elm disease, elm leaf beetle infestation, powdery mildew, and verticillium wilt.

While these terms typically describe pathological conditions affecting the tree's health, management strategies are employed to mitigate their impact. For instance, controlling elm leaf beetle infestation helps prevent significant foliage loss. Addressing powdery mildew and verticillium wilt is also essential to maintaining the overall vitality of the specimen.

Protecting the species from dutch elm disease remains a primary concern for arborists.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Dutch elm disease
  • Elm leaf beetle infestation
  • Powdery mildew
  • Verticillium wilt

What are the herbal preparations of Ulmus parvifolia?

Ulmus parvifolia is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or an infusion depending on whether the bark, leaves, or inner root are being utilized. The bark is most commonly boiled in water to create a medicinal tea that addresses inflammation or skin issues. Dried leaves can be steeped into a mild infusion to soothe digestive discomfort. Many practitioners also grind the dried components into a fine powder to be mixed into larger liquid preparations or used in topical pastes. These various forms allow for different concentrations of active compounds to be administered to the patient.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Ulmus parvifolia can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, itching, and redness when individuals come into direct contact with its bark, leaves, or sap. While the tree is generally considered non-toxic to humans if ingested in small amounts, consuming significant portions may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or nausea. Some people may experience respiratory irritation if they inhale high concentrations of pollen during the flowering season. Sensitivity to the fine hairs found on certain parts of the plant can also trigger localized skin irritation or minor rashes. Monitoring for these reactions is advisable for those with known plant allergies.
