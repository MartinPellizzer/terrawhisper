Ulmus parvifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Ulmus parvifolia?

Ulmus parvifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Rosales. This plant is a member of the family Ulmaceae and falls within the genus Ulmus, eventually reaching its specific designation as the species parvifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ulmus parvifolia has small, elliptical leaves with serrated margins and a textured surface characterized by prominent veins and a scabrous feel. The foliage typically displays a dark green upper surface and a paler underside, often featuring a distinctively rough texture. Its bark is highly distinctive, peeling away in thin, exfoliating flakes to reveal a mosaic of grey, orange, and brown patches. The tree produces small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge in early spring before the leaves fully expand. Its overall structure is spreading and irregular, supporting a dense canopy of fine-textured branches.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across China, Korea, and Japan. It thrives in a variety of habitats ranging from temperate forests to urban environments within these regions. While its primary range is concentrated in the Eastern Hemisphere, it has been widely introduced to other parts of the world. Many populations now exist in North America and Europe due to its popularity in landscaping and urban forestry. This adaptability allows it to colonize diverse climates outside its original ancestral territory.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ulmus parvifolia contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its inner bark, leaves, and roots. The inner bark is frequently harvested to extract mucilaginous substances that serve as soothing agents for gastrointestinal inflammation. Extracts from the leaves are often utilized in traditional practices to address skin irritations and feverish conditions. Additionally, the roots may be decocted to treat various ailments related to respiratory health or digestive upset. These different botanical components work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ulmus parvifolia?

Ulmus parvifolia has therapeutic properties derived primarily from its high mucilage content, which provides demulcent, astringent, anti-inflammatory, and hemostatic actions. The presence of soothing mucilage allows the plant to act as a demulcent, coating and protecting irritated mucous membranes in the digestive and respiratory tracts. Its astringent qualities help to contract tissues and reduce excessive secretions or swelling in various bodily systems. Additionally, the plant functions as an anti-inflammatory agent by mitigating redness and irritation within inflamed areas. The hemostatic effects of the species can also assist in slowing minor bleeding through its ability to constrict blood vessels and promote tissue cohesion.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic skin reactions, respiratory irritation, and potential digestive upset if ingested. The fine hairs on the leaves and bark often trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Inhaling pollen or dust from the tree may exacerbate asthma or cause hay fever symptoms. While not considered highly toxic, consuming parts of the plant can lead to nausea or stomach discomfort. Always use caution when handling the foliage to avoid direct skin contact.
