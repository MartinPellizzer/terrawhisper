Scientific and Botanical Profile

Common Clubmoss, with botanical name Scolopendrium vulgare, is a member of the Lycopodiaceae family and is known by numerous common names including Whipsnake Fern, Common Horsetail, Scallop Fern, Scouring Rush, Field Horsetail, Common Hart's Tongue, Whipsnake, Scallop Fern, Common Spore Fern, Adder's Tongue, Common Adder's Tongue, Tamarisk, Crown Fern, Scallop Clubmoss, Salt Cedar, Scallop-Fern, Scolopendrum, Redcedar.

This evergreen plant is characterized by its creeping rhizomes and upright, spore-bearing cones that resemble small clubs, giving it its common name. It features slender, needle-like leaves that are often arranged in whorls, and its sporophylls are typically clustered in a distinctive, brush-like structure. Native to regions such as the United Kingdom, Ireland, France, Spain, Portugal, Italy, Albania, Greece, the Balkan Peninsula, the Mediterranean Region, Central Europe, and Eastern Europe, it thrives in moist, shaded environments.

Its historical use as a natural scouring agent, due to its abrasive leaves, has led to its common name Scouring Rush.

Scolopendrium vulgare can be used as a medicinal plant with potential benefits, but it requires careful consideration due to its possible safety concerns.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a qualified healthcare provider before starting any treatment with this plant and to follow recommended dosages and duration guidelines.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Scolopendrium vulgare grows in moist, well-drained soils in partially shaded to sunny locations, often found in woodland areas or along stream banks.

It thrives in acidic to neutral soil with high organic content and requires consistent moisture but should not be waterlogged. Regular watering during dry periods and mulching to retain soil moisture are essential for optimal growth. Harvesting is best done in late spring to early summer when the plant is most active, using clean, sharp tools to cut the stems without damaging the plant.

After harvesting, the plant material should be dried in a cool, shaded area to preserve its potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to maintain its medicinal properties.
