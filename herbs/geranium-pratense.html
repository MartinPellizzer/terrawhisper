Geranium pratense

What's the taxonomical classification of Geranium pratense?

Geranium pratense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage through the order Geraniales and the family Geraniaceae. Within this taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is placed in the genus Geranium, specifically identified by the species name pratense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Geranium pratense has deeply palmately lobed leaves that are typically 5 to 10 centimeters wide and bright green in color. The stems are slender, hairy, and can grow up to 60 centimeters tall in well-nourished soil. Its flowers feature five distinct, violet-blue petals that surround a central cluster of numerous stamens. These blooms are held atop long pedicels that branch out from the main upright stem. After flowering, the plant produces long, beak-like seed capsules that contribute to its common name, cranesbill.

What are the pharmacological activities of Geranium pratense?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties that contribute to its therapeutic potential. The antioxidant capabilities of Geranium pratense help neutralize free radicals and prevent oxidative stress within cellular structures. Its antimicrobial action allows it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms effectively. Furthermore, the plant exhibits anti-inflammatory effects that can help reduce swelling and pain in biological tissues. These combined chemical profiles make the species a subject of interest for developing natural medicinal treatments.

As discussed in a study, aqueous extracts from Geranium pratense L. herb facilitate the formation of urolithin A, B and C by human gut microbiota. These urolithins serve as bioavailable metabolites that demonstrate anti-inflammatory activity on the THP-1 cell line-derived macrophages model. For the most potent urolithin A, inhibition of TNF-α production was observed at nanomolar concentrations, specifically showing 29.2±6.4% of inhibition at 0.625 μM. Additionally, urolithin C was the only compound identified as inhibiting IL-6 production at 0.625 μM with 13.9±2.2% of inhibition. These findings indicate that the bioactivity of metabolites produced from Geranium pratense must be considered during oral use.
