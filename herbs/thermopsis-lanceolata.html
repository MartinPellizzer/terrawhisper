Thermopsis lanceolata

What's the taxonomical classification of Thermopsis lanceolata?

Thermopsis lanceolata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic placement continues through the order Fabales and into the family Fabaceae, which characterizes it as a member of the legume group. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Thermopsis, and its unique species designation, lanceolata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Thermopsis lanceolata has lanceolate leaves that are pinnately compound with several pairs of narrow, pointed leaflets arranged along a central rachis. The plant typically features a creeping or ascending growth habit with slender, green stems. Its flowers are small, pea-like, and often display colors ranging from yellow to pale purple. The reproductive structures consist of a standard, wings, and a keel characteristic of the Fabaceae family. What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Thermopsis lanceolata contains toxic alkaloids primarily located within its roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are the most concentrated sources of cytisine, a potent nitrogenous compound. Historically, practitioners have utilized these specific parts to prepare decoctions for various therapeutic applications. However, the high toxicity of the plant parts requires extreme caution during any medicinal preparation. Due to the risk of poisoning, the roots are rarely used in modern clinical settings.

What are the pharmacological activities of Thermopsis lanceolata?

This plant has anti-tobacco mosaic virus activity, antitomato spotted wilt virus activity, antiviral properties, and insecticidal activity. These specific pharmacological capabilities allow the species to function as a potent natural defense mechanism against various pathogens. Its antiviral characteristics are particularly effective at suppressing the replication of certain viral agents. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant insecticidal activity that can deter many types of pests. These diverse biological actions make it a subject of interest for both agricultural and medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, the seeds of Thermoopsis lanceolata R. Br. contain two new cytisine-type alkaloids, identified as compounds 1 and 2, which are chemically inseparable isomers present in a 1:1 ratio. Compound 1 displays significant anti-tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) activity. Additionally, both compounds 1 and 2 demonstrate moderate insecticidal activities against Aphis fabae. The LC50 values for these activities were recorded as 43.15 mg/L for compound 1 and 46.47 mg/L for compound 2.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe toxic reactions including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, dizziness, muscle weakness, respiratory depression, convulsions, cardiac arrhythmia, and hypotension. These dangerous symptoms occur because the plant contains potent alkaloids that act on the central nervous and cardiovascular systems. Ingestion may lead to significant gastrointestinal distress alongside neurological instability. More critical complications involve life-threatening changes to heart rhythm and breathing patterns. The presence of these substances makes any consumption of the plant extremely hazardous to human health.
