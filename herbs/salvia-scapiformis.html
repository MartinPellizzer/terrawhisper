Salvia scapiformis

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia scapiformis?

Salvia scapiformis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant falls into the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Lamiaceae. Specifically, it is identified by the genus Salvia and is designated by the species name scapiformis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia scapiformis has opposite, heart-shaped leaves that are characterized by a distinctively broad and somewhat concave blade shape. These leaves feature serrated margins and long petioles that attach them to the square-shaped stems typical of the Lamiaceae family. The plant produces tubular, bilabiate flowers that emerge from verticillate inflorescences. Its woody base transitions into herbaceous upper growth as the plant matures. The overall structure is defined by a bushy habit with branching stems that support the dense foliage.

What are the pharmacological activities of Salvia scapiformis?

This plant has antiangiogenic pharmacological activity. This specific property allows the plant to inhibit the formation of new blood vessels that support tumor growth. By suppressing angiogenesis, the compounds within the plant may help restrict the spread of cancer cells throughout the body. Researchers study these biological mechanisms to understand how the plant's chemical constituents interact with cellular signaling pathways. Such studies are essential for exploring the potential therapeutic applications of the species in oncology.

As discussed in a study, Salvia scapiformis produces glechomanamides A-C (1-3) and two pairs of 7,12-hemiketal sesquiterpenoid epimers (4a/4b, 5a/5b). The compound glechomanamide B (2) exhibits antiangiogenic activity by suppressing vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)-induced tube formation through modulation of VEGFR2-mediated signaling pathways in human umbilical vascular endothelial cells (HUVECs). Furthermore, compound 2 shows significant suppression of mRNA expression associated with glycolysis and angiogenesis biomarkers in HUVECs induced by 30 mM high glucose. These findings suggest that compound 2 might be a potential lead compound candidate for the management of diabetic retinopathy.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical investigation of the whole plants of Salvia scapiformis Hance afforded eight compounds. These include one new lignan, (+)-8α-hydroxypinoresinol-8-O-[6''-O-(4'''-hydroxybenzoyl)]-β-d-glucopyranoside (1). The investigation also identified four known lignans: (+)-8α-hydroxy-pinoresinol-8-O-β-d-glucopyranoside (2), (+)-8α-hydroxypinoresinol (3), (+)-pinoresinol (4), and (+)-medioresinol (5). Additionally, there were three known triterpenoids found, which are ursolic acid (6), 4-epi-niga-ichigoside F1 (7), and niga-ichigoside F1 (8). The absolute configuration of the new compound 1 was determined by chemical transformation and GC analysis.
