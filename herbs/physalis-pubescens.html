Physalis pubescens

What's the taxonomical classification of Physalis pubescens?

Physalis pubescens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Solanales and is a member of the family Solanaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Physalis, and its unique species designation, pubecens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Physalis pubescens has an herbaceous growth habit characterized by erect, branching stems that reach heights of approximately 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are primarily ovate or lanceolate in shape with entire or slightly wavy margins and a pubescent surface texture. Small, bell-shaped flowers emerge from the leaf axils, typically displaying a pale yellow or creamy coloration. A defining morphological feature is the presence of a persistent, inflated calyx that expands to form a papery, bladder-like husk around the developing fruit. These distinctive husks protect the small, fleshy berries that mature within the protective enclosure.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds physalin B, physalin L, physalin O, withanolide glucosides, and withanolides. These specific chemical constituents are known for their diverse biological activities and potential therapeutic applications. The physalins belong to a group of highly oxygenated diterpene glycosides that often exhibit cytotoxic properties. Meanwhile, the withanolides and their corresponding glucosides are steroidal lactones frequently studied for their anti-inflammatory and anticancer effects. Together, these bioactive molecules represent the primary pharmacological interest in the plant's chemical profile.

As discussed in a study, the ethyl acetate extract (PHY-EA) from the leaves of Physalis pube sce n s contains the medicinal compounds physalin B, physalin O, and physalin L. This extract was investigated for its proliferation inhibitory activity on tumor cells including MCF-7, CA-46, Hela, HepG2, and B16. The PHY-EA blocked the cell cycle of G2/M to G0/G1 in lymphoma cells and induced apoptosis. Furthermore, mouse transplantation tumor experiments demonstrated that the extract significantly reduced tumor volume and weight. These findings indicate that Physalis pube sce n s has potential medicinal value for its antiproliferative effect on Burkkit lymphoma.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe physiological distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach cramps, dizziness, confusion, hallucinations, increased heart rate, and dry mouth. These toxic reactions primarily stem from the presence of tropane alkaloids found within the plant's tissues. Consuming even small amounts can lead to significant neurological impairment such as disorientation or vivid visual disturbances. Physical symptoms often include a rapid pulse and an uncomfortable sensation of dryness in the mouth. Such a wide range of symptoms indicates that the plant acts as a potent anticholinergic agent in the human body.
