Physalis pubescens

What's the taxonomical classification of Physalis pubescens?

Physalis pubescens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Solanales and is a member of the family Solanaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Physalis, and its unique species designation, pubecens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Physalis pubescens has an herbaceous growth habit characterized by erect, branching stems that reach heights of approximately 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are primarily ovate or lanceolate in shape with entire or slightly wavy margins and a pubescent surface texture. Small, bell-shaped flowers emerge from the leaf axils, typically displaying a pale yellow or creamy coloration. A defining morphological feature is the presence of a persistent, inflated calyx that expands to form a papery, bladder-like husk around the developing fruit. These distinctive husks protect the small, fleshy berries that mature within the protective enclosure.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant Physalis pubescens, commonly known as groundcherry, requires well-drained soil and full sun exposure to produce its characteristic edible berries.

Gardeners should plant seeds indoors several weeks before the last frost to ensure a strong start in warmer temperatures. Providing organic mulch around the base helps retain soil moisture and suppress competing weeds.

What are the pharmacological activities of Physalis pubescens?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, hypoglycemic, and immunomodulatory pharmacological activities. Its diverse chemical profile allows it to combat oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals in the body. The plant also demonstrates a capacity to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and reduce systemic inflammation. Furthermore, its hypoglycemic properties assist in regulating blood glucose levels, making it a subject of interest for metabolic health. These combined effects contribute to its potential role in modulating the immune system and enhancing overall physiological defense mechanisms.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", investigation on the extract of Physalis pubescens fruit led to the isolation and identification of 4 new withanolides, specifically physapubescins J-M (1, 2, 4 and 5). The process also identified 4 known analogues (3, 6-8) and 15 other compounds. Among these isolates, physapubescin J (1) contained an unusual sulphide linkage. Additionally, 4 withanolides (3, 5, 7 and 8) demonstrated anti-inflammatory potential in LPS-induced RAW264.7 cells. These findings suggest that Physalis pubescens fruit could serve as a valuable source of withanolides.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including physalin B, physalin L, physalin O, physapubescin J, and various withanolides. These specific physalins are known for their potent cytotoxic activities against different cancer cell lines. The presence of physapubescin J further contributes to the complex chemical profile used in pharmacological research. Additionally, the withanolides within the plant are studied for their potential anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective properties. Together, these constituents make the species a significant subject of interest in drug discovery and natural product chemistry.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in pharmacology", the ethyl acetate extract of Physalis pubescens leaves, known as PHY-EA, contains the medicinal compounds physalin B, physalin O, and physalin L. This PHY-EA extract was found to block the cell cycle of G2/M to G0/G1 in lymphoma cells and induce apoptosis in tumor cells. The study used high performance liquid chromatography to identify the chemical composition and sulforhodaimine B to detect proliferation inhibitory effects on cells such as MCF-7, CA-46, Hela, HepG2, and B16. In mouse transplantation tumor experiments, the PHY-EA extract resulted in significantly reduced tumor volume and weight. These findings indicate that Physalis pubescens has a good antiproliferative effect on Burkitt lymphoma.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Burkitt lymphoma, prostate cancer, renal cell carcinoma, and various other forms of cancer and lymphoma. Researchers have identified specific steroidal lactones within the plant that may trigger programmed cell death in malignant cells. These bioactive compounds show significant potential in inhibiting the growth of diverse tumor types during laboratory studies. Ongoing clinical investigations aim to further understand how these extracts can target specific malignancies like lymphoma effectively. Such studies highlight the importance of the plant in developing new therapeutic strategies for oncology.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in pharmacology", Physalis pubescens L. is used in traditional medicine for treating sore throats, coughs, urinary discomfort, and astringent pain. In northern China, it is also used externally for pemphigus and eczema. Research investigated the ethyl acetate extract (PHY-EA) from the leaves and found it contains physalin B, physalin O, and physalin L. This PHY-EA extract demonstrated a proliferation inhibitory effect on tumor cells including MCF-7, CA-46, Hela, HepG2, and B16. Specifically, PHY-EA blocked the cell cycle of G2/M→G0/G1 in lymphoma cells and showed a good antiproliferative effect on Burkitt lymphoma. Mouse transplantation tumor experiments further showed that PHY-EA significantly reduced tumor volume and weight.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe physiological distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach cramps, dizziness, confusion, hallucinations, increased heart rate, and dry mouth. These toxic reactions primarily stem from the presence of tropane alkaloids found within the plant's tissues. Consuming even small amounts can lead to significant neurological impairment such as disorientation or vivid visual disturbances. Physical symptoms often include a rapid pulse and an uncomfortable sensation of dryness in the mouth. Such a wide range of symptoms indicates that the plant acts as a potent anticholinergic agent in the human body.
