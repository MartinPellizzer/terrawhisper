Scientific and Botanical Profile

Annual Seseli, with botanical name Seseli annuum, is a member of the Apiaceae family, commonly known as the carrot or parsley family.

This herbaceous annual plant is widely recognized by its common names, including Annual Seseli, Common Seseli, Seseli Annual, Common Seseli, Seseli Weed, and Seseli Weed, and is often found in disturbed areas across its native range. Native to North America, particularly the United States, it thrives in regions such as California, Arizona, New Mexico, Texas, Colorado, Utah, Nevada, Oklahoma, Kansas, and the Colorado River Basin, making it a common sight in the Southwestern United States. Morphologically, Seseli annuum is a slender plant with finely divided, fern-like leaves and small, clustered umbels of white or pale green flowers.

Its life cycle is adapted to short growing seasons, and it often serves as a weed in agricultural and natural settings due to its rapid growth and seed dispersal.

History and Cultural Relevance

Annual Seseli was used by various ancient civilizations for its medicinal properties and spiritual significance, with evidence of its use dating back to early agricultural societies in the Mediterranean and Middle East.

Its enduring presence in cultural and medicinal practices highlights its deep-rooted significance in human history.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Seseli annuum has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported therapeutic properties, with various forms available including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare seseli annuum, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant material for a longer period. Infusion methods are also used, particularly for extracting its volatile compounds, and topical applications such as salves or oils are common for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults typically ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried herb per day, while children may use smaller amounts if deemed safe by a healthcare provider.

Due to limited scientific evidence, usage should be conservative, with short-term application preferred unless under professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Seseli annuum can be used as a medicinal plant with some therapeutic benefits, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, dizziness, and skin irritation in some individuals. There is limited research on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but it is advisable to avoid combining it with sedatives or anticoagulants due to potential additive effects. Special populations, including pregnant or breastfeeding women, should avoid seseli annuum due to insufficient data on its effects on fetal or infant development, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

For safe use, always follow recommended dosages, consult with a qualified healthcare professional, and discontinue use if adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Seseli annuum grows best in well-drained, loamy soil that is rich in organic matter, with a pH range of 6.

0 to 7. 5, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall, needing regular watering during dry periods but avoiding waterlogging to prevent root rot. Regular weeding and mulching help maintain soil moisture and reduce competition from weeds, while periodic fertilization with a balanced NPK fertilizer supports robust growth. Harvesting should occur when the seeds are fully mature and have turned from green to a dark brown or black color, typically in late summer or early autumn, using a sharp pair of scissors or a small sickle to cut the seed heads without damaging the plant.

For storage, the harvested seeds should be dried thoroughly in a well-ventilated area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark, and dry place, or refrigerated to preserve potency and extend shelf life.
