Gnaphalium uliginosum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gnaphaliuum uliginosum has erect, woody-based perennial stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and are covered in dense, white, woolly hairs. The leaves are small, linear to lanceolate in shape, and possess a distinctive mucronate tip that gives them a slightly pointed appearance. These leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and exhibit a grayish-green color due to the thick layer of fine pubescence. The plant produces small, discoid flower heads that are clustered in terminal corymbiform cymes. These flower heads are characterized by their lack of ray florets and their yellowish-white color, often appearing quite compact. The overall texture of the plant is soft and felt-like because of the pervasive tomentum.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the western and southwestern United States, specifically ranging from California through the Great Basin and into parts of Arizona and Nevada. The species typically thrives in well-drained soils at various elevations ranging from low-lying basins to higher montane areas. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of specific soil types and moisture levels found in these dry western landscapes. What are the herbal preparations of Gnaphalium uliginosum?

Gnaphalium uliginosum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice to utilize its medicinal properties. The dried leaves and flowering tops are often simmered in water to create a strong tea used for internal consumption. For external applications, the plant material can be crushed into a paste to soothe skin irritations. Some traditional practices also involve using the plant in steam inhalations to clear respiratory passages. These various methods allow the active compounds to be delivered effectively depending on the ailment being treated.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Gnaphaliuum uliginosum can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many members of the Asteraceae family are known to trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Touching the plant may result in redness, itching, or localized swelling on the skin surface. If consumed accidentally, it might lead to nausea or stomach upset depending on the quantity ingested. Caution is advised when handling this species to avoid these adverse physical responses.
