Scientific and Botanical Profile

Chrysanthemum, with botanical name Chrysanthemum indicum, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the daisy or sunflower family.

It is also referred to as Chinese Chrysanthemum, Mum, Garden Chrysanthemum, Flower of the Sun, Chrysanthemum Flower, Indian Chrysanthemum, and Chrysantha, reflecting its widespread cultural and botanical significance. Native to East Asia, particularly China, Korea, and Japan, this species has been cultivated for centuries for its ornamental and medicinal properties. Morphologically, it is a herbaceous perennial with a bushy growth habit, featuring alternate, pinnately divided leaves and a variety of flower heads that range from single to double blooms, typically in shades of yellow, orange, red, and white.

Its diverse forms and vibrant colors make it a popular garden plant, while its compounds have been studied for potential therapeutic uses in traditional medicine.

History and Cultural Relevance

Chrysanthemum was used in ancient China as a medicinal herb in traditional Chinese medicine, valued for its anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and cooling properties.

It has been cultivated for thousands of years, with the species Chrysanthemum indicum playing a key role in both herbal remedies and ornamental horticulture. In Japanese culture, chrysanthemums are deeply symbolic, representing longevity, honor, and the imperial family, and are prominently featured in festivals such as the Chrysanthemum Festival. The plant has also been incorporated into various folk traditions, including rituals to ward off evil spirits and promote good health.

Today, chrysanthemum extracts are still used in modern medicine for treating conditions like headaches, fevers, and skin ailments, highlighting its enduring cultural and therapeutic significance.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Chrysanthemum contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, terpenes, alkaloids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

These compounds, such as apigenin and luteolin, are known for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Nutritional-wise, chrysanthemum is rich in vitamins like A, C, and K, as well as minerals such as iron and calcium, making it a nutrient-dense plant. The active compounds in chrysanthemum work by scavenging free radicals, modulating enzyme activity, and reducing oxidative stress in the body.

Its unique chemical profile supports its traditional use in promoting eye health, reducing inflammation, and supporting immune function.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Chrysanthemum indicum has been widely recognized for its medicinal properties and numerous health benefits, particularly in traditional Chinese medicine.

It is known to support the respiratory, cardiovascular, and digestive systems by reducing inflammation, improving circulation, and aiding in digestion. Its active compounds, such as flavonoids and sesquiterpenes, contribute to its ability to lower fever, relieve headaches, and promote liver detoxification. Compared to similar herbs like Chrysanthemum morifolium, Indicum is often considered more potent in its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, making it a preferred choice for treating conditions like hypertension and seasonal allergies.

Additionally, its calming properties make it a valuable herb for reducing stress and improving mental clarity, offering a unique combination of physical and mental health benefits.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Chrysanthemum indicum has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms such as fresh flowers, dried flowers, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare chrysanthemum indicum, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the flowers for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion or applied topically for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 2-4 grams of dried flowers per cup of tea, while children may require a reduced dose if deemed safe by a healthcare provider.

It is advised to use chrysanthemum indicum no more than 2-3 times daily for short durations, staying conservative to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Chrysanthemum indicum can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antipyretic properties, but its use requires careful consideration due to potential side effects and interactions.

Common side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea or stomach upset, and allergic reactions in individuals sensitive to plants in the Asteraceae family. It may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and antihypertensive drugs, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or lowering blood pressure excessively. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, while individuals with chronic illnesses like diabetes or hypertension should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, it is recommended to use chrysanthemum indicum under the guidance of a qualified healthcare professional, start with small doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Chrysanthemum indicum grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without waterlogging. For optimal growth, it should be planted in spring after the last frost, with spacing of about 30-45 cm between plants to allow for proper air circulation and root development. Regular care includes pruning dead or diseased foliage, fertilizing every 4-6 weeks with a balanced fertilizer, and monitoring for pests such as aphids and spider mites. The best time to harvest chrysanthemum indicum is during early morning or late afternoon when the flowers are fully open but before they begin to wilt, using sharp scissors to cut the flower heads just below the stem to preserve the plant's energy.

To preserve its potency, the harvested flowers should be air-dried in a cool, shaded area or dried in a low-temperature oven, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties.
