What is Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum Indicum)?

Chrysanthemum indicum, also known as the Indian chrysanthemum, is a flowering plant native to Asia and is widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, and antimicrobial properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Chrysanthemum is Chrysanthemum indicum. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Chrysanthemum. It is known as chrysanthemum in China, Korea, and Japan, and as marguerite in France and England. The synonyms include Chrysanthemum morifolium, Chrysanthemum zawadskii, and regional names such as chrysanthemum in India and Korea.

Plant Description and Morphology

Chrysanthemum indicum is a herbaceous perennial plant characterized by its upright growth habit, with hairy stems that are often reddish-brown in color, and alternate, pinnately lobed leaves that are typically gray-green in appearance.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chrysanthemum indicum is native to southern Asia, including regions of India, China, and Southeast Asia, and has been historically cultivated for its medicinal and ornamental properties.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and roots of Chrysanthemum indicum are used medicinally, with the flowers being the most commonly utilized part for their therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Chrysanthemum indicum contains a variety of bioactive compounds including flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, phenolic acids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Chrysanthemum indicum possesses anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and essential oils, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Mechanisms of Action

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antipyretic properties, which are mediated by the modulation of immune responses, inhibition of pro-inflammatory cytokines, and scavenging of free radicals.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Chrysanthemum indicum is traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat conditions such as fever, inflammation, hypertension, and respiratory disorders, with its active compounds exhibiting antipyretic, anti-inflammatory, and antihypertensive properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is typically prepared as a dried herb, often used in decoctions, teas, or powdered form, with the dried flower heads being the primary part utilized in traditional herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Chrysanthemum indicum for herbal use typically ranges from 3 to 10 grams per day, prepared as a decoction or infusion, and should be taken consistently under the guidance of a qualified herbal practitioner or healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a herbal remedy, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by individuals with known allergies to the Asteraceae family.

Drug Interactions

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, and drugs metabolized by the liver, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or altering drug efficacy.

Toxicity and Precautions

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, so it should be used with caution and under professional guidance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chrysanthemum indicum is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and partial shade, requiring regular watering and pruning to promote healthy growth and optimal flower production.

Processing and Extraction

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is typically processed by drying the fully bloomed flowers, followed by extraction methods such as solvent extraction, ethanol extraction, or supercritical fluid extraction to obtain active compounds like flavonoids and essential oils.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent spoilage, with a typical shelf life of 1 to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence on Chrysanthemum indicum have shown its potential pharmacological activities, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, supported by both in vitro and animal studies, though more clinical trials are needed to confirm its efficacy in human applications.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chrysanthemum indicum has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, and detoxifying properties, with historical applications ranging from treating fevers and headaches to aiding digestion and respiratory conditions.

Regulatory Status

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is classified as a traditional herbal medicine in many countries, with varying levels of regulation depending on the region, and its use is often governed by local pharmaceutical and herbal medicine laws.
