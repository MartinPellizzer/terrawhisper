Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is a flowering plant native to Asia, widely cultivated for its ornamental value and traditional medicinal properties, particularly for its ability to improve conditions such as inflammation, fever, and respiratory issues.

What is Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum Indicum)?

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is a flowering plant native to Asia, widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Chrysanthemum indicum, commonly known as the chrysanthemum, is a flowering plant in the family Asteraceae, native to Asia and widely cultivated for its ornamental and medicinal properties.

Common Names and Synonyms

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is commonly known by various names such as Chrysanthemum morifolium, Chrysanthemum japonicum, and in some regions, it is referred to as the Chinese chrysanthemum, autumn flower, or garden chrysanthemum.

Plant Description and Morphology

Chrysanthemum indicum is a herbaceous perennial plant characterized by its upright growth habit, with opposite, pinnately compound leaves that are usually serrated and covered in fine hairs, and large, daisy-like flowers that range in color from white to yellow, orange, and red, depending on the variety.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chrysanthemum indicum is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, including India, China, Japan, and parts of Southeast Asia, where it thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and roots of Chrysanthemum indicum are used medicinally, with the dried flowers being the most commonly utilized part for traditional herbal preparations.

Phytochemical Composition

Chrysanthemum indicum contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, alkaloids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Chrysanthemum indicum exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, and antimicrobial effects, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and essential oils.

Mechanisms of Action

Chrysanthemum indicum exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, which are attributed to the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, terpenoids, and polysaccharides that modulate immune responses and cellular signaling pathways.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Chrysanthemum is used to treat conditions such as headaches, fever, inflammation, eye disorders, hypertension, respiratory infections, skin conditions, and digestive issues.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is typically prepared as a dried herb, often used in teas, decoctions, or as powdered extracts, with the flowers being the primary part utilized in traditional herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) for medicinal use ranges from 6 to 12 grams per day, usually prepared as a decoction or infusion, and should be taken according to the guidance of a qualified practitioner.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in excess or improperly, Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, and interactions with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system.

Drug Interactions

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, and central nervous system depressants, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or enhancing sedative effects.

Toxicity and Precautions

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interactions with certain medications, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and lactation.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chrysanthemum indicum is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil, requiring full sun exposure and regular watering during its growing season.

Processing and Extraction

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is typically processed by drying the flowers, which are harvested at peak bloom, and then either used whole or ground into a powder for incorporation into herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 1 to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Extensive scientific research has demonstrated that Chrysanthemum indicum contains bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, terpenoids, and phenolic acids, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, and antioxidant properties, with historical applications dating back to ancient Chinese and Indian medicinal practices.

Regulatory Status

Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum indicum) is classified as a traditional herbal medicine in many countries, with varying levels of regulatory oversight depending on the region, including specific guidelines for its use in pharmaceutical products and dietary supplements.
