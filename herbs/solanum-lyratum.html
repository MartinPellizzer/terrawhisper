Solanum lyratum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum lyratum?

Solanum lyratum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Solanales and falls within the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Solanum and its unique species designation, lyratum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum lyratum has a woody or herbaceous growth habit characterized by stems covered in dense, stellate hairs and leaves that are deeply lobed with a lyre-shaped appearance. The foliage typically presents as large, spreading leaves that are often covered in a fine layer of pubescence on both the upper and lower surfaces. Its flowers are actinomorphic and pentamerous, featuring bright yellow corollas and prominent central anthers. The fruit is a fleshy berry that transitions from green to a dark purple or black color upon maturity. These morphological traits distinguish the species within the Solanaceae family by its specific leaf architecture and hairy textures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical and tropical regions of eastern Australia, specifically spanning from Queensland down through New South Wales. It is frequently found inhabiting diverse environments such as rainforest margins, coastal scrubs, and moist sclerophyll forests. Within these habitats, it often grows in shaded areas or along the edges of dense vegetation where moisture is readily available. While its primary range is concentrated along the eastern coastline, isolated populations may exist in similar climatic zones across the continent. This specific distribution pattern reflects the species' preference for warm, humid climates.

What are the pharmacological activities of Solanum lyratum?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anti-proliferative, anti-tumor, apoptosis induction, and cytotoxicity. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds work to inhibit the growth and spread of malignant cells. These chemical constituents trigger apoptosis induction, which leads to programmed cell death in targeted cancer lineages. The observed cytotoxicity plays a crucial role in suppressing tumor progression through direct cellular damage. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory properties help manage the biological processes that support chronic disease environments.

As discussed in a study, the crude fraction E11 from Solanum lyratum Thunb revealed significant anti-inflammatory activity in in vivo and in vitro experiments at low concentrations. Through further separation and purification, 4 compounds were isolated from the plant by continuous anti-inflammatory screening. Among these, compound 4, which is diosgenin3-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1->2)-beta-D-glucopyranosiduronic acid, showed significant anti-inflammatory activity. This activity was observed at a concentration of 16 µg/mL. Furthermore, this specific compound showed no toxicity to human microvascular endothelial cells.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apiagenin, caffeic acid, daidzein, genistein, and rutin. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile and potential health benefits. Apiagenin and rutin are known for their antioxidant properties, while caffeic acid offers significant anti-inflammatory support. The isoflavones daidzein and genistein are particularly noted for their roles in hormonal regulation and cellular signaling. Together, these bioactive molecules work through various biological pathways to support overall wellness.

As discussed in a study, a 95% alcohol extract of Solanum lyratum was investigated to identify its chemical constituents. Eleven compounds were isolated and identified, including formononetin (1), vanillic acid (2), genistein (3), apiigenin (4), N-trans-feruloyltyramine (5), N-p-coumaroyltyramine (6), daidzein (7), caffeic acid (8), protocatechuic acid (9), daizdin (10), and N-trans-feruloyl-3-methyldopamine (11). Within this group, compounds 5 and 10 were specifically isolated from Solanum. Furthermore, compounds 1, 3, 4, 7, and 9 were obtained from this plant for the first time.
