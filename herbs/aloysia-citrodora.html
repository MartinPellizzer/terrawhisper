Citronella (Aloysia citrodora): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Citronella (Aloysia Citrodora)?

Citronella, or Aloysia citrodora, is a flowering plant native to the Mediterranean region and is widely used in herbal medicine for its aromatic properties and various therapeutic benefits.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Citronella is Aloysia citrodora. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Verbenaceae, genus Aloysia, and species Aloysia citrodora. It is commonly known as citronella in tropical regions and as sweet geranium in parts of Europe. The synonyms for this plant include Aloysia triphylla and Citrosma citrora.

Plant Description and Morphology

Citronella is a perennial shrub that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters, with a dense, bushy structure and a woody stem.

Its leaves are elliptical to lance-shaped, with a smooth, slightly serrated margin and a dark green, glossy upper surface. The flowers are small, tubular, and clustered in dense inflorescences, with a pale yellow to white color and a strong, sweet fragrance. The root system is fibrous and deep-reaching, anchoring the plant firmly in the soil.

The plant has a distinctive lemony scent, with a rough, slightly hairy texture on the leaves and stems.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Citronella is native to the Mediterranean region, particularly southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East.

It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters and warm, sunny summers. The plant is commonly found in coastal areas, rocky soils, and dry, well-drained habitats. Its natural range extends from southern France to the Canary Islands and parts of Turkey.

Citronella prefers moderate rainfall and is adapted to low-nutrient soils.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in citronella are the leaves, flowers, and essential oil extracted from the leaves.

The leaves are most commonly utilized due to their high concentration of citral, a compound known for its insect-repelling properties. Flowers are also used, particularly in traditional remedies, though less frequently than leaves. The essential oil is often distilled from the leaves and applied topically or diffused for its aromatic and therapeutic benefits.

Each part contributes uniquely to the plant's medicinal applications in herbal medicine.

Phytochemical Composition

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) contains a variety of active compounds that contribute to its medicinal properties.

These include essential oils, flavonoids, and phenolic acids, which are known for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Secondary metabolites such as terpenes and monoterpene glycosides play a key role in the plant's defense mechanisms and therapeutic applications. Chemical markers like citral, geraniol, and limonene are particularly prominent and are responsible for the plant's characteristic aroma and bioactive properties.

These compounds collectively define the pharmacological profile of citronella in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that make it valuable in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antispasmodic effects. The biological activity of citronella is attributed to its essential oils, particularly citral and geraniol, which have potent bioactive compounds. Pharmacodynamically, citronella interacts with various physiological systems to modulate immune responses and reduce oxidative stress.

System-level effects include its ability to support digestive health and alleviate symptoms associated with gastrointestinal disorders.

Mechanisms of Action

Citronella exerts its effects primarily through the inhibition of cellular membrane permeability and disruption of ion channels, which leads to altered cell signaling.

At the cellular level, it interacts with lipid bilayers, reducing membrane fluidity and affecting the function of ion channels and transporters. Biochemically, citronella contains compounds like citral and geraniol that inhibit the activity of key enzymes such as cyclooxygenase and lipoxygenase, reducing inflammatory responses. These compounds also modulate the arachidonic acid pathway, influencing prostaglandin and leukotriene production.

Target systems include the nervous system, where it affects neurotransmitter release, and the respiratory system, where it exhibits antispasmodic properties.

Therapeutic Uses

Citronella, or Aloysia citrodora, is widely used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties.

It is commonly employed to treat respiratory conditions such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma due to its expectorant and antispasmodic effects. The plant is also used to alleviate digestive issues like indigestion and nausea because of its carminative and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, citronella has been traditionally used to manage skin conditions such as eczema and psoriasis due to its antiseptic and soothing qualities.

Its essential oils are also utilized in aromatherapy to reduce stress and improve mood.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) primarily involve using its leaves and flowers, which are harvested and processed into various forms.

The most common forms include essential oils, dried herbs, and infusions. Essential oils are extracted through steam distillation of the fresh leaves and flowers. Dried herbs are often used in teas or as part of herbal blends.

Infusions are made by steeping the dried leaves in hot water to release their active compounds.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of citronella for herbal use is typically 100 to 200 milligrams per day, depending on the form and concentration.

Citronella extract should be diluted in a carrier oil or water to avoid skin irritation or gastrointestinal upset. It is recommended to take citronella in divided doses throughout the day for optimal absorption and reduced risk of side effects. The duration of treatment should not exceed two weeks unless under the guidance of a healthcare professional.

Overuse can lead to nausea, headaches, or allergic reactions, so it is important to follow recommended dosages strictly.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, citronella is generally safe for most adults, but it can cause side effects such as skin irritation, gastrointestinal discomfort, and allergic reactions in some individuals.

Prolonged or excessive use may lead to more severe side effects, including nausea, vomiting, and liver damage. Citronella should be avoided by individuals with known allergies to related plants in the mint family, as cross-reactivity is possible. It is contraindicated for use in children under six years old and should be used with caution in individuals with epilepsy or seizure disorders.

During pregnancy, citronella is considered unsafe due to potential risks to fetal development, and it should be avoided unless specifically recommended by a healthcare provider.

Drug Interactions

Citronella, derived from Aloysia citrodora, may interact with certain pharmaceuticals and supplements, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.

It can interfere with medications that affect the liver's ability to metabolize drugs, such as anticoagulants and anticonvulsants. These interactions may lead to increased blood thinning effects or reduced therapeutic outcomes. Citronella may also interact with supplements like St. John's Wort, which can enhance its own metabolism and reduce its potency.

Always consult a healthcare provider before combining citronella with any medication or supplement.

Toxicity and Precautions

Toxicity and Precautions: Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but excessive consumption can lead to adverse effects.

The toxic dose for adults is typically above 500 mg per day, though this can vary based on individual sensitivity. Long-term safety data is limited, and prolonged use may cause gastrointestinal discomfort or skin irritation. While rare, poisoning risks exist if ingested in large quantities, particularly in children or pets.

Symptoms of poisoning include nausea, vomiting, and dizziness, and medical attention should be sought if these occur.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is typically grown in well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5, which supports optimal root development and nutrient uptake.

It requires full sun exposure for at least 6 to 8 hours daily to ensure robust growth and essential oil production. Regular watering is necessary, especially during dry periods, but the soil should not remain waterlogged to prevent root rot. Propagation is most effectively done through stem cuttings taken in spring, which root easily in moist sand or a potting mix. The plant reaches maturity in about 12 to 18 months, with the best harvest time occurring in late summer to early autumn when essential oil content is highest.

Harvesting involves cutting the top 10 to 15 cm of the stem, ensuring the plant continues to grow and produce new shoots.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and Extraction of Citronella involves several key steps to obtain its essential oils and active compounds.

The first step is drying the fresh plant material to reduce moisture content and prevent microbial growth. Once dried, the plant is ground into a fine powder to increase surface area for more efficient extraction. Extraction methods typically involve steam distillation, which captures the volatile oils through heat and pressure.

The distillation process separates the essential oil from the plant residue, yielding a concentrated form of citronella oil.

Storage and Shelf Life

Citronella, or Aloysia citrodora, should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain its potency.

The shelf life of citronella typically ranges from 1 to 2 years when stored properly. It is light sensitive, so exposure to sunlight can degrade its active compounds over time. To prevent moisture damage, keep the herb in a sealed container to avoid humidity.

Proper storage conditions are essential to preserve its medicinal properties and ensure effectiveness.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) has primarily focused on its essential oil components, particularly citral and geraniol, which have shown potential antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and repellent properties in both in vitro and clinical studies.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties, often applied externally to treat wounds, skin infections, and inflammatory conditions, and internally to aid digestion and relieve gastrointestinal discomfort.

Regulatory Status
