Citronella (Aloysia citrodora): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is a fragrant shrub native to the Mediterranean region, known for its lemon-scented leaves and flowers, and it has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, particularly in improving symptoms of digestive disorders and respiratory conditions.

What is Citronella (Aloysia Citrodora)?

Citronella, or Aloysia citrodora, is a perennial shrub native to the Mediterranean region, known for its strong lemony scent and various uses in herbal medicine.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Citronella, scientifically known as Aloysia citrodora, is a perennial shrub belonging to the verbena family (Verbenaceae) and is native to the Mediterranean region.

Common Names and Synonyms

Citronella, also known as Aloysia citrodora, is commonly referred to by various names including lemon verbena, sweet verbena, and lemon beebrush, and is widely recognized for its aromatic properties and use in herbal medicine.

Plant Description and Morphology

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is a perennial shrub characterized by its aromatic, narrow leaves with a strong lemon scent, yellowish-white flowers arranged in terminal panicles, and a distinctive, branched stem covered in fine, white hairs.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, and has been introduced to other regions with suitable climates for cultivation.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and essential oils of Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) are primarily used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including essential oils rich in citral, geraniol, and limonene, along with flavonoids, phenolic acids, and terpenoids that contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and analgesic effects, primarily due to its high concentration of volatile oils such as citral, geraniol, and limonene, which contribute to its therapeutic potential in herbal medicine.

Mechanisms of Action

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) exerts its effects primarily through the release of volatile compounds such as citral, limonene, and geraniol, which interact with sensory receptors and have antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and repellent properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Citronella is used to treat digestive issues, nausea, motion sickness, headaches, muscle pain, skin infections, and as a natural insect repellent.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is typically prepared as an essential oil, tincture, or dried herb for use in herbal medicine, with the most common forms being steam-distilled essential oil, alcohol-based tinctures, and dried leaves or flowers for teas or poultices.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of citronella (Aloysia citrodora) for herbal use ranges from 200 to 500 mg per day, often divided into two or three doses, with the exact amount depending on the specific preparation and the intended therapeutic effect.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While citronella is generally considered safe when used in recommended amounts, it may cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal discomfort, or allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly with excessive or prolonged use.

Drug Interactions

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) may interact with medications that affect the liver's ability to metabolize drugs, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects when used concurrently with certain prescription medications.

Toxicity and Precautions

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is generally safe when used in moderate amounts as a culinary herb or in aromatherapy, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset or skin irritation in some individuals, and large quantities may be toxic, particularly to pets and livestock.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is typically cultivated in warm, sunny climates with well-drained soil, requiring regular watering and pruning to promote healthy growth and essential oil production.

Processing and Extraction

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is processed by steam distillation of its leaves and flowers to extract the essential oil, which contains the primary active compound, citral, responsible for its aromatic and medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically around 1 to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) has primarily focused on its essential oil components, particularly citral, which has shown potential antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and repellent properties, though clinical evidence for its use in human medicine remains limited.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties, with historical applications ranging from treating respiratory infections to alleviating digestive discomfort and skin irritations.

Regulatory Status

Citronella (Aloysia citrodora) is not approved as a drug by the FDA, but it is permitted for use in herbal medicine under the Dietary Supplement and Herbal Medicine Act, with specific regulations varying by country.
