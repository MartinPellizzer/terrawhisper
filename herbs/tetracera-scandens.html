Tetracera scandens

What's the taxonomical classification of Tetracera scandens?

Tetracera scandens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its descent through the order Dilleniales and the family Dilleniaceae, the plant is placed in the genus Tetracera, with its specific designation being the species scandens.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various conditions including stomach ailments, intestinal worms, and skin infections. The roots and leaves are often prepared as decoctions to alleviate digestive discomfort or to purge parasites from the body. In traditional medicine, topical applications of the plant are employed to soothe dermatological irritations and wounds. Some regional practices also utilize the plant to manage symptoms associated with inflammation. Its medicinal properties are primarily centered around its perceived antimicrobial and anthelmintic qualities.

What are the herbal preparations of Tetracera scandens?

Tetracera scandens is prepared primarily as a decoction of its roots, a tea from its leaves, or a topical infusion of its crushed plant material. Practitioners often boil the woody roots to extract concentrated medicinal compounds for internal consumption. The leaves are frequently steeped in hot water to create a gentle infusion used for various ailments. When applied externally, the plant can be mashed into a paste or soaked in water to treat skin conditions. These diverse methods allow for the specific delivery of the plant's bioactive constituents depending on the intended therapeutic goal.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Tetracera scandens can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or localized swelling if the plant comes into direct contact with human flesh. While specific toxicological studies on humans are limited, the presence of certain chemical compounds in many climbing vines suggests a risk of dermatological reactions. Contact with the sap or leaves may trigger redness, itching, or a burning sensation on sensitive areas of the skin. Some individuals might also experience systemic sensitivity if the plant is ingested, though detailed data on internal toxicity is sparse. It is generally advisable to avoid touching the foliage to prevent these unpredictable physiological responses.
