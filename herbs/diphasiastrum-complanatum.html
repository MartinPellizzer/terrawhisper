Diphasiastrum complanatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Diphasiastrum complanatum?

Diphasiastrum complanatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Lycopodiidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Lycopodiales. Its taxonomic position is further refined within the family Lycopodiaceae, placing it specifically in the genus Diphasiastrum. Ultimately, the plant is identified at the species level as complanatum.

What are the pharmacological activities of Diphasiastrum complanatum?

This plant has potent pharmacological activities including anti-apoptotic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties. Its antioxidant capabilities help neutralize harmful free radicals to prevent cellular oxidative stress. By exerting anti-inflammatory effects, the plant can effectively reduce swelling and systemic irritation within biological tissues. Additionally, its anti-apoptotic mechanisms function to inhibit programmed cell death in targeted cell populations. These combined therapeutic actions suggest significant potential for its use in managing various inflammatory and degenerative conditions.

As discussed in a study, alkaloid fractions (AFs) isolated from Diphasiastrum complanatum were investigated for their effects against sodium nitroprusside (SNP)-induced oxidative injury in PC12 cells. These selected AFs protected cells against oxidative stress, DNA and mitochondrial damage, as well as apoptosis caused by SNP via reduction of nitric oxide (NO) liberation. The AF containing Lycopodine, Serratidine, Lycoposerramine-G and (probably) Cermizine B completely inhibited the SNP-induced expression of interferon-gamma (Ifng) and cyclooxygenase 2 (Ptgs2). Additionally, this fraction significantly down-regulated the expression of 12/15-lipoxygenase (Alox12) and tended to decrease the mRNA level of interleukin-6 (Il6) gene. These results suggest that the AFs from Diphasiastrum complanatum could be potential candidates for developing drugs to prevent oxidative damage and apoptosis in age-related neurodegenerative disorders.
