What's the taxonomical classification of Diphasiastrum complanatum?

Diphasiastrum complanatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Lycopodiidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Lycopodiales. Its taxonomic position is further refined within the family Lycopodiaceae, placing it specifically in the genus Diphasiastrum. Ultimately, the plant is identified at the species level as complanatum.

What are the pharmacological activities of Diphasiastrum complanatum?

This plant has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anti-apoptotic pharmacological activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent oxidative stress within cellular structures. The anti-inflammatory effects allow the plant to modulate immune responses and reduce systemic swelling or pain. Additionally, its anti-apoptotic activity can influence programmed cell death pathways, which is significant for protecting healthy cells from premature destruction. These combined biological actions suggest that the species may play a role in managing various chronic diseases.

According to a study published by "Folia neuropathologica", alkaloid fractions isolated from Diphasiastrum complanatum demonstrate beneficial activity against sodium nitroprusside-induced oxidative injury in PC12 cells. These selected alkaloid fractions protect cells against oxidative stress, DNA and mitochondrial damage, as well as apoptosis by reducing nitric oxide liberation. An alkaloid fraction containing Lycopodine, Serratidine, Lycoposerramine-G and potentially Cermizine B completely inhibited the expression of interferon-gamma and cyclooxygenase 2. This same fraction significantly down-regulated the expression of 12/15-lipoxygenase and showed a tendency to decrease the mRNA level of the interleukin-6 gene. These findings suggest that the compounds within these fractions could serve as potential candidates for developing drugs to prevent oxidative damage and apoptosis in age-related neurodegenerative disorders.
