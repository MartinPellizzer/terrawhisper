Celery

Apium graveolens

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a biennial herb native to southern Europe and western Asia, commonly cultivated for its edible stalks and leaves, and historically used for its medicinal properties to support cardiovascular health and reduce inflammation.

Taxonomy

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a flowering plant in the family Apiaceae, belonging to the genus Apium within the order Apiales, class Magnoliopsida, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae.

Morphology

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a biennial herb with a slender, ridged stem that is typically green or reddish in color, bearing alternate, deeply lobed leaves that are often bluish-green in hue.

Distribution

Celery (Apium graveolens) is native to southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, with a wide distribution across temperate regions, including parts of the United States and Canada.

Traditional Uses

Celery has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties, often prepared as a tea, tincture, or poultice to treat conditions such as urinary tract infections, kidney stones, and gastrointestinal discomfort.

Medicinal Systems

In traditional medicinal systems such as Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, and Native American herbalism, Celery (Apium graveolens) has been utilized for its purported health benefits, including digestive support, respiratory relief, and anti-inflammatory properties.

Phytochemistry

Celery contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including volatile oils such as limonene and myrcene, alkaloids like apigenin and luteolin, and polyacetylenes, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic effects.

Pharmacology

Celery exhibits anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and diuretic properties, making it effective in reducing inflammation, relieving muscle spasms, and promoting urinary function.

Botanical Identification

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a biennial herb in the Apiaceae family. Common names include sweet celery, garden celery, and Chinese celery. Native to the Mediterranean region, it has aromatic leaves, slender stalks, and small yellow flowers. Synonyms in herbal literature include Apium dulce and Apium petiolatum.

Active Compounds

Celery contains essential oil, coumarin, and flavonoids as its main bioactive compounds. These compounds work together to provide its therapeutic effects, with essential oils contributing to anti-inflammatory properties and coumarin offering antispasmodic benefits. Flavonoids support antioxidant activity, enhancing the herb's overall efficacy in herbal medicine.

Safety Profile

Celery is generally safe when used in moderate amounts as a food or herbal remedy. However, it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals. It is contraindicated for those with known allergies to the Apiaceae family, which includes carrots and parsley. Prolonged use or high doses may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or interact with certain medications.
