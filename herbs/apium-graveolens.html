Apium graveolens

What's the taxonomical classification of Apium graveolens?

Apium graveolens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It further falls under the order Apiales and is a member of the family Apiaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Apium and the species graveolens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Apium graveolens has a hollow, succulent stem that reaches heights of up to one meter, complemented by pinnate leaves featuring serrated edges and a deep green color. The plant develops a taproot system that provides stability and nutrient uptake from the soil. Small, white flowers grow in compound umbels, which are characteristic of the Apiaceae family. These floral clusters eventually produce small, oval-shaped fruits that contain numerous seeds. The overall structure is herbaceous and can vary in size depending on the specific cultivar and growing environment.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the wetlands and marshy areas of Europe, western Asia, and northwestern Africa. It thrives in coastal regions and along the banks of freshwater streams where the soil remains consistently moist. While its natural range is concentrated in these specific temperate zones, it has become naturalized in many other parts of the world through human introduction. Cultivated varieties are now grown globally for culinary purposes, particularly in North America and parts of Asia. Its ability to adapt to various maritime and riparian environments allows it to spread easily in suitable climates.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, photosensitivity, allergic reactions, dizziness, hypoglycemia, and electrolyte imbalance. Some individuals may experience gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or abdominal pain after consumption. Skin sensitivity to sunlight can lead to photosensitivity, while others might suffer from allergic reactions or dizziness. Consuming large amounts could potentially trigger hypoglycemia or lead to a dangerous electrolyte imbalance. These various side effects highlight the need for caution when using Apium graveolens.
