Celery (Apium graveolens): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a biennial herb native to southern Europe and western Asia, widely cultivated for its aromatic, crisp stalks and leaves, and historically valued in herbal medicine for its purported therapeutic properties.

What is Celery (Apium Graveolens)?

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a flowering plant in the familyApiaceae, native to southern Europe and western Asia, and is widely cultivated for its crisp, aromatic stalks and leaves, which are used in culinary and medicinal applications.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Celery is Apium graveolens. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Apium, and species Apium graveolens. Common names for Celery include "Celery" in Europe and "Apium" in parts of Asia. In North America, it is often referred to as "Sweet Celery" or "Wild Celery." Synonyms for Celery include "Celeriac" (botanical) and "Dill weed" (regional).

Plant Description and Morphology

Celery is a biennial herb that typically grows to a height of 1 to 2 meters, with a sturdy, hollow stem that is ridged and slightly hairy.

Its leaves are alternate, broadly triangular, and have a crisp texture with a distinct celery-like aroma. The flowers are small, greenish-white, and arranged in umbrella-like clusters called umbels, which are borne on slender stalks. The root system consists of a taproot that is thick, white, and fibrous, often used as the primary edible part.

The plant has a pale green color, a sharp and pungent smell, and a crisp, watery texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Celery is native to the temperate regions of southern Europe, the Mediterranean Basin, and parts of western Asia.

It thrives in areas with cool climates and moderate rainfall, typically between 15°C and 20°C. The plant prefers well-drained, fertile soils with a slightly acidic to neutral pH. It is commonly found in coastal areas, river valleys, and open fields.

Its natural range extends from southern Europe to the Middle East and parts of North Africa.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in celery are the leaves, stems, and seeds.

The leaves are commonly used for their cooling properties and to aid digestion. The stems, which are fibrous and crisp, are valued for their diuretic and anti-inflammatory effects. The seeds, often dried and ground, are used to relieve digestive discomfort and reduce inflammation.

Each part contains bioactive compounds that contribute to its medicinal profile.

Phytochemical Composition

Celery contains a variety of active compounds that contribute to its medicinal properties, including volatile oils, alkaloids, and flavonoids.

Secondary metabolites such as apigenin and luteolin are present in significant amounts and exhibit antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. The plant also contains coumarins, which are known for their anticoagulant and antimicrobial activities. Chemical markers like 3-n-butylphthalide are unique to celery and are responsible for its distinct aroma and some of its therapeutic effects.

These compounds work synergistically to support the plant's role in traditional herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Celery (Apium graveolens) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that contribute to its use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and diuretic effects, which support its traditional use in treating urinary tract issues and digestive disorders. Biologically, celery contains compounds such as apigenin and myristicin, which exhibit antioxidant and antithrombotic activities. Pharmacodynamically, these compounds interact with various receptors and enzymes, modulating pathways related to inflammation and oxidative stress.

System-level effects of celery include support for the cardiovascular system and potential protective effects on the liver and kidneys.

Mechanisms of Action

Celery exerts its effects through multiple mechanisms that involve interactions at the cellular, biochemical, and systemic levels.

At the cellular level, it contains compounds like apigenin and luteolin that act as antioxidants and anti-inflammatory agents, modulating intracellular signaling pathways. These compounds inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines and reduce oxidative stress by scavenging free radicals. Biochemically, celery influences pathways such as the NF-κB and MAPK pathways, which are central to inflammation and immune responses.

Its active components also target the gastrointestinal and cardiovascular systems, promoting digestive health and improving blood flow.

Therapeutic Uses

Celery has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its potential therapeutic benefits, particularly in supporting digestive health and reducing inflammation.

It is commonly employed to alleviate symptoms of digestive disorders such as bloating, gas, and indigestion. The plant contains compounds like apigenin and lutein, which may help reduce oxidative stress and inflammation in the body. Celery is also used to manage conditions like arthritis due to its anti-inflammatory properties.

Additionally, it is believed to support kidney function and may help lower blood pressure.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Celery (Apium graveolens) is commonly prepared as fresh juice, dried powder, or tincture, with each form influencing its potency and method of application in herbal medicine.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Celery (Apium graveolens) for herbal use ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried root per day, often prepared as a tea or tincture, with specific formulations and dosages varying based on individual health conditions and traditional practices.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While celery is generally considered safe when consumed in typical food amounts, it may cause side effects such as digestive discomfort, allergic reactions, or interactions with certain medications when used in medicinal doses.

Drug Interactions

Celery may interact with medications that affect the liver or blood pressure, and it is important to consult a healthcare provider before using celery if you are on any prescription drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Celery (Apium graveolens) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a food or in traditional herbal remedies, but it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with kidney disorders due to its potential to increase urine production and affect electrolyte balance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Celery (Apium graveolens) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and consistent moisture, harvested primarily during the cooler months when the stalks are mature and the flavor is most concentrated.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Celery (Apium graveolens) typically involve washing, drying, and grinding the fresh or dried plant material to obtain a fine powder, which is then used for medicinal preparations or further chemical extraction to isolate active compounds such as apiol and limonene.

Storage and Shelf Life

Celery (Apium graveolens) should be stored in a cool, dry place in a sealed container to maintain its potency and prevent moisture damage, with a typical shelf life of up to 6 months when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Celery (Apium graveolens) has explored its potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and cardiovascular benefits, with some studies suggesting it may support digestive health and reduce oxidative stress.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Celery has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its digestive, anti-inflammatory, and circulatory benefits, with historical references dating back to ancient Greek and Roman civilizations.

Regulatory Status

Celery (Apium graveolens) is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) when used as a food, but its use as a herbal medicine is not regulated by the FDA, and there are no standardized regulations governing its safety, efficacy, or quality in medicinal applications.
