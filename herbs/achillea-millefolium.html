Achillea millefolium

What's the taxonomical classification of Achillea millefolium?

Achillea millefolium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. The plant is a member of the family Asteraceae and is specifically categorized under the genus Achillea, with its unique species designation being millefolium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Achillea millefolium has finely dissected, bipinnate to tripinnate leaves that measure several centimeters in length and possess a feathery, fern-like texture. These leaves are arranged alternately along a creeping rhizomatous stem and typically exhibit a grayish-green color due to a dense covering of fine hairs. The plant produces terminal corymbs consisting of numerous small, white or pinkish flower heads held atop slender, upright stalks. Each individual flower head contains several disc florets surrounded by a ring of petal-like ray florets. The overall structure is characterized by a low-growing, spreading habit that allows the foliage to form dense mats across the soil surface.

Due to its adaptability, it has been widely introduced to other continents such as Australia and South America. What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Achillea millefolium contains medicinal compounds located primarily in the aerial parts of the plant, including the leaves, flowers, and stems. These components are harvested during the flowering stage to ensure a high concentration of essential oils and flavonoids. The leaves are frequently utilized in teas and topical applications to support wound healing and digestive health. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse range of bioactive substances including the flavonoids apiogenin and luteolin, the sesquiterpene azulene derivative chamazulene, the coumarin achillin, and the glycoside rutin. These specific chemical constituents work together to provide the herb with its characteristic anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Luteolin and apiogenin are primarily recognized for their ability to modulate various cellular signaling pathways. The presence of chamazulene contributes to the soothing effects often sought in topical applications. Additionally, achillin and rutin play significant roles in supporting the overall medicinal profile of the species.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Achillea millefolium targets cyclooxygenase-1 and cyclooxygenase-2 enzymes, lipoxygenase enzymes, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. The plant contains bioactive compounds like sesquiterpene lactones and flavonoids that inhibit the arachidonic acid cascade, thereby reducing the production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes. These phytochemicals also interact with nuclear factor kappa B to downregulate the expression of genes involved in the inflammatory response. Additionally, certain components may modulate nitric oxide synthase activity to influence vascular tone and tissue healing. Through these diverse protein interactions, the plant provides significant anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antimicrobial properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Achillea millefolium?

Achillea millefolium has diverse therapeutic actions including being a vulnerary, hemostatic, anti-inflammatory, diaphoric, antispasmodic, emmenagogue, bitter, stomachic, and cholagogue. As a vulnerary and hemostatic agent, it effectively aids in wound healing and the management of bleeding. The plant acts as an antispasmodic to soothe muscle contractions and serves as an emmenagogue to stimulate menstrual flow. Its bitter and stomachic properties support healthy digestion, while its cholagogue action promotes bile secretion. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory effects reduce swelling and its diaphoric nature helps regulate body temperature through perspiration.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation or contact dermatitis, allergic reactions, nausea, dizziness, and stomach cramps. Individuals with sensitivities to the Asteraceae family may experience itchy rashes or swelling upon topical contact with the leaves. When ingested, the plant's compounds may lead to digestive distress such as nausea or intense stomach cramps. Some people might also report neurological symptoms like dizziness after consumption. It is important to monitor for these adverse effects when using the herb.
