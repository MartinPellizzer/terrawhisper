Yarrow

Achillea millefolium

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is a member of the Asteraceae family, commonly used in traditional medicine for its purported benefits. The plant parts, including leaves and flowers, have historically been employed for wound healing, digestive issues, and anti-inflammatory purposes. It has been utilized in various cultural and regional practices across Europe and Asia.

Botanical Identification

Yarrow is scientifically known as Achillea millefolium, belonging to the Asteraceae family. Common names include common yarrow and milfoil, with regional variations. It is a perennial herb native to temperate regions of Europe, Asia, and North America. The plant has feathery leaves and flat, composite flower heads, and has been referred to by various synonyms in historical herbal texts.

Active Compounds

Yarrow contains essential oil, flavonoids, and coumarin as its primary active compounds. These components work together to exert anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antispasmodic effects. Flavonoids and coumarin may synergize with the essential oils to enhance the herb's therapeutic properties in herbal medicine.

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for respiratory issues and digestive ailments.

Decoction: Used for digestive problems and as an antispasmodic.

Tincture: Used for pain relief and as an anti-inflammatory.

Safety Profile

Yarrow is generally safe when used in moderate amounts. However, it should be avoided during pregnancy due to potential uterine stimulant effects. Long-term use may cause gastrointestinal irritation, and individuals with bleeding disorders should consult a healthcare provider before use.
