Yarrow (Achillea millefolium): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Yarrow, scientifically known as Achillea millefolium, is a perennial plant native to Europe, Asia, and North America, widely used in herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and digestive properties.

What is Yarrow (Achillea Millefolium)?

Yarrow, or Achillea millefolium, is a perennial flowering plant native to temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, known for its feathery leaves and yellow or white flowers, and traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, astringent, and digestive properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Yarrow, scientifically known as Achillea millefolium, is a flowering plant in the family Asteraceae, characterized by its finely divided leaves and yellow to pinkish flowers.

Common Names and Synonyms

Yarrow, scientifically known as Achillea millefolium, is commonly referred to by several other names including common yarrow, thousand-leaf, and nosebleed plant, and is also known by regional and historical names such as garden yarrow, bloodwort, and woundwort.

Plant Description and Morphology

Yarrow is a perennial herb characterized by its finely divided, fern-like leaves and flat, clustered inflorescences of white to pinkish flowers, typically growing 30 to 150 centimeters tall with a robust, branching stem.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is native to temperate regions of Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, with a natural range extending into North America where it has been introduced and widely naturalized.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and roots of Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) are commonly used medicinally, with the dried herb being the most frequently prepared form for internal and external applications.

Phytochemical Composition

Yarrow contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, alkaloids, volatile oils, and polyacetylenes, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) possesses a range of pharmacological properties, including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antispasmodic, and hemostatic effects, primarily attributed to its active compounds such as flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and essential oils.

Mechanisms of Action

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) exerts its effects through a combination of anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and mild antimicrobial properties, primarily due to its active compounds such as flavonoids, alkaloids, and sesquiterpene lactones, which interact with various physiological systems to reduce inflammation, soothe gastrointestinal discomfort, and promote wound healing.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to address a variety of conditions including digestive issues, respiratory infections, skin wounds, and as a mild sedative to reduce anxiety and promote sleep.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Yarrow is typically prepared as a tea, tincture, or topical salve, with the dried herb or fresh leaves used to make infusions, decoctions, or by soaking in alcohol for tinctures.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) for herbal use ranges from 200 to 400 mg of dried herb per day, divided into two or three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the preparation method and intended therapeutic use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While yarrow is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts for short periods, it may cause mild side effects such as stomach upset, nausea, or skin irritation, and should be avoided by individuals with bleeding disorders, those taking blood-thinning medications, or during pregnancy.

Drug Interactions

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) may interact with medications that affect blood clotting, such as anticoagulants and antiplatelet drugs, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding.

Toxicity and Precautions

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, so it should be used with caution and under the guidance of a qualified healthcare practitioner.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is typically cultivated in well-drained soil in full sun, with harvesting occurring primarily during the early flowering stage, usually between June and August, when the plant's chemical composition is most potent.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) typically involve harvesting the aerial parts of the plant, usually during the flowering stage, followed by drying to reduce moisture content before further preparation.

Storage and Shelf Life

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has demonstrated that Yarrow contains compounds such as achilleine, azulene, and essential oils, which contribute to its traditional uses in treating inflammation, wounds, and digestive issues.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Yarrow has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to reduce fever, promote sweating, and treat wounds, with historical records dating back to ancient Greek and Roman civilizations.

In many countries, Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is considered a herbal medicine and is regulated under various national and international guidelines, with specific rules governing its sale, labeling, and use depending on the region.
