What's the taxonomical classification of Clausena anisum-olens?

Clausena anisum-olens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it resides in the order Sapindales and the family Rutaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Clausena, and its unique species designation, anisum-olens.

What are the pharmacological activities of Clausena anisum-olens?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its PTP1B inhibitory activity, anti-HIV properties, lipid-lowering effects, and α-glucosidase inhibitory activity. These diverse biological actions suggest that the phytochemicals within Clauseuna anisum-olens could play a role in managing metabolic disorders such as diabetes and obesity. Specifically, the inhibition of α-glucosidase and PTP1B provides a mechanism for regulating blood glucose levels and insulin sensitivity. Furthermore, its ability to lower lipid profiles and combat HIV viral processes highlights its broad therapeutic versatility. Continued research into these specific pathways may eventually lead to the development of new medicinal treatments derived from this species.

As discussed in a study, the fruits of Clausena anisum-olens contain 18 carbazole alkaloids, including six new ones named clausenanisine A-F. These 18 isolated compounds were assessed for their in vitro protein tyrosine phosphatase 1B (PTP1B) and α-glucosidase inhibitory activities. Compounds 1-18 exhibited remarkable PTP1B inhibitory activities with IC50 values in the range of 0.58 ± 0.05 to 38.48 ± 0.32 μM. Additionally, compounds 1-18 displayed significant α-glucosidase inhibitory activities with IC50 values ranging from 3.28 ± 0.16 to 192.23 ± 0.78 μM. These results suggest that these alkaloids from Clausena anisum-olens could be significant for developing new inhibitors for the treatment of diabetes mellitus.

Research into clausanisumine and 3-methylcarbazole helps scientists understand how these substances interact with biological systems.

As discussed in a study, the fruits of Clausena anisum-olens contain the unique prenylated bicarbazole alkaloid clausanisumine (1). The plant also yields two biogegetically related known monomer carbazole alkaloids, mukonal (2) and 3-methylcarbazole (3). Among these isolates, compound 1 exhibited remarkable anti-HIV-1 reverse transcriptase effects. This specific anti-HIV activity was demonstrated through an EC50 value of 18.58 nM. These findings regarding the isolates 1-3 could be meaningful for developing new anti-HIV drugs.
