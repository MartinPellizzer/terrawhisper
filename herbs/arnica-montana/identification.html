Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Mountain Arnica is Arnica montana, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It belongs to the genus Arnica in the family Asteraceae. Common names include mountain tobacco and leopard's bane, while regional names vary across Europe. Historically, it was also known as wolf's bane and devil's bread.

Morphological Characteristics

Mountain Arnica (Arnica montana) is a perennial herb with a robust root system and a basal rosette of leaves. It has a sturdy, upright stem that can reach up to 60 cm in height. Its leaves are lance-shaped, hairy, and arranged in a rosette. The plant bears a tall, terminal inflorescence with numerous yellow, disc-like flowers. The flowers are surrounded by leaf-like bracts and have a central cluster of tubular florets. The fruit is a capsule that contains numerous tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Mountain Arnica (Arnica montana) can be distinguished by its tall, unbranched stem with dense yellow-orange flowers, narrow lance-shaped leaves, and a basal rosette of dark green, hairy leaves. It is typically found in alpine meadows and is often confused with other arnica species, but its unique leaf arrangement and flower color are key identifiers.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Mountain Arnica (Arnica montana) is native to Europe and parts of Asia, thriving in temperate climates. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and grows at altitudes between 1,000 and 2,500 meters. It is commonly found in alpine meadows and mountainous regions.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Mountain Arnica blooms from June to August, producing bright yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer, typically by September. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested in early summer when the flowers are fully open and the leaves are mature.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Mountain Arnica (Arnica montana) belongs to the genus Arnica in the family Asteraceae. Its closest relatives include other arnica species such as Arnica chamissonis and Arnica fulva. Within the genus, it shares key morphological and genetic traits. These relationships are important for understanding its ecological and medicinal properties.
