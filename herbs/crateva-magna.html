What is Cape Gooseberry (Crateva Magna)?

Cape Gooseberry, or Crateva magna, is a climbing shrub native to tropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Pacific, known for its edible berries and traditional use in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cape gooseberry is Crateva magna. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Sapindaceae, genus Crateva, and species Crateva magna. It is commonly known as Cape gooseberry in South Africa, and as murrami or kopi in parts of East Africa. The synonyms for this plant include Crateva grandis and Crateva trifoliata, with regional names such as murrami in Kenya and kopi in Tanzania.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cape gooseberry is a small, shrub-like plant that typically grows to a height of 1 to 2 meters, with a woody stem and a dense, branching structure.

Its leaves are ovate to elliptical in shape, with a pointed tip and a slightly serrated margin, and are arranged alternately along the stem. The flowers are small, clustered in umbels, and have five white petals with yellow stamens, producing a mild, sweet scent that attracts pollinators. The root system is shallow and fibrous, allowing the plant to anchor itself in loose, well-drained soils.

The fruit is a small, round, green berry that turns red when ripe, with a smooth, waxy texture and a tart, slightly sweet flavor.

Native Habitat and Distribution
