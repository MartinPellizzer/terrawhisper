Amaranthus viridis

What's the taxonomical classification of Amaranthus viridis?

Amaranthus viridis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Caryophyllales and the family Amaranthaceae, eventually reaching the genus Amaranthus. Finally, it is identified by its specific epithet, making the complete scientific name Amaranthus viridis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Amaranthus viridis has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 100 centimeters and features branching patterns with simple or slightly lobed leaves. The leaves are generally ovate or lanceolate in shape, possessing entire margins and a bright green color. Small, inconspicuous flowers are arranged in dense, terminal panicles that can appear greenish or reddish depending on the variety. These flowering structures often consist of numerous tiny, bisexual flowers packed closely together. The plant typically develops a taproot system that provides stability and nutrient absorption.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to tropical regions of the Americas, specifically spanning from the southern United States through Central America and into much of South America. Due to its resilient nature, it has naturalized in various tropical and subtropical zones across Africa, Asia, and Oceania. It frequently colonizes disturbed habitats, such as roadsides, agricultural fields, and waste areas, throughout these global regions. The species thrives in warm climates where consistent moisture and sunlight are available. Its wide distribution is largely attributed to its ability to adapt to diverse environmental conditions and human-disturbed landscapes.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Amaranthus viridis contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its whole aerial parts, which include the leaves, stems, and seeds. The leaves are frequently utilized in traditional medicine to treat inflammatory conditions and digestive issues due to their nutrient density. Extracts from the stems are sometimes applied topically to soothe skin irritations or minor wounds. The seeds can also be processed for their nutritional and therapeutic properties, offering a source of dietary support. These various components work together to provide a wide range of pharmacological benefits in folk healing practices.

What are the pharmacological activities of Amaranthus viridis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antihyperglycemic, antioxidant, antipyretic, and antiviral properties. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize harmful free radicals that cause cellular damage within the body. The anti-inflammatory and antipyretic effects allow the plant to effectively reduce swelling and lower body temperatures during fever. Additionally, its antihyperglycemic action assists in managing elevated blood sugar levels. Research also suggests that its antiviral potential provides a defense against various pathogenic infections.

As discussed in a study, Amaranthus viridis is utilized in traditional Ayurvedic medicine as antipyretic agents and for the treatment of inflammation, ulcer, diabetic, asthma and hyperlipidemia. The methanolic extract of Amaranthus viridis exhibited DPPH radical scavenging activity with an IC50 of 47.23 ± 0.66 μg/mL and NO radical scavenging activity with an IC50 of 64.33 ± 2.01 μg/mL. Additionally, the extract showed hydrogen peroxide radical scavenging activity at an IC50 of 33.21 ± 3.3 μg/mL and ABTS radical scavenging activity at an IC50 of 47.61 ± 1.31 μg/mL. Its metal chelating activity was measured at an IC50 of 32.1 ± 1.11 μg/mL, while its lipid peroxidation inhibiting activity reached an IC50 of 112 ± 1.21 μg/mL. The study also evaluated the antityrosinase and antigenotoxicity properties of the plant.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains amaranthin, betalains, kaempferol, polyphenols, and vitamin C, which function as potent medicinal compounds. The betalains and amaranthin contribute to the plant's vibrant pigmentation while providing significant antioxidant support. Kaempferol is a specific flavonoid present that helps reduce inflammation throughout the body. Additionally, the high concentration of polyphenols works to protect cells from oxidative stress. Vitamin C further strengthens the immune system and aids in overall metabolic health.

As discussed in a study, the leaves of Amaranthus viridis contain an antiviral protein named amaranthin. This protein has an estimated molecular mass of about 30 kDa determined by SDS-PAGE. Through isoelectric focusing analysis, the pI of the protein was measured as 9.8. The cytotoxicity of amaranthin was assessed using an in vitro translation inhibition assay, which showed an IC50 of 25 pM. Additionally, the protein demonstrated depurination activity against animal rRNA.

This plant is used for treating cardiovascular diseases, asthma, diabetic conditions, gastrointestinal disorders, and inflammation. Its bioactive compounds help regulate blood sugar levels to assist those who are diabetic. The plant also contains properties that soothe the respiratory tract to help manage asthma symptoms. It serves as a natural remedy for reducing inflammation throughout the body. Additionally, its medicinal qualities are utilized to alleviate various gastrointestinal disorders.

As discussed in a study, Amaranthus viridis is among 92 plants examined for their CVD therapy efficacy. This group of plants includes 15 terrestrial plants used to treat cardiovascular diseases. The active phytochemicals found in these plants include flavonoids, polyphenols, plant sterol, plant sulphur compounds, and terpenoids. These specific compounds work through various mechanisms such as preventing low-density lipoprotein oxidation and decreasing cholesterol absorption in the blood. More research is required to understand the specific phytochemicals in plants like Amaranthus viridis responsible for treating CVD.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion by steeping the leaves in hot water to create a soothing tea, or as a decoction by boiling the tougher stems to extract deeper medicinal compounds. Freshly squeezed juice from the leaves is often used for its nutritional properties or applied topically to soothe skin irritations. A thick poultice made from crushed plant material can be applied directly to wounds or inflammations to promote healing. For long-term storage and concentrated potency, the herb is frequently processed into a tincture using alcohol as a solvent. These various methods allow for both internal consumption and external therapeutic applications.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause kidney stones due to its high oxalate content, as well as nausea, stomach upset, and diarrhea. Consuming excessive amounts may lead to digestive distress and abdominal discomfort. The presence of oxalates is a particular concern for individuals prone to renal issues. These gastrointestinal reactions often occur shortly after ingestion. Monitoring intake is essential to avoid these adverse physiological effects.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Kidney stones (due to oxalates)
  • Nausea
  • Stomach upset
  • Diarrhea
