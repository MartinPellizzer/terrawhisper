Botanical Identification of Tamarind (Tamarindus indica)

To identify Tamarind (Tamarindus indica) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Tamarind (Tamarindus indica) belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Tamarindus, and species Tamarindus indica. This classification places it within the legume family, highlighting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Tamarind is Tamarindus indica, first described by the botanist Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century. This name is widely accepted in botanical literature. Synonyms include Tamarindus tamari and Tamarindus lophantha, reflecting historical taxonomic revisions. Common names such as "tamarind" and "Java tamarind" are used globally, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Tamarind is a deciduous tree with a straight, often buttressed trunk. Its roots are deep and taproot-like, providing stability. The stem is smooth and flaky, with a brownish-gray bark. Leaves are alternate, odd-pinnate, and compound, with 7-13 pairs of lance-shaped leaflets. Inflorescences are terminal panicles, bearing small, yellowish-green flowers. The fruit is a pod-like drupe, turning from green to reddish-brown, containing 3-5 seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Tamarind can be distinguished by its long, narrow, dark green leaves with 3-5 pairs of leaflets, and its distinctive yellowish-brown pods containing sour, fleshy pulp and flat, kidney-shaped seeds. It is often confused with species like Cassia or Acacia, but its unique pod shape and leaf structure help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Tamarind is native to tropical regions of Africa, the Middle East, and southern Asia. It thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soils. It is typically found at altitudes between 200 and 800 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Tamarind trees bloom in late spring to early summer, producing yellowish-green flowers. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. Harvesting of mature pods typically happens in late autumn to early winter, when the fruit turns a deep orange-brown, indicating readiness for collection.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Tamarind (Tamarindus indica) is closely related to species in the genus Tamarindus, which is part of the Fabaceae family. It shares morphological and genetic traits with other legumes, such as pod structure and nitrogen-fixing capabilities. Its phylogenetic placement reflects its evolutionary ties to other tropical legumes.
