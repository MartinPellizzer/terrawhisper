Croton tiglium

What's the taxonomical classification of Croton tiglium?

Croton tiglium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae, falling under the genus Croton, and is specifically identified by its species name, tiglium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Croton tiglium has woody stems that reach heights of up to 15 meters and large, elliptical leaves measuring between 15 and 30 centimeters in length. The foliage is typically dark green with prominent, pale venation that creates a distinct pattern on the leaf surface. These leaves are arranged alternately along the branches and possess entire margins. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are organized into axillary racemes. Its fruit is a small, three-lobed capsule that contains seeds typically enclosed in a fleshy aril.

Its presence is often noted in secondary forests and disturbed habitats within these tropical zones. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including 12-O-acetylphorbol-13-decanoate, crotocaudiin, flavonoids, phorbol esters, and steroids. These bioactive constituents contribute to the complex chemical profile that defines the plant's pharmacological potential. The phorbol esters, specifically 12-O-acetylphorbol-13-decanoate and crotocaudiin, are known for their potent biological activities. Additionally, the presence of flavonoids and steroids suggests various antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Together, these diverse molecules interact with biological systems in highly specific ways.

As discussed in a study, eight phorbol diesters, including 5 new ones, were isolated from the seeds of Croton tiglium collected in Egypt. These medicinal compounds include 12-O-acetylphorbol-13-decanoate (6) and 12-O-decanoylphorbol-13-(2-methylbutyrate) (4). The compound 6 inhibited the HIV-1-induced cytopathic effect on MT-4 cells with an IC100 value of 7.6 ng/ml and a CC50 value of 62.5 micrograms/ml. Additionally, the compound 4 demonstrated inhibition with an IC100 value of 7.81 micrograms/ml and a CC50 value of 31.3 micrograms/ml. These effects occurred without the activation of protein kinase C.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain if ingested. These digestive symptoms are often the most immediate reaction to consuming the toxic components within its seeds or sap. Some individuals might also experience dizziness as a systemic response to the toxins. Therefore, extreme caution must be taken when handling any part of this species.
