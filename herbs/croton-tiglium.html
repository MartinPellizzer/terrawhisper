Croton tiglium

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Croton tiglium has woody stems that reach heights of up to 15 meters and large, elliptical leaves measuring between 15 and 30 centimeters in length. The foliage is typically dark green with prominent, pale venation that creates a distinct pattern on the leaf surface. These leaves are arranged alternately along the branches and possess entire margins. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are organized into axillary racemes. Its fruit is a small, three-lobed capsule that contains seeds typically enclosed in a fleshy aril.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Africa, ranging from West Africa through Central Africa to East Africa and extending into parts of Madagascar. It primarily flourishes in lowland rainforests and coastal forest areas where moisture is abundant. Its presence is often noted in secondary forests and disturbed habitats within these tropical zones. The species follows a distribution pattern closely aligned with the humid equatorial climate belts of the continent. Such environmental conditions allow it to maintain stable populations across its broad longitudinal range.

What are the herbal preparations of Croton tiglium?

Croton tiglium is prepared primarily as a crude latex or an oil extracted from the seeds for topical application to treat skin conditions. This viscous substance is often applied directly to warts, corns, or fungal infections to induce localized irritation and healing. In some traditional practices, the seeds are crushed to create a paste that serves as a potent emetic or purgative when administered internally. However, extreme caution is required because the chemical components are highly caustic and can cause severe tissue damage if used incorrectly. The preparation methods must be carefully controlled to manage the plant's intense biological activity.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Croton tiglium can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and intense inflammation if the plant's milky sap comes into direct contact with human tissue. The chemical compounds within the latex act as potent irritants that may trigger allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. If accidental ingestion occurs, the toxins can lead to violent vomiting, abdominal pain, and significant gastrointestinal distress. Direct contact with the eyes is particularly dangerous and can result in temporary or permanent vision impairment due to corneal damage. Therefore, extreme caution must be taken when handling any part of this plant to avoid these painful physiological reactions.
