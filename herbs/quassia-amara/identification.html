Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Quassia is Quassia amara, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the family Simaroubaceae and is also known by several synonyms, including Quassia sessilis and Quassia macrophylla. Common names such as quassia wood and bitter wood are widely used, while regional and historical names reflect its traditional use in various cultures.

Morphological Characteristics

Quassia amara is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree with a straight trunk and dense crown. Its root system is shallow and fibrous, supporting its growth in tropical climates. The stem is smooth and pale green, with a slight reddish tinge. Leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptic, with a waxy texture and a prominent midrib. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small, greenish-yellow flowers. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Quassia amara can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow bark, opposite leaves with entire margins, and clusters of small yellow flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing several seeds. It is often confused with other genus members, but its unique leaf arrangement and bark color help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Quassia amara is native to tropical regions of South America, particularly Brazil, Guyana, and Suriname. It thrives in warm, humid climates with high rainfall. The plant prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It is typically found at low to mid-elevation areas, usually between 100 and 600 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Quassia amara blooms from April to June, producing small, yellowish-green flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to September. For identification purposes, the best time to observe key features is during the flowering period, typically between April and June.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Quassia amara belongs to the Simaroubaceae family, closely related to species like Melia azedarach and Picrasma excelsa. It shares morphological and chemical traits with these relatives, including the presence of quassinoids. Its genus, Quassia, is distinct but shows close phylogenetic ties to other tropical hardwoods.
