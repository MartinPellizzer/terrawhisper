Nephelium chryseum

What's the taxonomical classification of Nephelium chryseum?

Nephelium chryseum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Sapindales and is a member of the family Sapindaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Nephelium, with its specific designation being the species chrysemu.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, inflammatory conditions, respiratory issues, and gastrointestinal disturbances. The leaves and bark often contain bioactive compounds that possess antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. These medicinal qualities help reduce swelling and combat pathogens that cause localized skin irritations. In some traditional practices, preparations from the plant are administered to alleviate coughing or digestive distress. What are the herbal preparations of Nephelium chryseum?

Nephelium chryseum is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or topical poultices depending on whether the leaves, bark, or fruit pulp is being utilized. The dried leaves are often simmered in water to create a potent medicinal tea intended for internal consumption. For external skin ailments, the fleshy fruit pulp can be mashed into a thick paste and applied directly to the affected area. Some traditional practitioners also extract essential oils from the seeds through steam distillation for concentrated therapeutic use. These various methods allow for the targeted delivery of the plant's bioactive compounds to different parts of the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Nephelium chryseum can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if the plant parts are ingested or touched directly. Sensitive individuals may experience redness, itching, or swelling upon contact with the sap or leaves. Ingestion of certain components might lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping. While specific toxicity levels are not extensively documented in all literature, caution is advised when handling the specimen. Always use gloves to prevent unexpected dermatological reactions.
