Biophytum sensitivum

What's the taxonomical classification of Biophytum sensitivum?

Biophytum sensitivum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Oxalidales. This plant is further categorized into the family Oxalidaceae, within the genus Biophytum, and is specifically identified by the species name sensitivum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Biophytum sensitivum has a creeping rhizomatous stem, large heart-shaped leaves that exhibit rapid nyctinastic movement, and solitary flowers with five petals. The leaves are characterized by their sensitive nature, folding inward quickly when touched or during the night to protect themselves. These leaves emerge from a thick, fleshy rootstock that allows the plant to spread across the forest floor. The flowers are typically pale yellow or white and appear on long stalks rising above the foliage. Small, fleshy fruits develop from the flowers after successful pollination.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Dalton's lymphoma ascites (DLA), Ehrlich ascites carcinoma (EAC), Ehrlich ascites tumor, solid tumor, and various other types of tumors. Researchers have investigated its chemical compounds for their potential to inhibit the growth of these specific malignant cells. Studies often focus on its ability to reduce tumor volume and suppress the progression of both liquid and solid cancerous growths. By targeting these specific conditions, the plant serves as a significant subject in pharmacological studies regarding anti-tumor activity.

According to a study published by "Asian Pacific journal of cancer prevention : APJCP", Biophytum sensitivum extract showed 100% toxicity at a concentration of 0.5 mg/ml against Dalton's lymphoma ascites (DLA) and Ehrlich ascites carcinoma (EAC) cells. The extract was also cytotoxic to L929 cells at a concentration of 0.1 mg/ml. Administration of 500 microg/dose/animal inhibited solid tumor development in mice with DLA cells and increased the lifespan of mice with EAC tumors by 93.3%. In ascites tumor bearing animals, the extract significantly (p<0.001) reduced tumor cell glutathione (GSH), serum gamma glutamyl transpeptidase (GGT), and nitric oxide (NO) levels. Furthermore, the total WBC count increased to 14,087 cells/mm(3) on the 12th day in BALB/c mice, while the number of plaque forming cells also enhanced significantly (p<0.001).
