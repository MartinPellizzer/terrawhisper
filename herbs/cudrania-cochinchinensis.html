Cudrania cochinchinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Cudrania cochinchinensis?

Cudrania cochinchinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant further follows the evolutionary path of the order Rosales and is a member of the family Moraeaae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Cudrania, and its unique species designation, cochinchinenis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cudrania cochinchinensis has a woody climbing shrub morphology characterized by slender, twining stems and trifoliate leaves with ovate to elliptical leaflets. The leaves feature serrated margins and a distinctively textured surface that aids in moisture retention. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in clusters along the branches, eventually developing into fleshy, globose drupes. These fruits transition from green to a deep reddish or purplish hue when fully mature. The root system is robust, providing the necessary stability for its climbing habit within forest ecosystems.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Southeast Asia, specifically occurring within the tropical regions of Vietnam, Laos, Thailand, and parts of southern China. It typically thrives in moist, lowland areas and is often found growing in secondary forests or along riverbanks. The species prefers warm, humid climates that characterize the subtropical and tropical zones of its range. Within these habitats, it can be found at various elevations depending on the specific local terrain. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds cocchinol A, cocchinol B, kaempferol, dihydrokaempferol, and isoalvaxanthone. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile observed in Cudrania cochinchiensis. The presence of flavonoids like kaempferol and dihydrokaempferol often relates to antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Additionally, the unique cocchinois and xanthone derivatives play significant roles in the plant's biological functions. Researchers study these specific molecules to better understand their potential therapeutic applications in modern medicine.

According to a study published by "Zhong yao cai = Zhongyaocai = Journal of Chinese medicinal materials", thirteen compounds were isolated from the 70% ethanol extract of the root of Cudrania cochinchinensis. These identified compounds include beta-sitosterol (1), butyrospermol (2), butyrospermol acetate (3), (+) syringarenol (4), 1, 3, 6-trihydroxy-7-methoxy xanthone (5), 1, 3, 6, 7-tetrahydroxy-8-prenylxanthone (6), kaempferol (7), dihydrokaempferol (8), umbelliferone (9), 4-hydroxybenzyl ethyl ether (10), 2,4-dihydroxybenzaldehyde (11), 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde (12) and vanillin (13). The research notes that compounds 2 - 6 and 10 - 13 are isolated from Cudrania cochinchinensis for the first time. The isolation and purification processes utilized silica gel column, sephadex LH-20 chromatography and recrystallization.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating human oral squamous carcinoma, inflammation, and various skin infections. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds possess significant anticancer properties that may inhibit the growth of malignant cells in the mouth. The plant also exhibits potent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects when applied topically or ingested in controlled amounts. These medicinal qualities make it a subject of interest for managing diverse dermatological and oral health issues. Such applications stem from the high concentration of polyphenols and flavonoids found within its extracts.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", Cudrania cochinchinensis roots contain 4 new benzophenones called cudraphenone A-D (1-4) and 3 new xanthones called cudraxanthones P-R (5-7) alongside 19 known phenolic compounds. Some of these isolated compounds exhibited weak cytotoxicity against human oral squamous carcinoma cells (HSC-2) and normal human gingival fibroblasts (HGF). Benzophenones 1-4 demonstrated more potent cytotoxic activities against HSC-2 cells than against HGF cells. In contrast, xanthones bearing isoprenoid groups showed much lower tumor specificity compared to the benzophenones, with the exceptions of geronthxanthone H and isoalvaxanthone.
