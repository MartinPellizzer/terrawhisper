Solanum torvum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum torvum?

Solanum torvum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Solanales. This plant is a member of the family Solanaceae, falling into the genus Solanum, and is specifically identified by its species name, torvum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum torvum has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by erect, branching stems that are densely covered in fine, stellate hairs. The leaves are large, alternate, and broadly ovate with irregularly lobed or wavy margins. Small, actinomorphic flowers appear in clusters, featuring pale violet or white petals and prominent yellow anthers. The fruit consists of small, globose berries that transition from green to a ripe yellow or orange color. These botanical features are typical of the Solanaceae family and serve to distinguish it from related species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, including countries such as India, Pakistan, and Thailand. It thrives primarily in disturbed areas, open fields, and along roadsides where sunlight is abundant. The species is often found growing in low-lying coastal plains as well as inland agricultural landscapes. While its primary range is centered in Asia, it has occasionally been introduced to other warm climates due to human activity. Its ability to adapt to various soil types allows it to spread across diverse ecological zones within these regions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Solanum torvum?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antidiabetic, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anticancer pharmacological activities that make it a significant subject for medicinal research. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize harmful free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also exhibits potent antimicrobial effects that can inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, its antidiabetic potential assists in regulating blood glucose levels, while its anti-inflammatory actions help mitigate swelling and pain. Research into its anticancer capabilities suggests that specific compounds within the plant may disrupt the proliferation of malignant cells.

As discussed in a study, Solanum torvum, also known as pea eggplant, is one of the 8 plant extracts that inhibited NO and TNF-alpha production in a dose-dependent manner without exerting cytotoxicity. The study utilized plant extracts at concentrations ranging from 1-100 microg/mL to examine these effects. Within the group of effective extracts, pea eggplant followed Limnophila aromatica, dill, kaffer lime, chili, Teaw, mint, and sweet basil. The 8 extracts tested showed a range of 50% inhibitory concentration (IC50) between 11.4-74.6 microg/mL. These results suggest that certain spices and herbs in the Thai diet possess anti-inflammatory activity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds oleanolic acid, ursolic acid, flavonoids, chlorogenic acid, and kaempferol. These bioactive substances work together to provide various health benefits such as antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of triterpenoids like oleanolic and ursolic acid is particularly noted for supporting cellular health. Additionally, the flavonoids and kaempferol contribute to the plant's ability to combat oxidative stress in the body. Chlorogenic acid further enhances these effects by assisting in metabolic regulation.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Solanum torvum swartz were extracted with 95% alcohol to isolate its chemical constituents. Through chromatographic silica gel and Sephadex LH-20, six triterpene compounds were identified. These include 3beta-acetyloleanolic acid (1), 3-O-acetyl-11alpha, 12alpha-epoxy-oleanan-28, 13beta-olide (2), oleanolic acid (3), ursolic acid (4), 2alpha-hydroxy-oleanolic acid (5), and 2alpha, 3beta-dihydroxyursolic acid (6). The structures of these constituents were determined by NMR and MS spectral analysis. All 6 of these compounds were obtained from this plant for the first time.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress and neurological issues due to the presence of toxic solanine. Consuming even small amounts may lead to intense nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Individuals may also experience significant abdominal pain during the onset of poisoning. The toxicity from solanine can further impact the nervous system, resulting in dizziness. Monitoring for these symptoms is essential if accidental ingestion occurs.
