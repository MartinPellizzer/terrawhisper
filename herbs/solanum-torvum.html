Solanum torvum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum torvum?

Solanum torvum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Solanales. This plant is a member of the family Solanaceae, falling into the genus Solanum, and is specifically identified by its species name, torvum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum torvum has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by erect, branching stems that are densely covered in fine, stellate hairs. The leaves are large, alternate, and broadly ovate with irregularly lobed or wavy margins. Small, actinomorphic flowers appear in clusters, featuring pale violet or white petals and prominent yellow anthers. The fruit consists of small, globose berries that transition from green to a ripe yellow or orange color. These botanical features are typical of the Solanaceae family and serve to distinguish it from related species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, including countries such as India, Pakistan, and Thailand. It thrives primarily in disturbed areas, open fields, and along roadsides where sunlight is abundant. The species is often found growing in low-lying coastal plains as well as inland agricultural landscapes. While its primary range is centered in Asia, it has occasionally been introduced to other warm climates due to human activity. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds such as oleanolic acid, ursolic acid, kaempferol, flavonoids, and chlorogenic acid. These specific substances contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile and therapeutic potential. The triterpenoids oleanolic acid and ursolic acid are often recognized for their anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Additionally, the presence of flavonoids and kaempferol provides significant bioactive benefits to the body. Chlorogenic acid further enhances these effects by offering protective qualities against oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Solanum torvum swartz were extracted using 95% alcohol to investigate its chemical constituents. Through chromatographic silica gel and Sephadex LH-20, researchers isolated and purified six triterpene compounds. These identified compounds include 3beta-acetyloleanolic acid (1), 3-O-acetyl-11alpha, 12alpha-epoxy-oleanan-28, 13beta-olide (2), oleanolic acid (3), ursolic acid (4), 2alpha-hydroxy-oleanolic acid (5), and 2alpha, 3beta-dihydroxyursolic acid (6). The structures for these 6 substances were determined using NMR and MS spectral analysis. All of these specific compounds were obtained from this plant for the first time.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress and neurological issues due to the presence of toxic solanine. Consuming even small amounts may lead to intense nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Individuals may also experience significant abdominal pain during the onset of poisoning. The toxicity from solanine can further impact the nervous system, resulting in dizziness. Monitoring for these symptoms is essential if accidental ingestion occurs.

    The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

    • Nausea
    • Vomiting
    • Diarrhea
    • Abdominal pain
    • Toxicity from solanine
    • Dizziness
