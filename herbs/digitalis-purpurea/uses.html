Purple Foxglove (Digitalis purpurea) Uses

Purple Foxglove is used to treat heart failure, arrhythmia, and hypertension. It helps manage edema and congestive heart failure by strengthening heart muscle function. It may also support recovery from myocardial infarction and treat cardiomyopathy. Venous insufficiency and other circulatory disorders are also addressed with its cardiac-stimulating properties.

Purple Foxglove (Digitalis purpurea) Monograph

1. Heart failure

Digitalis purpurea has been used historically to treat heart failure due to its ability to strengthen heart contractions and regulate heart rhythm. The active compound digoxin helps improve cardiac output by increasing the force of myocardial contractions and slowing the heart rate. It is typically used under medical supervision due to its narrow therapeutic range and potential toxicity.

2. Arrhythmia

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that strengthen heart contractions and regulate heart rhythm. It has been historically used to treat arrhythmias by slowing an overactive heart rate. However, it is toxic and requires precise dosing under professional supervision. Modern medicine uses purified derivatives for specific cardiac conditions.

3. Hypertension

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that strengthen heart contractions and slow heart rate, making it historically used for hypertension. However, it is highly toxic and requires strict medical supervision. Modern use is limited due to safety concerns, though its principles influence contemporary cardiovascular treatments.

4. Edema

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that strengthen heart contractions, making it historically used for edema caused by heart failure. It improves circulation and reduces fluid retention by enhancing cardiac output. However, it is highly toxic and requires strict medical supervision. Its use is limited today due to safer alternatives and the risk of poisoning.

5. Congestive heart failure

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that strengthen heart muscle contractions and regulate heart rate. It is used in treating congestive heart failure by improving cardiac output and reducing fluid retention. However, it must be used with caution due to its potent effects and potential toxicity.

6. Myocardial infarction

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that increase heart contractions and lower heart rate, making it historically used for heart failure and arrhythmias. However, it is not typically used for myocardial infarction due to its toxicity and the availability of more effective modern treatments.

7. Cardiomyopathy

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that strengthen heart contractions and regulate heart rate. It has been historically used to treat congestive heart failure and cardiomyopathy by improving cardiac output. However, it requires careful dosing due to its potent and toxic nature. Modern usage is limited to prescription medications derived from its compounds.

8. Venous insufficiency

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that improve venous return by strengthening heart muscle contractions. It is used in herbal medicine to support circulation and alleviate symptoms of venous insufficiency, such as leg swelling and heaviness. However, it should only be used under professional guidance due to its potency and potential toxicity.

9. Circulatory disorders

Purple Foxglove is used to support circulatory health by strengthening heart contractions and improving blood flow. Its active compound, digoxin, helps regulate heart rhythm and manage conditions like congestive heart failure. It is typically used under professional guidance due to its potency and potential side effects.

10. Cardiac arrhythmias

Purple Foxglove contains cardiac glycosides that regulate heart rhythm by strengthening cardiac contractions and slowing heart rate. It is used to treat arrhythmias, particularly atrial fibrillation, by stabilizing electrical impulses in the heart. However, it requires careful dosing due to its potent and potentially toxic effects.
