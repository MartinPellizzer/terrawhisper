Aquilegia ecalcarata

What's the taxonomical classification of Aquilegia ecalcarata?

Aquilegia ecalcara belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, following the evolutionary lineage of the order Ranunculales. Within this order, the plant is a member of the family Ranunculaceae. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Aquilegia, with its unique biological identity defined by the species name ecalcara.

What are the therapeutic actions of Aquilegia ecalcarata?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin diseases, wounds, and respiratory infections. It possesses significant antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties that assist in healing external injuries. Some traditional practices utilize its extracts to soothe skin irritations and manage fungal infections. Additionally, it is sometimes employed in folk medicine to alleviate symptoms of coughs and other bronchial issues. Its medicinal versatility makes it a subject of interest in studying natural remedies for diverse pathologies.

What are the herbal preparations of Aquilegia ecalcarata?

Aquilegia ecalcarata is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture to extract its medicinal constituents. These liquid forms allow the active compounds to be more easily absorbed by the body during therapeutic use. In traditional practices, the dried roots or leaves are often boiled in water to create a potent decoction. Tinctures made with alcohol serve as a concentrated method for long-term storage and standardized dosing. Some practitioners may also utilize the plant in the form of a topical poultice for localized applications.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Aquilegia ecalcarata can cause severe skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap may lead to localized redness, itching, or blistering on sensitive areas of the skin. If someone accidentally consumes the plant, they might experience nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Some individuals may also develop systemic allergic symptoms such as swelling or respiratory discomfort. Because the specific toxicity profile is not fully documented, cautious handling is always recommended to avoid these adverse effects.
