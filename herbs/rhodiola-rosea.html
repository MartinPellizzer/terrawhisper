What to know about Rhodiola rosea (golden root) before using it medicinally

Rhodiola rosea, commonly known as golden root, is a potent adaptogenic herb that has been used for centuries to boost mental and physical resilience, improve mood, and enhance cognitive function.

As a hardy, low-maintenance plant, it is often cultivated for its fragrant, yellow flowers and ability to thrive in high-altitude, arid environments, making it a popular choice for gardeners and horticulturists. From a botanical standpoint, Rhodiola rosea is a member of the Crassulaceae family, characterized by its distinctive, fleshy leaves and stems that contain high levels of salidroside and rosavin, the herb's active medicinal compounds.

The ancient Greeks and Chinese have long revered Rhodiola rosea for its medicinal properties, with historical references dating back to the 2nd century AD, where it was used to treat fatigue, depression, and even impotence.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Rhodiola rosea.

What are the medicinal properties of Rhodiola rosea?

Rhodiola rosea helps with reducing stress and anxiety, improving mental performance, and boosting mood. It is also used to alleviate fatigue, depression, and anxiety disorders. Clinical studies have demonstrated its efficacy in reducing symptoms of fatigue and improving mental performance.

The active constituents of Rhodiola rosea responsible for its medicinal properties include rosavin, salidroside, and flavonoids. These compounds are responsible for its adaptogenic, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory effects. Rosavin is the primary compound responsible for its adaptogenic effects, while salidroside is responsible for its antioxidant properties.

The root of the Rhodiola rosea plant is the most commonly used part for medicinal purposes. The root is harvested in the summer months when it is mature and contains high levels of the active constituents. The root is then dried and processed into various forms, including capsules, tablets, and teas.

When used improperly, Rhodiola rosea may cause gastrointestinal upset, insomnia, and headaches. High doses of the plant may also cause increased heart rate and blood pressure. Additionally, the plant may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners, and exacerbate underlying medical conditions.

When using Rhodiola rosea medicinally, it is essential to follow proper dosing and precautions. A recommended dose is 100-200 mg per day, standardized to contain 3-4% rosavin and 1-2% salidroside. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult with a healthcare professional before using the plant, as well as individuals taking medications or with underlying medical conditions.

What are the horticulural aspects of Rhodiola rosea?

Rhodiola rosea grow in areas with well-draining soil and full sun to partial shade. It thrives in temperatures between 20-40°C (68-104°F) and requires a pH range of 4.5-7.0 to grow optimally.

To plant Rhodiola rosea, select a location with good air circulation and soil with a mix of sand and peat moss. Plant seeds 1-2 mm deep and 1-2 cm apart in spring or fall, after the last frost. Water regularly until establishment.

Rhodiola rosea is typically harvested when in full bloom, around mid-summer. Pinch off flower buds when young to promote leaf growth and encourage root development. Dig up roots and separate them by hand, usually every 3-4 years, as the plant's rhizome can become woody.

Pests commonly affecting Rhodiola rosea include aphids, spider mites, and root weevils. Diseases include root rot, leaf spot, and downy mildew, often caused by overwatering or poor air circulation. Regular pruning and good garden hygiene can help prevent infestations and infections.
