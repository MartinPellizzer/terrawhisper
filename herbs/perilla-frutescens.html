Scientific and Botanical Profile

Shiso, with botanical name Perilla frutescens, is an aromatic herb belonging to the Lamiaceae family, commonly known as the mint family.

It is also referred to as Chinese Basil, Chinese Mint, Japanese Perilla, and Perilla, and is native to East Asia, including China, Japan, and Korea. Morphologically, it is an upright annual herb with square stems, opposite leaves that are usually ovate or lanceolate, and small purple or white flowers arranged in whorls. The plant is characterized by its pungent, slightly licorice-like scent, which comes from volatile oils such as perillaldehyde and linalool.

Shiso is widely used in culinary and medicinal applications across its native regions, contributing to both local cuisines and traditional herbal medicine.

History and Cultural Relevance

Shiso was used in ancient China and Japan as both a culinary herb and a medicinal plant, with its leaves and seeds valued for their aromatic and therapeutic properties.

In traditional Chinese medicine, shiso was employed to aid digestion, relieve respiratory ailments, and reduce inflammation, while in Japanese culture, it was incorporated into herbal remedies for colds, asthma, and skin conditions. The plant holds deep cultural significance in Japan, where it is used in Shinto rituals and festivals, symbolizing purity and renewal, and is often offered to deities during ceremonies. In modern times, shiso continues to be used in traditional remedies, such as shiso tea for respiratory support and shiso oil for muscle relief, reflecting its enduring relevance in holistic health practices.

Its rich history and cultural integration make shiso not only a versatile herb but also a symbol of tradition and natural healing.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Shiso contains a diverse array of bioactive compounds, including alkaloids, flavonoids, essential oils, and terpenes, which contribute to its distinct flavor and medicinal properties.

It is rich in vitamins such as vitamin C, vitamin A, and several B vitamins, along with minerals like potassium, magnesium, and iron, making it a nutrient-dense leafy green. The plant also contains significant amounts of antioxidants, including quercetin and kaempferol, which help combat oxidative stress and inflammation in the body. These compounds work synergistically to support immune function, reduce inflammation, and promote digestive health by interacting with various physiological pathways.

Additionally, the essential oils in shiso, such as camphor and limonene, may have antimicrobial and antispasmodic effects, enhancing its therapeutic potential.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Perilla frutescens has been widely used in traditional medicine for its diverse medicinal properties and health benefits, particularly in supporting the respiratory, digestive, and circulatory systems.

It is known to alleviate symptoms of asthma and bronchitis by reducing inflammation and improving lung function, while also aiding digestion by promoting the secretion of digestive enzymes and reducing bloating. Compared to similar herbs like ginger or turmeric, perilla frutescens offers a unique combination of anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antispasmodic effects, making it more effective in treating respiratory and digestive disorders. Its high concentration of polyphenols and essential oils contributes to its potency, surpassing many common herbs in terms of therapeutic strength.

Additionally, perilla frutescens supports immune function and may help in managing conditions like diabetes and hypertension, further distinguishing it as a versatile and powerful medicinal plant.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Perilla frutescens has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its diverse therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms such as fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. Topical applications include using the crushed leaves or essential oil for skin conditions, while the powder form can be added to food or supplements. For adults, a typical dosage is 1-3 grams of dried herb per day, divided into two or three doses, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

It is recommended to use perilla frutescens for short durations, typically up to two weeks, to avoid potential side effects and to monitor for any adverse reactions.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Perilla frutescens can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential health benefits, but it is important to consider its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, perilla frutescens may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or skin irritation in some individuals. It may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and diabetes treatments, so caution is advised when combining it with other herbs or drugs. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid perilla frutescens due to insufficient research on its effects during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to use perilla frutescens in moderation, follow proper dosage guidelines, and consult a qualified healthcare professional before incorporating it into one's regimen.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Perilla frutescens grows best in well-drained, fertile soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, thriving in full sun to partial shade and requiring regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without waterlogging.

It benefits from regular weeding and mulching to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds, while periodic pruning can encourage bushier growth and improve air circulation. The best time to harvest perilla frutescens is when the leaves are fully developed but before flowering, typically in late spring to early summer, using clean scissors to cut the leaves and stems without damaging the plant. To preserve its potency, the harvested plant material should be thoroughly dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area, then stored in airtight containers away from light and moisture to maintain its medicinal properties.

Proper storage ensures that the active compounds remain intact, making the plant effective for its traditional uses in herbal medicine.
