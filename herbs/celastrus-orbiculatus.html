Celastrus orbiculatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Celastrus orbiculatus?

Celastrus orbiculatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant's taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Celastrales and the family Celastraceae, eventually reaching its specific designation within the genus Celastrus. Finally, it is identified at the species level as orbiculatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Celastrus orbiculatus has opposite, simple, elliptical to ovate leaves with entire margins and a woody, upright shrubby stem. The foliage typically features a smooth texture and a dark green color on the upper surface. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils in clusters during the growing season. These blooms eventually give way to distinctive, rounded capsules that split open to reveal bright red or orange arils. The plant's overall structure is characterized by its compact, spreading habit and branching woody architecture.

What are the pharmacological activities of Celastrus orbiculatus?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidative, anti-tumor, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to reduce pain sensations and suppress inflammatory responses in biological systems. The presence of bioactive compounds also allows it to neutralize free radicals through its potent anti-oxidative properties. Furthermore, research suggests the plant can inhibit the growth of certain cancer cells and combat various infectious microorganisms. These diverse medicinal qualities make it a significant subject for drug development and natural therapy studies.

As discussed in a study, Celastrus orbiculatus Thunb. is used as an anti-inflammatory, antitumor, and analgesic in various diseases. The extract, known as COE, inhibits the epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) and metastasis of gastric cancer cells both in vitro and in vivo. Treatment with COE significantly reduces the expression of Matrix metalloproteinases MMP-2 and MMP-9. This extract also accelerates the degradation of the key regulatory protein Cofilin-1 (CFL1) and changes the distribution of skeletal proteins. Furthermore, the number of peritoneal metastases of gastric cancer cells is significantly reduced in animals following COE treatment.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (-) -epiafzelechin, (-) -epicatechin, (-) -epicatechin-3-benzoate, (-) -epicatechin-5-O-beta-D-glucosyl-3-benzoate, and celastrol. These specific flavan-3-ols and flavanones contribute to the complex chemical profile of the species. The presence of these polyphenolic substances is often studied for their potential biological activities. Researchers examine how these unique molecules interact with various cellular pathways. Understanding these constituents helps clarify the plant's overall therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Celastrus orbiculatus contain the compound (-)-epiafzelechin, which is a member of flavan-3-ols. This compound serves as a cyclooxygenase-1 inhibitor and anti-inflammatory agent. It demonstrated a dose-dependent inhibition on COX activity with an IC50 value of 15 microM. This inhibitory potency was about 3-fold weaker than the positive control, indomethacin. Additionally, significant anti-inflammatory activity was observed when 100 mg/kg of the compound was orally administered 1 h before carrageenin treatment.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating gastric cancer, liver cancer, inflammation, tumor growth, and various forms of physical pain. Researchers study its bioactive compounds to determine how they might inhibit the progression of a tumor within the body. Its medicinal properties are often applied to reduce systemic inflammation in different organs. Furthermore, it is investigated for its potential to combat malignant cells in the digestive tract and the liver. The plant also serves as a traditional remedy for managing persistent pain.

As discussed in a study, Celastrus orbiculatus Thunb. extract (COE) inhibits the epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) and metastasis of gastric cancer cells. COE treatment results in movement distances and average movement speeds that are significantly lower than those of the control group. This extract significantly reduces the expression of Matrix metalloproteinases, specifically MMP-2 and MMP-9, as well as other proteins. Furthermore, COE can significantly accelerate the degradation of the key regulatory protein Cofilin-1 (CFL1). In vivo, COE can reverse the levels of EMT-related proteins while reducing the expression levels of CFL1 protein. These effects are achieved by reducing the stability of CFL1 and inhibiting the assembly of actin in gastric cancer cells.
