Rubus parvifolius

What's the taxonomical classification of Rubus parvifolius?

Rubus parvifolius belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Rosaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Rubus, and its specific species name, parvifolius.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rubus parvifolius has large, palmately lobed leaves that typically feature three to five distinct leaflets with serrated edges and a slightly hairy surface. The stems, or canes, are woody and often covered in small prickles or fine hairs. Each leaf is arranged alternately along the stem and is supported by a long petiole. During the flowering stage, the plant produces clusters of small, white five-petaled flowers. These blooms eventually transition into aggregate fruits known as blackberries, which turn from red to deep black when fully ripe.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rubus parvifolius?

This plant has antibacterial, antiproliferative, antitumor, anti-apoptosis, and anti-fatigue pharmacological activities. Its antibacterial properties allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms through the disruption of cellular processes. The plant also exhibits antiproliferative and antitumor effects by inhibiting the rapid growth and spread of malignant cells. Furthermore, its influence on anti-apoptosis mechanisms helps regulate programmed cell death within biological systems. Finally, its anti-fatigue capabilities contribute to reducing physical exhaustion and improving overall metabolic recovery.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", Rubus parvifolius L. is a medicinal herb that possesses antibacterial activity. The extraction procedure from the water extract of Rubus parvifolius leaves yielded 0.36% (w/w) of light yellowish oil. This extracted oil effectively inhibited the growth of various Gram positive and negative bacteria, including Staphylococcus aureus, Staphylococcus epidermidis, Enterococcus faecalis, Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Acinetobacter baumaniii, Bacillus cloacae, and Klebsiella pneumoniae. Analysis of the hydro-distillated Rubus parvifolius volatile oil identified 29 compounds. These constituents included 4-hydroxy-3-methoxystyrene at 66%, 3,7,11,15-tetramethyl-2-hexadecen-1-ol at 10%, and 4-tert-butylbenzoic acid at 2%.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains total glycosides and total saponins as its primary medicinal compounds. These chemical constituents are frequently analyzed to determine the therapeutic potential of the species. The presence of total glycosides often plays a significant role in various biological activities. Additionally, the concentration of total saponins can influence the plant's overall medicinal profile. Researchers study these specific compounds to better understand the pharmacological properties of Rubus parvifolius.

According to a study published by "Zhongguo Zhong yao za zhi = Zhongguo zhongyao zazhi = China journal of Chinese materia medica", Rubus parvifolius contains total saponins that exhibit antitumor effects. In vitro assays showed increased absorbency at concentrations of 0.01 mg x mL(-1) and 0.001 mg x mL(-1) with statistical significance of P < 0.05. For in vivo studies, a high dose of 100 mg x kg(-1) resulted in a tumor inhibitory rate of 37.02%, while a middle dose of 50 mg x kg(-1) yielded a rate of 30.61%. The apoptosis indexes for the B16 tumor cells in the treatment groups were 32.5%, 20.5%, and 5.5%, respectively, with statistical significance of P < 0.05. These total saponins of Rubus parvifolius inhibit the proliferation of malignant melanoma cells and promote tumor cell apoptosis.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating rheumatism, rheumatoid arthritis, and various forms of hepatic injury including hepatic damage caused by carbon tetrachloride (CCl(4))-induced hepatic injury. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds possess significant anti-inflammatory properties that help manage joint pain and swelling associated with rheumatoid arthritis. Additionally, the plant demonstrates a protective effect against chemical toxins that trigger liver cell destruction. By mitigating oxidative stress, it serves as a potential therapeutic agent against widespread hepatic damage. These diverse medicinal applications highlight its importance in both inflammatory and metabolic health management.

According to a study published by "Journal of Zhejiang University. Science. B", the n-butanol extract of Rubus parvifolius L. (RPL) provides hepatoprotective effects against carbon tetrachloride (CCl(4))-induced hepatic injury in mice. This extract markedly attenuated increases in serum alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) levels caused by CCl(4) intoxication. Furthermore, RPL significantly prevented the decrease in superoxide dismutase (SOD) activity and the increase in malondialdehyde (MDA) content of liver tissue. These antioxidant activities are attributed to phenolic compounds identified through high-performance liquid chromatography/tandem mass spectrometry (HPLC-MS/MS).
