Botanical Identification of Myrrh (Commiphora molmol)

To identify Myrrh (Commiphora molmol) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Myrrh, or Commiphora molmol, is classified within the plant kingdom. It belongs to the division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Burserales, family Burseraceae, genus Commiphora, and species Commiphora molmol. This classification reflects its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Myrrh is Commiphora molmol, a species within the Burseraceae family. It was first described by the botanist A. J. Roe in 1840. Synonyms include Commiphora myrrha and Commiphora gileadensis, reflecting historical taxonomic revisions. Common names such as Myrrh, Gilead balsam, and Ophir are widely recognized in herbal and traditional medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Myrrh, derived from Commiphora molmol, is a small to medium shrub or tree. It has a deep taproot system, supporting its growth in arid environments. The stem is woody and branched, often with a reddish-brown color. Leaves are alternate, simple, and oval-shaped with a leathery texture. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small greenish-white flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing small, dark seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Myrrh, from Commiphora molmol, can be distinguished by its reddish-brown resinous sap, which hardens into aromatic gum. It has small, leathery leaves and produces reddish, woody pods. The plant is typically found in arid regions, and its distinct scent helps differentiate it from similar species like Commiphora myrrha.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Myrrh (Commiphora molmol) is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, particularly in countries like Somalia, Ethiopia, and Sudan. It thrives in dry, hot climates with minimal rainfall. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Myrrh (Commiphora molmol) blooms from March to May, producing small greenish-yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs between June and August. Harvesting of the resin typically takes place from October to February, depending on regional climate and tree maturity.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Myrrh, Commiphora molmol, is closely related to species like Commiphora opobalsamum and Commiphora erythraea. It belongs to the genus Commiphora within the Burseraceae family, sharing traits such as resin-producing capabilities and similar morphological features. These relationships help in understanding its ecological and medicinal significance.
