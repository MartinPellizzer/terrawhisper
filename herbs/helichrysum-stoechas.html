Scientific and Botanical Profile

Stoechas Helichrysum, with botanical name Helichrysum stoechas, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

Native to the Mediterranean region, including southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of Western Asia, this plant is also found in South Africa, where it is often referred to as Cape Gold or Cape Chamomile. Morphologically, Helichrysum stoechas is a herbaceous perennial with a compact, bushy growth habit, featuring silver-gray, hairy leaves and clusters of small, yellowish-white flowers that resemble tiny pineapples, giving it the common name Pineapple Weed. It is also known by numerous other names such as Golden Everlasting, Sticky Helichrysum, and Scorpion Plant, reflecting its distinctive appearance and aromatic properties.

The plant's resilience, ornamental value, and historical use in herbal medicine have contributed to its widespread recognition and cultivation across various regions.

Its enduring presence across cultures highlights its deep-rooted historical and therapeutic value.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Helichrysum stoechas has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

For internal use, a tea can be prepared by steeping dried leaves in hot water, while a decoction involves boiling the plant material for a longer period. Topical applications include using the essential oil diluted in a carrier oil or applying a powder directly to the skin for localized effects. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision if safe.

It is advised to use helichrysum stoechas cautiously, with a short duration and limited frequency to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Helichrysum stoechas can be considered relatively safe when used in appropriate doses for short-term periods, but it is important to be aware of its potential side effects, which may include gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and skin irritation.

While no major drug interactions have been reported, it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before combining it with other medications or herbs to avoid any unforeseen interactions. Special populations such as pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should exercise caution and seek medical advice. For safe use, it is recommended to start with a low dose, monitor for adverse reactions, and discontinue use if any negative effects occur.

Always consult a qualified healthcare professional before incorporating helichrysum stoechas into your regimen, especially if you have pre-existing health conditions or are taking other medications.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Helichrysum stoechas grows in well-drained, loamy soil that is slightly acidic to neutral, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering during the growing season while avoiding waterlogging.

It thrives in temperate climates and is relatively drought-tolerant once established, making it suitable for Mediterranean and subtropical regions. For optimal growth, it benefits from annual pruning to encourage bushier growth and prevent legginess, as well as regular weeding to reduce competition for nutrients. The plant is typically harvested during the early morning or late afternoon when essential oils are most concentrated, using sharp scissors to cut the flower heads just above the foliage to preserve the plant's vitality.

After harvesting, the flowers should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation to retain their potency, then stored in airtight glass containers in a dark, cool place or refrigerated to extend shelf life and maintain therapeutic properties.
