What's the taxonomical classification of Thelasis pygmaea?

Thelasis pygmaea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asparagales. This specific plant is a part of the Orchidaceae family, and its taxonomic classification is completed through its designation within the genus Thelasis and the specific species epithet pygmaea.

      What are the herbal preparations of Thelasis pygmaea?

      Thelasis pygmaea is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a topical paste depending on the specific medicinal application. The dried whole plant is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid extract used for internal consumption. When used as a powder, the desiccated plant material is finely ground to be mixed with honey or warm water. For external skin ailments, the fresh or dried plant is crushed into a thick paste to be applied directly to the affected area. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the potency and delivery of the plant's bioactive compounds to the patient's needs.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Thelasis pygmaea can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and mild gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap may trigger redness or itching in individuals with sensitive skin. Some users have reported swelling or rashes following prolonged exposure to the leaves. If consumed accidentally, the plant might lead to stomach cramps or nausea. These adverse reactions are typically localized but should be monitored closely.
