What's the taxonomical classification of Piper kadsura?

Piper kadsura belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Piperales and is a member of the family Piperaceae. Finally, it is identified within the genus Piper as the specific species kadsura.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Piper kadsura has simple, ovate to elliptical leaves with an acute apex and a cordate base, growing on slender, woody climbing stems. The leaf margins are entire, and the venation pattern is prominent, typically featuring several pairs of lateral veins curving toward the tip. The plant produces small, greenish-white flowers arranged in dense, cylindrical spikes that emerge from the leaf axils. These inflorescences eventually develop into small, fleshy drupes that serve as the primary fruit structure. The stems exhibit a somewhat quadrangular cross-section in younger growth before maturing into a more cylindrical form.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds piperkadsin A, piperkadsin B, piperlactam S, N-p-coumaroyl tyramine, and futoquinol. These specific phytochemicals are responsible for the unique pharmacological profile of the species. Researchers study these molecules to understand their potential bioactive properties in various therapeutic applications. The presence of these diverse chemical structures highlights the complexity of the plant's secondary metabolites. Understanding these components is essential for developing new treatments derived from natural sources.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", the stems of Piper kadsura contain two new neolignans, piperkadsin A (1) and piperkadsin B (2), alongside 11 known neolignans, three known alkaloids, stigmasterol, and the highly oxygenated compound (+)-crotepoxide. The anti-inflammatory activities of these isolated compounds were evaluated through the inhibition of PMA-induced ROS production in human polymorphonuclear neutrophils. Specifically, compounds 1, 2, futoquinol (3), piperlactam S (4), and N-p-coumaroyl tyramine (5) showed potent inhibition with IC50 values of 4.3 +/- 1.0, 12.2 +/- 3.2, 13.1 +/- 5.3, 7.0 +/- 1.9, and 8.4 +/- 1.3 microM, respectively.
