Borage (Borago officinalis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Borage, also known as Borago officinalis, is a flowering plant native to Europe and western Asia, widely cultivated for its medicinal properties and ornamental value.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Borage (Borago Officinalis)?

Borage, or Borago officinalis, is an herb native to Europe and western Asia, known for its blue star-shaped flowers and historical use in herbal medicine for its purported calming and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Borage is Borago officinalis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Borago, and species Borago officinalis. It is commonly known as borage in Europe and as starflower in North America. The plant is also known by synonyms such as Borago latifolia and Borago vulgaris in botanical contexts, and by regional names like blueweed and honeyflower in various parts of the world.

Plant Description and Morphology

Borage is an annual herb that typically grows to a height of 30-90 cm, featuring a branching stem covered in fine hairs.

Its leaves are ovate to triangular in shape, with serrated edges and a fuzzy texture. The flowers are bright blue or purple, star-shaped with five petals, and arranged in clusters at the ends of the stems. The root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing the plant to thrive in well-drained soils.

The leaves and stems have a mild, cucumber-like scent, while the flowers are slightly aromatic and tender in texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Borage is native to southern Europe, the Mediterranean region, and parts of western Asia.

It thrives in temperate climates with well-drained soils and full sun exposure. The plant is commonly found in meadows, along roadsides, and in disturbed areas such as fields and waste ground. It prefers mild winters and warm summers, making it adaptable to a range of ecological conditions.

Its natural range extends from southern France through Italy, Spain, and into Turkey.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves and flowers of Borage are the primary parts used medicinally.

The leaves are often harvested during the early flowering stage for their highest concentration of beneficial compounds. Flowers are commonly used in infusions or tinctures due to their mild flavor and therapeutic properties. Both parts contain essential oils and flavonoids that contribute to their medicinal effects.

The roots are occasionally used in traditional preparations but are less common compared to the leaves and flowers.

Phytochemical Composition

Borage contains a variety of active compounds, including essential fatty acids such as gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), which contribute to its therapeutic properties.

Secondary metabolites like flavonoids and polyphenols are also present and play a role in the plant's medicinal effects. The plant is known for its high content of omega-6 fatty acids, particularly in its oil, which is a key component of its pharmacological profile. Chemical markers such as echinacoside and other bioactive compounds are used to authenticate the quality and potency of borage preparations.

These compounds collectively support the plant's use in herbal medicine for inflammatory and skin-related conditions.

Pharmacological Properties

Borage (Borago officinalis) possesses pharmacological properties that include anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and sedative effects, primarily due to the presence of gamma-linolenic acid and other bioactive compounds.

Mechanisms of Action

Borage (Borago officinalis) exerts its effects primarily through the presence of gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), an omega-6 fatty acid that modulates inflammation, supports skin health, and influences hormonal balance by interacting with cellular receptors and enzymatic pathways.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Borage (Borago officinalis) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support respiratory health, alleviate inflammation, and promote skin healing due to its anti-inflammatory and expectorant properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Borage (Borago officinalis) is typically prepared as a tea, tincture, or infused oil, with the leaves and flowers used most commonly, though the seeds can also be processed into a powder or oil for medicinal use.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of borage (Borago officinalis) for herbal use ranges from 1 to 2 grams of dried leaves per day, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic effect and individual health considerations.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Borage (Borago officinalis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, and it should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and by individuals with known allergies to plants in the Boraginaceae family.

Drug Interactions

Borage (Borago officinalis) may interact with medications that affect the central nervous system, such as sedatives, antidepressants, and antipsychotics, due to its potential sedative and anti-inflammatory properties.

Toxicity and Precautions

Borage (Borago officinalis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, or skin irritation in some individuals, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with known allergies to the Asteraceae family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Borage (Borago officinalis) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, requiring regular watering and moderate fertilization during the growing season.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of borage involve harvesting the leaves and flowers, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as maceration or distillation to obtain essential oils and active compounds like gamma-linolenic acid.

Storage and Shelf Life

Borage (Borago officinalis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, and its dried leaves and flowers can maintain potency for up to 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Borage (Borago officinalis) has primarily focused on its chemical composition, particularly the presence of gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), and its potential therapeutic effects in conditions such as inflammatory disorders, skin conditions, and hormonal imbalances.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Borage has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its soothing properties, particularly to alleviate respiratory ailments, reduce inflammation, and as a calming agent for nervous system disorders.

Regulatory Status

Borage (Borago officinalis) is not approved as a drug by the FDA, but it is permitted for use in herbal medicine under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) as a dietary supplement.
