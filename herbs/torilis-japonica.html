Torilis japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Torilis japonica?

Torilis japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Apiales. This plant is a member of the family Apiaceae, which places it within the genus Torilis, ultimately identifying it as the specific species japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Torilis japonica has an erect, annual herbaceous habit characterized by slender, branching stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are bipinnate or tripinnate, featuring finely divided, lanceolate leaflets that give the foliage a delicate, feathery appearance. At the terminals of the stems, the plant produces small, white, umbel-shaped flower clusters consisting of numerous tiny florets. Each individual flower possesses five petals and is supported by a structure of bractlets known as an involucre. The resulting seed heads are small, dry schizocarps that often feature hooked bristles to aid in animal-mediated dispersal.

    As discussed in a study, a sesquiterpene compound named torilin was isolated from the fruits of Torilis japonica. This specific compound was identified as a sesquiterpene that reverses multidrug-resistance in cancer cells. Toriilin was shown to potentiate the cytotoxicities of adriamycin, vinblastine, taxol and colchicine. These effects were specifically observed against multidrug-resistant KB-V1 and MCF7/ADR cells.

      What health problems is this plant used for?

      This plant is used for treating Staphylococcus aureus infections, dysentery, inflammation, lymphadenitis, and uterine tumors. Its medicinal properties are frequently applied to combat bacterial issues and intestinal distress. Practitioners also utilize its compounds to reduce systemic inflammation and swelling in the lymph nodes. Furthermore, the plant's components are studied for their potential role in addressing uterine tumors. These diverse therapeutic applications demonstrate the broad pharmacological potential of the species.

      As discussed in a study, Torilis japonica ethanol extract (TJE) was evaluated for its inhibitory effects on biofilm production in MSSA KCTC 1927, MRSA KCCM 40510, and MRSA KCCM 40511. Biofilm assays demonstrated that TJE could inhibit biofilm formation in all of these strains. When the strains were treated with TJE, the hemolysis of sheep blood was found to be reduced. The expression of hla reduced in a concentration-dependent manner upon treatment with TJE. Additionally, the expression levels of agrA, sarA, icaA, and RNAIII were significantly reduced compared to those in the control group. TJE can suppress biofilm formation and virulence factor-related gene expression in MSSA and MRSA strains.

        What side effects this plant can have?

        This plant can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and allergic dermatitis upon contact with the human body. The sap contains chemical compounds that react with sunlight to trigger phytophotodermatitis in sensitive individuals. Once the plant touches the skin, redness and intense itching often follow shortly after exposure. In some cases, the reaction may lead to painful swelling or even long-lasting discoloration of the affected area. Careful avoidance is necessary to prevent these painful dermatological complications.
