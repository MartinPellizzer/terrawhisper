Jasminum nudiflorum

What's the taxonomical classification of Jasminum nudiflorum?

Jasminum nudiflorum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Oleaceae, specifically situated within the genus Jasminum, and is identified by the species name nudiflorum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Jasminum nudiflorum has arching, deciduous woody stems that produce small, oval, dark green leaves arranged in opposite pairs.

The plant typically grows as a sprawling shrub with slender, flexible branches that can reach several feet in length. Unlike many other jasmine species, this plant produces bright yellow, tubular flowers that bloom on bare stems before the foliage emerges in spring. The leaves are evergreen in milder climates but often fall during colder periods.

Each leaf possesses a smooth margin and a pointed tip, contributing to its dense, bushy appearance once the new growth matures.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Jasminum nudiflorum contains medicinal properties primarily derived from its whole flowering stems and individual blossoms. These botanical parts are frequently utilized in traditional medicine to treat various ailments through infusions or topical applications. The flowers are often steeped to create fragrant teas believed to possess calming and sedative effects on the nervous system. Additionally, extracts from the stems may be applied to the skin to help alleviate minor inflammatory conditions. This plant remains a valued resource in herbal practices due to the diverse chemical compounds found throughout its structure.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Jasminum nudiflorum targets specific inflammatory enzymes including cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), lipoxygenase (LOX), and various pro-inflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha). Its bioactive compounds, such as jasmine alkaloids and various phenolic acids, exert their effects by modulating the signaling pathways associated with these protein mediators. By inhibiting the COX-2 enzyme, the plant helps regulate the production of prostaglandins that drive pain and swelling. Furthermore, its interaction with nitric oxide synthase (NOS) allows it to mitigate oxidative stress and vascular inflammation. These multi-target protein interactions underpin the plant's traditional use in treating inflammatory conditions and promoting overall systemic homeostasis.

      What are the herbal preparations of Jasminum nudiflorum?

      Jasminum nudiflorum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical medicinal oil. The dried flowers are often simmered in water to create a tea that is consumed for its traditional calming properties. Some practitioners grind the plant material into a fine powder to be encapsulated or mixed into larger medicinal formulas. For external use, the botanical components can be macerated in a carrier oil to soothe skin irritations. These diverse methods allow the plant's active constituents to be utilized for both internal and external therapeutic purposes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Jasminum nudiflorum can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive upset if ingested. While the plant is primarily used for ornamental purposes, contact with its sap or foliage may trigger dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Some people might experience redness, itching, or swelling on the skin after handling the stems or leaves. If accidentally consumed, it may lead to nausea or stomach discomfort. It is always best to avoid direct contact if you have known sensitivities to flowering shrubs.
