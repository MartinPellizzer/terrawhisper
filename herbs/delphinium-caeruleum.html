Delphinium caeruleum

What's the taxonomical classification of Delphinium caeruleum?

Delphinium caeruleum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Ranunculales. This plant is a member of the family Ranunculaceae, falling specifically into the genus Delphinium, with its unique identification completed by the species name caeruleum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Delphinium caeruleum has upright, herbaceous stems that reach heights of up to 1.5 meters and bear large, terminal racemes of deep blue to violet flowers. Each individual blossom features a prominent nectar-producing spur at the back of the petal structure. The foliage is composed of deeply lobed, palmately divided leaves that create a lush, dark green base for the plant. These leaves are typically arranged in a basal rosette or along the lower portions of the stem. The overall structure supports heavy flower spikes that can contain dozens of individual blooms.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a variety of potent diterpene alkaloids, most notably delphinine, delphinidine, and methyllycaconitine. These specific chemical compounds function as neurotoxins by acting as competitive antagonists at nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. Due to their high toxicity, these alkaloids are primarily studied for their physiological effects on the nervous system rather than for safe therapeutic use. Researchers examine these substances to understand how they interact with neuromuscular junctions in biological models. Consequently, the chemical profile of the plant is defined by its ability to disrupt essential nerve signaling.
