Cassytha filiformis

What's the taxonomical classification of Cassytha filiformis?

Cassytha filiformis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Laurales and is a member of the family Lauraceae. Within this family, the plant is categorized under the genus Cassytha, and its specific identification is determined by its species name, filiformis.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-draining, sandy or rocky soil and full sunlight to thrive in warm, tropical to subtropical climates.

You should water it moderately, allowing the substrate to dry out between applications to prevent root rot. Because it is a climbing or scrambling shrub, providing a trellis or support structure will encourage its natural growth habit. Regular pruning helps maintain its shape and promotes denser foliage throughout the growing season.

Avoid over-fertilizing, as excessive nitrogen can lead to weak stem development.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cassytha filiformis?

This plant has analgesic, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antiplatelet aggregation, and vasorelaxing pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. The plant also demonstrates antimicrobial capabilities by inhibiting the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, its ability to induce vasorelaxation and provide antiplatelet aggregation activity suggests potential benefits for cardiovascular health. These diverse medicinal effects highlight the therapeutic potential of Cassytha filiformis in various biological systems.

According to a study published by "TheScientificWorldJournal", Cassytha filiformis exhibits various pharmacological activities including antimicrobial, anti-biofilm, analgesic, and diuretic effects. The hydroethanolic extracts showed antibacterial activity against Staphylococci and uropathogenic Enterobacteria with inhibition zones of 13.8-21.2 mm and MICs of 3.5-9 mg/mL. Both aqueous and hydroethanolic extracts were able to reduce bacterial biofilm formation to varying degrees. At a dosage of 200 mg/kg, the hydroethanolic extracts significantly reduced acetic acid-induced pain. This plant also exhibited diuretic activity comparable to furosemide. No toxic effects were observed at a dose of 2000 mg/kg.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds cassyformine, cassythic acid, cassythine, cathafiline, and cathaformine. These specialized alkaloids and organic acids are primarily concentrated within the roots of the species. Researchers study these specific chemical constituents to understand their potential pharmacological activities in human health. Each unique molecule contributes to the overall bioactive profile of the plant material. Detailed chemical analysis helps identify how these substances interact with biological systems.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", three new compounds were isolated and characterized from the MeOH extract of the fresh herbs of Cassytha filiformis using a bioassay-directed fractionation method. These new substances include the aporpine alkaloid cassyformine (4), the oxoaaporphine alkaloid filiformine (8), and the lignan (+)-diasyringaresinol (10). In addition to these three new findings, 14 known compounds were identified from the plant. Among the specific isolates, cathafiline (1), cathaformine (2), actinodaphnine (3), N-methylactinodaphnine (5), predicentrine (6), and ocoteine (7) exhibited significant antiplatelet aggregation activity.

According to a study published by "Journal of photochemistry and photobiology. B, Biology", Cassytha filiformis is used for the synthesis of silver nanoparticles with various biomedical applications. These nanoparticles exhibit significant antibacterial activities against Gram positive Streptococcus aureus and Gram negative Escherichia coli, showing higher activity on E. coli than on S. aureus. Distinct antifungal activity was found against Candida albicans with an MIC of 5.244μg/ml. Furthermore, the nanoparticles demonstrated a remarkable anticancer activity against HCT116 colorectal carcinoma cells with an IC50 of 10μg/ml.
