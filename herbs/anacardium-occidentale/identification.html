Botanical Identification of Cashew (Anacardium occidentale)

To identify Cashew (Anacardium occidentale) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Cashew, or Anacardium occidentale, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically within the flowering plants, eudicots, and the Sapindales order. It is part of the Anacardiaceae family, genus Anacardium, and species Anacardium occidentale, which are classifications that help identify its biological relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Cashew is Anacardium occidentale, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It has several synonyms, including Anacardium occidentale L. and Anacardium oblongifolium L. Common names include cashew tree, cashew apple, and cashew nut, while regional names vary across cultures. Historically, it was known by names like "cashew" in Portuguese and "kashew" in English.

Morphological Characteristics

Cashew is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree with a shallow, fibrous root system that spreads widely near the soil surface. Its stem is typically straight and cylindrical, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark. The leaves are alternate, simple, and elliptic to ovate in shape, with a leathery texture and a pointed tip. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small, clustered flowers. The fruit is a modified peduncle that develops into a kidney-shaped, woody drupe, containing a single seed enclosed in a toxic, resinous aril.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cashew can be distinguished by its unique compound leaf with 5-9 pairs of oval leaflets, a smooth, elliptical fruit with a single nut inside, and a distinctive reddish-brown, spiny husk. The leaves have a serrated margin and emit a strong odor when crushed. These features help differentiate it from similar species like Anacardium microcarpum.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cashew trees are native to South America, particularly Brazil, and are now widely cultivated in tropical regions. They thrive in warm, humid climates with well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. They typically grow at altitudes between 200 and 600 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cashew trees bloom between April and June, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs from August to November. Harvesting typically takes place between November and January, when the fruit is mature and ready for collection.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cashew belongs to the Anacardiaceae family, sharing traits with other members like the mango and sumac. It is closely related to species such as the poison ivy and the cashew apple tree. Its genus, Anacardium, includes about 120 species, many with similar chemical properties and uses in traditional medicine.
