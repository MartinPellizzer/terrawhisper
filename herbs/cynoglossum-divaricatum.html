Cynoglossum divaricatum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cynoglossum divaricatum has erect, branched stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 80 centimeters and possess simple or slightly serrated leaves. The leaves are generally lanceolate or oblong in shape with entire margins and are arranged alternately along the stem. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils and are often white or pale in color. The plant produces small, nut-like fruits that are characteristic of the Boraginaceae family. These morphological features help distinguish the species from other closely related members of its genus.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cynoglossum divaricatum can cause toxic reactions in humans and animals due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids which lead to severe liver damage. These chemical compounds can trigger hepatic veno-occlusive disease, a condition where small veins in the liver become obstructed. Prolonged exposure or ingestion may result in permanent scarring of the liver tissue, known as cirrhosis. In addition to liver toxicity, the plant can cause systemic distress if consumed in significant quantities. Symptoms of poisoning often include abdominal pain, nausea, and jaundice.
