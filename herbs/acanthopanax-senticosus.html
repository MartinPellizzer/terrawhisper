Acanthopanax senticosus

What's the taxonomical classification of Acanthopanax senticosus?

Acanthopanax senticosus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Apiales and is a member of the family Araliaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Acanthopanax, and its unique species designation, senticosus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acanthopanax senticosus has woody, deciduous stems characterized by dense, sharp, recurved prickles and compound leaves consisting of five to seven leaflets. The leaflets are typically ovate or elliptical with serrated margins and a dark green, glossy upper surface. These leaves are arranged alternately along the branches, which often exhibit a spreading or arching growth habit. During the flowering season, small, inconspicuous flowers appear in dense, terminal panicles. The plant eventually produces small, fleshy berries that transition from green to a dark red or black color when ripe.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds eleutheroside B, eleutheroside E, syringin, sesamin, and chlorogenic acid. These bioactive molecules work together to provide various physiological benefits to the human body. Specifically, the eleutherosides are known for their adaptogenic properties that help manage physical and mental stress. Compounds like sesamin and syringin contribute to antioxidant support and metabolic health. Additionally, the presence of chlorogenic acid provides significant neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory effects.

As discussed in a study, aqueous root extract from Acanthopanax senticosus contains selected phytochemical components including caffeic acid and chlorogenic acid. These specific components, along with the aqueous root extract, interact with H2S/HS- to increase the reduction of the ●cPTIO stable radical. While caffeic acid and chlorogenic acid show these effects, protocatechuic acid, eleutheroside B, and eleutheroside E do not. The study also found that the aqueous root extract and its components inhibited plasmid DNA cleavage induced by polysulfides. The antioxidant properties of the aqueous root extract are influenced by the concentrations of cysteine, glutathione, and H2S/HS-.
