Ixeris chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Ixeris chinensis?

Ixeris chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the biological hierarchy, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the lineage further, it is placed in the order Asterales and the family Asteraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Ixeris, and its species name, chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ixeris chinensis has slender, creeping rhizomes that produce narrow, linear leaves and small, pale pink to white tubular flowers. The leaves are typically thin and grass-like, measuring only a few millimeters in width. Its floral structure consists of small, inconspicuous petals that emerge from a compact inflorescence. The plant often forms dense mats through its spreading underground stems. These morphological traits allow it to colonize moist or muddy habitats effectively.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically including countries such as China, Vietnam, Thailand, and Malaysia. It thrives in wetland habitats like marshes, riverbanks, and shallow ponds throughout these humid climates. The species is frequently found in moist soils where it can access consistent water sources. Its distribution is primarily constrained by its preference for warm temperatures and high moisture levels. Consequently, it is rarely located in arid or temperate zones far from coastal or freshwater ecosystems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ixeris chinensis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its anti-inflammatory, anti-leukemic, hepatoprotective, immunomodulatory, and antidepressant activities. Scientific investigations suggest that its bioactive compounds can effectively reduce systemic inflammation and modulate immune responses to enhance bodily defense mechanisms. Research also highlights its ability to protect liver cells from damage and inhibit the proliferation of certain leukemia cell lines. Furthermore, the plant exhibits neuroprotective qualities that may contribute to its observed antidepressant effects. These diverse biological properties make Ixeris chinensis a subject of intense interest for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the arabinogalactan extracted from Ixeris chinensis (Thunb.) Nakai., known as ICPA, exhibited excellent immunomodulatory activity. ICPA ameliorated symptoms of DSS-induced ulcerative colitis, including loss of body weight, decrease of disease activity index, shortness of colon length, and reduction of spleen index. The treatment inhibited the overproduction of TNF-α, IL-1β, and NLRP3 while promoting the secretion of IL-10 in colon tissues. It also restored intestinal barrier integrity by increasing the expression of ZO-1 and occluidin and regulated gut microbiota by elevating Turicibacter and Bifidobacterium while decreasing the Bacteroidetes/Firmicutes ratio. Furthermore, ICPA improved depression-like behavior and reduced the expression of proteins NLRP3, GFAP, and Iba-1 in brain tissues.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Apigenin-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside, Chinensiolide B, Chinensiolide C, Chinensiolide E, and Luteolin. These specific phytochemicals are often studied for their potential bioactive properties in various biological systems. The presence of these flavones and sesquiterpene lactones contributes to the plant's complex chemical profile. Researchers examine these individual molecules to understand how they interact with human cells. Such detailed chemical analysis helps identify the therapeutic potential of the species.

As discussed in a study, fifteen compounds were isolated from Ixeris chinensis through silica gel column chromatography, HPLC, and recrystallization. The identified medicinal constituents include Methyl-4-hydroxyphenylacetate (1), Daucosterol (2), Sitosterol (3), Luteolin-7-O-beta-D-glucoside (4), 3,11(13)-triene-12,6-lactone (5), Chinensiolide B (6), Chinensiolide E (7), Ixerochinoside (8), Chinensiolide C (9), 10alpha-hydroxy-guaia-4(15)-ene-12,6-lactone (10), 10alpha-hydroxy-guaia-4(15),11(13)-diene-12,6-lactone-3beta-O-beta-D-(6'-p-hydroxyphenylacetyl) glucopyranoside (11), Epiloliolide (12), Apigenin-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (13), Luteolin (14), and Lutein (15). Specifically, compounds 1, 10, 11, 12, and 15 were isolated from this plant for the first time. Structures for these fifteen compounds were elucidated using spectral analysis.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating acute hepatitis, various forms of hepatitis, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, fatty liver diseases, and systemic inflammation. Its medicinal properties focus heavily on supporting liver function and reducing cellular damage. By addressing inflammation, it helps mitigate the progression of chronic liver conditions. It is often employed in traditional practices to manage the complications arising from fatty liver diseases. These therapeutic actions target both immediate and long-term hepatic ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the roots or stems in water to extract its medicinal properties for treating inflammatory conditions. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or touched. Contact with the leaves or stems may lead to contact dermatitis characterized by redness and itching. Some individuals might experience swelling or rashes if they have a specific sensitivity to its chemical compounds. If consumed accidentally, it could trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. It is important to handle the specimen with care to avoid these adverse physical responses.
