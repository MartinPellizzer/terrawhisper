Camellia sinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Camellia sinensis?

Camellia sinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Ericales and the family Theaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Camellia, and its specific species name, sinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Camellia sinensis has an evergreen, woody shrub or small tree structure characterized by elliptical, leathery leaves with serrated margins and a glossy dark green upper surface. The plant features a complex root system that provides stability and nutrient absorption from acidic soils. Small, white, fragrant flowers emerge from the leaf axils, showcasing prominent yellow stamens. The foliage is arranged in an alternate pattern along the stems, which can reach several meters in height under ideal conditions. These morphological traits are essential for the production of high-quality tea leaves.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and eastern China, specifically within the provinces of Fujian, Zhejiang, and Anhui. Its natural habitat consists of subtropical climates characterized by high humidity and consistent rainfall. Beyond its indigenous range, it has been widely introduced to other parts of East and Southeast Asia to support tea production. Significant commercial cultivation also occurs in India, Sri Lanka, and Kenya due to their favorable environmental conditions. These diverse geographical areas share the essential requirements of acidic soil and moderate temperatures needed for growth.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, Camellia sinensis, requires well-drained, acidic soil and a subtropical climate with consistent rainfall to thrive.

Growers typically plant these evergreen shrubs in regions that experience warm temperatures and high humidity levels. Regular pruning is essential to maintain a low height, which facilitates easier leaf harvesting. Once established, the bushes can be harvested multiple times a year to produce various types of tea.

Proper soil management and protection from frost ensure a productive and sustainable crop.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Camellia sinensis contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its leaves, though the buds and stems also contribute to its therapeutic profile. The leaves are harvested and processed to extract polyphenols, specifically catechins, which provide potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits. These chemical constituents help in regulating metabolism and improving cardiovascular health by lowering cholesterol levels. Additionally, the plant contains caffeine and L-theanine, which work together to enhance cognitive function and promote mental alertness. Regular consumption of these extracts can also aid in digestion and support immune system regulation.

What traditional systems uses this plant?

This plant, known as Camellia sinensis, has been cultivated and utilized in East Asia for thousands of years primarily for the production of tea through various processing methods.

Ancient Chinese texts suggest that early practitioners consumed the leaves as a medicinal tonic to aid digestion and enhance mental clarity. During the Tang and Song dynasties, the ritualistic preparation of tea became a cornerstone of spiritual and social life, particularly within Buddhist monastic traditions. Throughout history, different processing techniques such as steaming, pan-firing, and oxidation were developed to transform the raw leaves into green, oolong, or black varieties.

These diverse preparation styles allowed the plant to serve both as a daily beverage and a significant element in complex ceremonial practices.

What are the pharmacological activities of Camellia sinensis?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer, anti-diabetic, and anti-hyperlipidemic properties. The high concentration of polyphenols and catechins allows it to neutralize free radicals and reduce systemic inflammation. These bioactive compounds also play a role in inhibiting tumor growth and regulating cellular processes to prevent cancer progression. Furthermore, regular consumption helps manage blood glucose levels and improves lipid profiles by lowering cholesterol. Such multi-faceted benefits contribute to its significant role in preventative medicine and metabolic health.

According to a study published by "Journal of dietary supplements", Camellia sinensis has bioactive components with neuroprotective, antioxidant, anti-proteinopathies, neural-vasodilation, anti-inflammatory, and iron chelating potential. These components may treat Parkinson's disease at the cellular level by decreasing microglia activation and attenuating damage from radical oxygen species. They can also support correct protein folding, chelate iron, and increase the substantia nigra blood flow. Furthermore, Camellia sinensis may promote dopaminergic cell growth.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including the potent antioxidant (-)‐Epigalocatechin‐3‐gallate, the amino acid theanine, the stimulant caffeine, and various types of catechin. Among these, epigallocatechin gallate (EGCC) is widely studied for its significant role in metabolic health and cellular protection. The presence of theanine helps to promote relaxation and mitigate the jittery effects often associated with stimulant intake. Caffeine acts as a central nervous system stimulant that enhances cognitive alertness and physical performance. Additionally, the general category of catechin provides essential polyphenolic properties that support overall cardiovascular function.

According to a study published by "Food & function", the green tea polyphenol (-) -Epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG) is studied for its biological activities in the prevention of chronic diseases. The study focuses on EGCG oxidation-derived polymers, specifically those identified as >10 kDa EGCG polymers. These >10 kDa EGCG polymers gained an advantage over native EGCG in increasing insulin sensitivity in type 2 diabetic mice. When these >10 kDa EGCG polymers were incubated with CaCo2 colon cancer cells, they coated the cell surface and regulated multiple components of the RAS. While the >10 kDa EGCG polymers exhibited compromised activities in scavenging or initiating reactive oxygen species compared to EGCG, they gained a higher reactivity toward sulfhydryl groups.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing Alzheimer's disease, diabetes mellitus, cardiovascular disease, liver disease, and obesity. The polyphenols found in its leaves help reduce inflammation and oxidative stress associated with neurodegenerative decline. Regular consumption can improve insulin sensitivity and blood glucose regulation in patients with metabolic disorders. Its antioxidants also support heart health by managing cholesterol levels and blood pressure. Furthermore, the compounds assist in regulating metabolic rates and protecting hepatic cells from damage.

According to a study published by "Antioxidants (Basel, Switzerland)", Camellia sinensis is used for medicinal purposes. This plant contains polyphenolic compounds that may contribute to anti-amyloidogenic properties. These compounds can attenuate amyloid-β toxicity and disrupt the aggregation of toxic amyloid-β protein fibrils. Such processes are linked to the onset and progression of Alzheimer's Disease. The study analyzed the antioxidant effects of these extracts using a 2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) assay.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be processed into an infusion by steeping the leaves in hot water, an extract through concentrated liquid solvents, a decoction by boiling the tougher plant parts, a tincture using alcohol, and an essential oil via steam distillation. The most common preparation is the infusion, which serves as the traditional method for brewing tea. Concentrated extracts are frequently used in modern dietary supplements to provide standardized doses of antioxidants. Tinctures offer a long-lasting way to preserve the plant's bioactive compounds in a shelf-stable liquid form. Finally, the essential oil captures the delicate aromatic compounds used primarily in perfumery and aromatherapy.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, insomnia, anxiety, jitteriness, restlessness, stomach upset, dizziness, headache, tachycardia, iron deficiency, dehydration, and vomiting. Excessive consumption of its caffeine content often leads to neurological symptoms like restlessness and anxiety or physical sensations such as tachycardia and dizziness. Digestive issues including nausea, vomiting, and general stomach upset are also common side effects for many drinkers. High doses may result in dehydration or interfere with nutrient absorption, potentially leading to iron deficiency. Additionally, the stimulating effects can trigger headaches and significant insomnia if consumed late in the day.
