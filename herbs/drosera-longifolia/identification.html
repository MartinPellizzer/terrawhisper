Botanical Identification of Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia)

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Long-Leaved Sundew is Drosera longifolia, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It belongs to the Droseraceae family and is known by several synonyms, including Drosera longifolia L. and Drosera longifolia subsp. longifolia. Common names such as Long-Leaved Sundew and Sticky Leaf are widely recognized in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia) is a low-growing herb with a rhizomatous root system. It has a prostrate stem that branches at the base. Its leaves are long, slender, and covered in sticky glands. The inflorescence is a terminal raceme, bearing small, white flowers. The fruit is a capsule that releases small, wind-dispersed seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Drosera longifolia can be distinguished by its long, slender leaves that are often curved and its pale yellow to greenish flowers. It typically grows in wet, acidic environments and has a more upright growth habit compared to other sundews. Its leaves are longer and less densely covered with glands than those of Drosera rotundifolia.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia) is native to Southeast Asia, including regions like Malaysia and Indonesia. It thrives in warm, humid climates and prefers acidic, nutrient-poor soils. This species typically grows at low to moderate altitudes, often in wetlands or shaded forest floors.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Long-Leaved Sundew blooms from May to July, producing small white flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to September. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested in early spring before flowering, when the leaves are most distinctive.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Drosera longifolia belongs to the Droseraceae family, closely related to species like Drosera binata and Drosera peltata. It shares key traits with other sundews, such as sticky glands and carnivorous habits, but differs in leaf structure and habitat preferences. Its phylogenetic position highlights evolutionary adaptations within the genus.
