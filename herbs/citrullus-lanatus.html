Citrullus lanatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrullus lanatus?

Citrullus lanatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of biological classification, it falls under the order Cucurbitales and the family Cucurbitaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Citrullus, and its species designation, lanatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Citrullus lanaus has a trailing, prostrate growth habit characterized by elongated, fleshy stems that spread horizontally across the ground. These stems are typically covered in fine, bristly hairs that provide a textured surface. The leaves are deeply lobed and arranged alternately along the vine, exhibiting a bright green color. Small, yellow flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves to facilitate pollination. Once fertilized, the plant produces large, oval fruits with a thick rind and sweet, watery pulp.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, spanning across much of the continent from West Africa to East Africa and down to Southern Africa. It typically flourishes in warm climates where seasonal rainfall provides sufficient moisture for growth. Within these regions, it is often found in sandy soils or along riverbanks where the ground remains relatively moist. Its distribution is closely tied to areas that experience high temperatures and minimal frost. Consequently, its presence is most concentrated in the equatorial belt and surrounding savanna ecosystems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Citrullus lanatus?

This plant has angiotensin I converting enzyme inhibitory, antihypertensive, antioxidant, diuretic, and α-amylase inhibitory pharmacological activities. Its ability to inhibit the angiotensin I converting enzyme directly contributes to its potent antihypertensive effects by regulating blood pressure. The presence of antioxidant properties helps mitigate oxidative stress within biological systems. Additionally, its diuretic activity assists in the excretion of excess fluids and salts from the body. Finally, the plant exhibits α-amylase inhibitory potential, which suggests a role in managing glucose levels by slowing carbohydrate digestion.

As discussed in a study, the phenolic extracts of Citrullus lanatus inhibit α-amylase, α-glucosidase, and angiotensin I converting enzyme in a dose-dependent manner. The free phenolic extracts of melon were found to be weaker inhibitors of ACE compared to their corresponding bound phenolic extracts. Furthermore, the phenolic extracts significantly (p<0.05) inhibited Fe2+-induced oxidative stress in the pancreas in vitro. Despite these pharmacological activities, the melon seed ranked least in terms of enzyme inhibitory potentials and antioxidant properties.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains cucurbitacins, flavonoids, phenolic compounds, apigenin, and kaempferol which provide its medicinal properties. The presence of cucurbitacins is particularly notable for their potential anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor activities. Various flavonoids and phenolic compounds within the plant contribute to its significant antioxidant capacity. Within the flavonoid group, apigenin offers specific neuroprotective and calming effects. Additionally, kaempferol serves as a key constituent known for its ability to combat oxidative stress and support immune function.

As discussed in a study, various cucurbitacins have been isolated from watermelon (Citrullus lanatus) and identified as active pharmacological compounds. These cucurbitacin derivatives are assessed for their cytotoxicity in various cancer cells due to their economic potential. The study investigated the enzymatic activities of 2 acetyltransferases (ACTs) and 1 UDP-glucosyltransferase (UGT) against these cucurbitacins. These 3 enzymes are involved in the dynamic conversion of cucurbitacins by catalyzing acetylation and glucosylation at moieties in the cucurbitacin skeleton. Such processes contribute to the diversity of the cucurbitacins found in the plant.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing diabetes mellitus, hypertension, urolithiasis, and various urinary disorders through its natural diuretic properties. The active compounds within the plant help regulate blood glucose levels in individuals suffering from metabolic imbalances. Its ability to promote fluid excretion makes it an effective natural diuretic for reducing systemic blood pressure. Furthermore, the plant assists in the flushing of minerals that contribute to the formation of kidney stones. These combined actions provide significant therapeutic support for the overall health of the renal and cardiovascular systems.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse physiological reactions such as low blood pressure, diarrhea, bloating, nausea, headache, and dizziness. These side effects often manifest as gastrointestinal distress including abdominal discomfort and irregular bowel movements. Some individuals may also experience neurological symptoms like persistent head pain or a spinning sensation. Changes in cardiovascular stability can lead to a significant drop in blood pressure levels. Monitoring how your body reacts to this species is essential to avoid these various complications.
