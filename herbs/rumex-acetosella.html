Rumex acetosella

What's the taxonomical classification of Rumex acetosella?

Rumex acetosella belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Caryophyllales and falls under the family Polygonaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Rumex, and its species designation, acetosella.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rumex acetosella has a perennial life cycle characterized by a slender, erect stem that typically reaches heights of 10 to 40 centimeters and supports clusters of small, greenish flowers. The leaves are primarily basal, forming a rosette of oblong or lanceolate shapes with smooth or slightly wavy margins. Upper stem leaves are much smaller, lanceolate, and arranged alternately along the flowering stalk. The inflorescence consists of dense, terminal panicles of tiny, inconspicuous flowers lacking petals. Each flower is enclosed by three distinct tepals that often turn reddish or brownish as the plant matures. Small, three-sided nutlets develop from these flowers, serving as the primary method of seed dispersal.

How is this plant cultivated?

What are the pharmacological activities of Rumex acetosella?

This plant has antibacterial, antioxidant, jaundice treatment, prostaglandin-synthesis inhibitor, and antiaflatoxigenic pharmacological activities. The presence of diverse phytochemicals allows it to combat various bacterial strains and mitigate oxidative stress in biological systems. Its medicinal properties include the ability to inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, which can help manage inflammatory responses. Furthermore, the plant shows potential in treating jaundice and exerting antiaflatoxigenic effects against toxic substances. These multifaceted biological actions highlight its importance in traditional and pharmacological research.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", the antibacterial screening of 14 Rumex species including R. acetosella was investigated against 11 bacterial strains such as Staphylococcus epidermidis, S. aureus, MRSA, Bacillus subtilis, Moraxella catarrhalis, Streptococcus pyogenes, S. pneumoniae, S. agalactiae, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae. The study used the disc diffusion method to test the antibacterial effects of n-hexane, chloroform and aqueous fractions of methanol extracts. Mainly the n-hexane and chloroform extracts prepared from the roots of the plants displayed high antibacterial activity with inhibition zones >15mm against one or more bacterial strains.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a variety of bioactive medicinal compounds including flavonoids such as apiogenin and luteolin. These specific phytochemicals are known for providing significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the organism. The presence of these flavonoids contributes to the overall therapeutic potential found within the plant's chemical profile. Researchers often study these compounds to understand how they interact with biological systems to promote health. Such chemical complexity makes the plant a subject of interest in pharmacological investigations.

According to a study published by "Antioxidants (Basel, Switzerland)", Rumex acetosella is characterized by a high total content of phenolic compounds ranging from 111-131 mg g-1. The leaves of this species contain the maximum content of flavonoids among the studied plants. Analysis via high-performance liquid chromatography with diode-array detection (HPLC-DAD) shows that derivatives of flavones, specifically apiigenin and luteolin, predominate in the leaves of Rumex acetosella. The phenolic profile of the plant also corresponds to its classification within the subgenus Acetosella.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, stomach pain, diarrhea, and potential kidney irritation if ingested. These gastrointestinal symptoms often arise due to the high concentration of oxalic acid found within its tissues. Consuming significant amounts of this acid can disrupt digestive processes and lead to acute discomfort. Furthermore, the accumulation of oxalates in the body poses a serious risk of kidney irritation or the formation of stones. Individuals should exercise extreme caution and avoid consuming any part of this species to prevent such toxic reactions.
