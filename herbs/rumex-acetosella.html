Rumex acetosella

What's the taxonomical classification of Rumex acetosella?

Rumex acetosella belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Caryophyllales and falls under the family Polygonaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Rumex, and its species designation, acetosella.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rumex acetosella has a perennial life cycle characterized by a slender, erect stem that typically reaches heights of 10 to 40 centimeters and supports clusters of small, greenish flowers. The leaves are primarily basal, forming a rosette of oblong or lanceolate shapes with smooth or slightly wavy margins. Upper stem leaves are much smaller, lanceolate, and arranged alternately along the flowering stalk. The inflorescence consists of dense, terminal panicles of tiny, inconspicuous flowers lacking petals. Each flower is enclosed by three distinct tepals that often turn reddish or brownish as the plant matures. Small, three-sided nutlets develop from these flowers, serving as the primary method of seed dispersal.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rumex acetosella?

This plant has antibacterial, antioxidant, prostaglandin-synthesis inhibitor, antiaflatoxigenic, and anticholinesterase pharmacological activities. Its phytochemical profile allows it to combat bacterial infections and neutralize harmful free radicals in the body. The presence of specific compounds enables it to inhibit the production of prostaglandins, which is useful for managing inflammatory responses. Additionally, the plant exhibits properties that prevent the formation of aflatoxins and can influence neurological functions through anticholinesterase effects. These diverse biological actions highlight its significant potential for medicinal research and therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, Rumex acetosella is one of 14 Rumex species native to the Carpathian Basin that were investigated for antibacterial effects. The screening utilized the disc diffusion method to test n-hexane, chloroform, and aqueous fractions of methanol extracts against various bacterial strains. While the study notes that n-hexane and chloroform extracts from roots of the genus displayed high antibacterial activity with inhibition zones greater than 15mm, specific data for R. acetosella alone was not detailed. The research focused on the antibacterial screening of these species to address diseases like bacteria-related dermatologic conditions, dysentery, and enteritis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains apiagenin, luteolin, and various other flavonoids that provide significant therapeutic benefits. These medicinal compounds function primarily as powerful antioxidants within the human body. The presence of flavonoids helps to reduce systemic inflammation and protect cells from oxidative stress. Specifically, apiagenin and luteolin are known for their potential neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory properties. Utilizing these specific phytochemicals can support overall cellular health and immune function.

As discussed in a study, Rumex acetosella is characterized by a high total content of phenolic compounds ranging from 111 to 131 mg g-1. The leaves of this plant contain a maximum content of flavonoids. According to high-performance liquid chromatography with diode-array detection analysis, derivatives of flavones such as apigenin and luteolin predominate in the leaves of Rumex acetosella. These findings contribute to the identification of the plant's phenolic profile.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, stomach pain, diarrhea, and potential kidney irritation if ingested. These gastrointestinal symptoms often arise due to the high concentration of oxalic acid found within its tissues. Consuming significant amounts of this acid can disrupt digestive processes and lead to acute discomfort. Furthermore, the accumulation of oxalates in the body poses a serious risk of kidney irritation or the formation of stones. Individuals should exercise extreme caution and avoid consuming any part of this species to prevent such toxic reactions.
