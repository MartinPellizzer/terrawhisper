Jasminum lanceolaria

What's the taxonomical classification of Jasminum lanceolaria?

Jasminum lanceolaria belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized under the order Lamiales and falls within the family Oleaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement in the genus Jasminum, with its unique designation as the species lanceolaria.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Jasminum lanceolaria targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, lipoxygenase, and various inflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. The bioactive compounds within the plant, such as specific terpenoids and phenolic derivatives, act as potent inhibitors of the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. By suppressing the activity of cyclooxygenase-2, these molecules significantly reduce the production of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins. Furthermore, the plant's constituents interact with nuclear factor kappa B signaling pathways to modulate the immune response. These multi-target interactions contribute to the plant's documented analgesic and anti-inflammatory properties.

    What are the herbal preparations of Jasminum lanceolaria?

    Jasminum lanceolaria is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using its dried leaves and flowers. The leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for its traditional calming properties. Floral infusions are also common, where hot water is poured over the blossoms to extract essential aromatic oils. For external applications, the plant material can be crushed into a thick poultice to soothe skin irritations. These various methods allow practitioners to utilize the plant's bioactive compounds for both internal and external therapeutic purposes.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Jasminum lanceolaria can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or localized swelling if the plant's sap comes into direct contact with human skin. While specific toxicological studies are limited, many members of the jasmine family contain compounds that may trigger hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. Contact with the leaves or stems might result in redness, itching, or a mild rash. Ingestion of the plant is generally discouraged due to the potential for gastrointestinal distress or unknown systemic effects. Users should exercise caution and wear protective gloves when handling the foliage to avoid these adverse reactions.
