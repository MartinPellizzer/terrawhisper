Syringa vulgaris

What's the taxonomical classification of Syringa vulgaris?

Syringa vulgaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Lamiales. Its taxonomic journey continues through the family Oleaceae, eventually reaching its specific classification within the genus Syringa. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its unique species designation, Syringa vulgaris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Syringa vulgaris has opposite, simple leaves that are typically ovate or heart-shaped with entire or slightly lobed margins and a smooth, glabrous texture. The foliage is often a deep green color, though some cultivars exhibit variegated patterns. This deciduous shrub produces large, terminal panicles composed of many individual four-lobed tubular flowers. These blossoms emerge in shades of purple, lilac, white, or pink and are highly fragrant. The plant's woody stems can grow quite robustly, eventually forming a dense, rounded spreading shrub.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate regions of Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa. It thrives in various habitats ranging from sunny woodland edges to more shaded riverbanks and hedgerows. Due to its popularity in ornamental gardening, it has been widely introduced to many other temperate zones across the globe. Many populations can now be found in North America and parts of South America where the climate allows it to establish itself. Its ability to adapt to different soil types has contributed to its widespread presence in many cultivated landscapes worldwide.

What are the pharmacological activities of Syringa vulgaris?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antitumoral, cytotoxic, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, which protects cells from oxidative stress and damage. The anti-inflammatory actions can reduce swelling and pain by modulating various immune responses. Research also indicates that its cytotoxic effects may inhibit the growth of certain cancer cells, contributing to its antitumoral potential. Additionally, the plant exhibits hepatoprotective qualities that assist in safeguarding liver functions against chemical or toxic insults.

According to a study published by "Naunyn-Schmiedeberg's archives of pharmacology", verbascoide can be biotechnologically produced by Syringa vulgaris plant cell cultures. This glycosylated phenylpropanoid was isolated from cultured cells of the medicinal plant Syringa vulgaris for use in an experimental mice model of spinal cord injury. In the study, mice were treated with verbascoide extract at a dose of 2 mg/kg via intraperitoneal administration at 1 and 6 h after injury. Treatment with this extract attenuated inflammatory parameters and significantly ameliorated the recovery of function evaluated by motor recovery score. These results demonstrate that verbascoide from Syringa vulgaris reduces the development of inflammation and tissue injury events associated with spinal cord trauma.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse range of bioactive secondary metabolites, including the phenylethanoid glycoside acteoside, various caffeic acid derivatives, the secoiridoid oleuropein, the glycoside syringin, and the compound verbascoide. These specific medicinal compounds are primarily responsible for the plant's documented antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of caffeic acid derivatives contributes significantly to the overall radical scavenging capacity of the extracts. Furthermore, the synergistic interaction between oleuropein and acteoside enhances the pharmacological potential of the species. Researchers often study these chemical constituents to develop new therapeutic agents for oxidative stress-related conditions.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", Syringa vulgaris L. contains significant amounts of bioactive compounds including phenyl-propanoids such as syringin, acteoside, and echinacoside. The plant also provides flavonoids like quercetin and kaempferol derivatives. Furthermore, secoiridoids identified in the extracts include secologanin, oleuropein, 10-hydroxy oleuropein, demethyloleuropein, syringalactone A, nuzhenide, and lingstroside. These chemical components were obtained from the flowers, leaves, and bark extracts. The study utilized methods such as HPLC-DAD-ESI+, GC-MS, UV-Vis, and FT-IR to characterize these medicinal compounds.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, oxidative stress, central nervous system inflammation, oxidative-stress-related neurodegenerative diseases, and tissue damage. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals that cause cellular harm. By mitigating systemic inflammation, it helps protect various bodily structures from chronic irritation. These properties are particularly effective in addressing neurodegeneration by reducing stress within the brain. Furthermore, the plant promotes healing processes that help repair significant tissue damage.

According to a study published by "Naunyn-Schmiedeberg's archives of pharmacology", Syringa vulgaris is used to produce verbascoide for treating spinal cord injury. In an experimental model involving a four-level T5-T8 laminectomy, mice were treated with a verbascoide extract at a dose of 2 mg/kg. This treatment was administered at 1 and 6 h after the injury occurred. The extract was shown to attenuate inflammatory parameters and reduce tissue injury events associated with spinal cord trauma. Additionally, the treatment significantly ameliorated recovery of function as evaluated by a motor recovery score.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach pain, and dizziness if it comes into contact with or is ingested by a person. Direct contact with the foliage or stems may lead to localized redness or an itchy rash on the skin. If swallowed, the plant often triggers significant gastrointestinal distress including intense stomach pain and frequent bouts of vomiting or diarrhea. Some individuals may also experience neurological symptoms such as dizziness following exposure. These adverse reactions underscore the importance of avoiding unnecessary handling of the species.
