Polygala tenuifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Polygala tenuifolia?

Polygala tenuifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, eventually reaching the order Fabales. Within this order, it is situated in the family Polygalaceae. The plant is further categorized under the genus Polygala, and its specific taxonomic designation is completed by the species name tenuifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Polygala tenuifolia has slender, erect stems that reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters and bear small, linear to lanceolate leaves arranged alternately along the length. The leaves are typically narrow and possess smooth margins with entire edges. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often presenting in a dense, spike-like arrangement. These flowers feature a characteristic zygomorphic structure with two larger lateral petals that resemble wings. The plant's delicate architecture and fine foliage give it a light, airy appearance in its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically distributed across China, Japan, Korea, and parts of Southeast Asia. It thrives in diverse temperate and subtropical regions where moisture levels are consistent. Within these territories, it is frequently found growing in moist soil near riverbanks, meadows, and forest edges. Its presence is most concentrated in the mountainous and hilly terrains of the eastern continental landmass. Local climates in these areas support its growth throughout various seasonal cycles.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Polygala tenuifolia contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its dried aerial parts, which consist of the leaves, stems, and roots. The rhizomes and roots are the most valued components due to their high concentration of saponins and triterpene acids. These active constituents are frequently used in traditional practices to treat respiratory ailments, inflammation, and cognitive issues. Practitioners often prepare decoctions or powders from these dried structures to harness their sedative and expectorant properties. The efficacy of the plant relies heavily on the specific extraction of these phytochemicals from the subterranean organs.

What are the pharmacological activities of Polygala tenuifolia?

This plant has antidepressant, anti-inflammatory, anxiolytic, antioxidant, and neuroprotective pharmacological activities. Its diverse chemical profile allows it to combat oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals in the body. These antioxidant properties contribute significantly to its neuroprotective capabilities, helping to safeguard brain cells from degenerative damage. Furthermore, the plant exerts calming effects on the central nervous system to reduce anxiety and alleviate symptoms of depression. By modulating inflammatory pathways, it also functions as a potent anti-inflammatory agent against various physiological stressors.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Polygala tenuifolia contain five new oligosaccharide derivatives known as polygalatenosides A-E (1-5). These substances were obtained through [(125)I]RTI-55-membrane binding assay-guided fractionation and separation of a water-soluble extract. Among these, polygalatenosides A and B (1 and 2) displayed significant inhibitory activity. Polygalatenoside A demonstrated an IC50 value of 30.0 microM, while polygalatenoside B showed an IC50 value of 6.04 microM. These two compounds act as norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors by blocking norepinephrine transport.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds onjisaponins A, onjisaponins E, onjisaponins F, onjisaponins G, and polygalatenosides A-E. These specific saponins and glycosides serve as the primary bioactive constituents within the plant's chemical profile. Researchers often study these molecules to understand their potential pharmacological properties and therapeutic applications. The presence of both onjisaponins and polygalatenoside series suggests a complex chemical structure capable of diverse biological interactions. Such detailed chemical compositions are essential for identifying the plant's medicinal value in various scientific studies.

As discussed in a study, the root of Polygala tenuifolia contains active substances identified as onjisaponins A, E, F, and G. When 10 microg of each onjisaponin was intranasally inoculated with 10 microg of influenza vaccine, serum hemagglutination-inhibiting antibody titers increased 3-14 times over control mice after 4 weeks. Using 10 microg of each onjisaponin and 10 microg of vaccine followed by vaccine alone after 3 weeks, serum antibody titers increased 27-50 fold. Two inoculations with 1 microg of onjisaponin F and 1 microg of influenza HA vaccine at 3 week intervals significantly increased antibody titers after 1 week. Furthermore, separate intranasal vaccinations with 10 microg each of onjisaponins A, E, F, and G and 10 microg of diphtheria-pertussi-tetanus vaccine showed significant increases in antibody titers. All onjisaponins showed little hemolytic activity at concentrations up to 100 microg/ml.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause gastric irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness in some individuals. These gastrointestinal issues often arise because the plant contains saponins that may irritate the lining of the digestive tract. Patients consuming the herb might experience significant stomach discomfort or upset along with changes in bowel habits. Some users have also reported feeling lightheaded or experiencing dizziness after ingestion. It is important to monitor these physical reactions closely when using this botanical.
