Toxicodendron sylvestre

What's the taxonomical classification of Toxicodendron sylvestre?

Toxicodendron sylvestre belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Sapindales and is a member of the family Anacardiaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Toxicodendron, with its specific designation being the species sylvestre.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Toxicodendron sylvestre has pinnately compound leaves featuring seven to thirteen lanceolate leaflets with serrated margins and a waxy, dark green upper surface. These leaflets are arranged oppositely along a central woody rachis that extends from the stem. The plant develops woody stems that possess a smooth, grayish bark in younger growth. Small, inconspicuous flowers typically emerge in clusters from the axillary buds. During the fruiting stage, the plant produces small, drupaceous berries that transition from green to a distinct reddish hue.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

What are the pharmacological activities of Toxicodendron sylvestre?

This plant has antibacterial and antimicrobial activities that stem from its complex chemical composition. The secondary metabolites found within Toxicodendron sylvestre allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. These bioactive compounds serve as a natural defense mechanism against bacterial colonization. Researchers study these properties to understand how the plant's extracts might combat infectious agents. Such pharmacological potential highlights the importance of investigating its chemical constituents for medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extract of Toxicodendron sylvestre can significantly inhibit Ralstonia solanacearum in vitro and in planta. One compound responsible for this antibacterial activity is methyl gallate, which was isolated from the ethyl ether extract. This compound displayed broad-spectrum activity against plant-pathogenic bacteria and strong inhibitory effects on the growth of plant-pathogenic oomycetes. Through the agar dilution method, the half inhibition concentration of methyl gallate on R. solanacearum was determined to be 8.3 mg/liter. Disease control trials in planta demonstrated that methyl gallate can effectively reduce the incidence of tomato bacterial wilt.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe allergic contact dermatitis characterized by skin rash, intense itching, redness, swelling, and inflammation. Direct contact often results in painful blistering and localized urticaria at the site of exposure. If the oils are inhaled, they may lead to significant respiratory irritation. Systemic reactions to the plant's toxins can also include nausea and dizziness. These various physiological responses highlight the significant toxicity of the species.
