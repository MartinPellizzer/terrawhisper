Erythrina corallodendron

What's the taxonomical classification of Erythrina corallodendron?

Erythrina corallodendron belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Fabales. The plant is a member of the family Fabaceae, which places it within the genus Erythrina, specifically identified by its species name, corallodendron.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Erythrina corallodendron contains bioactive compounds in its bark, leaves, and roots that are utilized for various medicinal purposes. The bark is frequently harvested to prepare decoctions used for treating inflammatory conditions and skin ailments. Extracts from the leaves are often employed in traditional medicine to manage symptoms related to fever or digestive issues. Additionally, the roots are sometimes processed to create remedies intended to soothe the nervous system. These various parts are valued in ethnobotanical practices for their diverse pharmacological potential.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Erythrina corallodendron targets GABA-A receptors, acetylcholinesterase, and various monoamine oxidase enzymes to produce its sedative and neuroprotective effects. The plant contains diverse alkaloids that act as ligands for neurotransmitter receptors in the central nervous system. By modulating the GABAergic system, these bioactive compounds induce anxiolytic and anticonvulsant activities. Additionally, the inhibition of acetylcholinesterase suggests potential applications in managing cholinergic deficits. These interactions with specific protein sites drive the plant's traditional use in treating insomnia and neurological distress.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including malaria, stomach aches, skin infections, and intestinal parasites. Local practitioners often prepare decoctions from the bark or leaves to alleviate digestive issues. The roots are sometimes employed in traditional medicine to manage symptoms of fever and inflammation. Some cultures also utilize specific extracts to address wounds or topical skin irritations. These medicinal applications vary significantly depending on the specific part of the plant used and the preparation method.

What are the herbal preparations of Erythrina corallodendron?

Erythrina corallodendron is prepared as decoctions of the bark, infusions of the leaves, and topical applications of crushed plant parts. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a potent medicinal liquid used for various systemic treatments. Leaves are often steeped in hot water to produce a tea that serves as a traditional remedy. In some local practices, the fresh leaves or sap are applied directly to the skin to address external ailments. These diverse methods allow practitioners to extract different active components depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Erythrina coralodendron can cause toxic reactions including nausea, vomiting, and dizziness if its parts are ingested. The plant contains various alkaloids that may affect the central nervous system when consumed in significant amounts. Skin contact with certain elements of the tree might lead to localized irritation or allergic dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Because the specific toxicity levels of its chemical compounds are not fully documented for humans, accidental ingestion should be treated as a medical emergency. Careful avoidance of the plant is recommended to prevent these adverse physiological responses.
