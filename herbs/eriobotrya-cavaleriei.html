Eriobotrya cavaleriei

What's the taxonomical classification of Eriobotrya cavaleriei?

Eriobotrya cavaleriei belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Rosales. As a member of the family Rosaceae, it is further categorized under the genus Eriobotrya, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species cavaleriei.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Eriobotrya cavaleriei contains medicinal properties primarily found in its leaves, fruit, and seeds. The leaves are frequently harvested to prepare decoctions or infusions used to treat respiratory ailments such as coughs and asthma. Extracts from the fruit are often utilized for their antioxidant properties and to aid in digestive health. Additionally, the seeds can be processed into oils or powders to address various inflammatory conditions. These different components are valued in traditional medicine for their diverse therapeutic applications.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Eriobotrya cavaleriei targets various enzymes and inflammatory mediators including cyclooxygenase, lipoxygenase, and tumor necrosis factor-alpha to exert its pharmacological effects. The bioactive compounds within the plant, such as ursolic acid and various flavonoids, modulate these specific protein pathways to reduce systemic inflammation. By inhibiting the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, these molecules help regulate the body's immune response. Additionally, certain extracts interact with antioxidant enzymes like superoxide dismutase to mitigate oxidative stress at a cellular level. These multi-target interactions contribute to the plant's traditional use in treating respiratory and inflammatory conditions.

    What are the therapeutic actions of Eriobotrya cavaleriei?

    Eriobotrya cavaleriei has pharmacological properties that primarily function as an antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial agent through its rich concentration of triterpenoids and flavonoids. These bioactive compounds work by neutralizing free radicals and reducing oxidative stress within cellular structures. The plant extracts also demonstrate an ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Additionally, its chemical constituents assist in modulating immune responses and soothing mucosal tissues. These combined actions make the species a subject of interest for treating respiratory ailments and skin inflammations.

    What diseases this plant treats?

    This plant is used to treat various respiratory conditions including coughs, bronchitis, and asthma. It contains bioactive compounds that help soothe irritated mucous membranes in the throat and lungs. These properties allow it to function as an effective expectorant to help clear excess phlegm. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory effects can reduce swelling in the airways during seasonal illnesses. Regular application of its extracts is often sought to alleviate the discomfort of persistent chest congestion.

    What are the herbal preparations of Eriobotrya cavaleriei?

    Eriobotrya cavaleriei is prepared as decoctions, infusions, and concentrated syrups primarily utilizing the leaves, fruits, and bark for medicinal purposes. The leaves are often boiled in water to extract bioactive compounds used in traditional cough remedies. Fruit extracts are frequently processed into syrups or juices to soothe throat irritation and manage digestive issues. Some practitioners also create topical washes or poultices from crushed plant materials to treat skin inflammation. These diverse preparations allow the plant's medicinal properties to be administered through oral or external routes.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Eriobotrya cavaleriei can cause gastrointestinal distress, allergic skin reactions, and potential toxicity if large quantities of its seeds or unripe fruit are ingested. The seeds contain cyanogenic glycosides which may release cyanide into the bloodstream upon digestion. Some individuals might experience itching, redness, or hives if they come into direct contact with the plant's sap or foliage. Excessive consumption of the fruit can lead to nausea or stomach cramps in sensitive populations. Monitoring intake is essential to avoid these adverse physiological responses.
