Eriobotrya cavaleriei

What's the taxonomical classification of Eriobotrya cavaleriei?

Eriobotrya cavaleriei belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Rosales. As a member of the family Rosaceae, it is further categorized under the genus Eriobotrya, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species cavaleriei.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eriobotrya cavaleriei has large, leathery, evergreen leaves that are elliptical to oblong in shape and feature a densely pubescent, velvety underside.

The leaf margins are typically finely serrated, while the upper surface remains relatively smooth and dark green. Its woody stems develop a rugged bark texture as the plant matures into a small tree or large shrub. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from axillary clusters, eventually giving way to fleshy, pome-like fruits.

These fruits are characterized by their distinct shape and are often covered in a fine layer of pubescence.

        What are the herbal preparations of Eriobotrya cavaleriei?

        Eriobotrya cavaleriei is prepared as decoctions, infusions, and concentrated syrups primarily utilizing the leaves, fruits, and bark for medicinal purposes. The leaves are often boiled in water to extract bioactive compounds used in traditional cough remedies. Fruit extracts are frequently processed into syrups or juices to soothe throat irritation and manage digestive issues. Some practitioners also create topical washes or poultices from crushed plant materials to treat skin inflammation. These diverse preparations allow the plant's medicinal properties to be administered through oral or external routes.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Eriobotrya cavaleriei can cause gastrointestinal distress, allergic skin reactions, and potential toxicity if large quantities of its seeds or unripe fruit are ingested. The seeds contain cyanogenic glycosides which may release cyanide into the bloodstream upon digestion. Some individuals might experience itching, redness, or hives if they come into direct contact with the plant's sap or foliage. Excessive consumption of the fruit can lead to nausea or stomach cramps in sensitive populations. Monitoring intake is essential to avoid these adverse physiological responses.
