Dogwood (Cornus florida)

Dogwood (Cornus florida) is a member of the Cornaceae family, native to Eastern United States, Southeastern United States, and Eastern Canada. Traditionally, its flowers, leaves, and bark have been used for infusions, decoctions, and poultices.

This herb is particularly valued for its tonic, astringent, and anti-inflammatory actions, and has a long history of use in native american herbal medicine, european herbal medicine, and traditional chinese medicine.

Botanical Description

Growth Habit
Perennial herbaceous plant.
Height
It typically reaches a height of 5 to 12 meters.
Leaves
Simple leaves with opposite phyllotaxy, upper surface glabrous, lower surface with prominent stomatal bands, both surfaces pale green to dark green.
Flowers
Flowers are white to pinkish with five petals arranged in a solitary cluster, exhibiting actinomorphic symmetry, and bearing red bracts that enclose the true flowers.
Stems
Stems are erect, branched in a sympodial pattern, with smooth, glabrous surfaces and nodes that are slightly swollen.
