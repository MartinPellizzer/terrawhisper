Botanical Identification of Wormwood (Artemisia cina)

To identify Wormwood (Artemisia cina) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Wormwood, or Artemisia cina, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Artemisia, and species Artemisia cina. These classifications help identify its place within the plant kingdom and its relationships to other plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Artemisia cina is the scientific name for Wormwood. It was first described by the botanist Carl von Linné, and its author citation is L. The plant has several synonyms, including Artemisia absinthium and Artemisia vulgaris, though these are often confused with other species. Common names include Wormwood, Sweet wormwood, and Southern wormwood, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Wormwood (Artemisia cina) is an upright, perennial herb with a deep taproot system. Its stems are slender, branched, and covered in fine white hairs. The leaves are silvery-green, aromatic, and have a jagged, serrated margin. The inflorescence consists of small, clustered flower heads, and the flowers are yellow, with tubular corollas. The fruit is a small, dry achene with a pappus for wind dispersal.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Wormwood (Artemisia cina) can be distinguished by its gray-green, aromatic foliage with silvery-white veins, and its small, yellow, button-like flowers. It has a strong, distinctive smell when crushed. It is often confused with other Artemisia species, but its specific leaf shape and scent are key identifiers.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Wormwood (Artemisia cina) is native to the Mediterranean region, parts of Europe, and Western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with full sun. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It is typically found at elevations between 500 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Wormwood (Artemisia cina) blooms from late summer to early autumn, typically between July and September. Seed formation occurs in late summer, with seeds maturing by early fall. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the flowering stage, usually in August and September, when the plant is most distinct and the essential oils are concentrated.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Wormwood (Artemisia cina) is closely related to other Artemisia species, sharing characteristics such as aromatic foliage and composite flowers. It belongs to the Asteraceae family, which includes plants like thyme and chamomile. Its genus relations highlight its role in the Artemisia lineage, with shared traits like silvery leaves and medicinal properties.
