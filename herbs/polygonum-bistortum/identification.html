Botanical Identification of Bistort (Polygonum bistortum)

To identify Bistort (Polygonum bistortum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Bistort, or Polygonum bistortum, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of seed plants. It is classified under the class Caryophyllopsida, order Polygonales, family Polygonaceae, genus Polygonum, and species Polygonum bistortum. This classification reflects its botanical relationships and characteristics within the plant world.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Polygonum bistortum is the scientific name for Bistort. It was first described by the botanist Carl von Linné in the 18th century. The species has several synonyms, including Filipendula ulmaria and Polygonum bistorta, which reflect historical classification changes. Common names such as Bistort, Bloodroot, and Scurvygrass are used in various regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Bistort (Polygonum bistortum) is a perennial herb with a rhizomatous root system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and can grow up to 1 meter tall. The leaves are lance-shaped, with a prominent midrib and toothed margins. The inflorescence is a dense spike of small, clustered flowers. Each flower has five petals and is typically pink or red. The fruit is a three-lobed capsule containing small, wind-dispersed seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Bistort can be distinguished by its distinctive purple-tinged stems and kidney-shaped leaves with a hairy underside. It produces small, pinkish flowers in dense clusters, and its rhizomatous growth habit sets it apart from similar Polygonum species. The presence of a red or purple stem is a key identifier, especially when compared to the more greenish stems of other species.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Bistort (Polygonum bistortum) is native to temperate regions of Europe and Asia. It thrives in moist, shady environments with cool climates. It prefers rich, loamy soils and is commonly found in meadows, woodlands, and stream banks. It typically grows between 500 and 1,500 meters in elevation.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Bistort (Polygonum bistortum) blooms from June to August. Seed formation occurs from July to September. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested in early summer when the leaves are most distinct and the stems are sturdy.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Polygonum bistortum is closely related to other Polygonum species, sharing key morphological and genetic traits. It belongs to the Polygonaceae family, which is characterized by distinctive leaf shapes and specialized reproductive structures. Its closest relatives include other bistort species, differing mainly in habitat and geographic distribution.
