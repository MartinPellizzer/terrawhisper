Carallia diplopetala

What's the taxonomical classification of Carallia diplopetala?

Carallia diplopetala belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows a specific evolutionary lineage that places it under the order Malpighiales. Within this order, the plant is further categorized into the family Rhizophoraceae, which leads to its placement in the genus Caralliia. Ultimately, the specific identification of this organism is defined by its species name, diplopepala.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

These secondary metabolites interact with the cyclooxygenase-2 enzyme to inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, which serves to reduce systemic inflammation. Additionally, its bioactive compounds act on superoxide dismutase and catalase to neutralize reactive oxygen species within cells. This multifaceted interaction with protein signaling pathways underpins the plant's reported analgesic and anti-inflammatory properties.

Caralliia diplopetala has antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties that are utilized to treat various infections and oxidative stress. What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat inflammatory conditions, skin infections, intestinal parasites, and digestive ailments. The leaves and roots are often prepared as decoctions to combat various bacterial and fungal pathogens. Its medicinal properties are frequently utilized in traditional practices to soothe gastrointestinal distress and reduce swelling. Local healers also apply topical preparations to manage dermatological issues and wound healing. These diverse applications highlight the plant's significant role in folk medicine for managing infectious and inflammatory processes.

What are the herbal preparations of Carallia diplopetala?

Caralliia diplopetala is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using the dried leaves and roots of the plant. The decoction is typically made by boiling the woody parts in water to extract concentrated alkaloids. Infusions are often created by steeping the softer leaves in hot water for milder therapeutic effects. For external applications, the plant material is ground into a fine powder and mixed with a liquid to form a paste for skin ailments. These various methods allow for both internal consumption and direct application to the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Caralliia diplopetala can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and localized swelling upon direct contact with the plant's tissues. The presence of specific secondary metabolites may trigger inflammatory responses in sensitive individuals. Some users might experience redness or itching if the sap touches exposed skin. Prolonged exposure to the plant parts could potentially lead to more severe dermatological reactions. It is important to handle this species with caution to avoid these adverse effects.
