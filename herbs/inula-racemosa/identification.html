To identify Racemose Inula (Inula racemosa) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Inula racemosa is the scientific name for Racemose Inula. It was first described by the botanist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck in 1786. This species has several synonyms, including Inula racemosa var. lutea and Inula racemosa subsp. lutea. It is also known by various common names such as yellow gumweed and raceme inula.

Morphological Characteristics

Racemosa Inula is a herbaceous perennial with a branched root system. It has erect, somewhat hairy stems that are often branched. Its leaves are alternate, lanceolate, and have toothed margins. The inflorescence is a tall, branched raceme of yellow flowers. Each flower has five petals and numerous stamens. The fruit is a cylindrical achene with a pappus for wind dispersal.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Racemose Inula can be distinguished by its tall, branched stem with yellow-green leaves and large, showy yellow flower heads. It differs from similar species like Inula helenium by its more upright growth and less dense, racemose inflorescence. The leaves are also more lance-shaped and less hairy.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Racemose Inula is native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia, particularly in countries like Afghanistan and Pakistan. It thrives in temperate climates with cold winters and warm summers. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils and is commonly found at elevations between 1,500 and 3,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Racemosa Inula blooms from June to August, producing yellow flowers in raceme clusters. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the most reliable harvest time is during the flowering period, when the plant's characteristics are most distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Inula racemosa is closely related to species like Inula helenium and Inula crassifolia. It belongs to the genus Inula, which is part of the Asteraceae family. The family traits include composite flowers and alternate leaves, which are shared with its closest relatives.
