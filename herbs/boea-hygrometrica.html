Boea hygrometrica

What's the taxonomical classification of Boea hygrometrica?

Boea hygrometrica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Lamiales and the family Gesneriaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Boea, and its species designation, hygrometrica.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, intestinal parasites, and various types of inflammation. The crushed leaves are often applied topically as a poultice to soothe skin irritations and promote healing in open sores. Some traditional practices involve using the plant to address internal ailments such as stomach discomfort or parasitic infestations. Its medicinal properties are primarily harnessed through direct contact with the skin or controlled ingestion. Local healers rely on these specific applications to manage diverse minor medical conditions.

What are the herbal preparations of Boea hygrometrica?

Boea hygrometrica is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice to treat various ailments. The leaves are often boiled in water to create a liquid extract used for internal consumption. This decoction serves as a traditional remedy for digestive issues or feverish conditions. When applied externally, the crushed fresh leaves are formed into a paste to soothe skin irritations. These diverse methods allow the plant's active compounds to be utilized for both systemic and localized healing.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Boea hygrometrica can cause skin irritation, redness, and swelling if the plant's sap comes into direct contact with human skin. This plant contains various chemical compounds that act as irritants to sensitive tissues. Contact with the eyes may result in severe inflammation or discomfort. Additionally, accidental ingestion of any part of the plant can lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or vomiting. It is always recommended to wear gloves when handling this species to prevent these adverse reactions.
