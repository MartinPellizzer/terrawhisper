What's the taxonomical classification of Carpesium faberi?

Carpesium faberi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a representative of the family Asteraceae, and its specific taxonomic designation is defined by the genus Carpesium and the species faberi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Carpesium faberi has an upright, herbaceous stem structure characterized by elongated, lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and a prominent central vein. The foliage displays a deep green pigmentation and a slightly textured surface due to fine pubescence. Its reproductive organs consist of small, actinomorphic flowers that emerge from the axillary nodes along the primary stem. The root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing for efficient nutrient uptake in its native soil environments. These morphological traits collectively enable the plant to maximize sunlight absorption and maintain structural stability.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse group of bioactive sesquiterpene lactones known as carpespene A through carpespene J. These specific medicinal compounds are primarily concentrated within the plant's chemical profile. Carpespene A serves as a foundational member of this chemical series. The entire group of carpespenes A-J exhibits unique structural variations that contribute to their biological activity. Researchers study these specific molecules to understand their potential therapeutic applications in modern medicine.

As discussed in a study, the whole plants of Carpesium faberi contain ten previously undescribed sesquiterpenes known as carpespene A-J (1-10) and eight known compounds (11-18). One specific compound, carpespene A (1), is a eudesmanolide-type sesquiterpene lactone characterized by an open five membered ring involving C-2 and C-3. This compound 1 showed significant cytotoxic effects against four cancer cell lines with IC50 values ranging from 8.20 to 18.45 μM. Mechanistically, compound 1 induces apoptosis in HepG2 cells by triggering excessive ROS accumulation. While ROS accumulation induces cytoprotective autophagy that impairs the cytotoxicity of compound 1, simultaneous autophagy inhibition augments its cytotoxic effects on HepG2 cells.
