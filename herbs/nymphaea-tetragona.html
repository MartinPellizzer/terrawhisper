Nymphaea tetragona

What's the taxonomical classification of Nymphaea tetragona?

Nymphaea tetragona belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is placed in the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Nymphaeales. This aquatic plant is a member of the family Nymphaeaceae and is situated within the genus Nymphaea, specifically identified as the species tetragona.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Nymphaea tetragona contains diverse bioactive alkaloids, flavonoids, and terpenoids that serve as the foundation for its medicinal properties. What are the therapeutic actions of Nymphaea tetragona?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, digestive issues, inflammatory conditions, and certain types of fever. The extracts from the leaves and roots are frequently applied topically to soothe irritations and promote healing of wounds. When ingested in controlled doses, it is believed to help regulate bodily functions and reduce internal swelling. Researchers are currently studying its specific biochemical properties to better understand its impact on bacterial and fungal pathogens. This traditional medicinal use continues to be a subject of interest in ethnobotanical studies.

What are the herbal preparations of Nymphaea tetragona?

Nymphaea tetragonas is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice using the plant's various parts. The roots and rhizomes are frequently boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid medicine for internal use. Flower extracts can also be steeped in alcohol to produce tinctures that preserve the plant's active compounds. For external applications, the leaves are often crushed into a paste to soothe skin irritations. These different methods allow practitioners to tailor the potency and delivery of the plant's medicinal properties.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Nymphaea tetragona can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled without protection. The plant contains specific alkaloids and chemical compounds that may trigger localized redness or itching upon contact with sensitive skin. If any part of the plant is consumed, individuals might experience nausea, stomach cramping, or more severe digestive disruptions. Detailed toxicological studies are limited, so caution is necessary when interacting with its foliage or sap. It is generally advised to avoid direct contact to prevent unpredictable systemic or topical reactions.
