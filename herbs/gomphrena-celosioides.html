Gomphrena celosioides

What's the taxonomical classification of Gomphrena celosioides?

Gomphrena celosioides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls into the order Caryophyllales. This plant is a member of the family Amaranthaceae, specifically within the genus Gomphrena, and is identified by the unique species name celosioides.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gomphrena celosioides has erect, branched stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 100 centimeters and support opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is characterized by a smooth texture and a light green coloration that provides a backdrop for the inflorescence. Its flowers are small and clustered into dense, globular heads that transition from pink to deep magenta as they mature. These colorful flower heads are subtended by papery, bract-like structures that give the blooms a distinctive, everlasting appearance. The entire plant structure is adapted to thrive in warm, sunny environments through its sturdy, herbaceous growth habit.

As discussed in a study, Gomphrena celosioides is a medicinal plant used as a natural analgesic. The ethanolic extract from the aerial parts of Gomphrena celosioides (EEGC) was investigated through acute and subacute toxicity models in rodents. In the acute toxicity test, 2000 mg/kg of EEGC was administered orally to male and female Wistar rats. Swiss mice received 75, 150, or 300 mg/kg of EEGC during the subacute toxicity test. Animals treated with an only dose of 2000 mg/kg EEGC showed no clinical signs of toxicity, meaning the LD50 is higher than this dose. According to the Globally Harmonized System of classification, the EEGC dosages can be in Category 5, which is the least toxic or non-toxic one.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds aurantiamide and aurantiamide acetate. These bioactive substances are primarily classified as coumarins and are known for their distinct chemical structures. Researchers study these compounds because they often demonstrate significant biological activities in various pharmacological models. The presence of both the parent compound and its acetate derivative contributes to the complex chemical profile of the species. Understanding these constituents helps scientists evaluate the plant's potential for developing new therapeutic treatments.

As discussed in a study, Gomphrena celosioides is a weed grown in lawns whose biological activity of the extract had earlier been established. The plant was collected, air dried, ground and soxhlet extracted with n-hexane to isolate specific compounds. Column chromatography using 5% chloroform in n-hexane effected the separation of these substances. Through spectroscopic analysis using IR, NMR (1H and 13C) and EI-MS, two compounds were identified. These isolated compounds were found to be aurantiamide and aurantiamide acetate. This study represents the first report of the isolation of these compounds in Gomphrena celosioides.
