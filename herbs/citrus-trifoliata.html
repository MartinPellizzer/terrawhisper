Citrus trifoliata

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrus trifoliata?

Citrus trifoliata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Sapindales. The plant is further categorized into the family Rutaceae, placing it within the genus Citrus, and is specifically identified by the species name trifoliata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Citrus trifoliata has trifoliate leaves consisting of three distinct elliptical leaflets that are dark green, glossy, and arranged on a woody stem. The leaflets possess smooth margins and a slightly leathery texture characteristic of many citrus species. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the axils of the branches to eventually develop into small, rounded fruits. These fruits often exhibit a bright color when ripe and contain acidic juice. The overall structure of the plant includes a spreading canopy with a branched woody framework.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including coumarins, furocoumarins, imperatoriin, limonin, and various limonoids. The presence of coumarins provides significant pharmacological potential for diverse therapeutic applications. Within this chemical profile, the furocoumarins and the specific compound imperatoriin contribute to the plant's unique biological activities. Additionally, the limonin and other limonoids serve as key bioactive constituents that influence its medicinal properties. These secondary metabolites work together to define the plant's overall chemical complexity.

As discussed in a study, Citrus trifoliata is a medicinal plant containing characteristic secondary metabolites such as furocoumarins and limonoids. From the peel and seeds, six pure compounds were isolated from the peel, including phelopecterin (2), scoparone (3), myrsellin (4), triphasiol (6), umbelliferone (7), and citropten (8). The seeds yielded four compounds consisting of imperatorin (1), auraptene (5), limonin (9), and deacetyl nomilin (10). These substances are classified as furocoumarin (1, 2), coumarin (3-8), and limonoid derivatives (9, 10). Regarding bioactivity, the furocoumarins (1-2) showed moderate activity on the human colorectal adenocarcinoma tumor cell line COLO 320. Additionally, compound 2 demonstrated remarkable P-glycoprotein inhibitory activity, while compound 5 showed significant activity on the COLO 320 cell line in antiproliferative assays and P-glycoprotein inhibitory activity.
