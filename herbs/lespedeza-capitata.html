What to know about Lespedeza capitata (bush clover) before using it medicinally

Lespedeza capitata, commonly known as bush clover, is a herb that has been prized for its health benefits, which include reducing inflammation, improving cardiovascular health, and acting as an antioxidant.

It is a low-maintenance plant that is easy to grow and requires minimal care, making it a popular choice for gardeners and landscapers. From a botanical standpoint, Lespedeza capitata is a member of the Fabaceae family and is characterized by its clusters of small, purple flowers and its trifoliate leaves.

In traditional Chinese medicine, bush clover has been used for centuries to treat a range of ailments, including digestive problems and fever.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Lespedeza capitata.

What are the medicinal properties of Lespedeza capitata?

Lespedeza capitata helps with anxiety, insomnia, and menopausal symptoms. It is used to reduce stress, promote relaxation, and balance the body's energy. Its anti-inflammatory properties also alleviate menstrual cramps and other pain-related conditions.

Its active constituents, such as isoflavones and flavonoids, give it its medicinal properties. These compounds have been shown to have estrogen-like effects, which contribute to its ability to alleviate menopausal symptoms. Isoflavones have also been found to have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

The aerial parts of the plant, particularly the leaves and flowers, are most used for medicinal purposes. They are commonly used to make teas, infusions, and tinctures, which are taken internally to promote relaxation and reduce anxiety. The roots are also used in some traditional medicine practices.

When used improperly, Lespedeza capitata may cause gastrointestinal side effects, such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. It may also interact with certain medications, including blood thinners and hormone replacement therapy medications. Excessive consumption may lead to estrogen dominance.

Precautions when using Lespedeza capitata medicinally include consulting with a healthcare professional before use, especially for women with estrogen-sensitive conditions. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid using it. Also, individuals with kidney or liver disease should use it under medical supervision.

What are the horticulural aspects of Lespedeza capitata?

Lespedeza capitata grow in full sun to partial shade and thrive in well-drained soils with a pH range of 6.0 to 7.0. It tolerates a wide range of soils but prefers slightly acidic to neutral soils. Bush clover can grow in USDA zones 4-9.

To plant bush clover, choose a location with good air circulation, as it is susceptible to root rot. Plant seedlings 12-18 inches apart in the spring or fall, and water thoroughly after planting. Fertilize lightly in the spring with a balanced fertilizer. Prune established plants in late winter or early spring to maintain shape and promote new growth.

Bush clover flowers from mid-summer to early fall, with a peak bloom period of 6-8 weeks. Harvest the flowers in the morning, when they are fully open and still wet with dew. Use scissors or pinch off individual flowers to collect the seed pods. Dry the seed pods on a paper bag or in a warm, dry place.

Common pests that affect bush clover include Japanese beetles, aphids, and spider mites. Common diseases include powdery mildew, leaf spot, and root rot. Regularly inspect plants for signs of pests and diseases, and apply integrated pest management techniques as necessary to maintain healthy plants.
