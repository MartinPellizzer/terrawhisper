Cercis siliquastrum

What's the taxonomical classification of Cercis siliquastrum?

Cercis siliquastrum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Fabales. The plant is a member of the family Fabaceae, which places it within the genus Cercis, ultimately defining its specific biological identity as the species siliquastrum.

Cercis siliquastrum has demonstrated therapeutic potential through its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties derived from its bioactive phytochemicals. What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions, skin irritations, and gastrointestinal disturbances through its medicinal properties. Some traditional practices utilize the plant to address internal issues like stomach aches or digestive discomfort. The phytochemical constituents are believed to provide mild antiseptic and anti-inflammatory effects. However, clinical research is limited regarding its specific efficacy in modern medicine.

What are the herbal preparations of Cercis siliquastrum?

Cercis siliquastrumb is prepared as decoctions, infusions, tinctures, and topical poultices using its bark, flowers, and leaves. The bark is often simmered in water to create a concentrated decoction used for its astringent properties. Floral infusions are frequently brewed to utilize the mild therapeutic benefits of the blossoms in teas. Tinctures made from the plant parts allow for long-term storage and more potent extraction of active compounds. Additionally, crushed leaves or bark can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to soothe local irritations.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cercis siliquastrum can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or digestive upset if ingested by humans or animals. While the plant is widely used for ornamental purposes, some individuals may experience redness or itching upon direct contact with the bark or foliage. There is limited clinical data regarding the systemic toxicity of the species, but caution is advised when handling the plant in large quantities. Ingestion of the various plant parts might lead to nausea or stomach discomfort in sensitive subjects. Always consult a medical professional if an adverse reaction occurs after exposure.
