Scientific and Botanical Profile

Iberis Amara, with botanical name iberis amara, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Brassicaceae, commonly known as the mustard family.

It is also referred to by various common names such as Bitter Iberis, Candytuft, Bitter Candytuft, White Candytuft, Celandine, Cocklebur, Caper Spurge, Hedge Celandine, White Caper, Common Iberis, Wild Celandine, Black Caper, Bittercress, and Goat's Beard. Native to Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, this species thrives in disturbed soils and along roadsides. Morphologically, it is a low-growing annual or biennial herb with upright, branched stems, and small, white or pale yellow flowers arranged in clusters.

Its leaves are pinnately divided, and the plant produces small, capsule-like fruits that contain several seeds.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Iberis amara has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh plant, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. For topical application, it can be diluted with a carrier oil or used in compresses to treat skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried herb per cup of water, taken up to three times daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision.

Due to its potency, it is advised to use iberis amara sparingly and for short durations, typically no longer than two weeks, unless guided by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Iberis amara can be used with caution due to its potential toxicity, particularly when consumed in high doses or over prolonged periods.

It may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, and in more severe cases, it can lead to liver damage or even liver failure. There are limited studies on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when using it alongside medications that affect the liver or have anticoagulant properties. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid iberis amara due to the risk of harm to the fetus or infant, and individuals with chronic liver disease or other serious illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, it is recommended to use iberis amara only under the guidance of a qualified healthcare professional, start with low doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Iberis amara grows in well-drained, loamy soils that are slightly alkaline to neutral, in full sun to partial shade, and requires moderate water during the growing season.

It thrives in temperate climates and benefits from regular watering, especially during dry spells, but should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. To ensure healthy growth, it should be planted in early spring, with regular weeding and occasional fertilization to promote robust foliage and flowering. The best time to harvest Iberis amara is during the late spring to early summer when the leaves are fully developed and the plant is at its peak potency, using sharp scissors to cut the stems and leaves without damaging the plant.

After harvesting, the plant material should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to preserve its medicinal potency.
