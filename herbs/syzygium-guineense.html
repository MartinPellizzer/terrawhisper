Syzygium guineense

What's the taxonomical classification of Syzygium guineense?

Syzygium guineense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical classification, the plant is placed in the order Myrtales and the family Myrtaceae. Finally, it is situated within the genus Syzygium, with its specific designation being the species guineense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Syzygium guineense has an evergreen habit characterized by simple, opposite, elliptical to oblong leaves that measure between 5 and 15 centimeters in length and possess an entire margin and an acute apex. The leaf texture is typically coriaceous, meaning it feels leathery to the touch, and the surfaces often exhibit a glossy appearance. The stems are woody and can develop a smooth, grayish bark as the plant matures into a large shrub or small tree. Small, white, fragrant flowers emerge in terminal or axillary clusters, containing numerous prominent stamens. Following pollination, the plant produces small, fleshy berries that transition from green to a dark purple or black color when ripe.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of West and Central Africa, spanning from Senegal and Guinea eastward through Nigeria and Cameroon to the Democratic Republic of the Congo. It primarily inhabits lowland rainforests, gallery forests, and swampy areas where moisture is abundant. The species is frequently found growing along riverbanks and in moist thickets within these humid biomes. Its distribution is closely tied to the availability of high rainfall and dense canopy cover characteristic of the Guineo-Congolian forest zone. Because it thrives in such specific environments, its presence is a key indicator of tropical riverine and forest ecosystems across the continent.

What are the pharmacological activities of Syzygium guineense?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, anti-diabetic, and antioxidant pharmacological activities that contribute to its medicinal importance. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize harmful free radicals within the body to prevent cellular damage. The presence of bioactive compounds also allows it to act as an effective antibacterial agent against various pathogens. Additionally, the plant demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects by reducing pain and swelling. It further shows promise in managing blood glucose levels due to its documented anti-diabetic capabilities.

As discussed in a study, Syzygium guineense is traditionally used by 44 healers in four different districts in Mali to treat dermatosis, pain, malaria/fever, wounds, inflammation, stomach problems, and various female disorders. The methanol extract of its leaves demonstrated activity as a radical scavenger and as an inhibitor of xanthine oxidase and 15-lipoxygenase, while remaining non-toxic to Artemia salina nauplii. Among the ten newly identified polyphenols, pentagalloylglucose was the best enzyme inhibitor with an IC50 of 25±4μM for 15-lipoxygenase and 8±1μM for xanthine oxidase. The highest radical scavenging activities were shown by casuarictin with an IC50 of 3.9±0.1μM, casuarinin with an IC50 of 4.5±0.3μM, and pentagalloylglucose with an IC50 of 5±1μM. These findings provide a rationale for the widespread medicinal usage of the plant.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds betulinic acid, oleanolic acid, flavonoids, terpenoids, and tannins. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile and therapeutic potential. Betulinic acid and oleanolic acid are known for their significant anti-inflammatory and antitumor properties. Furthermore, the presence of flavonoids and terpenoids provides essential antioxidant and antimicrobial activities. Tannins also play a crucial role by offering astringent effects that assist in wound healing and tissue protection.

As discussed in a study, column separation of the Syzygium guineense extract yielded 14 fractions. Three of these fractions produced significant (p<0.001) inhibition of small intestinal propulsion. Sub-fraction 7 contained betulinic acid, ursolic acid, and oleanolic acid, while sub-fraction 16 contained a mixture of luteolin and friedelane-type triterpene compounds. These sub-fractions, along with sub-fraction 1, were obtained from an active column fraction and exhibited relaxant effects on isolated rabbit jejunum. The study also compared the extract's inhibitory effect to atropine at a dose of 0.3 mg/kg.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating malaria, asthma, coughs, diarrhea, and various stomach problems. The medicinal properties of its leaves and bark help alleviate respiratory distress associated with asthma and persistent coughs. It is also frequently utilized in traditional medicine to manage intestinal issues like diarrhea and general stomach problems. Furthermore, its bioactive compounds are applied in efforts to combat the symptoms and effects of malaria. These diverse applications highlight the significant role the species plays in local healthcare practices.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

What side effects this plant can have?

It is important to monitor for any signs of swelling or respiratory changes after exposure.
