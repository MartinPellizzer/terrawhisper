Euphorbia sapinii

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia sapinii has upright, unbranched stems that reach heights of up to 50 centimeters and are topped by terminal clusters of small, yellowish-green flowers called cyathia. The leaves are lanceolate to linear in shape, possessing smooth margins and an alternating arrangement along the slender stalks. These leaves are typically bright green and can vary in size depending on their position on the plant. During the flowering period, the plant displays a characteristic yellowish hue from its specialized bracts. The overall structure is herbaceous and relatively delicate compared to other members of the Euphorbia genus.

      What are the herbal preparations of Euphorbia sapinii?

      Euphorbia sapinii is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical application of the latex or crushed plant material. The milky sap, known as latex, is often applied directly to the skin for localized medicinal purposes. In traditional practices, the roots or stems may be boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction for internal use.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Euphorbia sapinii can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and eye damage due to the caustic milky latex it produces upon being broken. When this sap comes into contact with human skin, it often results in redness, swelling, and painful chemical burns. If the fluid enters the eyes, it can lead to intense inflammation or even temporary blindness. Accidental ingestion of the plant is also dangerous and can cause significant irritation to the mouth, throat, and digestive tract. Protective gear should always be worn when handling this species to prevent accidental exposure to these toxic compounds.
