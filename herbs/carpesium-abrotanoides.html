Carpesium abrotanoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Carpesium abrotanoides?

Carpesium abrotanoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Asterales. As a member of the family Asteraceae, it is placed within the genus Carpesium, with its specific classification being the species abrotanoides.

What are the therapeutic actions of Carpesium abrotanoides?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat gastrointestinal issues such as stomach aches, intestinal spasms, and digestive irregularities. Its components act as carminatives to help reduce bloating and expel gas from the digestive tract. It is also frequently utilized to alleviate menstrual cramps by relaxing smooth muscle tissue. The plant possesses mild sedative properties that can help soothe nervous tension associated with physical discomfort. Practitioners often employ its extracts to manage symptoms of mild indigestion and colic.

What are the herbal preparations of Carpesium abrotanoides?

Carpesium abrotanoides is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture to extract its active phytochemical constituents. The dried fruit is often boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction used in traditional medicinal applications. Alternatively, soaking the plant material in alcohol allows for the production of a potent tincture that offers longer shelf stability. These liquid preparations are frequently used to facilitate the absorption of the plant's specific chemical properties. Practitioners may also utilize these forms to standardize dosages for specific therapeutic purposes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Carpesium abrotanoides can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including intense abdominal cramping, nausea, vomiting, and violent diarrhea if ingested. These symptoms often arise because the plant contains potent purgative compounds that irritate the lining of the digestive tract. Excessive consumption may lead to significant dehydration and electrolyte imbalances due to the rapid loss of fluids. In some cases, the toxic effects can manifest as systemic irritation or more serious metabolic disturbances. Monitoring for these reactions is crucial when dealing with any part of the plant.
