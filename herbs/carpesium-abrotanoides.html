Carpesium abrotanoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Asterales. As a member of the family Asteraceae, it is placed within the genus Carpesium, with its specific classification being the species abrotanoides.

According to a study published by "Journal of food science", the extract from the roots of Carpesium abrotanoides L. (PCA) exerts anti-breast cancer activity by inhibiting the expression of glycolysis-related genes like glucose transporter 1, lactate dehydrogenase A, and hexokinase 2. The PCA was shown to downregulate the expression of pyruvate kinase M2 and alter its cellular translocation. Furthermore, PCA acts as an inhibitor of the PKM2/hypoxia-inducible factor-1α axis. These findings demonstrate that PCA has a noticeable anticancer effect against breast cancer in vitro by regulating glucose metabolism and PKM2 expression. This indicates that PCA is a promising agent for the development of functional food containing Carpesium abrotanoides L. root extracts.

Detailed analysis of these molecules helps scientists map the complex chemical profile of the organism.

According to a study published by "RSC advances", the medicinal plant Carpesium abrotanoides contains a novel meroterpenoid known as Carpesabrolide A (1). This compound features an unprecedented fumaric acid-guaiane sesquiterpenoid hybrid structure. The absolute configuration of this isolated structure was established using spectroscopic methods and single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. Carpesabrolide A (1) demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting NO production. This inhibitory effect was measured with an IC50 value of 2.7 μM.
