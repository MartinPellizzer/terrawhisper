Bombax buonopozense

What's the taxonomical classification of Bombax buonopozense?

Bombax buonopozense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is part of the order Malvales and the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Bombax, with its specific designation being the species buonopozense.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Bombax buonopozense has therapeutic properties used primarily to treat gastrointestinal issues, inflammation, and skin conditions through its various plant parts. What are the herbal preparations of Bombax buonopozense?

Bombax buonopozense is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or poultices using various parts of the plant such as the bark, roots, and flowers. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal liquid used for treating internal ailments. Flower extracts are sometimes infused into liquids to harness their specific properties for consumption. For external wounds or skin irritations, the crushed material from the plant is applied directly as a topical poultice. These diverse preparation methods allow practitioners to utilize the different chemical components found within each specific anatomical part of the tree.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Bombax buonopozense can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, or allergic reactions if ingested or applied improperly to the body. While many parts of this tree are used in traditional medicine, the concentration of bioactive compounds can lead to unpredictable physiological responses. Some individuals may experience localized redness or itching upon contact with the plant's sap or latex. Excessive consumption of preparations derived from its bark or flowers might result in nausea or stomach cramping. Because scientific clinical trials are limited, the full spectrum of potential toxicity remains not entirely understood by modern medicine.
