Dillenia indica

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dillenia indica has large, leathery, obovate leaves that measure up to 30 centimeters in length and are characterized by prominent, parallel secondary veins. The plant grows as a medium to large tree with spreading branches and a thick trunk. Its flowers are showy and consist of numerous white petals arranged around a central cluster of numerous stamens. Following pollination, the plant develops a distinctive large, fleshy, and many-seeded fruit that is often referred to as the elephant apple. The bark is typically greyish and can become deeply fissured as the tree matures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically occurring in countries such as India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of Indonesia. It typically thrives in moist, lowland tropical forests and along the banks of streams or rivers where humidity is high. Within its natural range, it is most frequently found in the evergreen and semi-evergreen forest ecosystems of the Indian subcontinent. The species prefers well-drained, nutrient-rich soils that are characteristic of these warm, wet environments. Because of these specific climatic requirements, its distribution is strictly limited to the equatorial and subtropical zones of the region.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Dillenia indica targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) and lipoxygenase (LOX), along with various pro-inflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). These interactions are primarily driven by bioactive phytochemicals including tannins, flavonoids, and phenolic acids found within the plant's extracts. By inhibiting the COX-2 pathway, the plant effectively reduces the synthesis of prostaglandins that trigger pain and swelling. Furthermore, its ability to modulate cytokine expression suggests a significant role in regulating the systemic immune response. These multi-target protein interactions collectively underpin the plant's documented anti-inflammatory and antioxidant medicinal properties.

Ingestion of large amounts could potentially lead to stomach upset or nausea due to the concentration of certain phytochemicals.
