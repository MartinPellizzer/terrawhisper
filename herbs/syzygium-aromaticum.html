Cloves (Syzygium aromaticum): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Cloves, derived from the dried flower buds of the Syzygium aromaticum tree, are a widely used spice and herbal remedy known for their potent medicinal properties and historical significance in traditional medicine.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Cloves (Syzygium Aromaticum)?

Cloves (Syzygium aromaticum) are the dried flower buds of a tropical evergreen tree native to the Maluku Islands in Indonesia, widely used in traditional herbal medicine for their potent aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cloves is Syzygium aromaticum. This plant belongs to the family Myrtaceae, genus Syzygium, and species Syzygium aromaticum, within the order Sapindales, class Eudicots, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. Commonly known as cloves in the Mediterranean region, it is referred to as kari in parts of India and Indonesia. Synonyms include botanical names like Eugenia aromatica and regional names such as cinnamomum in some parts of Southeast Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cloves is an evergreen tree that typically grows to a height of 8-12 meters with a broad, spreading crown.

Its leaves are alternate, elliptical, and glossy dark green, measuring approximately 7-15 centimeters in length. The flowers are small, fragrant, and white or pale pink, arranged in clusters at the ends of branches. The root system is shallow and fibrous, allowing the plant to absorb nutrients from the upper soil layers.

The dried flower buds, known as cloves, are dark brown, have a rough texture, and emit a strong, aromatic scent.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cloves is native to the Maluku Islands, also known as the Spice Islands, in Indonesia.

The plant thrives in tropical climates with high humidity and warm temperatures. It is primarily found in regions with well-drained, fertile soils and ample sunlight. The tree prefers a humid climate with an average annual rainfall of 1500-2000 mm.

Its natural distribution extends to parts of Madagascar, India, and the coastal areas of eastern Africa.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of cloves primarily involves the dried flower buds, known as clove buds, which are the most commonly utilized part.

These buds contain high concentrations of essential oils, including eugenol, which contribute to their therapeutic properties. The inner bark and twigs are also occasionally used, though less frequently than the buds. In traditional medicine, the entire plant may be employed in certain preparations.

The active compounds found in these parts are responsible for the plant's antiseptic, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory effects.

Phytochemical Composition

Cloves contain a variety of active compounds, including eugenol, which is responsible for their aromatic properties and therapeutic effects.

Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids and phenolic acids contribute to the plant's antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Chemical markers like cineole and camphor are also present and play roles in the pharmacological profile of clove. These compounds work synergistically to provide the medicinal benefits associated with clove in herbal medicine.

The phytochemical composition of clove is essential for understanding its traditional and modern applications.

Pharmacological Properties

Cloves (Syzygium aromaticum) possess a range of pharmacological properties that make them valuable in herbal medicine.

Therapeutic actions of cloves include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects, which are attributed to their rich composition of essential oils and flavonoids. Biologically, clove extracts demonstrate activity against various pathogens, including bacteria and fungi, due to the presence of eugenol and other phytochemicals. Pharmacodynamically, the active compounds in cloves interact with cellular receptors and enzymes to modulate inflammatory responses and pain signaling pathways.

System-level effects of clove consumption may include gastrointestinal protection, enhanced immune function, and potential anti-cancer properties.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of cloves involve multiple biological processes that contribute to their therapeutic effects.

At the cellular level, clove compounds such as eugenol and alpha-humulene interact with cell membranes, modulating ion channels and receptor activity. These interactions can lead to anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects by inhibiting pro-inflammatory signaling pathways. Biochemically, clove components inhibit enzymes like cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase, which are key in the production of inflammatory mediators.

Target systems include the nervous system, where clove compounds exhibit neuroprotective properties by reducing oxidative stress and modulating neurotransmitter activity.

Therapeutic Uses

Cloves, derived from the dried flower buds of Syzygium aromaticum, are widely used in herbal medicine for their therapeutic properties.

They are particularly effective in treating dental pain and oral infections due to their high concentration of eugenol, a natural analgesic and antiseptic. Cloves are also used to alleviate digestive issues such as bloating, indigestion, and nausea, thanks to their carminative effects. Additionally, they have been traditionally employed to manage respiratory conditions like coughs and colds by acting as an expectorant and decongestant.

This medicinal plant is also known to support immune function and may help in reducing inflammation and pain in various ailments.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Preparation methods for cloves involve various forms in which the plant is utilized.

The most common forms include whole dried flower buds, ground powder, and essential oil. Whole cloves are often used whole in cooking or herbal remedies. Ground clove powder is widely used in spice blends and medicinal formulations.

Essential oil extracted from cloves is applied topically or in aromatherapy.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of cloves (Syzygium aromaticum) for general use in herbal medicine is typically 0.3 to 1 gram per day, depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

Standard doses are often prepared as a powder or tincture, with the powder form being the most commonly used. Extracts should be diluted in a carrier oil or liquid to avoid irritation, as concentrated forms can be harsh on the digestive system. Cloves should be taken no more than 2 to 3 times per day, with at least 4 hours between doses to minimize gastrointestinal discomfort.

The duration of use should not exceed 7 to 14 days without consulting a healthcare professional to prevent potential toxicity or adverse interactions.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Cloves are generally safe when used in recommended amounts, but they can cause side effects such as irritation, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Overconsumption may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, including stomach pain and vomiting. Individuals with known allergies to plants in the Myrtaceae family should avoid clove use. Cloves are contraindicated in people with bleeding disorders due to their potential to increase bleeding risk.

During pregnancy, clove use should be avoided, especially in large quantities, as it may pose risks to fetal development.

Drug Interactions

Cloves may interact with certain pharmaceuticals and supplements, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.

When taken with anticoagulant medications like warfarin, cloves may enhance their blood-thinning effects, leading to an increased risk of bleeding. Cloves can also interact with antiplatelet drugs such as aspirin or clopidogrel, further affecting blood clotting mechanisms. Some supplements, including garlic or ginkgo biloba, may have additive effects when combined with cloves, increasing the likelihood of gastrointestinal or bleeding-related complications.

It is important to consult a healthcare provider before using cloves alongside any medications or supplements.

Toxicity and Precautions

When used in typical therapeutic doses, cloves are generally safe, but toxicity can occur with excessive consumption.

The toxic dose for adults is considered to be around 2 to 3 grams per day, though this can vary based on individual sensitivity. Long-term safety studies on clove use are limited, but prolonged use may lead to gastrointestinal irritation or allergic reactions. Clove oil, which contains high concentrations of eugenol, poses a higher risk of poisoning if ingested in large amounts.

Individuals with sensitive stomachs or allergies should exercise caution and consult a healthcare provider before regular use.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cloves are grown primarily in tropical climates, requiring specific conditions to thrive.

They prefer well-drained, fertile soil with a pH between 5.5 and 6.5. Full sunlight is essential for optimal growth and flower development. Regular watering is necessary, especially during the growing season, but overwatering should be avoided to prevent root rot. Cloves are typically propagated through cuttings or seeds, with cuttings being the most common and reliable method. The plant reaches maturity in about 3 to 5 years, and the best harvest time is when the flower buds are still green and firm.

Harvesting involves plucking the unopened buds by hand, which are then dried to become the aromatic spice known as clove.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of cloves begins with drying the flower buds to reduce moisture content and preserve their aromatic compounds.

After drying, the buds are typically ground into a fine powder to release the essential oils. Extraction methods often involve steam distillation, which effectively captures the volatile compounds responsible for clove's fragrance and medicinal properties. Distillation yields clove oil, a concentrated form used in both pharmaceutical and aromatherapy applications.

Grinding further refines the powder for use in herbal formulations and culinary preparations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cloves should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain their potency and flavor.

The shelf life of whole cloves is typically 1 to 2 years when stored properly. Exposure to light can degrade the essential oils in cloves, reducing their effectiveness over time. To prevent moisture absorption, cloves should be kept in an airtight container.

Ideal storage conditions include a temperature range of 15-20°C (59-68°F) and low humidity.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence on cloves (Syzygium aromaticum) has shown its potential in various therapeutic applications, including antimicrobial, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects, with studies supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Cloves have been historically used in traditional medicine across various cultures for their antimicrobial, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory properties, often applied to treat dental pain, digestive issues, and respiratory infections.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, cloves are considered a food seasoning and are generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by regulatory bodies, but their use in herbal medicine may vary depending on local regulations and the specific formulations in which they are included.
