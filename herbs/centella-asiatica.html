Centella asiatica

What's the taxonomical classification of Centella asiatica?

Centella asiatica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical organization, the plant is placed in the order Apiales and is a member of the family Apiaceae. Finally, it is defined by its specific biological classification under the genus Centella and the species asiatica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Centella asiatica has a creeping, prostrate growth habit characterized by long, slender stolons that form a dense ground cover through rhizomatous spreading. The leaves are reniform to orbicular in shape, featuring crenate margins and a distinctive fan-like venation pattern. These fleshy, kidney-shaped leaves are borne on long petioles that emerge from the nodes of the creeping stems. Small, inconspicuous white or pinkish flowers bloom in clusters from the leaf axils on delicate stalks. The plant typically develops a shallow, fibrous root system that stabilizes the stolons as they traverse the soil surface.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, specifically spanning across India, China, Southeast Asia, and parts of Australia. It thrives primarily in moist, marshy environments such as wetlands, riverbanks, and shaded forest floors. While its core distribution is centered in the Asian continent, it has been introduced to various other warm climates globally for medicinal cultivation. The species prefers humid conditions where it can spread via stolons across damp soil. Its ability to adapt to varying levels of moisture allows it to occupy diverse ecological niches within these warm zones.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Centella asiatica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its whole aerial parts, which include the leaves and the stems. The leaves are the most frequently harvested component because they harbor the highest concentrations of active triterpenoids like asiaticoside and madecassoside. While the entire plant can be utilized in traditional preparations, the leaves serve as the main source for extracting substances that promote skin healing and wound recovery. Some practitioners also utilize the roots, though the leaf extracts remain the standard in modern pharmaceutical applications. These plant parts are often processed into topical creams, gels, or oral supplements to leverage their neuroprotective and dermatological benefits.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of bioactive triterpene saponins and triterpenoids, specifically asiatic acid, madecassic acid, madecassoside, asiaticoside, and various centellosides. These compounds function primarily as key drivers for skin regeneration and the stimulation of collagen synthesis. Asiaticoside and madecassoside are major glycosides that help reduce inflammation and promote wound healing processes. The aglycones, known as asiatic acid and madecassic acid, play critical roles in modulating cellular responses and protecting tissue integrity. Together, these medicinal constituents provide the plant with its potent dermatological and neuroprotective properties.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Centella asiatica targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, matrix metalloproteinases, and various growth factor receptors to exert its therapeutic effects. By inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2, the plant reduces the production of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins, which helps alleviate pain and swelling. It also suppresses matrix metalloproteinases such as MMP-1 and MMP-9 to prevent the degradation of the extracellular matrix during wound healing. Furthermore, its triterpenoids stimulate the TGF-beta signaling pathway to promote collagen synthesis and structural skin integrity. These combined interactions with enzymatic and signaling proteins underpin its ability to treat inflammatory skin conditions and accelerate tissue repair.

What are the therapeutic actions of Centella asiatica?

Centella asiatica has diverse therapeutic properties including cicatrisant, venotonic, anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, vulnerary, adaptogen, and lymphatic actions. As a powerful vulnerary and cicatrisant, it accelerates the healing of wounds and skin tissue by stimulating collagen production. Its venotonic and lymphatic effects improve microcirculation and reduce edema by strengthening vessel walls and supporting fluid drainage. The plant also serves as an anti-inflammatory agent that reduces swelling and irritation in various bodily systems. Furthermore, its roles as an adaptogen and a neuroprotective substance help the body manage stress and support neurological health.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a variety of adverse reactions including nausea, dizziness, skin rash, itching, stomach cramps, headache, confusion, drowsiness, and liver toxicity. Some individuals may experience dermatological issues such as a localized skin rash or persistent itching upon contact or ingestion. Gastrointestinal distress often manifests as nausea and painful stomach cramps. Neurological side effects can range from a simple headache to more severe symptoms like dizziness, drowsiness, or mental confusion. In certain cases, heavy or prolonged use may lead to the serious complication of liver toxicity.
