Centella (Centella asiatica): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Centella asiatica, also known as gotu kola, is a creeping herb native to tropical regions of Asia and is widely used in traditional medicine for its purported cognitive, circulatory, and skin-healing benefits.

What is Centella (Centella Asiatica)?

Centella (Centella asiatica) is a perennial herb native to Asia, commonly known as gotu kola, and is widely used in traditional medicine for its purported cognitive and skin-healing benefits.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Centella is Centella asiatica. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rubiales, family Rubiaceae, genus Centella, and species Centella asiatica. It is commonly known as gotu kola in South Asia, and as Indian pennywort in North America. It is also referred to as mandukaparni in Ayurvedic texts and as centella in botanical references.

Plant Description and Morphology

Centella is a creeping perennial herb that grows up to 30 cm in height, characterized by its stoloniferous stems that form a dense mat.

Its leaves are ovate to heart-shaped, with a smooth margin and a slightly hairy surface. The small, white flowers are arranged in umbel-like clusters and bloom from June to September. The root system consists of fibrous roots that spread horizontally beneath the soil.

The leaves are light green, have a fresh, slightly aromatic scent, and a soft, leathery texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Centella is native to tropical and subtropical regions of southern and southeastern Asia, including India, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, and parts of Indonesia.

It thrives in wetland environments, riverbanks, and marshy areas, where it forms dense mats on the soil surface. The plant prefers warm, humid climates with consistent moisture levels, typically found in regions with monsoonal rainfall patterns. It grows best in well-drained to slightly waterlogged soils, often in areas with partial to full sunlight exposure.

Centella's natural range extends into the Pacific Islands and northern Australia, where similar ecological conditions exist.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in Centella (Centella asiatica) are the leaves, stems, and roots.

The leaves are most commonly harvested and used due to their high concentration of active compounds. The stems are often used in conjunction with the leaves, especially in traditional formulations. Roots are less frequently utilized but are occasionally included in specific herbal preparations.

Each part contains bioactive constituents that contribute to the plant's therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Centella asiatica contains a variety of active compounds that contribute to its medicinal properties, including asiatic acid, madecassic acid, and centellinic acid.

These compounds are primarily responsible for the plant's wound-healing and skin-repairing effects. Secondary metabolites such as triterpenoids and flavonoids play a significant role in the plant's therapeutic activity. Chemical markers like asiatic acid and madecassic acid are used to authenticate the quality and potency of Centella asiatica products.

These compounds are often analyzed through chromatographic techniques to ensure standardization and efficacy.

Pharmacological Properties

Centella asiatica exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that underpin its traditional and modern use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include wound healing, cognitive enhancement, and anti-inflammatory effects, supported by extensive clinical and experimental studies. The biological activity of Centella is attributed to compounds such as asiatic acid, madecassic acid, and centellin, which modulate cellular processes and signaling pathways. Pharmacodynamically, these compounds enhance collagen synthesis, improve microcirculation, and reduce oxidative stress.

System-level effects of Centella involve improvements in skin integrity, neurological function, and immune response, demonstrating its broad therapeutic potential.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of Centella asiatica involve a complex interplay of cellular, biochemical, and systemic processes that contribute to its therapeutic effects.

At the cellular level, it promotes wound healing by stimulating fibroblast activity and increasing collagen synthesis. Biochemically, the active compounds in Centella, such as asiatic acid and madecassic acid, modulate inflammatory pathways through interactions with various enzymes and receptors. These compounds also enhance the production of growth factors and extracellular matrix proteins, which are critical for tissue repair.

Target systems include the skin, nervous system, and circulatory system, where Centella exerts its beneficial effects through anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and vasodilatory actions.

Therapeutic Uses

Centella asiatica is widely used in traditional medicine for its therapeutic properties that support skin healing, cognitive function, and circulatory health.

It is particularly effective in treating wounds, ulcers, and varicose veins due to its ability to enhance collagen synthesis and improve blood circulation. The plant is also used to address cognitive decline and memory loss, as it promotes the growth of nerve cells and supports brain function. Additionally, Centella asiatica has been employed to alleviate symptoms of anxiety and stress by balancing neurotransmitter activity.

It is commonly applied topically for skin conditions such as eczema and psoriasis, and taken internally for digestive issues and liver support.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Centella asiatica involve various forms in which the plant is utilized.

It is commonly used as a fresh herb, dried powder, or in liquid extracts. The leaves are often crushed or sliced for topical application. It can also be brewed into a tea by steeping the dried leaves in hot water.

Additionally, it is used in capsules, tablets, and tinctures for internal consumption.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Centella asiatica varies depending on the form of the herb, with typical oral dosages ranging from 200 to 400 mg of standardized extract per day, divided into two or three doses, though specific formulations may require professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Centella asiatica is generally safe with minimal side effects, though it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system or blood pressure.

Drug Interactions

Centella asiatica may interact with medications that affect blood pressure, anticoagulants, and diuretics due to its potential to influence vascular tone and platelet function.

Toxicity and Precautions

Centella asiatica is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as stomach upset, and pregnant or breastfeeding women should consult a healthcare provider before use.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Centella asiatica is typically cultivated in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil, often grown in shaded areas or under partial sun, and harvested primarily during the monsoon season when the leaves are most abundant and nutrient-rich.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Centella asiatica involve harvesting the aerial parts of the plant, typically during its flowering stage, followed by drying to reduce moisture content and then using methods such as steam distillation, solvent extraction, or supercritical CO₂ extraction to obtain its active compounds, including asiatic acid, centellase, and madecassic acid.

Storage and Shelf Life

Centella (Centella asiatica) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, and its shelf life is typically 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of Centella asiatica in herbal medicine primarily focus on its active compounds, such as asiatic acid, madecassic acid, and brahminic acid, which have been shown to enhance wound healing, improve cognitive function, and exhibit anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Centella asiatica has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic, Chinese, and other traditional medicine systems for centuries to promote wound healing, enhance cognitive function, and support skin health.

Regulatory Status

Centella asiatica is regulated as a dietary supplement in the United States under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) and is also classified as a medicinal herb in many countries, with varying levels of oversight and standardization depending on the region.
