Centella asiatica

What's the taxonomical classification of Centella asiatica?

Centella asiatica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical organization, the plant is placed in the order Apiales and is a member of the family Apiaceae. Finally, it is defined by its specific biological classification under the genus Centella and the species asiatica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Centella asiatica has a creeping, prostrate growth habit characterized by long, slender stolons that form a dense ground cover through rhizomatous spreading.

The leaves are reniform to orbicular in shape, featuring crenate margins and a distinctive fan-like venation pattern. These fleshy, kidney-shaped leaves are borne on long petioles that emerge from the nodes of the creeping stems. Small, inconspicuous white or pinkish flowers bloom in clusters from the leaf axils on delicate stalks.

The plant typically develops a shallow, fibrous root system that stabilizes the stolons as they traverse the soil surface.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Centella asiatica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its whole aerial parts, which include the leaves and the stems. The leaves are the most frequently harvested component because they harbor the highest concentrations of active triterpenoids like asiaticoside and madecassoside. While the entire plant can be utilized in traditional preparations, the leaves serve as the main source for extracting substances that promote skin healing and wound recovery. Some practitioners also utilize the roots, though the leaf extracts remain the standard in modern pharmaceutical applications. These plant parts are often processed into topical creams, gels, or oral supplements to leverage their neuroprotective and dermatological benefits.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including triterpenoids, flavonoids, and sterols.

Among these chemical constituents, the triterpene saponins asiaticoside and madecassoside are particularly significant for their skin-healing properties. The plant also provides asiatic acid and madecassic acid, which contribute to its potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. These specific triterpenoids work alongside flavonoids to promote collagen synthesis and tissue repair.

Together, this complex mixture of sterols and acids makes the species highly effective in dermatological applications.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Centella asiatica targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, matrix metalloproteinases, and various growth factor receptors to exert its therapeutic effects. By inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2, the plant reduces the production of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins, which helps alleviate pain and swelling. It also suppresses matrix metalloproteinases such as MMP-1 and MMP-9 to prevent the degradation of the extracellular matrix during wound healing. Furthermore, its triterpenoids stimulate the TGF-beta signaling pathway to promote collagen synthesis and structural skin integrity. These combined interactions with enzymatic and signaling proteins underpin its ability to treat inflammatory skin conditions and accelerate tissue repair.

What are the therapeutic actions of Centella asiatica?

Centella asiatica has diverse therapeutic properties including cicatrisant, venotonic, anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, vulnerary, adaptogen, and lymphatic actions.

As a powerful vulnerary and cicatrisant, it accelerates the healing of wounds and skin tissue by stimulating collagen production. Its venotonic and lymphatic effects improve microcirculation and reduce edema by strengthening vessel walls and supporting fluid drainage. The plant also serves as an anti-inflammatory agent that reduces swelling and irritation in various bodily systems.

Furthermore, its roles as an adaptogen and a neuroprotective substance help the body manage stress and support neurological health.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat skin wounds, eczema, psoriasis, venous insufficiency, and cognitive decline.

It is frequently applied topically to promote collagen synthesis and accelerate the healing of cuts or burns. In many traditional medicine practices, it is consumed to reduce inflammation and improve blood circulation in the legs. Some studies also suggest its extracts may support memory and focus by protecting neurological functions.

These various applications make it a versatile remedy in both dermatological and neurological contexts.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Wound healing
  • Skin inflammation
  • Atopic dermatitis
  • Venous insufficiency
  • Acne

What are the herbal preparations of Centella asiatica?

Centella asiatica is prepared as topical creams, gels, tinctures, dried powders, and liquid infusions for both medicinal and cosmetic applications. In dermatological products, the plant is often concentrated into serums and lotions to promote skin healing and collagen production. Traditional herbalists frequently utilize decoctions or teas made from the dried aerial parts to support cognitive function and circulation. Tinctures offer a potent way to extract active triterpenes for oral consumption or concentrated skin applications. Many modern skincare formulations also incorporate stabilized extracts into lightweight gels to soothe irritation.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Centella asiatica can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal upset such as nausea or stomach pain when used improperly. Some individuals may experience localized redness, itching, or swelling at the site of topical application. If ingested in large amounts, it might lead to more significant side effects like dizziness or liver issues. It is also important to note that the plant could potentially trigger photosensitivity in certain people. Consulting a medical professional is recommended to avoid these adverse reactions.
