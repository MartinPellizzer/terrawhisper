Centella (Centella asiatica): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Centella (Centella asiatica) is a creeping herb native to Asia that has been traditionally used for its ability to improve circulation and support wound healing.

What is Centella (Centella Asiatica)?

Centella (Centella asiatica) is a flowering plant native to Southeast Asia, commonly known as gotu kola, and is widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported cognitive and circulatory benefits.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Centella asiatica, commonly known as gotu kola, is a creeping perennial herb belonging to the family Apiaceae, characterized by its distinctive heart-shaped leaves and widespread use in traditional herbal medicine.

Common Names and Synonyms

Centella (Centella asiatica) is commonly known by various names such as Gotu Kola, Brahmi, and Mandukaparni, which reflect its widespread use in traditional medicine across different regions.

Plant Description and Morphology

Centella (Centella asiatica) is a low-growing perennial herb with a creeping rhizomatous stem, trifoliate leaves that are heart-shaped and covered in fine hairs, and small, pale pink to white flowers that appear in clusters.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Centella asiatica is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, including India, China, Thailand, and Indonesia, and thrives in moist, shaded environments such as wetlands, forests, and rice paddies.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves of Centella asiatica are the primary plant part used medicinally, containing active compounds such as triterpenoids and alkaloids that contribute to its traditional and modern therapeutic applications.

Phytochemical Composition

Centella asiatica contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including triterpenoids such as asiatic acid, madecassic acid, and centellic acid, as well as flavonoids, alkaloids, and glycosides, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Centella asiatica exhibits a range of pharmacological properties including wound healing, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective effects, primarily attributed to its active compounds such as asiatic acid, centellide, and madecassic acid.

Mechanisms of Action

Centella asiatica exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through the stimulation of collagen synthesis, enhancement of capillary formation, and modulation of inflammatory responses via its active compounds such as asiatic acid, madecassic acid, and centella alkaloids.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Centella is used to support wound healing, enhance cognitive function, alleviate symptoms of anxiety and depression, reduce inflammation, promote skin health, and manage conditions such as diabetes and high cholesterol.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Centella asiatica is commonly prepared as a dried herb, used in formulations such as tinctures, capsules, powders, and topical creams, with the most traditional form being the dried leaf used in decoctions or as a powder in herbal remedies.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Centella asiatica varies depending on the form of the herb, with typical oral doses ranging from 200 to 400 mg of standardized extract per day, though it is important to follow the specific guidelines provided by a qualified healthcare practitioner.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in appropriate doses and under proper guidance, Centella (Centella asiatica) is generally considered safe, but some individuals may experience mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, headaches, or skin irritation.

Drug Interactions

Centella asiatica may interact with medications that affect blood clotting, blood pressure, or the central nervous system, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects or reducing the effectiveness of certain drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Centella (Centella asiatica) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as stomach upset, headache, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to limited safety data.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Centella asiatica is typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions with well-drained soil, partial shade, and consistent moisture, often in wetlands or marshy areas where it thrives naturally.

Processing and Extraction

Centella (Centella asiatica) is typically processed by drying the whole plant, including the leaves, stems, and roots, to preserve its active compounds and ensure stability for use in herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Centella (Centella asiatica) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, and its shelf life is typically 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence have increasingly supported the traditional use of Centella asiatica for its potential cognitive-enhancing, wound-healing, and anti-inflammatory properties, with numerous studies demonstrating its active compounds such as asiatic acid and madecassic acid contributing to these effects.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Centella asiatica has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic, Chinese, and traditional Indonesian medicine for centuries to promote wound healing, enhance cognitive function, and treat skin conditions.

Regulatory Status
