Ardisia maclurei

What's the taxonomical classification of Ardisia maclurei?

Ardisia maclurei belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Ericales, it falls into the family Primulaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Ardisia, with its specific designation being the species maclurei.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Ardisia maclurlei targets various enzymes and receptors involved in inflammation and oxidative stress, primarily through its bioactive alkaloids and polyphenolic compounds. These phytochemicals act as inhibitors of cyclooxygenase enzymes, which prevents the synthesis of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins. Furthermore, the plant's constituents interact with antioxidant enzymes to neutralize reactive oxygen species within cellular environments. By modulating these specific protein pathways, the plant exerts its documented antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory therapeutic effects. These molecular interactions suggest a multi-target mechanism that supports its traditional use in treating infections and swelling.

    What are the herbal preparations of Ardisia maclurei?

    Ardisia maclurlei is prepared as a decoction of the leaves or bark, a juice extracted from the crushed fruit, or a topical poultice made from the fresh plant matter. These different methods are utilized to address various ailments ranging from skin irritations to digestive issues. The decoction method involves boiling the plant components in water to extract active medicinal compounds. For localized skin treatments, the plant is often mashed into a paste and applied directly to the affected area. Traditional practitioners select the specific preparation form based on the desired systemic or localized effect of the treatment.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Ardisia maclurei can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive upset if ingested or handled improperly. The plant contains secondary metabolites that may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals upon skin contact. When consumed, the chemical constituents could lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. There is currently limited clinical data regarding its long-term toxicity in humans. Users should exercise caution and avoid direct contact with the leaves and berries to prevent adverse reactions.
