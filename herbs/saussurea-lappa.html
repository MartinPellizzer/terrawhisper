Scientific and Botanical Profile

Gao Ben, with botanical name Saussurea lappa, is a perennial herb belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

It is native to the mountainous regions of Asia, particularly China, India, and the Himalayas, where it thrives in temperate climates. Morphologically, it features a thick, fibrous root system with a distinctive, irregularly shaped root that is often yellowish to brownish in color, and it has basal leaves that are lanceolate with rough, hairy surfaces. Common names for this plant include Chinese Costus, Costus Root, Ginseng, Gao Liang Cao, Garden Burdock, Huang Jing, Tang-Kuei, Chinese Wormwood, Sang Zhi, Lappa Burdock, Shu-Gan, Huang Ya, Saussurea Root, Thorned Costus, Saw-Wort, Lappa Root, Therapeutic Burdock, and Sangus.

It is widely used in traditional Chinese medicine for its purported therapeutic properties, including anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects.

History and Cultural Relevance

Gao Ben was used in traditional Chinese medicine for centuries to treat ailments such as inflammation, digestive issues, and respiratory conditions, with its roots tracing back to ancient texts like the Shennong Ben Cao Jing.

The plant, also known as Saussurea lappa, has been a staple in both traditional Chinese and Ayurvedic medicine, valued for its purported anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. In various cultural rituals, Gao Ben has been used in purification ceremonies and to ward off negative energies, particularly in Tibetan and Mongolian communities. Its essential oil is still incorporated into modern herbal remedies for arthritis and menstrual pain, reflecting its continued relevance in alternative health practices.

Today, Gao Ben remains an important symbol of holistic healing and cultural heritage, bridging ancient wisdom with contemporary wellness trends.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Saussurea lappa has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh root, dried root, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare saussurea lappa, it can be made into a tea by boiling the dried root in water, or as a decoction by simmering it for a longer period, while an infusion involves steeping the dried root in hot water. For topical use, the powdered form or essential oil can be applied directly to the skin, though caution is advised due to potential irritation. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 250-500 mg of standardized extract in capsule form twice daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

It is advisable to use saussurea lappa for short durations, no longer than two weeks, and to consult a healthcare provider before prolonged or regular use.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Saussurea lappa can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, but it should be used with caution due to its possible side effects and contraindications.

Common side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, and vomiting, particularly when taken in high doses or over prolonged periods. It may interact with certain drugs, such as anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents, increasing the risk of bleeding, and should not be combined with other herbs that have similar effects. Special populations, including pregnant or breastfeeding women, should avoid saussurea lappa due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, it is recommended to use saussurea lappa under the guidance of a qualified healthcare professional, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if any adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Saussurea lappa grows best in well-drained, loamy soils with a slightly alkaline pH, in full sun to partial shade, and requires consistent moisture without waterlogging.

It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and cold winters, and should be planted in early spring after the last frost. Regular weeding and mulching help control weeds and retain soil moisture, while periodic fertilization with organic matter supports healthy growth. The plant is typically harvested in late summer to early autumn when the roots are most mature, using a spade or fork to carefully lift the roots without damaging them.

After harvesting, the roots are washed, sliced, and dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place to preserve their medicinal potency.
