Clematis intricata

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis intricata has woody, twining stems that grow as climbing vines with opposite, simple to trifoliate leaves and small, bell-shaped cream or yellowish flowers. The foliage typically consists of leaflets that are ovate or lanceolate in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. Its inflorescence features small, actinomorphic flowers that lack showy petals but possess prominent stamens. During the fruiting stage, the plant develops numerous achenes characterized by long, feathery styles that aid in wind dispersal. These structural adaptations allow the species to weave through dense thickets and forest undergrowth.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Clematis intricata can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of protoanemonin. This specific chemical compound is known to act as a severe irritant to the mucous membranes and sensitive skin tissues. Contact with the plant may result in redness, itching, or blistering on the hands or legs. If any part of the vine is swallowed, it can lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and stomach pain. Because of these risks, caution should be exercised when handling the plant to avoid accidental exposure.
