Clematis intricata

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis intricata?

Clematis intricata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the biological hierarchy further, it falls under the order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Ranunculaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by its genus, Clematis, and its specific species designation, intricata.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Clematis intricata contains various secondary metabolites including glycosides, flavonoids, and tannins that contribute to its chemical profile. These bioactive compounds are often studied for their potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. While specific clinical applications for this particular species are limited in modern pharmacology, related plants in the Clematis genus are known to possess bioactive alkaloids. Researchers often investigate these chemical constituents to understand their interactions with biological systems. The presence of these specific compounds suggests the plant may have traditional uses in treating minor ailments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Clematis intricata?

Clematis intricata has medicinal properties that are primarily utilized for its ability to act as an anti-inflammatory and a topical astringent for skin ailments. Traditional healers often employ the plant to soothe irritated tissues and reduce swelling in localized areas. Its chemical constituents assist in tightening skin cells, which makes it useful for treating minor abrasions or weeping sores. Some indigenous practices also suggest its use in managing internal discomfort through controlled applications. This plant serves as a versatile component in various herbal preparations aimed at promoting tissue repair.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including respiratory issues, skin inflammation, and digestive discomfort. Traditional practitioners often utilize its properties to soothe coughs and bronchial irritation. When applied topically, it can assist in reducing swelling or irritation on the skin's surface. Some historical medicinal practices also suggest its role in managing internal discomfort through controlled doses. Careful attention to dosage is necessary because the plant contains bioactive compounds that can be potent.

What are the herbal preparations of Clematis intricata?

Clematis intricata is prepared primarily as a decoction, a tea made by simmering the woody stems or roots in water to extract its medicinal components. Practitioners may also utilize the plant in the form of a tincture, which involves macerating the dried material in alcohol to create a concentrated liquid extract. Some traditional applications involve using the plant in topical poultices or washes applied directly to the skin. These different preparations allow for varying absorption rates and dosages depending on the specific therapeutic goal. Choosing the correct form is essential for maximizing the bioavailability of the plant's active constituents.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Clematis intricata can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of protoanemonin. This specific chemical compound is known to act as a severe irritant to the mucous membranes and sensitive skin tissues. Contact with the plant may result in redness, itching, or blistering on the hands or legs. If any part of the vine is swallowed, it can lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and stomach pain. Because of these risks, caution should be exercised when handling the plant to avoid accidental exposure.
