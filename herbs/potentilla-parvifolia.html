Potentilla parvifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Potentilla parvifolia?

Potentiella parvifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Potentiella, with its specific designation being the species parvifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Potentiella parvifolia has small, palmately compound leaves typically consisting of five to seven leaflets that are elliptical to obovate in shape. These leaflets often feature serrated margins and a fine coating of hairs on both the upper and lower surfaces. The plant produces small, yellow, five-petaled flowers that emerge from terminal or axillary clusters. Its stems are woody at the base and can exhibit a spreading or prostrate growth habit depending on the environment. The root system is generally fibrous, helping the plant anchor itself in rocky or sandy substrates.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of Western North America, ranging from Alaska and the Yukon through much of Canada and into the western United States. It typically inhabits high-elevation environments such as alpine tundras, rocky slopes, and subalpine meadows. Within these habitats, the species thrives in well-drained, often acidic soils characteristic of northern latitudes and high altitudes. Its distribution is closely tied to cool, moist climates found in these specific ecological niches. Consequently, the plant is most frequently encountered in the rugged terrains of the Rocky Mountains and the Pacific Northwest.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds astragalin, isoquercitrin, miquelianin, quercetin, and tiliroside. These specific flavonoids and glycosides are responsible for much of the biological activity found within the species. Research suggests that these chemical constituents contribute to the plant's antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of quercetin and its derivatives plays a significant role in its potential therapeutic applications. Scientists continue to study how these individual compounds interact to provide health benefits.

According to a study published by "Chemistry & biodiversity", seven flavonoids were separated and purified from ethyl acetate extractive fractions from ethanol extracts of Potentiella parvifolia Fisch. These identified compounds include ayanin (1), tricin (2), quercetin (3), tiliroside (4), miquelianin (5), isoquercitrin (6), and astragalin (7). The research evaluated the neuroprotective effects of these seven substances in an in vitro human neuroblastoma SH-SY5Y cell model. Results from Western blot and morphology analysis suggested that compounds 3 and 4 demonstrated the better neuroprotective effects.
