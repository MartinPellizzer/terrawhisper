To identify Hoodia (Hoodia gordonii) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Hoodia gordonii belongs to the kingdom Plantae. Its family is Asclepiadaceae. The genus is Hoodia, and the species is Hoodia gordonii. The division, class, and order are not provided in the data.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Hoodia gordonii is the scientific name of the plant, first described by the botanist James Hutton Gordon in 1832. It has several synonyms, including Hoodia abyssinica and Hoodia mirabilis, which reflect historical taxonomic revisions. Common names include Hoodia, desert gourd, and khomadiba, while regional and historical names vary across indigenous and colonial sources.

Morphological Characteristics

Hoodia gordonii is a succulent plant with a short, thick stem and a branching root system. Its leaves are fleshy, spiny, and arranged in a rosette pattern. The plant produces an inflorescence of yellow-green flowers, which are small and clustered. The fruit is a capsule that contains tiny, smooth seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Hoodia gordonii can be distinguished by its unique tubular flowers, which are typically yellow with red or orange markings. It has a succulent, branched stem and produces small, fleshy leaves. These features differentiate it from other similar Hoodia species and related plants. Its habitat and growth patterns also contribute to its identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Hoodia gordonii is native to the arid regions of southern Africa, particularly Namibia, South Africa, and Botswana. It thrives in hot, dry climates with minimal rainfall. It prefers well-drained sandy or gravelly soils. It is typically found at altitudes between 600 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Hoodia gordonii blooms during late summer to early autumn, typically between August and October. Seed formation occurs in the following spring, from late winter to early spring. Harvesting is best done during the flowering stage for accurate identification, as this is when the plant's characteristics are most distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Hoodia gordonii belongs to the Apocynaceae family, sharing traits with other genera like Stapelia and Ceropegia. Its closest relatives include species such as Hoodia pseudogordonii and Ceropegia. These relationships are supported by both morphological and molecular studies, highlighting its unique position within the family.
