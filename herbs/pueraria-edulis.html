Pueraria eduilis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae, which is characteristic of many nitrogen-fixing legumes. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Pueraria, and its unique species designation, edulis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pueraria edulis has a perennial climbing habit characterized by woody stems, trifoliate leaves, and large, tuberous starchy roots. The foliage consists of three leaflets that are typically ovate or elliptic in shape with smooth margins. Its climbing mechanism relies on flexible stems that can reach significant lengths as they sprawl across supporting structures. The plant produces small, pale purple or white flowers that grow in axillary racemes. Most significantly, the underground tubers serve as the primary storage organs and are the most economically important part of the plant.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, known as yam bean, is a perennial climbing vine cultivated in tropical regions by planting its tuberous roots in well-drained, fertile soils under partial shade.

Farmers typically propagate the crop using small pieces of the tuber or seeds during the onset of the rainy season. The vines require sturdy support structures like fences or trellises to climb and maximize sunlight exposure for leaf development. Regular weeding and moisture management are essential to ensure the tubers expand properly without rot.
