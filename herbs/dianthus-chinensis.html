Dianthus chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Dianthus chinensis?

Dianthus chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Caryophyllales. The plant is a member of the family Caryophyllaceae, falling under the genus Dianthus, with its specific designation being the species chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dianthus chinensis has erect, perennial stems that reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters and feature narrow, lanceolate leaves arranged in opposite pairs. The foliage is typically a dull green color with a smooth, linear shape that tapers to a point. At the terminal ends of these stems, the plant produces solitary, fragrant flowers characterized by serrated petals and a central eye. These blooms often exhibit a variety of colors, including shades of pink, red, or white, and can possess a fringed texture. The plant also develops a basal rosette of leaves that provides a structural foundation for its upward growth.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant thrives when grown in well-draining, fertile soil with full sun exposure and regular watering that avoids waterlogging the roots.

It prefers temperatures between 60 and 75 degrees Fahrenheit for optimal flowering performance. To maintain healthy growth, you should deadhead spent blooms to encourage continuous production throughout the season. Providing consistent moisture during dry spells is essential, yet the soil must allow for excellent drainage to prevent fungal diseases.

Applying a balanced fertilizer during the spring will further enhance its vibrant floral displays.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several specific cyclic dipeptides including cyclo-(L-Glu-L-Glu), cyclo-(L-N-methyl Glu-L-N-methyl Glu), cyclo-(L-methyl Glu ester-L-Glu), and cyclo-(L-methyl Glu ester-L-methyl Glu ester). These medicinal compounds belong to a class of bioactive molecules often studied for their potential pharmacological properties. The structural diversity of these glutamate-derived peptides suggests complex interactions within biological systems. Researchers analyze these specific esterified and methylated derivatives to understand their unique chemical stability. Such detailed chemical profiling helps identify the therapeutic potential inherent in Dianthus chinensis.

According to a study published by "Yao xue xue bao = Acta pharmaceutica Sinica", four dicyclopeptides were isolated from the aerial parts of Dianthus chinensis L. These include 1 new dicyclopeptide cyclo-(L-N-methyl Glu-L-N-methyl Glu) (1) and 1 new natural dicyclopeptide cyclo-(L-methyl Glu ester-L-methyl Glu ester) (2). Additionally, the plant contains 2 known dicyclopeptides, which are cyclo-(L-methyl Glu ester-L-Glu) (3) and cyclo-(L-Glu-L-Glu) (4). The structures of these 4 compounds were determined by spectroscopic analyses and chemical methods.

What side effects this plant can have?

Ingesting the plant can potentially lead to nausea or stomach discomfort depending on the individual's sensitivity. It is important to exercise caution when handling these flowers if you have a history of plant allergies.
