Munronia pinnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Munronia pinnata?

Munronia pinnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized as Equisetopsida, falling under the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Sapindales and the family Meliaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Munronia, and its specific species epithet, pinnata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Munronia pinnata has opposite, decussate leaves that are typically ovate to elliptic in shape with entire margins and acute tips. The plant is characterized by its woody, climbing habit and slender stems that allow it to scramble through dense vegetation. Its foliage is often dark green and possesses a slightly leathery texture. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary clusters, which eventually give way to small, fleshy fruits. The overall structure of the plant supports its lifestyle as a hemi-epiphyte or forest climber.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds munropin G, munropin H, munropin I, and munropin J. These individual chemical constituents belong to a broader category of bioactive substances known as munropins G-J. Researchers study these specific molecules to determine their potential therapeutic applications in modern medicine. The unique chemical structures of munropin G, munropin H, munropin I, and munropin J are essential to the plant's overall pharmacological profile. Understanding how these compounds interact with biological systems is a primary focus of ongoing botanical research.

As discussed in a study, Munroina pinnata is recognized as a rich source of structurally diverse limonoids. Four new prieurianin-type limonoids, known as munropins G-J (1-4), were isolated from the aerial parts of the plant. Munropins G (1) and H (2) feature a C-12 β-D-glucosylated α-methyl-2'-hydroxypentanoate side chain and a C-17 β-substituted furan ring, where 1 is the 7-O-acetyl derivative of 2. Munropins I (3) and J (4) possess a formyl group at C-11, a 3-methyl-2-hydroxypentanoate ester at C-12, and a C-17 γ-hydroxy-α,β-unsaturated γ-lactone unit, specifically 21-hydroxy for 3 and 23-hydroxy for 4. Although these compounds were evaluated for cytotoxicity and anti-inflammatory activity, none exhibited significant effects at a concentration of 80 μM.

Because scientific studies on human toxicity are limited, caution is advised when using this species.
