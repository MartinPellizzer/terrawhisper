Hypericum henryi

What's the taxonomical classification of Hypericum henryi?

Hypericum henryi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Hypericaceae. Specifically, it is placed within the genus Hypericum, and its scientific designation is completed by the species name henryi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hypericum henryi has woody, mounding stems that support lanceolate, dark green leaves arranged in opposite pairs along the branches. The foliage is typically evergreen or semi-evergreen, providing a dense textural base for the plant's floral displays. During the blooming season, small, five-petaled flowers emerge in clusters from the leaf axils. These blossoms are bright yellow and feature prominent, central stamens that create a fuzzy appearance. The overall structure of the plant remains compact and bushy, making it suitable for groundcover or garden borders.

What are the pharmacological activities of Hypericum henryi?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-colon cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, cytotoxic, and tumor suppressive properties. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively induce cell death in various malignant cell lines through potent cytotoxic mechanisms. These chemical constituents also demonstrate the ability to inhibit tumor growth and act as a tumor suppressive agent in experimental models. Furthermore, the plant exhibits strong anti-inflammatory effects that help modulate immune responses within the body. Together, these diverse biological actions highlight its therapeutic potential in oncology and inflammation management.

As discussed in a study, 46 dearomatized isoprenylated acylphloroglucinols, representing approximately 70% of all DIAPs, were discovered from the roots of Hypericum henryi. Among these, compounds 1-7, 39, and 41-42 exhibited remarkable cytotoxicities with IC50 values ranging from 0.84-5.63 μM in human colon cancer HCT116 cells. Specifically, compounds 2 and 6 possessed selective cytotoxicities towards colon cancer cells and could significantly suppress the expressions of NFκB and FAT1 while promoting the tumor suppressor gene PDCD4. In HCT116 colon xenograft-bearing mouse models, treatments with 2 and 6 reduced the growth of xenograft tumors in a dose-dependent manner. These findings suggest that metabolites from Hypericum henryi can be considered a new type of anti-colon cancer lead agent for further drug development.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains prenylated α-pyrones, dearomatized isoprenylated acylphloroglucinols, hyperhenols A-E, kielcorin, and α-pyrone derivatives (1-14). These specific secondary metabolites serve as the primary chemical constituents responsible for its biological activity. The presence of various α-pyrone derivatives, specifically numbered 1 through 14, highlights the structural diversity within the plant's chemical profile. Additionally, the unique dearomatized isoprenylated acylphloroglucinols and hyperhenols contribute to its complex medicinal properties. Kielcorin further adds to this specialized array of bioactive compounds found within the species.

As discussed in a study, Hypericum henryi contains fourteen previously undescribed α-pyrone derivatives, labeled 1-14, alongside four known analogs, 15-18. Compounds (+) / (-) 1, 2, and 3 possess a rare 6/6/4/6/6 polycyclic skeleton. Within these isolated substances, compound 14 represents the first example of a 7,7-dimethyl-pyran-4-one moiety. During screening in lipopolysaccharide-induced RAW264.7 cells, compounds 14, (+) 18, and (-) 18 showed inhibitory effects against nitric oxide production. Specifically, compound 14 suppressed the expression of cyclooxygenase-2 and inducible nitric oxide synthase in these RAW264.7 cells.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin sensitivity to sunlight, digestive upset, and potential interactions with various pharmaceutical medications. Because it contains hypericin, individuals may experience increased photosensitivity which leads to easier sunburns or skin rashes when exposed to UV rays. Some people might also report mild gastrointestinal issues such as stomach pain or nausea after contact or ingestion. It is important to note that its chemical compounds can alter how the liver processes certain drugs, potentially reducing their effectiveness.
