Aglaia cucullata

What's the taxonomical classification of Aglaia cucullata?

Aglaia cucullata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Sapindales and is a member of the family Meliaceae. Specifically, it is identified by the genus Aglaias and is distinguished at the species level as Aglaias cucullata.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Aglaia cucullaata contains a diverse array of bioactive secondary metabolites including limonoids, triterpenoids, steroids, and various fatty acids. These complex chemical constituents, particularly the highly oxygenated limonoids, are primarily responsible for the plant's biological activities. Researchers have identified these compounds as having significant potential for anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and cytotoxic properties. Some studies suggest that specific extracts may also exhibit antioxidant effects that protect cells from oxidative damage. Ongoing pharmacological investigations continue to explore how these specific molecular structures interact with biological targets to treat various ailments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Aglaia cucullata?

Aglaia cucullata has medicinal properties that include analgesic, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities derived from its bioactive secondary metabolites. The plant contains various alkaloids and terpenoids that work together to reduce swelling and alleviate pain in affected tissues. These chemical compounds also demonstrate the ability to inhibit the growth of certain pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Researchers often investigate its extracts for potential use in treating skin infections and managing inflammatory conditions. Such pharmacological actions make it a significant subject of study in traditional and modern herbal medicine.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin diseases, gastrointestinal infections, and inflammation. The extracts from its bark and leaves are often applied topically to manage wounds and fungal infections. In traditional medicinal practices, components of the plant are also utilized to alleviate digestive discomfort and intestinal parasites. Some studies suggest its bioactive compounds may assist in reducing systemic inflammation throughout the body. These therapeutic applications rely on the plant's specific chemical properties to combat different pathogens.

What are the herbal preparations of Aglaia cucullata?

Aglaia cucullata is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or an infusion using the various parts of the plant including the bark, leaves, and seeds. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for treating internal ailments. When the dried leaves are finely ground, they form a powder that can be encapsulated or mixed into other substances. Some traditional practices involve steeping the seeds to extract specific bioactive compounds for topical or oral applications. These different methods allow for the targeted delivery of the plant's chemical constituents depending on the intended therapeutic use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Aglaia cucullata can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if the plant is ingested or handled improperly. While specific clinical data on human toxicity is limited, many species within the Meliaceae family contain bioactive compounds that may act as irritants. Direct contact with the sap or leaves might lead to localized dermatitis or redness in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of various parts of the plant could potentially result in nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Therefore, caution should be exercised when handling this species to avoid adverse biological responses.
