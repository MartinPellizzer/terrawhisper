Crocus alatavicus

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crocus alatavicus has slender, linear leaves that are grayish-green in color and feature a distinct white central stripe running longitudinally. The plant produces small, funnel-shaped flowers that typically exhibit shades of pale purple or violet with darker veins on the tepals. These blooms emerge from underground corms that serve as the primary nutrient storage organs for the species. The reproductive structures include three prominent stamens and a central style that often branches at the tip. Each flower is supported by a protective sheath known as a prophyll during its initial development.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous and semi-arid regions of Central Asia, specifically spanning across parts of Iran, Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan, and Tajikistan. Its natural habitat typically consists of rocky slopes, dry steppes, and alpine meadows located at varying elevations. Within these diverse landscapes, it thrives in areas where seasonal moisture is available during its growth cycle. The distribution is largely influenced by the specific soil compositions and climatic patterns found in these high-altitude territories.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Crocus alatavicus can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested by humans or pets. Small children and domestic animals should be monitored closely to prevent accidental consumption. Always consult a medical professional or veterinarian if symptoms occur after contact or ingestion.
