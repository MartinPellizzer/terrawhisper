Scientific and Botanical Profile

Dragon Herb, with botanical name Houttuynia cordata, is a perennial herbaceous plant belonging to the Saururaceae family.

Native to Southeast Asia, including China, Japan, India, and Vietnam, it is also found in parts of the Pacific Islands and tropical regions of the Americas. Morphologically, it features heart-shaped leaves that are typically bright green with a reddish underside, and small, fragrant flowers that are usually white or greenish-yellow, arranged in clusters. Commonly known by various names such as Heartleaf Sorrel, Monkey Grass, False Shamrock, and Vietnamese Arrowhead, it is also referred to as Gotu Kola, Fish Mint, and Radiator Plant.

This versatile plant is valued for its medicinal properties and is often used in traditional medicine systems across its native regions.

Today, it remains a popular remedy in herbal medicine, with modern studies supporting its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, making it relevant in both traditional and contemporary wellness practices.

Its enduring presence in cultural and medicinal contexts highlights its significance as a versatile and revered plant across generations.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Houttuynia cordata has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its diverse therapeutic properties, and it is available in several forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping fresh or dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant material for a longer period. Topical applications include making an infusion for poultices or using essential oil diluted in a carrier oil for skin treatments. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-3 grams of dried herb per cup of tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

Due to limited scientific evidence, it is advisable to use houttuynia cordata sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to minimize potential risks.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Houttuynia cordata can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when consumed in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea in some individuals. There is limited clinical evidence on its interactions with medications, but caution is advised as it may interfere with anticoagulants or other drugs due to its potential blood-thinning effects. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid using houttuynia cordata due to insufficient data on its safety during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to use houttuynia cordata only under medical supervision, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Houttuynia cordata grows best in moist, well-drained soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, thriving in partial to full shade and requiring consistent moisture throughout the growing season.

It prefers warm climates and can be planted in early spring after the last frost, with regular watering to maintain soil humidity without causing waterlogging. For optimal growth, regular pruning to encourage bushy growth and fertilization during the growing season are recommended, along with monitoring for pests such as aphids and spider mites. The leaves are best harvested in the early morning when moisture is high, using sharp scissors to cut the entire leaf, ensuring minimal damage to the plant.

After harvesting, the leaves should be dried in a cool, shaded area or in a dehydrator at low temperatures to preserve their potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain freshness and medicinal properties.
