Gypsophila oldhamiana

What's the taxonomical classification of Gypsophila oldhamiana?

Gypsophila oldhamiana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant falls under the order Caryophyllales. It is further categorized into the family Caryophyllaceae, which is the group to which the genus Gypsophila belongs. Finally, the specific biological identity of this plant is defined by its species designation, oldhamiana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gypsophila oldhamiana has slender, erect stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters and feature small, lanceolate leaves arranged oppositely along the length of the plant. The foliage is often characterized by a light green color and a smooth, slightly textured surface. Its flowers are small, five-petaled, and arranged in dense, terminal cymes that create a delicate, airy appearance. The petals are generally white or pale pink, emerging from a calyx that is deeply divided into narrow lobes. This perennial herb develops a fibrous root system that helps anchor it within its native rocky or gravelly habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Gypsophila oldhamiana?

This plant has alpha-glucosidase inhibitory, anti-proliferative, anticancer, antiplatelet aggregation, and antitumor pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate a significant ability to inhibit the enzymes responsible for carbohydrate digestion, which may aid in managing blood glucose levels. Research also indicates that the plant possesses potent anti-proliferative properties that target the growth of malignant cells. These specific mechanisms contribute to its broader anticancer and antitumor effects by disrupting cancer cell progression. Furthermore, the plant exhibits antiplatelet aggregation activity, which suggests potential benefits for cardiovascular health and blood flow regulation.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Gypsophila oldhamiana contain an unusual new cyclic peptide known as gypsophin (1) which features a pyrrolidine-2,5-dione unit. This specific compound exhibits inhibitory activity against alpha-glucosidase. The potency of this pharmacological activity is characterized by an IC50 of 305 microM. The structure of this peptide was elucidated through spectroscopic evidences and its stereochemistry was determined using Marfey's method and single-crystal X-ray diffraction.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains triterpenoid saponins, beta-carboline alkaloids, oldhamiaines A, oldhamiaines B, and beta-sitosterol. These diverse chemical constituents contribute to the complex pharmacological profile of the species. The presence of specific alkaloids like oldhamiaines A and B suggests potential bioactivity that researchers continue to investigate. Additionally, the triterpenoid saponins and beta-sitosterol are known for their various biological properties in different medicinal contexts. Together, these compounds represent the primary secondary metabolites responsible for the plant's therapeutic value.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Gypsophila oldhamiana are rich in triterpenoid saponins with antitumoor properties. A triterpenoid saponin-rich G. oldhamiana root extract (TGOE) was prepared and evaluated for its anti-proliferative activities. Using the MTT assay, TGOE selectively inhibited the proliferation of SMMC-7721 cells with an IC50 value of 19.50±3.63 µg/mL. In comparison, the cytotoxic effects of TGOE on L02 cells were much lower, showing an IC50 value of 40.48±3.74 µg/mL. These findings suggest that TGOE may have potential beneficial effects against hepatocellular carcinoma.
