Botanical Identification of Wild Mallow (Malva sylvestris)

To identify Wild Mallow (Malva sylvestris) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Wild Mallow (Malva sylvestris) belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Malvales, family Malvaceae, genus Malva, and species sylvestris. These classifications reflect its position within the plant kingdom and its relationships to other flowering plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Wild Mallow is Malva sylvestris, first described by Carl von Linne in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Malva eriophora and Malva pulcherrima, reflecting historical classification changes. Common names such as Common Mallow and Wild Mallows are widely used, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Wild Mallow (Malva sylvestris) is an upright perennial herb with a taproot system. Its stems are hairy and branched, often reddish in color. Leaves are heart-shaped with serrated edges, and the plant produces a cluster of pink or white flowers. The fruit is a dry capsule containing numerous small, smooth seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Wild Mallow can be distinguished by its palmate leaves with 5-7 lobes, pink to purple flowers with 5 petals, and fuzzy, hairy stems. It differs from related species like Marsh Mallow by its smaller flowers and less hairy stems. The leaves are broader and more lobed compared to other Malva species.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Wild Mallow (Malva sylvestris) is native to Europe, western Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers loamy or sandy soils and is commonly found at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Wild Mallow blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between May and September. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the most accurate harvest timing is during the flowering period when the leaves and flowers are fully developed.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Wild Mallow (Malva sylvestris) belongs to the Malvaceae family, closely related to species like Malva neglecta and Malva moschata. It shares genus traits such as palmate leaves and woolly stems, while family traits include capsule fruits and sticky hairs. These relationships aid in distinguishing it from similar species in herbal identification.
