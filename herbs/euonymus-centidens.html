Euonymus centidens

What's the taxonomical classification of Euonymus centidens?

Euonymus centidens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Celastrales. As a member of the family Celastraceae, it is further categorized into the genus Euonymus, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species centidens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euonymus centidens has opposite, elliptical leaves with serrated margins and woody stems that support small, four-lobed capsules.

The foliage typically displays a deep green color and a smooth texture on the upper surface. These capsules eventually split open to reveal bright orange or red arils that surround the seeds. The plant often develops a spreading or shrubby growth habit depending on its specific environment.

Small, inconspicuous flowers appear before the fruit develops to facilitate pollination.

        What are the herbal preparations of Euonymus centidens?

        Euonymus centidens is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical tincture derived from its bark or leaves. These liquid preparations are typically used to extract the plant's bioactive alkaloids for medicinal purposes. Decoctions involve boiling the tougher plant parts in water to ensure a concentrated essence is released. Infusions are often used for more delicate leaf material to prevent overheating. Tinctures serve as long-lasting shelf-stable versions that utilize alcohol as a solvent for extraction.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Euonymus centidens can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential death if any part of the plant is ingested by humans or animals. The toxic compounds within the plant primarily target the digestive system, leading to symptoms such as intense vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. Beyond digestive issues, the toxins can affect the central nervous system, resulting in symptoms like tremors, dizziness, or seizures. In extreme cases of poisoning, the ingestion of this species may lead to respiratory failure or cardiac complications. Careful avoidance is necessary because even small amounts of the plant matter can trigger these dangerous reactions.
