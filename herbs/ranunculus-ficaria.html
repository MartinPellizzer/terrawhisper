Ranunculus ficaria decoctions are traditionally prepared by simmering the dried roots or rhizomes in water for an extended period, allowing the extraction of bioactive compounds such as alkaloids and flavonoids.

These decoctions are often used in herbal medicine for their purported diuretic and anti-inflammatory properties, though they should be used with caution due to the herb's potential toxicity. Tinctures made from Ranunculus ficaria are another common preparation, involving the soaking of the plant material in alcohol to create a concentrated extract that can be taken orally or applied topically. Teas made from the herb are less common due to its strong bitterness and potential irritant effects, but some traditional practices still utilize it for digestive or respiratory ailments.

In addition to decoctions and tinctures, mucillages, poultices, and ointments have been historically used to address skin conditions and inflammatory issues, though modern usage is limited due to safety concerns and regulatory restrictions.
