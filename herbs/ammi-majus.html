Scientific and Botanical Profile

Ammi Plant, with botanical name Ammi majus, is a member of the Apiaceae family, commonly known as the carrot or parsley family.

It is widely recognized by various common names such as Wild Carrot, Queen Anne's Lace, Flossflower, Bishop's Weed, Cow Parsnip, Fool's Parsley, Death Carrot, Cow's Parsley, Whitethorn, Wild Angelica, Sweet Cicely, Yellow Fennel, and Ammi Plant. Native to regions across Europe, Asia, North Africa, Western Europe, Southern Europe, South Africa, the Middle East, Central Asia, Eastern Europe, Northern Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, the Persian Gulf, and the Caucasus Region, this plant has a long history of ecological and medicinal significance. Morphologically, it is an annual or biennial herb with a tall, hollow, branched stem that can reach up to 2 meters in height.

Its leaves are pinnately divided, with a feathery appearance, and it produces small, white to pale yellow flowers arranged in umbrella-like clusters, known as umbels, which give it a distinctive and ornamental look.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Ammi majus has several medicinal preparations, such as teas, tinctures, and decoctions, which have been traditionally used for their therapeutic properties.

The dried roots and seeds of the plant are commonly used to make teas that are believed to aid in digestion and reduce inflammation. Tinctures prepared from the plant's extracts are often utilized in herbal medicine for their potential antispasmodic and carminative effects. These preparations are typically taken internally to support gastrointestinal health and alleviate symptoms of bloating or indigestion.

However, due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids, which can be toxic to the liver, Ammi majus should be used with caution and under the guidance of a qualified herbalist or healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Ammi majus can be used cautiously in medicinal preparations due to its potential therapeutic properties, but it is important to be aware of its safety profile.

While it may have anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic effects, it can also cause gastrointestinal upset, including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, particularly at higher doses. There are limited studies on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when using it concurrently with anticoagulants or sedatives due to potential additive effects. It is contraindicated during pregnancy and breastfeeding because of the risk of uterine stimulation and possible harm to the fetus or infant.

For safe use, individuals should consult a healthcare provider before use, start with low doses, and discontinue if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Ammi majus grows best in well-drained, loamy soil that is rich in organic matter, with a slightly acidic to neutral pH.

It prefers full sun to partial shade and requires regular watering, especially during dry periods, but should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. To ensure healthy growth, it is important to weed the area regularly and apply a balanced fertilizer in early spring. Harvesting should occur when the plant is in full bloom, typically in late summer to early autumn, by cutting the flower heads with scissors or a sharp knife to avoid damaging the stems.

After harvesting, the flowers should be dried in a cool, shaded place with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark location to preserve their potency and medicinal properties.
