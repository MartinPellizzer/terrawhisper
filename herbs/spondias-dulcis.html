Spondias dulcis

What's the taxonomical classification of Spondias dulcis?

Spondias dulcis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Further taxonomic refinement places the plant in the order Sapindales and the family Anacardiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Spondias and the specific epithet dulcis, forming its complete scientific name.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Spondias dulcis contains a diverse profile of bioactive secondary metabolites including alkaloids, flavonoids, saponins, tannins, and phenolic compounds. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Research suggests that the extracts from the fruit and leaves may exhibit antimicrobial activity against various pathogens. Some studies also indicate that these compounds possess potential cytotoxic effects that could be useful in cancer research. The presence of these phytochemicals supports the traditional use of the plant in treating various ailments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Spondias dulcis?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat digestive issues like diarrhea, dysentery, and stomach aches, as well as skin conditions such as wounds and rashes. The fruit and leaves possess various medicinal properties that help manage inflammatory responses within the body. Some traditional practices utilize extracts from the plant to address urinary tract infections and kidney-related ailments. Additionally, the bark and leaves may be applied topically to soothe minor burns or infections. These applications highlight the plant's diverse role in folk medicine across tropical regions.

What are the herbal preparations of Spondias dulcis?

Spondias dulcis is prepared as decoctions, infusions, syrups, powders, and topical pastes using various parts of the plant including the fruit, bark, leaves, and seeds. The fruit is commonly processed into juices, syrups, or jams to utilize its nutritional and medicinal properties. Infusions made from the leaves are often consumed as teas to address systemic ailments. Additionally, the bark and seeds can be dried and ground into fine powders for internal use or mixed with oils to create medicinal pastes for skin applications. These diverse preparation methods allow for the extraction of different bioactive compounds depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Spondias dulcis can cause allergic reactions such as contact dermatitis, oral itching, or swelling in individuals sensitive to the Anacardiaceae family. Because it belongs to the same botanical family as mangoes and poison ivy, the sap contains urushiol-like compounds that may irritate the skin. Some people might experience gastrointestinal distress or stomach upset if they consume the fruit in excessive amounts. While generally safe for most, those with specific fruit allergies should exercise caution when handling the skin or pulp. Monitoring for any redness or discomfort is recommended when introducing this plant into a diet.
