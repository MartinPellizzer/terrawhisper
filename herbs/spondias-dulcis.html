Spondias dulcis

What's the taxonomical classification of Spondias dulcis?

Spondias dulcis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Further taxonomic refinement places the plant in the order Sapindales and the family Anacardiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Spondias and the specific epithet dulcis, forming its complete scientific name.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Spondias dulcis has a large, evergreen tree structure characterized by a spreading canopy, grayish bark, and trifoliate leaves that are typically 10 to 25 centimeters long. The compound leaves consist of three leaflets with an elliptical shape and an acute apex. Its flowers are small, five-petaled, and white or cream-colored, often blooming in dense clusters. The fruit develops from these flowers into a fleshy, drupe-like structure that is oval or round in shape. These fruits feature a smooth, thin skin that transitions from green to yellow or red when ripe.

What are the pharmacological activities of Spondias dulcis?

This plant has antioxidant, acetylcholine esterase inhibitory, butyrylcholinesterase inhibitory, tyrosinase inhibitory, and alpha-amylase inhibitory activities. These pharmacological properties suggest that Spoondias dulcis extracts may play a significant role in managing various metabolic and neurological conditions. The antioxidant capacity helps protect cells from oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals. Its inhibitory effects on cholinesterase enzymes indicate potential therapeutic value for treating neurodegenerative disorders like Alzheimer's disease. Furthermore, the inhibition of tyrosinase and alpha-amylase suggests the plant could be useful in managing skin pigmentation and regulating blood glucose levels.

According to a study published by "Antioxidants (Basel, Switzerland)", Spondias dulcis leaves and stem bark exhibit various pharmacological activities, including inhibition of acetylcholinesterase, butyrylcholinesterase, tyrosinase, α-amylase, and α-glucosidase. The leaves infusion of Spondias dulcis showed the highest inhibition against acetylcholinesterase (AChE) with 10.10 mg galantamine equivalent (GALAE)/g. Ethyl acetate extracts of the stem bark of Spondias dulcis actively inhibited α-glucosidase. Additionally, methanolic extracts of the leaves and stem bark exhibited the highest tyrosinase inhibitory action. These results suggest that the species could be considered natural phyto-therapeutic agents in medicinal and cosmeceutical applications.
