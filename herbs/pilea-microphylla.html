Pilea microphylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Pilea microphylla?

Pilea microphylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Urticaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Pilea and the species microphylla.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pilea microphylla has small, ovate to elliptical leaves that typically measure between 5 and 15 millimeters in length and feature finely serrated margins. The foliage is characterized by a soft, slightly fleshy texture and a vibrant green color that can vary depending on light exposure. These diminutive leaves are arranged oppositely along slender, creeping stems that allow the plant to form dense, moss-like mats. The plant produces tiny, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the leaf axils rather than large, showy blooms. This compact growth habit and fine leaf structure make it an ideal specimen for terrariums and miniature landscapes.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires bright, indirect light, consistently moist but well-draining soil, and high humidity levels to thrive.

You should place it in a spot where it receives filtered sunlight to prevent delicate leaves from scorching. Using a peat-based potting mix ensures that the root system stays hydrated without becoming waterlogged. Frequent misting or a pebble tray can help maintain the humid environment necessary for its lush appearance.

Monitoring the soil moisture daily prevents the common issue of drying out, which can cause the tiny leaves to wilt.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnobiology and ethnomedicine", Pilea microphylla is used for childbirth and infertility. The research involved interviewing 30 respondents, including 10 males, from September 1996 to September 2000. These respondents were located across 13 different sites, with 12 sites in Trinidad and 1 site in Tobago. The study utilized a non-experimental method to validate the remedies used in the region.
