Pilea microphylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Pilea microphylla?

Pilea microphylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Urticaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Pilea and the species microphylla.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Pilea microphylla targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, lipoxygenase proteins, and various inflammatory signaling cytokines to mediate its therapeutic properties. The plant contains bioactive phytochemicals like flavonoids and phenolic acids that act as inhibitors to reduce prostaglandin synthesis. These compounds interact with the active sites of COX-1 and COX-2 proteins to alleviate pain and swelling. Furthermore, its secondary metabolites can modulate the expression of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and other pro-inflammatory mediators in the cellular environment. Such interactions at the protein level support its traditional use in treating skin irritations and inflammatory conditions.

      What are the herbal preparations of Pilea microphylla?

      Pilea microphylla is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice to utilize its medicinal properties. Practitioners often boil the plant material in water to extract active compounds for oral consumption. Alternatively, steeping the leaves in hot water creates a milder tea suitable for gentle internal use. For external ailments, the fresh or dried leaves are crushed into a paste and applied directly to the skin. These various methods allow for different concentrations of the plant's constituents depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Pilea microphylla can cause skin irritation or allergic reactions such as redness, itching, and dermatitis if the sap comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. While there is limited toxicological data available for humans, handling the plant without protection may trigger discomfort in some individuals. Pets may also experience mild gastrointestinal upset if they ingest any part of the foliage. It is generally considered safe for most people when handled with caution. Always wash your hands thoroughly after gardening with this species to prevent any potential irritation.
