Erigeron multifolius

What's the taxonomical classification of Erigeron multifolius?

Erigeron multifolius belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Asterales, the plant is situated within the family Asteraceae, a group known for its diverse floral structures. Finally, it is identified by the genus Erigeron and the specific species name multifolius.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Erigeron multifolius contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its leaves and flowering heads. The leaves are often valued for their potential anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties when processed correctly. Additionally, the small blossoms may be utilized in topical preparations to soothe skin irritations. Local healers have historically relied on these specific components to address minor ailments.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Erigeron multifolius contains bioactive secondary metabolites including various phenolic acids, flavonoids, and sesquiterpene lactones. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Erigeron multifolius targets cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. The plant contains bioactive secondary metabolites, such as flavonoids and phenolic acids, that inhibit the enzymatic activity responsible for prostaglandin synthesis. These compounds interact with the arachidonic acid pathway to reduce systemic inflammation and oxidative stress. Additionally, specific polyphenols in the extract may modulate the expression of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6. Such molecular interactions support the plant's traditional uses in treating inflammatory conditions and skin ailments.

Erigeron multifolius is prepared as an infusion, a decoction, or a tincture to extract its active botanical compounds. Tinctures made with alcohol allow for a long shelf life and efficient extraction of specific phytochemicals. These diverse preparations enable different methods of administration depending on the intended medicinal application.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Erigeron multifolius can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, redness, or itching in individuals sensitive to the Asteraceae family. While specific toxicological data for this exact species is limited, members of the daisy family often contain sesquiterpene lactones that act as skin irritants. People with known allergies to ragweed, marigolds, or daisies should exercise extreme caution when handling the plant. Accidental ingestion could potentially lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or mild digestive upset. It is generally advised to avoid direct skin contact and internal consumption unless supervised by a medical professional.
