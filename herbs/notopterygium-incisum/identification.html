Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the plant is Notopterygium incisum, first described by the botanist Carl Peter Thunberg in 1794. It has several synonyms, including Angelica incisa and Heracleum incisum, reflecting its historical classification within the Apiaceae family. Common names include Chinese angelica and huang qi, while regional names vary across different cultures and regions. Historically, it was also known by names like "sheng ma" in traditional Chinese medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Notopterygium incisum is a perennial herb with a creeping root system that forms dense mats. Its stems are aromatic, branched, and covered in fine hairs. The leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptic, and have serrated margins. The inflorescence is a terminal umbel, bearing small white to pale yellow flowers. The fruit is a dry, two-lobed achene with a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Notopterygium incisum can be distinguished by its pinnately compound leaves with serrated margins and opposite arrangement. The stems are aromatic and have a distinctive greyish-brown color. It differs from similar species through its unique leaf shape and the presence of characteristic rhizomes with a strong, camphor-like odor.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Notopterygium incisum is native to the mountainous regions of East Asia, particularly China, Korea, and Japan. It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and cold winters. The plant prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found at elevations between 1,500 and 3,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Notopterygium incisum blooms from April to June, producing small yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to September. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested in late summer to early autumn when the leaves are fully developed and the stems are mature.
