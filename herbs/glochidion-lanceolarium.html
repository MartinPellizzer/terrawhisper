Glochidion lanceolarium

What's the taxonomical classification of Glochidion lanceolarium?

Glochidion lanceolarium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Phyllanthaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Glochidion, and its unique species designation, lanceolarium.

    What diseases this plant treats?

    This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, and various types of inflammatory conditions. The leaves and bark are often prepared as a decoction or a poultice to apply directly to affected areas. This method helps to soothe irritations and promote the healing of minor cuts or abrasions. Some traditional practices also utilize the plant to manage internal ailments such as digestive disturbances. Local knowledge regarding its medicinal properties is passed down through generations of herbal practitioners.

    What are the herbal preparations of Glochidion lanceolarium?

    Glochidion lanceolarium is prepared primarily as a decoction of the bark or as a topical paste made from crushed leaves to treat various skin conditions. The bark is often boiled in water for an extended period to extract its medicinal compounds for internal consumption. When used for external ailments, the leaves are macerated into a thick consistency to serve as a soothing poultice. Some practitioners also utilize infusions made from the plant's foliage to create liquid tonics. These different methods allow for the targeted application of the plant's bioactive properties depending on the ailment being addressed.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Glochidion lanceolarium can cause intense skin irritation, localized inflammation, and painful stinging sensations due to the presence of irritating substances or tiny hairs on its surfaces. Contact with the plant often leads to contact dermatitis, characterized by redness, itching, and small bumps on the affected area. Some individuals may experience swelling or a burning sensation that persists for several days after exposure. If the plant's components accidentally enter the eyes or mucous membranes, they can trigger severe discomfort and inflammation. Proper protective clothing should be worn to prevent any accidental physical contact with the foliage.
