Chamomile (Matricaria recutita): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is a flowering plant widely used in herbal medicine for its calming, anti-inflammatory, and digestive benefits.

What is Chamomile (Matricaria Recutita)?

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is an annual herb native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in herbal medicine for its calming and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Chamomile is Matricaria recutita. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division, class, order, family Compositae, genus Matricaria, and species Matricaria recutita. Common names for Chamomile include German chamomile in Europe and Roman chamomile in Italy. It is also known as wild chamomile in North America and apple mint in some parts of Asia. Synonyms for Chamomile include Chamaemelum nobile (botanical) and various regional names such as bauernkamille in Germany and camomilla in Italy.

Plant Description and Morphology

Chamomile is an annual herb that typically grows to a height of 30-60 cm with a branching, upright stem covered in fine, white hairs.

Its leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and have serrated edges with a hairy surface. The flowers are composite, consisting of a yellow central disk surrounded by pale yellow to white, oval-shaped ray florets. The root system is fibrous and shallow, supporting the plant's growth in well-drained soils.

The plant has a mild, apple-like scent, with a slightly bitter taste, and its texture is soft and feathery when fresh.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is native to southern and central Europe, parts of northern Africa, and western Asia, and has been widely cultivated in temperate regions around the world for its medicinal and aromatic properties.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The aerial parts of Chamomile, including the flowers, leaves, and stems, are primarily used medicinally, with the dried flowers being the most commonly utilized component in herbal preparations.

Phytochemical Composition

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids such as apigenin and quercetin, terpenoids like bisabolol and chamazulene, and essential oils that contribute to its therapeutic properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) exhibits pharmacological properties characterized by anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, anxiolytic, and sedative effects, primarily attributed to the presence of volatile oils such as bisabolol and chamazulene, as well as flavonoids like apigenin.

Mechanisms of Action

Chamomile exerts its therapeutic effects through a combination of anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anxiolytic, and sedative properties, primarily mediated by the active compounds such as apigenin, bisabolol, and flavonoids.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Chamomile (Matricaria recutima) is widely used in herbal medicine for its calming effects on the nervous system, gastrointestinal soothing properties, and anti-inflammatory actions, commonly indicated for conditions such as anxiety, insomnia, digestive disorders, and skin irritations.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Chamomile is commonly prepared as a tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water, or as tinctures, capsules, essential oils, and topical preparations such as creams and salves.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of chamomile for adults is 200-400 mg of standardized extract containing 0.3-1.0% essential oil, taken two to three times daily, or as directed by a healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Chamomile is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as allergic reactions, digestive upset, or drowsiness, and it should be avoided by individuals with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family or those taking certain medications due to potential interactions.

Drug Interactions

Chamomile may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, sedatives, and blood pressure medications, due to its potential to enhance their effects or cause additive effects, which could increase the risk of bleeding, drowsiness, or hypotension.

Toxicity and Precautions

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as allergic reactions, gastrointestinal discomfort, or sedation, and should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and by individuals with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, requiring careful attention to moisture levels and pest management during its growth cycle.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Chamomile (Matricaria recutima) typically involve harvesting the flowers when they are fully open, drying them to preserve their active compounds, and then using methods such as steam distillation or solvent extraction to obtain essential oils and other bioactive components.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, in an airtight container to maintain its potency and shelf life of up to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of chamomile (Matricaria recutita) in herbal medicine primarily focus on its anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and anxiolytic properties, with studies indicating its efficacy in treating conditions such as insomnia, digestive disorders, and skin irritations.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chamomile has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to treat digestive issues, insomnia, and skin irritations, with historical records tracing its use back to ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome.

Regulatory Status

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is classified as a dietary supplement in the United States under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994, and it is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the FDA when used appropriately.
