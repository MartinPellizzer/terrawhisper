Chamomile (Matricaria recutita): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is a flowering plant widely used in herbal medicine for its calming, anti-inflammatory, and digestive benefits.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Chamomile (Matricaria Recutita)?

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is an annual herb native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in herbal medicine for its calming and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Chamomile is Matricaria recutita. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division, class, order, family Compositae, genus Matricaria, and species Matricaria recutita. Common names for Chamomile include German chamomile in Europe and Roman chamomile in Italy. It is also known as wild chamomile in North America and apple mint in some parts of Asia. Synonyms for Chamomile include Chamaemelum nobile (botanical) and various regional names such as bauernkamille in Germany and camomilla in Italy.

Plant Description and Morphology

Chamomile is an annual herb that typically grows to a height of 30-60 cm with a branching, upright stem covered in fine, white hairs.

Its leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and have serrated edges with a hairy surface. The flowers are composite, consisting of a yellow central disk surrounded by pale yellow to white, oval-shaped ray florets. The root system is fibrous and shallow, supporting the plant's growth in well-drained soils.

The plant has a mild, apple-like scent, with a slightly bitter taste, and its texture is soft and feathery when fresh.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chamomile is native to temperate regions of Europe, particularly southern and central Europe, and parts of western Asia.

It has been widely distributed across the globe due to its medicinal value and cultivation for commercial use. The plant thrives in mild climates with moderate temperatures, typically between 15°C to 25°C. It prefers well-drained soil and full sun exposure, often growing in dry meadows and grasslands.

Chamomile is adapted to regions with low rainfall and seasonal variations, making it resilient in diverse ecological conditions.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in chamomile are the flowers, specifically the yellow disk and the surrounding white petals.

The flowers are harvested when fully open and at the peak of their potency. They are typically dried and used in various preparations such as teas, tinctures, and capsules. The essential oil extracted from the flowers is also a key component in many pharmaceutical and cosmetic products.

The leaves and stems are occasionally used but are less common in traditional medicinal applications.

Phytochemical Composition

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) contains a variety of active compounds, including flavonoids, terpenoids, and essential oils.

These compounds contribute to its therapeutic effects and are responsible for its anti-inflammatory and calming properties. Secondary metabolites such as apigenin and bisabolol are particularly prominent and play a key role in the plant's medicinal profile. Chemical markers like camazulene and alpha-bisabolol are used to authenticate the quality and potency of chamomile extracts.

These components collectively define the pharmacological activity of the herb.

Pharmacological Properties

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that underpin its use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and anxiolytic effects, making it effective for conditions such as gastrointestinal disorders and anxiety. Biologically, chamomile contains compounds like flavonoids and terpenoids, which contribute to its active mechanisms. Pharmacodynamically, these compounds interact with receptors and enzymes to modulate physiological responses.

System-level effects of chamomile involve calming the central nervous system and reducing oxidative stress throughout the body.

Mechanisms of Action

Chamomile exerts its therapeutic effects through multiple mechanisms involving cellular, biochemical, and systemic interactions.

At the cellular level, it modulates inflammatory responses by inhibiting pro-inflammatory cytokines and reducing oxidative stress. Biochemically, chamomile contains compounds like bisabolol and flavonoids that interact with various enzyme systems and receptor sites. These compounds target the central nervous system, gastrointestinal tract, and immune system to produce its anti-inflammatory, sedative, and antispasmodic effects.

The combined action of these pathways contributes to chamomile's broad range of therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

Therapeutic Uses

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is widely used in herbal medicine for its calming and anti-inflammatory properties.

It is commonly employed to treat anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues such as bloating and indigestion. The plant's active compounds, including apigenin and essential oils, contribute to its effectiveness in soothing the nervous system and reducing inflammation. Chamomile is also used to alleviate symptoms of skin conditions like eczema and acne.

Its mild nature makes it a popular choice for natural remedies across various age groups.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Chamomile is commonly used in various forms, including teas, tinctures, essential oils, and topical applications.

The most popular method is preparing a herbal tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water. Chamomile flowers can also be infused into oils or alcohol to create tinctures for medicinal use. Additionally, the plant is used in the production of creams and salves for skin care.

These forms allow for different therapeutic applications depending on the desired effect.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of chamomile for most adults is typically 200 to 400 milligrams of dried herb per day, divided into two to three doses.

It is important to use a standardized extract concentration of 1:5 to 1:10, which ensures consistent potency and safety. Chamomile should be taken regularly, with a recommended frequency of 2 to 3 times daily, depending on the formulation. The duration of use should not exceed two weeks without consulting a healthcare professional.

To avoid overuse and potential adverse effects, always follow the guidance of a qualified practitioner.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Chamomile is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, drowsiness, and allergic reactions in some individuals.

Common side effects include nausea, vomiting, and stomach discomfort, particularly when taken in high concentrations or on an empty stomach. Allergic reactions can range from mild skin rashes to more severe symptoms like swelling of the face, lips, or throat, especially in people with allergies to related plants like ragweed. Chamomile should be avoided by individuals with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family.

During pregnancy, chamomile is not recommended for use beyond the first trimester due to potential risks to fetal development.

Drug Interactions

Chamomile may interact with certain pharmaceuticals, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.

It can enhance the sedative effects of central nervous system depressants like benzodiazepines and opioids, leading to excessive drowsiness. Chamomile may also interfere with blood thinners such as warfarin, increasing the risk of bleeding. It can interact with supplements like St. John's wort, which may reduce the effectiveness of other medications.

Additionally, chamomile may affect the metabolism of certain drugs due to its influence on liver enzymes.

Toxicity and Precautions

Toxicity and precautions are important considerations when using chamomile, as it is generally safe but can pose risks in certain cases.

The toxic dose of chamomile varies depending on the form and method of consumption, but excessive intake may lead to gastrointestinal upset or allergic reactions. Long-term safety studies are limited, but regular use in recommended amounts is typically considered safe for most individuals. However, prolonged use may increase the risk of adverse effects, particularly in individuals with pre-existing health conditions.

Poisoning risks are rare but possible, especially in children or those who consume large quantities of chamomile tea or extracts.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chamomile is typically grown in temperate climates and requires well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5.

It thrives in full sun, needing at least 6 to 8 hours of direct sunlight daily for optimal growth. Regular watering is necessary during the growing season, but the soil should not become waterlogged to prevent root rot. Chamomile is usually propagated from seeds sown in early spring, though cuttings can also be used for quicker establishment. The plant matures in about 100 to 120 days, with the best harvest time occurring just before the flowers fully open.

Harvesting is done by cutting the flower heads with scissors or shears, ensuring the stems remain intact for drying.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of chamomile involve several key steps to preserve its active compounds.

The first step is drying, where harvested chamomile flowers are spread out in a well-ventilated area or in drying cabinets to reduce moisture content. Once dried, the flowers are ground into a fine powder, which facilitates the extraction process. Extraction is typically achieved through steam distillation, where the dried and ground material is heated to release essential oils.

The distillation process separates the volatile oils from the plant material, resulting in a concentrated chamomile essential oil.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain its potency and quality.

The shelf life of chamomile typically ranges from 1 to 2 years when stored properly. It is highly sensitive to light, which can degrade its active compounds over time. Moisture control is essential to prevent mold growth and maintain the integrity of the plant material.

Store in an airtight container to protect against humidity and pests.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of chamomile (Matricaria recutita) in herbal medicine primarily focus on its anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and anxiolytic properties, with studies indicating its efficacy in treating conditions such as insomnia, digestive disorders, and skin irritations.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chamomile has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to treat digestive issues, insomnia, and skin irritations, with historical records tracing its use back to ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome.

Regulatory Status

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is classified as a dietary supplement in the United States under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994, and it is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the FDA when used appropriately.
