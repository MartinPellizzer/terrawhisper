Chamomile (Matricaria recutita): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is a flowering plant native to Europe and widely cultivated for its calming properties, commonly used to soothe digestive issues and promote relaxation.

What is Chamomile (Matricaria Recutita)?

Chamomile, or Matricaria recutita, is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in herbal medicine for its calming and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Chamomile, scientifically known as Matricaria recutita, is an annual flowering plant in the sunflower family (Asteraceae) native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa.

Common Names and Synonyms

Chamomile, scientifically known as Matricaria recutita, is also referred to by common names such as German chamomile, wild chamomile, and apple weed, and is sometimes confused with the related species Chamomilla officinalis, which is also known as Roman or sweet chamomile.

Plant Description and Morphology

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is an annual herb with a slender, branched stem that grows up to 30-60 cm tall, bearing opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves with hairy surfaces and yellow flowers that develop into brown, hairy, spherical fruits.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is native to southern Europe and western Asia, with its natural range extending from the Mediterranean region to parts of the Middle East and Central Asia.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The aerial parts of Chamomile, including the flowers and leaves, are primarily used medicinally, with the dried flower heads being the most commonly utilized component in herbal preparations.

Phytochemical Composition

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) contains a complex array of phytochemical compounds, including essential oils such as bisabolol and chamazulene, flavonoids like apigenin and quercetin, and various terpenoids and phenolic acids that contribute to its therapeutic properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anxiolytic, and antispasmodic effects, primarily attributed to its active constituents such as flavonoids, terpenoids, and essential oils.

Mechanisms of Action

Chamomile exerts its effects through the interaction of its bioactive compounds, such as flavonoids and terpenoids, with various physiological systems, including the central nervous system, gastrointestinal tract, and immune response.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Chamomile is used to relieve mild digestive issues such as bloating, gas, and indigestion, alleviate symptoms of mild anxiety and insomnia, reduce inflammation and irritation of the mucous membranes, and ease menstrual cramps and menopausal symptoms.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Chamomile is commonly prepared as a tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water, and it is also available in capsule, tincture, and topical膏 forms for various applications.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of chamomile for adults is 200-400 mg of standardized extract containing 0.3-1.0% essential oil, taken two to three times daily, or as directed by a healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While chamomile is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause side effects such as allergic reactions, gastrointestinal discomfort, drowsiness, and in rare cases, interactions with certain medications.

Drug Interactions

Chamomile may interact with medications such as anticoagulants, sedatives, and blood pressure medications due to its potential to enhance their effects or cause adverse reactions.

Toxicity and Precautions

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly those allergic to plants in the Asteraceae family, and should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding unless advised by a healthcare professional.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, requiring careful management of moisture levels and pest control to ensure optimal yield and quality of its medicinal compounds.

Processing and Extraction

Chamomile is typically processed by drying the flower heads after they have fully matured and turned from green to a yellowish-brown color, ensuring the essential oils and active compounds are preserved.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chamomile (Matricaria recutima) should be stored in a cool, dry, and dark place in an airtight container to maintain its potency and prevent moisture, light, and air exposure, which can degrade its active compounds and reduce shelf life to approximately 1 to 2 years when properly stored.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Numerous scientific studies have demonstrated that chamomile (Matricaria recutita) possesses anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anxiolytic properties, with clinical evidence supporting its use in treating conditions such as insomnia, digestive disorders, and mild anxiety.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) has been traditionally used for centuries in herbal medicine to treat digestive issues, reduce inflammation, alleviate anxiety, and promote sleep due to its mild sedative and antispasmodic properties.

Regulatory Status

Chamomile (Matricaria recutita) is classified as a dietary supplement in the United States under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994, and it is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the FDA when used as directed.
