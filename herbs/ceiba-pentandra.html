Ceiba pentandra

What's the taxonomical classification of Ceiba pentandra?

Ceiba pentandra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic journey continues through the order Malvales and the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Ceiba, reaching its specific identification at the species level as pentandra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ceiba pentandra has a large deciduous tree structure characterized by a thick, grayish-brown trunk that often features prominent, conical spines on its younger branches.

The foliage consists of large, paripinnate leaves with five leaflets that are typically elliptical or ovate in shape. During its flowering stage, the tree produces large, cream-colored flowers that are solitary or arranged in small clusters. Once pollinated, these flowers develop into woody, capsule-like fruits that eventually split to release numerous small, winged seeds.

The overall architecture of the tree is spreading, often forming a wide and majestic canopy.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ceiba pentandra contains medicinal properties found in its bark, leaves, flowers, and roots. The bark is frequently used in traditional remedies to treat fever and skin diseases. Extracts from the leaves are often applied topically to heal wounds or consumed to manage digestive issues. The flowers are utilized for their calming effects and to help regulate internal bodily functions. Additionally, the roots are processed to create decoctions that assist in treating various inflammatory conditions.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Ceiba pentandra targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, various inflammatory cytokines, and specific protein kinases to exert its pharmacological effects. The plant's bioactive compounds, such as tannins and flavonoids, inhibit the COX-2 protein to reduce prostaglandin synthesis and alleviate pain. These phytochemicals also interfere with the signaling pathways of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6 to suppress systemic inflammation. Additionally, certain alkaloids present in the species may interact with acetylcholinesterase or other neuroreceptors to influence central nervous system activity. Such multi-target interactions facilitate the plant's documented antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and wound-healing properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ceiba pentandra?

Ceiba pentandra has therapeutic actions that include being bitter, astringent, diuretic, emmenagogue, anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, and hemostatic.

Its bitter properties help stimulate digestion, while its astringent qualities are effective for treating tissue irritation. The plant acts as a diuretic to increase urine output and serves as an emmenagogue to regulate menstrual cycles. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory and antipyretic effects help reduce swelling and lower fevers.

Finally, its hemostatic properties assist in controlling or stopping bleeding.

What are the herbal preparations of Ceiba pentandra?

Ceiba pentandra is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, pastes, and decocted juices using various parts of the plant including the bark, leaves, roots, and flowers. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create medicinal decoctions used for treating skin ailments and digestive issues. Leaves are often crushed into a fresh paste or steeped to make a soothing infusion for topical application or consumption. Root extracts are sometimes processed into fine powders to be mixed with honey or water for internal therapeutic use. These diverse preparations allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be utilized for a wide range of traditional medicinal purposes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Ceiba pentandra can cause allergic skin reactions, digestive upset, and potential toxicity if the bark or leaves are consumed in improper quantities. Some individuals may experience localized irritation or contact dermatitis when touching the plant's exterior surfaces. Ingesting certain parts of the tree without proper preparation can lead to stomach discomfort or nausea. There is limited clinical data regarding the long-term systemic effects of its chemical constituents on human health. Therefore, cautious use is recommended to avoid unexpected adverse reactions.
