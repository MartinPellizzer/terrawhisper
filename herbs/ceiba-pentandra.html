Ceiba pentandra

What's the taxonomical classification of Ceiba pentandra?

Ceiba pentandra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic journey continues through the order Malvales and the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Ceiba, reaching its specific identification at the species level as pentandra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ceiba pentandra has a large deciduous tree structure characterized by a thick, grayish-brown trunk that often features prominent, conical spines on its younger branches. The foliage consists of large, paripinnate leaves with five leaflets that are typically elliptical or ovate in shape. During its flowering stage, the tree produces large, cream-colored flowers that are solitary or arranged in small clusters. Once pollinated, these flowers develop into woody, capsule-like fruits that eventually split to release numerous small, winged seeds. The overall architecture of the tree is spreading, often forming a wide and majestic canopy.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning from India and Sri Lanka through Myanmar, Thailand, and into parts of Indochina. It thrives predominantly in moist deciduous forests and along riverbanks where soil moisture is consistent. Its range extends across various elevations within these tropical climates, allowing it to colonize diverse ecological niches. The distribution is largely dictated by seasonal monsoon patterns that provide the necessary rainfall for its growth. Consequently, these trees are most commonly found in areas characterized by high humidity and warm temperatures throughout the year.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds pentandrin, pentandrins glucoside, mangiferin, beta-sitosterol, and various phenolic compounds. These specific constituents contribute to the diverse therapeutic properties often associated with the species. The presence of mangiferin and phenolic compounds suggests significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory potential. Furthermore, the phytosterols like beta-sitosterol play a role in the plant's biological activity. Together, these bioactive molecules form the chemical basis for its traditional medicinal uses.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Ceiba pentandra targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, various inflammatory cytokines, and specific protein kinases to exert its pharmacological effects. The plant's bioactive compounds, such as tannins and flavonoids, inhibit the COX-2 protein to reduce prostaglandin synthesis and alleviate pain. These phytochemicals also interfere with the signaling pathways of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6 to suppress systemic inflammation. Additionally, certain alkaloids present in the species may interact with acetylcholinesterase or other neuroreceptors to influence central nervous system activity. Such multi-target interactions facilitate the plant's documented antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and wound-healing properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ceiba pentandra?

Ceiba pentandra has therapeutic actions that include being bitter, astringent, diuretic, emmenagogue, anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, and hemostatic. Its bitter properties help stimulate digestion, while its astringent qualities are effective for treating tissue irritation. The plant acts as a diuretic to increase urine output and serves as an emmenagogue to regulate menstrual cycles. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory and antipyretic effects help reduce swelling and lower fevers. Finally, its hemostatic properties assist in controlling or stopping bleeding.

What health problems are treated using this plant?

This plant is used for treating various medical conditions including Trypanoosoma brucei brucei, leishmaniasis, diabetes mellitus, angiogenesis, and sickle cell anemia. Researchers investigate its chemical properties to combat parasitic infections caused by protozoa like Trypanoosoma brucei brucei and the agents responsible for leishmaniasis. Its bioactive compounds also show potential in managing blood glucose levels for those suffering from diabetes mellitus. Furthermore, the plant is studied for its ability to influence angiogenesis, which is crucial in understanding tumor growth and wound healing. Finally, its medicinal applications extend to providing therapeutic benefits for patients living with sickle cell anemia.
