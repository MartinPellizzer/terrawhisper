Ipomoea turbinata

What's the taxonomical classification of Ipomoea turbinata?

Ipomoea turbinata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Solanales. It is a member of the family Convolvulaceae and is further categorized within the genus Ipomoea, ultimately reaching its specific identification as the species turbinata.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Ipomoea turbinata contains various bioactive alkaloids, flavonoids, and phenolic compounds that contribute to its traditional medicinal properties. These chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's documented antioxidant and antimicrobial activities. Researchers have identified specific glycosides within the species that may assist in regulating biological processes. Some studies suggest that the presence of these secondary metabolites offers potential therapeutic benefits for inflammatory conditions. The complex profile of these organic molecules makes the plant a subject of interest in ethnopharmacological studies.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ipomoea turbinata?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, stomach aches, wounds, and inflammation. The leaves are often crushed or prepared into a paste to apply directly to skin ailments. In some traditional practices, the plant is also used to address gastrointestinal discomfort through specific preparations. Its medicinal properties are primarily valued for their ability to soothe irritation and promote healing in localized areas. These applications are rooted in various ethnobotanical traditions where the plant serves as a natural remedy.

What are the herbal preparations of Ipomoea turbinata?

Ipomoea turbinata is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a juice extracted from the plant's leaves and roots. The leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for various ailments. In some traditional practices, the plant material is dried and ground into a fine powder to be mixed with other substances. Freshly squeezed juice from the leaves can also be applied topically or ingested in small quantities. These different methods allow practitioners to tailor the concentration and delivery of the plant's bioactive compounds.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Ipomoea turbinata can cause gastrointestinal distress, neurological disturbances, and potential skin irritation if handled improperly. Ingesting parts of the plant may lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping due to its chemical composition. Some individuals might experience dizziness or altered mental states if toxic alkaloids are consumed in high quantities. Topical contact with the foliage or sap can trigger allergic reactions such as redness, itching, or localized rashes on sensitive skin. It is important to avoid all contact with the plant to prevent these adverse physiological responses.
