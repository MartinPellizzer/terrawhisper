10 Possible Side Effects Of Rheum Officinale (Rhubarb)

Rheum officinale has some side effects when used improperly, such as digestive issues, allergic reactions, and electrolyte imbalance.

These side effects can occur due to excessive consumption, individual intolerance, or improper preparation.

For example, severe diarrhea from rhubarb consumption can lead to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance, causing dizziness and fatigue, which can hinder daily activities and productivity, ultimately affecting a person's quality of life.

This article explains in details the 10 most common side effects of Rheum officinale if used imporperly.

1. Triggers respiratory issues

Rheum officinale causes diarrhea due to its high content of anthraquinone glycosides, particularly rhein and emodin.

These compounds stimulate the intestinal muscles, increasing bowel movement frequency and water secretion into the colon, leading to loose stools or watery diarrhea.

Additionally, rhubarb's bitter properties can irritate the digestive tract, further contributing to this gastrointestinal side effect.

2. Triggers respiratory issues

Rheum officinale induces vomiting in some individuals.

This side effect is likely caused by the high concentration of anthraquinone glycosides, particularly emodin and rhein, which are responsible for its purgative properties. As these compounds stimulate the gut, they can trigger nausea and vomiting as the body attempts to eliminate them through elimination processes.

The intensity of this side effect may vary depending on individual tolerance, dosage, and preparation methods used in consuming Rheum officinale.

Rheum officinale produces abdominal pain due to its laxative properties.

When ingested, rheum officinale stimulates the muscles in the intestines, causing a sudden and intense bowel movement, which can lead to abdominal cramping, spasms, and discomfort.

This is because the plant contains anthraquinone glycosides, which are responsible for its cathartic effects and can irritate the intestinal lining, leading to inflammation and pain.

Rheum officinale triggers allergic reactions due to its high content of anthraquinone glycosides, which can cause an immune response in some individuals.

These compounds are responsible for the plant's laxative properties but may also stimulate an allergic reaction, manifesting as skin rashes, hives, or even anaphylaxis in rare cases.

The exact mechanisms underlying these reactions are not fully understood and may vary depending on individual sensitivities and exposure levels to the plant.

These compounds can cause gastrointestinal irritation, leading to increased blood flow to the gut, which may result in spontaneous bleeding or hemorrhage.

Additionally, the anticoagulant properties of Rheum officinale may contribute to its potential for bleeding complications.

This hypersensitivity reaction may occur in people with pre-existing allergies or sensitivities to related plants.

These compounds can increase the risk of liver damage and injury by disrupting normal liver function and increasing oxidative stress. Additionally, the breakdown products of these glycosides may accumulate in the body and further exacerbate liver toxicity.

As a result, individuals taking Rheum officinale may experience adverse effects on their liver health.

Rheum officinale inhibits blood clotting due to its content of anthraquinone glycosides, which have antiplatelet properties.

These compounds prevent platelets from aggregating and forming clots, potentially leading to increased bleeding risk when used in combination with anticoagulant medications or in individuals with bleeding disorders.

This effect is thought to be responsible for the reported instances of bleeding and hemorrhaging associated with rhubarb use.
