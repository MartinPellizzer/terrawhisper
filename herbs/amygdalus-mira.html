Amygdalus mira

What's the taxonomical classification of Amygdalus mira?

Amygdalus mira belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae, eventually reaching its specific classification within the genus Amygdalus and the species mira.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Amygdalus mira contains amygdalin, cyanogenic glycosides, and various phenolic compounds that serve as its primary medicinal constituents. These bioactive molecules are often studied for their potential cytotoxic properties against specific malignant cell lines. The presence of organic acids and essential oils also contributes to the plant's broader therapeutic profile. Researchers examine these chemical structures to understand how they interact with human metabolic pathways. Ongoing studies aim to isolate these specific elements for targeted pharmacological applications.

What are the therapeutic actions of Amygdalus mira?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat respiratory ailments like coughs, asthma, and bronchitis, as well as skin conditions such as eczema and psoriasis. The medicinal properties are often derived from the seeds or bark through decoctions and topical applications. These extracts work by reducing inflammation and soothing irritated mucosal membranes in the lungs. Additionally, some practitioners utilize it to manage digestive issues by regulating bowel movements. Careful dosage is required to avoid toxicity from natural compounds found within the plant.

What are the herbal preparations of Amygdalus mira?

Amygdalus mira is prepared as a concentrated liquid extract, a dried powder for capsules, or a topical infused oil. The liquid extract is typically produced by soaking the seeds in a high-proof alcohol solvent to pull out active compounds. Practitioners often grind the dried seeds into a fine powder that can be easily encapsulated or mixed into food. For external use, the plant material is macerated in a carrier oil to create a soothing salve or ointment. These diverse forms allow for both internal ingestion and localized skin application depending on the intended therapeutic goal.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Amygdalus mira can cause severe cyanide poisoning, leading to symptoms such as headaches, dizziness, nausea, vomiting, and rapid breathing. If ingestion is significant, the toxins may progress to more dangerous neurological effects like seizures or loss of consciousness. The chemical compounds within the plant interfere with the body's ability to utilize oxygen at a cellular level. This metabolic disruption can eventually result in respiratory failure or cardiac arrest in extreme cases. Regular monitoring of physiological responses is essential when handling or consuming any part of this species.
