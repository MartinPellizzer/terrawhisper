Clematis armandi

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis armandi?

Clematis armandi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Ranunculales. This plant is a distinguished member of the family Ranunculaceae, falling under the genus Clematis, and is specifically identified by its species name, armandi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis armandii has woody, climbing stems with opposite, ovate-shaped leaves that typically measure up to 10 centimeters in length and possess entire margins. The foliage is characterized by a dark green color and a leathery texture that provides a dense backdrop for its blooms. During the flowering season, the plant produces large, four-petaled white flowers that can reach up to 5 centimeters in diameter. These blossoms emerge from terminal clusters and are accompanied by prominent, yellowish stamens that create a striking visual contrast. As the plant matures, it develops a vigorous growth habit capable of reaching significant heights through its twining stems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Clematis armandi?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-tumor, antiproliferative, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities derived from its bioactive chemical constituents. Research indicates that extracts from Clematis armandi can effectively inhibit the growth of various malignant cell lines. These substances demonstrate significant antiproliferative effects by disrupting the normal cell cycle of diseased cells. Furthermore, the plant exhibits potent cytotoxic properties that allow it to induce programmed cell death in targeted tumor tissues. Such multi-faceted biological actions highlight its potential importance in modern oncological pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, the lignan boehmeanan from the dried stems of Clematis armandii exhibits potent cytotoxic effects against many cancer cell lines. In A549 non-small cell lung cancer cells, boehmeanan concentration-dependently suppressed proliferation and induced G1 phase arrest. This treatment led to a reduction in migration and colony formation alongside an increase in apoptosis. The substance also induced mitochondrial membrane potential collapse and markedly modulated apoptosis-related proteins including p53, p21, cleaved caspase 3, and cleaved PARP, as well as cyclin D1 expression. Additionally, boehmeanan concentration-dependently inhibited EGF-induced activation of EGFR and downstream signaling molecules such as MEK, Akt, ERK1/2, and STAT3.

As discussed in a study, boehmenan is a natural product isolated from the dried stem of Clematis armandi. This medicinal compound significantly inhibits the growth of human epidermoid carcinoma A431 cells with an IC50 of 1.6 µM. The substance blocks cell cycle progression at the G2/M phase and modulates mitochondrial apoptosis-related proteins. Boehmenan also induces intracellular reactive oxygen species production and ΔΨm depolarization in a concentration-dependent manner. Additionally, the compound attenuates epidermal growth factor mediated phosphorylation of STAT3 and p70S6/S6 in a concentration-dependent manner.
