Coreopsis tinctoria

What's the taxonomical classification of Coreopsis tinctoria?

Coreopsis tinctoria belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the widely recognized family Asteraceae, which places it among the sunflowers and daisies. Specifically, it is categorized within the genus Coreopsis, and its unique scientific designation is completed by the species name tinctoria.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Coreopsis tinctoria has erect, herbaceous stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and feature deeply lobed or pinnatifid leaves. The foliage is characterized by narrow, linear segments that are arranged alternately along the slender stalks. Its most prominent morphological feature is the composite flower head, which displays bright yellow ray florets surrounding a central disc of darker yellow or orange tubular florets. The involucral bracts at the base of the flower head are often distinct and help protect the developing buds. These plants often produce multiple blooms throughout the growing season, contributing to a dense, bushy appearance.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the central and southern regions of the United States, extending from Texas through the Gulf Coast states and up into the Midwest. It thrives primarily in open prairies, meadows, and disturbed sites across the Great Plains. While its natural range is concentrated in North America, it has been widely introduced to other temperate regions for ornamental purposes. Many gardeners and landscapers plant it globally due to its ability to grow in diverse soil types. Its distribution is often limited by its preference for sunny, well-drained environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Coreopsis tinctoria?

This plant has anti-diabetic, anti-hyperglycemic, hypoglycemic, anti-inflammatory, and anti-oxidant pharmacological activities. Its ability to lower blood sugar levels makes it a significant subject of interest for managing metabolic disorders. The presence of bioactive compounds allows it to act as an effective hypoglycemic agent in various biological models. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory properties help in reducing systemic swelling and irritation. These combined effects are supported by its potent anti-oxidant capacity which protects cells from oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the alcohol extract of Coreopsis tinctoria Nutt (AC) was administered to 30 db/db mice at a dosage of 300 mg/kg/day for 10 weeks. This treatment helped to decrease body weight (BW), fasting blood glucose (FBG), and 24 h urinary albumin excretion (UAE) in diabetic nephropathy mice. AC treatment also reversed the expression changes of proteins such as PI3K p85α, P-AKT, P-smad3, and COL4 α1 that were increased in the kidneys of DN mice. Furthermore, the extract may protect the kidneys by decreasing miR-192 and miR-200b to modulate the activity of the PTEN/PI3K/AKT pathway. These pharmacological activities ultimately work to reduce the degree of renal fibrosis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apiagenin, kaempferol, luteolin, naringenin, and quercetin. These specific flavonoids serve as potent bioactive agents that contribute to the plant's therapeutic profile. Many of these molecules are recognized for their ability to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. Researchers often study these chemical constituents to understand how they interact with cellular pathways to prevent oxidative stress. The presence of such a diverse array of polyphenols makes this species a valuable subject for pharmacological investigation.

As discussed in a study, Coreopsis tinctoria flowering tops contain medicinal compounds including chlorogenic acid, quercetin, luteolin, apiigenin, and kaempferol. When extracted for 15.0 min with ethanol at a concentration of 60.4% and a liquid-solid ratio of 27.5:1, the plant yielded 18.9% total flavonoids and polyphenols. This specific extract demonstrated high scavenging activity in ABTS and hydroxyl radical assays, though it did not show higher activity in the DPPH test. These findings suggest that Coreopsis tinctoria possesses antioxidant and free radical scavenging potentials.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or localized rashes if the sap or foliage comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. While it is often used in landscaping, some individuals may experience itching or redness upon touching the petals or leaves. There is also a potential for respiratory sensitivity if the pollen is inhaled in large quantities. Because clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, caution is advised when handling the plant. People with known allergies to the Asteraceae family should avoid contact to prevent an adverse reaction.
