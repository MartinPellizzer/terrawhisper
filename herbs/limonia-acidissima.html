Limonia acidissima

What's the taxonomical classification of Limonia acidissima?

Limonia acidissima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Sapindales and the family Rutaecaeae. Finally, it is categorized under the genus Limonia, with the specific designation of the species acidissima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Limonia acidissima has a medium-sized tree characterized by a spreading crown, thorny branches, and pinnate leaves that typically consist of five to nine leaflets. The bark is greyish and often becomes fissured or scaly as the tree reaches maturity. Its leaves are leathery in texture with an elliptical shape and a dull green color on the upper surface. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the axils of the branches during the flowering season. The fruit is a woody, globose drupe with a hard, thick rind and a pulpy, acidic interior.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of the Malay Peninsula. It thrives in various tropical and subtropical climates where seasonal rainfall is abundant. The species is frequently found in dry deciduous forests and coastal regions throughout its primary range. Due to human cultivation, it has also been introduced to other tropical zones in Africa and parts of the Caribbean. Its ability to withstand heat makes it well-suited for the diverse landscapes of the Indian subcontinent.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Limonia acidissima contains medicinal compounds found in its fruit pulp, seeds, bark, leaves, and roots. Extracts from the bark and roots are often employed in traditional remedies to manage fever and inflammation. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Limonia acidissima targets various enzymes and receptors including cyclooxygenase-2, alpha-glucosidase, and specific inflammatory mediators to produce its therapeutic effects. The bioactive compounds within the fruit and leaves interact with cyclooxygenase-2 to inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, which explains its potent anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the plant contains phytochemicals that act as inhibitors of alpha-glucosidase, helping to regulate blood glucose levels by slowing carbohydrate digestion. It also influences antioxidant pathways by modulating enzymes like superoxide dismutase and catalase to mitigate oxidative stress. These diverse molecular interactions allow the plant to address conditions ranging from diabetes to systemic inflammation.

What are the therapeutic actions of Limonia acidissima?

Limonia acidissima has therapeutic properties characterized by its bitter, astringent, carminative, digestive, antidiarrheal, antipyretic, and haemostatic actions. Its bitter components help stimulate the appetite and improve overall digestive functions. Due to its astringent nature, the plant serves as an effective antidiarrheal agent to control intestinal issues. The carminative qualities assist in relieving gas and bloating within the gastrointestinal tract. Additionally, its antipyretic properties help in reducing fever, while its haemostatic effects aid in stopping bleeding.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause gastrointestinal distress, allergic skin reactions, or potential toxicity if consumed in excessive amounts due to its high acidity and specific chemical compounds. While the fruit is often used medicinally, eating too much can lead to stomach aches, bloating, or diarrhea. Some individuals may experience contact dermatitis or itchy rashes after touching the skin or sap of the fruit. Because the acidity levels are quite high, it may also irritate the lining of the mouth or throat in sensitive people. Always exercise caution and consult a professional before using this plant for therapeutic purposes.
