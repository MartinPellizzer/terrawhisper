Convolvulus arvensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Convolvulus arvensis?

Convolvulus arvensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Solanales. This plant is a member of the family Convolvulaceae, falling under the genus Convolvulus, and is specifically identified by the species name arvensis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Convolvulus arvensis contains various alkaloids, saponins, and glycosides that contribute to its chemical profile. These secondary metabolites are often studied for their potential biological activities in pharmacological research. Specific alkaloids within the plant may exhibit neuroactive properties or influence cellular signaling pathways. Some researchers also investigate the presence of flavonoids, which are known for their antioxidant effects. While these compounds show promise in laboratory settings, the plant is not widely used in mainstream clinical medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Convolvulus arvensis?

Convolvulus arvensis has traditionally been utilized for its purgative and emmenagogue properties to induce bowel movements or stimulate menstrual flow. The plant contains various alkaloids and saponins that contribute to its ability to act as a potent herbal laxative. Historical applications often focused on its use in treating various internal congestions through its stimulating effects on the digestive system. However, due to its high toxicity and potential to cause severe gastrointestinal irritation, its therapeutic use is strictly limited and requires extreme caution. Modern herbalism generally advises against its internal consumption because the margin between a medicinal dose and a poisonous one is dangerously narrow.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat respiratory ailments like coughs, asthma, and bronchitis, as well as digestive issues like constipation and stomach pain. The roots and leaves contain bioactive compounds that can help soothe irritated mucous membranes in the throat. Some traditional practices also utilize its extracts to manage skin inflammations or minor wounds. However, it is important to note that many parts of the plant can be toxic if ingested in incorrect amounts. Modern medical consensus generally advises caution due to these potential side effects.

What are the herbal preparations of Convolvulus arvensis?

Convolvulus arvensis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture to extract its active constituents for medicinal use. The roots are most commonly used in decoctions where they are boiled in water to release chemical compounds. Infusions of the aerial parts may also be crafted by steeping the leaves and stems in hot water. Tinctures are frequently made using alcohol as a solvent to create a more concentrated and shelf-stable liquid extract. These various preparations allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended therapeutic application.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Convolvulus arvensis can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, including intense vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea, if any part of the plant is ingested. The seeds contain alkaloids that are toxic to both humans and livestock when consumed in significant quantities. These chemical compounds can lead to systemic symptoms such as increased heart rate or neurological disturbances in sensitive individuals. While direct contact with the leaves is generally not considered highly toxic, some people may experience skin irritation or allergic reactions. It is essential to keep this weed away from grazing animals and children to prevent accidental poisoning.
