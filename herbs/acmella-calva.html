Acmella calva

What's the taxonomical classification of Acmella calva?

Acmella calva belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Acmella, and its unique species designation, calva.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acmella calva has a prostrate or ascending growth habit characterized by glandular-pubescent stems and opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves with entire or slightly serrated margins.

The plant typically reaches heights of 10 to 30 centimeters and features a branched structure. Its inflorescences consist of small, yellowish flower heads arranged in terminal clusters. These heads are surrounded by involucral bracts that protect the developing florets.

The overall morphology is defined by its hairy texture and compact, herbaceous form.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Acmella calva contains bioactive compounds primarily located in its entire aerial portion, specifically within the leaves and the flowering heads. The leaves are frequently harvested for their ability to provide topical relief due to their high concentration of alkylamides. These chemical constituents are responsible for the characteristic numbing or tingling sensation experienced when the plant is applied to the skin or mucous membranes. Medicinal preparations often utilize the whole plant to treat various dermatological conditions and oral ailments. The effectiveness of these extracts depends heavily on the concentration of spilanthol found throughout the vegetative parts.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, terpenoids, phenolic compounds, and alkaloids that contribute to its diverse pharmacological properties.

These chemical constituents work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. The presence of phenolic compounds specifically aids in neutralizing free radicals within biological systems. Furthermore, the various alkaloids and terpenoids found in the plant are often linked to its antimicrobial and bioactive activities.

Together, these medicinal compounds make Acmella calva a subject of interest for therapeutic research.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Acmella calva targets specific ion channels, primarily the voltage-gated sodium channels and nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, to induce its characteristic numbing effects. The bioactive spilanthol molecule acts as a potent local anesthetic by modulating these membrane proteins to inhibit nerve impulse conduction. Additionally, the plant interacts with various inflammatory mediators, including cyclooxygenase enzymes, which helps reduce swelling and pain. Its ability to influence neurotransmitter release further contributes to its role in muscle relaxation and topical analgesia. These diverse protein interactions explain why the plant is widely used in traditional medicine for treating dental pain and skin irritation.

What are the therapeutic actions of Acmella calva?

Acmella calva has diverse therapeutic properties including antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, analgesic, anesthetic, astringent, and bitter actions.

The plant contains spilanthol, which provides a potent numbing sensation through its anesthetic and analgesic effects. Its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory qualities help reduce infections and swelling in various tissues. The bitter components stimulate digestion and bile production when consumed.

Additionally, its astringent properties assist in tightening tissues and managing moisture in oral applications.

  • Bitter

  • Anti-inflammatory

  • Analgesic

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat skin inflammation, wounds and cuts, digestive issues, diarrhea, and fever.

Its topical application helps soothe irritated skin and promotes the healing of open lacerations. When ingested, it serves as a traditional remedy for stabilizing gastrointestinal distress and managing diarrhea. The plant is also frequently utilized in folk medicine to help reduce a person's fever.

These diverse medicinal properties make it a versatile tool in various herbal practices.

What are the herbal preparations of Acmella calva?

Acmella calva is prepared as a topical paste, a concentrated liquid extract, or a decoction of the dried aerial parts. The plant's characteristic numbing sensation is most effectively utilized when the fresh leaves are crushed directly onto the skin for localized relief. Practitioners often create tinctures using high-proof alcohol to stabilize the bioactive alkylamides for long-term storage. Some traditional methods involve simmering the plant material in water to create a tea used for oral applications. These diverse forms allow for varying concentrations of the active compounds depending on the intended therapeutic use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Acmella calva can cause localized skin irritation, allergic reactions, and temporary numbness of the mouth and tongue. The plant contains spilanthol, a bioactive compound that induces a tingling or numbing sensation upon contact with mucous membranes. Some individuals may experience swelling or redness if the extract is applied directly to sensitive areas of the skin. Allergic contact dermatitis is a documented risk for those with sensitivities to the Asteraceae family. Because it is often used in topical applications, it is important to monitor for any signs of inflammatory responses.
