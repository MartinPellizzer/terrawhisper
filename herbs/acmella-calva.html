Acmella calva

What's the taxonomical classification of Acmella calva?

Acmella calva belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Acmella, and its unique species designation, calva.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Acmella calva contains high concentrations of spilanthol, a bioactive alkylamide that provides its characteristic numbing and anesthetic effects. This primary compound acts on sensory neurons to induce localized desensitization of the mucous membranes. In addition to spilanthol, the plant possesses various essential oils and terpenoids that contribute to its antimicrobial properties. These diverse secondary metabolites work together to support its traditional use in treating oral pain and inflammatory conditions. The synergy of these chemical constituents makes the plant a significant subject for dermatological and dental pharmacological research.

What are the therapeutic actions of Acmella calva?

Acmella calva has potent analgesic, anesthetic, and anti-inflammatory properties primarily due to the presence of spilanthol, a bioactive alkylamide. When applied topically, this compound acts as a local anesthetic by inhibiting nerve impulses in the skin. It also helps to reduce swelling and soothe irritation through its ability to suppress inflammatory mediators. Many traditional remedies use the plant to alleviate dental pain and oral discomfort because of its numbing effect on mucous membranes. These combined therapeutic actions make it a valuable natural ingredient for treating minor skin irritations and oral ailments.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, intestinal parasites, and digestive issues. It contains bioactive compounds that provide significant antibacterial and antifungal properties for topical applications. When consumed, the plant helps expel worms and other intestinal parasites from the body. It is also frequently utilized in traditional medicine to soothe gastrointestinal distress and inflammation. Local healers often prepare extracts from the plant to accelerate the healing process of various skin ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Acmella calva?

Acmella calva is prepared as a topical paste, a concentrated liquid extract, or a decoction of the dried aerial parts. The plant's characteristic numbing sensation is most effectively utilized when the fresh leaves are crushed directly onto the skin for localized relief. Practitioners often create tinctures using high-proof alcohol to stabilize the bioactive alkylamides for long-term storage. Some traditional methods involve simmering the plant material in water to create a tea used for oral applications. These diverse forms allow for varying concentrations of the active compounds depending on the intended therapeutic use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Acmella calva can cause localized skin irritation, allergic reactions, and temporary numbness of the mouth and tongue. The plant contains spilanthol, a bioactive compound that induces a tingling or numbing sensation upon contact with mucous membranes. Some individuals may experience swelling or redness if the extract is applied directly to sensitive areas of the skin. Allergic contact dermatitis is a documented risk for those with sensitivities to the Asteraceae family. Because it is often used in topical applications, it is important to monitor for any signs of inflammatory responses.
