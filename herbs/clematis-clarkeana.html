Clematis clarkeana

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis clarkeana?

Clematis clarkeana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and follows the order Ranunculales. This plant is a member of the family Ranunculaceae, specifically falling under the genus Clematis, with its unique biological identity defined by the species clarkeana.

Clematis clarkeana contains no documented medicinal parts because it is not recognized as a primary medicinal plant in traditional or modern pharmacology. Instead of being utilized for healing, the plant is primarily valued by botanists for its ornamental beauty and role in its native ecosystem. Therefore, researchers focus on its ecological significance rather than its potential pharmacological properties.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Clematis clarkeana can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential poisoning if ingested due to the presence of protoanemonin. This specific chemical compound is a toxin found in many members of the Ranunculaceae family. Contact with the plant's leaves or stems often results in redness, itching, or blistering on the skin. If a person accidentally swallows any part of the vine, they may experience severe stomach cramps, vomiting, or diarrhea. While it is a common wildflower in certain regions, caution is necessary when handling it to avoid these adverse reactions.
