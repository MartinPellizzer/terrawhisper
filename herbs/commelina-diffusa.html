Commelina diffusa

What's the taxonomical classification of Commelina diffusa?

Commelina diffusa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Commelinales and is the sole representative of its family, Commelinaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is completed within the genus Commelina, specifically as the species diffusa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Commelina diffusa has a spreading, decumbent growth habit characterized by slender, branched stems and small, lanceolate leaves that are arranged oppositely along the axes. The foliage is typically thin and elliptic to linear in shape, often displaying a subtle pubescence on the leaf surfaces. Its flowering structure consists of small, inconspicuous blooms that emerge from the leaf axils. The plant's overall architecture is diffuse, meaning the branches spread outward rather than growing strictly upright. These morphological traits allow the species to colonize open, rocky habitats effectively.

What are the pharmacological activities of Commelina diffusa?

This plant has antibacterial, antidiabetic, and antihyperglycemic pharmacological activities that make it a significant subject of medicinal research. Its antibacterial properties allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms by inhibiting their growth or survival. In terms of metabolic health, the plant demonstrates potent antidiabetic effects through its ability to regulate glucose levels. Specifically, its antihyperglycemic activity helps lower high blood sugar concentrations in the body. These diverse biological actions highlight its potential utility in treating infectious diseases and managing diabetes.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Commelina diffusa exhibits antibacterial activity by inhibiting the growth of Staphylococcus aureus at a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) of ≥4 μg/mL. Furthermore, this plant did not produce toxicity towards the standard line of normal adult human dermal fibroblasts, with an IC50 > 128 μg/mL. These pharmacological properties suggest that the species can be used for the development of new wound healing agents.
