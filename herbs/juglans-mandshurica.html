Juglans mandshurica

What's the taxonomical classification of Juglans mandshurica?

Juglans mandshurica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the biological hierarchy, it is placed in the order Fagales and the family Juglandaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Juglans and is specifically distinguished as the species mandshurica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Juglans mandshurica has large, odd-pinnately compound leaves measuring 30 to 60 centimeters in length, consisting of 11 to 25 leaflets with serrated margins and acute tips. The leaflets are typically dark green on the upper surface and slightly paler underneath, often displaying a smooth or slightly rugose texture. Its bark is characterized by deep, longitudinal furrows that provide a rugged texture to the mature trunk. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers in catkins, which are monoecious and emerge in early spring. The fruit is a large, fleshy drupe containing a hard, wrinkled nut that is encased in a thick green husk.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad region across Northeast Asia, including parts of northern China, the Korean Peninsula, and the Russian Far East. It typically inhabits temperate deciduous forests where it can thrive in varied mountainous and lowland terrains. The species is frequently found in provinces such as Heilongjiang and Jilin in China as well as throughout the Sakhalin and Primorsky regions of Russia. Its range extends southward into the northernmost reaches of the Korean Peninsula. These specific environmental conditions allow the plant to maintain stable populations across its fragmented eastern distribution.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Juglans mandshurica contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its hard nut shells, inner hulls, and the kernels of its seeds. The outer shells are frequently utilized in traditional practices to treat skin conditions and inflammatory ailments due to their high tannin content. Meanwhile, the nutrient-dense kernels are consumed to support cardiovascular health and improve cognitive function. Some traditional formulations also incorporate the leaves to address various systemic imbalances. These diverse components work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic benefits.

What are the pharmacological activities of Juglans mandshurica?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidant, anti-tumor, antimicrobial, and immunomodulatory pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within cellular environments. These chemical properties also allow the plant to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms and suppress harmful inflammatory responses. Furthermore, the species demonstrates significant potential in regulating immune system functions and interfering with cancer cell proliferation. Such diverse biological actions make Juglans mandshurica a subject of intense interest in medicinal research.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in pharmacology", Juglans mandshurica has been traditionally used to treat cancer, gastric ulcers, diarrhea, dysentery, dermatosis, uterine prolapse, and leukopenia. More than 400 constituents including quinones, phenolics, flavonoids, triterpenoids, coumarins, lignans, phenylpropanoids, diarylheptanoids, and steroids were isolated from different plant parts. Major bioactive substances such as quinones, phenolics, triterpenoids, and diarylheptanoids have displayed significant bioactivity. The plant and its active components exhibit properties including antitumoor, immunomodulatory, anti-inflamatory, neuroprotective, anti-diabetic, antiviral, antimicrobial, and anti-melanogenesis activities.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive secondary metabolites including naphthoquinone, various flavonoids, kaempferol, quercitrin, and tannins. The presence of naphthoquinone is particularly significant for its potential antimicrobial and antifungal properties. Within the flavonoid group, specific compounds such as kaempferol and quercitrin contribute to the plant's antioxidant and anti-inflammatory capabilities. Additionally, the tannins found in Juglans mandshurica assist in astringent activities and help protect the plant from environmental stressors. Together, these medicinal compounds form a complex chemical profile that supports many of the plant's traditional therapeutic uses.

According to a study published by "Zhongguo Zhong yao za zhi = Zhongguo zhongyao zazhi = China journal of Chinese materia medica", naphthoquinone are the major markers in samples of Juglans mandshurica from different harvest periods. Thirty-eight of naphthalenequinone were identified or inferred in Juglans mandshurica, and their contents decline gradually. The analysis involved eighteen batch samples of Juglans mandshurica from six harvest periods. This study utilized UPLC-Q-TOF-MS/MS to analyze changes in effective components. The identified main chemical constituents can be used for quality evaluation and efficacy material research.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for providing anti-tumor activity against various forms of cancer, specifically targeting hepatic cancer, human colon carcinoma, and human lung carcinoma. Research indicates that the bioactive compounds within Juglans mandshurica possess significant potential in inhibiting the growth of malignant cells. These medicinal properties are particularly noted for their ability to combat liver-related malignancies and digestive tract tumors. Furthermore, studies have demonstrated its effectiveness in addressing cellular abnormalities found in lung tissue. This plant serves as a critical subject in pharmacological studies focused on suppressing tumor progression.

According to a study published by "Journal of separation science", Juglans mandshurica is used as a traditional medicinal plant for the treatment of cancer. Different organs of the plant have shown anti-tumor activity in both clinical and laboratory settings. Through analysis, 111 compounds were identified, which consist of 56 tannins, 29 flavonoids, 13 organic acids, 8 naphthalene derivatives, and 5 anthracenes. These identified compounds were distributed such that 76 were found in the root extract, 67 in the branch extract, and 37 in the leaf extract. Only six compounds, including four organic acids and two tannins, were present in all three plant extracts.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction of the bark or hulls, a tincture, an infusion, a powder, an extract, or an oil. Practitioners often use a decoction of the bitter bark or hulls to address digestive or parasitic concerns. A tincture or a concentrated extract provides a potent way to administer the plant's active compounds in small doses. Infusions of the leaves or softer parts of the plant offer a gentler method for daily consumption. The dried components can also be ground into a fine powder for topical applications or dietary supplementation. Finally, the oil extracted from the seeds is frequently utilized for its emollient and skin-soothing properties.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and general digestive upset in sensitive individuals. Contact with its components may trigger skin irritation or respiratory issues for those with known sensitivities. Ingesting parts of the plant can lead to significant gastrointestinal distress. Symptoms such as nausea and vomiting often occur shortly after exposure. Prolonged or severe cases may result in persistent diarrhea and overall digestive upset.
