Ipomoea batatas

What's the taxonomical classification of Ipomoea batatas?

Ipomoea batatas belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Solanales. As a member of the family Convolvulaceae, this plant is specifically categorized under the genus Ipomoea, with its unique identity defined by the species name batatas.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ipomoea batatas has a prostrate or climbing growth habit characterized by herbaceous, perennial vines with alternate, cordate, or lobed leaves and tuberous adventitious roots. The leaves typically exhibit varying shapes ranging from heart-shaped to deeply palmate with serrated margins. These vines produce trumpet-shaped flowers that are usually pale yellow, white, or pink depending on the cultivar. The plant's most distinctive morphological feature is the development of large, starchy storage roots that swell beneath the soil surface. These tubers serve as the primary energy reservoir for the plant throughout its life cycle.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Central and South America, specifically spanning from Mexico through the Caribbean and into Brazil. Due to its high adaptability, it has been widely naturalized across nearly all tropical and subtropical zones globally. It is frequently found in various ecosystems ranging from coastal areas to inland agricultural landscapes in Africa, Asia, and the Pacific Islands. The species thrives in warm climates with consistent moisture, allowing it to spread rapidly through many different continents. Its ability to tolerate diverse soil types has facilitated its presence in many different parts of the world.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ipomoea batatas contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its edible roots, leaves, and stems. The starchy tubers serve as a foundational source of nutrition and are often utilized to treat digestive issues or provide energy during recovery. The leaves are frequently processed into poultices or infusions to address skin inflammations and minor wounds. Additionally, the stems can be used in traditional preparations to alleviate certain internal ailments. What are the pharmacological activities of Ipomoea batatas?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidant, anti-tumor, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities. These diverse therapeutic properties are primarily attributed to the presence of bioactive secondary metabolites such as phenolics, flavonoids, and alkaloids. The anti-oxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals, which in turn supports the plant's anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor mechanisms. Furthermore, its anti-cancer potential is studied for its ability to inhibit the growth of malignant cells. The antimicrobial action provides an additional layer of defense by suppressing the proliferation of various pathogenic microorganisms.

As discussed in a study, Ipomoea batatas polysaccharides (IBPs) have been implicated in inhibiting diverse cancer types. These IBPs significantly impede the proliferation of lung cancer cells by inducing cytostatic macroautopaghia both in vitro and in vivo. The mechanism involves IBPs specifically promoting ubiquitination-mediated degradation of PAK1 (p21-activated kinase 1) to block its downstream Akt1/mTOR signaling pathway. This process leads to an increased autopaghic flux that suppresses tumor development in lung cancer xenografts in mice. Ultimately, the study characterizes IBPs as a potential therapeutic agent for lung cancer treatment.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains chlorogenic acid, anthocyanins, flavonoids, saponins, and carotenoids which serve as its primary medicinal compounds. The anthocyanins and flavonoids are powerful antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress and inflammation. Chlorogenic acid contributes to metabolic health by assisting in blood sugar regulation and weight management. Carotenoids are essential for maintaining healthy vision and supporting a robust immune system. Additionally, the presence of saponins provides potential antimicrobial and cholesterol-lowering benefits.

As discussed in a study, chlorogenic acid compounds (CGAs) are extracted from Ipomoea batatas leaves using ionic liquids-ultrasound (IL-UAE). These CGAs consist of six isomers of chlorogenic acid with a total purity of 95.69%. These compounds facilitate megakaryocyte differentiation and thrombocytopoiesis via the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway. The medicinal value of these CGAs involves promoting platelet formation in a murine model of ITP. This process relies on the activation of the PI3K/AKT pathway to facilitate platelet production.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause serious illness due to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides which release toxic cyanide upon ingestion. Consuming large quantities of these compounds can lead to severe gastrointestinal distress. Common symptoms resulting from this toxicity include nausea and vomiting. Individuals may also experience significant diarrhea and acute abdominal pain. These adverse reactions underscore the importance of careful preparation or avoidance of certain parts of the plant.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Toxicity from cyanogenic glycosides
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Abdominal pain
