Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Elderberry, scientifically known as Sambucus canadensis, is a shrub native to North America that has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported immune-boosting properties and various therapeutic applications.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Elderberry (Sambucus Canadensis)?

Elderberry, or Sambucus canadensis, is a deciduous shrub native to North America that is widely used in herbal medicine for its berries, flowers, and leaves, which contain compounds with antiviral, antioxidant, and immune-supporting properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Elderberry is Sambucus canadensis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Dipsacales, family Caprifoliaceae, genus Sambucus, and species Sambucus canadensis. It is commonly known as American elderberry in North America and European elderberry in Europe. The plant is also referred to as black elderberry in some regions and is sometimes called the elder in Britain. Synonyms include Sambucus nigra subsp. canadensis (botanical) and red elderberry (regional).

Plant Description and Morphology

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) is a deciduous shrub or small tree that typically grows to a height of 3 to 12 feet, featuring compound leaves with 5 to 7 oval-shaped leaflets, gray-green upper surfaces, and white undersides, along with clusters of small, white, fragrant flowers that appear in late spring to early summer.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) is native to eastern North America, ranging from southern Canada through the northeastern United States, including states such as New York, Pennsylvania, and parts of the Midwest.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The plant parts used medicinally in Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) include the berries, flowers, and leaves, with the berries being the most commonly utilized for their high content of flavonoids and anthocyanins.

Phytochemical Composition

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) contains a variety of bioactive compounds including flavonoids such as quercetin and rutin, anthocyanins, phenolic acids, and glycoproteins, which contribute to its medicinal properties and antioxidant effects.

Pharmacological Properties

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) exhibits pharmacological properties including antiviral, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, primarily attributed to its high content of flavonoids, anthocyanins, and organic acids.

Mechanisms of Action

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) exerts its effects through the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, anthocyanins, and polyphenols, which exhibit antiviral, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and immune-modulating properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support immune function, alleviate symptoms of colds and flu, and reduce inflammation due to its high content of flavonoids, antioxidants, and vitamin C.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Elderberry is typically prepared as a syrup, tea, tincture, or capsule, with the most common method involving simmering the dried or fresh berries with sugar and water to create a thick, dark syrup that is then used as a base for various remedies.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) for immune support ranges from 1 to 5 mL of standardized extract three times daily, or 10 to 30 mL of non-standardized tincture, depending on the concentration and form of the preparation.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While elderberry is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals, and should be avoided by those with autoimmune disorders, pregnant or breastfeeding women, and individuals taking certain medications due to potential interactions.

Drug Interactions

Elderberry may interact with medications such as anticoagulants, diabetes medications, and immunosuppressants, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding, hypoglycemia, or reduced drug effectiveness.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it contains compounds such as sambucol and mucilage that may cause gastrointestinal upset, and improper preparation or excessive consumption can lead to nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea, particularly in children or individuals with sensitive digestive systems.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and partial to full sun, requiring regular watering during dry periods and pruning to encourage fruit production and maintain plant health.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) typically involve harvesting the ripe berries, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as cold pressing, ethanol extraction, or aqueous maceration to obtain active compounds like anthocyanins and flavonoids, which are then used in herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, and dried berries can maintain potency for up to one year when properly sealed in an airtight container.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) has primarily focused on its antiviral, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties, with studies suggesting potential benefits in reducing the severity and duration of cold and flu symptoms.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Elderberry has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to support immune function, reduce fever, and alleviate symptoms of respiratory infections, with historical records tracing its use back to ancient European and Indigenous North American practices.

Regulatory Status

In the United States, Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis) is not approved as a drug by the FDA, but it is recognized as a dietary supplement under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994, allowing it to be sold without prior approval as long as it is labeled appropriately and does not make unproven therapeutic claims.
