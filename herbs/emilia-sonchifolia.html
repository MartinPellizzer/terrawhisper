Emilia sonchifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Emilia sonchifolia?

Emilia sonchiifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and follows the order Asterales. As a member of the family Asteraceae, this plant is categorized under the genus Emilia, with its specific designation being the species sonchiifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Emilia sonchifolia has deeply lobed, pinnatifid leaves that are typically 3 to 10 centimeters wide and characterized by a bright green color and a somewhat succulent texture. The leaf margins are irregular and often feature rounded or blunt teeth that give them a coarse appearance. Its stems are slender, somewhat hairy, and can grow upright or sprawl along the ground. The plant produces small, tubular, pinkish-purple flowers that are clustered in dense, head-like inflorescences. These flower heads are surrounded by specialized bracts and sit atop long, thin peduncles.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically occurring within the territories of Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia. Its distribution is largely confined to humid, lowland environments where rainfall is consistent throughout the year. Within these regions, it is most frequently found in undisturbed forest understories or shaded riparian zones. Localized populations may also exist in specific island ecosystems across the Malay Archipelago. The species thrives primarily in areas characterized by high humidity and stable, warm temperatures.

What are the pharmacological activities of Emilia sonchifolia?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anti-tumour, antibacterial, antifungal, and antioxidant properties. Its diverse chemical profile allows it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungal species. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively neutralize free radicals to provide potent antioxidant protection. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates a capacity to reduce inflammation and disrupt the development of certain cancer cell lines. These multifaceted biological effects make Emilia sonchiifolia a subject of interest for drug development.

As discussed in a study, Emilia sonchifolia (L.) DC demonstrates significant anti-tumour, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant activities. When a whole plant genuine extract of 25 mg/kg bodyweight was administered intraperitoneally to C57BL/6 mice, it significantly inhibited pulmonary tumour formation and increased the life span of animals. The treatment significantly lowered levels of lung collagen hydroxyproline, uronic acid, hexosaamine, serum sialic acid, γ-glutamyl transpeptidase, vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), granulocyte monocyte colony-stimulating factor, and other cytokine levels. Furthermore, Emilia sonchifolia down regulated the expression of matrix metalloproteinases, extracellular signal-regulated kinases, and VEGF while up regulating the expression of tissue inhibitor of matrix metalloproteinases. This study provides the first demonstration of the anti-metastatic potential of this medicinal herb.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds quercetin, luteolin, rhamnetin, isorrhamnetin, and senkirkine. These specific flavonoids and alkaloids are known for providing various biological activities within the human body. Quercetin and luteolin are frequently studied for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of rhamnetin and isorrhamnetin further contributes to the complex chemical profile of the species. Additionally, the alkaloid senkirkine plays a distinct role in the plant's overall pharmacological potential.

As discussed in a study, fifteen compounds were separated from the ethyl acetate fraction of a 90% ethanolic extract of the aerial parts of Emilia sonchifolia. These identified compounds include rhamnetin (1), isorrhamnetin (2), quercetin (3), luteolin (4), tricin-7-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (5), 8-(2"-pyrrolidinone-5"-yl) -quercetin (6), 5, -2', 6'-trihydroxy-7, 8-dimethoxyflavone-2'-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (7), succinic acid (8), fumaric acid (9), p-hydroxybenzoic acid (10), 4-hydroxy isophthalic acid (11), 3, 4-dihydroxycinnamic acid (12), esculetin (13), isowedelolactone (14) and uracil (15). All of these constituents were separated from this genus for the first time with the exception of compound 3.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, redness, itching, and potentially painful blistering upon direct contact with sensitive areas of the body. The sap or foliage may trigger contact dermatitis in individuals who are particularly susceptible to plant allergens. Some people might experience swelling or a stinging sensation if the juices are applied to broken skin. While systemic toxicity is less documented, it is always best to avoid ingestion to prevent internal discomfort. Always wash your hands thoroughly after handling any part of this species to mitigate these risks.
