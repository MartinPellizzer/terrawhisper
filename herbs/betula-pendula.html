Betula pendula

What's the taxonomical classification of Betula pendula?

Betula pendula belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Fagales and is a member of the family Betulaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is completed by its designation within the genus Betula and its specific species name, pendula.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Betula pendula has a slender, upright trunk characterized by smooth, silvery-white bark that develops dark, diamond-shaped fissures near the base as the tree matures. The branches are notably pendulous, drooping gracefully toward the ground to give the species its common name, the silver birch. Its leaves are small, triangular, and possess doubly serrated margins with pointed tips. During the growing season, these leaves are a vibrant green, but they transition to a bright yellow in autumn before falling. The tree produces small, catkin-like flowers that eventually develop into winged seeds for wind dispersal.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to most of Europe and parts of northern and central Asia, spanning from the British Isles and Scandinavia through to Siberia. It thrives in temperate climates where it can colonize open landscapes and disturbed soils. The species is frequently found in mountainous regions and across various latitudes as long as the conditions remain relatively cool. Its range extends significantly eastward across the vast forests of Russia. Because it is highly adaptable, it can inhabit a wide variety of terrains across the Eurasian landmass.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Betula pendula contains medicinal compounds primarily found in its leaves, bark, and sap. The leaves are frequently utilized in herbal teas to act as a natural diuretic and to support kidney function. Sap collected from the trunk serves as a nutrient-rich tonic that helps flush toxins from the body during the spring. The bark contains betulin, a triterpene that possesses anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. These various components are often processed into tinctures or topical ointments to treat skin conditions and urinary tract issues.

What are the pharmacological activities of Betula pendula?

This plant has diuretic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, wound-healing, and diaphoretic pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents help reduce bodily inflammation and combat oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals. The leaves and bark also promote fluid excretion and stimulate perspiration to assist in detoxification processes. Additionally, the plant contains bioactive compounds that accelerate the recovery of damaged tissues during the wound-healing process. These diverse therapeutic properties make Betula pendula a significant subject in herbal pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, Betula pendula has been widely used in Russian and Chinese traditional medicine mainly as a diuretic and diaphoratric agent, but also as an antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic. The chemical composition of extracts from the buds of this species was investigated using GC-MS, which identified a total of 150 substances of different classes. The main components of extracts from Betula pendula buds were triterpenoids. Biological assays indicated that the birch bud extracts demonstrated time- and concentration-dependent and differential cytotoxicity. Furthermore, the bud extracts could be a promising source of compounds with cytotoxic activity against various cancers.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds acacetin, apiogenin, betulin, betulinic acid, and kaempferol. These specific phytochemicals contribute to a wide range of biological activities within the human body. For instance, the flavonoids apiogenin and kaempferol are often recognized for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The triterpenoids betulin and betulinic acid are also significant due to their potential roles in supporting cellular health and immune responses. Additionally, acacetin provides further therapeutic benefits through its unique chemical structure. Together, these compounds form a complex profile of bioactive substances.

As discussed in a study, Silver birch contains the medicinal compound acacetin. This compound is a di-hydroxy and mono-methoxy flavone. Acacetin exhibits diverse therapeutic potential in cancer, inflammation, infections, and other metabolic disorders. It has demonstrated chemopreventive and cytotoxic properties in cancer cell lines. The literature also reveals its ability to prevent injury induced by LPS, MPP+, and MPTP.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, rheumatism, wound healing, urinary tract infections, and skin cancer. The bark and leaves contain bioactive compounds that help reduce swelling and soothe aching joints associated with rheumatic conditions. Its antiseptic properties also assist in the process of wound healing by preventing infection in damaged tissue. What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a leaf infusion for mild diuretic effects, a bark decoction for treating urinary tract issues, a tincture from both leaves and bark for concentrated therapeutic use, an essential oil from the leaves for aromatherapy, and an extract from the leaves for standardized herbal supplementation. The infusion of the leaves is often consumed as a gentle tea to support kidney function. For tougher materials like the bark, a long-simmering decoction is required to extract beneficial compounds. Tinctures provide a shelf-stable way to utilize both the foliage and the woody bark in liquid form. The essential oil distilled from the leaves offers a potent aromatic application, while leaf extracts serve as concentrated doses for specific medicinal protocols.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions including skin rashes and itching, respiratory symptoms, nausea, and gastrointestinal upset. Individuals with sensitivities to birch pollen may experience significant respiratory symptoms such as sneezing or congestion upon contact. Contact with the plant or its sap can lead to localized skin reactions like redness, itching, or a persistent rash. Ingesting parts of the plant might trigger digestive issues including nausea and general gastrointestinal upset. These adverse effects vary depending on the individual's level of sensitivity to the tree's components.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Allergic reactions (skin rash, itching)
  • Allergic reactions (respiratory symptoms)
  • Nausea
  • Gastrointestinal upset
