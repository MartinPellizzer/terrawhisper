What to know about Cynara scolymus (artichoke) before using it medicinally

Artichoke, scientifically known as Cynara scolymus, is a nutrient-rich herb that has been prized for its impressive health benefits.

Not only does it help to lower cholesterol levels and improve digestion, but it also boasts anti-inflammatory properties that can alleviate symptoms of arthritis and other inflammatory diseases. In the culinary world, artichoke is a versatile ingredient used in everything from savory soups to tangy sauces, while its tender leaves are often enjoyed as a delicacy when steamed or grilled. Horticulturally, artichokes thrive in well-drained soil with full sun, making them an attractive addition to many home gardens and landscapes.

As for its botanical properties, the artichoke is classified as a perennial thistle, with a unique flower head that is both edible and ornamental, a feature that has been noted by botanists since ancient times, including the Greek physician Dioscorides, who wrote extensively on its medicinal uses in his 1st-century treatise "De Materia Medica".

What are the pest and diseases tips of Cynara scolymus?

Cynara scolymus, also known as artichoke, is a popular perennial crop that is susceptible to various pests and diseases.

One of the most common pests affecting artichokes is the artichoke plume moth (Plasmatica chalepata), which lays its eggs on the leaves and stems, causing damage to the plant's growth and development. Additionally, aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites can also infest artichokes, feeding on their sap and spreading diseases such as virus yellows and leaf spot.

As for diseases, artichokes are prone to fungal infections like botrytis blight, caused by Botrytis cinerea, which attacks the flowers and leaves, causing grayish-black lesions and a foul odor. Another common disease is powdery mildew, caused by Leveillula taurica, which covers the leaves with a white powder-like coating, reducing photosynthesis and plant vigor. Other diseases affecting artichokes include leaf spot caused by Colletotrichum spp., crown rot caused by Fusarium oxysporum f. sp.

cynarae, and root rot caused by various species of Pythium and Phytophthora.
