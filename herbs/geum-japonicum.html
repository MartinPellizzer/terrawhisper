Geum japonicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Geum japonicum?

Geum japonicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent through the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae, the plant is further defined by its genus, Geum, and its specific species, japonicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Geum japonicum has a perennial growth habit characterized by a creeping rhizome and basal leaves that are typically kidney-shaped with serrated margins and long petioles. The flowering stems rise from the base and are covered in fine, soft hairs that give the plant a slightly pubescent texture. Each stem eventually produces small, five-petaled flowers that range in color from pale yellow to a deep, reddish-orange hue. The petals are often slightly asymmetrical, and the central reproductive structures are surrounded by numerous stamens. After pollination, the plant produces small, ribbed achenes that facilitate its dispersal.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically occurring in the wild throughout Japan, Korea, and parts of China. Within these regions, it typically thrives in moist environments such as meadows, forest edges, and mountain slopes. Its range extends across various elevations, allowing it to inhabit both lowland areas and higher altitudes. While primarily found in its indigenous territory, it is sometimes encountered in cultivated gardens elsewhere. This specific distribution patterns reflect its preference for temperate climates with sufficient moisture.

What are the pharmacological activities of Geum japonicum?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-HIV, anti-herpes simplex virus, anticoagulant, antioxidant, and inhibition of MMP-2 activity. Its antiviral properties specifically target the replication of both HIV and herpes simplex viruses to prevent cellular damage. The antioxidant capabilities of the plant help mitigate oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals in the body. Additionally, its anticoagulant effects influence blood clotting mechanisms to improve circulatory health. Finally, the ability to inhibit MMP-2 activity suggests potential applications in regulating tissue remodeling and inflammatory processes.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extract from the whole plant of Geum japonicum was found to inhibit the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV-1) protease. Through bioassay-directed fractionation of the extract, 1 new triterpene acid was isolated along with 5 known triterpene acids. The structure of the new compound was determined to be 2 alpha, 19 alpha-dihydroxy-3-oxo-12-ursen-28-oiic acid (1). Among the isolated compounds, 1, ursolic acid, and maslinic acid showed potent inhibitory activity against HIV-1 protease.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds ellagic acid, maslinic acid, tormentic acid, ursolic acid, and casuariin. These bioactive substances are primarily known for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Ellagic acid and casuariin contribute to the plant's ability to protect cells from oxidative stress. Meanwhile, the triterpenoids maslinic acid, tormentic acid, and ursolic acid support various therapeutic functions within biological systems. Together, these specific chemical constituents define the plant's pharmacological profile.

As discussed in a study, Geum japonicum contains ellagic acid as one of its main active ingredients. This compound is utilized globally in food, cosmetics, and medicinal goods. To extract this component, choline chloride-oxalic acid was found to be the most effective natural deep eutectic solvent compared to methanol, ethanol, and acetone. The ChCl:Oa extract demonstrated a stronger DPPH radical scavenging ability in in vitro antioxidant experiments than other solvent extracts. This extraction method can destroy the tissue structure of Geum japonicum to improve the yield of ellagic acid.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating HIV-1, atherosclerotic lesions, herpes simplex virus, and issues involving the blood coagulation cascade. Research indicates that extracts from Geum japonicum possess antiviral properties capable of inhibiting the replication of HIV-1 and herpes simplex virus. Its chemical constituents also demonstrate an ability to mitigate the formation of atherosclerotic lesions within the vascular system. Furthermore, the plant contains bioactive compounds that can influence the blood coagulation cascade to manage clotting processes. These diverse pharmacological activities make it a subject of interest for managing various systemic and infectious conditions.

What side effects this plant can have?
