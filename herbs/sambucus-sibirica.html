Sambucus sibirica

What's the taxonomical classification of Sambucus sibirica?

Sambucus sibirica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this botanical hierarchy, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic ranks, it falls under the order Dipsacales and is a member of the family Viburnaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Sambucus and the species sibirica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sambucus sibirica has deciduous, opposite leaves that are typically pinnate with 5 to 7 lanceolate, serrated leaflets and a shrubby growth habit reaching heights of up to 3 meters. The plant produces dense, flat-topped cymes of small, creamy white flowers that emerge during the summer months. These inflorescences eventually give way to clusters of dark purple to blackish berries that are highly distinctive. The stems are woody and often feature lenticels, while the overall structure remains multi-stemmed and somewhat spreading. This morphology allows the species to thrive in the moist, shady environments typical of its Siberian habitat.

The species thrives in various climates ranging from continental to maritime influences across the Asian continent. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of bioactive phytochemicals including the glycosides astragalin and rutin, the flavonoid quercetin, the flavonol isoquercitrin, and the phenolic acid chlorogenic acid. These specific compounds contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Rutin and quercetin work together to stabilize cell membranes and scavenge free radicals within the body. The presence of chlorogenic acid further supports metabolic health and provides additional protective effects against oxidative stress. Additionally, astragalin and isoquercitrin enhance the overall medicinal efficacy of the botanical extracts.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Sambucus sibirica targets various inflammatory mediators and viral proteins, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), lipoxygenase (LOX), and various cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). These interactions are primarily driven by high concentrations of anthocyanins and polyphenols that modulate the NF-kappaB signaling pathway to reduce systemic inflammation. Additionally, the plant's bioactive compounds demonstrate the ability to bind to viral hemagglutinin proteins, which can prevent the attachment of certain respiratory viruses to host cells. By interfering with these specific enzymatic and protein-binding processes, the plant exerts its antioxidant and immunomodulatory effects. This multi-target mechanism allows the plant to influence both the innate immune response and the biochemical pathways involved in oxidative stress.

The plant also serves as a diaphoric to assist in regulating body temperature through sweating. Additionally, its astringent qualities provide structural benefits to various bodily tissues.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness if the raw parts are consumed. These adverse reactions are primarily due to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides found in the seeds, leaves, and stems. Ingesting these toxic compounds can lead to significant gastrointestinal distress and neurological symptoms like dizziness. Most side effects occur when the plant is not properly cooked or processed to neutralize its natural toxins. Ensuring all parts of the plant are thoroughly heated is essential to prevent these physical complications.

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
