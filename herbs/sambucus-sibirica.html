Sambucus sibirica

What's the taxonomical classification of Sambucus sibirica?

Sambucus sibirica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this botanical hierarchy, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic ranks, it falls under the order Dipsacales and is a member of the family Viburnaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Sambucus and the species sibirica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sambucus sibirica has deciduous, opposite leaves that are typically pinnate with 5 to 7 lanceolate, serrated leaflets and a shrubby growth habit reaching heights of up to 3 meters.

The plant produces dense, flat-topped cymes of small, creamy white flowers that emerge during the summer months. These inflorescences eventually give way to clusters of dark purple to blackish berries that are highly distinctive. The stems are woody and often feature lenticels, while the overall structure remains multi-stemmed and somewhat spreading.

This morphology allows the species to thrive in the moist, shady environments typical of its Siberian habitat.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Sambucus sibirica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its ripe berries, flowers, and bark. The dark purple berries are frequently harvested to create syrups and infusions used to support the immune system during viral infections. The aromatic flowers are often processed into teas to help reduce fever and soothe inflammation. Additionally, the bark and stems contain specific alkaloids that have been studied for their potential bioactive properties. These various parts are carefully collected to ensure the highest concentration of therapeutic constituents.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, anthocyanins, phenolic acids, alkaloids, and terpenoids that contribute to its diverse therapeutic profile.

The anthocyanins are responsible for the deep pigmentation of the berries and provide potent antioxidant activities. Phenolic acids and flavonoids work together to scavenge free radicals and reduce systemic inflammation. Additionally, the presence of alkaloids and terpenoids adds complexity to its chemical structure and biological interactions.

These combined bioactive compounds support various medicinal applications ranging from immune modulation to cellular protection.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Flavonoids
  • Anthocyanins
  • Phenolic acids
  • Alkaloids
  • Terpenoids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Sambucus sibirica targets various inflammatory mediators and viral proteins, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), lipoxygenase (LOX), and various cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). These interactions are primarily driven by high concentrations of anthocyanins and polyphenols that modulate the NF-kappaB signaling pathway to reduce systemic inflammation. Additionally, the plant's bioactive compounds demonstrate the ability to bind to viral hemagglutinin proteins, which can prevent the attachment of certain respiratory viruses to host cells. By interfering with these specific enzymatic and protein-binding processes, the plant exerts its antioxidant and immunomodulatory effects. This multi-target mechanism allows the plant to influence both the innate immune response and the biochemical pathways involved in oxidative stress.

What are the therapeutic actions of Sambucus sibirica?

Sambucus sibirica has therapeutic properties that include acting as an antioxidant, antiviral, immunomodulator, astringent, and diaphoric agent.

This flavonoid-rich plant works to bolster the immune system through its complex bioactive compounds. Its antiviral actions help inhibit the replication of certain pathogens during infection. The plant also serves as a diaphoric to assist in regulating body temperature through sweating.

Additionally, its astringent qualities provide structural benefits to various bodily tissues.

The primary therapeutic actions of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Diaphoretic
  • Flavonoid-rich
  • Astringent
  • Antioxidant
  • Antiviral
  • Immunomodulator

This plant is used to treat respiratory infections, fever, coughs, and inflammation.

Its bioactive compounds work to bolster the immune system during seasonal illnesses. People often consume preparations of the berries to alleviate the discomfort of a persistent cough. The plant also possesses properties that help reduce fever and systemic inflammation.

These traditional uses make it a valuable resource for managing various upper respiratory ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Sambucus sibirica?

Sambucus sibirica is prepared as dried flowers for infusions, berry syrups for cough remedies, and concentrated tinctures for medicinal use. The elderberry fruit is frequently boiled with honey or sugar to create a thick syrup that supports the immune system. Dried blossoms are often steeped in hot water to produce a fragrant tea used for soothing respiratory symptoms. Additionally, alcohol-based extracts allow for the long-term preservation of the plant's active compounds. These various methods ensure that the therapeutic properties of the plant are accessible in both liquid and dried states.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Sambucus sibirica can cause severe nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and diarrhea if the raw berries, leaves, bark, or stems are consumed. These adverse reactions occur because the plant contains cyanogenic glycosides, which release toxic cyanide into the body during digestion. While cooking the berries typically neutralizes these compounds, consuming any part of the plant unprepared remains a significant health risk. Individuals with sensitivities to the elderberry family should also be cautious of potential allergic reactions. Always ensure the fruit is fully ripe and thoroughly processed to avoid these dangerous gastrointestinal effects.
