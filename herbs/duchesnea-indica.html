Duchesnea indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Duchesnea indica?

Duchesnea indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this botanical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Rosaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Duchesnea, and its unique species designation, indica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Duchesnea indica has creeping, rhizomatous stems that produce fleshy, heart-shaped to ovate leaves measuring approximately 2 to 5 centimeters in length.

These leaves are characterized by a glossy, dark green upper surface and often feature a lighter green or silver underside with prominent venation. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the leaf axils, typically displaying a vibrant red or orange hue. The plant lacks true woody tissue, instead relying on its succulent-like stems to spread across the forest floor.

This spreading growth habit allows it to form dense, ground-covering mats in humid environments.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Duchasnea indica targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX), as well as various pro-inflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). These interactions occur primarily through bioactive secondary metabolites, including flavonoids and phenolic compounds present in the plant's extract. By inhibiting the COX pathway, the plant's components effectively reduce the production of prostaglandins, which are key mediators of pain and swelling. Furthermore, the modulation of cytokine signaling helps suppress the overactive immune responses associated with chronic inflammation. These molecular pathways collectively contribute to the plant's traditional use in treating skin ailments and inflammatory conditions.

        What are the herbal preparations of Duchesnea indica?

        Dutchesnea indica is prepared as a topical decoction, a poultice of crushed leaves, or an infused liquid for external application. The fresh leaves are often macerated directly into a paste to soothe skin irritations or inflammation.         What side-effects this plant can have?

        Duchesnea indica can cause mild skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis if its juices come into direct contact with sensitive skin. While there are no widely documented cases of severe toxicity in humans, some caution is advised when handling the foliage. Individuals with known sensitivities to common tropical plants may experience redness, itching, or a localized rash. It is generally recommended to wash your hands thoroughly after pruning or touching the plant to prevent any unexpected reactions. Always monitor for any signs of discomfort if you decide to incorporate this species into your indoor garden.
