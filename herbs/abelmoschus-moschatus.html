Abelmoschus moschatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Abelmoschus moschatus?

Abelmoschus moschatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Malvales and is a member of the family Malvaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by the genus Abelmoschus and is specifically categorized as the species moschatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Abelmoschus moschatus has an erect, herbaceous structure characterized by branched stems that can reach heights of up to one meter. The leaves are typically palmately lobed with serrated margins and a soft, pubescent texture. Large, funnel-shaped flowers emerge from the axils, displaying a pale yellow to creamy white coloration with a distinct dark center. The plant produces capsule-like fruit that contains numerous small, dark seeds. These morphological features are characteristic of the Malvaceae family and aid in the plant's reproductive success.

What are the pharmacological activities of Abelmoschus moschatus?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antiproliferative, and antispasmodic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work effectively to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. The antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. Furthermore, the plant exhibits antiproliferative effects that can potentially limit the spread of abnormal cell growth. It also demonstrates antispasmodic actions by helping to relax smooth muscle tissues.

As discussed in a study, Abelmoschus moschatus leaves and seeds are used for aphrodisiac, ophthalmic, cardio tonic, antispasmodic, and intestinal complaint treatments. The antioxidant activities determined by total phenol, flavonoids, total antioxidant, and FRAP methods were higher in the leaf than in the seed extracts. The aqueous overnight seed extract (AMS-I) showed significant radical scavenging activity in 1, 1-Diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH), hydrogen peroxide, hydroxyl radical, superoxide, and lipid peroxidation. The AMS-I and AML-IV extracts showed activity against 6 and 7 microorganisms respectively, including 2 gram-positive, 4 gram-negative, and 1 fungal strain. Furthermore, AMS-IV and AML-IV demonstrated potential antiproliferative activity against 2 human cancer cell lines, specifically colorectal adenocarcinoma (COLO-205) and retinoblastoma (Y79).

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating childbirth complications, infertility, intestinal complaints, menstrual pain, and various unspecified female complaints. Its medicinal properties are frequently applied to assist with reproductive health and ease the difficulties associated with labor. Practitioners also utilize the plant to soothe digestive issues and alleviate discomfort in the intestinal tract. Additionally, it serves as a remedy for managing painful menstrual cycles and addressing general gynecological concerns. This versatile herb remains a significant component in traditional medicine for supporting women's wellness.

As discussed in a study, Abelmoschus moschatus is one of the plants used for childbirth and infertility. The research involved 30 respondents, including 10 males, who were interviewed from September 1996 to September 2000. These participants were located across 13 different sites, with 12 sites in Trinidad and 1 site in Tobago. Abelmoschus moschatus is also listed among the plants used for menstrual pain and unspecified female complaints.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause allergic reactions such as skin rashes, itching, or swelling if the sap or foliage comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals may experience digestive distress including nausea or stomach cramps if the plant is ingested improperly. The specific chemical composition of its mucilaginous properties can lead to unpredictable sensitivities in people with preexisting allergies. There is limited clinical data regarding long-term toxicity, so caution is advised during any form of topical or oral exposure. It is important to consult a medical professional if any adverse symptoms arise after contact.
