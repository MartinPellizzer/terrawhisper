What to know about Pelargonium graveolens (geranium) before using it medicinally

Botanically, Pelargonium graveolens is a member of the Geraniaceae family, characterized by its distinctive pungent scent and slightly bitter taste, which are attributed to the presence of essential oils such as geraniol and linalool.

Historically, the herb has been used in traditional medicine for centuries, with ancient Egyptians and Greeks citing its use for various health benefits, and more recently, it has gained popularity in aromatherapy and herbal medicine for its calming and soothing effects.

What are the medicinal properties of Pelargonium graveolens?

Pelargonium graveolens helps with anxiety, stress, and insomnia due to its calming and sedative properties. It has been traditionally used to treat nervous disorders, headaches, and skin conditions. Its aroma also has a decongestant effect.

The active constituents responsible for the medicinal properties of Pelargonium graveolens are geraniol, linalool, and limonene, which have been shown to have anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant effects. These compounds are responsible for its anxiolytic, antiseptic, and antispasmodic properties.

The leaves and flowers of Pelargonium graveolens are primarily used for medicinal purposes, as they contain the highest concentration of essential oils. The leaves are often used for treating anxiety, insomnia, and headaches, while the flowers are used to soothe skin conditions and wounds.

When used improperly, Pelargonium graveolens can cause allergic reactions, skin irritation, and respiratory problems in some individuals. Excessive consumption may also lead to dizziness, nausea, and stomach upset.

When using Pelargonium graveolens medicinally, it is essential to follow proper precautions. Pregnant and breastfeeding women, as well as individuals with allergies or respiratory conditions, should use it under medical supervision. Additionally, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare professional before using it for medicinal purposes, especially when combined with other medications or treatments.

What are the horticulural aspects of Pelargonium graveolens?

Pelargonium graveolens grow well in temperate climates with moderate temperatures between 15-30°C. They thrive in full sun to partial shade, requiring at least 4-6 hours of direct sunlight. Soil should be well-draining and fertile with a pH between 6.0-7.0.

Planting Pelargonium graveolens in well-draining soil with a mix of compost and perlite can improve soil structure. Water thoroughly after planting and keep the soil consistently moist during the first few weeks. Space plants 30-60 cm apart to allow for proper air circulation and growth.

Harvesting the leaves and flowers of Pelargonium graveolens should be done regularly to promote new growth. Leaves can be harvested at any time, while flowers should be picked in the morning when they are fully open. Cut back the stems after flowering to encourage new growth and prevent seed production.

Common pests that affect Pelargonium graveolens include aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites. Fungal diseases such as root rot, leaf spot, and powdery mildew can also occur due to overwatering or poor air circulation. Regular monitoring and treatment can help prevent these issues and maintain healthy plants.
