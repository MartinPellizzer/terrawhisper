Geranium

Pelargonium graveolens

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) is a member of the Geraniaceae family, commonly used in traditional medicine for digestive issues, skin conditions, and respiratory infections. The plant parts, including leaves and essential oils, have been historically employed in various cultural and regional practices.

Botanical Identification

Geranium, also known as Pelargonium graveolens, is a member of the Geraniaceae family. Common names include pelargonium and geranium, with regional variations like scented geranium. It is an annual plant native to South Africa, characterized by its aromatic leaves, pink flowers, and fibrous root system. Historically, it has been referred to by various synonyms in herbal texts.

Geranium Identification.

Active Compounds

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) contains essential oils, flavonoids, and terpenoids as its primary active compounds. These bioactive components work synergistically to provide therapeutic effects, with essential oils contributing to its aromatic and antimicrobial properties. Flavonoids and terpenoids support anti-inflammatory and antioxidant actions.

Preparation Methods

Essential oil: Used for treating respiratory infections and skin conditions due to its antimicrobial properties.

Infusion: Used for digestive issues and cold symptoms due to its mild antispasmodic and decongestant effects.

Decoction: Used for respiratory infections and muscle pain due to its warming and anti-inflammatory properties.

Safety Profile

Geranium, or Pelargonium graveolens, is generally safe when used appropriately. However, it may cause skin irritation or allergic reactions in some individuals. It is contraindicated during pregnancy and should be used with caution in people with kidney disorders. Always consult a healthcare provider before use.
