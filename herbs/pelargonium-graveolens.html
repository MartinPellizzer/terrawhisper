Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) is a flowering plant commonly known as the geranium or pelargonium, which has been traditionally used for its various medicinal properties, particularly in improving conditions such as digestive issues and skin ailments.

What is Geranium (Pelargonium Graveolens)?

Geranium, also known as Pelargonium graveolens, is a flowering plant native to South Africa, commonly used in herbal medicine for its aromatic leaves and roots, which contain compounds with antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antispasmodic properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Geranium, commonly known as Pelargonium graveolens, is a species of flowering plant in the family Geraniaceae, characterized by its upright growth habit, lobed leaves, and showy, often fragrant flowers.

Common Names and Synonyms

Common names and synonyms for Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) include garden geranium, pelargonium, and stork's bill, though it is often confused with other species within the Geraniaceae family.

Plant Description and Morphology

Geranium, or Pelargonium graveolens, is a flowering plant characterized by its upright, bushy growth habit, simple ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins, and showy, clustered flowers in shades of red, pink, and white, typically blooming in summer.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Geranium, also known as Pelargonium graveolens, is native to the coastal regions of South Africa, specifically the Cape Province, where it grows in sandy soils and coastal dunes.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, stems, and roots of Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) are primarily used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly employed part for their aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including essential oils rich in geraniol, linalool, and citronellol, as well as flavonoids, tannins, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) possesses pharmacological properties that include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant activities, primarily due to the presence of essential oils containing compounds such as geraniol, citral, and limonene.

Mechanisms of Action

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through the presence of bioactive compounds such as pelargonidin, geraniol, and other essential oils, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties by interacting with cellular pathways and modulating enzyme activity.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Geranium is used to treat respiratory infections, menstrual cramps, digestive issues, skin infections, inflammation, and as a general tonic to support overall health.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) is typically prepared as a tincture, essential oil, or dried herb for use in herbal medicine.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) for herbal use typically ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried herb per day, taken in divided doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic use and preparation method.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, excessive consumption or prolonged use may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals.

Drug Interactions

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system, due to its potential sedative and antispasmodic properties, which could enhance or counteract the effects of drugs like sedatives, anticonvulsants, or antidepressants.

Toxicity and Precautions

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) is generally considered safe when used in recommended dosages, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset or skin irritation in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy and lactation has not been fully established.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) is typically cultivated in well-drained soil with full sun exposure, requiring regular watering during the growing season and minimal fertilization to prevent excessive foliage growth.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) for use in herbal medicine typically involves harvesting the leaves and flowers, drying them to preserve their active compounds, and then grinding them into a powder or using them in tinctures and essential oils.

Storage and Shelf Life

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 1 to 2 years when properly preserved, though potency may decrease over time.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens) has explored its potential pharmacological properties, including antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, with some studies suggesting its use in traditional herbal medicine for treating respiratory and skin conditions.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Geranium, also known as Pelargonium graveolens, has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its antiseptic, astringent, and anti-inflammatory properties, with historical applications ranging from treating wounds and respiratory infections to managing digestive issues and skin conditions.

Regulatory Status
