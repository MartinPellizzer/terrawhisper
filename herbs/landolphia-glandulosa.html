Landolphia glandulosa

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Landolphia glandulosa has an evergreen habit characterized by large, leathery, oblong leaves that can reach up to 30 centimeters in length and possess distinctively wavy margins. The foliage is typically dark green on the upper surface with a slightly paler underside and a smooth, glossy texture. Its reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the axils of the leaves. These flowers eventually develop into fleshy, globose drupes that transition from green to a deep purple or black color when mature. The woody stems are sturdy and provide structural support for the dense, spreading canopy of the tree.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Africa, ranging from West Africa through Central Africa to East Africa. It is commonly found in lowland rainforests and moist forest margins where humidity levels are high. The species thrives in the humid environments of countries such as Nigeria, Cameroon, and Gabon. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of dense canopy cover and tropical precipitation patterns. This specific ecological niche allows it to spread across various equatorial river basins.

What are the herbal preparations of Landolphia glandulosa?

Landolphia glandulosa is prepared primarily as a decoction of the bark, a leaf infusion, or a topical poultice made from crushed foliage. These liquid extracts are commonly used in traditional medicine to address various inflammatory conditions and skin ailments. The bark is often boiled in water to create a potent medicinal tea meant for internal consumption. Additionally, the fresh leaves can be macerated or applied directly to wounds to utilize their antiseptic properties. Some practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of expressed juices for localized treatments.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Landolphia glandulosa can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and intense inflammation upon direct contact with its sap. This plant belongs to the Euphorbiaceae family, which is known for producing caustic milky latex. If this substance touches the eyes, it may lead to temporary or even permanent vision impairment through chemical burns. Accidental ingestion of any part of the plant can result in significant gastrointestinal distress, including vomiting and abdominal pain. Allergic reactions are also common among individuals who are hypersensitive to its chemical components.
