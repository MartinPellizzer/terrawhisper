What to know about Monarda didyma (bee balm) before using it medicinally

Bee balm, scientifically known as Monarda didyma, is a herb that has been prized for its numerous health benefits and versatility in the kitchen.

Its antiseptic and anti-inflammatory properties make it a popular natural remedy for soothing sore throats, calming skin irritations, and reducing fever. Bee balm is also a common ingredient in herbal teas, salads, and desserts, thanks to its sweet, minty flavor. In terms of horticulture, bee balm is a relatively low-maintenance perennial that attracts pollinators and requires minimal care beyond regular watering and pruning.

Botanically speaking, bee balm belongs to the mint family (Lamiaceae) and features distinctive red, pink, or purple flowers that are shaped like lips, giving it its common name.

What are the botanical characteristics of Monarda didyma?

Monarda didyma, also known as wild bergamot or bee balm, is a perennial herb that exhibits several distinct botanical characteristics.

It has square stems, typically growing between 2-4 feet tall, which are often tinged with purple or red. The leaves of M. didyma are elliptical to ovate in shape, measuring around 3-6 inches long and 1-2 inches wide, with a distinctive pointed tip and serrated margins.

The leaves are opposite, meaning they occur in pairs on the stem, and have a soft, hairy texture with a minty aroma when bruised. The flowers of M. didyma are highly distinctive, consisting of red, pink, or purple tubes surrounded by white to yellowish lips, forming a distinctive "beard-like" structure that is rich in nectar.

These tubular flowers are arranged in whorls at the top of the stem and are often clustered in small groups, attracting bees, butterflies, and hummingbirds as pollinators.
