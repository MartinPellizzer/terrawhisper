Lithocarpus henryi

What's the taxonomical classification of Lithocarpus henryi?

Lithocarpus henryi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant falls under the order Fagales and is a member of the family Fagaceae. Ultimately, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Lithocarpus and the species henryi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lithocarpus henryi has simple, evergreen leaves that are elliptical to oblong in shape, measuring approximately 5 to 15 centimeters in length, and characterized by a coriaceous texture with entire or slightly serrated margins.

The leaf surfaces are typically dark green and glossy on the upper side, while the undersides may appear slightly paler or pubescent. Its woody stems and branches develop a rugged, fissured bark as the tree matures into a large canopy specimen. The reproductive structures consist of inconspicuous flowers that eventually produce hard-shelled acorns held within a cupule.

These nuts are a key morphological feature used to identify the species within its forest habitat.

      What are the herbal preparations of Lithocarpus henryi?

      Lithocarpus henryi is prepared primarily as a decoction of the dried fruits, bark, or leaves to extract its medicinal compounds. Practitioners often boil these plant parts in water to create a concentrated liquid tea for oral consumption. In some traditional applications, the powdered form of the dried material is used to facilitate easier ingestion or topical application. Some formulations may also involve soaking the plant components in alcohol to create a tincture. These diverse methods allow for different concentrations of the active constituents depending on the intended therapeutic use.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Lithocarpus henryi can cause allergic skin reactions, digestive distress, or mild toxicity if the raw parts of the plant are ingested in large quantities. While specific clinical data on human consumption is limited, many species within the Lithocarpus genus contain tannins that may irritate the gastrointestinal lining. These chemical compounds can lead to nausea, abdominal pain, or vomiting if not processed correctly. Some individuals might also experience contact dermatitis after touching the leaves or bark. Caution should always be exercised when handling or consuming any unknown wild plant.
