Celtis bungeana

What's the taxonomical classification of Celtis bungeana?

Celtis bungeanana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Rosalales. Further taxonomic refinement places this plant in the family Cannabaceae, specifically within the genus Celtis. Ultimately, its unique biological identity is defined by its specific classification as the species bungeanana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Celtis bungeanana has an upright woody habit characterized by an alternate phyllotaxy of simple, ovate to elliptical leaves with serrated margins and prominent pinnate venation.

The branches often exhibit distinct lenticels on the bark, which provides a textured surface as the tree matures. Its reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into fleshy, drupe-like fruits. These fruits are typically small and globular, serving as a primary food source for various avian species.

The overall architecture of the plant supports a dense canopy suited for its native forest environments.

        What are the herbal preparations of Celtis bungeana?

        Celtis bungeanana is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a powder derived from dried fruit, or a topical poultice made from crushed leaves. The decoction is typically brewed by boiling the bark in water to extract its medicinal compounds for internal consumption. For digestive issues, the powdered fruit is often mixed into food or taken with water to facilitate easier ingestion. The leaves can be crushed into a thick paste and applied directly to the skin to treat localized inflammation or wounds. These diverse preparation methods allow different parts of the plant to be utilized for specific therapeutic purposes.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Celtis bungeanana can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, itching, or redness if its sap or leaves touch sensitive skin. While the plant is not widely documented as acutely toxic, individual sensitivities to its chemical compounds may vary. Some people might experience mild digestive upset if any part of the plant is ingested accidentally. It is generally recommended to avoid direct contact with the plant to prevent unexpected dermatological issues. Always consult a medical professional if a reaction occurs after exposure to this species.
