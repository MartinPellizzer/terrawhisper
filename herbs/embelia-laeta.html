Embelia laeta

What's the taxonomical classification of Embelia laeta?

Embelia laeta belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the taxonomic order Ericales. The plant is further categorized into the family Primulaceae, within which it is placed in the genus Embelia. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by its species name, laeta.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Embelia laeta has opposite, elliptical to oblong leaves that are entire in margin and possess a glossy, dark green surface. The stems are woody and often exhibit a climbing or scandent growth habit typical of many members of the Primulaceae family. Small, actinomorphic flowers emerge from the axils, featuring a tubular corolla that transitions into spreading lobes. These blooms are typically white or pale in color and are supported by short pedicels. The fruit develops into a small, fleshy drupe that facilitates seed dispersal.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apiagenin-7-O-glucoside, chrysoeriol, hyperin, kaempferol, and rutin. These specific phytochemicals are categorized as flavonoids and serve as important bioactive constituents within the species. Each of these substances contributes to the diverse chemical profile that defines the plant's therapeutic potential. Researchers study these individual molecules to understand how they interact with biological systems. The presence of such a variety of glycosides and aglycones highlights the complex secondary metabolism of Embelia laeta.

According to a study published by "Zhong yao cai = Zhongyaocai = Journal of Chinese medicinal materials", eleven compounds were isolated and identified from the EtOAc fraction of the ethanol extract of Embelia laeta. These identified constituents include p-sitosterol (1), beta-daucosterel (2), gallic acid (3), vanillic acid (4), rutin (5), hyperin (6), quercetin (7), kaemperol (8), chrysoeriol (9), physcion (10), and apiagenin-7-O-glucoside (11). The separation and purification of these substances were achieved using column chromatography with silica gel and polyamide. Notably, compounds 5 through 11 are isolated from this genus for the first time.
