Lithospermum officinale

What's the taxonomical classification of Lithospermum officinale?

Lithospermum officinale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Boraginales, it falls under the family Boraginaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Lithospermum and is specifically categorized as the species officinale.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lithospermum officinale has erect, hairy stems that reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bear alternate, lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically dark green and possesses a rough, scabrous texture due to fine hairs covering the surface. Small, funnel-shaped flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying a white or pale pink corolla with five lobes. These blossoms eventually develop into small, globular nutlets that are characterized by a hard, stony texture. The plant's root system consists of a thick, woody taproot that anchors the structure firmly in the soil.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate regions of Europe, North Africa, and Western Asia. It is commonly found growing in various habitats across the continent, ranging from meadows to forest edges. While its primary range is centered in Europe, individual populations can be discovered throughout much of the Mediterranean basin. Some records also indicate its presence in parts of Central Asia where conditions remain suitable for its growth. The species thrives in temperate climates that provide the necessary seasonal variations for its life cycle.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Lithospermum officinale contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are harvested and processed to extract alkaloids used in traditional remedies. The root is often prepared as a tincture or an oil to treat skin ailments and inflammatory conditions. Historically, practitioners have also utilized the powdered root to address digestive issues and internal ailments. Careful preparation is required because the concentration of active ingredients can vary significantly between different plant specimens.

What are the pharmacological activities of Lithospermum officinale?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and wound healing acceleration pharmacological activities. The presence of naphthoquinones like shikonin contributes significantly to its potent ability to inhibit tumor cell proliferation and combat various pathogens. Its strong antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, which reduces oxidative stress within biological systems. Additionally, the plant exhibits significant anti-inflammatory effects by modulating chemical mediators of inflammation. These combined therapeutic actions also facilitate the rapid repair of damaged tissues, making it effective for wound healing acceleration.

As discussed in a study, Lithospermum officinale is a medicinal herb that produces secondary metabolites with antioxidant, antimicrobial and/or anti-cancer effects. Methanolic extracts of 17 and 31-day-old Lithospermum officinale callus were evaluated for anti-inflammatory capacity on primary microglia from neonatal Wistar rats aged 1 to 3 days old. The methanolic extract of the 17-day-old callus exhibited anti-inflammatory effects on LPS-stimulated microglial cells much higher than those observed for CfA. Pretreatment with an effective dose of 0.8 mg mL-1 of the extract resulted in the decreased expression of Nos2, Tnf-α, and Cox-2 mRNA and the suppression of TNF-α and IL-1β release. Because the extract shows a high antioxidant capacity and lacks pyrrolizidine alkaloids, it is anticipated to be a reliable substitute for preparing neuroprotective pharmaceutical formulas.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds rosmarinic acid, shikonin, ellagic acid, rutin, and pyrrolizidine alkaloids. Rosmarinic acid and rutin serve as potent antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress. The presence of shikonin provides significant anti-inflammatory and wound-healing properties. Ellagic acid contributes to the plant's potential anticancer and astringent effects. However, the inclusion of pyrrolizidine alkaloids necessitates caution due to their potential toxicity to the liver.

As discussed in a study, the extract of Lithospermum officinale with CAS 90063-58-4 is used in medicinal and cosmetic industries. While cultivation takes at least 2 years, its callus culture proliferated 8.3 times with 4.9-fold biomass in less than 30 days. Under the applied conditions, the cell extract contained no toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids. The phenylpropanoid pathway was active toward phenolic acid formation, with rosmarinic acid produced as the main constituent. The total phenolic content and antioxidant capacity of the proliferated cell extracts were similar to those of the extracts of the natural plant tissues, specifically from the root.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cutaneous injuries, microbial infections, inflammation, and various inflammatory reactions. Its medicinal properties are particularly effective at promoting wound healing through the application of topical preparations. By reducing localized inflammation, the plant helps soothe irritated skin tissues. It also serves as a defense against harmful microbial infections that may compromise the skin barrier. These combined effects make it a valuable natural remedy for restoring damaged skin.

As discussed in a study, the application of 50 mg/site of Lithospermum officinale ether extract markedly inhibited increased vascular permeability and acute edema induced by histamine, serotonin, bromelain, anti-rat rabbit serum, and heat. This extract also inhibited an increase of local cutaneous temperature induced by ultraviolet irradiation and heat while accelerating wound healing in rats. The most potent activity of the Lithospermum officinale ether extract on inflammatory reactions occurred at an ointment concentration of approximately 0.1% to 0.2%. Additionally, pigments of Lithospermum officinale have been reported to possess antibiotic activity.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a tincture, a decoction, an infusion, or an infused oil to utilize its medicinal properties. A tincture made from the roots is often used for its concentrated alkaloids in topical or internal applications. Practitioners may create a decoction by simmering the tough roots in water to extract deeper chemical constituents. For more delicate parts or milder uses, a hot water infusion serves as a gentle method of preparation. Finally, the dried roots can be steeped in a carrier liquid to create an infused oil for soothing skin irritations.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause liver toxicity, kidney toxicity, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and stomach cramps. Consuming the herb may lead to severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and painful stomach cramps. Serious systemic complications include significant damage to the liver and kidneys. These toxic effects are primarily linked to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids within the plant. Careful avoidance is necessary to prevent these dangerous health outcomes.

  • Nausea
