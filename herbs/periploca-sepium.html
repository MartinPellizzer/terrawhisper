Periploca sepium

What's the taxonomical classification of Periploca sepium?

Periploca sepium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Gentianaales. Within this order, it is situated in the family Apocynaceae, specifically falling under the genus Periploca, with the formal species designation being sepium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Periploca sepium has opposite, simple, elliptical leaves that measure 5 to 15 centimeters in length and possess entire margins with distinctively prominent pinnate venation.

The plant is a deciduous shrub or small tree that typically reaches heights of up to five meters. Its bark is smooth and grayish, often becoming slightly fissured as the specimen matures. During the summer, it produces small, inconspicuous, creamy-white flowers that are arranged in axillary panicles.

The resulting fruit consists of a small, fleshy, dark berry that contains several tiny seeds.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Periploca sepium targets various enzymes and receptors including acetylcholinesterase, cyclooxygenase-2, and specific ion channels to exert its pharmacological effects. The bioactive compounds found in this plant, such as cardiac glycosides and various triterpenoids, act primarily by inhibiting the sodium-potassium adenosine triphosphatase pump. This inhibition leads to increased intracellular calcium levels, which contributes to its observed cardiotonic properties. Additionally, certain constituents interact with inflammatory mediators to modulate the expression of cytokines and suppress oxidative stress pathways. These diverse molecular interactions explain the plant's traditional use in treating various inflammatory and cardiovascular conditions.

    What are the herbal preparations of Periploca sepium?

    Periploca sepium is prepared primarily as a decoction of the roots or as a topical paste made from crushed leaves to treat skin ailments.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Periploca sepium can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and localized inflammation upon contact with the plant's sap. This botanical species contains various bioactive compounds that may trigger allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. If the sap accidentally enters the eyes, it can lead to intense stinging and potential corneal damage. Ingestion of the plant is considered dangerous and can result in systemic toxicity or gastrointestinal distress. Therefore, extreme caution must be exercised when handling any part of this shrub to avoid these adverse reactions.
