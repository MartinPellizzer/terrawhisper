Lilium brownii

What's the taxonomical classification of Lilium brownii?

Lilium brownii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the evolutionary hierarchy, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As a member of the order Liliales, it falls specifically into the family Liliaceae. The taxonomic identity of this plant is completed by its placement within the genus Lilium, culminating in its unique species designation, Lilium brownii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lilium brownii has narrow, linear leaves that are arranged in whorls or sometimes alternately along a slender, upright stem. The flowers are typically trumpet-shaped and exhibit a creamy white to pale yellow coloration often marked with dark spots. Each petal or tepal possesses a delicate texture and may curve gracefully outward during full bloom. The plant's reproductive structures consist of prominent stamens with elongated filaments and a central pistil. These morphological features collectively define its elegant and delicate physical appearance in its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of the Himalayas, specifically distributed across parts of India, Nepal, Bhutan, and southwestern China. It primarily inhabits alpine and subalpine meadows where the terrain is rocky and well-drained. These populations are typically found at elevations ranging from 3,000 to 4,500 meters above sea level. The specific microclimates within these mountain ranges provide the necessary moisture and cool temperatures required for its survival. Consequently, its range is restricted to these specific montane ecosystems rather than being widespread across the continent.

What are the pharmacological activities of Lilium brownii?

This plant has potent pharmacological activities including anti-monoamine oxidase activity, antidepressant, antitumor, modification of lipid metabolism, and neuroprotective effects. Its ability to inhibit monoamine oxidase enzymes contributes significantly to its classification as a natural antidepressant. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates antitumor properties that may aid in suppressing various cancer cell growths. It also plays a critical role in the modification of lipid metabolism, which helps regulate systemic fat levels. Finally, its neuroprotective capabilities offer potential benefits for maintaining long-term brain health and preventing neurological decline.

As discussed in a study, the bulbs of Lilium brownii are used clinically as Baihe for the treatment of neurological disorders. Two new phenylpropanoid compounds, 3-O-acetyl-1-O-caffeoylglycerol (1) and 3-O-acetyl-1-O-p-coumaroylglycerol (2), were isolated from these bulbs. These compounds exhibit anti-monoamine oxidase activity when tested using an enzyme labeling method. Specifically, compound 1 shows an inhibition rate of 20.96% at a concentration of 50 μg/ml. Similarly, compound 2 demonstrates a 22.31% inhibition rate at 50 μg/ml.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 1-O-p-coumaroyl-3-O-feruloyl glycerol (CF), 3-O-acetyl-1-O-caffeoylglycerol, and 3-O-acetyl-1-O-p-coumaroylglycerol. These specific caffeoylcholoyl derivatives are known for their significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of these phenolic compounds contributes to the plant's overall therapeutic potential in biological systems. Researchers study these molecules to understand how they interact with cellular pathways to mitigate oxidative stress. Such chemical constituents play a vital role in the plant's pharmacological profile.

As discussed in a study, Lilium brownii contains a phenolic acid glycerol dimer known as 1-O-p-coumaroyl-3-O-feruloyl glycerol (CF). This specific compound, CF, exerts neuroprotective effects by modulating the p62-Keap1-Nrf2 signalling pathway. In studies involving PC12 cells, the administration of CF significantly reduced the apoptosis rate and reactive oxygen species (ROS) levels. The compound markedly upregulated the expression of proteins such as dopamine, tyrosine hydroxylase, and brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF). Additionally, CF demonstrated a favorable binding affinity with proteins including Sequestrosome 1 (P62) through molecular docking technology. Finally, the use of CF in Parkinson's disease mice helped to increase neuronal numbers within the substantia nigra region.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating hepatocellular carcinoma, neurological disorders, depression, obesity, and diabetes. Researchers investigate its chemical compounds to determine how they might inhibit the growth of liver cancer cells. The plant also shows potential in managing neurological disorders by influencing neurotransmitter pathways in the brain. Its bioactive properties are being studied for their ability to help regulate mood in patients suffering from depression. Furthermore, extracts from the species are explored for their role in metabolic regulation to combat obesity and diabetes.

As discussed in a study, two new isospirostanol-type saponins were isolated from the bulbs of Lilium brownii. These compounds were identified as 3-O-[α-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1→2)-β-D-glucopyranoside]-(22R,25R)-5α-spirosolane-3β-ol (1) and 3-O-{α-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1→2)-[β-D-glucopyranosyl-(1→4)]-β-D-glucopyranoside}-(22R,25R)-5α-spirosolane-3β-ol (2). Compound 2 significantly suppressed the proliferation of SMMC-7721 and HepG2 cells with IC50 values of 26.3±1.08 μM and 30.9±1.59 μM, respectively. While compound 2 effectively decreased levels of interleukin-1β and tumor necrosis factor-α, compound 1 did not inhibit either of the two hepatocellular carcinoma lines. The anti-hepatocellular carcinoma effect of compound 2 involves increasing the expression of Bax and Caspase-3 proteins while decreasing Bcl-2 expression.
