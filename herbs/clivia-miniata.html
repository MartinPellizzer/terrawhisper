Clivia miniata

What's the taxonomical classification of Clivia miniata?

Clivia miniata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further organized into the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant follows the biological order Asparagales and is a member of the family Amaryllidaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Clivia and the species miniata.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Clivia miniata targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, various inflammatory cytokines, and specific antioxidant signaling pathways to exert its pharmacological effects. The plant contains bioactive alkaloids and flavonoids that inhibit the production of prostaglandins by modulating cyclooxygenase-2 activity. These phytochemicals also interfere with the expression of pro-inflammatory mediators such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6. Furthermore, its secondary metabolites interact with oxidative stress-related proteins to enhance the cellular antioxidant defense system. Through these diverse protein interactions, the plant demonstrates potential anti-inflammatory and cytoprotective properties.

What are the herbal preparations of Clivia miniata?

Clivia miniata is prepared as a decoction of the roots, a topical poultice made from crushed leaves, or an aqueous extract of the flowering parts. These traditional methods are primarily used to address localized inflammation or digestive issues depending on the specific part utilized. The roots are typically simmered in water to concentrate their active alkaloids for oral consumption. For external skin conditions, the fleshy leaves are macerated into a paste and applied directly to the affected area. Practitioners must exercise caution because the plant contains cardiac glycosides that can be toxic if prepared or dosed incorrectly.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Clivia miniata can cause skin irritation, mouth swelling, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested due to the presence of calcium oxalate crystals. When the plant's sap comes into contact with sensitive skin, it may trigger redness, itching, or a localized rash. If a person chews on the leaves or flowers, these microscopic crystals can embed in the soft tissues of the mouth and throat. This process often leads to intense burning sensations, swelling of the lips, and difficulty swallowing. Pets should also be kept away from the plant to prevent similar toxic reactions.
