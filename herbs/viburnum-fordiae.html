Viburnum fordiae

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum fordiae?

Viburnum fordiae belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Dipsacales and is a member of the family Viburnaceae. Further classification places it within the genus Viburnum, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species foridae.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum fordiae has opposite, simple, ovate leaves that measure two to four inches long and feature serrated margins with prominent pinnate venation. The foliage often transitions to vibrant shades of red, purple, or bronze during the autumn months. Its woody stems are multi-stemmed and can reach heights of six to ten feet in a spreading shrub habit. Small, white, five-petaled flowers emerge in flat-topped cymes during late spring to form fragrant clusters. These blossoms eventually give way to small, dark blue to black drupes that persist into the winter season.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viburnum fordiae?

This plant has potent antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antidiabetic properties characterized by its ability to perform ABTS•+ scavenging, DPPH• scavenging, inhibiting NO production, and α-glucosidase inhibitory activities. The high concentration of bioactive compounds allows it to effectively neutralize free radicals through both ABTS•+ and DPPH• scavenging mechanisms. Its anti-inflammatory potential is further demonstrated by its capacity for inhibiting NO production in cellular models. Additionally, the plant serves as an α-glucosidase inhibitory agent, which suggests a significant role in managing postprandial blood glucose levels. These diverse pharmacological activities highlight the therapeutic potential of Viburnum fordiae in managing oxidative stress and metabolic disorders.

As discussed in a study, the fruits of Viburnum fordiae Hance contain nine new neolignan glycosides named viburfordosides A-I, two new neolignans called fordianes A and B, and seven known analogues. These metabolites were evaluated for their alpha-glucosidase inhibitory, ABTS•+ and DPPH• scavenging, and anti-inflammatory activities. Among the 14 metabolites capable of inhibiting NO production in LPS-stimulated RAW264.7 macrophage cells, compounds 2, 4, 6, 10, 11, 14, 17, and 18 were identified as potent. These specific potent compounds exhibited IC50 values ranging from 10.88-41.10 μM. Consequently, the neolignan compounds in Viburnum fordiae fruits may serve as a medicinal resource to prevent and treat type 2 diabetes.
