Viburnum fordiae

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum fordiae?

Viburnum fordiae belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Dipsacales and is a member of the family Viburnaceae. Further classification places it within the genus Viburnum, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species foridae.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum fordiae has opposite, simple, ovate leaves that measure two to four inches long and feature serrated margins with prominent pinnate venation. The foliage often transitions to vibrant shades of red, purple, or bronze during the autumn months. Its woody stems are multi-stemmed and can reach heights of six to ten feet in a spreading shrub habit. Small, white, five-petaled flowers emerge in flat-topped cymes during late spring to form fragrant clusters. These blossoms eventually give way to small, dark blue to black drupes that persist into the winter season.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viburnum fordiae?

This plant has potent antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and enzyme inhibitory properties, specifically demonstrating ABTS•+ scavenging, DPPH• scavenging, anti-inflammatory, inhibiting NO production, and α-glucosidase inhibitory activities. Its ability to scavenge free radicals through ABTS•+ and DPPH• mechanisms makes it a significant source of natural antioxidants. Furthermore, the plant exerts anti-inflammatory effects by effectively inhibiting NO production in cellular models. It also shows promise in metabolic health by acting as an α-glucosidase inhibitor to help manage glucose levels. These diverse pharmacological activities highlight its potential for various therapeutic applications.

According to a study published by "Journal of agricultural and food chemistry", the fruits of Viburnum fordiae Hance contain nine new neolignan glycosides named viburfordosides A-I, two new neolignnans called fordianes A and B, and seven known analogues. These secondary metabolites possess α-glucosidase inhibitory, ABTS•+ and DPPH• scavenging, and anti-inflammatory activities. Among the 14 metabolites capable of inhibiting NO production in LPS-stimulated RAW264.7 macrophage cells, compounds 2, 4, 6, 10, 11, 14, 17, and 18 were identified as potent. These specific compounds demonstrated IC50 values ranging from 10.88-41.10 μM. Such results suggest that the neolignan compounds in Viburnum fordiae may serve as a medicinal resource to prevent and treat type 2 diabetes.
