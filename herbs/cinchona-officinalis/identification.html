Botanical Identification of Quinine Tree (Cinchona officinalis)

To identify Quinine Tree (Cinchona officinalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

The Quinine Tree, Cinchona officinalis, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rubiales, family Rubiaceae, genus Cinchona, and species Cinchona officinalis. These classifications help identify its botanical relationships and place it within the broader plant kingdom.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the Quinine Tree is Cinchona officinalis, first described by the Swedish botanist Carl Peter Thunberg in 1784. It is also known by several synonyms, including Cinchona succirubra and Cinchona ledgeriana, reflecting historical taxonomic revisions. Common names such as Quinine Tree, Jesuit's Bark, and Cinchona are widely recognized in botanical and medicinal contexts.

Morphological Characteristics

The Quinine Tree, Cinchona officinalis, is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree with a straight trunk and dense crown. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, supporting its growth in tropical climates. The stem is smooth and cylindrical, with a bark that is reddish-brown and peels in thin layers.

Diagnostic Identification Features

The Quinine Tree can be distinguished by its opposite, elliptic leaves with entire margins, red to purple flowers in clusters, and dark purple, ellipsoid berries. It is often confused with other Cinchona species, but its leaf shape and floral color are key differentiators. The bark is thick and fissured, adding to its unique appearance.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

The Quinine Tree is native to tropical regions of South America, particularly the Andean foothills. It thrives in warm, humid climates with abundant rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils and is typically found at altitudes between 600 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cinchona officinalis blooms from April to June, producing small white to pink flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to September. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested during the dry season, from November to March, when the bark contains the highest concentration of quinine.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cinchona officinalis is closely related to other species within the Cinchona genus. It shares family traits with the Rubiaceae family, including opposite leaves and small flowers. Its closest relatives are other Cinchona species, which are also used for medicinal purposes. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary history and medicinal properties.
