Solanum lycopersicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum lycopersicum?

Solanum lycopersicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical descent, it falls under the order Solanales and belongs to the family Solanaceae. At the most specific taxonomic levels, it is identified by the genus Solanum and is designated as the species lycopersicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum lycopersicum has an herbaceous, branched stem that supports pinnately compound leaves with serrated margins and a fleshy, globose fruit containing numerous small seeds. The foliage is typically covered in fine trichomes that give the plant a slightly pubescent texture. Its flowers consist of five bright yellow petals arranged in a star shape, emerging from a calyx that protects the developing ovary. The fruit transitions from a green stage to a vibrant red or orange color upon reaching full physiological maturity. These morphological traits are characteristic of the Solanaceae family and are essential for its reproductive cycle.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Andean region of western South America, specifically spanning parts of Peru, Ecuador, Chile, and Bolivia. While its ancestral roots lie in these high-altitude environments, it has since been naturalized in many other temperate and subtropical regions across the globe. Modern cultivation has expanded its presence to nearly every continent where human agriculture is practiced. Large-scale production is particularly concentrated in major agricultural hubs like China, India, Turkey, and the United States. This widespread distribution is a direct result of its global importance as a primary food crop.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Solanum lycopersicum contains various phytochemicals in its fruit, leaves, and seeds that are utilized for their therapeutic properties. The ripe fruit is a primary source of lycopene, a potent antioxidant used to support cardiovascular health and reduce cancer risks. Extracts from the leaves are sometimes studied for their potential anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activities. Additionally, the seeds contain essential fatty acids and sterols that contribute to nutritional and medicinal value. These different components work together to provide a wide range of bioactive compounds for human health.

What are the pharmacological activities of Solanum lycopersicum?

This plant has anti-carcinogenic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antitumoral, and anticancer pharmacological activities derived from its rich concentration of bioactive compounds like lycopene. These secondary metabolites function by neutralizing free radicals to mitigate oxidative stress within cellular structures. Such antioxidant properties serve as a foundational mechanism that helps prevent DNA damage and subsequent malignant transformations. Furthermore, the plant exhibits potent anti-inflammatory effects that can inhibit the signaling pathways involved in chronic swelling and tissue damage. By regulating these biological processes, the extract demonstrates significant antitumoral potential by suppressing the growth and proliferation of abnormal cells.

As discussed in a study, lycopene from Solanum lycopersicum belongs to a subgroup of non-oxygenated carotenoids. This substance possesses antioxidant and anti-carcinogenic properties that are comparatively more powerful than the majority of plasma carotenoids. When foods containing lycopene are processed, the cell wall breaks down during the thermal process to enable extraction from chromoplasts and improve bioavailability. The study indicates that tomato sauces have a significant concentration of carotenoids and a specific content in the lycopene proportion compared with total carotenoids. Heat treatment was found not to interfere with the bioavailability of these carotenoids and lycopene in the tomato sauce and raw materials.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds alpha-Tomatine, Solanine, Chlorogenic acid, Rosmarinic acid, and various Flavonoids. Alpha-Tomatine and Solanine are glycoalkaloids that often serve as natural defense mechanisms with potential biological activities. Chlorogenic acid and Rosmarinic acid are powerful polyphenolic compounds known for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the presence of Flavonoids contributes to the plant's ability to neutralize free radicals within biological systems. Together, these diverse chemical constituents provide a complex profile of therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, Solanum lycopersicum contains steroid glycoalkaloids which are a class of cholesterol-derived metabolites. The major steroid glycalkaloid in the tomato is α-tomatine, which accumulates extensively in all plant tissues. This compound is particularly found in the leaves and immature green fruits. α-Tomatine exhibits diverse biological activities that contribute to plant defense against pathogens and herbivores. These activities also confer certain medicinal benefits for human health.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the management and prevention of cancer, cardiovascular diseases, diabetes mellitus, inflammation, and oxidative stress. The presence of high concentrations of lycopene provides potent antioxidant properties that neutralize free radicals to combat oxidative stress. These bioactive compounds also help regulate blood glucose levels, offering potential therapeutic benefits for individuals with diabetes mellitus. Furthermore, the plant's phytochemicals assist in reducing chronic inflammation throughout the body. Such biological activities contribute to a decreased risk of developing various cardiovascular diseases and certain types of cancer.

As discussed in a study, Solanum lycopersicum, specifically in the form of tomato pomace composed of peels and seeds, contains lycopene which is an antioxidant known for reducing the risk of cancer. Using a hexane/acetone (50/50) solvent mixture with a Hansen theoretical distance of 7.2, researchers achieved a lycopene yield of 20.05 mg/100 g and an extraction yield of 3.12%. This mixture also showed a total phenolic content of 24.06 mg equivalent gallic acid/100 g dry matter and a flavonoid content of 30.55 mg equivalent catechin/100 g dry matter. The antioxidant activity for this fraction resulted in an IC50 value of 13.51 µg/mL for 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl and 8.52 µg/mL for ABTS.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea if parts of the plant are ingested in toxic amounts. These gastrointestinal distress symptoms are typical indicators of solanine poisoning, which occurs when the glycoalkaloids in the plant reach dangerous levels in the body. Severe cases of solanine poisoning symptoms may also include neurological issues like dizziness or hallucinations. Individuals should avoid consuming any green or unripe parts of the plant to prevent these adverse reactions. Monitoring for these specific side effects is crucial if accidental ingestion is suspected.
