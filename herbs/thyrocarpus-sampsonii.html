Thyrocarpus sampsonii

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Thyrocarpus sampsonii has a perennial growth habit characterized by a woody caudex and stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters. The leaves are primarily basal, forming a rosette of narrow, linear to lanceolate blades that are often sparsely distributed. Its inflorescence consists of small, yellow flower heads arranged in terminal clusters or solitary units. Each individual flower head contains tubular disc florets that are characteristic of the Asteraceae family. The overall structure is adapted for survival in rocky or arid environments where moisture is limited.

      Thyrocarpus sampsonii is prepared primarily as a decoction, a concentrated liquid extract made by boiling the plant material in water to release its active constituents.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Thyrocarpus sampsonii can cause severe allergic skin reactions, gastrointestinal distress, and potential neurotoxicity if ingested or handled improperly. Individuals may experience localized contact dermatitis characterized by redness, itching, and swelling upon touching the plant tissues. If the plant is swallowed, it may lead to nausea, vomiting, or intense abdominal cramping. There is also a risk of systemic toxicity that could impact the central nervous system in sensitive subjects. Precautions should be taken to avoid any direct exposure to the plant's sap or spores.
