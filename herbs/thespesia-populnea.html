Thespesia populnea

What's the taxonomical classification of Thespesia populnea?

Thespesia populnea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it resides in the order Malvales and the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Thespesia, and its specific species name, populnea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Thespesia populnea has simple, alternate, ovate to elliptical leaves with entire margins and a lustrous, dark green upper surface. The foliage typically measures between five to twelve centimeters in length and possesses a prominent midrib. Its woody stems develop into medium to large trees characterized by a thick, greyish bark. Small, trumpet-shaped flowers emerge in clusters, displaying a vibrant yellow color that transitions to a deep reddish-purple as they age. These blossoms are accompanied by globose, green fruits that mature into dark, velvety drupes containing several seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of the Indo-Pacific, spanning from the coastal areas of East Africa through the Indian subcontinent to Southeast Asia and northern Australia. It thrives primarily in maritime environments such as sandy beaches, coastal forests, and riverbanks where moisture is abundant. Because it is highly salt-tolerant, the species frequently colonizes island habitats across the Pacific Ocean. Its distribution is closely tied to warm climates that prevent frost damage to its delicate foliage. Consequently, it is commonly found throughout the tropical belt where seasonal monsoons or consistent humidity support its growth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Thespesia populnea?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties. Its extracts function effectively to reduce pain and suppress inflammatory responses in biological models. The presence of various bioactive compounds allows the plant to inhibit the growth of diverse bacteria and microbes. Additionally, the phytochemical constituents provide strong antioxidant defense by neutralizing harmful free radicals. These combined therapeutic effects make the species a valuable subject for medicinal research and drug development.

As discussed in a study, the aqueous extract of the heartwood of Thespesia populnea is effective in the treatment of ulcerative colitis. When mice were treated with DNBS, the Hb concentration was reduced to 13.80 ± 0.5, but the aqueous extract restored it significantly to 17.20 ± 0.5. The DNBS treatment increased MDA levels to 10.82 nm/mg in tissue and 10.25 nm/ml in blood, which the aqueous extract reduced to 2.69 nm/mg in tissue and 3.59 nm/ml in blood. Furthermore, the aqueous extract significantly reduced MP0 levels from 412 U/mg in tissue and 404 U/ml in blood down to 205 U/mg in tissue and 219 U/ml in blood. The aqueous extract also significantly reduced protease activity that was markedly increased in DNBS-treated animals. These effects were observed when administering extracts at doses of 100 and 200 mg/kg.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds gossypol, flavonoids, kaempferol, apiigenin, and catechin. The presence of flavonoids provides a foundation for various antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities within the organism. Among these, kaempferol and apiigenin function as specific polyphenolic substances known for their potential cellular protective properties. Additionally, the inclusion of catechin contributes to the plant's complex chemical profile and biological potency. Together, these diverse constituents work to form a sophisticated array of bioactive agents.

As discussed in a study, Thespesia populnea is reported to contain flavonoids, quercetin, kaempferol, gossypetin, Kaempferol-3-monoglucoside, β-sitosterol, kaempferol-7-glucoside, and gossypol. The study focused on the simultaneous determination of quercetin and kaempferol using a mobile phase of toluene: ethyl acetate: formic acid in a 6:4:0.3 v/v/v ratio. Calibration curves for standard quercetin and kaempferol showed linear relationships over concentration ranges of 100-600 ng/spot and 500-3000 ng/spot with correlation coefficients (R2) of 0.9955 and 0.9967. Limits of detection and quantitation were recorded as 32.06 and 85.33 ng/spot and 74.055 and 243.72 ng/spot for quercetin and kaempferol, respectively.
