Cyclea insularis

What's the taxonomical classification of Cyclea insularis?

Cyclea insularis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Ranunculales. This specific plant is a representative of the family Menispermaceae, situated within the genus Cyclea, and is identified by its unique species name, insularis.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, and various inflammatory conditions through its medicinal properties. Local practitioners often apply its extracts or crushed leaves directly to affected areas to promote healing and reduce swelling. It is frequently utilized in traditional medicine to address minor cuts and abrasions that may become infected. Some studies suggest that its bioactive compounds possess antimicrobial activities that help combat specific pathogens. By utilizing its natural chemical constituents, healers aim to alleviate discomfort and accelerate the body's recovery processes.

What are the herbal preparations of Cyclea insularis?

Cyclea insularis is prepared as a decoction, a topical poultice, or a crushed juice extracted from the fresh leaves. The leaves are often boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal ailments. For skin conditions or wounds, the plant material is macerated and applied directly to the affected area as a paste. Some traditional practices involve squeezing the fresh plant matter to obtain a liquid used for cleansing or topical application. These various methods allow different bioactive compounds to be utilized depending on the specific therapeutic need.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cyclea insularis can cause significant toxicity if ingested, potentially leading to severe liver damage or fatal outcomes due to its high concentration of toxic alkaloids. While specific clinical studies on human side effects are limited, the presence of these chemical compounds suggests a high risk of hepatotoxicity. Ingesting parts of the plant may trigger acute gastrointestinal distress or systemic poisoning. Researchers have noted that the biological activity of its extracts can interfere with vital metabolic processes in animal models. Consequently, any accidental consumption requires immediate medical intervention to prevent organ failure.
