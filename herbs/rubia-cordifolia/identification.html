Botanical Identification of Indian Madder (Rubia cordifolia)

To identify Indian Madder (Rubia cordifolia) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Indian Madder, Rubia cordifolia, belongs to the plant kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rubiales, family Rubiaceae, genus Rubia, and species Rubia cordifolia. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the broader group of eudicots, which are characterized by their two seed leaves.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Indian Madder is Rubia cordifolia, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including R. tinctorum and R. graveolens. Common names such as "Indian Madder" and "Kali Madder" reflect its widespread use in traditional medicine. Regional and historical names further highlight its cultural significance across different regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Indian Madder (Rubia cordifolia) is a perennial herb with a fibrous root system. It has upright, branched stems that are hairy and reddish. The leaves are alternate, heart-shaped, and have a rough texture. The inflorescence is a dense cluster of small, pinkish-white flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous small, dark brown seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Indian Madder (Rubia cordifolia) can be distinguished by its heart-shaped leaves with serrated edges and red-orange roots. It differs from similar species like Rubia manas and Rubia pachystachya through leaf shape, root color, and habitat preferences. Key features include the distinctive leaf venation and the presence of a strong, aromatic scent.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Indian Madder (Rubia cordifolia) is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly India, Nepal, and Bhutan. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found at altitudes between 500 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Indian Madder (Rubia cordifolia) blooms from May to July, producing small pink to purple flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to September. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest the roots is in late autumn, when the plant has fully matured and the active compounds are most concentrated.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Indian Madder (Rubia cordifolia) is closely related to species within the Rubia genus, sharing common traits such as opposite leaves and flowering habits. It belongs to the Rubiaceae family, which includes other medicinal plants like R. tinctorum. Its phylogenetic placement reflects shared characteristics with other Rubia species and close relatives in the family.
