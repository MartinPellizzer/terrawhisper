Anthriscus sylvestris

What's the taxonomical classification of Anthriscus sylvestris?

Anthriscus sylvestris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and follows the order Apiales. The plant is a member of the family Apiaceae, which is characterized by its complex flowering structures, and it is specifically categorized under the genus Anthriscus, with the species epithet sylvestris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anthriscus sylvestris has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 150 centimeters and features a hollow structure with fine, bristly hairs. The leaves are highly dissected and bipinnate, characterized by a dark green color and a coarse, serrated texture. At the top of the plant, it produces large, flat-topped compound umbels composed of numerous small, white flowers. Each individual flower possesses five petals and is part of a complex branching arrangement. The root system consists of a stout, fleshy taproot that provides stability for the growing stem.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe and parts of Western Asia but has become widely naturalized across North America and other temperate regions of the world. It frequently thrives in disturbed soils along roadsides, meadows, and forest edges throughout these colonized areas. Its ability to spread rapidly allows it to establish dense populations in various climates ranging from temperate to subarctic zones. Because it is highly adaptable, it is now commonly found in many different habitats across many different continents.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Anthriscus sylvestris contains medicinal properties primarily within its seeds and occasionally its roots. The dried seeds are the most frequently utilized part for their essential oils and flavoring components. These seeds are often used in traditional herbalism to stimulate digestion and alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort. Some historical applications also involve using the root for its potential calming effects. However, careful identification is necessary because this species is easily confused with toxic plants.

What are the pharmacological activities of Anthriscus sylvestris?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antitussive, cytotoxic, and antitumor pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help mitigate oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also functions as an antitussive agent to suppress coughing reflexes. Research into its cytotoxic effects suggests potential applications in inhibiting the growth of various cell lines. These cellular interactions contribute to its broader role in demonstrating antitumor activity.

As discussed in a study, Anthriscus sylvestris HOFFM provides a medicinal herbal product called deoxypodophyllotoxin (Anthricin). This compound inhibits COX-2 and COX-1-dependent phases of PGD(2) generation in BMMC with IC50 values of 1.89 microM and 65.3 microM, respectively. It also inhibits the COX-1 and 2-dependent conversion of exogenous arachidonic acid to PGD(2) with IC50 values of 0.01 microM and 12.1 microM. While it does not inhibit COX-2 protein expression up to a concentration of 30 microM, it directly inhibits COX-2 activity. Additionally, the compound inhibits the production of LTC(4) in a dose dependent manner with an IC50 value of 0.37 microM. These results demonstrate that the compound has a dual cyclooxygenase-2 selective/5-lipoxygenase inhibitory activity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds angeloyl podophyllotoxin, deoxypodophyllotoxin, falcarindiol, morelensin, and bursehernin. These specific lignans are of significant interest in pharmacological research due to their potential biological activities. Many of these substances are studied for their ability to inhibit cell division in various types of cancer cells. Researchers analyze these chemical structures to develop new therapeutic agents with targeted effects. The presence of such a diverse array of bioactive molecules makes the species a valuable subject for phytochemical investigation.

As discussed in a study, activity-guided fractionation of the roots of Anthriscus sylvestris resulted in the isolation and characterization of 5 cytotoxic compounds. The hexane soluble fraction yielded deoxypodophyllotoxin (1), falcarindiol (2), and angeloyl podophyllotoxin (5). Additionally, morelensin (3) and bursehernin (4) were isolated from the chloroform soluble fraction. This research represents the first report of the occurrence of compound 5 in nature.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating asthma, cough, gastric disorders, immediate-type allergic reaction, and passive cutaneous anaphylaxis reaction. While certain traditional applications focus on respiratory relief and digestive ailments, the plant is more frequently noted for its potential to cause adverse medical issues. Exposure to its sap or pollen can trigger an immediate-type allergic reaction in sensitive individuals. This sensitivity may further manifest as a passive cutaneous anaphylaxis reaction upon skin contact. Consequently, its role in medical discussions often centers more on these allergic complications than on therapeutic benefits.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion of the leaves to soothe digestive upsets or as a decoction of the roots to address inflammatory conditions. A tincture made from the aerial parts serves as a concentrated liquid extract for more potent medicinal applications. For external skin irritations or minor wounds, a fresh poultice of crushed leaves can be applied directly to the affected area. These various methods allow practitioners to utilize the plant's phytochemical properties for both internal and external therapeutic needs.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation and dermatitis through contact with its sap, which often leads to painful blistering. Exposure to sunlight after touching the plant can trigger intense photosensitivity, making the skin extremely vulnerable to UV damage. If the plant is ingested, individuals may experience digestive distress such as nausea. Some people might also report feeling dizziness following consumption. These reactions make careful identification necessary to avoid accidental harm.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Skin irritation/dermatitis
  • Photosensitivity
  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
