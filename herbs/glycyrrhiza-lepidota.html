What to know about Glycyrrhiza lepidota (licorice) before using it medicinally

Glycyrrhiza lepidota, commonly known as licorice, is a herb that has been prized for centuries for its numerous health benefits, including its ability to soothe digestive issues, reduce inflammation, and even exhibit antimicrobial properties.

In cultivation, Glycyrrhiza lepidota is a relatively low-maintenance herb that thrives in well-drained soil and full sun, making it a popular choice for herbal gardens. Botanically, Glycyrrhiza lepidota is a legume that belongs to the Fabaceae family and is characterized by its distinctive root system, which is rich in glycosides and saponins.

Historically, Glycyrrhiza lepidota has been used for medicinal purposes in traditional Chinese, Ayurvedic, and Unani medicine, with the ancient Greek physician Dioscorides writing extensively about its therapeutic properties in his work "De Materia Medica".

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Glycyrrhiza lepidota.

What are the medicinal properties of Glycyrrhiza lepidota?

Glycyrrhiza lepidota helps with digestion, inflammation, and skin conditions, such as eczema and acne, due to its anti-inflammatory and antibacterial properties. It is also used to treat respiratory issues, such as coughs and bronchitis. Its expectorant properties help to loosen mucus.

The active constituents of Glycyrrhiza lepidota, including glycyrrhizin, isoflavones, and saponins, provide its medicinal properties. Glycyrrhizin is a natural anti-inflammatory that helps to reduce swelling and pain. Isoflavones have antioxidant and estrogenic effects. Saponins have antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties.

The roots of Glycyrrhiza lepidota are the most commonly used parts for medicinal purposes. The roots can be dried and powdered or extracted into a liquid. The plant can also be used topically in the form of a paste or salve to treat skin conditions.

Improper use of Glycyrrhiza lepidota can lead to side effects, including high blood pressure, hypokalemia (low potassium levels), and edema (fluid retention). Long-term use can also lead to adrenal insufficiency and dependence.

When using Glycyrrhiza lepidota medicinally, precautions should be taken to avoid overconsumption and interaction with other medications. It is recommended to use the plant under the guidance of a healthcare professional. Dosage and preparation methods should be carefully followed to avoid side effects.

What are the horticulural aspects of Glycyrrhiza lepidota?

Glycyrrhiza lepidota grow best in full sun and well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 8.0. It thrives in areas with moderate watering, but can tolerate some drought. Temperature range is between 64°F and 95°F (18°C and 35°C).

Plant Glycyrrhiza lepidota in spring or fall, when the risk of frost is low. Dig a hole twice as wide and as deep as the root ball, then mix in a 2-inch layer of compost or well-rotted manure. Space plants 10 to 15 feet apart to prevent overcrowding. Water thoroughly after planting.

Harvest Glycyrrhiza lepidota roots in late summer or early fall, when they are mature but still firm. Use a fork to gently loosen the soil around the roots, then lift them out of the ground. Cut the roots into sections, and dry them immediately to preserve their flavor and medicinal properties.

Glycyrrhiza lepidota is susceptible to pests like aphids, spider mites, and root-knot nematodes. It is also prone to diseases like powdery mildew, leaf spot, and root rot, which are often caused by overwatering or poor air circulation. Regular monitoring and maintenance can help prevent these issues.

The plant's root system is notable for its thick, fleshy taproot which can reach up to 10 feet in length and contains a high concentration of glycyrrhizin, the sweet-tasting compound responsible for its licorice flavor and aroma.
