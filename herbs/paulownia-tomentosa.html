Paulownia tomentosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Paulownia tomentosa?

Paubownia tomentoza belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the species is part of the order Lamiales and falls under the family Paulowniaceae. Finally, it is categorized within the genus Paubownia, with the specific epithet tomentosa identifying the individual species.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Paulownia tomentosa has large, heart-shaped leaves that are covered in dense, velvety gray hairs on both the upper and lower surfaces. These thick, felt-like trichomes provide a distinctive soft texture and help the plant retain moisture. The foliage can grow to considerable sizes, often reaching widths of up to thirty centimeters. As the plant matures, the leaves transition from a bright green to a more muted, grayish-green hue due to the hair density. This morphological trait is a key identifier that distinguishes the species from other members of its genus.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Staphylococcus aureus infection, Streptococcus faecium infection, Streptococcus pyogenes infection, airway inflammation, and various forms of respiratory disease. Its medicinal properties focus on combating specific bacterial pathogens that cause systemic and localized infections. The plant's bioactive compounds work to reduce swelling and irritation within the pulmonary tracts. By addressing airway inflammation, it helps alleviate the symptoms associated with chronic respiratory disease. This botanical remedy serves as a multi-functional agent against both bacterial threats and inflammatory conditions.

According to a study published by "Archives of pharmacal research", the butanol extract of Paulownia tomentosa stem showed antibacterial activity against Staphylococcus aureus (SG511, 285 and 503), Streptococcus pyogenes (A308 and A77) and Streptococcus faecium MD8b. The most active compound identified in the extract was campneoside I. This specific compound had a minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) of 150 micrograms/ml against Streptococcus and Staphylococcus species. The methoxy group of campneoside I was postulated to be the essential element for this antibacterial activity.
