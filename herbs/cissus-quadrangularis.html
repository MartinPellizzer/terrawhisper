What to know about Cissus quadrangularis (veldt grape) before using it medicinally

Cissus quadrangularis, commonly known as veldt grape, is a highly valued herb renowned for its profound health benefits, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anti-cancer properties that improve overall well-being and promote longevity.

In cultivation, this versatile herb is relatively easy to grow and maintain, as it thrives in a variety of environments and can tolerate some degree of neglect. Botanically, Cissus quadrangularis belongs to the Vitaceae family and is characterized by its distinctive, quadrangular stems and heart-shaped leaves.

With its rich history of use in traditional medicine, dating back thousands of years to ancient India and Africa, Cissus quadrangularis has been revered for its remarkable healing properties by cultures worldwide.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Cissus quadrangularis.

What are the medicinal properties of Cissus quadrangularis?

Cissus quadrangularis helps with joint pain and inflammation, osteoporosis, and muscle cramps. It is also used to improve bone density and to reduce the risk of bone fractures. Its anti-inflammatory properties are effective in reducing inflammation and pain.

The active constituents of Cissus quadrangularis include flavonoids, alkaloids, and saponins, which are responsible for its medicinal properties. The flavonoids in Cissus quadrangularis have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, while the alkaloids and saponins have anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic effects.

The most commonly used parts of the Cissus quadrangularis plant for medicinal purposes are the leaves, stem, and root. The leaves are rich in flavonoids, while the stem and root are rich in alkaloids and saponins. The leaves and stem are used to treat joint pain and inflammation, while the root is used to treat bone density and muscle cramps.

Improper use of Cissus quadrangularis can cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Allergic reactions to the plant may also occur, including hives, itching, and difficulty breathing. In rare cases, Cissus quadrangularis can interact with other medications and reduce their effectiveness.

When using Cissus quadrangularis medicinally, precautions should be taken to avoid interactions with other medications, particularly blood thinners and diabetes medications. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should also consult a healthcare professional before using Cissus quadrangularis, as its effects on the fetus and baby are not well understood.

What are the horticulural aspects of Cissus quadrangularis?

Cissus quadrangularis grow best in well-drained soil with a slightly acidic pH (5.5-6.5). It requires full sun to partial shade and tolerates a wide range of temperatures, from 15-30°C (59-86°F). High humidity and consistent moisture also contribute to its optimal growth.

Cissus quadrangularis is typically grown from cuttings or seed. Plant cuttings 15-30 cm long, with 2-3 nodes, 2.5-5 cm deep in well-draining soil, and water sparingly. Seeds are sown 1-2 cm deep in a warm, humid environment, with consistent moisture. Fertilize sparingly until established.

Harvest Cissus quadrangularis when the leaves are 10-15 cm long, and the stems are thickened. Cut the plant at the base, leaving a 10-15 cm stem. Allow the cut stem to dry for a few hours, then use it as a cut stem. Young leaves can be harvested for medicinal use, when 5-10 cm long.

Cissus quadrangularis is susceptible to mealybugs, scale, and spider mites. Root rot and leaf spot diseases, caused by Fusarium and Cercospora, respectively, can also occur. Regularly inspect the plant for pests and diseases, and treat promptly with appropriate insecticides and fungicides.

What are the botanical characteristics of Cissus quadrangularis?

Cissus quadrangularis, commonly known as the quarter-leaf vine or asthishrunkal in Ayurvedic medicine, is a perennial climber that exhibits several distinctive botanical characteristics.

The plant has a woody stem with four angled ridges, giving it a characteristic "quarter-leaf" shape. Each of these angles is adorned with sharp thorns, providing protection against herbivores and insects. The leaves are simple, elliptical in shape, and measure up to 10 cm in length, featuring a wavy margin and a prominent midvein.

The leaf surface is dark green and glossy, while the underside is lighter and hairy. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are white or yellowish in color, arranged in dense panicles at the apex of the stem. These flowers eventually give rise to clusters of red, berry-like fruit that contain multiple seeds each.

Overall, the unique combination of angled stems, thorns, and leaf shape makes Cissus quadrangularis a striking and easily recognizable plant species.

What is the taxonomy of Cissus quadrangularis?

Cissus quadrangularis belongs to the family Vitaceae, which includes grapes and other similar species.

It is further classified as a member of the genus Cissus, which comprises around 350 species of woody vines and shrubs.

Specifically, C. quadrangularis is considered a species of the subgenus Leptopus, characterized by its distinctive quadrangular stem and leaves with serrate margins.
