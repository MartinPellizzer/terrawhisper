Scientific and Botanical Profile

Devil's Ivy, with botanical name Cissus quadrangularis, is a member of the Rhamnaceae family and is native to regions such as India, Sri Lanka, Madagascar, Eastern Africa, Southern Arabia, Seychelles, and Comoros.

It is commonly known by various names including Devil's Ivy, Devil's Backbone, Cissus Vine, Cissus Ivy, Bamboo Vine, Ox Blood Vine, Ovalleaf Creeper, Creeping Fig, Horned Cucumber, Golden Phantom, Indian Rubber Plant, Golden Chain, Devil's Backbone, Golden Cissus, Indian Rubber Plant, Creeping Oxeye, Golden Trumpet Cissus, Uro, Four-Angle Creeper, Cissus Plant, Indian Liverwort. Morphologically, it is a climbing or trailing vine characterized by its four-angled, glossy, dark green stems and opposite, ovate leaves with a waxy surface.

The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers followed by green, fleshy fruits that turn red when mature, often used in traditional medicine.

History and Cultural Relevance

Devil's Ivy, scientifically known as Cissus quadrangularis, was used by ancient civilizations in Africa and the Middle East for its medicinal properties and spiritual significance.

In traditional African medicine, it has been employed for centuries to treat ailments such as wounds, fractures, and inflammation, with its high calcium and silica content believed to promote bone healing. The plant also holds cultural importance in various rituals, where it is sometimes used in ceremonies to symbolize strength, resilience, and renewal. In traditional Indian Ayurvedic practices, Devil's Ivy is still utilized today for its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, often in the form of powders or decoctions to support joint health and skin conditions.

Its enduring presence in both historical and contemporary medicinal systems highlights its deep-rooted cultural relevance and continued relevance in holistic health practices.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Cissus quadrangularis has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties and is available in various forms such as fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

It can be prepared by making a tea by steeping the dried leaves in hot water, or through decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. Topical applications include using the powder or essential oil directly on the skin for wounds or inflammation. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-3 grams of powder per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

It is advised to use cissus quadrangularis for short durations, no longer than a few weeks, to avoid potential side effects and to monitor for any adverse reactions.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Cissus quadrangularis can be a beneficial medicinal plant used for its potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and bone-supporting properties, but it is important to be aware of its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals. There is limited research on its interactions with medications, but it may interfere with anticoagulants or diabetes medications, so caution is advised. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with low doses, monitor for adverse reactions, and consult a healthcare professional, especially if taking other medications or supplements.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Cissus quadrangularis grows best in well-draining soil with a pH between 5.

5 and 7. 5, thriving in full sun to partial shade and requiring regular watering to maintain moist but not waterlogged conditions. It benefits from occasional fertilization during the growing season to promote healthy growth and medicinal compound production. Regular pruning and weed control are essential for maintaining plant health and encouraging new growth, while monitoring for pests like aphids and spider mites ensures optimal care. Harvesting is best done in the early morning or late afternoon when the plant's active compounds are most concentrated, using clean, sharp tools to cut the stems just above a node to encourage regrowth.

After harvesting, the plant material should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area to preserve potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain its medicinal properties.
