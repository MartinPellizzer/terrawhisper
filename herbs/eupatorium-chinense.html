Eupatorium chinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Eupatorium chinense?

Eupatorium chinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is part of the order Asterales and falls under the family Asteraceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Eupatorium and the species chinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eupatorium chinense has a woody stem structure, lanceolate leaves with serrated margins, and small, actinomorphic white flowers. The foliage is characterized by a deep green hue and a prominent central vein running through each blade. Its stems possess a slightly textured bark that becomes more rugged as the plant matures. The floral arrangement typically consists of clusters that emerge from the leaf axils during the blooming season. Small, fleshy fruits develop following pollination to facilitate seed dispersal.

What are the pharmacological activities of Eupatorium chinense?

This plant has cytotoxic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant pharmacological activities that demonstrate its potential for medicinal use. Research indicates that certain extracts from Eupatorium chinense can effectively inhibit the growth of various cancer cell lines through cytotoxic mechanisms. Furthermore, its ability to modulate inflammatory pathways suggests it could serve as a natural source for treating chronic swelling or pain. The presence of bioactive compounds also contributes to its capacity to neutralize free radicals within biological systems. These multifaceted properties highlight the plant's significance in pharmacological studies and drug development.

As discussed in a study, ten new sesquiterpenoids named eupachinilides A-J (1-10) were isolated from the whole plant of Eupatorium chinense. Along with these new compounds, seven known sesquiterpenoids were also identified, including eupachifolin D (11), budlein B (12), 8 beta-(4'-hydroxytiglyloxy)-2 beta-hydroxy-1 alpha H,5 alpha H,6 beta H,7 alpha H-guai-3,10(14),11(13)-trien-6,12-olide (13), 1,10-hydrobahia (14), 2 alpha-hydroxyeupatolide (15), eupaserrin (16), and mollisorin B (17). Regarding pharmacological activities, eupachinilides A (1), E (5), F (6), and I (9) exhibited moderate cytotoxic activities against several tumor cell lines. Spectral analysis and 2D NMR techniques were utilized to revise the previously assigned structures for eupachifolins B (11a), C (13a), and D (11).

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, intense abdominal cramping, nausea, and acute vomiting if ingested in significant quantities. These adverse reactions often occur shortly after consumption due to the presence of toxic alkaloids within the foliage. Some individuals may also experience neurological symptoms such as dizziness or temporary disorientation. Prolonged exposure to the plant's sap can lead to localized skin irritation and painful contact dermatitis. Monitoring for these specific symptoms is essential for anyone who accidentally encounters the species.
