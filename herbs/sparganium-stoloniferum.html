Sparganium stoloniferum

What's the taxonomical classification of Sparganium stoloniferum?

Sparganium stoloniferum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant falls under the order Poales and is a member of the family Typhaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Sparganium and the species stoloniferum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sparganium stoloniferum has a creeping growth habit characterized by slender, horizontal stolons that produce small, inconspicuous flowers at their nodes. These runners allow the plant to spread vegetatively across the soil surface to colonize new areas. The leaves are typically narrow and linear, emerging from the nodes along the creeping stems. Small, inconspicuous flowers are often borne on short pedicels near the ground. This reproductive strategy ensures both sexual propagation through seeds and rapid clonal expansion via its specialized stoloniferous structure.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Sparstolonin B and methyl 5-hydroxy-2-(2-hydroxyphenyl)benzofuran-4-carboxylate. These specific chemical constituents are studied for their potential bioactive properties and pharmacological significance. Researchers examine these molecules to understand how they interact with various biological pathways in the human body. The presence of such complex organic structures suggests the plant may possess unique therapeutic qualities. Further investigation into these compounds could lead to the development of new medicinal treatments.

As discussed in a study, Sparganium stoloniferum contains the isocumarin compound Sparstolonin B (SsnB), which possesses well documented anti-inflammatary activity. This compound demonstrates antithromboic effects by prolonging the clotting time of human platelet-poor serum at concentrations comparable to the clinical anticoagulant rivaroxaban. SsnB inhibits human platelet aggregation induced by adenosine diphosphate (ADP) or the thromboxane A2 analog U46619. The compound also inhibits U46619-induced and ADP-induced phosphorylation of phospholipase C (PLC)γ2/protein kinase C (PKC) and intracellular calcium mobilization. Furthermore, SsnB reduces coagulation factor Xa (FXa) catalytic activity and production by endothelial cells. Finally, SsnB injection reduces thrombus formation time, number, and size in mouse models of thromboembolism.

    What side effects this plant can have?

Some individuals may experience localized swelling or small bumps where the plant has touched the skin. While it is not known to be acutely toxic if ingested, consuming any part of it could lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or unpredictable digestive upset.
