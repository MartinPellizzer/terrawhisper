Lithospermum arvense

What's the taxonomical classification of Lithospermum arvense?

Lithospermum arvense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant falls under the order Boraginales and the family Boraginaceae. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by its placement within the genus Lithospermum, with the specific epithet arvense designating its unique species.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Lithospermum arvense contains naphthoquinones, specifically lithospermic acid and various shikonin derivatives, which serve as its primary bioactive compounds. These chemical constituents are known for possessing significant antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. The plant also contains various fatty acids and sterols that contribute to its complex chemical profile. Researchers study these specific molecules to understand their potential applications in dermatological treatments and wound healing. These compounds are often concentrated within the roots of the plant.

What are the therapeutic actions of Lithospermum arvense?

Lithospermum arvense has therapeutic actions primarily centered on its ability to serve as an anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial agent through its high concentration of naphthodianthrones. These specific pigments are believed to assist in soothing irritated tissues and reducing swelling in various applications. Traditional herbal practices often utilize the plant to address skin conditions due to these restorative properties. Some historical accounts also suggest it may aid in regulating internal secretions. However, modern clinical evidence remains limited regarding its systemic medicinal use in humans.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin conditions including eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis, and general inflammation of the dermis. Its medicinal properties are primarily derived from the presence of red pigments known as naphthodianthrones. These compounds exhibit significant anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activities that assist in healing wounded skin. Historically, extracts from the roots have been applied topically to soothe irritations and reduce redness. While primarily used for dermatological issues, some traditional practices also involve its use in addressing internal inflammatory processes.

What are the herbal preparations of Lithospermum arvense?

Lithospermum arvense is prepared primarily as a red-pigmented oil infusion, a decoction, or a tincture to extract its naphthoquinone constituents. The most distinctive preparation involves macerating the roots in a fixed oil, which produces a vibrant crimson color due to the presence of shikonin. This medicinal oil is frequently applied topically to treat skin inflammations, wounds, or bruises. Alternatively, the dried roots can be boiled in water to create a decoction for internal use. Tinctures made with alcohol serve as a concentrated way to preserve the plant's bioactive compounds for long-term storage.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Lithospermum arvense can cause skin irritation and allergic contact dermatitis due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids within its tissues. These specific chemical compounds are known to be hepatotoxic, meaning they can potentially cause significant damage to the liver if ingested. Long-term exposure or accidental consumption may lead to more severe systemic issues such as veno-occlusive disease. While topical contact is the most common concern, any part of the plant should be handled with caution to avoid these adverse reactions. Users should avoid direct skin contact and ensure the plant is not consumed in any medicinal preparation.
