Lithospermum arvense

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lithospermum arvense has slender, wiry, twining stems that reach up to 60 centimeters in length and bear alternate, lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically bright green and possesses a slightly rough texture due to fine hairs on the surface. Small, bell-shaped flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying a creamy white to pale yellowish coloration. These blossoms are characterized by five fused petals that form a distinct tubular shape. After flowering, the plant produces small, hard, nut-like fruits that are often dark or brownish in color.

This plant is native to much of Europe and parts of temperate Asia, ranging from the British Isles across the continent to Russia. While it is widespread across its natural range, it is often considered an opportunistic species in agricultural landscapes. Its presence is most consistent in temperate climates where seasonal cycles support its annual life cycle.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Lithospermum arvense contains naphthoquinones, specifically lithospermic acid and various shikonin derivatives, which serve as its primary bioactive compounds. These chemical constituents are known for possessing significant antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. The plant also contains various fatty acids and sterols that contribute to its complex chemical profile. Researchers study these specific molecules to understand their potential applications in dermatological treatments and wound healing. These compounds are often concentrated within the roots of the plant.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Lithospermum arvense targets enzymes and signaling proteins involved in inflammatory cascades, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX) pathways. The plant contains various naphthodianthrones and rosmarinic acid which modulate the expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6. These secondary metabolites also interact with nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) to downregulate the transcription of genes associated with oxidative stress. Furthermore, specific polyphenolic compounds within the plant may influence various protein kinases to regulate cellular defense mechanisms. This multi-target interaction provides the biochemical basis for its traditional use in treating skin inflammations and systemic oxidative damage.

What are the therapeutic actions of Lithospermum arvense?

Lithospermum arvense has therapeutic actions that include being an alterative, astringent, mucilaginous, emollient, and hemostatic agent. As an alterative, the plant helps to purify the blood and support the body's natural detoxification processes. Its astringent and hemostatic properties make it effective for stopping minor bleeding and tightening swollen tissues. The mucilaginous and emollient qualities provide a soothing effect that protects and softens irritated mucous membranes. These diverse actions allow the plant to address various inflammatory and restorative needs within the body.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause serious liver damage and kidney toxicity due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids. These toxic compounds are known to induce veno-occlusive disease, which obstructs the blood vessels in the liver. Prolonged exposure or ingestion can lead to progressive hepatic failure and permanent organ scarring. There is also a significant risk of systemic toxicity that may affect the renal system. Because of these severe physiological dangers, the plant should be handled with extreme caution and avoided for internal use.
