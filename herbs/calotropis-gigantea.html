Calotropis gigantea

What's the taxonomical classification of Calotropis gigantea?

Calotropis gigantea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Gentianales and is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Finally, it is defined by the genus Calotropis and is specifically identified by the species name gigantea.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Calotropis gigantea contains bioactive compounds such as cardenolides, flavonoids, triterpenoids, and various alkaloids that provide its therapeutic properties. The cardiac glycosides found within the plant are particularly significant for their ability to affect heart muscle contraction and ion transport. Additionally, its high concentration of flavonoids contributes potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects to its medicinal profile. These chemical constituents work together to support traditional treatments for skin diseases, respiratory ailments, and various inflammatory conditions. The synergy of these phytochemicals makes the plant a subject of interest for pharmacological research into new drug developments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Calotropis gigantea?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, inflammatory conditions, respiratory ailments, and certain types of tumors. The milky latex is often applied topically to manage warts, fungal infections, and various skin lesions. In traditional medicine, extracts are used to alleviate symptoms of asthma and bronchitis through its bronchodilatory properties. Some studies also suggest its potential in managing digestive issues and reducing localized swelling. Practitioners utilize its diverse chemical compounds to address both acute infections and chronic inflammatory responses.

What are the herbal preparations of Calotropis gigantea?

Calotropis gigantea is prepared as a juice extracted from fresh leaves, a paste made from crushed stems, or a decoction obtained by boiling the roots. The milky latex is frequently applied topically as a poultice to treat skin ailments and swellings. In traditional Ayurvedic practices, the dried leaves are often used in decoctions to address various internal inflammatory conditions. Some preparations involve smoking the dried plant material or consuming finely powdered dried roots in controlled medicinal doses. These diverse forms allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be administered for both external and internal therapeutic purposes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Calotropis gigantea can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential blindness due to the highly toxic cardiac glycosides present in its milky sap. Contact with the plant's latex often results in contact dermatitis, characterized by redness, itching, and painful blistering on the skin. If the sap accidentally touches the eyes, it can lead to intense inflammation and permanent damage to the cornea. Ingesting any part of the plant can trigger nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Furthermore, the toxic compounds can affect heart rhythm, potentially leading to serious cardiac complications if consumed in significant amounts.
