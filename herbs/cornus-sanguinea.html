Bloodtwig Dogwood (Cornus sanguinea): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Bloodtwig Dogwood (Cornus sanguinea) is a deciduous shrub native to Europe and Asia, valued in herbal medicine for its astringent properties and use in treating digestive and respiratory ailments.

What is Bloodtwig Dogwood (Cornus Sanguinea)?

Bloodtwig Dogwood, or Cornus sanguinea, is a deciduous shrub native to Europe and Asia, characterized by its red stems, small white flowers, and berries, and has been used in traditional herbal medicine for its astringent and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Bloodtwig Dogwood is Cornus sanguinea. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Saxifragales, family Cornaceae, genus Cornus, and species Cornus sanguinea. Common names for this plant include Bloodtwig Dogwood in Europe and Bloodtwig in North America. It is also known as Reddog in the UK and White Dogwood in parts of Scandinavia. Synonyms for Bloodtwig Dogwood include Cornus sanguinea subsp. sanguinea, Cornus sanguinea var. sanguinea, and Cornus sanguinea var. alba.

Plant Description and Morphology

Bloodtwig Dogwood is a deciduous shrub that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters, with a multi-stemmed structure and a dense, rounded form.

Its leaves are ovate to elliptic in shape, with a smooth margin and a pointed tip, measuring approximately 3 to 7 centimeters in length. The plant produces small, greenish-white flowers in clusters during early spring, before the leaves emerge, which are arranged in terminal and axillary panicles. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, spreading horizontally near the soil surface, which helps it thrive in moist, well-drained soils.

The bark is reddish-brown and peels in thin, papery strips, while the leaves are smooth on top and slightly hairy underneath, with a mild, somewhat woody scent.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Bloodtwig Dogwood is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, with its primary range spanning across the temperate regions of these continents.

It is commonly found in moist, well-drained soils in woodlands, streambanks, and rocky slopes, often in areas with partial to full shade. The plant thrives in cool to temperate climates, preferring moderate temperatures and consistent moisture levels throughout the growing season. It is well adapted to acidic to neutral soils and can tolerate a range of soil types as long as they remain adequately hydrated.

Ecologically, Bloodtwig Dogwood plays a key role in supporting local biodiversity by providing habitat and food for various insects and birds.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in Bloodtwig Dogwood are the roots, bark, and leaves.

The roots are commonly harvested in the spring or early summer when the plant is young. The bark is collected during the autumn when it is most easily stripped. Leaves are typically gathered in the summer when their medicinal compounds are most concentrated.

These parts are often dried and used in various preparations such as tinctures, decoctions, and poultices.

While Bloodtwig Dogwood (Cornus sanguinea) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts and for short periods, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and it should be avoided by those with known sensitivities to plants in the Cornaceae family.
