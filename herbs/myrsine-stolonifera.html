Myrsine stolonifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Myrsine stolonifera?

Myrsine stolonifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Ericales. Further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is a member of the family Primulaceae, specifically belonging to the genus Myrsine, with the specific epithet stolonifera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Myrsine stolonifera has a creeping growth habit characterized by elongated stolons that produce new plantlets at their nodes.

These runners allow the plant to spread vegetatively across the forest floor to colonize new areas. The leaves are typically simple, entire, and arranged in an opposite or sub-opposite pattern along the stems. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils, eventually developing into fleshy drupes.

The overall structure is adapted for ground cover within shaded, moist tropical environments.

        What are the herbal preparations of Myrsine stolonifera?

        Myrsine stolonifera is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste depending on the intended therapeutic application. The leaves and stems are often boiled in water to extract active compounds for internal consumption.         What side-effects this plant can have?

        Myrsine stolonifera can cause skin irritation, contact dermatitis, or localized allergic reactions when the plant comes into direct contact with human tissue. The chemical compounds present in the leaves and stems may trigger redness, itching, or a stinging sensation upon touching the foliage. Some individuals might experience swelling or small bumps if they handle the plant without protective gloves. While systemic toxicity is less documented, careful avoidance is necessary to prevent unpredictable dermatological sensitivities. Consistent exposure to the plant's juices could lead to more persistent inflammatory responses in sensitive users.
