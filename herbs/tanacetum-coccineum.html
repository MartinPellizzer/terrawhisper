Tanacetum coccineum

What's the taxonomical classification of Tanacetum coccineum?

Tanacetum coccineum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a representative of the family Asteraceae, a diverse group of flowering plants, and is specifically categorized under the genus Tanacetum. Finally, its unique biological identity is defined by its specific epithet, making its full scientific name Tanacetum coccineum.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Tanacetum coccineum contains bioactive compounds primarily located in the flowering heads and the essential oils extracted from the entire aerial portion of the plant. The dried flowers are most frequently utilized in traditional herbal preparations due to their concentrated chemical constituents. These parts are often processed into tinctures or infusions to harness their specific aromatic properties. Some applications involve using the plant's volatile oils to address various topical or systemic concerns. Researchers focus on these specific structures to identify the precise mechanisms behind the plant's biological activity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive molecules including flavonoids, terpenoids, phenolic acids, and steroids that contribute to its medicinal potential.

The presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids provides significant antioxidant activity, helping to neutralize free radicals within biological systems. Terpenoids found in the plant are often associated with antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the steroid compounds can influence various physiological processes through cellular signaling.

Together, these chemical constituents form the basis for the plant's therapeutic applications in traditional medicine.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Flavonoids
  • Terpenoids
  • Phenolic acids
  • Steroids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Tanacetum coccineum targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2 and lipoxygenase, as well as various pro-inflammatory cytokines. These bioactive compounds work by inhibiting the production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes, which are key mediators of the pain and swelling response. The plant's terpenoids and flavonoids also interact with nuclear factor kappa B to suppress systemic inflammatory signaling pathways. By modulating these specific protein interactions, the extracts exert significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects on cellular processes. These molecular mechanisms underlie the plant's traditional use in treating skin irritations and various inflammatory conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Tanacetum coccineum?

Tanacetum coccineum has antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties that are primarily driven by its high concentration of essential oils and bioactive terpenoids. These chemical constituents work together to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms and reduce swelling in localized tissues. When applied topically or used in traditional preparations, the plant helps to soothe skin irritations and alleviate minor aches. The presence of specific sesquiterpene lactones also contributes to its ability to modulate immune responses. These combined therapeutic actions make the plant a subject of interest for natural topical remedies.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat digestive issues, inflammatory conditions, and certain types of skin irritations. Its medicinal properties are often harnessed to alleviate stomach discomfort and promote better gastrointestinal function. Some traditional practices also utilize the plant to address minor external ailments through topical applications. The bioactive compounds within the leaves contribute to its reputation in folk medicine for managing various systemic imbalances. Careful application is necessary because its potency can lead to adverse reactions if not used correctly.

What are the herbal preparations of Tanacetum coccineum?

Tanacetum coccineum is prepared primarily as a dried herb for infusions, tinctures, and topical salves. The flowers and foliage are often steeped in hot water to create a tea used for its aromatic properties. Making a tincture involves soaking the plant parts in alcohol to extract specific volatile oils and bioactive compounds. For external applications, the plant material can be infused into carrier oils to produce soothing ointments for the skin. These various forms allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Tanacetum coccineum can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and gastrointestinal upset if ingested. Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or swelling upon direct contact with the plant's oils or leaves. If consumed, the plant might lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain in sensitive persons. There is also a risk of allergic reactions in people who are sensitive to the Asteraceae plant family. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should exercise extreme caution or avoid the plant entirely due to potential risks.
