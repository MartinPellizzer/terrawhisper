Breynia vitis-idaea

What's the taxonomical classification of Breynia vitis-idaea?

Breynia vitis-idaea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is situated under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a representative of the family Phyllanthaceae, falling under the genus Breynia, with its specific identification being the species vitis-idaea.

What are the therapeutic actions of Breynia vitis-idaea?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, wounds, stomach aches, and inflammation. The leaves are often prepared as a decoction or applied topically to soothe irritations and promote healing. Local traditional healers utilize different parts of the plant to address digestive distress and internal discomfort. Some communities also rely on its properties to manage minor injuries and dermatological issues. This medicinal application reflects the plant's significant role in regional ethnobotanical practices.

What are the herbal preparations of Breynia vitis-idaea?

Breynia vitis-idaea is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the specific ailment being treated. The leaves are often boiled in water to create a liquid extract used for internal consumption or washing skin irritations. For localized skin conditions, the fresh leaves may be crushed into a paste and applied directly to the affected area as a poultice. Some traditional practices also involve using the plant in steam inhalation to clear respiratory passages. These various methods allow for the extraction of bioactive compounds to address different health concerns.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Breynia vitis-idaea can cause severe skin irritation, contact dermatitis, and localized swelling if the plant's sap comes into contact with human tissue. This reaction occurs because the milky latex contains chemical compounds that act as potent irritants to the epidermis. Prolonged exposure to the sap may lead to blistering or intense redness on the affected area. If the substance accidentally enters the eyes, it can trigger painful inflammation and potential corneal damage. Caution should always be exercised when handling the plant to prevent these dermatological and ocular complications.
