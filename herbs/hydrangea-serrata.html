What to know about Hydrangea serrata (mountain hydrangea) before using it medicinally

Hydrangea serrata, commonly known as mountain hydrangea, is a versatile herb that has been revered for its medicinal properties, which include the ability to reduce inflammation and alleviate symptoms of anxiety and depression.

As a popular ornamental plant, it boasts a range of attractive features, including vibrant blue flowers and striking foliage, making it a favorite among gardeners and horticulturists. From a botanical standpoint, Hydrangea serrata is characterized by its serrated leaves and clusters of small, pink or blue flowers that are highly prized by botanists and naturalists.

With a rich history dating back to ancient China, where it was used in traditional medicine for over 2,000 years, Hydrangea serrata has also been mentioned in various historical texts, including the famous herbal text, "Ben Cao Gang Mu", which describes its medicinal properties in great detail.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Hydrangea serrata.

What are the medicinal properties of Hydrangea serrata?

Hydrangea serrata helps with treating fever, inflammation, and respiratory issues. Its medicinal properties include antipyretic, anti-inflammatory, and expectorant actions. It is also used to treat digestive problems and skin conditions. Its antibacterial and antiviral properties aid in wound healing. Traditional medicine relies on its astringent properties for treating diarrhea and urinary issues.

The active constituents of Hydrangea serrata include flavonoids, terpenoids, and phenolic acids. These compounds contribute to its medicinal properties, including anti-inflammatory and antioxidant actions. Flavonoids, such as quercetin, exhibit their antioxidant properties by reducing oxidative stress. Terpenoids, including sesquiterpenes, display antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activities.

The plant parts used for medicinal purposes are the roots and flowers. The roots are used to treat fever, rheumatism, and skin conditions. The flowers are used for respiratory issues, such as bronchitis and asthma. The flowers and leaves are also used to treat digestive problems and wounds.

Improper use of Hydrangea serrata can cause side effects, including gastrointestinal irritation, allergic reactions, and interactions with medications. The flavonoids and terpenoids in the plant may cause gastrointestinal upset when used in excessive doses. Individuals with allergies or sensitivities should use caution when handling the plant.

When using Hydrangea serrata medicinally, take precautions to avoid interactions with other medications, such as blood thinners, and consult with a healthcare professional regarding dosage and administration. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult with a healthcare professional before using the plant medicinally. Individuals with kidney or liver disease should use caution when using the plant due to its potential impact on these organs.

What are the horticulural aspects of Hydrangea serrata?

Hydrangea serrata grow well in partial shade and moist, well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. It can tolerate a wide range of soil types, from acidic to alkaline. Mountain hydrangea requires regular watering, but it can be drought-tolerant once established.

For optimal growth, plant Hydrangea serrata in a location with consistent moisture and moderate temperatures between 65°F to 75°F (18°C to 24°C). Avoid planting in areas with standing water or where water may collect. Plant in well-draining soil with organic matter to promote healthy root development.

To harvest Hydrangea serrata blooms, cut stems in late summer or early fall when the flowers are still in bud. Remove the seed heads and flowers after blooming to promote a second flowering period in the fall. Prune back the stems to about 6 inches from the ground to encourage new growth and blooming in the following year.

Hydrangea serrata is susceptible to various pests and diseases, including root rot caused by overwatering, leaf spot caused by fungi, and aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites. Regularly inspect the plant for signs of infestation or infection and treat promptly to prevent further damage and reduce the risk of spreading to other plants.
