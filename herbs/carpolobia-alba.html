Carpolobia alba

What's the taxonomical classification of Carpolobia alba?

Carpolobia alba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized under the order Fabales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Polygalaceae, which contains the genus Carpolobia, ultimately defining the specific species as Carpolobia alba.

What are the therapeutic actions of Carpolobia alba?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat stomach aches, intestinal worms, and various types of diarrhea. The extracts derived from the plant possess medicinal properties that target digestive distress. Local practitioners often prepare decoctions to expel parasites from the gastrointestinal tract. These traditional remedies focus on soothing inflammation within the gut. Specific parts of the plant are selected based on the severity of the ailment.

What are the herbal preparations of Carpolobia alba?

Carpolobia alba is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical tincture derived from its dried leaves and bark. Traditional practitioners often simmer the plant material in water to extract its active chemical constituents for oral consumption. Some regional methods involve steeping the dried parts in high-proof alcohol to create a concentrated tincture for longer shelf life. These liquid preparations are frequently used to treat inflammatory conditions through ingestion or skin application. The specific form chosen typically depends on whether the intended effect is systemic or localized to a particular area of the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Carpolobia alba can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including vomiting, abdominal cramping, and diarrhea if ingested. These symptoms typically arise from the plant's toxic chemical constituents which irritate the digestive tract lining. Prolonged exposure or high doses may lead to more systemic issues such as dehydration or electrolyte imbalances. Some individuals might also experience localized skin irritation or allergic reactions if the plant comes into direct contact with sensitive tissue. Because the specific toxicity levels are not fully documented in all clinical studies, caution is necessary when handling any part of the specimen.
