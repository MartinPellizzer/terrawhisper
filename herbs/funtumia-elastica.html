Funtumia elastica

What's the taxonomical classification of Funtumia elastica?

Funtumia elastica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Gentianales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Apocynaceae, falling under the genus Funtumia, and is specifically identified by its species name, elastica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Funtumia elastica has an evergreen tree habit characterized by a straight trunk, a spreading crown, and dark green, glossy, oblong-shaped leaves that are arranged oppositely on the branches. The foliage typically measures between 10 and 20 centimeters in length and possesses a leathery texture with prominent pinnate venation. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters to attract pollinators. These flowers eventually give rise to small, globose fruits that transition from green to red or orange as they mature. The plant is also noted for its milky white latex, which flows from the bark and leaves when the tissues are wounded.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of West and Central Africa, ranging from Guinea and Sierra Leone in the west to the Democratic Republic of the Congo in the east. It thrives primarily within the humid, lowland evergreen forests of the Guineo-Congolian phytogeographical region. The species is most commonly found in areas characterized by high rainfall and consistent temperatures typical of equatorial climates. Within these habitats, it often occupies the subcanopy or understory layers of dense jungle environments. Its distribution follows the specific moisture and soil requirements found across the African tropical belt.

What are the pharmacological activities of Funtumia elastica?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antifungal, antileishmanial, anti-malarial, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities. These therapeutic properties are primarily attributed to the presence of various bioactive secondary metabolites found within its roots and leaves. Researchers have demonstrated that specific extracts can effectively inhibit the growth of diverse pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, the plant shows significant potential in combating parasitic infections like malaria and leishmaniasis. Such biological actions suggest that Funtumia elastica could serve as a valuable source for developing new medicinal compounds.

As discussed in a study, Funtumia elastica has a long ethnopharmacological history for treating asthma, whooping cough, and fungal infections. Ethanol leaf extracts showed MIC ranges from 125 to 1550 µg/mL, while ethanol bark extracts ranged from 125 to 1750 µg/mL against several test organisms at 10, 25, and 50 mg/mL concentrations. The plant demonstrated significant anti-inflammatory activity (p ≤ 0.001) at doses of 30, 100, and 300 mg/kg per body weight in 7-day-old chicks. Tannin contents of the leaf and stem bark were measured at 2.4% w/w and 1.3% w/w, respectively. These antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties may account for the ethnomedicinal uses of the leaves and stem bark.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains alkaloids, flavonoids, hydrolysable tannins, saponins, and tannins that contribute to its diverse pharmacological profile. The alkaloids serve as potent bioactive agents that often interact with the nervous system to produce therapeutic effects. Flavonoids provide essential antioxidant properties by neutralizing free radicals within biological tissues. Both saponins and tannins play significant roles in antimicrobial defense and astringent actions. Additionally, the presence of hydrolysable tannins offers unique chemical stability for various medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, Funtumia elastica contains various secondary metabolites such as hydrolysable tannins, sapogenetic glycosides, steroids, and saponins in the bark, while the leaves contain hydrolysable tannins, flavonoids, starch, and alkaloids. The tannin contents of the leaf and stem bark are 2.4% w/w and 1.3% w/w relative to the dried material, respectively. Antimicrobial testing using 10, 25, and 50 mg/mL concentrations showed MIC ranges of 125 to 1550 µg/mL for leaf extracts and 125 to 1750 µg/mL for bark extracts. Anti-inflammatory activity was observed at doses of 30, 100, and 300 mg/kg per body weight in 7-day-old chicks. These activities were measured at hourly intervals for 5 h and showed significance at p ≤ 0.001.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating malaria, fungal infections, asthma, painful menstruation, and wounds. The bark and leaves contain bioactive compounds that help reduce the symptoms of respiratory distress associated with asthma. Extracts from different parts of the plant are applied topically to accelerate the healing of wounds and clear up fungal infections. It is also traditionally administered to alleviate the discomfort of painful menstruation in women. Furthermore, its antimalarial properties make it a significant resource in managing malaria symptoms.
