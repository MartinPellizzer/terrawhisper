Malvastrum coromandelianum

What's the taxonomical classification of Malvastrum coromandelianum?

Malvastrum coroandelianum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is placed in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Malvales. It is a member of the family Malvaceae, which is further categorized into the genus Malvastrum, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species coromandelianum.

What are the therapeutic actions of Malvastrum coromandelianum?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat inflammatory conditions, skin infections, digestive ailments, and respiratory issues. The leaves and stems are often applied topically as a poultice to soothe skin irritations and heal minor wounds. When prepared as a decoction or tea, it serves as a remedy for internal discomforts like stomach aches and indigestion. Its mucilaginous properties also make it useful in calming coughs and easing sore throats. Local traditional medicine relies on these various applications to manage a range of common physical ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Malvastrum coromandelianum?

Malvastrum coromandelianum is prepared primarily as an infusion, decoction, or topical poultice to utilize its mucilaginous and astringent properties. The leaves and flowers are often steeped in hot water to create a soothing tea used for digestive or respiratory relief. For skin irritations or inflammation, the plant material can be crushed into a moist paste and applied directly to the affected area. Decoctions made from the tougher parts of the plant help extract more concentrated tannins for more potent medicinal effects. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the herbal delivery to specific therapeutic needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Malvastrum coromandelianum can cause allergic reactions such as skin irritation, itching, or redness if direct contact occurs with the plant material. While specific toxicological studies on humans are limited, many plants in the Malvaceae family may trigger hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. Contact dermatitis is a primary concern for those handling the foliage or stems without protection. It is advisable to monitor for any swelling or localized rashes following exposure to the plant. Consult a medical professional immediately if any severe inflammatory symptoms develop.
