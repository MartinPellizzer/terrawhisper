Dialium guineense

What's the taxonomical classification of Dialium guineense?

Dialium guineense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Fabales and is a member of the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Dialium, and its specific species epithet, guineense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dialium guineense has a large woody structure characterized by a trunk that can reach up to thirty meters in height with bark that is typically greyish-brown and slightly fissured. The leaves are pinnate, arranged alternately along the branches, and consist of several pairs of elliptical leaflets with smooth margins. Its flowers are small and yellowish, often appearing in clusters from the axils of the leaves or on older wood. The plant produces distinctive woody pods that are hard, black, and contain a sweet, brownish pulp surrounding the seeds. These pods are highly durable and serve as a primary morphological feature for identifying the species in tropical forest environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforests and moist woodlands of West and Central Africa, extending from Senegal and Guinea through Nigeria to the Congo Basin. It is primarily found in the Guineo-Congolian regional center of endemism where humidity levels remain consistently high. Within these habitats, it thrives in secondary forests and gallery forests along riverbanks. Its distribution follows the belt of lowland vegetation across the equatorial African zone. Local populations are often concentrated in areas with well-drained, fertile soils characteristic of the tropical forest biome.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dialium guineense?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-ulcerr, anticancer, antioxidant, and bilharzia-preventing molluscicidal properties. Its antioxidant components help neutralize harmful free radicals within the body to prevent cellular damage. The plant also exhibits significant analgesic effects by reducing pain and demonstrates anti-ulcerr capabilities by protecting the gastric mucosa. Research indicates that its anticancer potential can inhibit the growth of certain malignant cells. Additionally, it serves a molluscicidal role that helps prevent bilharzia by controlling the snail populations that host the parasite.

According to a study published by "Annals of medical and health sciences research", Dialium guineense is used for the treatment of fever and headache. The methanolic stem bark extract was administered at three test doses of 250, 500, and 1000 mg/kg body weight. In the acetic acid-induced writhing reflex model, the extract significantly decreased abdominal constriction with a P value between 0.014 and 0.002. The percentage inhibition of the abdominal constriction reflex increased dose dependently from 0% in the negative control to 71% at the 1000 mg/kg dose. In the tail immersion model, the 1000 mg/kg dose significantly increased the pain reaction time with a P value of 0.048. The extract also significantly increased the mean pain reaction time at doses of 500 and 1000 mg/kg in the hot plate model with P values of 0.048 to 0.05, whereas the 250 mg/kg dose showed no analgesic activity in these two models.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, phenolics, proanthocyanidins, and saponins that contribute to its diverse therapeutic properties. These bioactive secondary metabolites work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects within the body. The presence of specific phenolic compounds helps in neutralizing free radicals to prevent cellular damage. Additionally, the proanthocyanidins and flavonoids are known for their ability to support cardiovascular health and immune functions. Saponins further enhance the medicinal profile by offering potential antimicrobial and cholesterol-lowering benefits.

According to a study published by "BMC complementary medicine and therapies", the ethanol extracts of Dialium guineense root (DGR) contain considerable amounts of phenolics, flavonoids, and proanthocyanidins. These extracts produced significant (p < 0.05) growth inhibitory effects against MCF-7, HUH-7, and HeLa cell lines in a dose-dependent manner. Specifically, the DGR extract exhibited 7-fold selectivity toward MCF-7 cancer cells relative to normal lung fibroblast (MRC-5) cells. This extract also significantly inhibited cell migration and colony formation in MCF-7-treated cells. These findings suggest that DGR may be developed as a potential chemotherapeutic agent.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating gastric disorders, gastric mucosal injury, peptic ulcers, and various forms of abdominal pain. The medicinal properties of Dialium guineense help in soothing the digestive tract and protecting the stomach lining from irritation. It is frequently employed to alleviate the intense discomfort associated with an active ulcer. By addressing the root of gastric mucosal injury, the plant aids in the healing process of the internal tissue. These therapeutic applications make it a vital resource for managing persistent digestive pain.

According to a study published by "Journal of applied biomedicine", Dialium guineense pulp has been investigated for its anti-ulcer activities and its potential for the curing of gastric disorders. The study examined the pulp's effects on gastric mucosal injury induced by 400 mg/kg bw oral aspirin administration in albino Wistar rats. Extracts were orally administered at doses of 250, 500 and 1000 mg/kg bw per day for 3 or 7 days. Results indicated that the plant caused a significant decrease at P ≤ 0.05 in the ulcer index, with the 1000 mg/kg bw per day dose causing the highest decrease over 7 days. The administration of the pulp increased antioxidant enzyme activities and decreased injury in the gastric mucosa.
