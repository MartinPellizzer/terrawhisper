Bougainvillea glabra

What's the taxonomical classification of Bougainvillea glabra?

Bougainvillea glabra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Caryophyllales and is a member of the family Nyctaginaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Bougainvillea and is specifically designated as the species glabra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bougainvillea glabra has woody, perennial stems covered in sharp, curved thorns and small, ovate, bright green leaves that lack hair. The plant is a climbing woody vine that uses its prickles to scramble over structures or through surrounding vegetation. Its most striking feature consists of colorful, papery bracts that surround tiny, inconspicuous white flowers. These bracts can appear in shades of pink, purple, or orange and serve to attract pollinators to the plant. The foliage remains relatively small compared to the expansive reach of its sprawling branches.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South America, specifically spanning across Brazil, Peru, and Argentina. It thrives in warm climates where frost is rare and sunlight is abundant. While its origins are centered in the Americas, it has been naturalized in many other tropical zones worldwide. You can frequently find it growing in gardens and landscapes throughout Florida, California, and the Mediterranean. Its ability to tolerate various soil types allows it to spread across diverse coastal and inland environments globally.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Bougainvillea glabra contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its flowers, leaves, and stems. The bracts and floral parts are often harvested to prepare decoctions used for treating respiratory ailments and inflammation. Extracts from the leaves are frequently applied topically to soothe skin irritations or swallowed as teas to manage menstrual irregularities. Some traditional practices also involve using the stem to address digestive issues and fever. These various plant parts work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic properties in folk medicine.

What are the pharmacological activities of Bougainvillea glabra?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, immunomodulatory, and acetylcholinesterase inhibitory activities. Its diverse chemical constituents allow it to combat oxidative stress and reduce harmful inflammation within biological systems. The presence of specific phytochemicals also provides the ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, the plant can modulate immune responses to improve bodily defenses against disease. Finally, its capacity to inhibit acetylcholinesterase makes it a subject of interest for potential treatments regarding cognitive functions.

As discussed in a study, Bougainvillea glabra was examined for its immunomodulatory and antioxidant activities using RAW 264.7 cells. The extract of Bougainvillea glabra contributed to the enhancement of macrophages phagocytic function. Furthermore, the generation of NO from activated macrophages was substantially suppressed by this extract. The study also demonstrated that Bougainvillea glabra exhibited some antioxidant activities. These results suggest that the extract may have considerable anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds catechin, chlorogenic acid, gallic acid, rutin, and tri-O-caffeoyl-shi kimic acid. These bioactive molecules contribute to the complex chemical profile that defines the plant's therapeutic potential. Chlorogenic acid and gallic acid are well-known for their significant antioxidant properties. Additionally, the presence of rutin helps support vascular health through its flavonoid structure. The specific combination of these polyphenols, including tri-O-caffeoyl-shi kimic acid and catechins, works synergistically to provide various biological benefits.

As discussed in a study, HPLC-PDA quantification of Bougainvillea glabra revealed the identification of nine different polyphenolics. The flower MeOH extract contained the highest amounts of catechin at 6.31 μg/g, gallic acid at 2.39 μg/g, and rutin at 1.26 μg/g. UHPLC-MS analysis of DCM extracts of the plant revealed the tentative identification of 27 secondary metabolites, primarily consisting of terpenoid, alkaloid, and phenolic derivatives. All extracts presented moderate activity against tyrosinase and α-amylases. For LOX inhibition, both methanolic extracts showed higher percentage inhibition compared with DCM extracts.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation upon contact and gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain if ingested. The thorns found on the stems often lead to physical puncture wounds and localized swelling or redness. When the plant is consumed, the toxins within it can trigger severe stomach upset and digestive complications. People with sensitive skin should exercise caution to avoid inflammatory reactions. These symptoms typically arise from direct physical interaction or accidental ingestion of the foliage.
