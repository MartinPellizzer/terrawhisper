Bougainvillea glabra

What's the taxonomical classification of Bougainvillea glabra?

Bougainvillea glabra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Caryophyllales and is a member of the family Nyctaginaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Bougainvillea and is specifically designated as the species glabra.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Bougainvillea glabra contains betacyanins, flavonoids, saponins, and tannins which serve as its primary bioactive medicinal compounds. These chemical constituents provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties when extracted for therapeutic use. The presence of saponins can contribute to antimicrobial activities, while flavonoids help in neutralizing free radicals within biological systems. Research into these phytochemicals suggests potential applications in managing oxidative stress and supporting cellular health. Traditional medicinal practices sometimes utilize these extracts to address various skin ailments and inflammatory conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Bougainvillea glabra?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat respiratory ailments such as coughs, asthma, and bronchitis, as well as digestive issues and skin infections. In various traditional medicine practices, the flowers and leaves are often prepared as decoctions or poultices to soothe internal inflammation. Some cultures utilize the plant to manage symptoms of fever and various types of indigestion. Additionally, its antimicrobial properties are sometimes harnessed to address minor dermatological conditions. These applications rely on the plant's complex chemical profile to provide therapeutic relief.

What are the herbal preparations of Bougainvillea glabra?

Bougainvillea glabra is prepared primarily as a decoction from the dried stems, an infusion made from the leaves, or a topical poultice using crushed fresh foliage. The stem decoction is often boiled in water to extract bitter compounds for internal use in traditional medicine. Leaf infusions serve as a milder alternative for addressing minor inflammatory conditions. For external applications, the succulent leaves are mashed into a paste and applied directly to the skin to soothe irritations. These various forms allow practitioners to tailor the concentration of active constituents to specific therapeutic needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Bougainvillea glabra can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and physical injury from its sharp thorns. When the plant's sap touches the skin, it may trigger redness, itching, or a stinging sensation in sensitive individuals. The prominent thorns present a significant risk of puncture wounds or scratches if someone brushes against the foliage. Some people may also experience an allergic reaction if they inhale pollen or touch the colorful bracts. Maintaining distance from the plant is recommended to avoid these localized dermatological and physical complications.
