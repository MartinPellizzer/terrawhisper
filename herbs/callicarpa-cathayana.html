Callicarpa cathayana

What's the taxonomical classification of Callicarpa cathayana?

Callicarpa cathayana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is categorized under the order Lamiales. Furthermore, the plant is a member of the family Lamiaceae, situated within the genus Callicarpa, and is specifically identified by the species name cathayana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Callicarpa cathayana has opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and a woody, deciduous stem structure.

The foliage typically displays a bright green color and can reach several inches in length depending on the specimen's maturity. Small, tubular flowers emerge in dense cymes from the leaf axils during the blooming season. These blossoms eventually give way to clusters of small, spherical drupes that turn a vibrant purple or magenta when ripe.

The overall architecture of the shrub is characterized by an upright or spreading growth habit.

    What are the herbal preparations of Callicarpa cathayana?

    Callicarpa cathayana is prepared as a decoction, a topical paste, or an infused liquid depending on the intended therapeutic application. The dried leaves and stems are most commonly boiled in water to create a medicinal tea for internal use. For skin conditions, the plant material is often ground into a fine powder and mixed with water or oil to form a soothing external paste. Some traditional practitioners also utilize alcohol-based tinctures to extract the active compounds for long-term storage. These diverse preparation methods allow for the versatile administration of the plant's phytochemical properties.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Callicarpa cathayana can cause localized skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested in significant amounts. While many species in this genus are used traditionally, direct contact with the leaves or stems may trigger redness and itching in sensitive individuals. Consumption of unverified quantities might lead to nausea or stomach discomfort due to various secondary metabolites present in the plant. Detailed toxicological studies on this specific species are limited, so caution is necessary when handling it. Always consult a medical professional if an adverse reaction occurs after exposure to the plant.
