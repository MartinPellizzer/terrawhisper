Botanical Identification of Bitterleaf (Vernonia amygdalina)

To identify Bitterleaf (Vernonia amygdalina) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Bitterleaf, or Vernonia amygdalina, belongs to the kingdom Plantae. Its family is Compositae, also known as Asteraceae. The genus is Vernonia, and the species is Vernonia amygdalina. The other taxonomic levels—division, class, and order—are not specified in the provided data.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Vernonia amygdalina is the scientific name for Bitterleaf. It was first described by the botanist Linnaeus in 1771. The plant is also known by several common names, including bitterleaf, African bitterleaf, and wild lettuce, and is widely recognized across various regions in Africa for its medicinal properties.

Morphological Characteristics

Bitterleaf, or Vernonia amygdalina, is a shrub or small tree with a straight, branching stem. Its roots are fibrous and deep, providing stability. Leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate, with a rough texture and prominent veins. The inflorescence is a dense spike of small yellow flowers, arranged in clusters. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Vernonia amygdalina can be distinguished by its lanceolate leaves with serrated edges, yellow-green flowers in conical clusters, and the presence of a bitter taste. It is often confused with other Vernonia species, but its unique leaf shape and distinctive floral arrangement help in accurate identification. The plant also has a strong, bitter smell when crushed, which is a key identifying feature.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Bitterleaf (Vernonia amygdalina) is native to tropical Africa, particularly West and Central regions. It thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soils. It is commonly found at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Bitterleaf (Vernonia amygdalina) blooms from April to September, with seed formation occurring from June to October. Harvesting is typically done during the dry season, from November to March, when leaves are most abundant and potent for medicinal use.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Vernonia amygdalina belongs to the genus Vernonia in the family Asteraceae. Its closest relatives include other Vernonia species such as Vernonia galamensis and Vernonia hirsuta. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, reflecting their close phylogenetic relationship within the genus.
