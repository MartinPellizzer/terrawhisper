Oenothera rosea

What's the taxonomical classification of Oenothera rosea?

Oenothera rosea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Myrtoales and is a member of the family Onagraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Oenothera and the specific epithet rosea, forming its unique scientific name.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oenothera rosea has an erect, herbaceous habit characterized by slender, branched stems that reach heights of approximately 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are primarily basal or cauline, appearing lanceolate to linear in shape with entire margins. Its most distinctive feature is the large, four-petaled flower that displays shades of pink to rose-colored petals surrounding a central cluster of numerous yellow stamens. The plant also produces elongated, narrow seed capsules that develop from the base of the ovary after pollination. These capsules eventually dehisce to release small, dark seeds that facilitate the next generation of growth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Oenothera rosea?

This plant has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, anticancer, antiproliferative, and protective effects against gastric cancer. These biological activities are driven by the presence of diverse bioactive compounds such as phenolic acids and flavonoids. The antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, which subsequently supports the plant's anti-inflammatory mechanisms. Furthermore, its antiproliferative nature plays a critical role in inhibiting the uncontrolled growth of malignant cells. Ultimately, these combined chemical attributes contribute to its significant potential in providing a protective effect against gastric cancer.

According to a study published by "Biomedicine & pharmacotherapy = Biomedecine & pharmacotherapie", the extracts of Oenothera rosea exhibit antiproliferative effects on the DU145 human prostate cancer cell line at 72 h. The ethanolic extract showed the best moderate activity at either 48 or 72 h. Through LC-MS/MS-based metabolomics, 307 compounds were identified from the aqueous, methanolic, ethanolic, and ethyl acetate extracts. From these, 40 putative metabolites were organized as having anti-inflammatory, anticancer, and/or antiproliferative activities. These findings suggest that O. rosea could function as an antiproliferative agent using its chemical contents as polypharmacy with low concentration levels.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating edema, gastric cancer, and prostate cancer. Researchers have studied its chemical properties to determine how its extracts might inhibit tumor growth in specific digestive and reproductive cells. The plant also contains bioactive compounds that may assist in reducing swelling and fluid retention associated with edema. Ongoing scientific investigations aim to further clarify the exact mechanisms by which these botanical elements interact with various pathological states.

As discussed in a study, Oenothera rosea is being evaluated for its potential protective effect against N-methyl-N-nitrosourea (NMU)-induced gastric cancer. In experiments involving 80 male Holtzman rats, the plant extract was administered at doses of 100, 200, and 300 mg/kg to evaluate its preventive capabilities over 16 weeks. Results showed that these doses led to fewer histopathological changes, such as anaplastic and hyperplastic cells, compared to the group receiving 500 μg/kg of the cancer inductor. Furthermore, the administration of the extract resulted in statistically lower levels of superoxide dismutase, malondialdehyde, and nitric oxide (P<0.05, P<0.01, and P<0.01). Ultimately, the study concluded that Oenothera rosea has a protective effect on experimentally induced gastric cancer.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive distress if ingested or touched directly. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many members of the evening primrose family can trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Contact with the sap or foliage may result in redness, itching, or localized swelling on the skin. If consumed accidentally, some people might experience nausea, stomach cramps, or more significant gastrointestinal upset. It is generally recommended to avoid direct skin contact and prevent ingestion to minimize these potential adverse effects.
