Geophila repens

What's the taxonomical classification of Geophila repens?

Geophila repens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression through the order Gentianales, the plant is placed within the family Rubiaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Geophila, and its species designation, repens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Geophila repens has a slender, creeping rhizome system that produces upright, erect stems with small, opposite, elliptical leaves and minute, inconspicuous flowers. The foliage is characterized by thin, somewhat fleshy textures and a deep green coloration that helps the plant maximize light absorption in shaded environments. Its stems are relatively weak and often rely on surrounding vegetation for structural support as they spread across the forest floor. The reproductive structures are tiny and often tucked within the leaf axils, making them difficult to observe without magnification. This specialized morphology allows the species to effectively colonize moist, low-light niches within its native habitat.

What are the pharmacological activities of Geophila repens?

This plant has anticholinesterase, anti-neuroinflammatory, neuroprotective, memory enhancing, and antioxidant pharmacological activities. Its ability to inhibit acetylcholinesterase helps maintain high levels of acetylcholine in the brain, which directly supports its memory enhancing properties. By suppressing inflammatory cytokines, the plant exerts a potent anti-neuroinflammatory effect that protects nerve cells from damage. The antioxidant properties further mitigate oxidative stress, providing a robust neuroprotective shield for the central nervous system. Together, these combined actions make the species a significant subject for studying neurodegenerative disease treatments.

As discussed in a study, Geophila repens is claimed to have memory-enhancing property and was assessed for its antioxidant and anticholinesterase activity. The ethyl acetate extract (EGR) exhibited the highest antioxidant activity with IC50 values of (38.33 ± 3.21), (45.14 ± 1.78), (59.81 ± 1.32), (39.45 ± 0.79), and (43.76 ± 0.81) μg/mL in DPPH, SOD, NO, OH, and TAC assays respectively. This same EGR fraction displayed competitive, reversible inhibition of AChE and BChE activities with IC50 values of (68.63 ± 0.45) and (59.45 ± 0.45) μg/mL. Additionally, the total phenolic and flavonoids contents of EGR were found to be 360.42 mg gallic acid equivalents and 257.31 mg quercetin equivalents per gram of extract. These results suggest that phenols and flavonoids could be responsible for the antioxidant and anticholinesterase activities of G. repens.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Alzheimer's disease, memory impairment, memory loss, neuroinflammation, and neurotoxicity. Researchers study its bioactive compounds to determine how they can protect the brain from progressive cognitive decline. The plant shows potential in reducing the chemical processes that cause neuroinflammation within neural tissues. It also demonstrates properties that may mitigate neurotoxicity caused by environmental factors or metabolic issues. By targeting these specific mechanisms, it may offer a way to combat the physical symptoms of memory loss.

As discussed in a study, the bioactive hydroalcohol extract of leaves of G. repens (GRHA) was investigated for mitigating scopolamine-induced neurotoxicity in experimental rats of Alzheimer's disease. Experimental rats were treated with 300 or 400 mg/kg of GRHA for 14 days following the administration of 3 mg/kg of scopolamine. GC-MS/MS analysis reported 31 compounds in the plant, including 8 bioactive compounds that possess antioxidant, neuroinflammation, and neuroprotective activities. GRHA 400 significantly improved learning and memory impairment, including oxidative stress markers, DNA damage, neurochemical markers, and neuroinflammation markers. The synergistic action of compounds such as genistin, quercetin-3-D-galactoside, 9,12,15-octadecatrienoic-acid methyl-ester, phytol, retinal, stigmasterol, n-hexadecanoic acid, and β-sitosterol in GRHA restores memory-deficits.
