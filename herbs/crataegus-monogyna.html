Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is a deciduous shrub or small tree native to Europe and western Asia, widely used in herbal medicine for its cardiovascular benefits and calming effects on the nervous system.

What is Common Hawthorn (Crataegus Monogyna)?

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is a small shrub native to Europe, widely used in herbal medicine for its cardiovascular and digestive benefits, containing compounds like flavonoids and oligomeric proanthocyanidins.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Common Hawthorn is Crataegus monogyna. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rosales, family Rosaceae, genus Crataegus, and species Crataegus monogyna. It is known as Common Hawthorn in Europe and Britain, and as Thornapple in parts of North America. The synonyms for this plant include botanical names such as Crataegus monogyna subsp. monogyna and regional names like Mayblossom and White Thorn.

Plant Description and Morphology

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is a deciduous shrub or small tree, typically growing to a height of 3-8 meters, with thorny branches, oval to lance-shaped leaves, and clusters of small, white or pinkish flowers that appear in spring, followed by red, berry-like fruits in autumn.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is native to southern and central Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, typically growing in woodlands, hedgerows, and rocky soils.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The dried flowers, berries, and leaves of Common Hawthorn are primarily used medicinally in herbal medicine.

Phytochemical Composition

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including flavonoids such as hyperoside, rutin, and quercetin, as well as essential oils, tannins, and polyphenols, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) exhibits pharmacological properties primarily attributed to its high content of flavonoids, oligomeric proanthocyanidins, and cardioactive glycosides, which contribute to its cardiovascular, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects.

Mechanisms of Action

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its cardioactive properties, including the modulation of cardiac rhythm, enhancement of coronary blood flow, and reduction of myocardial oxygen demand, which are mediated by its flavonoid and polyphenolic compounds.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support cardiovascular health, alleviate symptoms of mild depression, and promote digestive wellness through its mild sedative and tonic properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or decoction, with the fruit being the primary part used, often combined with other herbs in formulations for cardiovascular and digestive support.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) for herbal use ranges from 200 to 1200 mg of standardized extract per day, divided into two to three doses, with the exact amount depending on the concentration of active compounds and the specific condition being treated.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses for short-term periods, but it may cause mild side effects such as digestive discomfort, dizziness, or headaches, and should be avoided by individuals with certain heart conditions, those taking specific medications, and during pregnancy or breastfeeding.

Drug Interactions

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) may interact with medications such as beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, and antiarrhythmics, potentially increasing the risk of bradycardia or cardiac arrhythmias due to its effects on heart rate and rhythm.

Toxicity and Precautions

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort and should be avoided during pregnancy and by individuals with known allergies to plants in the Rosaceae family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) is typically cultivated in temperate climates, preferring well-drained soil and full sun, with harvesting of its berries occurring in late summer to early autumn when they turn from green to red or orange.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) typically involve harvesting the dried flowers, berries, or leaves, followed by methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain active compounds like flavonoids and oligomeric procyanidins.

Storage and Shelf Life

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its dried parts should be kept in a sealed container to maintain potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when stored properly.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence on Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) primarily focus on its traditional use in herbal medicine for cardiovascular support, with studies investigating its potential effects on heart function, blood pressure, and symptoms of mild to moderate heart failure.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for centuries to support cardiovascular health, alleviate symptoms of anxiety and insomnia, and treat digestive issues such as indigestion and bloating.

Regulatory Status
