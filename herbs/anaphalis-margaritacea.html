Anaphalis margaritacea

What's the taxonomical classification of Anaphalis margaritacea?

Anaphalis margaritacea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the species is placed in the order Asterales and the family Asteraceae. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by its membership in the genus Anaphalis, with the species designation being margaritacea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anaphalis margaritacea has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by erect stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 40 centimeters and feature simple or branched structures. The leaves are primarily basal or cauline, appearing lanceolate to oblong in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. Its inflorescence consists of small, discoid flower heads that lack ray florets, presenting a dense, white, or yellowish appearance. The involucral bracts are arranged in multiple rows, providing a protective layer around the developing florets. These morphological traits collectively define its distinct identity within the Asteraceae family.

These populations are often found in rocky outcrops or open grasslands where the climate is cool and temperate. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and localized swelling when its sap or crushed leaves come into direct contact with human skin. Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or a burning sensation at the site of application. If the plant is ingested, it may lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or stomach pain. While extensive clinical studies are limited, caution is advised due to its potential for hypersensitivity reactions in sensitive people.
