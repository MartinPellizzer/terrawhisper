Seriphidium thomsonianum has woody, perennial stems that branch extensively and bear pinnately compound leaves with multiple pairs of leaflets. Small, inconspicuous flowers are arranged in dense clusters or panicles at the ends of the branches. The plant also produces small, dry fruits known as legumes that contain several seeds.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Seriphidium thomsonianum can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled improperly. Contact with the plant's sap or foliage may lead to contact dermatitis, manifesting as redness, itching, or localized swelling. Some individuals may experience systemic allergic responses if the plant comes into contact with sensitive mucous membranes. If consumed, the chemical compounds within the plant can trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. It is essential to exercise caution and avoid direct skin contact to prevent these adverse reactions.
