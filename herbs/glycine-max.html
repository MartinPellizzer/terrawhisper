What to know about Glycine max (soybean) before using it medicinally

Glycine max, commonly known as soybean, is a herbaceous legume that has been used for centuries to improve heart health, reduce inflammation, and support bone health due to its rich content of isoflavones and protein.

Cultivated worldwide, it is a hardy crop that grows up to 3 meters in height, requiring a warm and humid climate to thrive. The botanical properties of Glycine max reveal that it belongs to the Fabaceae family and is a member of the genus Glycine, characterized by its compound leaves and white or purple flowers.

Ancient Chinese and Japanese civilizations have valued Glycine max for its medicinal properties, with records of its use dating back to the 17th century as a treatment for various ailments, including fever and indigestion.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Glycine max.

What are the medicinal properties of Glycine max?

Glycine max helps with cardiovascular health by reducing triglycerides, lowering LDL cholesterol, and improving HDL cholesterol levels. It also has antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, which may help protect against cancer and neurodegenerative diseases. Additionally, it has been shown to improve symptoms of menopause and osteoporosis.

The active constituents of Glycine max include isoflavones (genistein and daidzein), saponins (glycosides), and polyunsaturated fatty acids, which are responsible for its medicinal properties. These compounds have been shown to have estrogenic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects. They also have a potential role in reducing the risk of certain cancers and cardiovascular diseases.

The most commonly used parts of the plant for medicinal purposes are the seeds, leaves, and root. The seeds contain the highest concentration of isoflavones and saponins, which are believed to have the most medicinal benefits. The leaves and root are also rich in antioxidants and have anti-inflammatory properties.

Improper use of Glycine max may lead to side effects such as estrogen dominance, allergic reactions, and interference with blood clotting. Excessive intake of isoflavones may also cause gastrointestinal symptoms and interact with certain medications. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should use it with caution.

When using Glycine max medicinally, precautions should be taken to minimize potential side effects. These include starting with low doses, monitoring for signs of estrogen dominance, and avoiding use in individuals with thyroid disorders or bleeding disorders. Additionally, pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult a healthcare professional before using it.

What are the horticulural aspects of Glycine max?

Glycine max grow well in full sun to partial shade, requiring a temperature range of 65-95°F (18-35°C). Soybeans are sensitive to extreme temperatures, drought, and flooding, and require well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0.

For optimal growth, plant soybeans in rows 20-30 inches apart, with seeds 1-2 inches deep and 6-8 inches apart. Soil should be tilled to a depth of 8-10 inches to loosen and aerate the soil. Soybeans require 60-90 days of warm weather to mature, and are typically planted in late spring or early summer.

Soybeans are ready to harvest when the pods are dry and the leaves are yellowing. Cut the plant at the base, leaving 2-3 inches of stem attached. Dry the soybeans further by tying the plants in bundles or by spreading them on a dry surface. Harvest within a few days to prevent spoilage.

Soybeans are susceptible to several pests and diseases, including aphids, whiteflies, spider mites, powdery mildew, and root rot. Aphids and whiteflies can be controlled with insecticidal soap or neem oil, while powdery mildew can be treated with fungicides. Regular watering and crop rotation can help prevent root rot.

What are the botanical characteristics of Glycine max?

Glycine max, also known as soybean, is an annual legume that exhibits several distinct botanical characteristics.

It has a hairy stem (pubescent) and grows to be 0.5-2 meters tall, with trifoliate leaves arranged oppositely on the stem. Each leaflet is ovate in shape, measuring up to 10 cm long, and has a pointed tip and a smooth edge. The flowers are small, white, and clustered, typically occurring in axillary racemes.

Soybean plants are self-pollinating, with anthers that dehisce at maturity, releasing pollen onto the stigma. The pods, which are the reproductive structures of the plant, are flat, linear, and dehiscing along two sutures to release one or two seeds (beans) per pod. Each seed is approximately 4-10 mm long, elliptical in shape, and has a hard, brown coat that protects the edible cotyledon within.

Overall, Glycine max displays typical characteristics of legumes, with its ability to fix atmospheric nitrogen through symbiotic relationships with rhizobia, making it an important crop for human consumption and animal feed production.

What is the taxonomy of Glycine max?

Glycine max belongs to the kingdom Plantae, phylum Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Fabales, family Fabaceae (Leguminosae), and genus Glycine.

Specifically, it is classified as a species within the section Sojae of the genus Glycine, which consists of several species of soybeans.

nipponensis), Korean soybeans (G. max var. koreana), and Chinese soybeans (G. max var.

sinensis). There are also several cultivar groups within each variant, which can further be classified based on factors like seed size, oil content, protein content, and maturity period.

For instance, the "Williams" group is known for its high oil content and is commonly used in food processing, while the "Enrei" group is prized for its high protein content and is often used as a feedstock for animal feed.

What is the geographic distribution of Glycine max?

Glycine max, also known as soybean, is native to East Asia and has a wide geographic distribution across the world.

It originated in China and was first domesticated over 3,000 years ago. Today, soybeans are cultivated on every continent except Antarctica and are one of the most widely grown legume crops globally. They are primarily grown in tropical and subtropical regions with warm and humid climates, such as Asia, North America, South America, Europe, Africa, and Australia.

Soybean production is concentrated in China, the United States, Brazil, Argentina, and Indonesia, which together account for over 70% of global production.

What is the life cycle of Glycine max?

Glycine max, also known as soybean, has a relatively simple life cycle.

It begins with germination, where a soybean seed sprouts and grows into a seedling. The seedling develops roots and leaves over several weeks before producing flowers and pods containing seeds. The plant reaches maturity in about 90 to 120 days, depending on factors such as weather and soil quality.

After maturation, the soybeans are harvested, and new seeds can be planted for the next cycle.

What are the historical uses of Glycine max?

Glycine max, also known as soybean, has a rich history of utilization dating back over 3,000 years.

In ancient China, it was used as a food source and in traditional medicine to treat various ailments. The oil extracted from the seeds was used for cooking, lighting, and even as a form of currency. Later, soybeans became an important crop in Japan, Korea, and Southeast Asia, where they were used as a source of protein, oil, and animal feed.

Today, soybeans remain a crucial component of many cultures' diets and economies.

What are the historical texts of Glycine max?

Glycine max, the soybean plant, has a rich history that is documented in various ancient and medieval texts.

The earliest recorded references to soybeans date back to the Shang Dynasty (16th-11th centuries BCE) in China, where they were mentioned in the "Shu Jing" (Book of History).

Later, during the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE), soybeans were described in detail by agricultural writers such as Jia Sixie's "Qi Min Yao Shu" and Wang Fu's "Wujing Zongyao".

What are the historical artifacts of Glycine max?

Glycine max, also known as soybean, has a rich history dating back over 3,000 years.

Historical artifacts of this ancient crop include terra cotta figures found in ancient Chinese tombs, depicting soybeans and their use in traditional medicine and cooking.

Additionally, archaeological discoveries in China have uncovered soybean residue on pottery and utensils, indicating its widespread cultivation during the Han Dynasty (206 BCE - 220 CE).
