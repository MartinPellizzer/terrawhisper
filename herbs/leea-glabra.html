Leea glabra

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Leea glabra has opposite, simple, elliptical leaves that are typically five to fifteen centimeters long and characterized by a smooth, glabrous surface. The foliage is often a deep green color and possesses an entire margin without serration or lobes. These leaves are supported by slender, woody stems that grow in a shrubby or small tree-like habit. The plant also features small, axillary clusters of flowers that eventually develop into fleshy, rounded drupes. These morphological traits allow the species to thrive within its native tropical forest environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of East Africa, specifically ranging from Ethiopia and Kenya down to South Africa. It thrives in various habitats including coastal forests, riverine thickets, and moist woodlands throughout these territories. The species is most frequently found in areas where humidity is high and consistent rainfall supports dense vegetation. Within these zones, it often colonizes the understory of larger forest canopies. Its distribution is closely linked to the availability of shaded, moist environments characteristic of the African tropics.

      What are the herbal preparations of Leea glabra?

      Leea glabra is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste depending on the intended therapeutic use. The roots and leaves are most commonly boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal ailments. In some traditional practices, the crushed plant material is applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat localized swelling or irritation. These liquid preparations are often consumed in small doses to address various systemic conditions. The choice of preparation method typically depends on which part of the plant is being harvested and the specific symptoms being treated.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Leea glabra can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. While not widely documented as highly toxic, direct contact with the plant's sap or foliage may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Symptoms of skin irritation typically include redness, itching, or a localized rash at the site of contact. If any part of the plant is consumed, it might lead to stomach upset or nausea. Because specific toxicological studies on this species are limited, caution should always be exercised when handling it.
