What's the taxonomical classification of Maackia amurensis?

Maackia amurensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is part of the order Fabales and falls within the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Maackia, and its unique species designation, amurensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Maackia amurensis has woody, decumbent to ascending stems that support opposite, ovate to elliptical leaves with serrated margins and distinctively veined surfaces. The foliage typically measures between two and five centimeters in length and possesses a slightly textured, pubescent feel. During the flowering season, the plant produces small, bell-shaped blossoms that emerge from the leaf axils. These flowers are characterized by their pale pink to white coloration and delicate, tubular structure. The overall growth habit remains relatively low to the ground, forming dense, spreading mats of greenery.

What are the pharmacological activities of Maackia amurensis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-cancer, phytoestrogenic, estrogenic, anti-arthritis, and anti-cholecystitis properties. Its phytochemical constituents allow it to mimic or modulate natural hormones through its estrogenic and phytoestrogenic actions. Research indicates that these bioactive compounds may also inhibit the progression of certain tumor cells. Furthermore, the plant exhibits therapeutic potential in reducing inflammation associated with arthritis. It also shows promise in managing gallbladder inflammation by providing anti-cholecystitis effects.

As discussed in a study, the stem bark of Maackia amurensis has been used in folk medicine to treat cancer, cholecystitis, arthritis, and hyperthyroidism in females. An ethyl acetate fraction was obtained from a 70% ethanol extract of the plant to examine its effects on hormone activity. Both this fraction and the isolated constituent tectoridin activated thyroid-responsive reporters and increased thyroid hormone-dependent proliferation of rat pituitary GH3 cells. Furthermore, the ethyl acetate fraction and tectoridin activated reporter gene expression in MCF-7 cells while decreasing the estrogen receptor protein level. These results indicate that tectoriidin acts as a weak phytoestrogen and a thyroid hormone-like agent by activating both estrogen and thyroid hormone receptors.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains cytisine, various lupin alkaloids, daidzein, genistein, and isoflavones. The presence of cytisine is particularly notable for its potential application in smoking cessation treatments. These specific lupin alkaloids are characteristic chemical markers that define the plant's unique profile. Furthermore, the concentration of isoflavones provides significant therapeutic interest in biochemical research. Within this group, daidzein and genistein serve as potent examples of phytoestrogens found in the tissue.

As discussed in a study, one new cytisine-type alkaloid named [(3-hydroxy-6-pyridinyl)-methyl]-cytisine (1) was isolated from the stem bark of Maackia amurensis. This plant also contains 5 known alkaloids, which include cytisine, N-formylcytisine, N-(3-oxobutyl)cytisine, (-)-epibaptifoline, and N-methylcytisine. The structure of the new alkaloid 1 was elucidated using several spectral methods such as IR, CD, 1D and 2D NMR, HR-ESI-MS, and EI-MS.
