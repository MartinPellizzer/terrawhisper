What is Jasmine (Jasminum Grandiflorum)?

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is a flowering plant native to the Mediterranean region and parts of Asia, widely cultivated for its fragrant flowers and used in traditional herbal medicine for its calming and therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Jasmine is Jasminum grandiflorum. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Oleales, family Oleaceae, genus Jasminum, and species Jasminum grandiflorum. It is commonly known as Jasmine in the Middle East and South Asia, and as Common Jasmine in Europe. The synonyms for this plant include Jasminum officinale and Jasminum bulbocastanum.

Plant Description and Morphology

Jasmine is a deciduous shrub that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters, with a dense, bushy structure characterized by multiple branches and a compact growth habit.

Its leaves are simple, alternate, and ovate to lanceolate in shape, with a glossy dark green upper surface and a lighter underside. The flowers are small, fragrant, and tubular, usually white or pale yellow, with five petals and a sweet, intoxicating aroma that intensifies as the bloom matures. The root system is fibrous and shallow, spreading out just below the soil surface to absorb moisture and nutrients efficiently.

The plant has a smooth, woody stem covered in fine, soft hairs, with leaves that are leathery in texture and emit a subtle, fresh scent when crushed.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, northern Africa, and western Asia, and is commonly found in coastal areas, woodlands, and rocky soils.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The flowers, leaves, and fruits of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) are primarily used medicinally, with the flowers being the most commonly utilized part for their aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) contains a complex array of phytochemical compounds, including essential oils rich in linalool, benzyl acetate, and methyl benzoate, along with flavonoids, alkaloids, and saponins, which contribute to its medicinal properties and aromatic profile.

Pharmacological Properties

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) possesses pharmacological properties that include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant activities, supported by both traditional use and modern scientific research.

Mechanisms of Action

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) exerts its effects through a combination of bioactive compounds such as essential oils, alkaloids, and flavonoids, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and sedative properties by interacting with various physiological pathways including the central nervous system and immune response mechanisms.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat respiratory conditions such as bronchitis and asthma, as well as to alleviate symptoms of anxiety and depression due to its calming and sedative properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) in herbal medicine include the use of dried flowers, essential oils, tinctures, infusions, and decoctions, each tailored to specific therapeutic applications.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) for herbal use typically ranges from 200 to 400 mg of dried flower powder per day, divided into two or three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic effect and preparation method.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts for culinary and aromatic purposes, it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and by those with known allergies to the plant family.

Drug Interactions

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system, due to its potential sedative and antispasmodic properties, which could enhance or alter the effects of drugs such as benzodiazepines, antihistamines, and opioids.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as part of herbal medicine, it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and prolonged or excessive use could lead to skin irritation or allergic reactions.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is typically cultivated in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil and partial shade, requiring regular watering and pruning to promote flowering, with flowers harvested at dawn when their fragrance is most intense.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) involve harvesting the flowers at peak bloom, typically during the night when their fragrance is strongest, followed by steam distillation to capture the essential oils, which are then purified and concentrated for use in herbal medicine.

Storage and Shelf Life

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and extend its shelf life, which typically ranges from 1 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has shown that Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) contains compounds such as linalool and benzyl acetate, which have been studied for their potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and sedative properties in traditional and modern herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) has been historically used in traditional medicine for its calming properties, particularly to alleviate symptoms of anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues, with its essential oils and floral extracts being central to its therapeutic applications.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is regulated as a herbal medicine under national pharmaceutical laws, requiring compliance with quality control standards and safety assessments for its use in traditional and complementary medicine practices.
