Jasminum grandiflorum

What's the taxonomical classification of Jasminum grandiflorum?

Jasminum grandiflorum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As a member of the order Lamiales, it falls under the family Oleaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized within the genus Jasminum, specifically identified by its species name, grandiflorum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Jasminum grandiflorum has woody, climbing stems with opposite, ovate to elliptic leaves that measure approximately five to ten centimeters in length. The foliage is typically evergreen, displaying a glossy texture and a deep green hue on the upper surface. Its most distinguishing feature is the large, fragrant, white flowers that emerge in clusters from the axils of the leaves. Each blossom consists of five to nine petals that form a star-like shape, often reaching several centimeters in diameter. The plant develops a dense, sprawling habit as the vines weave through supporting structures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across countries like Thailand, Vietnam, and parts of Malaysia. It thrives primarily in the dense, humid undergrowth of evergreen forests where it can climb towards sunlight. Within these habitats, it prefers areas with well-drained soil and consistent moisture to support its vigorous climbing habit. Its distribution is closely tied to warm climates that prevent frost damage to its delicate foliage. Because it favors these specific environmental conditions, it is rarely found in more temperate or arid zones.

What are the pharmacological activities of Jasminum grandiflorum?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, cytotoxic, gastroprotective, and renoprotective properties. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize harmful free radicals within the body to prevent cellular damage. The anti-inflammatory actions assist in reducing swelling and pain associated with various physiological conditions. Furthermore, its cytotoxic effects show potential in inhibiting the growth of specific cancer cell lines. The plant also exhibits protective qualities that safeguard the gastric mucosa and maintain healthy kidney function.

As discussed in a study, Jasminum grandiflorum L. is a medicinal plant used to treat hepatitis and gastritis. The research analyzed the effects of 4 different extracts and 2 compounds from the flower of Jasminum grandiflorum in a mouse model of HCl/EtOH-induced gastric ulcer. These flower extracts alleviated gastric mucosal ulceration by increasing PGE2 production and the activity of antioxidant enzymes. Additionally, the extracts worked by the suppression of reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation, lipid peroxidation, apoptosis-related proteins, pro-inflammatory cytokines and nitric oxide (NO) production.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains oleacein, ursolic acid, isoquercitrin, various oxygenated terpenoids, and a high total phenolic content. These medicinal compounds work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the body. The presence of oleacein is particularly notable for its potential cardioprotective qualities. Additionally, the ursolic acid and isoquercitrin contribute to the plant's overall therapeutic profile. High levels of phenolic content further enhance its ability to combat oxidative stress at a cellular level. Finally, the oxygenated terpenoids provide additional bioactive properties that support general wellness.

As discussed in a study, fractionation of extracts of aerial parts of Jasminum grandiflorum resulted in the isolation of the previously reported ACE inhibitor, oleacein. The IC50 values of purified ACE inhibitors were 26-36 microM. Additionally, 2-(3,4-dihydroxyphenyl)-ethanol, isoquercitrin and ursolic acid were isolated from Jasminum grandiflorum. These compounds are formed from genuine iridoid glucosides during processing of the plant material.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive distress if ingested improperly. While the fragrant flowers are often used for scent, direct contact with the foliage or petals may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Some people report redness or itching upon touching the plant. If parts of the jasmine are consumed, it might lead to nausea or stomach upset. Always exercise caution and consult a professional before using any part of the species for topical or medicinal purposes.
