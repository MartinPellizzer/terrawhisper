Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is a fragrant flowering plant native to Asia, known for its delicate white flowers and pleasant aroma, and it has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to treat various ailments including anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Jasmine (Jasminum Grandiflorum)?

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is a flowering plant native to the Middle East and South Asia, commonly cultivated for its fragrant flowers and used in herbal medicine for its calming and therapeutic properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is a flowering plant species native to the Mediterranean region, belonging to the Oleaceae family, and is characterized by its fragrant white flowers and evergreen foliage.

Common Names and Synonyms

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is commonly known by various names such as Common Jasmine, Garden Jasmine, and Persian Jasmine, and it is also referred to by botanical synonyms like Jasminum officinale and Jasminum sambac in different regions and contexts.

Plant Description and Morphology

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is a flowering plant characterized by its fragrant, white to pale yellow flowers, opposite simple leaves with entire margins, and a woody, branched stem that typically grows to a height of 1-3 meters.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, and is commonly found in coastal areas, rocky soils, and woodland edges.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The flowers, leaves, and fruits of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) are the primary plant parts used medicinally, with the flowers being the most commonly utilized due to their high concentration of essential oils and aromatic compounds.

Phytochemical Composition

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) contains a complex array of phytochemical compounds, including essential oils rich in linalool, benzyl acetate, and methyl benzoate, as well as flavonoids, alkaloids, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its therapeutic properties in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) possesses pharmacological properties that include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant activities, with compounds such as linalool and methyl jasmonate contributing to its therapeutic potential in herbal medicine.

Mechanisms of Action

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) exerts its effects through the presence of bioactive compounds such as essential oils, flavonoids, and alkaloids, which interact with various physiological systems to exhibit antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and sedative properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Jasmine is used to treat digestive issues, respiratory infections, menstrual pain, skin conditions, and stress-related disorders.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) in herbal medicine include the use of its flowers in essential oils, infusions, tinctures, and dried flowers for aromatherapy and topical applications.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) in herbal medicine ranges from 200 to 500 mg of dried flower extract per day, divided into two to three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, excessive consumption or improper application may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or skin irritation.

Drug Interactions

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system, due to its potential sedative and calming properties, which could enhance or counteract the effects of drugs such as benzodiazepines, antihistamines, or antidepressants.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts for culinary and aromatic purposes, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with known allergies to the plant family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is typically cultivated in well-drained soil with partial shade, requiring consistent moisture and moderate temperatures for optimal growth and flowering.

Processing and Extraction

Processing Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) for use in herbal medicine typically involves harvesting the flowers at peak bloom, drying them to preserve their aromatic compounds, and then grinding them into a fine powder or distilling them to produce essential oils.

Storage and Shelf Life

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, with a shelf life of up to 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) has explored its potential medicinal properties, including antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory effects, with studies suggesting its use in traditional herbal medicine for conditions such as respiratory infections, skin disorders, and stress-related ailments.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its calming properties, often prepared as a tea or essential oil to alleviate anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Jasmine (Jasminum grandiflorum) is regulated as a herbal medicine, with specific guidelines for cultivation, processing, and safety standards depending on the region and intended use.
