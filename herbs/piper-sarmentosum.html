Piper sarmentosum

What's the taxonomical classification of Piper sarmentosum?

Piper sarmentosum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant follows the taxonomic order Piperales and is a member of the family Piperaceae. Specifically, it is situated within the genus Piper, which leads to its unique scientific designation as the species sarmentosum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Piper sarmentosum has elliptical to lanceolate leaves that are arranged alternately along its woody stems and feature distinctively serrated margins. The leaf blades are typically characterized by prominent venation and a slightly coriaceous texture. Small, inconspicuous flowers are borne on slender spikes that emerge from the leaf axils. These spikes eventually develop into small, fleshy drupes that change color as they reach maturity. The plant's overall structure is that of a compact shrub with a branching growth habit.

What are the pharmacological activities of Piper sarmentosum?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and antidiabetic pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to reduce pain and swelling by modulating various biological pathways in the body. The presence of bioactive compounds also allows it to neutralize free radicals and combat oxidative stress. Additionally, its antimicrobial properties help inhibit the growth of diverse pathogenic microorganisms. Research even suggests that it may assist in managing blood glucose levels to provide antidiabetic benefits.

As discussed in a study, Piper sarmentosum Roxb. is a medicinal plant reported to possess various biological properties, including anti-inflammatory, anti-psychotic and anti-oxidant activity. Extracts obtained from the roots of Piper sarmentosum, specifically RMEOH, significantly attenuated the production and mRNA expression of IL-1β, IL-6 and TNF-α in Aβ-induced BV2 microglial cells. The RHXN, REA and RMEOH extracts also significantly reduced nitric oxide (NO) levels. These Piper sarmentosum root extracts attenuated the production of cytokines by regulating the phosphorylation of p38α MAPK in microglia. Furthermore, the extracts have protective effects against Aβ-induced indirect neurotoxicity by inhibiting the production of NO, IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α in BV2 cells or by protecting SHSY5Y cells against these inflammatory mediators.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds piperine, piperlongumine, pellitorine, myristicin, and sarmentine. Piperine acts as a bioactive alkaloid that enhances the bioavailability of various nutrients and drugs. The compound piperlongumine is studied for its potential anti-cancer properties and its ability to target specific metabolic pathways in cells. Pellitorine and sarmentine contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile, often influencing sensory perception or physiological responses. Additionally, myristicin is a phenylpropene found within the plant that offers unique biochemical activities.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract of the Piper sarmentosum root showed specific activity against lung cancer cells with an IC50 of 32.91 microg/ml. While all water extracts had no cytotoxic activity, the ethanolic extract of the Piper sarmentosum root was tested alongside other components of the Benjakul preparation. The study investigated the cytotoxicity of various ingredients, including the Piper sarmentosum root, against three human cancer cell lines. These findings provide data regarding the medicinal potential of the plant's specific extract in relation to lung carcinoma cells.
