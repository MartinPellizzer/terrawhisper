Buddleja fallowiana

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Buddlejia fallowiaana has opposite, lanceolate to ovate leaves that are typically 5 to 15 centimeters long with serrated margins and a finely pubescent texture. The plant exhibits a woody, shrubby growth habit with slender, upright branches. Its inflorescences consist of dense, terminal panicles composed of numerous small, tubular flowers. These individual blossoms often display a pale lavender to white coloration with distinct nectar guides. The foliage maintains a soft, grayish-green appearance due to the presence of fine hairs on both the upper and lower surfaces.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of western China, specifically within the provinces of Yunnan, Sichuan, and Tibet. It typically thrives in montane environments located at elevations ranging from 2,500 to 4,000 meters above sea level. Within these mountainous landscapes, the species is commonly found growing along rocky slopes and the edges of forest margins. The specific microclimates of these regions provide the cool, well-drained conditions necessary for its survival. Its distribution remains localized to these specific eastern Himalayan foothills.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Buddlejia fallowiana can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or respiratory distress if its pollen or sap comes into direct contact with sensitive individuals. Some people may experience redness, itching, or a stinging sensation on the skin after touching the foliage. Inhaling the fine pollen particles can trigger sneezing, coughing, or more severe asthma symptoms in those with existing allergies. While direct toxicity to humans is not extensively documented, caution is advised when handling the plant to prevent these common reactions. Maintaining distance from the flowering parts can help mitigate the risk of accidental exposure.
