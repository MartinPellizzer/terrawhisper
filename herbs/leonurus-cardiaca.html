Scientific and Botanical Profile

Heartworts, with botanical name Leonurus cardiaca, are perennial herbs belonging to the Lamiaceae family, known for their distinctive heart-shaped leaves.

Native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, this plant is commonly referred to as Hart's Tongue, Heart's Ease, Heart-Leaved Speedwell, Redstem, Hare's-Foot, Motherwort, Lion's Tail, Common Heartwort, Cardiac Leonurus, Cardiac Leonturus, and Leonurus Heartleaf. Morphologically, Leonurus cardiaca features slender, erect stems with opposite, lanceolate to heart-shaped leaves that are often reddish or purple underneath, and small, tubular flowers that bloom in whorls along the stem.

It is a hardy plant that thrives in moist, shaded environments and has been historically used in traditional medicine for its purported cardiovascular benefits.

History and Cultural Relevance

Heartworts was used in traditional medicine across various cultures for its purported healing properties, particularly in ancient Egypt, Greece, and medieval Europe, where it was believed to promote heart health and ease respiratory ailments.

The plant, known scientifically as Leonurus cardiaca, was revered for its heart-shaped leaves, which were thought to symbolize love and protection, leading to its incorporation into folk rituals and love charms. In traditional Chinese medicine, it was used to treat menstrual disorders and infertility, while in Ayurveda, it was valued for its calming effects on the nervous system. Despite modern scientific skepticism, some traditional remedies involving Heartworts, such as poultices for skin inflammation or herbal teas for anxiety, are still practiced in rural communities today.

Its enduring presence in folklore and herbal traditions underscores its cultural significance as both a medicinal plant and a symbol of emotional and spiritual well-being.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Leonurus cardiaca has been traditionally used for its purported medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare the plant, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion, where the leaves are soaked in cold water for several hours, or applied topically as a poultice for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried herb per cup of tea, while for children, it is advised to consult a healthcare professional due to limited safety data.

Usage should be conservative, with short durations and limited frequency to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Leonurus cardiaca can be used cautiously as a medicinal plant, but it is important to note that it may have potential side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, dizziness, and in some cases, cardiac arrhythmias due to its cardiac glycoside content.

It should be avoided by individuals with a history of heart conditions or those taking medications that affect heart rhythm, such as digoxin, as it may lead to toxic interactions. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid leonurus cardiaca due to the risk of adverse effects on fetal or infant development, and its safety in these populations has not been well established. Individuals with chronic illnesses, particularly those affecting the heart or kidneys, should consult a healthcare provider before using this plant.

To ensure safe use, it is advisable to consult a qualified healthcare professional, start with a low dose, and monitor for any adverse reactions, especially when combining it with other herbs or medications.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Leonurus cardiaca grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without becoming waterlogged.

It thrives in temperate climates and prefers moderate temperatures, making it suitable for gardens in USDA hardiness zones 4 through 8. For optimal growth, it should be planted in early spring after the last frost, with spaced rows to allow for air circulation and prevent disease. Regular weeding and mulching help control weeds and retain soil moisture, while pruning spent flowers encourages continuous blooming and extends the harvesting period. Harvesting is most effective during the early morning or late afternoon when the plant's essential oils are most concentrated, using sharp scissors or shears to cut the stems just above the base, ensuring minimal damage to the plant.

After harvesting, the plant material should be dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area to preserve its potency, then stored in airtight containers away from light and moisture to maintain its medicinal properties.
