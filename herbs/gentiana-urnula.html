Gentiana urnula

What's the taxonomical classification of Gentiana urnula?

Gentiana urnula belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Gentianaales and belongs to the family Gentianaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Gentiana and is specifically designated as the species urnula.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gentiana urnula has erect, unbranched stems that reach heights of 5 to 20 centimeters and bear lanceolate to ovate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically arranged in opposite pairs along the stem, exhibiting a smooth texture and a deep green coloration. Its most distinctive feature is the funnel-shaped or urceolate flowers, which are dark violet to almost black in hue. These solitary blossoms emerge from the leaf axils and possess a narrow corolla tube that flares slightly at the mouth. The plant also produces small, capsule-like fruit that contains numerous tiny seeds for reproduction.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

It primarily inhabits subalpine and alpine meadows where rocky terrain and high elevations provide its preferred growing conditions. The species is often restricted to specific limestone or calcareous soil compositions within these mountainous zones. What are the pharmacological activities of Gentiana urnula?

This plant has potent antioxidative properties that help neutralize free radicals and prevent cellular damage. These biological effects are largely attributed to the high concentration of secoiridoid glycosides and phenolic compounds found within its tissues. Such chemical constituents contribute to its potential use in mitigating oxidative stress in various physiological systems. Researchers are currently investigating how these specific bioactive molecules interact with biological pathways to provide therapeutic benefits. Continued studies aim to fully characterize the full spectrum of its medicinal potential in clinical applications.

As discussed in a study, three known iridoid glucosides including gentiournoside A, gentiournoside E, and depressoside were isolated from the flowers of Gentiana urnula Harry Sm. All three of these compounds showed excellent DPPH radical scavenging activities with IC50 values of 10-20 μmol L(-1). Among these, depressoside exhibited a much higher scavenging activity against superoxide radicals with an IC50 of 45.5 μmol L(-1). This activity was significantly greater than the other two extracted compounds, which had IC50 values of more than 900 μmol L(-1).

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, including nausea, vomiting, and abdominal cramping, if ingested in significant amounts. The primary concern stems from the high concentration of bitter glycosides present in its roots and stems. These compounds can irritate the lining of the digestive tract and cause rapid purgative effects. While often used in traditional medicine for digestive stimulation, improper dosing may lead to dehydration or electrolyte imbalances. It is essential to exercise extreme caution as toxicity levels can vary between individual specimens.
