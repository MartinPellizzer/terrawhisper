Leea indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Leea indica?

Leea indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its lineage further, it falls under the order Vitales and is a member of the family Vitaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Leea and the species indica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Leea indica has opposite, decussate leaves that are typically elliptic to lanceolate in shape, measuring between 5 and 15 centimeters in length with serrated margins and acute tips. The plant is a perennial shrub or small tree that can reach heights of up to 5 meters. Its stems are woody and often exhibit a reddish or brownish tint. The inflorescences consist of terminal panicles containing small, pink to purple flowers arranged in dense clusters. These flowers produce small, fleshy drupes that transition from green to a dark purple or black color when ripe.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, Australia, and parts of the Pacific Islands. It thrives in humid environments found across India, Southeast Asia, and much of the South Pacific. Due to its resilience, it has become naturalized in various other tropical locales around the world. Its presence is most common in disturbed forest areas and along riverbanks where moisture is abundant. This widespread distribution is primarily facilitated by its ability to adapt to different tropical soil types.

What are the therapeutic actions of Leea indica?

Leea indica has medicinal properties that function as an astringent, anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, hemostatic, antimicrobial, and emetic agent. The plant's chemical constituents allow it to reduce swelling and alleviate pain through its anti-inflammatory capabilities. It can also help lower body temperature due to its antipyretic effects. Its astringent and hemostatic qualities make it useful for controlling bleeding and tightening tissues. Additionally, the species exhibits antimicrobial activity against various pathogens and can act as an emetic to induce vomiting when required.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (-)-epigallocatechin-3-O-gallate, gallic acid, methyl gallate, mollic acid arabinoside, and mollic acid xyloside. These specific polyphenols and glycosides contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile. Gallic acid and its derivatives like methyl gallate are often recognized for their antioxidant properties. The presence of specialized molecules such as mollic acid arabinoside and mollic acid xyloside adds to the chemical complexity of the species. Researchers study these constituents to understand how they interact with biological systems.

As discussed in a study, an ethyl acetate fraction of Leea indica leaves was analyzed for its phytoconstituent classes. A total of 31 compounds were identified, including benzoic acid derivatives, phenolics, flavonoids, catechins, dihydrochalcones, coumarins, megastigmanes, and oxylipins. Six specific compounds were isolated and identified by NMR analysis, which were gallic acid, methyl gallate, (-)-epigallocatechin-3-O-gallate, myricetin-3-O-rhamnoside, quercetin-3-O-rhamnoside, and 4',6'-dihydroxy-4-methoxydihydrochalcone 2'-O-beta-d-glucopyranoside. The study also led to the tentative identification of 3 novel dihydrochalcones. Altogether, 24 compounds, including these 3 new dihydrochalcones, were identified for the first time from the genus Leea.

For topical applications, a leaf paste is created by crushing the foliage into a thick consistency. Additionally, fresh leaf juice can be squeezed directly from the plant for immediate treatment.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, redness, and allergic reactions upon direct contact with the skin or mucous membranes. The sap of Leea indica often contains compounds that act as irritants to sensitive individuals. Contact with the eyes may lead to stinging sensations or temporary inflammation. Some people might experience localized swelling or a rash if they brush against the foliage or stems. It is generally recommended to avoid touching the plant to prevent these unpleasant physiological responses.
