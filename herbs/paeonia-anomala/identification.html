Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name of the Anomalous Peony is Paeonia anomala, a classification established by the Swedish botanist Carl von Linné. This name reflects its unique characteristics and distinguishes it from other peony species within the Paeoniaceae family.

Morphological Characteristics

Anomalous Peony (Paeonia anomala) is a herbaceous perennial with a rhizomatous root system. It has upright, slender stems that are smooth and slightly woody. The leaves are compound, with 5-7 leaflets that are ovate to lanceolate in shape. The inflorescence consists of a single flower on a peduncle. The flowers are large, showy, and typically pink or white, with five petals and numerous stamens. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release numerous seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

The Anomalous Peony can be distinguished by its distinctive pinkish-purple petals with a pale yellow center, deeply lobed leaves, and a unique scent. It grows in moist, shaded areas and has a rhizomatous root system. These features help differentiate it from similar species like Paeonia lactiflora and Paeonia suffruticosa.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Paeonia anomala is native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia, including parts of Russia, Kazakhstan, and Mongolia. It thrives in temperate climates with cold winters and warm summers. The plant prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 1,500 and 3,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Anomalous Peony blooms in late spring to early summer, typically between May and June. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to September. For identification purposes, harvest timing is critical during the flowering period to ensure accurate species recognition.
