Botanical Identification of Maypop (Passiflora incarnata)

To identify Maypop (Passiflora incarnata) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Maypop, or Passiflora incarnata, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Saxifragales, family Passifloraceae, genus Passiflora, and species Passiflora incarnata. This classification places it within the flowering plants, specifically in the order that includes other flowering species, and within the family known for its distinctive floral structures.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Passiflora incarnata is the scientific name for Maypop. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Common synonyms include Passiflora lutea and Passiflora tripartita. It is also known by regional names such as Maypop, Wild Passionflower, and Purple Passionflower.

Morphological Characteristics

Maypop, or Passiflora incarnata, is an herbaceous perennial with a sprawling growth habit. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, supporting its climbing or trailing stems. Leaves are trifoliate, with three heart-shaped leaflets, and the plant produces clusters of striking blue-violet flowers. The fruit is a yellowish, edible berry that matures into a dark red seed pod containing numerous small, hard seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Maypop can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow-orange flowers with five sepals, five petals, and a crown of filaments. The fruit is a round, greenish-yellow berry with a prominent, yellow, fleshy aril. It has a strong, sweet, and sometimes unpleasant odor, which helps in identification. Leaves are alternate, palmately lobed, and have a rough texture.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Maypop, or Passiflora incarnata, is native to the southeastern United States, particularly Florida and the Gulf Coast. It thrives in warm, humid climates with mild winters. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found at low to mid-elevation areas, often near water sources or in disturbed habitats.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Maypop (Passiflora incarnata) blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, following the flowering period. For identification purposes, the most distinctive features are visible during the flowering stage, making early summer the optimal time for accurate botanical recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Passiflora incarnata is closely related to other Passiflora species within the same genus. It shares key characteristics with its closest relatives, such as similar floral structures and leaf morphology. Its phylogenetic placement within the Passifloraceae family highlights its evolutionary ties to other tropical and subtropical species.
