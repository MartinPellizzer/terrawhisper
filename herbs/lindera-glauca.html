Lindera glauca

What's the taxonomical classification of Lindera glauca?

Lindera glauca belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Laurales and belongs to the family Lauraceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Lindera, and its specific species name, glauca.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lindera glauca has deciduous, lanceolate to ovate leaves that are typically 2 to 5 centimeters long and possess smooth or slightly serrated margins. The foliage displays a distinct glaucous coating, giving the undersides of the leaves a pale or bluish-green appearance. During the spring, the plant produces small, inconspicuous yellow flowers that emerge in clusters from the woody stems. These blossoms eventually give way to small, fleshy red drupes that persist on the branches through the winter. The bark is generally grayish and becomes increasingly furrowed as the shrub matures into a larger woody form.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southwest corner of Western Australia, specifically distributed within the kwongan heathlands of the Esperance and Albany regions. It typically occupies coastal and near-coastal environments characterized by sandy soils. Within these habitats, the species thrives in diverse shrubland communities ranging from the southern coast to inland areas. Its presence is often limited to specific ecological niches where nutrient-poor soils support its specialized growth patterns. This restricted range makes it an endemic species to this particular Mediterranean-type climate zone.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires full sun exposure and well-draining, sandy or loamy soil to thrive in a landscape.

You should plant it in a location that stays relatively dry, as it is highly susceptible to root rot from overwatering. Once established, the shrub is quite drought-tolerant and can withstand periods of low moisture without significant distress. Regular pruning after the flowering season helps maintain a compact shape and encourages future blooms.

It performs best in USDA zones 9 through 11 where temperatures remain mild throughout the year.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds alpha-cis-ocimene, camphene, caryophyllene oxide, eucalyptol, and lindeasestripentenoid C. These specific chemical constituents are primarily found within the essential oils extracted from the plant's foliage. Each of these organic molecules contributes to the unique therapeutic profile and aromatic properties of the species. Scientific analysis focuses on these terpenes and oxygenated compounds to understand their potential pharmacological effects. Research continues to investigate how these substances interact with biological systems to provide healing benefits.

According to a study published by "Zhong yao cai = Zhongyaocai = Journal of Chinese medicinal materials", 32 kinds of constituents were identified in the distilling oil and 22 kinds were identified in the solvent oil of Lindera glauca fruits. The main components in the distilling oil were n-carpric acid at 25.39%, germacrene A at 10.71%, n-dodecanole acid at 10.08%, epishyobunol acetate at 7.29%, and caryophyllene oxide at 5.44%. The solvent oil contained camphene at 17.55%, 3,6,6-trimethyl-2-norpinene at 16.85%, capric acid, ethyl ester at 13.61%, eucalyptol at 8.10%, and alpha-cis-ocimene at 7.38%. The distilling oil exhibited antifungal properties with a MIC between 0.03-0.5 ml/L for pathogenic fungi species and 1.0-1.5 ml/L for moulds.
