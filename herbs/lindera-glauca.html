Lindera glauca

What's the taxonomical classification of Lindera glauca?

Lindera glauca belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Laurales and belongs to the family Lauraceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Lindera, and its specific species name, glauca.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lindera glauca has deciduous, lanceolate to ovate leaves that are typically 2 to 5 centimeters long and possess smooth or slightly serrated margins. The foliage displays a distinct glaucous coating, giving the undersides of the leaves a pale or bluish-green appearance. During the spring, the plant produces small, inconspicuous yellow flowers that emerge in clusters from the woody stems. These blossoms eventually give way to small, fleshy red drupes that persist on the branches through the winter. The bark is generally grayish and becomes increasingly furrowed as the shrub matures into a larger woody form.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southwest corner of Western Australia, specifically distributed within the kwongan heathlands of the Esperance and Albany regions. It typically occupies coastal and near-coastal environments characterized by sandy soils. Within these habitats, the species thrives in diverse shrubland communities ranging from the southern coast to inland areas. Its presence is often limited to specific ecological niches where nutrient-poor soils support its specialized growth patterns. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds alpha-cis-ocimene, camphene, caryophyllene oxide, epishyobunol acetate, and eucalyptol. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's complex therapeutic profile and distinct aromatic properties. For instance, the presence of eucalyptol is often associated with respiratory support and antimicrobial effects. Meanwhile, compounds like camphene and caryophyllene oxide play significant roles in the plant's overall biological activity. Together, this diverse mixture of essential oils determines how the plant interacts with various physiological systems.

As discussed in a study, 32 kinds of constituents were identified in the distilling oil and 22 kinds were found in the solvent oil of Lindera glauca fruits. The main components in the distilling oil included n-carpric acid (25.39%), germacrene A (10.71%), n-dodecanole acid (10.08%), epishyobunol acetate (7.29%), and caryophyllene oxide (5.44%). In the solvent oil, the main components were camphene (17.55%), 3,6,6-trimethyl-2-norpinene (16.85%), capric acid, ethyl ester (13.61%), eucalyptol (8.10%), and alpha-cis-ocimene (7.38%). The distilling oil exhibited antifungal properties with an MIC between 0.03-0.5 ml/L for pathogenic fungi species and 1.0-1.5 ml/L for moulds.
