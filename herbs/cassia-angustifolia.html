Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is a flowering plant native to Sri Lanka, known for its use in traditional medicine to treat digestive disorders and improve symptoms of constipation.

What is Ceylon Cassia (Cassia Angustifolia)?

Ceylon Cassia, or Cassia angustifolia, is a small, deciduous tree native to Sri Lanka and parts of southern India, known for its yellow flowers and the medicinal use of its seeds in traditional herbal medicine.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Ceylon Cassia, scientifically known as Cassia angustifolia, is a species of flowering plant belonging to the legume family Fabaceae, and is native to the tropical regions of southern Asia, including Sri Lanka, India, and parts of Southeast Asia.

Common Names and Synonyms

Ceylon Cassia, also known as Cassia angustifolia, is commonly referred to by various regional names such as Indian Senna, Kanchanara, and Karanja, reflecting its widespread use in traditional herbal medicine across South Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is a shrub or small tree characterized by its slender, often multi-branched stem, narrow lance-shaped leaves with a smooth upper surface and a hairy underside, and clusters of small, bright yellow, pea-like flowers that bloom in spring.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is native to the tropical regions of Sri Lanka and is primarily distributed in the wet zone of the island, where it thrives in moist, fertile soils and humid climates.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and bark of Ceylon Cassia are the primary plant parts used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including flavonoids such as rutin and quercetin, tannins, saponins, and alkaloids, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antidiabetic effects, primarily due to the presence of flavonoids and other bioactive compounds.

Mechanisms of Action

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antidiabetic properties, which are mediated by the active compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and alkaloids that modulate cellular pathways involved in immune response, glucose metabolism, and microbial inhibition.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Ceylon Cassia is used to treat digestive issues, skin conditions, inflammation, fever, diabetes, and respiratory disorders.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is typically prepared as a powder, decoction, or infusion, with the dried leaves and flowers being the primary parts used, often harvested during the summer months for optimal potency.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) typically ranges from 250 to 500 mg per day, taken in divided doses, with the exact amount depending on the specific preparation and the intended therapeutic use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting blood sugar or blood pressure.

Drug Interactions

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) may interact with certain medications due to its effects on blood sugar, blood pressure, and gastrointestinal function, potentially altering the efficacy or increasing the risk of side effects when combined with drugs such as antidiabetics, antihypertensives, or antiplatelet agents.

Toxicity and Precautions

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, so it should be used with caution and under professional guidance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions with well-drained soil and a warm climate, often in shaded or semi-shaded environments to promote optimal growth and essential oil production.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of Ceylon Cassia involves drying the flowers and pods under the sun or using low heat to preserve their active compounds, followed by grinding into a fine powder or distilling into essential oil for various medicinal applications.

Storage and Shelf Life

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) has primarily focused on its phytochemical composition, with studies identifying compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and saponins that contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine for digestive and anti-inflammatory purposes.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and digestive properties, with applications in treating skin disorders, digestive ailments, and as a natural dye.

Regulatory Status

Ceylon Cassia (Cassia angustifolia) is recognized as a medicinal plant in several countries, including Sri Lanka, India, and parts of Southeast Asia, and is regulated under local herbal medicine frameworks, though specific regulations may vary by region.
