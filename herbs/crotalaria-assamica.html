Crotalaria assamica

What's the taxonomical classification of Crotalaria assamica?

Crotalaria assamica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further categorized under the order Fabales. It is a member of the family Fabaceae and is situated within the genus Crotalaria, specifically identified by the species name assamica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crotalaria assamica has an erect, perennial herb structure characterized by branched stems and pinnately compound leaves with several pairs of leaflets. The leaves typically feature oblong to lanceolate leaflets that are entire along the margins and possess distinct venation. Small, yellowish flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves, often displaying a characteristic papilionaceous shape typical of the Fabaceae family. The plant produces distinctive inflated pods that contain seeds, which serve as a primary diagnostic feature for the species. These pods eventually dry and rattle, contributing to the common name of many plants within this genus.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the northeastern regions of India, specifically concentrated within the state of Assam. It thrives primarily in the subtropical and tropical climates characteristic of this hilly and riverine landscape. Beyond its primary habitat in Assam, its distribution is closely tied to the moist, lowland areas of the Brahmaputra valley. Researchers often find this species growing in disturbed soils or along the edges of forested zones. These specific environmental conditions define the limited geographical range where the species can successfully establish itself.

What are the pharmacological activities of Crotalaria assamica?

This plant has anti-apoptotic, anti-inflammatory, anti-necrotic, antioxidant, and liver protection pharmacological activities. These biological properties suggest that the species can actively mitigate cellular damage caused by oxidative stress and programmed cell death. Its anti-inflammatory and anti-necrotic actions work to prevent tissue degradation and manage pathological swelling in various organs. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates a significant capacity for liver protection by defending hepatocytes against toxic insults. Through these combined mechanisms, Crotalaria assamicat offers a diverse range of therapeutic potential for treating inflammatory and degenerative conditions.

As discussed in a study, Crotalaria assamica Benth alleviates acetaminophen-induced liver injury through the upregulation of the Nrf2 pathway. The extract of C. assamica (ECA) significantly reduces serum lactate dehydrogenase, aspartate transaminase, and alanine transaminase contents while adjusting oxidative indices. In vivo, ECA up-regulates the expressions of NQO1, HO-1, Nrf2, p-AKT, BCL-2, AKT, and IL-6. Additionally, the bioactive compound sarosiensin V increases the viability of APAP-induced HepG2 cells and restores liver function activity. Both C. assamica and sarosiensin V inhibit oxidative stress, inflammation, apoptosis, and necrosis of liver tissues.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids, which function as potent secondary metabolites used for chemical defense against herbivores. These nitrogenous compounds are often distributed throughout the leaves and roots to discourage consumption by insects and mammals. While they serve a protective purpose for the plant, they can pose significant health risks to humans if ingested in large quantities. Chronic exposure to these specific alkaloids is known to cause severe liver damage or other systemic toxicities. Researchers study these substances to understand their complex biochemical pathways and ecological roles.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe liver damage and systemic toxicity due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids in its tissues. If ingested, these toxic compounds can lead to hepatic veno-occlusive disease, which obstructs the small veins in the liver. Chronic exposure often results in progressive liver fibrosis and potentially fatal liver failure. Symptoms of poisoning may include jaundice, abdominal pain, and swelling of the extremities. Because these alkaloids are cumulative, even small or infrequent doses can lead to lasting physiological harm.
