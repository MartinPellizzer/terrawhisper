Crotalaria assamica

What's the taxonomical classification of Crotalaria assamica?

Crotalaria assamica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further categorized under the order Fabales. It is a member of the family Fabaceae and is situated within the genus Crotalaria, specifically identified by the species name assamica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crotalaria assamica has an erect, perennial herb structure characterized by branched stems and pinnately compound leaves with several pairs of leaflets.

The leaves typically feature oblong to lanceolate leaflets that are entire along the margins and possess distinct venation. Small, yellowish flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves, often displaying a characteristic papilionaceous shape typical of the Fabaceae family. The plant produces distinctive inflated pods that contain seeds, which serve as a primary diagnostic feature for the species.

These pods eventually dry and rattle, contributing to the common name of many plants within this genus.

Crotalaria assamica targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to mediate its therapeutic effects. The plant contains bioactive pyrrolizidine alkaloids and flavonoids that interact with signaling pathways to inhibit the production of nitric oxide. These phytochemicals also demonstrate the ability to modulate protein kinases involved in cellular stress responses and immune regulation. By interfering with these specific protein interactions, the plant exhibits significant anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. This molecular interference helps reduce oxidative damage and systemic inflammation in biological systems.

    What are the herbal preparations of Crotalaria assamica?

    Crotalaria assamica is prepared as a decoction, a paste, or an infusion for various medicinal uses. The roots are often processed into a paste to be applied topically to skin ailments. Many practitioners boil the plant parts to create a decoction that is ingested for internal health. Additionally, the leaves can be steeped in hot water to produce a soothing infusion. These different preparations allow the plant's active compounds to be administered effectively depending on the specific ailment being treated.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Crotalaria assamica can cause significant toxicity in humans and animals due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids which lead to irreversible liver damage. These specific chemical compounds trigger hepatic veno-occlusive disease by obstructing the small veins within the liver. Consumption of the plant may result in nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain as initial symptoms of poisoning. If ingestion persists, the damage can progress to cirrhosis or even liver failure. Monitoring for these physiological signs is critical when accidental exposure is suspected.
