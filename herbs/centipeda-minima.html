Centipeda minima

What's the taxonomical classification of Centipeda minima?

Centipeda minima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is situated under the order Asterales. It is further categorized into the family Asteraceae, which is a large and diverse group of flowering plants. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Centipeda, and its specific identification is completed by its species name, minima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Centipeda minima has small, inconspicuous flowers with five petals that are typically white or pale pink and a distinctively narrow, linear leaf structure. The plant is characterized by its diminutive stature and slender, creeping stems that allow it to spread across the soil surface. Each leaf is lanceolate in shape and features finely serrated margins along the edges. The reproductive organs are centrally located within the small floral structure, often possessing short filaments and anthers. These morphological traits are adapted to its specific ecological niche, facilitating survival in low-growth environments.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains the medicinal compounds helenaliin, chlorogenic acid, arnicolide C, arnicolide D, and various sesquiterpene lactones. These chemical constituents work together to provide the plant with its unique therapeutic properties. The presence of chlorogenic acid offers significant antioxidant benefits to the system. Additionally, the specific sesquiterpene lactones and the arnicoliides are often studied for their bioactive potential. Helenaliin serves as another key component within this complex chemical profile.

    As discussed in a study, a compound identified as helenalin was isolated from Centipeda minima using bioassay-guided screening. This bioactive ingredient was evaluated using a liver fibrosis model in rats involving intragastric administration of alcohol within 24 weeks. Helenalin significantly prevented alcohol-induced hepatic injury and inhibited fibrosis indicators such as hyaluronic acid, type III precollaogen, laminin, hydroxyproline, and collagen α type I. The compound works by enhancing alcohol and aldehyde dehydrogenase activities and reducing inflammatory mediators like TGF-β1, TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β, and myeloperoxidase. Helenalin also decreases collagen deposition by reducing profibrotic cytokines including transforming growth factor-β, platelet-derived growth factor-β, and connective tissue growth factor. Finally, it inhibits HSC activation through the down-regulation of α-SMA and TGF-β levels.
