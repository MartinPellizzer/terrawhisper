Rauvolfia tetraphylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Rauvolfia tetraphylla?

Rauvolfia tetraphyllla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized as Equisetopsida, falling under the subclass Magnoliidae. It is a member of the order Gentianales and is situated within the family Apocynaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by the genus Rauvolfia and is specifically designated as the species tetraphylla.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rauvolfia tetraphylla has opposite, decussate leaves that are typically arranged in whorls of four, characterized by an elliptical to oblong shape and an entire margin. The foliage is predominantly evergreen with a glossy, dark green upper surface and a paler underside. Its stems are woody and can develop a somewhat spreading or shrubby growth habit depending on the environment. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the axils, often displaying a white to pale pink coloration. The plant eventually produces small, fleshy drupes that transition from green to a dark color upon maturity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds ajmaline and reserpine. Ajmaline is an alkaloid primarily used in clinical settings to diagnose specific types of cardiac arrhythmias. Reserpine acts as an antipsychotic and antihypertensive agent by depleting monoamine neurotransmitters in the brain. These bioactive substances are extracted from the plant tissues for pharmaceutical research and development. Understanding the chemical profile of this species is essential for its potential application in modern medicine.

As discussed in a study, Rauvolfia tetraphyllia contains a methanolic extract (RTE) and isolated compounds such as Ajmaline (RTC1) and Reserpine (RTC2). The research investigated these phytocompounds against the MDA-MB-231 triple-negative breast cancer cell line. Among the substances tested, RTE and RTC2 showed promising cytotoxic effects compared to RTC1. In cell cycle analysis, around 80% of cells were observed in the G0/G1 phase following treatment. Additionally, while the migration of cells after the scratch was 60.45% in the control, it was 90% in treated cells, demonstrating inhibition.
