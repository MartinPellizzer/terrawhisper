Veronica biloba

What's the taxonomical classification of Veronica biloba?

Veronica biloba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Lamiales and the family Plantaginaceae, eventually reaching its specific designation within the genus Veronica. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its specific epithet, biloba.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Veronica biloba has opposite, oblong to lanceolate leaves that are typically 1 to 3 centimeters long with serrated margins and a hairy texture. The plant grows as a perennial herb reaching heights of up to 30 centimeters from a creeping rhizome. Its inflorescence consists of small, bright blue flowers with four petals that are arranged in terminal racemes. The calyx is composed of four sepals that are often covered in fine pubescence. These delicate blooms are supported by slender, upright stems that characterize the plant's overall structure.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Veronica biloba contains high concentrations of iridoid glycosides, specifically aucubin and catalpol, along with various phenylethanoid glycosides and flavonoids. These specific chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's documented anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. The presence of aucubin is particularly significant due to its potential roles in modulating immune responses and protecting cellular structures from oxidative stress. Additionally, the flavonoid content contributes to the overall therapeutic profile by scavenging free radicals within biological systems. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Veronica biloba targets various inflammatory mediators and enzymes including cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6.
