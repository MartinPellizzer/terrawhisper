Garcinia oligantha

What's the taxonomical classification of Garcinia oligantha?

Garcinia oligantha belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical progression continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Malpighiales. Further taxonomic refinement identifies the plant as a member of the family Clusiaceae, specifically falling under the genus Garcinia. Ultimately, its unique biological identity is defined by its specific designation as the species oligantha.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Garcinia oligantha targets various enzymes and signaling proteins, most notably cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, lipoxygenase (LOX), and various proinflammatory transcription factors like NF-kappaB. The plant contains bioactive xanthones and polyphenols that inhibit these specific protein targets to mitigate inflammatory responses in biological systems. By modulating these pathways, the phytochemicals can effectively reduce the production of pro-inflammatory mediators such as prostaglandins and leukotrienes. These molecular interactions are believed to contribute to the plant's potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory medicinal properties. Furthermore, some studies suggest these compounds may influence protein kinases involved in cell cycle regulation and apoptosis.

What are the therapeutic actions of Garcinia oligantha?

Garcinia oligantha has bioactive compounds, primarily xanthones and polyphenols, that exhibit significant antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory properties.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Garcinia oligantha can cause gastrointestinal distress, including nausea, abdominal pain, and diarrhea, due to its high concentration of organic acids. These acidic compounds may irritate the lining of the stomach if consumed in large quantities or on an empty stomach. Some individuals might also experience allergic skin reactions or rashes after topical application of the plant extracts. There is also a risk of drug interactions if the plant's bioactive compounds interfere with how the liver processes other medications. Long-term consumption effects remain understudied, requiring cautious approach to avoid potential toxicity.
