Botanical Identification of Caraway (Carum carvi)

To identify Caraway (Carum carvi) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Caraway belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Carum, and species Carum carvi. These classifications help identify its botanical relationships and place it within the broader context of flowering plants and the carrot family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Caraway is Carum carvi, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It belongs to the family Apiaceae and is also known by several synonyms, including Carum sativum and Carum lucidum. Common names include caraway, wild cumin, and Persian cumin, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Caraway is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and often purple-tinged. The leaves are alternate, pinnately compound, and have a strong caraway scent. The inflorescence is a compound umbel, with small white flowers that bloom in early summer. The fruit is a dry, two-lobed capsule containing small, oblong seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Caraway can be distinguished by its distinctive umbels of small white flowers and finely divided, feathery leaves. The fruit is a curved, ridged achene with a single oil duct. It differs from similar species like parsley by its stronger aroma and the presence of a single oil tube in the fruit.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Caraway is native to Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained soils and is commonly found at altitudes between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Caraway blooms from June to August, producing small white or greenish flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn, with seeds maturing by late September. For identification, harvest timing is critical, as seeds are best collected when they are dry and fully developed, typically in late September to early October.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Caraway belongs to the Apiaceae family, sharing close relations with other umbelliferous plants like parsley and dill. Its closest species include Carum minimum and Carum linneanum, which are also members of the Carum genus. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, reflecting their phylogenetic proximity within the Apiaceae family.
