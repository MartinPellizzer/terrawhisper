Musa × paradisiaca

What's the taxonomical classification of Musa × paradisiaca?

Musa × paradisiaca belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Zingiberales. As a member of the family Musaceae, it is categorized under the genus Musa, specifically identified by the species name paradisiaca.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Musa × paradisiaca has a large pseudostem composed of tightly overlapping leaf sheaths, erect leaves with prominent midribs, and a terminal inflorescence bearing fleshy berries. The plant lacks a true woody stem, instead relying on the structural integrity of these layered leaf bases to support its height. Long, oblong leaves extend from the top of the pseudostem, featuring parallel venation that facilitates water runoff. The reproductive structure consists of a large heart-shaped bud that eventually reveals rows of flowers. These flowers develop into clusters of fleshy, seedless fruits known as bananas, which hang in heavy bunches.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia and the South Pacific islands, though it is now cultivated throughout nearly all equatorial zones worldwide. Its expansive distribution is driven by its requirement for frost-free environments and high humidity found in the tropics. Major production hubs include India, China, Indonesia, and Brazil, where vast plantations dominate the landscape. It has successfully spread to parts of Africa and Central America due to its adaptability to various tropical agricultural systems. Consequently, this hybrid species can be found in almost every warm, moist climate on the planet.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Musa × paradisiaca contains medicinal compounds in its fruit, flowers, stem, sap, and leaves. The edible fruit is frequently utilized to treat gastrointestinal issues such as diarrhea and indigestion due to its high starch and pectin content. Banana flowers are often consumed in traditional medicine to regulate menstrual cycles and reduce inflammation. The sap and stem extracts are sometimes applied topically to promote wound healing or used internally to soothe stomach ulcers. Additionally, the leaves are occasionally used in various cultures to wrap medicinal pastes or to assist in reducing fever through cooling properties.

What are the pharmacological activities of Musa × paradisiaca?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, leishmanicidical, phospholipase-A2 (PL-A2) inhibitory, and red blood cell (RBC) membrane stabilization activities. Its antioxidant properties allow it to effectively neutralize free radicals and mitigate oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also exhibits leishmanicidical effects, which suggest its utility in combating parasitic infections caused by Leishmania species. Furthermore, the ability to inhibit phospholipase-A2 helps regulate inflammatory pathways by preventing the release of arachidonic acid. Finally, its capacity for RBC membrane stabilization provides a protective mechanism for cellular integrity against various stressors.

As discussed in a study, Musa × paradisiaca fruit peels contain a compound with anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, red blood cell membrane stabilization, and phospholipase-A2 inhibitory effects. The hexane fraction of the methanol extract showed significant oedema reduction in rats at a dose of 200 mg/kg. Characterization of the steroid compound included a UV-VIS spectrum at 222 nm absorbance and FTIR absorption at 2922, 2855, 1707, 1643, 1453, and 1375 cm-1. This compound demonstrated stronger affinities for PL-A2 at -7.6 kcal/mol and NF-κB at -6.0 kcal/mol than the drugs indomethacin and ibuprofen. Additionally, the compound displayed Fe3+ reduction and the ability to scavenge free radical molecules and limit RBC haemolysis.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, inflammatory disease, and oedema. Its bioactive compounds act as natural agents to reduce swelling in various parts of the body. By calming the immune response, it helps manage the symptoms associated with chronic inflammatory disease. The plant also assists in reducing oedema by helping to regulate fluid retention in tissues. These medicinal properties make it a valuable resource for addressing systemic and localized swelling.

As discussed in a study, a compound with anti-inflammatory properties was isolated from Musa × paradisiaca fruit peels. Characterization of the steroid compound via UV-VIS showed absorbance at 222 nm, while FTIR absorption occurred at 2922, 2855, 1707, 1643, 1453, and 1375 cm-1. In vivo assays using the formalin-induced oedema model demonstrated that the hexane fraction provided significant oedema reduction at 200 mg/kg. The compound exhibited stronger affinities for PL-A2 at -7.6 kcal/mol and NF-κB at -6.0 kcal/mol than indomethacin and ibuprofen. This research highlights Musa × paradisiaca fruit peels as a sustainable source of a lead molecule for managing inflammatory disease.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions, digestive discomfort, or interference with blood clotting medication if consumed in excessive amounts. Individuals with a latex allergy may experience cross-reactivity, which can lead to itching or hives. High potassium content in the fruit can be dangerous for those with kidney disease who cannot regulate electrolyte levels properly. Some people might also experience bloating or gas due to the high fiber content found in the fruit. Monitoring portion sizes is recommended for sensitive populations to avoid these physiological responses.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Allergic reaction
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Nausea
  • Diarrhea
  • Constipation
