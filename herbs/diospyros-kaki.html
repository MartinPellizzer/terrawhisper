Diospyros kaki

What's the taxonomical classification of Diospyros kaki?

Diospyros kaki belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the order Ericales and belongs to the family Ebenaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Diospyros, with its specific designation being the species kaki.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Diospyros kaki has simple, entire, and opposite leaves that are oblong to ovate in shape with a leathery texture and a glossy dark green upper surface. These leaves are typically arranged in pairs along the woody stems and possess short petioles. The plant is a deciduous tree that can reach significant heights, characterized by smooth, greyish bark. During the flowering stage, it produces small, bell-shaped, cream-colored flowers that grow in clusters. The most distinctive morphological feature is the large, fleshy, globose fruit which transitions from green to a bright orange color upon ripening.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning the regions of China, Japan, Korea, and parts of Southeast Asia. While its ancestral roots are firmly planted in these temperate and subtropical zones, it has been widely naturalized across many other parts of the world. Cultivation has spread to much broader areas including North America, Europe, and various Mediterranean climates due to its popularity as a fruit tree. Because it thrives in diverse environments, you can find it growing in both domestic gardens and commercial orchards across multiple continents. What are the pharmacological activities of Diospyros kaki?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anticancer pharmacological activities derived from its diverse phytochemical profile. The presence of polyphenols and flavonoids enables it to combat various pathogens by inhibiting microbial growth. Its potent antioxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant anti-inflammatory properties that can mitigate swelling and immune responses. Research also indicates that its bioactive compounds possess anticancer potential by interfering with the progression of malignant cells.

As discussed in a study, Diospyros kaki fruit aqueous extract (DKFAE) possesses antidiarrheal, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties. The DKFAE is rich in tannins and contains 16 identified phenolic compounds, with quinic acid being the main one. In an in vivo study involving 60 male rats divided into 6 groups of 10 animals each, DKFAE was administered at doses of 100, 200, and 400 mg/kg b.w. p.o. The administration of DKFAE significantly decreased gastrointestinal transit in a dose-dependent manner. Furthermore, DKFAE pretreatment counteracted oxidative stress deregulation and provided protection against castor oil-induced diarrhea and intestinal fluid accumulation.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 19 alpha-hydroxy ursolic acid, ursolic acid, kaempferol, quercetin, and chlorogenic acid. These specific phytochemicals are known for their diverse biological activities, including anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Ursolic acid and its derivative, 19 alpha-hydroxy ursolic acid, often contribute to cellular health and metabolic regulation. The flavonoids kaempferol and quercetin serve as potent agents in neutralizing free radicals within the body. Additionally, chlorogenic acid provides significant metabolic support and helps manage oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the leaves of Diospyros kaki contain five triterpenoid compounds consisting of alpha-amyrin (A), uvaol (UV), ursolic acid (UA), 19 alpha-hydroxy ursolic acid (HU), and 19 alpha,24-dihydroxy ursolic acid (DHU). These compounds can suppress superoxide generation induced by N-formyl-methionyl-leucyl-phenylalanine (fMLP) in a concentration-dependent manner. When cells are stimulated by pho rbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA), UA, HU, and DHU suppress superoxide generation, while A and UV provide no effect. Furthermore, UA, HU, and DHU dose-dependently suppress the fMLP-induced tyrosyl phosphorylation of 45 kDa proteins in human neutrophils. These triterpenoid compounds from Diospyros kaki may have pharmaceutical applications due to their ability to suppress stimulus-induced superoxide generation and tyrosyl phosphorylation.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause constipation, nausea, abdominal pain, gastric obstruction, and diarrhea due to the high concentration of tannins found in its unripe fruit. Consuming the astringent flesh of an unripe persimmon can lead to the formation of a hard mass in the digestive tract. This mass often results in a serious condition known as gastric obstruction, which may require medical intervention. While some individuals might experience nausea or abdominal pain after ingestion, others may suffer from diarrhea if the fruit is consumed in certain ways. Careful attention should be paid to the ripeness of the fruit to avoid these adverse gastrointestinal effects.
