Botanical Identification of Basil (Ocimum basilicum)

To identify Basil (Ocimum basilicum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Basil (Ocimum basilicum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Basil, or Ocimum basilicum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Ocimum, and species Ocimum basilicum. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the mint family, reflecting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Basil is Ocimum basilicum, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the Lamiaceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Ocimum marum and Ocimum basilicum var. purpureum. Common names such as sweet basil, basilica, and holy basil reflect its widespread use and cultural significance across regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Basil (Ocimum basilicum) is an annual herb with a branching stem that grows upright to semi-woody. Its roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to nutrient-rich soils. Leaves are opposite, ovate to lanceolate, with a fragrant, glossy green surface. The inflorescence consists of whorled clusters of small, pale blue or white flowers. Each flower is bilaterally symmetrical, and the fruit is a small, dry, oblong capsule containing tiny, black seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Basil can be distinguished by its opposite, egg-shaped leaves with slightly serrated edges and a strong aromatic scent. It has square, hairy stems and produces small, pale purple flowers. It is often confused with oregano, but basil has lighter green leaves and a more delicate aroma. Its leaves are broader and less lobed compared to similar species.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Ocimum basilicum is native to the Mediterranean region, parts of Africa, and southern Asia. It thrives in warm climates with mild winters. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils. It typically grows at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Basil (Ocimum basilicum) typically blooms in late summer to early fall, around 60-90 days after germination. Seed formation occurs 2-3 weeks after blooming. For identification purposes, the plant is most identifiable for harvesting during the vegetative stage, just before flowering, when leaves are fully developed and aromatic.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Ocimum basilicum is closely related to other members of the Lamiaceae family, particularly species like Ocimum americanum and Ocimum sambaccanum. It shares key characteristics with its genus, such as square stems and opposite leaves. These relationships help in distinguishing it from similar species within the same family.
