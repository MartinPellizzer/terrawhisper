Angelica acutiloba

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica acutiloba?

Angelica acuutiloba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Apiales and falls under the family Apiaceae. Within this family, it is categorized under the genus Angelica, specifically identified as the species acuutiloba.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica acuutiloba has pinnately compound leaves characterized by sharply acute, serrated lobes and a hollow, ribbed stem structure. The foliage typically presents a bright green color with a delicate, feathery texture that distinguishes its specific leaf shape. Small, white flowers grow in dense, umbrella-like umbels situated at the top of the plant. These inflorescences are supported by sturdy peduncles that rise above the primary leaf canopy. The root system is robust and fleshy, providing the necessary stability and nutrient storage for this perennial species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of western China, specifically occurring within the provinces of Gansu, Qinghai, Sichuan, and Tibet. It primarily thrives in high-altitude environments found along the edges of mountain slopes and in subalpine meadows. The species prefers cool, moist habitats that are characteristic of the Eastern Tibetan Plateau. Its specific distribution is closely tied to the unique climatic conditions and soil compositions of these high-elevation ecosystems. Consequently, finding this species requires searching within the rugged, alpine terrain of central and western China.

What are the pharmacological activities of Angelica acutiloba?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antiproliferative, neuroprotective, and uterine contractile activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, which mitigates oxidative stress throughout the body. The anti-inflammatory actions assist in reducing swelling and systemic inflammation in various tissues. Furthermore, its antiproliferative effects show promise in inhibiting the uncontrolled growth of certain cell types. The neuroprotective capabilities suggest a role in safeguarding neurons against degenerative damage. Finally, the plant exhibits uterine contractile activity, making it relevant in reproductive health contexts.

As discussed in a study, Z-ligustilide is an active ingredient isolated from the medicinal plant Angelica acuutiloba. This substance significantly rescued UVB-induced NHEKs damage in a dosage-dependent manner. Pretreatment with Z-lig inhibited UVB-induced ROS production in NHEKs by upregulating the Nrf2/HO-1 pathway. Furthermore, Z-lig reduced the production of NF-κB-dependent inflammatory mediators, specifically IL-6, IL-8, and MCP-1, at both mRNA and protein levels. In the presence of Z-lig, the UVB-induced nuclear translocation of the NF-κB subunit p65 was abolished and IκBα degradation was suppressed.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds butylidene phthalide, ligustilide, ferulic acid, imperatoriin, and immunostimulating polysaccharide (ATP). These bioactive substances work through various biological pathways to provide therapeutic benefits. Ligustilide and butylidene phthalide are often studied for their roles in cardiovascular and neurological health. Ferulic acid provides antioxidant properties, while imperatoriin contributes to the plant's diverse chemical profile. Additionally, the presence of immunostimulating polysaccharide (ATP) supports the regulation of the immune system.

As discussed in a study, 47 chemical compounds were identified in the essential oils from the radix of Angelica acuutiloba. The total content of these identified constituents in the essential oils was 99.02%. Among these components, Ligustilide is a main constituent at 22.8%. Additionally, Butylidene phthalide was identified as a main constituent at 19.5%.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating acute inflammatory reaction, oxidative stress, skin inflammation, ovulatory disorders, and uterine contraction. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals within the body. The plant also demonstrates significant efficacy in soothing skin inflammation and managing an acute inflammatory reaction. In reproductive health, it is frequently applied to address various ovulatory disorders. Additionally, its pharmacological properties can help regulate or stimulate uterine contraction.

As discussed in a study, Tooki extract inhibited slightly an acute inflammatory reaction. This extract had no effect on increase of cutaneous temperature and wound healing in rats. The prescription Shiunko contains about 0.04% of Tooki extract. The effect of Shiunko was similar to that of Shikon ether extract, but the activity was slightly more potent than that of Shikon. Shiunko inhibited rubor, calor and tumor in acute inflammatory reaction and promoted wound healing.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause various adverse reactions including nausea, increased bleeding risk, digestive upset, photosensitivity, dizziness, diarrhea, and headache. Some individuals may experience significant gastrointestinal distress through symptoms like nausea and diarrhea. The chemical properties of the herb may also lead to digestive upset or a noticeable headache. Users should be cautious regarding an increased bleeding risk, especially if taking other medications. Additionally, skin sensitivity to sunlight can occur, resulting in photosensitivity, or one might feel lightheaded due to dizziness.
