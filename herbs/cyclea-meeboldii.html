What's the taxonomical classification of Cyclea meeboldii?

Cyclea meeboldii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Ranunculales. Within this order, the plant is a member of the family Menispermaceae. Finally, it is categorized under the genus Cyclea, with the specific designation of the species meeboldii.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Cyclea meeboldii targets various inflammatory enzymes and oxidative stress regulators, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and lipoxygenase (LOX). The bioactive alkaloids and flavonoids present in the plant exert these effects by modulating signal transduction pathways associated with the production of pro-inflammatory mediators. Through the suppression of these protein targets, the plant effectively reduces the synthesis of prostaglandins and leukotrienes in biological systems. Furthermore, its chemical constituents interact with antioxidant enzymes to mitigate cellular damage caused by reactive oxygen species. These multifaceted molecular interactions underpin the plant's documented anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective medicinal properties.

For internal issues, the plant is sometimes prepared as a decoction to alleviate digestive distress and abdominal pain. It is also valued for its ability to reduce swelling and soothe localized redness.

These traditional applications highlight the plant's role in managing both cutaneous and gastrointestinal ailments.

    What are the herbal preparations of Cyclea meeboldii?

    Cyclea meeboldii is prepared primarily as a decoction or infusion by boiling or steeping the fresh or dried leaves in water for medicinal use. These liquid extracts are often consumed orally to address various traditional health concerns.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Cyclea meeboldii can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. While some traditional uses exist, the plant contains various alkaloids that may trigger adverse physiological responses in sensitive individuals. Contact with the foliage or sap might lead to localized redness, itching, or dermatitis on the skin. If consumed, it could result in nausea, vomiting, or other unpredictable digestive issues. Due to these risks, cautious handling and professional medical advice are necessary before any direct exposure.
