Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Canadian Collinsonia is Collinsonia canadensis, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Hydrastis canadensis, which reflects its historical classification. Common names such as blue cohosh and smilacina are widely recognized, while regional and historical names highlight its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Canadian Collinsonia (Collinsonia canadensis) is a perennial herb with a rhizomatous root system. It has upright, branching stems that are hairy and often reddish. Its leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptic, with serrated margins. The plant produces a terminal inflorescence of showy, bell-shaped flowers in shades of pink to purple. The fruit is a capsule containing small, black seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Collinsonia canadensis can be distinguished by its narrow, lance-shaped leaves with serrated edges and its clusters of bell-shaped, pinkish-white flowers. It also has a distinctive, aromatic root that is often used in herbal preparations. The plant typically grows in moist, wooded areas and has a unique, somewhat woolly texture on its stems.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Collinsonia canadensis is native to eastern North America, primarily found in the eastern United States and southeastern Canada. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and is typically found at low to mid-elevation areas in woodlands or along stream banks.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Collinsonia canadensis blooms from June to August, producing showy pink to red flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, with seeds maturing by October. For identification, the plant is most identifiable during its flowering period, typically June to August, when its distinctive features are most visible.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Collinsonia canadensis is closely related to species in the genus Collinsonia, which is part of the family Styracaceae. It shares key family traits such as opposite leaves and capsule fruits, distinguishing it from other genera in the same family. Its closest relatives include other North American species with similar morphological and chemical characteristics.
