What's the taxonomical classification of Piptadeniastrum africanum?

Piptadenia strum africanum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Further taxonomic refinement places the plant in the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Finally, its specific identity is defined by its classification within the genus Piptadenia strum and its unique species designation, africanum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Piptadeniastrum africanum has an upright, woody growth habit characterized by simple, opposite leaves that are elliptic to oblong in shape with entire margins and prominent pinnate venation. Small, fragrant flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters or solitary arrangements. These blossoms lead to the development of small, fleshy fruits that are essential to the plant's reproductive cycle. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apiagenin, liquiritigenin, luteolin, naringenin, and oleanolic acid. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the overall therapeutic potential of the species through various biological mechanisms. For instance, the flavonoids apiagenin, luteolin, and naringenin are frequently studied for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Liquiritigenin also plays a significant role in modulating cellular responses within the body. Furthermore, the triterpenoid oleanolic acid is recognized for its ability to support liver health and reduce oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the stem bark of Piptadeniastrum africanum contains saponin at 867.42 mg quillaja equivalent/g and tannin at 33.81 mg catechin equivalent/g in the methanol extract. Characterized compounds include multiple dihydroxy-trimethoxy(iso)flavone isomers, loliolide, eriodictyol, naringenin, luteolin, chrysoeriol, apiigenin, and liquiritigenin. The methanol extract demonstrated inhibitory activity against acetylcholinesterase at 4.88 mg galantamine equivalent (GALAE)/g extract, butyrylcholinesterase at 5.37 mg GALAE/g extract, and tyrosinase at 154.86 mg kojic acid equivalent/g extract. Additionally, the ethyl acetate extract inhibited α-glucosidase at 15.22 mmol acarbose equivalent/g extract. Antioxidant properties of the methanol extract were measured via DPPH at 493.87 mg Trolox equivalent/g extract, ABTS at 818.12 mg Trolox equivalent/g extract, CUPRAC at 953.07 mg Trolox equivalent/g extract, and FRAP at 732.19 mg Trolox equivalent/g extract.
