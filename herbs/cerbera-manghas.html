Cerbera manghas

What's the taxonomical classification of Cerbera manghas?

Cerbera manghas belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Gentianaales. This plant is a member of the family Apocynaceae, falling under the genus Cerbera, and is specifically identified by its species name, manghas.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cerbera manghas has evergreen leaves that are elliptical or obovate in shape, measuring up to 15 centimeters in length with a glossy, dark green upper surface and a paler underside. The plant features a woody trunk that supports a spreading canopy of dense foliage. It produces large, white, fragrant flowers that possess five spreading petals and a distinct yellow center. These blossoms eventually develop into large, oval, green drupes that resemble small mangoes. The seeds within these fruits are enclosed in a fibrous husk and are capable of floating on water to facilitate dispersal.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical coastal regions of the Indian Ocean, specifically spanning from the eastern coast of Africa to Southeast Asia and parts of the Pacific. It primarily flourishes in maritime environments along the shores of Madagascar, Mauritius, and the Seychelles. Its distribution extends northward through the coasts of India and into the tropical islands of the Indo-Malayan archipelago. Many populations are also found throughout the coastal belts of southern China and northern Australia. Because it thrives in sandy, saline soils, its presence is strictly limited to low-lying coastal zones.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cerbera manghas contains medicinal compounds found primarily in its seeds, bark, and leaves. The seeds are the most potent part of the plant and are historically utilized for their potent purgative and emetic properties. In traditional medicine, extracts from the bark are sometimes applied topically to treat skin conditions or used in decoctions for internal ailments. The leaves can also be processed to serve as a treatment for various inflammatory issues. However, extreme caution is required during use because these plant parts contain highly toxic cardiac glycosides.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Cerbera manghas targets cardiac glycoside-sensitive sodium-potassium adenosine triphosphatase (Na+/K+-ATPase) pumps to inhibit cellular ion transport. The primary bioactive compounds in this plant, such as cerberin, act as potent inhibitors of this transmembrane enzyme found in myocardial cells. By binding to the alpha subunit of the Na+/K+-ATPase, these molecules alter intracellular sodium concentrations and subsequently increase calcium availability through the sodium-calcium exchanger. This specific mechanism of action results in enhanced cardiac contractility, which explains its traditional use in treating certain heart conditions. Furthermore, the modulation of these ion channels can trigger secondary signaling pathways involving protein kinase C and intracellular calcium signaling.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cerbera manghas?

Cerbera manghas has cardiotoxic, emetic, and purgative therapeutic actions due to the presence of cardiac glycosides like cerberin. These potent compounds act by inhibiting the sodium-potassium pump in cardiac cells, which can increase the force of heart contractions but poses a high risk of toxicity. When administered in controlled doses, the plant's extracts can induce vomiting through its emetic properties. Additionally, the chemical constituents act as a strong laxative by stimulating intestinal movements, resulting in a purgative effect. Because of the narrow margin between a medicinal dose and a lethal one, careful application is required to manage these intense physiological responses.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe poisoning characterized by gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. The toxic compounds in the seeds can further lead to serious neurological issues like seizures. Cardiovascular complications are also common, including cardiac arrhythmia, bradycardia, and hypotension. In many cases of ingestion, these physiological disturbances can escalate rapidly. Ultimately, the toxicity of the plant is high enough to result in death.
