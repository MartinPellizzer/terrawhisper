What to know about Chrysanthemum morifolium (chrysanth) before using it medicinally

Chrysanthemum morifolium, commonly known as chrysanth, is an herb that has been extensively studied for its impressive health benefits, particularly its ability to reduce inflammation and improve cardiovascular health.

From a horticultural standpoint, Chrysanthemum morifolium is a versatile and widely cultivated ornamental plant that thrives in well-drained soil and full sun, making it a popular choice for gardens and floral arrangements. Botanically speaking, the herb belongs to the Asteraceae family and is characterized by its daisy-like flowers and strong, pungent scent.

Historically, Chrysanthemum morifolium has been referenced in traditional Chinese medicine for over 3,000 years, with its flowers and leaves used to treat a variety of ailments, including fever, rheumatism, and digestive issues.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Chrysanthemum morifolium.

What are the medicinal properties of Chrysanthemum morifolium?

Chrysanthemum morifolium helps with fever reduction, headaches, and inflammatory conditions. Its extracts are also used to treat skin disorders, such as eczema and acne. The plant is also used in traditional medicine to treat respiratory issues, including coughs and bronchitis.

The active constituents of Chrysanthemum morifolium responsible for its medicinal properties include flavonoids, terpenoids, and alkaloids. These compounds have anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and antioxidant effects, which contribute to the plant's therapeutic benefits. These constituents also have potential antiviral properties.

The most commonly used parts of Chrysanthemum morifolium for medicinal purposes are the flowers, leaves, and roots. The flowers are used to treat fever and headaches, while the leaves are used to treat skin disorders and respiratory issues. The roots are used to treat inflammatory conditions and to enhance the effects of other medicinal compounds.

Improper use of Chrysanthemum morifolium can cause allergic reactions, such as skin rashes, itching, and anaphylaxis. The plant's extracts may also interact with certain medications, including blood thinners, and may worsen underlying health conditions, such as kidney or liver disease.

To use Chrysanthemum morifolium medicinally, it is essential to follow the recommended dosages and preparation methods. Pregnant women and individuals with certain health conditions should consult a healthcare professional before using the plant. Additionally, individuals taking medications should monitor their blood pressure and blood sugar levels while using the plant.

What are the horticulural aspects of Chrysanthemum morifolium?

Chrysanthemum morifolium grow best in well-draining soil with a pH between 5.5 and 6.5. They require full sun to partial shade, with temperatures between 65°F and 70°F (18°C and 21°C) for optimal growth.

For planting, choose a location with good air circulation and space chrysanth plants 12 to 18 inches apart. Plant the roots of the crown 1 to 2 inches deeper than the soil surface, and water thoroughly after planting. Chrysanthemum seeds can also be directly sown in the ground in the spring or fall.

Chrysanthemum flowers can be harvested in the late summer to early fall, when the blooms are in full color. Cut off the flower stalk at a 45-degree angle, leaving about 2 inches of stem attached to the plant. Remove any spent flowers to promote further blooming.

Common pests and diseases affecting chrysanth include aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites, as well as root rot, powdery mildew, and leaf spot caused by fungal infections. Regularly inspect the plant for signs of infestation or disease, and take action promptly if necessary to prevent further damage.

The plant's flowers are the most striking feature, appearing in late summer to early fall, consisting of a central disc of tiny florets surrounded by a ring of larger ray florets.
