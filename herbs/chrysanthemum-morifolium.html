Chrysanthemum, scientifically known as chrysanthemum morifolium, is a flowering plant widely recognized as a medicinal herb and adaptogen with a rich history in traditional medicine.

It is valued for its potential to reduce inflammation, support immune function, and alleviate symptoms of colds and allergies due to its high content of flavonoids and volatile oils. In traditional Chinese medicine, it has been used for centuries to treat fevers, headaches, and respiratory conditions, while in Japan, it is commonly consumed as a tea to promote relaxation and mental clarity. Notably, its unique camphor-like aroma and the presence of sesquiterpene lactones set it apart, making it a versatile and historically significant botanical in both culinary and therapeutic contexts.

Scientific and Botanical Profile

Chrysanthemum, with botanical name Chrysanthemum morifolium, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

It is also referred to as Mums, Flower of the Sun, Mum, Chrysanthemum Morifolium, Chrysanthemum Flower, and Chinese Chrysanthemum, reflecting its widespread cultural and horticultural significance. Native to East Asia, particularly China, Japan, and Korea, this plant has been cultivated for centuries for its ornamental value and medicinal properties. Morphologically, it is a herbaceous perennial with bushy, often multi-branched stems, and it produces a variety of flower heads ranging from single to double blooms in colors such as yellow, white, pink, red, and orange.

The plant typically grows to a height of 30-90 cm, featuring pinnately lobed leaves and showy composite flowers composed of ray and disk florets.

History and Cultural Relevance

Chrysanthemum was used in ancient China as a medicinal plant in traditional Chinese medicine, where it was valued for its ability to treat fevers, headaches, and inflammation.

Its use dates back over 3,000 years, with historical texts like the Shennong Ben Cao Jing documenting its therapeutic properties. In Japan, chrysanthemums have deep cultural significance, symbolizing longevity, rejuvenation, and the imperial family, and are prominently featured in festivals and traditional ceremonies. The plant also plays a role in Korean and Vietnamese folk traditions, where it is used in rituals to ward off evil spirits and promote good fortune.

Today, chrysanthemum tea and extracts are still used in modern herbal remedies for their anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, highlighting its enduring relevance in both medicine and culture.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Chrysanthemum contains a diverse array of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids such as quercetin and kaempferol, which are known for their antioxidant properties, as well as alkaloids, terpenes, and essential oils that contribute to its therapeutic effects.

It is also rich in vitamins like vitamin C and several B-complex vitamins, along with minerals such as calcium, iron, and potassium, making it a nutrient-dense plant. The presence of these compounds helps neutralize free radicals, reduce oxidative stress, and support immune function. Additionally, the essential oils in chrysanthemum can have anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects when ingested or applied topically.

Overall, its chemical composition and nutritional profile make it a valuable plant for both culinary and medicinal purposes.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Chrysanthemum morifolium has been widely recognized for its diverse medicinal properties and health benefits, particularly in supporting the respiratory, cardiovascular, and immune systems.

It contains compounds such as flavonoids and sesquiterpene lactones, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antiviral effects, making it effective in reducing symptoms of colds, allergies, and respiratory infections. Compared to similar herbs like echinacea or ginseng, chrysanthemum morifolium offers a more targeted approach to respiratory health, with its unique ability to soothe inflammation in the airways. Its potency in reducing fever and promoting detoxification also sets it apart, as it can be used both internally and externally for skin conditions.

Overall, while similar herbs may provide broad immune support, chrysanthemum morifolium stands out for its specific benefits to the respiratory and circulatory systems, making it a valuable component in traditional and modern herbal medicine.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Chrysanthemum morifolium has a variety of forms available, including fresh flowers, dried flowers, tinctures, powders, essential oils, and capsules, each offering different methods of preparation and application.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water, or as a decoction by simmering them for a longer period, while infusions involve using cold or room temperature water for a gentler extraction. For topical use, the plant can be applied as a poultice or diluted essential oil to treat skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 2-4 grams of dried flowers per cup of tea, taken 2-3 times daily, while children should only use it if deemed safe by a healthcare professional.

Due to its potency, it is advised to use chrysanthemum morifolium sparingly, with a duration of use not exceeding a few weeks unless under medical supervision.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Chrysanthemum morifolium can be used as a medicinal plant for its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and mild sedative properties, but it should be used with caution.

While generally considered safe when consumed in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, stomach upset, or diarrhea in some individuals. It may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and sedatives, potentially enhancing their effects and increasing the risk of adverse reactions. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid chrysanthemum morifolium due to limited safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to follow recommended dosages, consult with a healthcare professional, and discontinue use if any adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Chrysanthemum morifolium grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 0, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without waterlogging. It thrives in temperate climates and benefits from mulching to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Regular pruning and fertilization during the growing season promote bushier growth and enhance flower production. Harvesting is best done in the early morning when the flowers are fully open but still firm, using sharp shears to cut the stems just below the flower heads.

To preserve potency, harvested chrysanthemums should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area or in a dehydrator at low temperatures, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to maintain their medicinal properties.
