Botanical Identification of Mexican Mint (Plectranthus barbatus)

To identify Mexican Mint (Plectranthus barbatus) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Mexican Mint, or Plectranthus barbatus, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Plectranthus, and species Plectranthus barbatus. This classification places it within the mint family, known for aromatic herbs used in traditional medicine.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Mexican Mint is Plectranthus barbatus, first described by the botanist A. Rich in 1820. It has several synonyms, including Coleus barbatus and Plectranthus scutatus, reflecting historical classification changes. Common names include Mexican Mint, Blue Mint, and Indian Mint, while regional names vary across Latin America. Historically, it was also known as "Spanish Mint" in some areas.

Morphological Characteristics

Mexican Mint, or Plectranthus barbatus, is an upright, herbaceous perennial with a fibrous root system. Its stems are square and hairy, often branching from the base. Leaves are opposite, ovate to elliptic, with a serrated margin and a fragrant, minty aroma. The inflorescence consists of dense clusters of small, tubular flowers, typically purple or pink. The fruit is a dry, two-lobed capsule containing small, black seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Mexican Mint, or Plectranthus barbatus, can be distinguished by its square stems, opposite leaves with a velvety underside, and small purple flowers. It differs from similar species like Plectranthus amboinicus by its stronger mint scent and distinct leaf texture. The presence of a hairy stem and specific leaf shape also aid in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Mexican Mint is native to Central America and northern South America. It thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil. It typically grows at altitudes between 500 and 1,500 meters. It is commonly found in shaded, moist areas near streams or in forest understories.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Mexican Mint blooms from late spring to early summer, producing small purple flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested in early to mid-summer when the leaves are most aromatic and the plant is at its peak.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Mexican Mint, or Plectranthus barbatus, is closely related to species within the Plectranthus genus. It shares key characteristics with other members of the Lamiaceae family, such as square stems and opposite leaves. These traits help distinguish it from other mint-like plants commonly used in herbal medicine.
