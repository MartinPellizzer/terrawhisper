Botanical Identification of Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla)

To identify Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Chamomile, or Matricaria chamomilla, is classified within the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Matricaria, and species Matricaria chamomilla. This classification reflects its position as a flowering plant in the sunflower family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Matricaria chamomilla is the scientific name for chamomile, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is also known by synonyms such as Anthemis chamomilla and Chamaemelum nobile. Common names include German chamomile and wild chamomile, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is an annual herb with a slender, branched stem. Its leaves are alternate, hairy, and have a serrated margin. The plant produces a single, flat-topped inflorescence with small, white flowers surrounded by pale yellow petals. The fruit is a dry capsule containing tiny, oval seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) can be distinguished by its daisy-like white flowers with a yellow center, opposite linear leaves, and a strong, sweet scent. It is often confused with other chamomile species, but its distinct leaf shape and floral structure help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is native to temperate regions of Europe and western Asia. It prefers mild climates with moderate rainfall and well-drained, loamy soils. It thrives at elevations between 600 and 1,500 meters, often found in meadows and along roadsides.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between May and September, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs after flowering, usually in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is when the flowers are fully open but before the seeds begin to form.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is closely related to other species within the Asteraceae family, particularly those in the genus Anthemis. It shares key characteristics with its relatives, such as composite flower heads and aromatic compounds. Its phylogenetic placement highlights its evolutionary ties to other chamomile and related flowering plants.
