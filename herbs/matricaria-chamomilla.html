Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is a widely used herbal remedy known for its calming and anti-inflammatory properties, often employed to treat digestive issues, sleep disorders, and skin conditions.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Chamomile (Matricaria Chamomilla)?

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in herbal medicine for its calming and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Chamomile is Matricaria chamomilla. This plant belongs to the family Asteraceae, order Asterales, class Magnoliopsida, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. It is commonly known as chamomile in Europe and America, and as nan-cha or yin-cha in China. The synonyms for this plant include Matricaria recutita (botanical) and German chamomile, wild chamomile, and apple of Peru (regional).

Plant Description and Morphology

Chamomile is an annual herb that grows up to 30-60 cm tall, with a straight, branched stem covered in fine hairs.

Its leaves are alternate, broadly oval to spoon-shaped, with a serrated margin and a soft, velvety texture. The flowers are small, daisy-like blooms with a yellow center and white petals, typically 2-3 cm in diameter, and emit a sweet, apple-like fragrance. The root system is fibrous and shallow, supporting the plant's growth in well-drained soil.

The plant has a mild, aromatic scent, with a slightly bitter and spicy taste.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chamomile is native to temperate regions of Europe, particularly southern Germany, and has also been found in parts of northern Africa and western Asia.

It thrives in areas with well-drained soils and full sun exposure, commonly found in meadows, grasslands, and along roadsides. Its geographic distribution extends across the Mediterranean basin, the British Isles, and parts of the United States, especially in the Pacific Northwest. Chamomile prefers mild, temperate climates with moderate rainfall and good air circulation.

It grows best in ecological conditions that support its rapid growth cycle, typically in open, sunny habitats with low competition from other plants.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The aerial parts of Chamomile, including the flowers and leaves, are primarily used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids such as apigenin and quercetin, essential oils like bisabolol and chamazulene, and terpenoids, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic effects.

Pharmacological Properties

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, anxiolytic, and sedative effects, primarily attributed to its active constituents such as bisabolol, chamazulene, and flavonoids.

Mechanisms of Action

Chamomile exerts its effects primarily through the bioactive compounds such as flavonoids and terpenoids, which interact with the central nervous system to produce calming effects and reduce inflammation by modulating neurotransmitter activity and inhibiting pro-inflammatory pathways.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is widely used in herbal medicine for its calming, anti-inflammatory, and antispasmodic properties, commonly indicated for conditions such as insomnia, digestive disorders, anxiety, and skin irritations.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Chamomile is typically prepared as a tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water, though it is also available in capsule, tincture, and liquid extract forms for various applications in herbal medicine.

Dosage and Administration

The typical adult dosage of chamomile for mild digestive discomfort is 200-400 mg of standardized extract, taken twice daily, while for sleep support, a 2-3 mL tincture or 2-3 cups of brewed tea may be consumed 30 minutes before bedtime.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, chamomile is generally considered safe, but it may cause allergic reactions, gastrointestinal discomfort, or interact with certain medications, particularly in higher doses or with prolonged use.

Drug Interactions

Chamomile may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, sedatives, and drugs metabolized by the liver, potentially increasing the risk of side effects or reducing the effectiveness of these medications.

Toxicity and Precautions

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly those with allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family, and should be avoided during pregnancy and by people with known sensitivities to its components.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, with harvesting occurring primarily during the early morning when the essential oils are most concentrated.

Processing and Extraction

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is typically processed by harvesting the flowering heads just before they open, then drying them in a low-temperature environment to preserve their active compounds, followed by extraction using methods such as steam distillation or solvent extraction to obtain essential oils and other bioactive constituents.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) should be stored in a cool, dry, and dark place in an airtight container to preserve its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly stored.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Numerous scientific studies have investigated the pharmacological properties of Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla), demonstrating its anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and anxiolytic effects, with clinical trials supporting its use in managing conditions such as insomnia, digestive disorders, and inflammatory skin conditions.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) has been traditionally used for centuries in herbal medicine to treat a variety of ailments, including digestive issues, insomnia, and skin irritations, with its calming and anti-inflammatory properties being central to its historical applications.

Regulatory Status

In the United States, chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) is considered a dietary supplement and is regulated by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994, which means it is not subject to pre-market approval but is required to comply with good manufacturing practices and labeling regulations.
