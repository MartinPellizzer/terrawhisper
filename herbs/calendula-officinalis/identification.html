Botanical Identification of Marigold (Calendula officinalis)

To identify Marigold (Calendula officinalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Marigold, or Calendula officinalis, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Calendula, and species Calendula officinalis. These classifications reflect its position within the plant kingdom and its relationship to other flowering plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Calendula officinalis is the scientific name for marigold, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Calendula arvensis and Calendula indica. Common names such as pot marigold and garden marigold reflect its widespread use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Marigold (Calendula officinalis) is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branched stems that are hairy and aromatic. Its leaves are alternate, lance-shaped, and have serrated edges. The plant produces a single flower head at the end of each stem, consisting of yellow ray florets and brown disk florets. The fruit is a dry, cylindrical capsule that releases small, black seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Marigold, or Calendula officinalis, can be distinguished by its bright orange to yellow flowers with radiating petals and a central disk. It has hairy stems and alternate leaves that are lance-shaped with rough edges. It differs from similar species like Tagetes by its more compact flower heads and distinct leaf venation.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Calendula officinalis is native to southern Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with full sun. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It typically grows at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Marigold (Calendula officinalis) blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between May and September, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to October. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during full bloom, when the flowers are fully open and vibrant.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Calendula officinalis belongs to the Asteraceae family, closely related to other members like Chamomile and Tanacetum. It shares key traits such as composite flower heads and aromatic compounds. Its closest relatives include species within the tribe Anthemideae, which are characterized by similar morphology and chemical profiles.
