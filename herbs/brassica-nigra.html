Black mustard

Brassica nigra

Black Mustard (Brassica nigra) is a member of the Brassicaceae family and has been traditionally used for respiratory conditions, as a digestive aid, and for skin conditions. The plant parts commonly used include the seeds, leaves, and flowers, and it has a long history of use in various cultural and regional practices across Europe and Asia.

Botanical Identification

Black Mustard (Brassica nigra) is a flowering plant in the Brassicaceae family. It is also known as wild mustard or black cumin in some regions. An annual herb native to Europe and western Asia, it has hairy stems, narrow leaves, and small yellow flowers. Its scientific name is consistently used in herbal literature, with no widely recognized synonyms.

Active Compounds

Black Mustard (Brassica nigra) contains key bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, and tannins. These compounds work synergistically to provide anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. Their combined action enhances the herb's therapeutic potential in herbal medicine.

Preparation Methods

Tincture: Used for respiratory and digestive ailments due to its antimicrobial properties.

Culinary use: Used for general health and digestion due to its high nutrient content.

Poultice: Used for skin infections and wounds due to its anti-inflammatory properties.

Safety Profile

Black Mustard (Brassica nigra) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses. However, it may cause gastrointestinal irritation and is contraindicated in individuals with known allergies to the Brassicaceae family. Long-term use should be avoided due to potential toxicity.
