Scientific and Botanical Profile

Cornus Mas, with botanical name Cornus mas, is a deciduous shrub or small tree belonging to the family Cornaceae, commonly known as the dogwood family.

Native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of northern Africa, it is widely cultivated for its ornamental value and historical medicinal uses. The plant is also known by numerous common names, including European Dogwood, European Cornelian Cherry, Common Guelder Rose, and White Cornel, among others such as Cherry Dogwood and Swan Plant. Morphologically, Cornus mas features opposite, simple, ovate to elliptic leaves with serrated margins, and it produces clusters of small, yellowish-green flowers in early spring, followed by bright red, berry-like fruits that mature in late summer.

These fruits are edible, though typically tart, and the plant is often valued for its vibrant floral display and autumn foliage.

History and Cultural Relevance

Cornus Mas was used by ancient civilizations such as the Greeks, Romans, and various indigenous cultures for its medicinal and symbolic properties.

In traditional medicine systems like Ayurveda and Chinese medicine, Cornus Mas has been valued for its ability to support kidney health, improve vision, and treat ailments like arthritis and skin conditions. It has also held cultural significance in various regions, where it was often planted near homes to symbolize protection and longevity, and was used in rituals to mark seasonal changes and life transitions. Folk traditions in parts of Europe and Asia have incorporated Cornus Mas into celebrations, with its berries used to make jams, wines, and tinctures.

Today, its extracts are still used in herbal remedies for urinary tract support and as a natural antioxidant, maintaining its relevance in both traditional and modern health practices.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Cornus mas has a variety of forms available, including fresh fruit, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule, allowing for versatile use depending on the desired application.

For internal use, the fruit can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried berries in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the dried fruit for a longer period, which helps extract more active compounds. An infusion can also be made by soaking the dried fruit in cold water for several hours, suitable for those preferring a milder preparation. Topical applications include using the essential oil diluted in a carrier oil for skin conditions or applying a poultice made from fresh berries. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried fruit per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision, with a lower dose if safe.

It is advised to use cornus mas for short durations, no longer than two weeks, unless otherwise directed by a healthcare professional, to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Cornus mas can be used as a medicinal plant with various therapeutic properties, but it is important to consider its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals. There is limited research on interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with medications that affect the liver or have anticoagulant properties. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to follow dosage guidelines, consult with a healthcare professional, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Cornus mas grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, thriving in full sun to partial shade and requiring consistent moisture without waterlogging.

It prefers a temperate climate and should be planted in spring after the last frost, ensuring adequate space for root development. Regular watering during dry periods and mulching to retain soil moisture are essential for healthy growth, along with pruning to promote air circulation and prevent disease. Harvesting should occur in late summer to early autumn when the fruits turn a deep red or orange, using pruning shears to cut the fruit stems carefully to avoid damaging the plant.

For optimal potency, the harvested fruits should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area or using low-heat drying methods, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to preserve their medicinal properties.
