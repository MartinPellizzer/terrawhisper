Cornus Mas (Cornus mas): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Cornus mas, also known as the dogwood tree, is a traditional medicinal plant used for centuries in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and diuretic properties.

What is Cornus Mas (Cornus Mas)?

Cornus mas, also known as Cornus mas, is a deciduous shrub native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its potential health benefits and therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cornus mas is Cornus mas. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Saxifragales, family Cornaceae, genus Cornus, and species Cornus mas. It is commonly known as the cornelian cherry in Europe and the dogwood in North America. The plant is also referred to as the common cornel in Britain and the red cornel in some parts of Scandinavia. Synonyms for Cornus mas include Cornus sanguinea and Cornus officinalis.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cornus mas is a deciduous shrub or small tree that typically grows to a height of 3 to 8 meters, with a rounded crown and dense, branching structure.

Its leaves are opposite, ovate to elliptic in shape, with a pointed tip and serrated edges, measuring up to 6 centimeters in length. The flowers are small, white to pale yellow, clustered in flat-topped umbels, and appear in early spring before the leaves emerge. The root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing the plant to thrive in moist, well-drained soils.

The bark is gray-brown and smooth when young, becoming fissured with age, while the leaves are smooth and dark green on top with a lighter underside.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cornus mas is native to southern and central Europe, including parts of the Balkans, Italy, and the Iberian Peninsula, as well as western and central Asia.

It thrives in temperate climates with cold winters and warm summers, typically found in regions with moderate rainfall. The species prefers well-drained soils, such as sandy or loamy soils, and can tolerate a range of pH levels. It is commonly found in woodlands, along riverbanks, and in open areas with partial shade.

Its natural range extends from the western Caucasus to the Carpathian Mountains, adapting to diverse ecological conditions across its habitat.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of Cornus mas primarily involves its roots, bark, and fruits.

The roots are often harvested in the spring or autumn and dried for use. The bark, which is thick and dark in color, is typically collected during the winter months. Fruits are gathered when fully ripe, usually in late summer.

These parts are used either individually or in combination to prepare remedies.

Phytochemical Composition

Cornus mas contains a variety of active compounds, including flavonoids, phenolic acids, and tannins, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Secondary metabolites such as quercetin, kaempferol, and ellagic acid are present in significant amounts and are responsible for many of its therapeutic effects. These compounds exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities. Chemical markers like cornerin and maslinic acid are also identified in the plant, aiding in its authentication and quality control.

The combination of these phytochemicals supports its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Cornus Mas (Cornus mas) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that make it valuable in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, antidiabetic, and hepatoprotective effects. The biological activity of Cornus mas is attributed to its rich content of polyphenols, flavonoids, and triterpenoids. Pharmacodynamically, these compounds interact with various physiological pathways, modulating oxidative stress and immune responses.

System-level effects include improved metabolic function and enhanced liver health.

The mechanisms of action of Cornus mas involve multiple pathways that contribute to its therapeutic effects.

At the cellular level, it modulates signaling pathways such as MAPK and NF-κB, which are involved in inflammation and cell survival. Biochemically, the plant contains compounds like flavonoids and triterpenes that exhibit antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. These compounds interact with enzymes and receptors to inhibit oxidative stress and inflammatory responses.

The effects of Cornus mas are mediated through target systems including the immune system, nervous system, and metabolic pathways.

Therapeutic Uses

Cornus mas, also known as common dogwood, has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties.

It is commonly employed to treat digestive issues such as diarrhea and dysentery due to its astringent and anti-inflammatory effects. The plant is also used to address urinary tract infections and kidney disorders because of its diuretic and antimicrobial qualities. Additionally, Cornus mas has been applied in the management of fever and inflammation, often in formulations for respiratory and skin conditions.

Its use extends to supporting liver function and aiding in the treatment of jaundice.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Cornus mas involve various forms in which the plant is utilized.

The most common forms include decoctions, tinctures, and infusions made from the bark, roots, and berries. Decoctions are prepared by boiling the dried bark or roots in water for an extended period. Tinctures are created by soaking the plant material in alcohol or glycerin to extract active compounds.

Infusions involve steeping the dried berries or leaves in hot water to release their medicinal properties.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Cornus mas is generally considered safe for most people, but it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Side effects are typically mild and may occur with excessive consumption or in sensitive individuals. Contraindications include individuals with known allergies to the plant or its compounds, as well as those with certain chronic conditions like diabetes or hypertension, where its effects on blood sugar and pressure may be significant. Allergic reactions can range from mild skin irritation to more severe symptoms like difficulty breathing, requiring immediate medical attention.

Safety during pregnancy has not been extensively studied, and it is generally advised to avoid use during pregnancy unless under the guidance of a healthcare professional.

Drug Interactions

Cornus mas may interact with medications that affect the liver or blood pressure, and it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use if taking other herbal or prescription drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Cornus mas is generally considered safe when used in appropriate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and there is limited research on its toxicity, so it should be used with caution, especially during pregnancy and lactation.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cornus mas is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and partial to full sun exposure, requiring regular pruning to maintain its shape and promote healthy growth, with harvesting of its edible fruits occurring in late summer to early autumn.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Cornus mas involve methods such as maceration, distillation, and solvent extraction to obtain active compounds like anthocyanins, flavonoids, and essential oils, which are then used in herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cornus mas should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, and its dried parts can retain potency for up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Cornus mas has primarily focused on its pharmacological properties, including its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and potential antidiabetic effects, with studies highlighting the presence of compounds such as flavonoids, triterpenoids, and anthocyanins that contribute to its therapeutic potential in herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Cornus Mas has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to support urinary tract health, reduce inflammation, and promote diuresis, with historical references dating back to ancient Greek and Roman herbal practices.
