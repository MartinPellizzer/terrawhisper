Campylandra delavayi

What's the taxonomical classification of Campylandra delavayi?

Campylandra delavayi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant falls under the order Asparagales. It is further categorized within the family Asparagaceae, specifically belonging to the genus Campylandra, with its unique designation being the species delavayi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Campylandra delavayi has a woody, perennial habit characterized by erect stems that branch extensively from the base.

The leaves are oppositely arranged along the stems and typically possess an elliptical or lanceolate shape with entire margins. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying a distinct corolla structure characteristic of the family. The plant often features fine pubescence on its stems and leaf surfaces, providing a subtle texture to its overall appearance.

These morphological traits allow the species to adapt effectively to its specific ecological niche.

        What are the herbal preparations of Campylandra delavayi?

        Campylandra delavayi is prepared as a decoction, a concentrated tea made by boiling the dried roots in water to extract its active medicinal compounds. Practitioners often utilize this liquid form to ensure the heavy plant matter releases its full potency into the solution. Beyond simple decoctions, the plant material can be macerated in oils to create topical salves for skin application. Some traditional methods also involve drying the leaves to be used in milder infusions. These various preparations allow for both internal consumption and external therapeutic use.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Campylandra delavayi can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, localized skin irritation, and potential neurological impairment if ingested or handled improperly. Contact with the plant's sap often results in contact dermatitis, manifesting as redness, itching, or painful blistering on the skin. When consumed, the chemical compounds within the foliage may trigger nausea, vomiting, and intense abdominal cramping. Some documented cases suggest that high doses could lead to dizziness or temporary disorientation in sensitive individuals. Due to these unpredictable reactions, strict caution is required when interacting with this species.
