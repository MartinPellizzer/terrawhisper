What's the taxonomical classification of Viola mandshurica?

Viola mandshurica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its systematic progression continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Violaceae, eventually reaching its specific classification within the genus Viola. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its unique species designation, mandshurica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viola mandshurica has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by a creeping rhizome and basal leaves that are typically cordate or reniform with crenate margins. The leaves are petiolate and often exhibit a dark green color with slightly wavy edges. Its flowering stems are relatively short and bear zygomorphic flowers that are usually violet or pale purple in hue. Each flower consists of five petals, with the lower petal often featuring distinct nectar guides or pale markings. The fruit is a small, three-valved capsule that contains numerous tiny seeds for dispersal.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viola mandshurica?

This plant has potent anti-inflammatory and anti-asthmatic pharmacological activities that make it valuable for treating respiratory conditions. The bioactive compounds found within its tissues work to reduce swelling and systemic inflammation in the body. These medicinal properties specifically help in relaxing the bronchial muscles to alleviate symptoms of asthma. Furthermore, its ability to suppress inflammatory mediators can mitigate the severity of various allergic reactions. Such biological actions suggest that Viola mandshurica holds significant potential for further pharmaceutical development.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Viola mandshurica ethanolic extract significantly inhibited increases in total immunoglobulin E (IgE) and cytokines IL-4 and IL-13 levels in serum and bronchoalveolar lavage fluid (BALF). In a mouse model where female BALB/c mice were sensitized on days 0 and 14 and given intranasal OVA on days 28-30, the extract was administered orally from days 28 to 30. The extract effectively suppressed airway hyperresponsiveness (AHR), eosinophilia, and mucus hypersecretion in mice with OVA-induced asthma. These results suggest that Viola mandshurica may be a valuable lead material for anti-asthma drug development.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds hesperetin, luteolin, tectoriidin, cyclotides, and resveratrol. These diverse phytochemicals contribute to the biological activities and potential therapeutic uses of the species. Research indicates that the presence of these specific flavonoids and polyphenols may offer antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits. Furthermore, the unique cyclotides found within the plant are of significant interest in pharmacological studies due to their structural stability. Together, these substances form a complex chemical profile that characterizes the medicinal properties of Viola mandshurica.

According to a study published by "Journal of microbiology and biotechnology", the chloroform extract of fermented Viola mandshurica contains main phenolic compounds identified as tectoriidin, luteolin, resveratrol, and hesperetin. This chloroform extract of fermented Viola mandshurica, referred to as CEFV, significantly inhibited NO production and reduced the expression of inducible nitric oxide synthase at both protein and mRNA levels. CEFV also decreased PGE2 production and suppressed COX-2 expression. Furthermore, the extract inhibited the activation of the ERK and JNK pathways in RAW264.7 cells. These actions allowed CEFV to suppress NF-κB activation, which acts as a key regulator in the inflammatory response.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Viola mandshurica ethanolic extract was investigated for the treatment of bronchial asthma in an ovalbumin-induced asthma model using female BALB/c mice. The mice were sensitized with intraperitoneal ovalbumin on days 0 and 14, followed by intranasal administration on days 28-30. Furthermore, the extract effectively suppressed airway hyperresponsiveness, eosinophilia, and mucus hypersecretion in these mice. These results suggest that Viola mandshurica could be a useful herbal medicine for asthma treatment and a valuable lead material for drug development.
