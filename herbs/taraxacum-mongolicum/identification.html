Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the dandelion is Taraxacum mongolicum, a species within the genus Taraxacum in the family Asteraceae. It was first described by the Russian botanist Carl von Siebold in 1833. This name reflects its native origin in Mongolia and is widely recognized in botanical literature.

Morphological Characteristics

Taraxacum mongolicum is a perennial herb with a robust taproot system. It has a straight, hollow stem that grows upright. Its leaves are basal, spoon-shaped, and have a serrated edge. The plant produces a single, yellow, composite flower head surrounded by bracts. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous small, wind-dispersed seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Taraxacum mongolicum can be distinguished by its upright, hairy stems, deeply lobed leaves with a white marginal border, and bright yellow flowers. It differs from similar species like Taraxacum officinale by its more robust stems and slightly larger, more deeply cut leaves. The presence of a white border on the leaf margins is a key identifying feature.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Taraxacum mongolicum is native to regions in East Asia, including China, Mongolia, and Russia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers loose, well-drained soils and is commonly found at altitudes between 1,000 and 3,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Taraxacum mongolicum blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between May and September, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer, with seeds dispersing via the wind as the plant goes to seed. For identification purposes, the most accurate time to harvest is during the flowering stage, when the plant is most distinct and the leaves are fully developed.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Taraxacum mongolicum belongs to the Asteraceae family, sharing traits with other dandelions. It is closely related to species like Taraxacum officinale and Taraxacum brevicorniculatum. Its phylogenetic position reflects its close ties within the genus Taraxacum, characterized by similar morphology and genetic markers.
