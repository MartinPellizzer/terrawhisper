Tragopogon porrifolius

What's the taxonomical classification of Tragopogon porrifolius?

Tragopogon porrifolius belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant falls under the order Asterales. It is further categorized into the family Asteraceae, which is a diverse group of flowering plants. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Tragopogon, and its specific identification is completed by its species name, porrifolius.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tragopogon porrifolius has linear, grass-like leaves that are bright green, basal in arrangement, and typically measure several millimeters in width. These leaves are continuous throughout the plant and lack distinct petioles. The inflorescence consists of large, yellow, dandelion-like flower heads that bloom on long, leafless stalks. Each flower head contains numerous individual florets that eventually develop into a cluster of many small, bristly achenes. The seeds are equipped with a pappus, which facilitates dispersal by wind.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, sandy or loamy soil and full sun exposure to thrive in most temperate climates.

Once established, it is remarkably drought-tolerant due to its deep root system. Growers should avoid overwatering to prevent rhizome rot, which can quickly kill the specimen. While it demands minimal maintenance, periodic division of the clumps every few years helps maintain vigorous growth.

This species is particularly valued in xeriscaping for its ability to flourish in poor soil conditions.

According to a study published by "Biology", Tragopogon porrifolius belongs to the Asteraceae family. This plant was among 46 species of traditional medicinal plants evaluated in an experiment regarding the in vitro survivability of L1-2 and L3 larvae of Strongyloides papillosus and Haemonchus contortus. The experiment utilized aqueous solutions of ethanolic tinctures to test for anthelmintic properties. Tragopogon porrifolius did not affect the viability of parasitic nematode larvae.
