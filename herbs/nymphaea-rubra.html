Nymphaea rubra

What's the taxonomical classification of Nymphaea rubra?

Nymphaea rubra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Nymphaeales. Within this order, it is a member of the family Nymphaeaceae, which contains various aquatic flowering plants. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Nymphaea, and its specific species name, rubra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nymphaea rubra has large, circular floating leaves that can reach up to 60 centimeters in diameter and striking red to deep pink flowers with multiple layers of petals. The leaves feature a deep notch or sinus where the petiole attaches, allowing them to rest flat on the water surface. Its thick, fleshy rhizomes extend through the muddy substrate to anchor the plant and store nutrients. The long, flexible petioles connect the floating foliage to the submerged root system. These flowers typically emerge above the water level on sturdy stalks to facilitate pollination.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the freshwater wetlands and slow-moving water bodies found throughout Southeast Asia, specifically within countries such as Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, and Malaysia. It thrives in tropical climates where stagnant or shallow pond environments provide stable water levels. These aquatic lilies are most frequently observed in regions characterized by high humidity and consistent warmth. Their distribution is closely tied to the availability of nutrient-rich mud at the bottom of tropical basins. Because of these specific environmental requirements, the species remains localized to these specific subtropical and tropical zones.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires a permanent container of at least 12 inches in depth, placement in full sun for at least six hours daily, and water temperatures maintained between 70 and 85 degrees Fahrenheit.

Because it is a tropical species, you must bring the container indoors or move it to a heated greenhouse when temperatures drop below 60 degrees. Use a heavy, nutrient-rich aquatic soil mix to provide the necessary stability and food for the roots. Regular fertilization with aquatic plant tablets during the growing season will encourage the production of its large, vibrant red blooms.

Monitoring water quality is also essential to prevent algae buildup that could stifle the plant's growth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Nymphaea rubra?

This plant has anti-diabetic, dyslipidemic, and suppression of adipogenesis pharmacological activities. These therapeutic properties suggest that the species can play a significant role in managing metabolic disorders. By reducing glucose levels, the plant acts as an effective agent against diabetes. Furthermore, its ability to regulate lipid profiles helps combat high cholesterol and other dyslipidemic conditions. Finally, the suppression of adipogenesis indicates that the plant can inhibit the formation of new fat cells in the body.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", Nymphaea rubra flowers demonstrate antidiaabetic and dyslipidemic properties, as shown in STZ induced high fat diet fed diabetic rats. The ethyl acetate fraction of the ethanolic extract contains Nuciferine at 22.39% and 10, 11-dimethoxy-Apomorphine at 1.47%. In 3 T3-L1 cells, this extract promoted the suppression of adipogenesis. A dose-response effect of 100 mg/kg and 250 mg/kg of flower extract after 30 days showed a significant effect on body weight, food, water intake, serum insulin, FBG, OGTT, lipid profile, glycated haemoglobin, liver and kidney function test. These findings suggest the ethyl acetate fraction of ethanolic extract from Nymphaea rubra flowers can be applied for the treatment and control of diabetes and dyslipidemia.

Researchers are currently investigating how these specific molecules interact with the human central nervous system. Understanding these complex chemical profiles is essential for developing new therapeutic treatments derived from aquatic flora.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", GC-MS investigation of the ethyl acetate fraction of the ethanolic extract of Nymphaea rubra flowers revealed marker compounds including Nuciferine and Apomorphine. The detected concentrations of these compounds were 22.39% for Nuciferine and 1.47% for 10, 11-dimethoxy-Apomorphine. These bioactive chemicals, along with other alkaloids identified by GC-MS analysis, may exert a synergistic impact. A dose-response effect was observed using 100 mg/kg and 250 mg/kg of the flower extract over a period of 30 days. This extract also promoted the suppression of adipogenesis in 3 T3-L1 cells.
