Sterculia tragacantha

What's the taxonomical classification of Sterculia tragacantha?

Sterculia tragacantha belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Malvales. This plant is further categorized under the family Malvaceae and is situated within the genus Sterculia, ultimately being identified by its specific species name, tragacantha.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sterculia tragacantha has a medium-sized tree habit characterized by a woody trunk, trifoliate leaves, and small, inconspicuous yellowish flowers. The leaves consist of three distinct leaflets that are typically ovate or elliptical in shape with smooth margins. Its bark is generally smooth or slightly fissured, providing a protective layer for the internal vascular tissues. The reproductive structures include small, clustered flowers that eventually develop into woody, dehiscent follicles. These follicles contain seeds which are often encased in a mucilaginous substance that gives the species its name.

What are the pharmacological activities of Sterculia tragacantha?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, hypoglycaemic, and neuroprotective properties. Its chemical constituents work to reduce pain and swelling by inhibiting specific inflammatory mediators in the body. The antioxidant components help neutralize harmful free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative stress. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates an ability to lower blood glucose levels, making it relevant for metabolic health. These combined effects also contribute to neuroprotective qualities that assist in maintaining healthy brain function.

As discussed in a study, Sterculia tragacantha is utilized in some West African nations for the treatment of boils, diarrhea, dyspepsia, fever, gonorrhea, snake bite, syphilis, and tapeworm. A pilot toxicity test using an aqueous extract at 100 mg/mL showed no mortality, though animals receiving a 3,000 mg/kg dose exhibited slight dullness after 48 h. While the 200 and 3,000 mg/kg doses caused a significant increase in total protein, no other significant changes were observed in hematological or serum chemistry indices. The n-hexane, chloroform, ethyl acetate, and methanol extracts produced significant (p<0.05) inhibition of paw edema using histamine and carrageenan methods. Furthermore, a significant (p<0.05) reduction in writhed movements occurred at 100 mg/kg and 200 mg/kg for the n-hexane, chloroform, and ethyl acetate leaf extracts compared to indomethacin at 10 mg/kg. These different leaf extracts exhibited both anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties and are considered safe for medicinal use.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including the alkaloid, epicatechin, flavonoid, proanthocyanidin, and procyanidin B2. These bioactive substances contribute to the plant's complex chemical profile and therapeutic potential. Specifically, the presence of flavonoids and proanthocyanidins provides significant antioxidant properties. Within this group, epicatechin acts as a key component for cellular health. Additionally, the specific compound procyanidin B2 serves as a notable example of the plant's concentrated phytochemical richness.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical screening of the powdered material of Sterculia tragacantha showed that alkaloid, flavonoid, and reducing sugar were present. The study utilized 200 g of powdered material which was dissolved in hexane for 72 h before further processing in chloroform, ethyl acetate, and methanol. A pilot toxicity test using aqueous extract at a 100 mg/mL concentration recorded no mortality, although animals receiving a 3,000 mg/kg dose exhibited slight dullness after 48 h. While no significant changes were observed for most indices, the 200 and 3,000 mg/kg doses caused a significant increase in total protein levels. Furthermore, n-hexane, chloroform, ethyl acetate, and methanol extracts produced significant (p<0.05) inhibition of paw edema. Significant (p<0.05) reduction in writhing movements was also noted at 100 mg/kg and 200 mg/kg of n-hexane, chloroform, and ethyl acetate leaf extracts compared to indomethacin at 10 mg/kg.
