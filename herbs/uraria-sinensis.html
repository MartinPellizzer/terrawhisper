Uraria sinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Uraria sinensis?

Uraria sinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Fabales. This plant is a member of the family Fabaceae, and its specific taxonomic hierarchy is completed by its placement in the genus Uraria, with the specific epithet sinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Uraria sinensis has an erect, herbaceous growth habit characterized by slender, quadrangular stems that can reach heights of up to 1 meter. The leaves are arranged in an opposite pattern and consist of small, ovate to lanceolate leaflets with entire margins. Tiny, pale yellow flowers emerge from the leaf axils in small clusters, showcasing a delicate structure typical of the Fabaceae family. The plant produces small, linear pods that contain several seeds, which facilitate its reproductive cycle. These morphological features collectively define its identity as a climbing or sprawling medicinal herb.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains various bioactive chemical constituents including the triterpenoid saponin urariasinoside A, several flavonoids, and various phenolic acids. These specific medicinal compounds are primarily investigated for their potential pharmacological activities in traditional medicine. Urariasinoside A is particularly notable for its role in the plant's biological profile and its contribution to its therapeutic properties. Researchers often study these metabolites to understand their impact on cellular functions and overall health. The complex mixture of these substances provides the chemical basis for the plant's medicinal use.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", a new sesquilignan glucoside named urariasinoside A (1) was isolated from the aerial parts of Uraria sinensis. Along with this new compound, eight known compounds were identified, which included 2 lignans, 1 sesquilignan, 1 dilignan, and 4 flavonoid derivatives. The structure of compound 1 was determined through extensive spectroscopic analyses and comparison with literature data. This compound 1 was evaluated for in vitro cytotoxicity activity against 6 different cell lines, specifically HL-60, SMMC-7721, A549, MCF-7, SW480, and BEAS-2B.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe adverse reactions including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, dizziness, and hepatotoxicity. Gastrointestinal distress is a common occurrence for those who ingest the herb improperly. Some individuals may also experience neurological symptoms such as dizziness during consumption. Most critically, the plant has been linked to hepatotoxicity, which involves significant damage to the liver. These serious side effects necessitate extreme caution when considering its use.
