Viburnum henryi

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum henryi?

Viburnum henryi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical structure, the plant is placed in the order Dipsacales and is a member of the family Viburnacea. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Viburnum and the species henryi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum henryi has opposite, simple leaves that are elliptic to ovate in shape, measuring 5 to 10 centimeters in length, with finely serrated margins and a glossy, dark green upper surface.

The leaf bases are typically cuneate, transitioning into long petioles that support the foliage. During the blooming season, the plant produces flat-topped cymes of small, creamy white flowers that emerge from terminal clusters. These blossoms eventually give way to small, fleshy drupes that transition from green to a deep red or black color upon ripening.

The stems exhibit a woody structure with a somewhat spreading growth habit that defines its overall shrubby silhouette.

        What are the herbal preparations of Viburnum henryi?

        Viburnum henryi is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture to extract its medicinal properties. The berries and bark are most commonly boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction for internal use. For more subtle extraction, the leaves can be steeped in hot water to form a gentle infusion.         What side-effects this plant can have?

        Viburnum henryi can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if the berries or leaves are ingested by humans or pets. While generally considered non-toxic to most people, direct contact with the plant material may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of the fruit may lead to vomiting or stomach upset due to the presence of certain compounds. Pet owners should monitor animals for signs of lethargy or digestive issues if any part of the shrub is consumed. Always exercise caution and consult a professional if accidental exposure occurs.
