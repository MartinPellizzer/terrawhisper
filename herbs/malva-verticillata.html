Malva verticillata

What's the taxonomical classification of Malva verticillata?

Malva verticillata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malvales and the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is identified within the genus Malva, with its specific designation being the species verticillata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Malva verticillata has an erect, herbaceous stem that grows up to 1 meter tall and features whorls of leaves arranged at nodes. The leaves are typically cordate to reniform in shape with crenate margins and are often covered in fine, soft hairs. Its flowers are pentamerous with five pink or pale violet petals and are tucked within the leaf axils. The plant produces small, rounded capsules that contain numerous seeds, often referred to as cheese seeds. This species is characterized by its distinctive whorled arrangement, which sets it apart from many other members of the Malvaceae family.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Malva verticillata targets cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various proinflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. The plant's rich concentration of mucilage and flavonoids allows these compounds to modulate the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. By inhibiting the cyclooxygenase enzymes, the bioactive constituents significantly reduce the production of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins. Additionally, the phytochemicals interfere with the signaling cascades of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6 to mitigate systemic inflammation. These multi-target interactions contribute to the plant's documented anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and wound-healing properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Malva verticillata?

Malva verticillata has mucilaginous properties that allow it to function as a powerful demulcent, emollient, astringent, anti-inflammatory, and vulnerary agent. The high concentration of mucilage creates a soothing film that coats irritated mucous membranes and skin surfaces. This demulcent action effectively relieves coughs and digestive tract inflammation. As an emollient, it softens the skin and provides relief from dryness or irritation. The plant also acts as an astringent to help tone tissues and serves as an anti-inflammatory to reduce swelling. Finally, its vulnerary qualities support the healing of wounds and damaged integumentary tissues.
