Botanical Identification of Cilantro (Coriandrum sativum)

To identify Cilantro (Coriandrum sativum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Cilantro (Coriandrum sativum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Cilantro, or Coriandrum sativum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Coriandrum, and species Coriandrum sativum. These classifications place it within the broader group of flowering plants and the parsley family, which includes many aromatic herbs.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of cilantro is Coriandrum sativum, a member of the Apiaceae family. It was first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. Common names include coriander, Chinese parsley, and culantro, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Cilantro, or Coriandrum sativum, is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has slender, branched stems that grow upright. Its leaves are finely divided, with a distinctive parsley-like appearance. The plant produces umbels of small white flowers, followed by small, spherical fruits that contain aromatic seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cilantro, or Coriandrum sativum, can be distinguished by its distinctive fern-like leaves with fine, feathered divisions and small white flowers in umbels. It is often confused with other Apiaceae family plants, but its leaf structure and flowering pattern are key identifiers. The fruit, when mature, turns from green to brown and has a distinctive ridge.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cilantro, or Coriandrum sativum, is native to regions of southern Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cilantro (Coriandrum sativum) blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is harvested when the seeds are mature and the stems are dry, usually in late summer to early fall.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Coriandrum sativum is closely related to other species in the Apiaceae family, such as Anise and Caraway. It belongs to the tribe Coronae, which includes several aromatic herbs. Its close relatives share similar morphological and chemical characteristics, making it identifiable within the genus Coriandrum.
