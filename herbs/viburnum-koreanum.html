Viburnum koreanum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum koreanum has oppositely arranged, ovate to elliptical leaves that measure approximately 3 to 7 centimeters in length and feature finely serrated margins. The plant grows as a deciduous shrub reaching heights of up to 3 meters, characterized by its multi-stemmed growth habit and smooth, gray bark. During the spring, it produces terminal clusters of small, creamy white flowers that emit a strong fragrance. These blossoms eventually transition into small, fleshy drupes that turn from green to a deep red or black color upon ripening. The overall structure is compact and dense, making it a distinctive ornamental specimen in woodland settings.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of the Korean Peninsula, specifically throughout South Korea. It typically grows in temperate forests and rocky slopes where it can find adequate moisture. While its primary natural range is restricted to this East Asian territory, it has been introduced to various other parts of the world for ornamental use. In these non-native locations, it is often found in temperate gardens and landscaped parks due to its attractive flowering properties. Its distribution remains relatively localized in the wild compared to more widespread shrub species.

    What common ailments are treated using this plant?

    This plant is used to treat fungal infections and pest infestations such as powdery mildew, verticillium wilt, aphids, scale insects, and leaf spot. Gardeners often monitor Korean viburnum for these specific issues to maintain the health of the shrub. White, flour-like patches of powdery mildew can appear on the foliage, while leaf spot causes unsightly discoloration. Vascular issues like verticillium wilt may cause sudden wilting or branch dieback. Additionally, managing pests like aphids and scale insects is necessary to prevent damage to the stems and leaves.
