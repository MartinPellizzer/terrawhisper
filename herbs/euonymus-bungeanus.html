What's the taxonomical classification of Euonymus bungeanus?

Euonymus bungeanus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its evolutionary lineage continues through the order Celastrales and the family Celastraceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Euonymus, specifically identifying as the species bungeanus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euonymus bungeanus has an upright, deciduous shrub habit characterized by slender, arching branches and oppositely arranged, ovate to elliptical leaves that measure approximately 2 to 5 centimeters in length.

The foliage typically presents a bright green color during the growing season and transitions to reddish hues in autumn. During late spring, the plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually give way to striking, bright pink or red capsules. These four-lobed fruit capsules split open to reveal fleshy, orange-colored arils that surround the seeds.

This distinctive fruit display serves as a primary morphological feature for identifying the species in the field.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Euonymus bungeanus can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if any part of the plant is ingested. The berries and leaves contain compounds that may lead to nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in humans and pets. Contact with the foliage or stems can sometimes trigger contact dermatitis, resulting in redness or itching on the skin. Because the plant is considered toxic, it is important to keep children and animals away from the ripening fruit. Careful handling is recommended to avoid accidental consumption or skin reactions.
