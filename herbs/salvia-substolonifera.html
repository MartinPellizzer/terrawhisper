Salvia substolonifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia substolonifera?

Salvia substolonifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Lamiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Salvia, and its specific species designation, substolonifera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia substolonifera has a decumbent to ascending growth habit characterized by quadrangular stems that produce short, creeping stolons to facilitate clonal spreading.

The leaves are typically ovate to lanceolate with serrated margins and a textured, rugose surface. Its inflorescence consists of verticillasters arranged along an elongated spike, bearing small, tubular flowers that are often blue or lavender in hue. The plant's root system is fibrous and supports the development of these lateral runners.

This morphological structure allows the species to effectively colonize open ground through both seed production and vegetative expansion.

By inhibiting these proteins, the plant effectively reduces the production of prostaglandins and nitric oxide in biological systems. These molecular interactions contribute significantly to its observed anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. Furthermore, certain constituents may modulate antioxidant enzymes to mitigate oxidative stress at a cellular level.

      What are the herbal preparations of Salvia substolonifera?

      Salvía substolonifera is prepared as a concentrated decoction, a dried leaf infusion, or a topical tincture for medicinal use. The leaves are typically harvested and dried to preserve their volatile oils before being steeped in hot water. This liquid extract is often consumed orally to address various systemic ailments.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Salviasubstolonifera can cause allergic skin reactions such as redness, itching, or dermatitis if the plant makes direct contact with sensitive skin. While there is limited clinical data on its toxicity, some individuals may experience mild digestive upset if any part of the plant is ingested. The plant contains essential oils that might trigger sensitivities in people with known allergies to the Lamiaceae family. It is generally considered safe for ornamental use, but caution is advised when handling it with broken or highly sensitive skin. Always consult a medical professional if you suspect an adverse reaction after contact.
