What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Peucedanum morisonii has an erect, perennial growth habit characterized by deeply pinnatisect leaves and small, white, umbel-shaped flower clusters. The stems are typically hollow and can reach heights of up to 40 centimeters. Its foliage consists of fine, thread-like segments that give the plant a delicate, feathery appearance. The tiny flowers are arranged in compound umbels, which are a hallmark of the Apiaceae family. During the fruiting stage, the plant produces small, oblong schizocarps that facilitate seed dispersal.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Peucedanum morisonii can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and blistering upon direct contact with the plant's sap. This species belongs to a group of plants containing furanocoumarins, which react with ultraviolet light to damage skin cells. Such a reaction is known as phytophotodermatitis and can lead to redness and painful swelling. The irritation often intensifies when the affected area is exposed to sunlight shortly after contact. In some cases, the resulting lesions may leave behind dark spots or pigmentation changes on the skin.
