Lysimachia fistulosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Lysimachia fistulosa?

Lysimachia fistulosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Ericales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Primulaceae, where it is placed within the genus Lysimachia, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species fistulosa.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Lysimachia fistulosa targets various inflammatory mediators and oxidative stress enzymes, primarily through the modulation of cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and antioxidant proteins like superoxide dismutase (SOD). These biochemical interactions occur as bioactive secondary metabolites, such as flavonoids and phenolic acids, bind to the active sites of these proteins to inhibit pro-inflammatory signaling pathways. By suppressing the expression of COX-2, the plant effectively reduces the synthesis of prostaglandins, which are key drivers of pain and swelling. Additionally, the activation of antioxidant enzyme systems helps neutralize reactive oxygen species that cause cellular damage. This multi-target mechanism explains the plant's traditional use in treating inflammatory conditions and promoting tissue repair.

    What are the herbal preparations of Lysimachia fistulosa?

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Lysimachia fistulosa can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive distress if handled or ingested improperly. While scientific documentation on its toxicity is limited, many plants in the Primulaceae family contain compounds that may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Direct contact with the stems or leaves might lead to redness, itching, or localized swelling on the skin. If consumed, the plant could potentially cause nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. It is essential to exercise caution and avoid ingestion due to the lack of comprehensive safety studies regarding human consumption.
