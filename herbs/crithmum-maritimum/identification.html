To identify Crithmum (Crithmum maritimum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the plant is Crithmum maritimum, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Crithmum litorale and Crithmum maritimum subsp. litorale. Common names include sea fennel, rock samphire, and sea holly. Regional and historical names vary, with some ancient references using terms like "littoral herb."

Morphological Characteristics

Crithmum maritimum is a low-growing perennial plant with a rhizomatous root system. It has creeping stems that form dense mats. The leaves are narrow, linear, and aromatic, with a distinctive sea-like scent. The plant produces umbels of small, yellow flowers, followed by spherical fruits containing several small, flat seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Crithmum maritimum can be distinguished by its aromatic, fern-like leaves with serrated edges and yellow flowers in clusters. It grows in coastal areas, unlike similar species that prefer inland soils. Its strong scent and specific habitat are key identifiers. Look for its unique leaf structure and flowering pattern for accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Crithmum maritimum is native to the Mediterranean region, including southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. It thrives in coastal areas with a temperate climate and prefers well-drained, sandy or gravelly soils. It typically grows at low altitudes, often near the sea, where it tolerates salt spray and coastal conditions.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Crithmum maritimum blooms from May to July, producing yellow flowers in clusters. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during early flowering to ensure accurate species recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Crithmum maritimum is closely related to species within the genus Crithmum, which belongs to the family Umbelliferae. It shares key family traits such as compound umbels and aromatic foliage. Its phylogenetic position highlights its evolutionary ties to other umbelliferous plants with similar morphological and chemical characteristics.
