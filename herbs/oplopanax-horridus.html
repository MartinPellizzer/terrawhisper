Scientific and Botanical Profile

Devil's Club, with botanical name Oplopanax horridus, is a member of the Araliaceae family, native to regions across North America, including Canada, the United States, and parts of Alaska, British Columbia, Washington, Oregon, California, and the Northwest Territories.

This evergreen shrub is known by numerous common names such as Devil's Ivy, Poison Ivy, Horned Club, and Horrid Oxeye, reflecting its toxic properties and distinctive appearance. Morphologically, it features a thick, woody stem covered in sharp, spiny bracts, with large, dark green, leathery leaves that have a waxy texture and serrated edges. Its flowers are small, clustered in umbels, and are followed by red berries that are toxic to humans but serve as a food source for wildlife.

Despite its ominous reputation, Devil's Club has been used traditionally by Indigenous peoples for medicinal purposes, though its potent toxins require careful handling.

History and Cultural Relevance

Devil's Club was used by Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest for centuries as a powerful plant with both medicinal and spiritual significance.

The plant, known as Oplopanax horridus, holds a deep place in the traditions of the Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian peoples, who have long utilized its roots and leaves in traditional medicine to treat ailments such as infections, inflammation, and digestive issues. In many tribes, Devil's Club was also considered a sacred plant, often used in ceremonies to purify the body and spirit, or to mark rites of passage such as coming-of-age rituals. Its cultural importance extends beyond medicine, as it is sometimes associated with protection, transformation, and the connection between the physical and spiritual worlds.

Today, some of its traditional remedies, like root extracts used for skin conditions or as a stimulant, are still being explored in modern herbal medicine, reflecting its enduring relevance in both cultural and scientific contexts.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves or roots in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the dried plant for a longer period. Due to limited research, it is advised to use oplopanax horridus cautiously, with a short duration and frequency, to minimize potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Oplopanax horridus can be used as a medicinal plant with potential benefits for stress relief and cognitive enhancement, but it must be approached with caution due to its potential safety concerns.

Side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort, dizziness, and in some cases, more severe reactions such as liver toxicity, particularly with prolonged use or high doses. It may interact with certain medications, including antidepressants and sedatives, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects or reducing the efficacy of these drugs. Special populations such as pregnant or breastfeeding women, as well as individuals with chronic illnesses, should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data and potential risks to health.

For safe use, it is advisable to consult a healthcare professional before starting, follow recommended dosages, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Oplopanax horridus grows in temperate climates, typically in moist, well-drained soils rich in organic matter, and prefers partial to full sun exposure.

It thrives in cool, humid environments, such as forest understories or shaded gardens, and requires consistent moisture without waterlogging. Regular watering is essential, especially during dry periods, but the soil should not remain saturated to prevent root rot. To ensure healthy growth, mulching helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds, while periodic pruning of dead or damaged foliage promotes air circulation and reduces disease risk.

Harvesting is best done in late summer to early fall when the roots are most potent, using a spade to carefully excavate the roots without damaging the plant, and allowing the roots to dry in a shaded, well-ventilated area before storing them in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to preserve their medicinal potency.
