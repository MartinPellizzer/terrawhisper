Nicotiana tabacum

What's the taxonomical classification of Nicotiana tabacum?

Nicotiana tabacum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Solanales and the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Nicotiana and the species tabacum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nicotiana tabacum has large, elliptical, or ovate leaves that are typically dark green, hairy, and arranged alternately along a thick, upright stem. The plant reaches significant heights, often growing between one and two meters tall depending on environmental conditions. Its inflorescence consists of terminal clusters of trumpet-shaped flowers that vary in color from white to pale pink. These tubular blossoms possess long corolla tubes and are designed to attract nocturnal pollinators like hawkmoths. The root system is composed of a sturdy taproot that provides stability for the heavy foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas, stretching from the southern United States through Mexico and Central America to South America. Its natural range primarily encompasses warm, humid environments found throughout the Caribbean islands. Due to human cultivation and trade, it has been introduced to various other tropical zones across the globe. The species thrives in diverse habitats including disturbed soils, forest edges, and agricultural landscapes. This widespread distribution is a result of both its biological adaptability and its historical use in various cultures.

Nicotiaana tabacum contains medicinal alkaloids primarily concentrated in its leaves, though some applications utilize the roots and stems. What are the pharmacological activities of Nicotiana tabacum?

This plant has pharmacological activities including anti-tobacco mosaic virus activity, parasympathetic-like effects, neuroprotective qualities, antiparasitic properties, and significant toxicity. The presence of alkaloids like nicotine allows it to function as a parasympathomimetic agent by stimulating cholinergic receptors. Research also suggests the plant may exhibit neuroprotective benefits under specific controlled conditions. Its antiparasitic properties can be utilized to combat certain biological pests. However, the high concentration of bioactive compounds makes the species inherently toxic to many organisms.

As discussed in a study, seven diterpenes were isolated from oriental tobacco Nicotiana tabacum YNOTBS1. These compounds include two new seco-cembranoid basmanoids A (1) and B (2), two new labdane basmadanes A (3) and B (4), and three known compounds (-) -dehydro-nor-ambreinolid (5), (13E)-8-hydroxy-13-labden-12-one (6), and 14,15,16-dinor-7-oxo-labda-8-ene-13-oic acid (7). Compounds 1-3 and 5 demonstrated anti-tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) activity with inhibition rates ranging from 30-69% at a concentration of 1 mg/mL. None of these compounds exhibited inhibitory effects against nitric oxide (NO) production in LPS and IFN-γ-induced RAW 264.7 murine macrophages. Additionally, they showed no anti-proliferative activities against cancer cell lines SMMC-7721 and A-549.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal and alkaloid compounds nicotine, cotinine, anabatinine, anabaseine, and N-nitrosonicotine. Nicotine serves as a potent stimulant that acts primarily on the central nervous system. Cotinine is a major metabolite of nicotine often used to track exposure levels in biological studies. Anabatinine and anabaseine are related pyridine alkaloids that exhibit various neurobiological activities. Finally, N-nitrosonicotine is a potent nitrosamine derivative found within the plant's chemical profile.

As discussed in a study, tobacco contains nicotine which acts as the key ingredient triggering addiction. The FDA has classified more than 90 chemical components of tobacco and related smoke as hazardous or potentially hazardous. These components can lead to cancer, cardiovascular, respiratory, and reproductive disorders. Tobacco-related diseases cause more than 8 million deaths every year. Among these, more than 7 million fatalities are due to direct tobacco use among smokers, while 1.2 million occur due to second-hand smoke exposure.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating nicotine addiction, inflammation, external and internal parasite disorders, wounds, and eye infections. The alkaloids found within the leaves act as a potent agent to combat various types of parasitic infestations. When applied topically, the plant material helps to clean and accelerate the healing process of a wound. Its medicinal properties are also utilized to reduce swelling and manage systemic inflammation. Additionally, diluted extracts can be employed in traditional remedies to address certain types of eye infections.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a tincture, smoke, infusion, or poultice to utilize its various alkaloids. A concentrated tincture is often created by soaking the leaves in alcohol to extract medicinal compounds for liquid dosing. Many users consume the dried leaves as smoke through traditional smoking methods. An infusion involves steeping the plant material in hot water to create a medicinal tea. Finally, a poultice can be made by applying crushed, moist leaves directly to the skin for topical application.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a wide range of adverse physiological reactions including nausea, dizziness, increased heart rate, increased blood pressure, headache, tachycardia, insomnia, anxiety, vomiting, palpitations, diarrhea, tremors, anorexia, and constipation. The presence of nicotine often leads to cardiovascular stimulation characterized by palpitations, tachycardia, and elevated blood pressure. Gastrointestinal distress frequently manifests through symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, and anorexia. Neurological and psychological impacts may include headaches, dizziness, anxiety, insomnia, and physical tremors. These varied side effects reflect the potent stimulant properties inherent to the plant's chemical composition.
