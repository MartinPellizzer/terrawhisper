Stellaria media

What's the taxonomical classification of Stellaria media?

Stellaria media belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant follows the order Caryophyllales and is a member of the family Caryophyllaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is completed within the genus Stellaria, specifically as the species media.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Stellaria media has slender, branching stems that are typically reddish or green and feature small, opposite, oval-shaped leaves with entire margins. The plant produces tiny, white, star-shaped flowers that consist of five spreading petals and are held in the leaf axils. Its root system is composed of a delicate, shallow fibrous structure that allows it to colonize moist soil quickly. Small, rounded seed capsules develop after pollination to facilitate the dispersal of numerous minute seeds. This herbaceous annual remains low to the ground, often forming dense, spreading mats in disturbed habitats.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe and parts of Asia but has become naturalized across much of the temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, including North America and Australia. It is commonly found in disturbed soils such as gardens, roadsides, and agricultural fields where sunlight is abundant. Due to its ability to thrive in various climates, it can be located from tropical to temperate zones provided there is adequate moisture. Its widespread distribution is largely driven by its tendency to spread through human activity and seed dispersal in cultivated landscapes. The species continues to expand its range wherever disturbed ground offers a suitable growing environment.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Stellaria media contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its entire aerial portion, including the leaves, stems, and flowers. These succulent green parts are often prepared as a decoction or an infusion to utilize their diuretic and anti-inflammatory properties. The plant is frequently used in topical applications to soothe skin irritations or minor wounds due to its cooling effects. Additionally, the high vitamin and mineral content found throughout the leafy structure makes it a valuable component in herbal tonics. Careful harvesting of the fresh above-ground biomass ensures the highest concentration of these beneficial phytochemicals.

What are the pharmacological activities of Stellaria media?

This plant has antibacterial, antiproliferative, antitumor, cytotoxicity, and anti-HSV-2 pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents demonstrate the ability to inhibit the growth of various bacterial strains through direct action. The plant also exhibits significant antiproliferative and antitumor properties by interfering with the cell cycle of malignant cells. Research has confirmed its cytotoxicity against specific cancer cell lines, which aids in evaluating its potential for therapeutic use. Additionally, its anti-HSV-2 activity provides a defense mechanism against the herpes simplex virus type 2.

As discussed in a study, the pharmacological activities of Stellaria media L. were investigated among 51 different extracts prepared with 3 types of solvents. The research evaluated antibacterial activity against 10 bacteria using the disc diffusion method. Additionally, the antitumor activity was assessed through an Agrobacterium tumefaciens-induced potato disc tumor assay. This study was conducted on 16 different plant species grown in Turkey.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Stellarmedin A, apigenin 6-C-alpha-L-arabinopyranosyl-8-C-beta-D-galactopyranoside, apigenin 6-C-beta-D-galactopyranosyl-8-C-alpha-L-arabinopyranoside, apigenin 6-C-beta-D-galactopyranosyl-8-C-beta-L-arabinopyranoside, and apigenin 6-C-beta-D-glucopyranosyl-8-C-beta-D-galactopyranoside. These specific glycosylated derivatives of apigenin are significant for their unique chemical structures. Each compound features a different arrangement of sugar moieties attached to the flavone core. Such complex molecular variations often influence the biological activities and pharmacological properties of the plant. Researchers study these constituents to understand their potential therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, Stellaria media contains a novel antiviral protein named Stellarmedin A. This highly basic glycoprotein has a molecular weight of 35.1 kDa and an isoelectric point of ∼8.7. The protein inhibited HSV-2 replication in vitro with an IC50 of 13.18 µg/ml and a therapeutic index exceeding 75.9. It also inhibits the proliferation of HL-60 cells with an IC50 of 9.09 µM and LoVo cells with an IC50 of 12.32 µM. Furthermore, Stellarmedin A exhibits a peroxidase activity of 36.6 µmol/min/mg protein when using guaiacol as a substrate.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Leishmania, colon carcinoma, hepatoma, herpes simplex virus type 2, and promyelocytic leukemia. Researchers have investigated its bioactive compounds for their potential to inhibit the growth of various cancer cell lines. Specifically, its medicinal properties show promise in addressing complex malignancies like hepatoma and colon carcinoma. The plant also exhibits antiviral activities that may help combat herpes simplex virus type 2 infections. Furthermore, studies suggest it could play a role in managing certain blood disorders such as promyelocytic leukemia and parasitic infections like Leishmania.

As discussed in a study, Stellaria media was collected from different areas of Pakistan to evaluate its phytochemical, toxicity, and antileishmanial potential. The dry powder of the plant was extracted with crude methanol and fractionated using n-hexane, chloroform, ethyl acetate, n-butanol, and water solvents. Qualitative phytochemical analysis tested for various classes of compounds such as alkaloids, saponins, terpenoids, anthraquinones, cardiac glycosides, coumarins, phlobatannins, flavonoids, phenolics, and tannins. Antileishmanial activity was specifically performed against the Leishmania tropica KWH23 promastigote. In a brine shrimp toxicity evaluation assay, the plant extract and its derived fractions exhibited toxicity in a safety range with an LC50 > 100.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion, tincture, poultice, decoction, or juice to utilize its various therapeutic properties. An infusion of the leaves is commonly used to support kidney function and act as a mild diuretic. For more concentrated systemic support, a tincture can be administered in small doses. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Direct contact with the sap often results in redness, itching, or painful blistering on sensitive areas of the skin. Individuals with pre-existing allergies may experience swelling or hives upon exposure to the plant's chemical compounds. If any part of the foliage is consumed, it can trigger nausea, abdominal cramping, and vomiting. These reactions can vary in intensity depending on the concentration of toxins present in the specific specimen.
