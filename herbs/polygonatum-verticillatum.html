Polygonatum verticillatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Polygonatum verticillatum?

Polygonatum verticillatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic progression continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, eventually placing it in the order Asparagales. Within this order, the plant is a member of the family Asparagaceae. Finally, it is identified under the genus Polygonatum, with the specific epithet verticillatum designating its unique species.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Polygonatum verticillatum has rhizomatous stems that produce whorls of three to five lanceolate, leathery leaves arranged around the stem. These leaves feature entire margins and acute tips, often displaying a deep green color on the upper surface. The plant produces small, bell-shaped, greenish-white flowers that hang downward from the leaf axils. These blossoms eventually give way to elongated, fleshy berries that turn a dark color upon ripening. The overall structure is characterized by its arching habit and distinct leaf arrangement.

What are the pharmacological activities of Polygonatum verticillatum?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, lipoxygenase inhibitory, tonic, and vitality strengthening pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help mitigate oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals within the body. Through its lipoxygenase inhibitory activity, the plant can effectively reduce the production of inflammatory mediators. These combined biochemical effects contribute to its classification as a potent tonic for systemic health. Ultimately, the plant serves as a traditional remedy for vitality strengthening and overall physiological support.

As discussed in a study, the rhizomes of Polygonatum verticillatum exhibit tracheorelaxaant and anti-inflammatory activities. In isolated guinea-pig tracheal tissues, the crude extract (PR) caused complete inhibition of contractions induced by carbachol and 80 mM K+. The PR extract demonstrated a 65.22% protection against carrageenan-induced rat paw edema when administered at a dose of 200 mg/kg. Furthermore, the plant showed lipoxygenase inhibitory activity in an in-vitro assay with an IC50 of 102 ± 0.19 μg/mL. These activities, which include a possible Ca2+ channel blockade mechanism, support the medicinal use of the plant in treating hyperactive airway complaints and inflammatory disorders.
