Elsholtzia splendens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its evolutionary lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by its specific genus, Elsholtzia, and its unique species designation, splendens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Elsholtzia splendens has a deciduous habit characterized by a spreading crown, pinnately compound leaves with 7 to 15 leaflets, and small, inconspicuous yellowish-green flowers. During the fruiting season, the plant produces small, winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal.     As discussed in a study, Elsholtzia splendens is rich in flavonoids and has been reported to benefit cardiovascular systems. The total flavonoids of Elsholtzia splendens, referred to as TFES, caused a concentration-dependent vasorelaxation in endothelium-intact rings at concentrations of 5-200mg/L. The endothelium-dependent vasorelaxation of TFES was significantly blocked by the NOS inhibitor N(ω)-nitro-l-arginine methyl ester at 100μM and the guanylate cyclase inhibitor 1H-[1,2,4]oxadiazolo[4,2-α]quinoxalin-1-one at 30μM. While indomethacin at 10μM did not affect the vasorelaxation, the endothelium-independent effect was attenuated by the KATP channel blocker glibenclamide. These findings suggest that TFES acts through the NOS/NO/cGMP pathway and by activating KATP channels to influence vascular smooth muscle cells.

      As discussed in a study, Elsholtzia splendens Nakai has been used in North-East Asia as an ingredient of folk medicines for treating cough, headache and inflammation. The 75% ethanol extract of the aerial part of E. splendens significantly inhibited mouse croton oil-induced ear edema with a 44.6% inhibition at 400 mg/kg. This plant material also showed significant inhibitory activity against mouse ear edema induced by multiple treatment of phorbol ester for 3 days. E. splendens exhibited significant analgesic activity against mouse acetic acid-induced writhing at 50% inhibition at 400 mg/kg, whereas indomethacin at 5 mg/kg demonstrated 95% inhibition. Furthermore, E. splendens at 5-100 microg/mL significantly inhibited PGE2 production by pre-induced cyclooxygenase-2 of lipopolysaccharide-treated RAW 264.7 cells.
