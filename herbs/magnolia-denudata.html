Magnolia denudata

What's the taxonomical classification of Magnolia denudata?

Magnolia denudaata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Magnoliales and the family Magnoliaceae, eventually reaching its specific designation within the genus Magnolia. Ultimately, its scientific identity is completed at the species level as Magnolia denudata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Magnolia denudata has deciduous, elliptical to oblong leaves that are typically five to fifteen centimeters long and possess a smooth, glabrous surface. The foliage displays a lustrous dark green color on the upper side and a slightly paler hue underneath. During the flowering season, the plant produces large, fragrant, cream-colored blossoms that emerge before the leaves fully develop. These solitary flowers feature prominent tepals arranged in a spiral pattern around a central cluster of stamens and carpels. The woody stems and branches are characterized by a grayish bark that provides structural support for the heavy seasonal blooms.

What are the pharmacological activities of Magnolia denudata?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including potent anti-proliferative, sleep-inducing, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties. The anti-proliferative effects are particularly significant because certain extracts can inhibit the growth of various cancer cell lines. Its sleep-inducing capabilities are attributed to bioactive compounds that act on the central nervous system to promote relaxation. Additionally, the plant contains various flavonoids and lignans that contribute to its overall medicinal profile. These chemical constituents work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic potential in traditional and modern medicine.

As discussed in a study, the compounds magnoshinin and magnosalin from Magnolia denudaata exhibit anti-proliferative effects on subcultured rat aortic endothelial cells. When cells were stimulated by 5% fetal bovine serum, the inhibitory effects of magnoshinin were 2-fold greater at 10 micrograms/ml than those of magnosalin regarding the increase in cell number. Magnoshinin at concentrations of 0.1-3 micrograms/ml prolonged the starting time of DNA synthesis and reduced the incorporation rate monitored at 3 hr-intervals. Magnosalin at concentrations of 0.3-3 micrograms/ml reduced only the incorporation rate. These results suggest that magnoshinin inhibits both the competence phase and progression phase, whereas magnosalin preferentially inhibits the progression phase.
