Spineapple (Zanthoxylum clava-herculis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Spineapple (Zanthoxylum Clava-Herculis)?

Spineapple, also known as Zanthoxylum clava-herculis, is a deciduous shrub native to the southeastern United States, characterized by its spiny, compound leaves and clusters of bright red berries that are traditionally used in herbal medicine for their stimulating and digestive properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Spineapple is Zanthoxylum clava-herculis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Rutaceae, genus Zanthoxylum, and species Zanthoxylum clava-herculis. It is commonly known as Spineapple in the southeastern United States and as Hercules' Club in parts of Europe. The synonyms for this plant include Zanthoxylum clava-herculis, Zanthoxylum hirsutum, and Zanthoxylum clava-herculis var. hirsutum.

Plant Description and Morphology

Spineapple is a perennial shrub that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters, characterized by its upright, branching structure and thorny stems.

Its leaves are compound, with elongated, narrow leaflets arranged in an alternate pattern, giving the plant a distinctive, fern-like appearance. The flowers are small and yellowish-green, clustered in dense, branched inflorescences at the ends of the branches, and they bloom in late spring to early summer. The root system is fibrous and shallow, with a network of roots that spread near the soil surface, aiding in water absorption.

The plant has a woody texture, with a sharp, resinous smell, and its bark and leaves are covered in fine, bristle-like spines.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Spineapple is native to the southeastern United States, particularly Florida and the coastal regions of the Gulf Coast, as well as parts of Texas and Louisiana.

It thrives in wetlands, marshes, and swampy areas, often along the edges of water bodies. Its geographic distribution extends into the Caribbean and parts of Central America, where it grows in similar moist, tropical environments. The plant prefers warm, humid climates with high rainfall and well-drained, nutrient-rich soils.

It commonly occurs in ecologically diverse habitats such as mangrove forests and tidal flats.
