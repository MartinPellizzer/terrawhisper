Selaginella moellendorffii

What's the taxonomical classification of Selaginella moellendorffii?

Selaginella moellendorffii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Lycopodiidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Selaginellales and the family Selaginellaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized within the genus Selaginella, with its specific identification as the species moellendorffii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Selaginella moellendorffii has a creeping rhizomatous habit with small, scale-like microphylls that are arranged in four distinct rows along the stems. These diminutive leaves are typically lanceolate or ovate in shape and exhibit a bright green color. The plant grows in dense, moss-like tufts or mats, often forming intricate clusters in humid environments. Its branching pattern is relatively regular, contributing to a highly textured and delicate appearance. The stems are slender and serve as the primary structural support for the tightly packed foliage.

As discussed in a study, the genome of Selaginella moellendorffii v1.0 contains ten novel, chitin-specific Hevein-like lectins that show potential as anti-SARS-CoV-2 molecules. The lectin Smo446851 interacted with Asn343-located complex N-glycan with a binding free energy of -17.5 Kcal/mol. The Smo125663 lectin showed a binding free energy of -13.0 Kcal/mol, while the Smo99732 lectin interacted with RBD residues with a binding free energy of -26.5 Kcal/mol. Among the complexes, the Smo99732-RBD complex was characterized by higher stability and minimum deformity during molecular dynamics simulations. These in silico findings suggest these lectins are potential candidates targeting key N-glycan sites crucial to viral virulence and infection.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds carnosol, gingketin, miltiradiene, selamoellenin B-D, and tripitolide. These diverse chemical constituents contribute to the complex pharmacological profile of the species. Each specific compound offers unique biochemical properties that researchers study for potential therapeutic applications. For example, certain molecules within this group are being investigated for their anti-inflammatory or cytotoxic effects. Understanding the concentration and interaction of these substances is essential for developing new medical treatments.

As discussed in a study, Selaginella moellendorffii miltiradiene synthase (SmMDS) catalyzes the cyclization of (E,E,E)-geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP) to miltiradiene. This miltiradiene serves as a crucial precursor for important medicinal compounds including tripolide, ecabet sodium, and carnosol. The synthetic process involves intermediates such as (+)-copalyl diphosphate (CPP), pimar-15-en-8-yl+, and copa-15-yl+. The study utilized mutagenesis to confirm the presence of the pimar-15-en-8-yl+ intermediate. Researchers also determined both the apo-state and the GGPP-bound state crystal structures of SmMDS to analyze the catalysis process.
