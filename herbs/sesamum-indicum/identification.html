Botanical Identification of Sesame (Sesamum indicum)

To identify Sesame (Sesamum indicum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Sesame (Sesamum indicum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Sesame, or Sesamum indicum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Liliopsida, order Pedaliales, family Pedaliaceae, genus Sesamum, and species Sesamum indicum. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the grass-like group, highlighting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Sesamum indicum is the scientific name for sesame, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known as Sesamum orientale and Sesamum abyssinicum. Common names include sesame seed, gingelly, and til, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Sesamum indicum is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and ridged. Leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate, with hairy surfaces. Inflorescences are terminal panicles, bearing small, yellow flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous elliptical, brown seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Sesamum indicum can be distinguished by its alternate, narrow ovate leaves with slightly serrated edges, yellow flowers in clusters, and elongated capsules containing small, oblong seeds. It differs from similar species like S. orientale through leaf shape and capsule structure. The plant has a distinct, aromatic scent and grows up to 1-2 meters tall.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Sesame (Sesamum indicum) is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa and southern Asia. It thrives in warm climates with distinct dry seasons. It prefers well-drained sandy or loamy soils. It is typically grown at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Sesame plants typically bloom 60-90 days after planting, depending on climate and variety. Seed formation occurs 30-45 days after flowering. Harvesting begins when the capsules turn from green to yellowish-brown, usually 100-120 days after planting.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Sesamum indicum belongs to the genus Sesamum in the family Pedaliaceae. Its closest relatives include other species within the same genus, such as Sesamum orientale. The family Pedaliaceae is characterized by plants with opposite leaves and capsule fruits, which are key traits shared by Sesamum indicum.
