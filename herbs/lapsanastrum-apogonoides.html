Lapsanastrum apogonoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Lapsanastrum apogonoides?

Lapsanastrum apogonoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Lapsanastrum, and its unique species designation, apogonoide.

What are the therapeutic actions of Lapsanastrum apogonoides?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin conditions, digestive ailments, and respiratory issues through its traditional medicinal applications. It is frequently employed in folk medicine to soothe inflammatory skin eruptions and minor wounds. Some practitioners also utilize its extracts to address gastrointestinal discomfort and stomach pain. Additionally, the plant has been documented in traditional practices for relieving symptoms associated with coughs and phlegm. These therapeutic uses are primarily based on the plant's phytochemical properties found in its leaves and stems.

What are the herbal preparations of Lapsanastrum apogonoides?

Lapsanastrum apogonoides is prepared as a decoction by boiling the roots or stems in water to extract its medicinal components. This traditional method allows the active compounds to dissolve thoroughly into a concentrated liquid form. Practitioners may also use the plant to create infused oils or topical salves for external application. Some preparations involve drying the plant material to create powdered forms for easier storage and measurement. These various methods ensure that the plant's properties are accessible for different therapeutic uses.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Lapsanastrum apogonoides can cause localized skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap or foliage may lead to redness, itching, or swelling on sensitive areas of the skin. Some individuals might experience an inflammatory response characterized by hives or a rash upon touching the leaves. If the plant is consumed, it could result in stomach cramps, nausea, or vomiting. There is currently limited clinical data regarding the long-term toxicological effects of this specific species on humans.
