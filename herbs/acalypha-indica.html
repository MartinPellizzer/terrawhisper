Acalypha indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Acalypha indica?

Acalypha indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it falls into the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Euphorbiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Acalypha, and its species designation, indica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acalypha indica has an erect, herbaceous structure characterized by slender, cylindrical stems that can reach heights of up to 60 centimeters. The leaves are simple, oppositely arranged, and possess an ovate shape with serrated margins and acute tips. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary spikes, typically displaying a greenish to yellowish hue. The plant features a branched system with numerous lateral shoots emerging from the main stem. Its root system is composed of a central taproot that provides stability to the upright growth habit.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to tropical and subtropical regions across Asia, Africa, and Australia, frequently appearing in disturbed soils and open habitats. It is widely distributed throughout the Indian subcontinent and extends through much of Southeast Asia. Many populations have become naturalized in various parts of the Americas due to human activity and seed dispersal. Its presence is most common in warm, moist climates where it can thrive in waste areas or along roadsides. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including flavonoids, gallic acid, rutin, saponins, and tannins. These bioactive molecules work together to provide various therapeutic properties such as antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. The presence of gallic acid and rutin specifically helps to protect cells from oxidative damage. Additionally, the saponins and tannins contribute to the plant's ability to interact with biological membranes and provide astringent qualities. Together, these diverse chemical constituents form the basis of the plant's pharmacological potential.

As discussed in a study, Acalypha indica root methanolic extract (AIRME) contains a rich content of phenols at 70.92 mg of gallic acid/g and flavonoids at 16.01 mg of rutin/g. Phytochemical analyses identified 101 compounds via HR-LC-MS and 14 compounds via GC-MS. The predominant phytomolecule concentrations include ramipril glucuronide (1.563%), antimycin A (1.324%), swietenine (1.134%), quinone (1.152%), oxprenolol (1.118%), choline (0.847%), bumetanide (0.847%), and fenofibratrate (0.711%). AIRME scavenges DPPH and hydroxyl radicals at concentrations of 10-50 μg/mL, while inhibiting hydrogen peroxide and lipid peroxidation at 20-100 μg/mL. In vivo, AIRME pretreatments of 150/300 mg/kg bodyweight significantly decreased edema volume and reduced WBC, platelets, and CRP.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness if ingested or touched. Direct contact with the leaves or stems may lead to localized redness or itching on the skin. If the plant is swallowed, it frequently triggers significant gastrointestinal distress including stomach cramps and bowel issues. Some individuals may also experience systemic reactions such as feeling lightheaded or unsteady. These various physical symptoms highlight the potential toxicity associated with improper handling or consumption.
