Scientific Classification

Kalmegh, or Adhatoda vasica, is classified within the plant kingdom. It belongs to the division Magnoliophyta, class Liliopsida, order Asphodelales, family Acanthaceae, genus Adhatoda, and species Adhatoda vasica. This classification reflects its botanical relationships and medicinal significance in herbal medicine.

The scientific name of Kalmegh is Adhatoda vasica, a widely accepted binomial classification in botanical taxonomy. This name was first introduced by the botanist R. Br. in 1835, and it is recognized in various botanical databases and herbal literature.

Morphological Characteristics

Kalmegh (Adhatoda vasica) is an erect, perennial herb with a fibrous root system. It has slender, branched stems that grow up to 1-2 meters tall. The leaves are alternate, ovate to elliptic, with a slightly hairy surface. The inflorescence is a terminal spike, bearing small, pale blue to purple flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous tiny, oblong seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Kalmegh can be distinguished by its distinctive elliptical leaves with a prominent midrib and a unique bluish-green color. The flowers are tubular with two lips, and the plant has a strong, spicy aroma. These features help differentiate it from similar species like Adhatoda heterophylla.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Kalmegh (Adhatoda vasica) is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly India, Nepal, and Sri Lanka. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils and is commonly found at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Kalmegh (Adhatoda vasica) blooms from April to June, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs from July to September. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested during its flowering period, from April to June, when the leaves and flowers are most distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Adhatoda vasica belongs to the Acanthaceae family, sharing close relationships with species like Adhatoda heterophylla and Justicia adhatodifolia. It is closely related to other members of the Adhatoda genus, which are characterized by their similar morphological and chemical traits. These phylogenetic links highlight its evolutionary position within the family and inform its traditional and modern medicinal uses.
