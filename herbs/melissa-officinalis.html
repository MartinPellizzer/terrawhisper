Melissa officinalis

What's the taxonomical classification of Melissa officinalis?

Melissa officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae, eventually reaching the genus Melissa. Finally, it is identified at the species level as officinalis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Melissa officinalis has a perennial, square-stemmed habit with opposite, ovate leaves that feature serrated margins and a wrinkled, rugose texture. The foliage is characterized by a light green color and a distinctively aromatic, lemon-scented quality due to essential oils. Its stems are herbaceous and often develop a woody base as the plant matures over several seasons. During the flowering period, the plant produces small, two-lipped flowers that are typically white or pale lilac. These blossoms emerge from axillary clusters and are primarily arranged in verticillasters along the upper portions of the stem.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a wide geographic range spanning Southern Europe, North Africa, and Western Asia. It thrives in various climates ranging from the Mediterranean basin to the temperate regions of the Middle East. Due to its popularity in herbal medicine and culinary applications, it has been widely naturalized in many other parts of the world. Human cultivation has successfully introduced the species to North America and parts of South America. Today, it can be found growing in gardens and disturbed soils across most temperate latitudes globally.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Melissa officinalis contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its aromatic leaves and occasionally its flowering stems. The essential oils and polyphenols found within the foliage are harvested to create infusions, tinctures, and topical preparations. These leaf extracts are widely utilized for their calming effects on the nervous system and their ability to soothe digestive discomfort. Some practitioners also utilize the plant's stems when they are heavily laden with blossoms to enhance the potency of herbal teas. Because the medicinal properties are most potent in the fresh green tissue, careful timing of the harvest is essential for efficacy.

What are the therapeutic actions of Melissa officinalis?

Melissa officinalis has diverse therapeutic actions including carminative, nervine, anxiolytic, antispasmodic, mild sedative, cholagogue, antiviral, and cephalic properties. Its nervine and anxiolytic effects help to soothe the nervous system and reduce feelings of anxiety. As a mild sedative, it can assist in calming restlessness while its antispasmodic and carminative qualities relieve gastrointestinal cramping and gas. The plant also functions as a cholagogue to promote bile flow and possesses antiviral properties that may combat certain pathogens. Finally, its cephalic action supports cognitive function and mental clarity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains rosmarinic acid, ursolic acid, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and carvacrol, all of which contribute to its diverse medicinal profile. The presence of rosmarinic acid and various phenolic acids provides the plant with significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the inclusion of flavonoids supports cellular health by neutralizing harmful free radicals. Ursolic acid is another key component known for its potential role in promoting skin health and reducing inflammation. Finally, the essential oil component carvacrol adds antimicrobial qualities that help defend against various pathogens.

As discussed in a study, rosmarinic acid is found in medicinal and spice plants such as lemon balm. The study investigated the effects of rosmarinic acid administered orally at a dose of 10 mg/kg or 50 mg/kg daily for 28 days. In ovariectomized rats, administration of rosmarinic acid at both doses induced decreases in the fructosamine concentration and HOMA-IR. Furthermore, the dose of 50 mg/kg induced a decrease in the total cholesterol and triglyceride concentrations. These results indicate that rosmarinic acid may be useful in the prevention of metabolic disorders associated with estrogen deficiency.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause sedation, nausea, dizziness, vomiting, abdominal pain, and skin irritation in some individuals. Excessive consumption of the herb may lead to a feeling of drowsiness or lightheadedness. Some people might experience digestive distress, including stomach aches or the urge to throw up. When applied topically, the leaves can sometimes trigger a localized rash or skin irritation. It is important to monitor how your body reacts to any herbal remedy to avoid these adverse effects.
