Dysosma majoensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Dysosma majoensis?

Dysosma majoensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Ranunculales. Taxonomically, it is a member of the family Berberidaceae, specifically placed within the genus Dysosma. The complete scientific designation for this plant is defined by its specific epithet, majoensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dysosma majoensis has erect, slender stems that typically reach heights between 10 and 40 centimeters and bear small, ovate leaves arranged in whorls of three to six.

The leaves are characterized by their smooth margins and relatively thin texture, often displaying a subtle greenish hue. Its inflorescence consists of small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These blossoms feature five petals and are typically yellowish or pale in color.

The plant also develops small, rounded capsules that contain tiny seeds for reproduction.

        What are the herbal preparations of Dysosma majoensis?

        Dysosma majoensis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture using the dried roots and rhizomes of the plant. These liquid preparations are the primary methods used to extract the active phytochemical constituents for medicinal purposes.         What side-effects this plant can have?

        Dysosma majoensis can cause severe neurological impairment including hallucinations, confusion, and profound disorientation due to its high concentration of tropane alkaloids. These toxic compounds interfere with the central nervous system by blocking acetylcholine receptors in the brain. Individuals exposed to the plant may experience rapid heartbeat, extreme dryness of the mouth, and dilated pupils. In more serious cases, the ingestion of this species can lead to seizures or even death. Careful avoidance of this plant is necessary to prevent life-threatening anticholinergic toxicity.
