Scientific and Botanical Profile

Tagetes, with botanical name Tagetes erecta, is a species of flowering plant commonly known as Marigold, African Marigold, French Marigold, Mexican Marigold, and African Marigold.

It belongs to the family Asteraceae, which is also known as the sunflower family, and is native to regions of Central and South America, as well as parts of Africa. Morphologically, Tagetes erecta is an annual herb characterized by upright, branching stems, narrow lance-shaped leaves with a rough, hairy texture, and bright yellow to orange flowers that are typically radiate with a central disk.

The plant is widely cultivated for its ornamental value and is also used in traditional medicine and as a natural pest repellent due to the presence of volatile oils and alkaloids in its foliage and flowers.

History and Cultural Relevance

Tagetes was used extensively in ancient civilizations such as the Aztecs, Mayans, and Egyptians for its medicinal and aromatic properties.

In Mesoamerican cultures, Tagetes, particularly the marigold, held deep spiritual significance and was used in religious ceremonies, including rites of passage and funerary practices, to guide the deceased to the afterlife. The plant was also incorporated into traditional medicine systems, where it was used to treat ailments such as headaches, fevers, and skin infections due to its anti-inflammatory and antiseptic qualities. Today, Tagetes remains relevant in folk remedies, with its dried petals still used in teas to alleviate digestive issues and as a natural insect repellent.

Its enduring presence in cultural rituals and traditional healing practices highlights its lasting impact across generations and geographies.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Tagetes erecta has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms such as fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

The plant can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used topically in the form of a poultice or infused oil for skin conditions. For adults, a typical dosage is 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision.

Due to the potential for adverse effects, it is recommended to use tagetes erecta sparingly and for short durations, ideally no longer than a few weeks without professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Tagetes erecta can be used as a medicinal plant with potential benefits for skin conditions and as a natural insect repellent, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when used in appropriate amounts, some individuals may experience mild side effects such as skin irritation, gastrointestinal discomfort, or allergic reactions. There is limited research on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with anticoagulants or immunosuppressants due to potential synergistic effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using Tagetes erecta due to insufficient evidence on its safety during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with small doses, monitor for adverse reactions, and consult a qualified healthcare professional before incorporating it into a treatment regimen.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Tagetes erecta grows best in well-drained soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, requiring full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in warm climates and needs regular watering, especially during dry periods, but should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. To ensure healthy growth, mulching helps retain moisture and suppress weeds, while periodic fertilization supports robust foliage and flower development. Harvesting should occur when the flowers are fully open but before they begin to wilt, typically in the morning to preserve their potency; the flowers can be cut with sharp scissors or shears for clean, damage-free collection.

After harvesting, the flowers should be dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area to maintain their medicinal properties, and stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to extend shelf life.
