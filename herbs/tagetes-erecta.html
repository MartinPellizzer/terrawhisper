Scientific and Botanical Profile

Tagetes, with botanical name Tagetes erecta, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

It is also referred to by various common names such as Mexican Marigold, Marigold, African Marigold, French Marigold, and Golden Marigold, reflecting its widespread cultivation and cultural significance. Native to Central America, Mexico, and South America, T. erecta has a broad geographic range that includes regions such as Mexico City, Tropical America, Southern Mexico, Central Mexico, the Caribbean, the United States, Northern Mexico, Tropical Regions of the Americas, Highland Regions of Mexico, and the Southern United States. Morphologically, it is an annual herb with erect, branching stems, narrow lanceolate leaves, and showy, bright yellow to orange flower heads composed of ray florets surrounding a central disk.

The plant is valued not only for its ornamental appeal but also for its use in traditional medicine and as a natural pest repellent.

History and Cultural Relevance

Tagetes was used by ancient civilizations such as the Aztecs, who revered it as a sacred plant with powerful medicinal and spiritual properties.

The Aztecs utilized Tagetes for its ability to treat ailments like headaches, fevers, and skin infections, while also incorporating it into religious ceremonies and offerings to the gods. In various cultures, Tagetes has been associated with protection against evil spirits and has been used in rituals to purify spaces and ward off negativity. Today, Tagetes remains a key ingredient in traditional remedies, with its essential oils still used to treat digestive issues and respiratory infections.

Its enduring presence in both traditional medicine and cultural practices highlights its deep historical significance and continued relevance in modern wellness and spiritual traditions.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Tagetes erecta has been utilized for its medicinal properties in various forms, including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer duration. Topical applications include using the crushed leaves or essential oil for skin conditions, while the powder can be taken orally or mixed into food. For adults, a typical dosage is 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision, with a lower dose.

Due to the potential for side effects, it is recommended to use tagetes erecta sparingly and for short durations, typically no longer than two weeks unless advised by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Tagetes erecta can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory, antifungal, and antimicrobial properties, but it is important to consider its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, some individuals may experience gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea or vomiting, especially with high doses. It may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and anti-inflammatory drugs, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or reducing the effectiveness of these treatments. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using Tagetes erecta due to insufficient evidence on its safety during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with small doses, monitor for adverse reactions, and always consult a qualified healthcare professional before incorporating this plant into a treatment regimen.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Tagetes erecta grows best in well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without waterlogging.

It thrives in temperate climates and is typically planted in spring after the last frost, with seeds sown directly in the ground or started indoors for transplanting. Regular weeding, mulching, and occasional fertilization help maintain healthy growth, while monitoring for pests like aphids and nematodes ensures optimal plant health. The flowers should be harvested in the early morning when they are fully open but before midday, using sharp scissors or pruning shears to cut the stems just above the node for the best yield.

After harvesting, the flowers should be dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area to preserve their potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to extend their shelf life.
