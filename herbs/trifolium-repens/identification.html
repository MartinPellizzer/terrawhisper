Botanical Identification of White Clover (Trifolium repens)

To identify White Clover (Trifolium repens) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

White Clover, Trifolium repens, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Trifolium, and species Trifolium repens. These classifications reflect its role as a flowering plant in the legume family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of White Clover is Trifolium repens, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the genus Trifolium in the legume family. Common synonyms include Trifolium pratense var. repens and Trifolium alpinum. Regional names vary, with "white clover" being widely recognized. It has also been historically referred to as "lawn clover" and "meadow trefoil."

Morphological Characteristics

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is a low-growing perennial herb with a dense, rhizomatous root system that allows it to spread easily. Its stems are creeping and often form mats close to the ground. The leaves are trifoliate, consisting of three oval, serrated leaflets, and the plant produces small, spherical flower heads that bloom in shades of white or pink. The fruit is a three-lobed capsule containing three seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

White Clover can be distinguished by its trifoliate leaves with three oval-shaped leaflets, often with a small white or pale green mark at the base. It produces small white flowers in clusters, and its stems are prostrate, often rooting at the nodes. It is commonly found in lawns and disturbed soils, differing from other clovers like Red Clover through its leaf shape and flower color.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and can grow at altitudes up to 1,500 meters. It is commonly found in lawns, meadows, and along roadsides.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

White Clover blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between May and September, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn, around August to October. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the flowering stage, usually between June and August, when the leaves are fully developed and the flowers are present.

Phylogenetic Relationships

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is closely related to other Trifolium species within the legume family. It shares genus traits with related clovers, such as trifoliate leaves and nitrogen-fixing abilities. Its family traits include compound leaves and small, pea-like flowers, which are characteristic of the Fabaceae family.
