Cydonia oblonga

What's the taxonomical classification of Cydonia oblonga?

Cydonia oblonga belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Rosales and is a member of the family Rosaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Cydonia and the specific species epithet oblonga.

According to a study published by "Current drug delivery", Cydonia oblonga possesses anti-inflammatory, anti-ulcer, and skin-protecting properties that are primarily attributed to phenolic compounds. The total phenol concentration of the dried extract was determined to be 13.34 ± 0.90 mg gallic acid equivalents per gram. A fruit extract using 70% methanol was utilized to prepare the niosomal hydrogel for evaluating these bioactive compounds. On the 12th day of treatment, histopathological findings showed that the niosomal gel promoted angiogenesis, fibroblast maturation, collagen deposition, keratinization, and epidermal layer formation. This treatment also markedly reduced inflammation in the studied full-thickness wounds.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse range of bioactive flavonoids, specifically dihydrokaempherol, kaempferol, naringenin, and quercetin. These polyphenolic compounds are primarily concentrated within the fruit's pulp and peel. Scientists have studied these specific molecules for their potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties in human health. The presence of naringenin and quercetin suggests that the plant may offer significant protective benefits against oxidative stress. Together, these medicinal elements contribute to the overall therapeutic profile of the species.

According to a study published by "Antioxidants (Basel, Switzerland)", the plant Cydonia oblonga contains four major groups of antioxidants consisting of flavonoids, phenolic acids and aldehydes, catechins, and procyanidins. The study examined two specific genotypes for Cydonia oblonga, which are O-LE-14 and O-LE-21, specifically using sample BA 29. Within the screened plant roots, the flavonoid dihydrokaemphol had an average concentration of 0.43 ± 0.01 μg·g-1. The least represented p-Coumaric acid exhibited an average concentration of 0.34 ± 0.01 μg·g-1 in the plant roots. Additionally, the catechins included epigallocatechin, which had an average concentration of 0.95 ± 0.08 μg·g-1, while the procyanidin compound procyanidin a2 represented an average concentration of 40.35 ± 1.61 μg·g-1.
