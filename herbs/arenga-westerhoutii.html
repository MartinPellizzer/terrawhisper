Arenga westerhoutii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant is placed in the order Arecales and belongs to the family Arecaceae. Ultimately, it is identified within the genus Ahenga as the specific species westerhoutii.

What are the herbal preparations of Arenga westerhoutii?

Arenga westerhoutii is prepared as a decoction of the roots, a topical poultice made from crushed leaves, and a traditional infusion using the inner bark. These various methods allow different parts of the plant to address specific medicinal needs in local practices. The root decoction is often consumed to treat internal ailments through boiled extracts. Meanwhile, the leaves are frequently mashed into a paste for external application on skin irritations. This versatility in preparation demonstrates the widespread utility of the plant in ethnobotanical applications.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Arenga westerhoutii can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if its components are ingested or handled improperly. While the plant is often utilized for its starch and fiber, raw or unprocessed parts may contain compounds that trigger sensitivity in certain individuals. Contact with the plant's sap or leaves can lead to localized redness, itching, or a burning sensation on the skin. If consumed without adequate processing, it might result in stomach upset or nausea. Users should exercise caution and ensure all parts are thoroughly prepared before any practical application.
