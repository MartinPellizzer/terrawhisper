Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the Coca-Cola Plant is Cola vera. It was first described by the botanist Carl Peter Thunberg in 1784. This name is widely accepted in botanical literature. Other synonyms include Cola acuminata and Cola nitida, reflecting historical classification variations.

Morphological Characteristics

The Coca-Cola Plant, or Cola vera, is an evergreen shrub with a compact growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly. The stem is short and thick, supporting the plant's dense foliage. Its leaves are large, oval, and glossy, arranged in a spiral pattern. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small white flowers that bloom in clusters. The fruit is a capsule containing several seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

Diagnostic Identification Features

The Coca-Cola Plant, or Cola vera, can be distinguished by its unique swollen, berry-like fruit that turns red when mature. It has large, dark green, elongated leaves and produces a stimulant alkaloid, caffeine. It is often confused with other cola species, but its fruit shape and caffeine content are key identifiers.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

The Coca-Cola Plant, or Cola vera, is native to tropical regions of West Africa, particularly Nigeria and the Congo. It thrives in warm, humid climates with high rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils and is typically found at low to moderate altitudes, usually below 1,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Coca-Cola Plant (Cola vera) blooms during the dry season, typically between December and April. Seed formation occurs after flowering, usually from May to July. Harvest timing for leaves is generally during the wet season, from August to November, when the plant is most productive.

Phylogenetic Relationships

The Coca-Cola Plant, or Cola vera, is closely related to other species within the genus Cola. It shares genetic and morphological similarities with species like Cola acuminata and Cola nitida. These relationships are evident in their shared family traits, such as capsule fruits and bitter leaves, which are characteristic of the Malvaceae family.
