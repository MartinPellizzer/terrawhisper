Dendrocalamus hamiltonii

What's the taxonomical classification of Dendrocalamus hamiltonii?

Dendrocalamus hamiltonii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Poales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Poaceae, specifically categorized within the genus Dendrocalamus, and is identified by its unique species name, hamiltonii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dendrocalamus hamiltonii has a robust, clumping growth habit characterized by large, woody culms that can reach heights of up to 25 meters and diameters exceeding 10 centimeters. The internodes are relatively long and possess a smooth, yellowish-green surface that often develops distinct ring scars. Each leaf consists of a long, slender petiole supporting an elliptical or lanceolate blade with prominent parallel venation. The branching pattern is typically concentrated in the upper portions of the culms, creating a dense, overlapping canopy. Small, inconspicuous inflorescences occasionally emerge from the branches during flowering cycles.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dendrocalamus hamiltonii?

This plant has hypoglycaemic, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities. Its hypoglycaemic properties make it a significant subject for research into natural treatments for diabetes management. The antioxidant capacity helps protect cells from oxidative stress caused by free radicals. Additionally, the plant exhibits antimicrobial effects that can inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. These diverse medicinal qualities suggest that Dendrocalamus hamiltonii holds great potential for therapeutic applications in modern medicine.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Dendrocalamus hamiltonii was among 30 hypoglycaemic medicinal plants selected for thorough studies using vacuum dried 95% ethanolic extracts. The experiments confirmed a definite blood glucose lowering effect within 2 weeks in allloxan diabetic albino rats. Using a dose of 250 mg/kg once, twice, or thrice daily, blood glucose values were brought down close to normal fasting levels. Dendrocalamus hamiltonii was identified as one of 24 samples showing significant blood glucose lowering activities. This comparative study of the experimental herbal samples helps identify more potent indigenous hypoglycaemic herbs.
