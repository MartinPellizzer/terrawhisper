Oxalis corymbosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Oxalis corymbosa?

Oxalis corymbosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Oxalidales and is a member of the family Oxalidaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Oxalis, and its specific species designation, corymbosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxalis corymbosa has trifoliate leaves characterized by heart-shaped leaflets, delicate pinkish flowers with five petals, and a slender, creeping rhizomatous stem system.

The foliage consists of small, clover-like leaves that often exhibit a reddish tint on the undersides. Its flowers emerge from the leaf axils and feature a distinct funnel-shaped structure with fine veining. The plant typically develops in low-growing mats, spreading through its underground stems to colonize available soil.

Tiny, capsule-like seed pods eventually form to facilitate the reproduction of the species.

    What are the herbal preparations of Oxalis corymbosa?

    Oxalis corymbosa is prepared as an aqueous infusion, a decoction, or a topical poultice depending on the specific medicinal application required. The leaves and stems are typically steeped in hot water to create a mild tea used for internal consumption. For more concentrated therapeutic effects, the plant material is boiled in water to extract its soluble compounds through decoction. When treating external skin irritations or wounds, the fresh plant parts are crushed into a pulp to form a direct poultice. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the dosage and delivery method to the patient's needs.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Oxalis corymbosa can cause kidney stones and mineral deficiencies because it contains high concentrations of oxalic acid. This organic acid binds with calcium in the body to form calcium oxalate crystals. When these crystals accumulate in the renal system, they can lead to the formation of painful kidney stones. Regular consumption may also interfere with the body's ability to absorb essential nutrients like calcium and magnesium. Therefore, individuals with existing kidney issues should avoid consuming this plant in large amounts.
