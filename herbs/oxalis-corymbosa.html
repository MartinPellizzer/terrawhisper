Oxalis corymbosa

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxalis corymbosa has trifoliate leaves characterized by heart-shaped leaflets, delicate pinkish flowers with five petals, and a slender, creeping rhizomatous stem system. The foliage consists of small, clover-like leaves that often exhibit a reddish tint on the undersides. Its flowers emerge from the leaf axils and feature a distinct funnel-shaped structure with fine veining. The plant typically develops in low-growing mats, spreading through its underground stems to colonize available soil. Tiny, capsule-like seed pods eventually form to facilitate the reproduction of the species.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause kidney stones, nausea, stomach cramps, and electrolyte imbalance due to its high concentration of oxalic acid. Consuming large amounts of this species leads to the accumulation of oxalates in the renal system, which frequently results in the formation of painful kidney stones. These compounds also irritate the gastrointestinal lining, triggering acute stomach cramps and persistent nausea. Furthermore, the excessive intake of oxalates can interfere with calcium absorption, potentially leading to a dangerous electrolyte imbalance throughout the body. Monitoring consumption is essential to avoid these specific physiological complications.
