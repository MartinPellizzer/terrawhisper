Coleus scutellarioides

What's the taxonomical classification of Coleus scutellarioides?

Coleus scutellarioides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it falls under the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Coleus, and its unique species designation, scutellarioides.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Coleus scutellarioides has upright, herbaceous stems that reach heights of up to one meter and are characterized by square-shaped cross sections and colorful, often purplish, pigmentation.

The leaves are oppositely arranged on the stem and possess an ovate to lanceolate shape with serrated margins. Many varieties display striking variegation, featuring patterns of deep green, bright yellow, or magenta across the leaf surface. The foliage often exhibits a velvety texture due to fine hairs covering the leaf blades.

Small, inconspicuous flowers typically emerge from terminal spikes in the later stages of growth.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Coleus scutellarioides targets various enzyme systems and signaling pathways, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes and modulating proinflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha). These interactions primarily occur through bioactive compounds such as diterpenoids and polyphenols that disrupt the arachidonic acid cascade. By suppressing COX-2 activity, the plant reduces the production of prostaglandins responsible for inflammation and pain. Additionally, its secondary metabolites interact with nuclear factor kappa B (NF-kB) signaling to downregulate the expression of various inflammatory mediators. This multi-targeted approach provides the basis for its observed anti-inflammatory and antioxidant medicinal properties.

      What are the herbal preparations of Coleus scutellarioides?

      Coleus scutellarioides is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice using its leaves and roots. Practitioners often boil the dried plant parts in water to extract concentrated medicinal compounds for oral consumption. Liquid extracts or tinctures may also be produced by soaking the plant in alcohol to ensure a longer shelf life. For external ailments, the crushed fresh leaves are applied directly to the skin as a warm compress. These various forms allow for both systemic absorption through digestion and localized treatment of skin irritations.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Coleus scutellarioides can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive upset if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap may result in redness, itching, or a localized rash on sensitive skin. Some individuals might experience more severe dermatological responses such as swelling or blistering upon exposure. If accidentally swallowed, the plant could trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal discomfort. It is generally recommended to use gloves when handling the foliage to minimize these risks.
