Vicia sativa

What's the taxonomical classification of Vicia sativa?

Vicia sativa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae, which is characteristic of many nitrogen-fixing legumes. Finally, the plant is defined by the genus Vicia and is specifically identified by the species epithet sativa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Vicia sativa has an erect or ascending herbaceous habit characterized by slender, cylindrical stems that often reach heights of 20 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are pinnately compound with multiple pairs of leaflets and terminate in a tendril used for climbing. Small, ovate leaflets are typically arranged along the leaf axis, providing a dense green appearance to the foliage. The flowers are papilionaceous in shape, displaying a range of colors from violet to pale blue or white. These blooms are borne in small clusters or individually from the leaf axils. Each plant typically features a branched root system that supports its climbing or scrambling growth pattern.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe and Western Asia, spanning from the Atlantic coast of the Iberian Peninsula to the mountains of Central Asia. It thrives in diverse Mediterranean and temperate climates where it can be found in meadows, pastures, and disturbed soils. Its range extends through much of the Mediterranean basin and into the Middle East and parts of North Africa. Due to its use as forage and green manure, it has been widely introduced to other continents by human activity. Consequently, it is now commonly established in many temperate regions across North America and other parts of the world.

What are the pharmacological activities of Vicia sativa?

This plant has anti-acne, antifungal, cathepsin D inhibitor, and radical scavenging activities. Its antifungal properties allow it to combat various pathogenic fungal strains effectively. The inhibition of cathepsin D suggests potential roles in regulating lysosomal proteases within biological systems. Additionally, its radical scavenging activity provides significant antioxidant protection by neutralizing harmful free radicals. These diverse pharmacological actions highlight the therapeutic potential of Vicia sativa in medical research.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Vicia sativa exhibit anti-acne activity. This activity is being reported for the first time in the study. The research evaluated 157 extracts from 10 fungi and 58 plants to identify growth-inhibitory and anti-biofilm activity against P. acnes. Among the species that showed growth inhibitory activity, MICs ranged from 64-256 μg mL-1. The study concluded that species used in traditional medicine for the skin exhibited significantly greater (p < 0.05) activity than random species.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a complex profile of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, flavanone, saponins, terpenoids, and tannins. The presence of flavonoids and flavanone provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the botanical extract. Saponins contribute to the plant's ability to interact with cell membranes and may offer immune-modulating effects. Additionally, the terpenoids function as potent bioactive agents that often possess antimicrobial qualities. Finally, the tannins provide astringent properties that can assist in wound healing and digestive health.

As discussed in a study, Vicia sativa is one of the most promising medicinal plants of the Pothohar region for future studies. Phytochemical analysis indicates that flavonoids, phenolics, saponins, tannins, and terpenoids were present in almost all plants studied. Among the tested biological activities, Vicia sativa demonstrated maximum fungal growth inhibition against four pathogenic fungal strains. The study utilized extracts prepared in 3 different solvents, which were benzene, chloroform, and methanol. The investigation involved testing antioxidant activities through total antioxidant capacity, total reducing power, and 2, 2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) scavenging assay.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment of acne vulgaris and the prevention of fungal growth. These specific applications leverage the biological properties of the species to address skin-related issues. By targeting the underlying causes of breakouts, it helps manage inflammatory acne vulgaris. Furthermore, its natural components work to inhibit the development of various types of fungal growth. This dual action makes it a versatile option for dermatological concerns.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe poisoning in livestock if consumed in large quantities due to the presence of toxic alkaloids. These compounds primarily target the central nervous system, often leading to symptoms such as tremors, convulsions, and ataxia. In some instances, animals may exhibit muscle weakness or sudden death following ingestion. Monitoring grazing patterns is essential because the severity of these effects depends on the amount eaten and the specific species of animal involved. Because of these risks, farmers must ensure that vetch does not dominate pasturelands.
