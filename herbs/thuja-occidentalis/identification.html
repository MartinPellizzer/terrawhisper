Botanical Identification of White Cedar (Thuja occidentalis)

To identify White Cedar (Thuja occidentalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

White Cedar, or Thuja occidentalis, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Coniferophyta, class Pinopsida, order Pinales, family Cupressaceae, genus Thuja, and species Thuja occidentalis. These classifications help identify its role in the plant kingdom and its evolutionary relationships.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Thuja occidentalis is the scientific name for White Cedar. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Common names include White Cedar, Arborvitae, and Eastern White Cedar. It is also known by regional names such as Red Cedar in some areas. Historically, it has been referred to as the tree of life due to its medicinal properties.

Morphological Characteristics

White Cedar has a slow growth rate and a conical to columnar shape. Its roots are shallow and fibrous, providing stability in moist soils. The stem is straight and cylindrical, with a smooth, aromatic bark. Leaves are scale-like, tightly packed, and measure about 1-2 mm in length. The inflorescence is inconspicuous, with male and female cones on separate trees. Flowers are small and wind-pollinated. The fruit is a small, winged seed enclosed in a cone.

Diagnostic Identification Features

White Cedar can be distinguished by its scale-like leaves arranged in crossed pairs, its flattened, aromatic twigs, and its distinctive reddish-brown, scaly bark. It is often confused with other thuja species, but its unique leaf arrangement and twig structure help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

White Cedar is native to eastern North America, primarily found in the northeastern United States and southeastern Canada. It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and cold winters. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and is commonly found in upland areas ranging from 200 to 1,500 meters in elevation.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

White Cedar blooms in early spring with inconspicuous yellow-green catkins. Seed formation occurs in late summer, with cone development taking several months. For identification purposes, harvest timing is best in late fall when the cones are mature and seeds are ready for collection.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Thuja occidentalis belongs to the family Cupressaceae and is closely related to other members of the genus Thuja. Its closest species include Thuja plicata, with which it shares similar morphological and genetic traits. Within the genus, it exhibits close relationships with Thuja standishii, and both share characteristic features such as scale-like leaves and aromatic wood. These relationships are key for accurate identification and understanding its ecological and medicinal significance.
