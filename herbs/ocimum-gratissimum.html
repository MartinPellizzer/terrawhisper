Ocimum gratissimum

What's the taxonomical classification of Ocimum gratissimum?

Ocimum gratissimum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Ocimum and is specifically designated as the species gratisimum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ocimum gratissimum has an erect, perennial woody subshrub structure characterized by quadrangular stems and opposite, ovate leaves with serrated margins. The foliage typically presents a bright green color and a slightly rugose texture on the leaf surface. Its inflorescence consists of terminal racemes containing small, tubular flowers that can vary in color from white to pale purple. These flowers emerge from clusters of bracts and eventually develop into small, nutlet-like seeds. The entire plant possesses a distinct aromatic quality due to the presence of essential oil glands embedded within the leaf tissue.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, Asia, and parts of the Pacific Islands. It thrives in warm climates where humidity is relatively high and temperatures remain consistent throughout the year. Its distribution spans from the vast landscapes of sub-Saharan Africa to the various archipelagoes in Southeast Asia. Due to its medicinal and culinary uses, humans have introduced it to many other tropical zones across the globe. It can often be found growing in disturbed soils or along roadsides in these diverse geographical areas.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ocimum gratissimum contains bioactive compounds primarily concentrated in its leaves, seeds, and essential oils extracted from the entire aerial portion of the plant. The leaves are the most frequently utilized part in traditional medicine due to their high concentration of essential oils like eugenol. These leaves are often prepared as decoctions or infusions to treat digestive issues, respiratory ailments, and skin infections. The seeds are also valued for their mucilaginous properties, which can help soothe sore throats and gastrointestinal discomfort. Additionally, the volatile oils derived from the plant's stems and foliage provide potent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ocimum gratissimum?

Ocimum gratissimum has diverse therapeutic actions including carminative, antispasmodic, antimicrobial, digestive bitter, diaporetic, expectorant, and antipyretic properties. It functions as a digestive bitter to stimulate appetite and improve gastrointestinal function. The plant acts as a carminative and antispasmodic to relieve gas and soothe intestinal cramping. Its antimicrobial qualities help combat various pathogens throughout the body. Additionally, it serves as a diaphoric and antipyretic to reduce fever through sweating, while its expectorant properties assist in clearing respiratory congestion.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds eugenol, rosmarinic acid, chicoric acid, luteolin, and kaempferol. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the plant's diverse therapeutic potential through various biological mechanisms. For instance, eugenol often provides significant analgesic and antiseptic properties. The presence of rosmarinic acid and chicoric acid enhances the plant's ability to act as an antioxidant and anti-inflammatory agent. Additionally, the flavonoids luteolin and kaempferol offer further protection against oxidative stress and cellular damage.

As discussed in a study, Ocimum gratissimum essential oil and its two active components, eugenol and myrcene, possess antihypernociceptive activity. The essential oil was tested at doses of 10, 20, or 40 mg/kg, while eugenol and myrcene were tested at doses of 1, 5, or 10 mg/kg. Treatment with 20 and 40 mg/kg of the Ocimum gratissimum essential oil and at doses of 5 and 10 mg/kg of the active components promoted antihypernociception. Additionally, the treatment with the essential oil or eugenol was effective in reducing levels of interleukin-1β in the sciatic nerve. These findings were observed in adult male C57BL/6 J mice over a period of 14 days following surgery.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion or a decoction by steeping or boiling the leaves in water to create medicinal teas. Practitioners often extract its potent aromatic compounds through steam distillation to produce a concentrated essential oil. For more intense applications, the plant is macerated in alcohol to create a tincture or crushed into a topical poultice for skin ailments. Steam inhalation of the plant's vapors is frequently used to alleviate respiratory congestion. These diverse methods allow the various active constituents to be utilized for both internal and external therapeutic purposes.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions, nausea, dizziness, gastrointestinal upset, hypoglycemia, and blood thinning effects in some individuals. Users may experience digestive issues such as nausea or general gastrointestinal upset after consumption. Some people might encounter neurological symptoms like dizziness or skin-related allergic reactions. The plant may also influence blood sugar levels leading to hypoglycemia. Additionally, its properties can contribute to blood thinning effects in the body.
