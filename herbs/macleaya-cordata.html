Macleaya cordata

What's the taxonomical classification of Macleaya cordata?

Macleaya cordata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic progression continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Ranunculales. As a member of the family Papaveraceae, this plant is further categorized into the genus Macleaya, with the specific species designation being cordata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Macleaya cordata has heart-shaped, succulent leaves that are typically fleshy, bright green, and arranged in a dense rosette at the base of the plant. The leaf margins are often smooth or slightly undulated, while the texture can vary from glabrous to finely pubescent depending on the specific specimen. This species features a compact growth habit where the leaves overlap to protect the central growing point. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the center of the rosette, often displaying vibrant colors like red or orange. The root system is generally shallow and adapted to store moisture within the succulent tissues of the foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the montane forests and cloud forests of southern Mexico, specifically concentrated in states like Veracruz and Oaxaca. It typically thrives in high-altitude environments where moisture levels are consistent due to frequent mist and fog. These populations are often found growing on rocky slopes or within the shaded understory of dense subtropical woodlands. The specific range is relatively restricted to these tropical mountain ranges, making it a localized species. Consequently, its distribution is closely tied to the unique microclimates found in these specific elevations.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Macleaya cordata contains bioactive alkaloids primarily concentrated in its roots, rhizomes, and seeds. These specific botanical components are harvested to extract isoquinoline alkaloids such as sanguinarine and chelerythrine. The roots are often processed to produce antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory compounds used in traditional medicine. What are the pharmacological activities of Macleaya cordata?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds work effectively to reduce pain and mitigate swelling in biological systems. The plant also demonstrates significant capacity to neutralize free radicals through its antioxidant properties. Furthermore, its ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogens highlights its potent antibacterial and antimicrobial potential. These diverse medicinal attributes suggest that Macleaya cordata could serve as a valuable source for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, some isoquinoline alkaloids from Macleaya cordata exhibited antibacterial, antiparasitic, antitumor, and analgesic effects. Using a pharmacophore-based approach, targets were predicted for 26 isoquinoline alkaloids identified from the plant. Out of 65 predicted targets identified by PharmaDB, 13 were confirmed by HypoDB screening. The interaction network involved 31 targets related to cancer, 12 for microorganisms, 10 for neurodegeneration, 8 for inflammation and autoimmunity, 5 for parasitosis, 4 for injury, and 3 for pain. Dihydrochelerythrine (C6) was found to hit 23 fitting targets, while Macrophaage migration inhibitory factor (MIF) hits 15 alkaloids (C1-2, C11-16, C19-25). The anticancer, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects of Macleaya cordata were noted as the most significant among many possible activities.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of isoquinoline alkaloids, specifically sanguinarine, chelerythrine, chelidonine, and dihydrochelerythrine. These specific medicinal compounds are known for their significant pharmacological activities and potential therapeutic applications. Sanguinarine and chelerythrine are particularly noted for their potent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of chelidonine adds to the complex chemical profile used in various traditional medicinal practices. Together, these isoquinoline alkaloids form a specialized chemical fingerprint that defines the plant's biological influence.

As discussed in a study, alkaloids are the primary bioactive compounds of Macleaya cordata that exhibit antibacterial, antiinflammatory, and antioxidant activities. Specific alkaloids from the plant include sanguinarine, 6-ethoxysanguinarine (6-ES), 6-methoxydihydrosanguinarine (6-MS), chelerythrine (CH), and dihydrochelerythrine (DICH). Among these, 6-ES demonstrated the ability to rapidly kill MRSA and directly suppressed the hemolytic activity of α-hemolysin. The 6-ES compound also eliminated intracellular MRSA and displayed low development of drug resistance in vitro. Furthermore, a 6-ES-loaded thermosensitive hydrogel was used to promote wound healing in mice infected with MRSA.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, bacterial infection, fungal infection, sores, and carbuncles. Its medicinal properties help reduce swelling and soothe irritated skin surfaces. People apply the plant to combat various types of infections caused by microbes or fungi. It serves as a topical remedy to promote healing in open sores and damaged tissue. Additionally, the plant is effective in managing painful carbuncles and localized skin abscesses.

As discussed in a study, Macleaya cordata is used in traditional Chinese medicine for its anti-inflammatory and antibacterial activities. In murine macrophage RAW264.7 cells, Macleaya cordata extract increased both mRNA and protein levels of HO-1. Of the alkaloids examined, only sanguinarine appeared to be responsible for these effects. At the concentration of 2 μM, sanguinarine induced nuclear accumulation of Nrf2, increased the expression of Hmox1 gene encoding HO-1 and elevated HO-1 protein levels. Sanguinarine-induced Hmox1 mRNA expression was suppressed by SB203580, a pharmacologic inhibitor of p38 mitogen-activated protein kinases (p38 MAPKs). Although the upregulation of HO-1 in RAW264.7 cells by sanguinarine was accompanied by a decrease in cell viability, sanguinarine at micromolar, non-cytotoxic concentrations elevated protein levels of HO-1 and thioredoxin 1 in primary cultures of human hepatocytes.
