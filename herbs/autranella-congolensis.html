What's the taxonomical classification of Autranella congolensis?

Autranella congolenensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized as Equisetopsida, which falls under the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Ericales and the family Sapotaecae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Autranella and is specifically designated as the species congolenis.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat stomach aches, intestinal worms, and various skin infections. Local communities often prepare decoctions from the leaves to alleviate digestive discomfort and gastrointestinal distress. The plant also possesses properties believed to help combat parasitic infestations within the gut. For topical applications, crushed parts of the plant are applied to wounds or rashes to soothe inflammation and prevent further infection. These medicinal uses are deeply rooted in traditional healing practices within its native African regions.

What are the herbal preparations of Autranella congolensis?

Autranella congolenis is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion made from its various plant parts to address specific ailments. Additionally, extracts from the plant may be administered orally in liquid form to treat internal discomfort. These different preparations allow for the versatile application of the plant's therapeutic properties.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Autranella congolensis can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive distress if ingested or handled improperly. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the chemical composition of similar species suggests potential dermal sensitivity. Contact with the plant's sap or leaves may lead to localized redness or itching in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of unprocessed parts could result in stomach aches or nausea due to unknown secondary metabolites. Users should exercise caution and avoid direct skin contact to prevent unpredictable physiological responses.
