Botanical Identification of Chinese Dodder (Cuscuta chinensis)

To identify Chinese Dodder (Cuscuta chinensis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Chinese Dodder, Cuscuta chinensis, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically within the division of Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Solanales, family Convolvulaceae, genus Cuscuta, and species Cuscuta chinensis. This classification places it among flowering plants and highlights its relationship to other members of the morning glory family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Chinese Dodder is Cuscuta chinensis, a species within the Convolvulaceae family. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Common synonyms include Cuscuta chinensis L. and Cuscuta chinensis var. chinensis. This name reflects its native origin in China and is widely recognized in herbal medicine literature.

Morphological Characteristics

Chinese Dodder (Cuscuta chinensis) is a parasitic plant with a twining growth habit. It lacks roots and attaches to host plants using haustoria. The stem is yellowish, slender, and covered with fine hairs. Leaves are scale-like and reduced, with no chlorophyll. The inflorescence is a cluster of small, greenish-white flowers. Each flower has five sepals and five petals. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Chinese Dodder (Cuscuta chinensis) can be distinguished by its pale yellow, thread-like stems and lack of leaves. It has small, white flowers in clusters and produces oblong, brown seeds. It is parasitic, lacking chlorophyll, and commonly found on host plants like soybeans and hemp. It is often confused with Cuscuta campestris but has a more slender stem and different flower structure.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Chinese Dodder (Cuscuta chinensis) is native to East Asia, particularly China, Korea, and Japan. It thrives in warm, temperate climates and prefers well-drained soils. It is commonly found at low to moderate altitudes, often growing on other plants in open, sunny habitats.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Chinese Dodder blooms from June to August, with flowers appearing in clusters. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn, typically September to October. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the early stages of seed development, when the stems are still green and the seeds are small and plump.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cuscuta chinensis belongs to the family Convolvulaceae and is closely related to other dodder species such as Cuscuta campestris and Cuscuta grata. It shares key genus traits like parasitic growth and the absence of chlorophyll. Its phylogenetic position reflects adaptations to host plants and evolutionary divergence within the family.
