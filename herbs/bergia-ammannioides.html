Bergia ammannioides

What's the taxonomical classification of Bergia ammannioides?

Bergia ammannioides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Elatinaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Bergia, and its unique species name, ammannioides.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bergia ammannioides has a small, creeping, branched rhizomatous structure with tiny, oval-shaped leaves that are arranged in opposite pairs along the stem. The plant typically grows in submerged or semi-aquatic environments, forming dense green mats. Its leaves are characterized by a single layer of cells, often appearing translucent or dark green depending on light exposure. Small, inconspicuous reproductive structures called archegonia and antheridia are produced on the underside of the leaves. This bryophyte lacks true roots, instead utilizing rhizoids for attachment to the substrate.

What are the pharmacological activities of Bergia ammannioides?

This plant has antibacterial, antioxidant, topical anti-inflammatory, and wound-healing pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. The antibacterial action allows it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. When applied to the skin, its topical anti-inflammatory effects reduce redness and swelling in affected areas. Additionally, the plant promotes wound-healing processes by accelerating the repair of damaged tissues.

As discussed in a study, Bergia ammannioides exhibits wound-healing, topical anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antibacterial activities. When formulated in 10% w/w ointments, the EtOH, HxFr, and EtFr applications resulted in 71.77%, 85.62%, and 81.29% wound healing, respectively. The HxFr fraction demonstrated a 64.5% anti-inflammatory potency relative to Voltaren and an antibacterial MIC of 104 μg/ml against Staphylococcus aureus. Additionally, EtFr showed strong antioxidant activity against DPPH, ABTS(•+), and super oxide radicals with IC50 values of 10.25 ± 0.01, 66.09 ± 0.76, and 167.33 ± 0.91 μg/ml. These findings provide scientific evidence for the efficacy of the plant in enhancing wound healing.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds quercetin, ellagic acid, kaempferol-3-O-alpha-l-rhamnoside, lupeol, and beta-sitosterol. These bioactive molecules contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of the species. Quercetin and ellagic acid are well-known flavonoids and phenolic acids that provide significant antioxidant properties. The presence of kaempferol-3-O-alpha-l-rhamnoside further enhances the plant's complex chemical structure. Additionally, the triterpenoids lupeol and beta-sitosterol offer potential anti-inflammatory and cholesterol-regulating benefits.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract and its fractions of Bergia ammannioides contain several medicinal compounds. The n-hexane fraction yielded beta-sitosterol, lupeol, cyclolaudenol, and cycloartenol. From the ethyl acetate fraction, researchers isolated quercetin, ellagic acid, kaempferol-3-O-alpha-l-rhamnoside, and quercetin-3-O-alpha-l-rhamnoside. These extracts showed significant healing results, with 10% w/w ointments of the ethanolic, n-hexane, and ethyl acetate fractions leading to 71.77%, 85.62%, and 81.29% wound healing. The n-hexane fraction demonstrated an antibacterial MIC of 104 ug/ml against Staphylococcus aureus and a 64.5% anti-inflammatory potency relative to Voltaren. Additionally, the ethyl acetate fraction exhibited antioxidant IC50 values of 10.25 +/- 0.01, 66.09 +/- 0.76, and 167.33 +/- 0.91 ug/ml against DPPH, ABTS, and superoxide radicals.
