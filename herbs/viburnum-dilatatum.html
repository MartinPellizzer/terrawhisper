Viburnum dilatatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum dilatatum?

Viburnum dilatatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Dipsacales. This plant is a member of the family Viburnaceae and falls under the genus Viburnum, specifically identified as the species dilatatum.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Viburnum dilatatum contains various bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, triterpenoids, phenolic acids, and tannins. These specific chemical constituents provide the plant with significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Research suggests that the extracted flavonoids can help neutralize free radicals within cellular structures. Additionally, the presence of triterpenoids may contribute to antimicrobial activities against certain pathogens. These medicinal compounds collectively support the plant's role in traditional herbal applications.

What are the therapeutic actions of Viburnum dilatatum?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including inflammation, digestive issues, and certain skin conditions. While not widely used in modern mainstream medicine, traditional herbal practices have utilized its components to address internal discomfort. Some historical applications suggest its efficacy in managing swelling or soothing gastrointestinal distress. Research into its phytochemical properties continues to explore how its chemical makeup might interact with bodily systems. The specific therapeutic uses often depend on the part of the plant being processed.

What are the herbal preparations of Viburnum dilatatum?

Viburnum dilatatum is prepared as a decoction of the dried berries, a concentrated extract using alcohol, or as a topical poultice made from crushed leaves or bark. The berries are frequently simmered in water to create a medicinal tea used to address digestive or inflammatory issues. Alcohol-based tinctures allow for a more potent and shelf-stable way to consume the plant's active compounds. For external applications, the raw plant material can be applied directly to the skin to soothe localized discomfort. These various methods ensure that the medicinal properties of the plant can be tailored to specific therapeutic needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Viburnum dilatatum can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive upset if the plant is ingested or touched. While the plant is generally considered non-toxic to humans, some individuals may experience contact dermatitis from handling the leaves or stems. Ingestion of the berries is not recommended as it could lead to nausea or stomach discomfort. There is limited scientific research regarding long-term systemic effects on humans. Always consult a medical professional if you suspect an adverse reaction has occurred.
