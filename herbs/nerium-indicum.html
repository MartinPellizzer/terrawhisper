Nerium indicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Nerium indicum?

Nerium indicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its higher taxonomic ranks, it falls under the order Gentianales and is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Nerium, and its specific species name, indicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nerium indicum has evergreen, lanceolate leaves that are arranged in whorls of three or four along its woody, upright stems. The foliage is characterized by a leathery texture and a deep green color on the upper surface. Its flowers are funnel-shaped and typically appear in terminal clusters, showcasing vibrant colors like pink, red, or white. The plant produces long, slender corollas that transition into small, rounded fruit capsules. These capsules contain numerous tiny seeds equipped with tufts of hair to facilitate wind dispersal.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across countries like India, Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia. It thrives primarily in warm, humid environments found throughout the Indo-Malayan floral realm. Due to its hardy nature, it has been widely introduced to other tropical territories as an ornamental species. Its distribution is often limited by frost, confining it to areas with consistently high temperatures. Consequently, it is most commonly encountered in coastal plains and low-altitude forest fringes.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Nerium indicum contains medicinal compounds found in its leaves, flowers, and stems, though the leaves are the most frequently utilized part in traditional practices. The leaves are often prepared as infusions or decoctions to treat skin diseases and inflammatory conditions. Extracts from the flowers are sometimes employed in topical applications for their soothing properties. Because the entire plant is highly toxic due to cardiac glycosides, careful dosage is critical when using any part for therapeutic purposes. Most medicinal applications focus on the concentrated alkaloids present within the foliage to manage specific ailments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Nerium indicum?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, antiviral, immunomodulatory, and neuroprotective activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce swelling and pain by inhibiting specific inflammatory mediators in the body. Researchers have also observed its ability to inhibit the growth of malignant cells and combat various viral pathogens. Furthermore, the plant helps regulate immune responses to enhance natural defenses against disease. Finally, its neuroprotective properties suggest a capacity to shield nerve cells from damage and degeneration.

As discussed in a study, Nerium indicum leaf (NILE) demonstrates immunomodulatory activity through the up-regulation of interleukin-2 (IL-2), IL-10, and interferon-gamma, alongside the down regulation of IL-4, tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), nitric oxide, cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1), and COX-2 activities. To confirm anti-inflammatroy activity, murine lymphocytes were stimulated with 5 μg/mL concanavalin A in RPMI-1640 supplemented with 50 U/mL penicillin, 50 U/mL streptomycin, 50 U/mL nystatin, and 10% fetal bovine serum. When NILE was added at concentrations ranging from 0-80 μg/mL and cultured for 48 h, the results showed a 2.26-fold inhibition of PGE2 level at 80 μg/mL. The half maximum inhibitory concentration (IC50) for this inhibition was calculated to be 44.95 ± 0.45 μg/mL. These findings confirm the anti-inflammatroy claim of Nerium indicum used in traditional medicine.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds cardiac glycosides, oleandrin, J2, J3, and J4. These specific chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's potent biological activities. Oleandrin serves as a major bioactive component within the cardiac glycoside group. The presence of the specific compounds J2, J3, and J4 further characterizes the complex chemical profile of the species. Understanding these individual elements is essential for studying the plant's pharmacological properties.

As discussed in a study, the host plant Nerium indicum contains cardiac glycosides that are transferred to its parasites through host-parasite interaction. A total of 29 cardiac glycosides were identified within the scope of the research involving this host. Specifically, 28 cardiac glycosides were found in Nerium indicum when it was parasitized by T. chinensis. Additionally, 25 cardiac glycosides were identified in the same host when it was under attack by S. parasitica. Five cardiac glycosides were identified in both T. chinensis and S. parasitica growing parasitically on Nerium indicum.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe toxicity leading to nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, bradycardia, arrhythmia, dizziness, confusion, hypotension, seizures, coma, and death. Ingestion of any part of the plant triggers gastrointestinal distress and dangerous cardiac irregularities. As toxins affect the nervous system, the victim may experience dizziness or profound confusion. Extreme cases involve a rapid drop in blood pressure and neurological failure through seizures and coma. Ultimately, the disruption of heart rhythms and vital organ functions can result in death.
