Lepisanthes rubiginosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Lepisanthes rubiginosa?

Lepisanthes rubiginosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Sapindales. The plant is a member of the family Sapindaceae, specifically falling under the genus Lepisanthes, and is identified as the species rubiginosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lepisanthes rubiginosa has woody climbing vines characterized by ovate to elliptical leaves with entire margins and an acute apex. The foliage typically exhibits a glossy texture and a deep green color on the upper surface. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from axillary positions to form compact inflorescences. These flowers eventually develop into fleshy, globose fruits that change color as they mature. The stems are often lenticellate and provide the structural support necessary for the plant to ascend through forest canopies.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains the medicinal compounds (E)-isosafrole, safrole, t-muurolol, α-amorphene, and α-cadinol. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the complex therapeutic profile and aromatic properties of the species. The presence of phenylpropenes like safrole and (E)-isosafrole often links the plant to significant biological activities. Additionally, the sesquiterpenes α-amorphene and α-cadinol, along with t-muurolol, play crucial roles in its chemical composition. Understanding these diverse compounds helps researchers identify the plant's potential applications in pharmacology.

    As discussed in a study, twenty-four components were identified in the essential oil from Lepisanthes rubiginosa leaves, representing 99.5% of the oil. The major identified components include α-cadinol at 40.0%, safrole at 12.6%, α-amorphene at 9.5%, (E)-isosafrole at 5.0%, δ-cadinene at 4.2%, and t-muurolol at 4.1%. At a concentration of 1000 μg/mL, the oil demonstrated moderate inhibitory activity against acetylcholinesterase with an I% of 75.2%. Additionally, the oil showed an I% of 70.2% against butyrylcholinesterase.
