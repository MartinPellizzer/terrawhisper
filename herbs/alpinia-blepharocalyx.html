Alpinia blepharocalyx

What's the taxonomical classification of Alpinia blepharocalyx?

Alpinia blepharocalyx belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Zingiberales. It is a member of the Zingiberaceae family, falling into the genus Alpinia, and is specifically identified by the species name blepharocalyx.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Alpinia blepharocalyx has lanceolate leaves with prominent parallel venation and large, terminal inflorescences bearing showy, cream-colored flowers with distinctively fringed or ciliate margins on the tepals. The plant grows as a perennial herb characterized by thick, fleshy rhizomes that extend underground. Its stems are upright and pseudostems are formed by tightly overlapping leaf sheaths. The floral structure includes a prominent labellum that serves as a landing platform for pollinators. These botanical features are typical of the Zingiberaceae family and help distinguish this species within its genus.

What are the pharmacological activities of Alpinia blepharocalyx?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including antiproliferative and antiplatelet properties that suggest potential therapeutic applications. Its antiproliferative effects demonstrate a capacity to inhibit the rapid growth of certain abnormal cell lines. Additionally, the plant exhibits antiplatelet activity which can assist in preventing unwanted blood clotting processes. These diverse biological actions are attributed to the specific bioactive compounds found within its chemical profile. Further research into these mechanisms could help clarify how the plant might be used in medicinal development.

As discussed in a study, a new diarylheptaenoid called 1,7-bis(4-hydroxyphenyl)-3-hydroxy-1,3-heptadien-5-one (1) was isolated from the seeds of Alpinia blepharocalyx along with 7 other known compounds. Among these, compounds 1 and 3 showed strong inhibition of platelet aggregation in human whole blood induced by collagen, arachidonic acid, and adenosine diphosphate. Additionally, compound 3 demonstrated strong inhibition of ristocetin-induced platelet aggregation.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 6-hydroxycalyxin F, epicalyxin F, calyxin B, diarylheptanoiids, and various phenolic compounds. These specific chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the biological activities observed in the species. The presence of diarylheptanoiids suggests potential therapeutic applications in inflammatory processes. Additionally, the diverse array of phenolic compounds contributes to the overall antioxidant capacity of the plant. Researchers continue to study how the synergy between 6-hydroxycalyxin F and other calyxins affects cellular health.

As discussed in a study, the 95% EtOH extract of the seeds of Alpinia blepharocalyx contains forty-four new (1-44) and one known (45) diarylheptanoiids, eleven phenolic compounds (46-56), and beta-sitosterol glucoside (57). Epicalyxin F (17) exhibited the most potent activity against colon 26-L5 carcinoma cells with an ED50 value of 0.89 microM. Calyxin B (2) showed the most potent activity against human HT-1080 fibrosarcoma cells with an ED50 value of 0.69 microM. Several compounds including calyxins B (2) and K (11), epicalyxins F (17), I (20) and K (22), 6-hydroxycalyxin F (25), blepharocalyxin B (27), and mixtures of 7 and epicalyxin G (18) and of calyxin J (10) and epicalyxin J (21) possessed more potent activity than the drug 5-fluorouracil against HT-1080 fibrosarcoma cells.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating human HT-1080 fibrosarcoma, murine colon 26-L5 carcinoma, and platelet aggregation. Research indicates that extracts from the plant demonstrate significant inhibitory effects against the HT-1080 fibrosarcoma cell line. It also shows medicinal potential in combating the growth of the murine colon 26-L5 carcinoma. Furthermore, the plant contains bioactive compounds that can interfere with the process of platelet aggregation. These properties suggest its utility in both oncological and cardiovascular health contexts.

As discussed in a study, epicalyxin F and calyxin I are two novel diarylheptanoiids isolated from a residual fraction of an EtOH extract of Alpinia blepharocalyx. Calyxin I represents a new carbon skeleton. Epicalyxin F possesses potent antiproliferative activity toward HT-1080 fibrosarcoma with an ED50 value of 1.71 microM. This same compound also showed activity toward colon 26-L5 carcinoma with an ED50 value of 0.89 microM.
