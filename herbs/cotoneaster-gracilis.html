Cotoneaster gracilis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cotoneaster gracilis has an arching, shrubby growth habit characterized by slender, woody stems and small, elliptical, dark green leaves that are typically 5 to 15 millimeters in length. The foliage often exhibits a leathery texture and may develop reddish tints during colder months. Small, white, five-petaled flowers emerge in clusters from the leaf axils during the spring blooming season. Following pollination, the plant produces small, globose drupes that transition from green to a bright red color in the autumn. These fleshy fruits serve as a significant food source for various bird species throughout the winter.

    This plant is used to treat powdery mildew, rust, leaf spot, and scale insects that affect various ornamental species.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Cotoneaster gracilis can cause gastrointestinal distress, vomiting, and potential neurological issues if its berries are ingested by humans or pets. The small red fruits contain cyanogenic glycosides which release toxic cyanide when metabolized by the body. Symptoms of poisoning may include abdominal pain, diarrhea, or more severe symptoms like dizziness and weakness. While skin contact is generally considered safe, some individuals might experience mild irritation from handling the foliage. It is important to keep this plant away from curious children and domestic animals to prevent accidental consumption.
