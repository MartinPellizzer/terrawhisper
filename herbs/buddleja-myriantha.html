Buddleja myriantha

What's the taxonomical classification of Buddleja myriantha?

Buddleja myriantha belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical descent, it is placed in the order Lamiales and belongs to the family Scrophulariaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Buddlejia, and its unique species designation, myriantha.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Buddlejia myriantha has opposite, lanceolate to narrowly elliptic leaves that are typically 5 to 15 centimeters long, featuring finely serrated margins and a soft, pubescent texture on both surfaces.

The foliage is characterized by a distinct grayish-green hue resulting from the dense covering of fine hairs. Its inflorescences consist of dense, terminal panicles composed of numerous small, tubular flowers. These individual blossoms exhibit a pale lilac to white coloration and are arranged in compact clusters along the flowering spikes.

The overall structure of the plant is a deciduous shrub that can reach several meters in height.

      What are the herbal preparations of Buddleja myriantha?

      Buddlejia myriantha is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using the plant's leaves and flowers. These liquid preparations are often consumed to address internal ailments or skin conditions. The leaves are typically boiled in water to extract active compounds for medicinal teas. Additionally, the crushed plant material can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to soothe local inflammation. These diverse methods allow for both systemic and localized therapeutic applications.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Buddlejia myriantha can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or respiratory distress if its pollen or foliage is inhaled or touched. While the plant is primarily known for its ornamental value, some individuals may experience redness or itching upon contact with the leaves. The fine pollen released by the flowers can also trigger hay fever-like symptoms in sensitive people. Ingesting parts of the plant may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or more severe toxic reactions in pets and humans. Therefore, it is important to handle the plant with care and avoid direct contact if you have known allergies.
