Begonia augustinei

What's the taxonomical classification of Begonia augustinei?

Begonia augustinei belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Cucurbitaleales. This plant is a member of the family Begoniaceae, falling under the genus Begonia, and is specifically identified by its species name, augustinei.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin infections, inflammatory conditions, and gastrointestinal issues through its medicinal properties. It contains bioactive compounds that help reduce swelling and soothe irritated tissues. The extracts are often applied topically to address dermatological ailments such as wounds or rashes. Additionally, some traditional practices utilize the plant to manage internal discomforts related to the digestive system. Research continues to explore the specific chemical constituents that contribute to these therapeutic effects.

What are the herbal preparations of Begonia augustinei?

Begonia augustinei is prepared as a decoction, a tea, or a topical poultice depending on the specific ailment being treated. The leaves are typically boiled in water to extract active compounds for oral consumption. For external skin conditions, the fresh plant material is crushed into a paste and applied directly to the affected area. Some traditional methods involve using a tincture where the plant is steeped in alcohol to create a concentrated liquid. These various forms allow for targeted delivery of the plant's properties to different parts of the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Begonia augustinei can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested by humans or pets. Contact with the plant's sap may lead to dermatitis or localized redness on sensitive skin areas. If the leaves or stems are eaten, the presence of oxalic acid can cause painful burning in the mouth and throat. Swallowing even small amounts may result in nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramps. Proper care should be taken to wash hands thoroughly after handling this species to avoid accidental exposure.
