Brucea mollis

What's the taxonomical classification of Brucea mollis?

Brucea mollis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Sapindales. This plant is further categorized into the family Simaroubaceae, within the genus Brucea, and is specifically identified by its species name, mollis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Brucea mollis contains a complex mixture of highly bioactive limonoids, triterpenes, and various phenolic compounds that drive its therapeutic properties. These specific limonoids, such as bruceoside and various highly oxygenated structures, serve as the primary agents for its anticancer and anti-inflammatory activities. The plant also features significant concentrations of sterols and fatty acids that contribute to its overall chemical profile. Researchers have identified that these compounds work by inducing apoptosis in malignant cells and modulating immune responses. This chemical diversity makes the species a significant subject of study for developing new pharmacological treatments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Brucea mollis?

Brucea mollis has potent purgative, anthelmintic, and anti-inflammatory properties primarily derived from its complex triterpenoids and cucurbitacins. These bioactive compounds work by stimulating intestinal motility to expel parasites and metabolic waste from the digestive tract. The plant is frequently utilized in traditional medicine to treat amoebic dysentery and various intestinal infections. Additionally, its chemical constituents demonstrate significant cytotoxic activities that may inhibit the growth of certain tumor cells. Researchers are currently investigating its potential to modulate immune responses and reduce systemic inflammation.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat intestinal parasites, amoebic dysentery, malaria, and various skin infections. The bitter compounds found in its seeds act as potent anthelmintics to expel worms from the digestive tract. In traditional medicine, decoctions of the plant are often applied topically to combat fungal growths and sores. Some studies also suggest its efficacy in managing certain fever-inducing conditions. However, its high toxicity requires careful dosage to avoid severe side effects.
