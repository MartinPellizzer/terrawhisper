Brucea mollis

What's the taxonomical classification of Brucea mollis?

Brucea mollis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Sapindales. This plant is further categorized into the family Simaroubaceae, within the genus Brucea, and is specifically identified by its species name, mollis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Brucea mollis has elliptical to oblong leaves that are covered in dense, soft, velvety hairs on both the upper and lower surfaces.

The plant typically grows as a small tree or shrub with a woody stem and a spreading canopy. Its flowers are small, yellowish, and arranged in clusters that emerge from the leaf axils. The fruit is a capsule that develops into a woody structure containing several seeds.

These seeds are often surrounded by a fleshy aril which helps in their dispersal.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Brucea mollis contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its seeds, bark, and leaves. The seeds are highly valued for their potent bitter principles, which are frequently used to treat intestinal parasites and liver ailments. Extracts from the bark are often utilized in traditional medicine to address inflammatory conditions and skin issues. Additionally, the leaves can be processed to create decoctions meant to reduce fever or manage digestive distress. These various components work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic applications in folk medicine.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive secondary metabolites including triterpenoids, flavonoids, alkaloids, and phenolic compounds that contribute to its therapeutic potential.

The presence of specific triterpenoids is often linked to the plant's significant anti-tumor and anti-inflammatory activities. Flavonoids and phenolic compounds within the species provide potent antioxidant properties that help mitigate oxidative stress in biological systems. Additionally, the alkaloids found in Brucea mollis are studied for their complex pharmacological effects on various physiological processes.

Together, these chemical constituents form the basis for its traditional and modern medicinal applications.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Triterpenoids
  • Flavonoids
  • Alkaloids
  • Phenolic compounds

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Brucea mollis targets various proteins including topoisomerase I and II, certain cyclin-dependent kinases, and pro-apoptotic factors like Bax to exert its medicinal effects. The bioactive diterpenoids and triterpenoids found in the plant inhibit topoisomerase enzymes, which effectively disrupts DNA replication in rapidly dividing cancer cells. Additionally, these compounds can modulate cell cycle progression by interacting with cyclin-dependent kinases, thereby inducing cell cycle arrest. The plant also triggers programmed cell death by influencing the ratio of pro-apoptotic and anti-apoptotic proteins within the mitochondrial pathway. Through these diverse molecular interactions, the plant demonstrates significant anti-tumor and anti-inflammatory activities.

What are the therapeutic actions of Brucea mollis?

Brucea mollis has diverse therapeutic actions including purgative, bitter, anthelmintic, hepatoprotective, antimalarial, and anti-inflammatory properties.

The plant contains bitter principles that stimulate digestion and serve as a potent purgative to clear the intestinal tract. Its anthelmintic qualities allow it to effectively expel parasitic worms from the host organism. Additionally, the species exhibits hepatoprotective effects that help safeguard liver functions against various toxins.

Researchers have also identified its potential as an antimalarial agent and a natural anti-inflammatory substance for reducing swelling and pain.

The primary therapeutic actions of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Purgative
  • Bitter
  • Anthelmintic
  • Hepatoprotective
  • Antimalarial
  • Anti-inflammatory

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat intestinal parasites, skin infections, malaria, and various types of digestive disorders.

Its bitter compounds serve as potent anthelmintic agents to expel worms from the gastrointestinal tract. In traditional medicine, topical applications are often employed to clear fungal growths or inflammatory skin lesions. The plant also contains alkaloids that are utilized in managing febrile conditions associated with parasitic infections.

These therapeutic properties make it a significant component in several regional herbal practices.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Malaria
  • Amoebic dysentery
  • Skin diseases
  • Intestinal worms

What are the herbal preparations of Brucea mollis?

Brucea mollis is prepared as a decoction, a topical oil, or a concentrated extract for medicinal use. The dried seeds are most commonly boiled in water to create a bitter liquid used to treat intestinal parasites. In some traditional practices, the plant matter is processed into an oil that can be applied externally to skin ailments. Extracts derived from the plant are also utilized in more refined pharmaceutical preparations. These various forms allow for both internal consumption and localized external treatment.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Brucea mollis can cause severe gastrointestinal irritation including intense abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and profuse diarrhea due to its highly purgative properties. The plant contains potent diterpenoids that act as strong laxatives, which may lead to dehydration or electrolyte imbalances if consumed in excess. Skin contact with the plant material can also trigger localized irritation or allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Because of its significant physiological impact, accidental ingestion can result in serious toxicity. Careful moderation is essential because the therapeutic window for this plant is quite narrow.
