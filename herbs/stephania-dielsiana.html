What's the taxonomical classification of Stephania dielsiana?

Stephania dielsiana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Ranunculales. This plant is a member of the family Menispermaceae, specifically belonging to the genus Stephania, with the scientific name designated as the species dielsiana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Stephania dielsiana has a perennial habit characterized by a large, woody tuberous caudex and slender, climbing stems. These stems produce opposite or subopposite leaves that are typically ovate or elliptical in shape. The foliage features prominent venation and can vary in texture from smooth to slightly pubescent. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves to facilitate reproduction. As the plant matures, the central tuber expands significantly to store nutrients and water for survival.

What are the pharmacological activities of Stephania dielsiana?

This plant has pharmacological activities that include acting as an Aurora kinase inhibitor, an angiogenesis suppressor, and a cytotoxic agent. These biological properties suggest that the secondary metabolites found within the species possess significant potential for cancer research. By inhibiting Aurora kinases, the plant can interfere with essential cell division processes in malignant cells. Furthermore, its ability to suppress angiogenesis helps prevent the formation of new blood vessels required for tumor growth. The cytotoxic nature of these compounds allows for the direct destruction of targeted cell populations.

As discussed in a study, oxostephanine extracted from Stephania dielsiana Y.C. Wu leaves acts as an Aurora kinase inhibitor. This compound prevents histone H3 phosphorylation at serine 10, causes the mislocalization of Aurora B, and induces aneuploidy. The substance is selectively cytotoxic to human umbilical vein endothelial cells (hUVECs), while showing less cytotoxicity to human fibroblasts and umbilical cord-derived mesenchymal stem cells. Furthermore, oxostephanine significantly attenuates the migration and tube formation ability of hUVECs. These findings demonstrate that the oxostephanine from Stephania dielsiana plays dual roles in inhibiting Aurora kinase activity and angiogenesis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a variety of medicinal compounds including aporphine alkaloids and oxostepahine. These specialized chemical constituents are often studied for their significant pharmacological potential in various biological systems. The presence of aporphine alkaloids is particularly noteworthy due to their diverse range of therapeutic applications in modern medicine. Additionally, the oxostepahine component contributes to the unique biochemical profile of the plant species. Researchers continue to examine how these specific molecules interact with human physiology to develop new treatments.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical studies of the stems and leaves of Stephania dielsiana yielded two new aporphine alkaloids, labeled 1 and 5, along with six known alkaloids, 2-4 and 6-8. Compound 2 exhibited particularly strong cytotoxic activities against HepG2, MCF7, and OVCAR8 cancer cell lines. Specifically, the IC50 values for compound 2 were 3.20 ± 0.18 µM for HepG2, 3.10 ± 0.06 µM for MCF7, and 3.40 ± 0.007 µM for OVCAR8. These findings regarding the secondary metabolites of Stephania dielsiana provide a scientific rationale for further investigations of its cytotoxicity.
