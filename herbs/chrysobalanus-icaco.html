Chrysobalanus icaco

What's the taxonomical classification of Chrysobalanus icaco?

Chrysobalanus icaco belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant falls under the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Chrysobalanaeceae. Finally, it is categorized within the genus Chrysobalanus, with its specific designation being the species icaco.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Chrysobalanus icaco has evergreen, elliptical leaves that measure up to 10 centimeters in length and feature a leathery texture with entire margins. The plant develops small, white, five-petaled flowers that grow in clusters from the leaf axils. These blossoms eventually give way to fleshy, drupe-like fruits that transition from green to a deep purple or reddish color when ripe. The stems are woody and can form dense, spreading shrubs or small trees depending on the environment. Its root system is well-developed to support its growth in coastal habitats.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

What are the pharmacological activities of Chrysobalanus icaco?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including antiangiogenic, antidiabetic, antifungal, antioxidant, and hypoglycemic properties. Its rich bioactive compound profile allows it to combat oxidative stress through potent antioxidant mechanisms. The plant also shows promise in managing blood glucose levels due to its effective hypoglycemic and antidiabetic actions. Furthermore, research indicates that its extracts possess antifungal capabilities to inhibit various pathogens. Finally, its antiangiogenic effects suggest potential utility in preventing the formation of new blood vessels in disease progression.

As discussed in a study, the aqueous extract (AECI) of Chrysobalanus icaco L. possesses antioxidant and alpha-amylase inhibitor activities. Phytochemical screening of the plant revealed the presence of tannins, flavones, triterpenoids, steroids, saponins, and alkaloids. Following an oral administration of 5 g/kg of AECI, no lethality signs were observed during lethality and behavioral screening. While the extract did not inhibit glycemia elevation in the oral glucose tolerance test, chronic administration at doses of 100, 200, and 400 mg/kg produced a significant (P<.05) effect. This treatment resulted in a reduction of glycemia from 335±27 to 197±15 mg/dL.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds myricetin, rutin, and various diterpenes, alongside a diverse profile of flavonoids. These specific flavonoids serve as potent antioxidant agents that help protect cells from oxidative stress. The presence of myricetin provides additional anti-inflammatory benefits to the biological system. Furthermore, the rutin content supports vascular health and overall circulation. Finally, the diterpenes within the plant contribute to its unique therapeutic and pharmacological properties.

As discussed in a study, Chrysobalanus icaco is a medicinal plant used to treat fungal infections in the Brazilian Amazonian region. The hydroalcoholic extract of Chrysobalanus icaco contains a high amount of flavonoid content measured at 132,959.33 ± 12,598.23 μg quercetin equivalent/g of extract. Through HPLC analyses, the flavonoids myricetin and rutin were detected within the extract. This chemical composition allows the plant to show antifungal activity against 11 oral clinical isolates of Candida spp. Specifically, the extract demonstrated minimum inhibitory concentrations of 3.12 mg/mL for Candida albicans and 6.25 mg/mL for Candida parapsilosis. The activity against the Candida albicans American Type Culture Collection was even lower with a minimum inhibitory concentration of <1.56 mg/mL.

As discussed in a study, Chrysobalanus icaco is used in folk medicine as a hypoglycemic agent for the treatment of diabetes. The proteins from this species were analyzed using the filter-assisted sample preparation (FASP) method and liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS). In total, 131 proteins were identified during the proteomic characterization. While insulin-like proteins were not detected, proteins involved in controlling glucose levels were identified. The main biological functions of these identified proteins were cellular respiration, transport, metabolism and photosynthesis.
