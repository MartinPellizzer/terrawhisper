What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia splendens?

Euphorbia splendens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is categorized under the order Malpighiales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Euphorbiaceae, which places it in the well-known genus Euphorbia, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species splendens.

What are the pharmacological activities of Euphorbia splendens?

This plant has molluscicidal, antibacterial, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities that make it a subject of significant medicinal interest. The presence of bioactive secondary metabolites allows its extracts to effectively combat various pathogenic bacteria and parasites. Specifically, the molluscicidal properties are particularly useful in controlling snail populations that act as intermediate hosts for various diseases. Research suggests that these diverse biological effects are driven by complex chemical compounds found within its latex and tissues. Further studies continue to investigate how these specific properties can be harnessed for therapeutic and public health applications.

As discussed in a study, aqueous solutions of Euphorbia splendens var. hislopii latex demonstrate seasonal stability of the molluscacidal principle with LD90 values of 1.14 ppm in spring, 1.02 ppm in fall, 1.09 ppm in winter, and 1.07 ppm in summer against Biomphalaria tenagophila. The molluscacidal action of the latex in natura is stable for up to 124 days at room temperature, while the lyophilized product remains stable for up to 736 days in a common refrigerator at 10 to 12 degrees C. A 5.0 ppm solution is 100% lethal for snails up to 13 days after preparation. However, the effect is gradually lost to almost total inactivity by the 30th day, indicating the active principle is unstable.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating intestinal parasites including schistosomeiasis, stomach aches, and various skin infections.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, eye irritation, swelling, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea if its milky sap comes into contact with the body. Touching the plant may lead to redness or painful swelling on the skin. If the sap accidentally splashes into the eyes, it can cause intense irritation and inflammation. Ingesting any part of the plant can trigger severe digestive distress, including nausea and vomiting. These internal reactions are often accompanied by significant abdominal pain and diarrhea.
