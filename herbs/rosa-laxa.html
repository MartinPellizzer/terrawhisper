Rosa laxa

What's the taxonomical classification of Rosa laxa?

Rosa laxa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is classified under the order Rosales. It further falls into the family Rosaceae, which places it within the genus Rosa. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by its species name, laxa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rosa laxa has pinnately compound leaves with seven to nine leaflets that feature serrated margins and an overall elliptical shape. The plant is characterized by its arching, somewhat pendulous stems that are armed with prickles of varying sizes. Its flowers typically present five broad, rounded petals that range in color from pale pink to deep rose. The fruit develops into a fleshy, globose hip that turns red or orange upon ripening. This species often displays a sprawling growth habit due to its flexible and elongated branches.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rosa laxa?

This plant has antibacterial, antioxidant, antitumor, and antiviral pharmacological activities that demonstrate its significant medicinal potential. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative damage. The plant also exhibits antibacterial effects by inhibiting the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Research suggests that its antitumor capabilities may assist in suppressing the development of malignant cells. Additionally, its antiviral activity provides a defense mechanism against specific viral infections.

As discussed in a study, the fruits of Rosa laxa Retz. (FRL) exhibit pharmacological effects such as antioxidant, antiviral, antibacterial, and antitumo r activities. These fruits contain a rich composition of flavonoids, polyphenols, amino acids, sugars, and other bioactive compounds. Optimized reflux extraction using 65% ethanol and a 1:35 (g/mL) material-to-liquid ratio for 140 min yielded an extract containing 126.55 ± 2.61 mg/g of total polyphenols. This extraction process resulted in a 51.00 ± 1.07% extracted amount and an average euscaphic acid content of 2.90 ± 0.08 mg/g. The components euscaphic acid and tiliroside demonstrated good membrane permeability, with an efflux ratio of less than 1.5. Optimal absorption efficiency for these components was observed under weakly acidic conditions at pH 6.0.

What side effects this plant can have?

Some individuals may experience redness or itching upon touching the foliage or petals. Consuming any part of the plant might lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramping. It is important to handle the specimen with care to avoid these adverse reactions.
