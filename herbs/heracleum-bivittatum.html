Heracleum bivittatum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Heracleum bivittatum has large, compound leaves with deeply lobed leaflets and an umbel inflorescence composed of numerous small, white flowers. The stems are robust, hollow, and often feature distinct purple spotting or pigmentation. The leaf margins are serrated, and the overall foliage structure allows for significant surface area to capture sunlight. Its flowering heads consist of multiple secondary umbels that branch out from a central point to form a broad, flat canopy. The root system is typically a thick taproot that provides stability for its tall, herbaceous growth habit.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of Central and Eastern Europe, specifically spanning across countries such as Austria, Switzerland, Germany, and the Balkan Peninsula. It typically thrives in alpine and subalpine zones where the terrain is rocky or sloped. These populations are often found in high-altitude meadows or along the edges of forest clearings. The specific distribution is heavily influenced by the availability of cool, moist microclimates found in these elevations. Because it favors certain mountain ranges, its presence is patchy rather than continuous across the continent.

        What side effects this plant can have?

        This plant can cause severe skin irritation, photosensitivity, blistering, dermatitis, nausea, vomiting, and dizziness. Contact with the sap often triggers a phototoxic reaction where sunlight intensifies the damage to the tissue. This process frequently results in painful blistering and various forms of dermatitis on the affected areas. If the plant is ingested, a person may experience systemic symptoms such as nausea and vomiting. Additionally, some individuals may report feeling dizziness following exposure or consumption.
