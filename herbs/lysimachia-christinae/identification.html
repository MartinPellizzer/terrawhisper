Botanical Identification of Creeping Jenny (Lysimachia christinae)

To identify Creeping Jenny (Lysimachia christinae) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Creeping Jenny, or Lysimachia christinae, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Myrtales, family Primulaceae, genus Lysimachia, and species Lysimachia christinae. These classifications help identify its botanical relationships and place it within the broader context of plant life.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Creeping Jenny is Lysimachia christinae, first described by Christin in 1788. It is also known by several synonyms, including Lysimachia nummularia and Lysimachia europaea. Common names include Creeping Jenny, Golden Jenny, and Gillyflower. It is sometimes called Devil's Bit and has historical names like Christin's Jenny.

Morphological Characteristics

Creeping Jenny (Lysimachia christinae) is a low-growing perennial with a spreading habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it to the soil. The stems are prostrate, often rooting at the nodes, and the leaves are opposite, ovate to lanceolate, with a smooth surface and prominent veins. The inflorescence is a terminal spike, bearing small, yellow, five-petaled flowers. The fruit is a three-lobed capsule that releases tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Creeping Jenny can be distinguished by its bright yellow flowers, opposite leaves, and creeping growth habit. It lacks the woolly leaves of Lysimachia nummularia and has smaller, more rounded leaves compared to Lysimachia clethroides. Its stems root at the nodes, aiding in identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Creeping Jenny (Lysimachia christinae) is native to Europe and parts of Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters and warm summers. It prefers moist, well-drained soil and can grow at altitudes up to 1,500 meters. It is commonly found in woodland edges, stream banks, and rocky areas.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Creeping Jenny blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering period, which is key for accurate botanical identification.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Creeping Jenny, Lysimachia christinae, is closely related to species within the same genus, Lysimachia. It shares key traits with other members of the Primulaceae family, such as opposite leaf arrangement and showy flowers. Its phylogenetic placement reflects its evolutionary ties to other flowering plants with similar morphological and genetic characteristics.
