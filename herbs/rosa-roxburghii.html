Rosa roxburghii

What's the taxonomical classification of Rosa roxburghii?

Rosa roxburghii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its taxonomic hierarchy, the plant falls under the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Ultimately, it is identified by its genus, Rosa, and its specific species name, roxburghii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rosa roxburghii has arching, deciduous stems that are heavily armed with sharp, recurved prickles and feature small, glandular hairs. The foliage consists of pinnately compound leaves typically composed of five to seven elliptical leaflets with serrated margins. Its flowers are solitary and fragrant, displaying five delicate petals that range in color from pale pink to deep rose. Following the bloom, the plant produces distinctive, fleshy hips that transition from green to a bright orange or red color upon ripening. These hips are often slightly compressed and serve as a key identifying feature of the species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate regions of China, specifically spanning across provinces such as Hubei, Hunan, Sichuan, and Yunnan. It typically flourishes in mountainous areas and forest margins where the terrain provides necessary drainage. Within these habitats, the species prefers well-drained soils and moderate elevations. Its range is concentrated in East Asia, where the climate supports its perennial growth cycles. This specific distribution allows the plant to thrive in diverse microclimates found throughout the central and southwestern parts of its home country.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rosa roxburghii?

This plant has demonstrated diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, hypoglycemic, hepatoprotective, and anti-cancer properties. Its antioxidant capabilities help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also exhibits significant anti-inflammatory effects that can mitigate various swelling and immune responses. Furthermore, its hypoglycemic action assists in regulating blood glucose levels, while its hepatoprotective qualities help shield the liver from chemical damage. Finally, research suggests that its bioactive compounds may possess anti-cancer potential by inhibiting the growth of malignant cells.

As discussed in a study, Rosa roxburghii Tratt has been widely used in the Miao and Dong regions of Southwest China for the relief of sleep disorders, indigestion, anti-inflammation, neurasthenia and neuroprotection. The total triterpene components of R. roxburghii were previously found to have certain neuroprotective effects. The main ingredient, Kaji-ichigoside F1 (KF1), was found to dramatically ameliorate LPS-induced depressive like behaviors, neuronal damage, and apoptosis. KF1 also suppressed the levels of pro-inflammatary cytokines in the serum and hippocampus of mice. Through in vitro experiments, KF1 significantly reduced cell viability and attenuated apoptosis in LPS-induced BV2 microglia. These pharmacological effects were associated with activating the PPARγ/CX3CR1/Nrf2 signaling pathway and suppressing the NF-κB/NLRP3 signaling pathway.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds ellagic acid, emodin, epicatechin, kaempferol, and quercetin. These specific phytochemicals are known for providing significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. Ellagic acid and epicatechin work to combat oxidative stress at a cellular level. Furthermore, the presence of emodin and various flavonoids like kaempferol and quercetin supports overall metabolic health. Research suggests that these combined compounds may offer protective effects against chronic diseases.

As discussed in a study, Rosa roxburghii juice contains key bioactive components including Quercetin, Epicatechin, Emodin, and Ellagic acid. These compounds were screened for their ability to interact with 44 core F-OA-associated genes. The study identified that these active constituents demonstrated strong binding affinity toward central targets such as TP53. These compounds significantly attenuated cellular injury induced by sodium fluoride in cellular experiments. Mechanistically, the components worked by suppressing p53 mRNA expression and restoring proteasome-mediated p53 degradation.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the woody stems or roots to extract deep medicinal compounds, an infusion of the fragrant flowers to soothe the nerves, a tincture using alcohol to preserve the essential oils, or a fine powder made from dried petals for topical application. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the dosage and absorption rate to specific health needs. A decoction is particularly useful when targeting more resilient parts of the plant like the bark. Conversely, a gentle infusion preserves the delicate aromatic properties of the blossoms. Tinctures offer a concentrated way to store the plant's bioactive constituents for long-term use. Finally, the powder provides a versatile form for mixing into food or applying directly to the skin.
