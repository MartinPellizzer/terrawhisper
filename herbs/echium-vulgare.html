Echium vulgare

What's the taxonomical classification of Echium vulgare?

Echium vulgare belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Boraginales. It is a representative of the family Boraginaceae, specifically situated within the genus Echium, with its scientific designation completed as the species vulgarle.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Echium vulgare has a basal rosette of hairy, lanceolate leaves and an erect, branched stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 60 centimeters. The entire plant is covered in stiff, bristly hairs that serve as a defensive mechanism against herbivores. Its inflorescence consists of a dense, terminal cyme featuring vibrant blue or purple funnel-shaped flowers with prominent protruding stamens. The fruit is composed of four small, nut-like seeds produced from each individual blossom. This biennial herb eventually develops a deep taproot that supports its structural integrity throughout its life cycle.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe and parts of western Asia, though it has become widely naturalized across North America and other temperate regions of the world. It thrives primarily in disturbed soils and open habitats ranging from coastal dunes to dry meadows. Because it is highly adaptable, it has spread extensively through much of the United States and Canada. Its ability to colonize various climates has allowed it to establish dense populations in many non-native territories. This global expansion is largely due to its efficient seed production and tolerance for poor soil conditions.

These chemical constituents work to neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. By inhibiting specific inflammatory mediators, the plant helps mitigate swelling and pain in various experimental models. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal naphthoquinones alkannin and shikonin along with their various derivatives. These specific compounds are primarily responsible for the plant's notable red pigmentation and its biological activities. Research indicates that these substances possess significant anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties. The chemical structure of these derivatives allows them to interact effectively with cellular processes to promote tissue regeneration. Consequently, these compounds are frequently studied for their potential applications in dermatological pharmacology and cancer research.

As discussed in a study, Echium vulgare produces alkannin/shikonin and their derivatives, referred to as A/Sd, within its roots. During two successive three-month greenhouse experiments, researchers evaluated the production of these compounds. While certain endophytic fungi did not impact A/Sd production, inoculation with the AMF strain Rhizophagus intraradiates MUCL 49410 resulted in a significantly higher concentration of alkannin/shikonin (A/S). This specific strain also increased levels of acetyl-A/S, beta,beta-dimethylacryl-A/S, isovaleryl-A/S, and total A/Sd compared to control plants.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe liver damage, kidney damage, and hemolysis in humans and animals. Consuming the plant often leads to acute gastrointestinal distress, including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. These symptoms typically arise from the toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids present in its tissues. Once absorbed, these chemicals can lead to progressive organ failure or the destruction of red blood cells. Careful avoidance is necessary to prevent these life-threatening physiological complications.
