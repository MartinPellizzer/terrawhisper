Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis Dracunculifolia)?

Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) is a medicinal plant native to Brazil, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and analgesic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Brazilian feverfew is Baccharis dracunculifolia. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Rutaceae, genus Baccharis, and species Baccharis dracunculifolia. It is commonly known as Brazilian feverfew in South America, while in the Caribbean it is referred to as feverfew or wild quinine. Synonyms for this plant include Baccharis longifolia and Baccharis tenuifolia, which are botanical and regional variations.

Plant Description and Morphology

Brazilian Feverfew, or Baccharis dracunculifolia, is a perennial shrub native to the Atlantic rainforest of Brazil, characterized by its erect, branched stems, aromatic gray-green leaves with serrated margins, and small yellowish-white flowers arranged in dense, terminal clusters.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) is native to the coastal regions of southeastern Brazil, particularly in the states of São Paulo, Paraná, and Santa Catarina, where it thrives in sandy soils and humid tropical climates.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and aerial parts of Brazilian Feverfew are typically used medicinally, often prepared as teas, tinctures, or extracts for their purported therapeutic effects.

Phytochemical Composition

The phytochemical composition of Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) includes a variety of compounds such as flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, phenolic acids, and essential oils, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antimicrobial effects, primarily attributed to its rich content of sesquiterpene lactones and flavonoids.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) involve its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic properties, primarily attributed to compounds such as flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and essential oils that modulate inflammatory pathways and reduce pain and fever.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to alleviate symptoms of inflammation, headaches, and digestive discomfort, with specific applications in treating conditions such as rheumatism, menstrual pain, and gastrointestinal spasms.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Brazilian Feverfew include decoctions, infusions, tinctures, and topical applications, with the most common method being the use of dried leaves and flowers in tea or liquid extracts.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) typically ranges from 300 to 600 mg per day, divided into two or three doses, with the exact amount depending on the specific preparation and individual health needs, and it is crucial to follow the guidance of a qualified healthcare provider to ensure safety and efficacy.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and it should be avoided by pregnant women, those with bleeding disorders, and individuals taking anticoagulant medications.

Drug Interactions

Due to its potential to affect blood clotting and interact with anticoagulant medications, Brazilian Feverfew should be used with caution and under medical supervision when taken alongside drugs such as warfarin, aspirin, or other blood thinners.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, so it is important to consult a healthcare professional before use.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) is typically cultivated in warm, sunny climates with well-drained soil, and harvested during the flowering period, usually between June and September, when the leaves and flowers are at their peak potency.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) typically involve harvesting the aerial parts of the plant, usually during the flowering stage, followed by drying to reduce moisture content and then using methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain the active compounds.

Storage and Shelf Life

Proper storage of Brazilian Feverfew in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight is essential to maintain its potency and extend its shelf life, which typically ranges from 1 to 3 years when stored correctly.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Brazilian Feverfew (Baccharis dracunculifolia) has shown promising evidence for its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antimicrobial properties, with studies indicating potential therapeutic applications in treating conditions such as arthritis, headaches, and skin infections.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Brazilian Feverfew, or Baccharis dracunculifolia, has been traditionally used by indigenous communities in Brazil for its medicinal properties, particularly for treating fever, inflammation, and digestive issues, with historical records dating back to the early 20th century.
