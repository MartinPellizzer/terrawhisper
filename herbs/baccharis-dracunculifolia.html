Scientific and Botanical Profile

Brazilian Feverfew, with botanical name Baccharis dracunculifolia, is a flowering plant native to South America, particularly Brazil, Argentina, and Paraguay, where it thrives in tropical and subtropical regions.

Belonging to the Asteraceae family, it is also known by numerous common names such as Brazilian Feverfew, Black Elderberry, Dracunculus Baccharis, Sambucus Nigra, White Sage, Brazilian Germander, Brazilian Tea, Candelabra Bush, and Cape Marigold, among others. Morphologically, it is a shrub or small tree with rigid, often thorny branches, grayish bark, and narrow, lance-shaped leaves with a leathery texture, while its flowers are small, yellowish, and arranged in clusters, giving it the appearance of a drumstick bush.

This versatile plant is also referred to as Brazilian Baccharis, False Ragweed, Baccharis Sage, and Common Baccharis, and it is sometimes called 'Flowering Baccharis' or 'White Baccharis' due to its white or pale flowers, and it is also known as Cape Ivy, Cape Cod Germander, or Brazilian Tea, reflecting its wide regional and cultural significance.

History and Cultural Relevance

Brazilian Feverfew was used by indigenous peoples of Brazil for centuries as a medicinal plant, particularly for treating fevers, digestive issues, and respiratory ailments.

It holds deep cultural significance among several indigenous groups, who have incorporated it into traditional healing practices and spiritual rituals, often using it in smudging ceremonies or as a component in herbal teas for purification and protection. In traditional medicine systems, the plant is valued for its anti-inflammatory and antiseptic properties, with leaves and flowers traditionally brewed into infusions to alleviate headaches, muscle pain, and skin infections. Today, Brazilian Feverfew remains relevant in folk remedies, with some communities still using it to treat migraines, colds, and inflammation, reflecting its enduring role in both healing and cultural heritage.

Its continued use highlights the importance of preserving indigenous knowledge and the plant's potential for modern herbal medicine.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Baccharis dracunculifolia has been traditionally used in folk medicine for its purported therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

Preparations can be made as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion for milder effects, or applied topically for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried leaves per cup of tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

Due to limited scientific evidence, it is advised to use baccharis dracunculifolia sparingly, with a maximum duration of use not exceeding two weeks unless otherwise directed by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Baccharis dracunculifolia can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic properties, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or dizziness in some individuals. There is limited research on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with medications that affect the central nervous system or blood pressure. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient data on safety during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, always follow recommended dosages, consult a qualified healthcare professional, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Baccharis dracunculifolia grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 5.

5 and 7. 5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires moderate watering to keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged. Regular weeding and mulching around the base of the plant help control weeds and retain soil moisture, while periodic pruning encourages bushier growth and prevents disease. The plant is typically harvested during the dry season, from late summer to early winter, when the leaves and stems contain the highest concentration of active compounds; harvesting should be done by cutting the tops of the plant with clean, sharp shears to avoid damaging the plant.

To preserve its potency, the harvested material should be thoroughly dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain its medicinal properties.
