Alstonia boonei

What's the taxonomical classification of Alstonia boonei?

Alstonia boonei belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Gentianales and falls under the family Apocynaceae. Ultimately, this specific plant is identified by its genus, Alstonia, and its species designation, boonei.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Alstonia boonei has woody stems, simple elliptical to lanceolate leaves with entire margins, and small yellowish to white flowers arranged in dense terminal panicles. The leaves are typically arranged in an opposite or sub-opposite pattern along the branches. The foliage often displays a coriaceous texture, which helps the plant retain moisture in its native environment. Small, fleshy fruits develop from the flowers following successful pollination. These morphological characteristics are key indicators used to distinguish this species within its botanical family.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 10-Hydroxyyohimbine, ferulic acid, chlorogenic acid, rutin, and isoquercitrin. These bioactive molecules contribute to the plant's complex pharmacological profile and therapeutic potential. Ferulic acid and chlorogenic acid are known for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The flavonoids rutin and isoquercitrin assist in strengthening capillary walls and supporting vascular health. Additionally, the alkaloid 10-Hydroxyyohimbine plays a specific role in modulating various physiological processes within the body.

As discussed in a study, the stem bark of Alstonia boonei contains medicinal compounds including 11 alkaloids in the crude alkaloid fraction (CAF) and 6 saponins in the crude saponin fraction (CSF). Using LCMS/MS, 41 compounds were identified from both fractions that demonstrated good binding properties with 3 enzymes. Among these, Estradiol-17-phenylpropionate showed binding energies of -11.0 kcal/mol with pancreatic amylase, -10.8 kcal/mol with pancreatic lipase, and -10.1 kcal/mol with α-glucosidase. Additionally, 3α-O-trans-Feruloyl-2 α -hydroxy-12-ursen-28-oic acid had a binding energy of -10.0 kcal/mol, while 10-Hydroxyyohimbine recorded -10.4 kcal/mol with pancreatic lipase and -9.9 kcal/mol with α-glucosidase. These fractions demonstrate antiadipogenic effects through the reduction of lipid content and clearance of 3T3-L1 adipocytes.
