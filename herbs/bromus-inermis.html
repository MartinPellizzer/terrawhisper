Bromus inermis

What's the taxonomical classification of Bromus inermis?

Bromus inerbis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the lineage of the order Poaales, the plant is a member of the family Poaceae. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Bromus, and its specific biological identity is defined by the species inermis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bromus inermis has slender, upright stems that typically reach heights of 20 to 100 centimeters and are topped by open, spreading panicles containing numerous spikelets. The leaves are narrow and linear, often possessing a fine pubescence along the margins and surfaces. Each spikelet consists of several florets enclosed by lemma and palea structures that may feature small awns. The plant develops a dense, fibrous root system that provides stability in its native grassland habitats. These morphological characteristics collectively define its structure as a perennial bunchgrass.

As discussed in a study, the inflorescence of Bromus inermis was analyzed for its antibacterial properties against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus. The greatest antibacterial activities were found in the n-hexane extract produced through maceration in methanol. A relatively polar substance within the plant was identified as an effective anti-MRSAs agent with a minimum inhibitory concentration value of about 8 μg/mL. This value was compared to the 32 μg/mL minimum inhibitory concentration value for chloramphenicol. Based on a full range UV-Vis scanning of the responsible band between 200-700 nm, the medicinal compound was identified as a flavonoid.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation and physical injury to animals through its sharp, abrasive leaves and stems. When livestock graze on Bromus inermis, the stiff blades can lacerate the delicate tissues of the mouth, tongue, and digestive tract. These mechanical injuries often lead to secondary infections or difficulty swallowing for the affected animals. Additionally, the plant's tendency to form dense, aggressive mats can displace more nutritious forage, potentially leading to nutritional deficiencies in grazing herds. While it is not considered toxic, the physical structure of the plant poses a direct risk to herbivore health.
