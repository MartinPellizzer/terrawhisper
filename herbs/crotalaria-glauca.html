Crotalaria glauca

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crotalaria glauca has an erect, herbaceous growth habit characterized by slender, glaucous stems and alternate, lanceolate leaves that exhibit a waxy, bluish-green coating. The foliage typically features entire margins and a smooth texture, which helps the plant conserve moisture. Small, yellow flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying a typical pea-like structure common to the Fabaceae family. As the plant matures, it produces distinctive, inflated seed pods that are somewhat bladder-like in appearance. These pods eventually dry and release small, dark seeds that facilitate the species' reproduction.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas, ranging from the southern United States through Central America and deep into South America. It is commonly found inhabiting sandy soils and coastal dunes within these diverse latitudes. Its presence is particularly notable in sandy areas of Florida, the Caribbean islands, and parts of Brazil. The species thrives in warm climates where coastal or disturbed environments provide suitable growing conditions. This wide range allows it to colonize various lowland habitats across the neotropics.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Crotalaria glauca contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids, flavonoids, and various sterols that serve as its primary bioactive components. These alkaloids are the most significant chemical constituents and are known for their potent pharmacological activity. Some of these compounds exhibit antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties when studied in a laboratory setting. However, many of these specific alkaloids are also highly toxic to the liver and can cause serious health complications if ingested.       What are the herbal preparations of Crotalaria glauca?

      Crotalaria glauca is prepared as a decoction of the roots, an infusion of the leaves, or a topical paste made from crushed plant parts. The root decoction is frequently used in traditional medicine to treat various internal ailments through oral ingestion. For external purposes, the leaves can be macerated into a paste to address skin irritations or localized swelling. Some practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of a wash to cleanse affected areas. These diverse methods allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be delivered according to the specific therapeutic need.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Crotalaria glauca can cause severe toxic reactions in humans and animals due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids which lead to irreversible liver damage. Ingestion of these alkaloids triggers hepatic veno-occlusive disease, a condition where the small veins in the liver become obstructed. This damage can progress to liver failure, jaundice, and potentially death if the exposure is significant. Symptoms often appear after a period of incubation and may include abdominal pain, nausea, and swelling. Because these toxins are cumulative, even repeated small doses can lead to permanent organ dysfunction.
