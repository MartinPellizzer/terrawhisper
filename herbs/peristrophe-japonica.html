Peristrophe japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Peristrophe japonica?

Peristrophe japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further classified into the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Lamiales and falls under the family Acanthaceae. Taxonomically, it is identified by the genus Peristrophe, with its specific designation being the species japonica.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases such as eczema, psoriasis, and various fungal infections. It is primarily utilized in traditional medicine for its topical application to soothe irritated or inflamed skin surfaces. The plant contains bioactive compounds that help reduce itching and accelerate the healing of minor wounds. Some practitioners also apply it to address localized swelling or allergic skin reactions. Its medicinal properties are often concentrated through specific preparation methods to ensure efficacy against dermatological ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Peristrophe japonica?

Peristrophe japonica is prepared primarily as a decoction of the dried leaves or as a topical poultice made from crushed fresh plant material. The leaves are often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for internal consumption to address various ailments. Additionally, the plant matter can be macerated in alcohol to produce a tincture for more potent medicinal applications. For external skin conditions, the raw foliage is applied directly to the affected area to utilize its soothing properties. These different preparations allow practitioners to tailor the delivery method to the specific therapeutic need.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Peristrophe japonica can cause severe skin irritation, contact dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if the plant parts are ingested. The sap contains various chemical compounds that act as irritants when they come into direct contact with sensitive human skin. Individuals handling the plant may experience redness, itching, or even blistering at the site of contact. If the plant is consumed accidentally, it may lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Due to these toxic properties, careful handling and avoidance of ingestion are necessary when encountering this species.
