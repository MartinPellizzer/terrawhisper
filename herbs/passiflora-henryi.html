Passiflora henryi

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora henryi?

Passiflora henryi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its progression through the order Malpighiales, the plant is situated within the family Passifloraceae. Finally, it is identified under the genus Passiflora, specifically as the species henryi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Passiflora henryi has a climbing habit characterized by slender, twining stems that produce large, deeply three-lobed leaves with serrated margins.

The foliage typically exhibits a vibrant green color and a somewhat coriaceous texture. Its inflorescences feature complex flowers with distinctive corona filaments that surround a prominent central structure of anthers and a trifid style. The fruit develops into an ovoid or globose berry that transitions in color as it reaches maturity.

These morphological traits are essential for identifying this specific species within the Passiflora genus.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Passiflora henryi targets gamma-aminobutyric acid receptors, serotonin receptors, and various antioxidant enzymes to produce its sedative and neuroprotective effects. The bioactive flavonoids within the plant act as modulators for GABA-A receptor binding sites, which helps reduce central nervous system excitability. Additionally, certain alkaloids interact with monoamine oxidase enzymes to influence neurotransmitter levels in the brain. These molecular interactions contribute to the plant's documented ability to alleviate anxiety and improve sleep quality. The synergistic effect of these protein interactions provides a multifaceted approach to managing neurological stress.

        What are the herbal preparations of Passiflora henryi?

        Passiflora henryi is prepared primarily as a liquid infusion, decoction, or tincture derived from its dried aerial parts to extract its bioactive compounds. These liquid forms are the most common methods used to deliver the plant's calming properties to the consumer. In some traditional practices, the dried leaves and stems may also be processed into powdered capsules for easier oral administration. Tinctures offer a more concentrated dose by using alcohol as a solvent to stabilize the plant's constituents. This variety of preparation methods allows for different strengths and rates of absorption depending on the desired therapeutic effect.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Passiflora henryi can cause side effects such as drowsiness, dizziness, and potential gastrointestinal upset if consumed improperly. Because many species in the Passiflora genus contain alkaloids, they may act as mild sedatives that impair motor skills or cognitive alertness. Some individuals might experience allergic reactions including skin rashes or digestive discomfort after exposure. There is also a risk of interactions with prescription medications, particularly those used for anxiety or sleep disorders. It is important to exercise caution as the safety profile for this specific species is not as extensively documented as other passionflower varieties.
