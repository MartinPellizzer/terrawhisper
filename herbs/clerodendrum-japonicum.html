Clerodendrum japonicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Clerodendrum japonicum?

Clerodendrum japonicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As a member of the order Lamiales, it falls under the family Lamiaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Clerodendrum, with its specific identification being the species japonicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clerodendrum japonicum has opposite, ovate leaves with serrated margins and a woody, perennial shrub-like habit that reaches heights of up to two meters. The foliage typically displays a dark green color with prominent venation on the upper surface. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal panicles that bear small, tubular, white flowers with distinct purple or lavender anthers. These blossoms are followed by the development of small, fleshy, globose drupes that transition from green to a dark purple or black color when ripe. The stems are square in cross-section, a characteristic common to many members of the Lamiaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East and Southeast Asia, with primary distributions found in China, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. It thrives in temperate and subtropical regions where it often grows in forest edges and shaded areas. Some populations have also been documented in parts of Southeast Asia, expanding its range across warmer climates. Its ability to adapt to various moisture levels allows it to colonize diverse habitats within these specific longitudinal belts. Consequently, the species maintains a wide but geographically concentrated presence throughout the Asian continent.

What are the pharmacological activities of Clerodendrum japonicum?

This plant has potent pharmacological properties including significant anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. The presence of various bioactive compounds allows it to effectively reduce oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals within biological systems. Furthermore, its ability to inhibit inflammatory mediators makes it a subject of interest for treating swelling and related immune responses. Studies suggest that these combined mechanisms provide a protective effect against cellular damage and chronic inflammation. Ongoing research continues to explore how these specific chemical constituents can be utilized for clinical applications.

As discussed in a study, Clerodendrum japonicum (Thunb.) Sweet extract (CJSE) demonstrates significant pharmacological activities by reducing LPS-induced nitric oxide (NO) production in a dose-dependent manner without detectable cytotoxicity. The extract decreases the expression of inflammatory mediators including inducible nitric oxide synthase and cyclooxygenase-2, along with proinflamatory cytokine genes such as tumor necrosis factor-α, interleukin-6, and interferon-β. Mechanistically, CJSE is associated with the reduced phosphorylation of extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) and nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB) in LPS-stimulated cells. Furthermore, the plant exhibits strong DPPH and ABTS radical scavenging activities supported by substantial levels of phenolic and flavonoid compounds. These findings suggest that CJSE can serve as a potential natural source of bioactive compounds for managing inflammation and oxidative stress.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction of its roots or stems to treat internal ailments, an infusion of the leaves for calming purposes, a topical poultice for skin inflammation, or a tincture for concentrated medicinal use. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or localized swelling if the sap comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals may experience redness or itching after touching the leaves or stems. While systemic toxicity is not extensively documented in humans, caution is advised when handling the foliage to prevent unexpected reactions. It is best to avoid applying any part of the plant to broken skin or mucous membranes. Always wash your hands thoroughly after any physical interaction with the vegetation to minimize risk.
