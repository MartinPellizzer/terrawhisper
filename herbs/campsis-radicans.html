Campsis radicans

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Campsis radicans has woody, twining stems that produce large, pinnately compound leaves consisting of seven to eleven leaflets with serrated margins. The plant features trumpet-shaped, bright orange or red flowers that grow in terminal clusters. These blossoms are characterized by a long, tubular corolla designed to attract pollinators like hummingbirds. The leaves are arranged oppositely along the vines, which can climb significantly using aerial rootlets. The foliage remains mostly green, though it may turn reddish in autumn.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southeastern United States, spanning from eastern Texas through the Gulf Coast states to North Carolina and up the Atlantic seaboard. It thrives naturally in moist, well-drained soils found in riverbanks, bottomland forests, and woodland edges. While its primary range is concentrated in the humid subtropical regions of the South, it has become widely naturalized in other temperate areas due to ornamental planting. Many gardeners and landscapers have introduced it to various parts of the country to utilize its vigorous climbing habit. Consequently, it can now be found in many cultivated landscapes far beyond its original wild habitats.

    What are the herbal preparations of Campsis radicans?

    Campsis radicans is prepared primarily as a decoction, a tincture, or a topical poultice using the dried roots or bark of the plant. These preparations are traditionally used to extract active compounds for medicinal purposes. In decoction form, the hard plant material is boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid extract. Tinctures are created by soaking the plant parts in alcohol to ensure a shelf-stable solution of its constituents. Additionally, crushed plant material can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat local inflammation or wounds.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Campsis radicans can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and localized swelling when its sap or foliage comes into direct contact with human skin. Individuals with sensitive skin may experience redness, itching, or a bumpy rash after touching the vine. Some people might also encounter gastrointestinal distress if the plant parts are accidentally ingested. There is limited clinical data regarding the long-term systemic toxicity of this species in humans. Therefore, cautious handling is recommended to avoid unpredictable dermatological reactions.
