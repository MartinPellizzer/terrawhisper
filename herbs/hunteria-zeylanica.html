Hunteria zeylanica

What's the taxonomical classification of Hunteria zeylanica?

Hunteria zeylanica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Gentianales. This plant is a member of the family Apocynaceae, specifically falling within the genus Hunteria, which culminates in its unique species designation, Hunteria zeylanica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hunteria zeylanica has an evergreen tree habit characterized by lanceolate, leathery leaves that are arranged in an opposite pattern on the branches. The foliage typically exhibits a glossy dark green upper surface and a paler underside. Small, white, fragrant flowers emerge from the leaf axils and possess a tubular structure. These blossoms eventually develop into small, fleshy, ovoid fruits that turn a distinct shade of orange or red when ripe. The woody stems and bark provide a sturdy structure for the dense canopy formed by its foliage.

What are the pharmacological activities of Hunteria zeylanica?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antinociceptive, antipyretic, sedative, and vasorelaxant properties. Scientific investigations demonstrate that its extracts can effectively reduce pain and alleviate fever in various experimental models. The bioactive compounds within the plant also exhibit significant potential to suppress inflammatory responses and induce relaxation in the central nervous system. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates a capacity to promote vasorelaxant activity by influencing the tension of blood vessels. These multifaceted biological effects suggest that Hunteria zeylanica serves as a valuable source for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, oral administration of 50 mg/kg H. zeylanica alkaloid extract significantly decreased the number of writhes induced by intraperitoneal acetic acid. The extract at 100-200 mg/kg significantly increased the nociceptive threshold of the inflamed paw in the Randall-Selitto test. In the formalin test, the 100 mg/kg dose significantly decreased licking activity during the late phase. The extract inhibited the increase of vascular permeability induced by acetic acid and dose-dependently reduced yeast-induced hyperthermia. While it did not affect pentobarbital-induced sleep, the extract significantly increased locomotor activity at 100 mg/kg. These results suggest that H. zeylanica alkaloid extract possesses antinociceptive and antipyretic effects.
