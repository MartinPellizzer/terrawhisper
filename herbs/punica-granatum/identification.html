Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Pomegranate is Punica granatum, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It belongs to the family Punicaceae and is commonly known by various synonyms such as P. granatina and P. cerasifera. Regional names include "Anar" in India and "Karakat" in Iran, reflecting its widespread use and cultural significance.

Morphological Characteristics

Pomegranate is a deciduous shrub or small tree with a spreading growth habit. Its root system is shallow and fibrous, often extending near the soil surface. The stem is upright, woody, and can reach up to 5 meters in height. Leaves are alternate, simple, and ovate with a leathery texture. Inflorescences are racemose, with flowers clustered in groups. Flowers are bisexual, with five sepals and petals, and a prominent stamen. The fruit is a large, round, thick-skinned berry, containing numerous seeds surrounded by a juicy, edible aril.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Pomegranate can be distinguished by its thick, red, spiny rind and segmented, juicy arils. It has alternate, ovate leaves with a rough texture. The flowers are bright red with five petals, and the fruit is typically round with a crown-like appearance. These features help differentiate it from other similar plants.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Pomegranate is native to the region stretching from Iran to the Himalayas. It thrives in warm, dry climates with mild winters. It prefers well-drained soils and is typically found at altitudes between 500 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Pomegranate trees bloom in spring, typically between March and May, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs during the summer months, usually from June to August. Harvesting takes place in fall, around September to November, when the fruit turns fully red and the outer skin becomes thick and leathery.

Phylogenetic Relationships
