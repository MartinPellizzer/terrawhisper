Acacia leucophloea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this classification, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae, eventually reaching the genus Acacia. Finally, it is identified at the species level as leucophloea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acacia leucophloea has a small to medium-sized tree structure characterized by a distinctive white or pale grey bark and bipinnate leaves. The canopy is typically spreading and somewhat irregular in shape, providing dense shade. Its leaves consist of numerous small, oblong leaflets arranged symmetrically along the rachis. The plant produces globular, bright yellow flower heads that emerge from the axils of the leaves. These blossoms eventually give way to dark, cylindrical seed pods that hang from the branches.

What are the pharmacological activities of Acacia leucophloea?

This plant has antibacterial, antidiarrheal, astringent, spasmolytic, and vulnerary pharmacological activities used in traditional medicine. Its astringent properties allow it to contract tissues, which aids in its function as a vulnerary for healing wounds. The plant also exhibits spasmolytic effects that help relieve smooth muscle contractions. Additionally, its antidiarrheal capabilities make it useful for managing gastrointestinal distress. Finally, the presence of specific bioactive compounds provides the antibacterial action necessary to combat various pathogens.

As discussed in a study, Acacia leucophloea is a traditional medicinal plant whose bark extract can be used for the green biosynthesis of stable silver nanoparticles. The extract contains primary amines, aldehyde/ketone, aromatic, azo, and nitro compounds that participate in the bioreduction and capping of the formed AgNPs. These synthesized nanoparticles exhibit a surface plasmon resonance at 440 nm and consist of polydisperse, mostly spherical particles measuring 17-29 nm. The plant extract itself and the synthesized AgNPs demonstrate potential antibacterial activity against pathogens such as Staphylococcus aureus (MTCC 737), Bacillus cereus (MTCC 1272), Listeria monocytogenes (MTCC 657), and Shigella flexneri (MTCC 1475). This approach combines the inherent antimicrobial activity of the extract with silver metals to yield enhanced antibacterial AgNPs.
