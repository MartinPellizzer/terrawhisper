Celastrus glaucophyllus

What's the taxonomical classification of Celastrus glaucophyllus?

Celastrus glaucophyllus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, representing a specific lineage of vascular plants. Within the order Celastrales, it is placed in the family Celastraceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Celastrus, with its specific identity defined by the species name glaucophyllus.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Celastrus glaucophyllus targets cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to mediate its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. The plant contains bioactive triterpenoids and polyphenols that inhibit the enzyme cyclooxygenase-2, which is responsible for the production of prostaglandins that trigger pain and swelling. These compounds also interfere with the arachidonic acid pathway by modulating lipoxygenase activity to reduce leukotriene synthesis. Additionally, the phytochemicals in the plant can suppress the expression of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6, which are critical signaling proteins in the inflammatory cascade. Through these diverse protein interactions, the plant exerts a systemic effect on various biochemical pathways associated with inflammation and oxidative stress.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, stomach ailments, and various types of fever. The bark and leaves are often prepared into medicinal decoctions to address internal inflammations. In certain traditional practices, the plant material is applied topically to soothe skin irritations or infections. It is also utilized in folk medicine to alleviate digestive discomfort and promote gut health. These applications demonstrate the plant's role in managing diverse physiological symptoms.

What are the herbal preparations of Celastrus glaucophyllus?

Celastrus glaucophyllus is prepared primarily as a decoction of the bark, a concentrated infusion of the leaves, or a topical poultice made from crushed plant material. These traditional methods aim to extract the specific alkaloids and chemical compounds required for medicinal efficacy. When used as a decoction, the woody bark is boiled in water to create a potent liquid for internal or external use. Leaf infusions provide a milder alternative that is often consumed to address various systemic ailments. Additionally, applying a fresh paste directly to the skin allows for localized treatment of inflammatory conditions.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Celastrus glaucophyllus can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of various secondary metabolites. Contact with the plant's sap or leaves may trigger contact dermatitis characterized by redness, itching, or swelling in sensitive individuals. If swallowed, the plant might lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain as the body reacts to its chemical composition. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the general properties of the Celastraceae family suggest caution is necessary. Always handle this species with gloves to prevent accidental skin exposure.
