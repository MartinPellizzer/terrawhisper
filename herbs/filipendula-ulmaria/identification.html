Botanical Identification of Queen Anne's Lace (Filipendula ulmaria)

To identify Queen Anne's Lace (Filipendula ulmaria) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Queen Anne's Lace, or Filipendula ulmaria, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of flowering plants, class of eudicots, order of Rosales, family of Rosaceae, genus Filipendula, and species Filipendula ulmaria. This classification places it within the rose family, highlighting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Queen Anne's Lace is Filipendula ulmaria, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It is also known as Anenome ulmaria and Anemonia ulmaria. Common names include Queen Anne's Lace, White Queen's Lace, and Wild Betony. It is sometimes called the "flower of the marsh" in historical texts.

Morphological Characteristics

Queen Anne's Lace is an herbaceous plant with a taproot system. It grows upright, reaching up to 1.5 meters tall. Its stems are hollow and branched. Leaves are pinnately compound, with serrated leaflets. The inflorescence is a flat-topped umbel. Flowers are white with a purple center. Fruit is a small, dry schizocarp with two ridged halves. Seeds are small, dark, and winged.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Queen Anne's Lace can be distinguished by its flat, umbrella-like compound umbel with white flowers and a single red flower at the center. It has slender, hollow stems and feathery leaves. It is often confused with poison hemlock, but lacks the purple spots and has a distinct scent.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Queen Anne's Lace is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers moist, well-drained soils and is commonly found in meadows, stream sides, and woodland edges. It grows at altitudes ranging from 0 to 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Queen Anne's Lace blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the most reliable time to observe key features is during its flowering period, which is also the optimal time for harvesting the plant.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Queen Anne's Lace, or Filipendula ulmaria, belongs to the Rosaceae family. It is closely related to other members of the genus Filipendula, such as Filipendula ulmaris. Its phylogenetic relationships are most closely tied to species within the same genus, sharing key morphological and genetic traits.
