Botanical Identification of Cloves (Eugenia caryophylla)

To identify Cloves (Eugenia caryophylla) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Cloves, scientifically known as Eugenia caryophylla, belong to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Myrtaceae, genus Eugenia, and species Eugenia caryophylla. This classification places it within the flowering plants, specifically in the myrtle family, which includes other aromatic plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of cloves is Eugenia caryophylla, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the Myrtaceae family. Common synonyms include Syzygium aromaticum, which is also widely used in botanical literature. Regional and historical names reflect its long use in traditional medicine and spice trade.

Morphological Characteristics

Cloves are small, evergreen trees or shrubs with a compact growth habit. They have a fibrous root system that anchors the plant and absorbs nutrients. The stem is smooth, woody, and branches near the base. Leaves are opposite, oval-shaped, and have a fragrant aroma. The inflorescence is a spike with small, fragrant flowers. The fruit is a dark purple berry containing a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cloves are distinguished by their elliptical, dark reddish-brown buds with a distinctive aromatic oil scent. The leaves are opposite, oval, and have a rough texture. The flowers are small, white, and clustered, while the fruit is a small, dark purple drupe. These features help differentiate them from similar species like Syzygium aromaticum.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cloves are native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, particularly Indonesia and Madagascar. They thrive in warm, humid climates with well-drained, fertile soils. They are typically found at altitudes between 200 and 800 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cloves bloom between April and June, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, harvesting takes place when the buds are fully developed but before they open, typically in late autumn to early winter.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cloves belong to the genus Eugenia in the family Myrtaceae. Their closest relatives include other Eugenia species such as E. aromatica and E. uniflora. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, including aromatic oils and small, clustered flowers. The family Myrtaceae is characterized by aromatic leaves and flowers, which are also prominent in clove.
