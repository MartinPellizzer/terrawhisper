Acer erianthum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer erianthum has opposite, palmately lobed leaves that typically feature five to seven distinct lobes with serrated margins and a bright green color. The tree displays a graceful, spreading habit with smooth, grayish bark that becomes more textured as the specimen matures. Its small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in clusters during the spring season to precede the foliage. During the autumn months, the leaves undergo a dramatic transformation, turning vibrant shades of red or orange. The plant also produces winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal of its seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate montane regions of central and eastern China, specifically inhabiting provinces such as Hubei, Hunan, Anhui, Zhejiang, and Jiangxi. It typically thrives in moist, well-drained soils found along mountain slopes and valley edges. The species prefers cool, shaded environments often situated at elevations ranging from several hundred to over two thousand meters. These specific ecological niches provide the necessary humidity and temperature regulation required for its growth. Consequently, its natural range is concentrated within the subtropical broadleaf forests of these mountainous territories.

      What are the herbal preparations of Acer erianthum?

      Acer erianthum is prepared primarily as a decoction made by boiling the bark or leaves in water to extract its medicinal compounds. This liquid extract can also be processed into concentrated tinctures using ethanol as a solvent for long-term storage.       Acer erianthum can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive discomfort if ingested or handled improperly. Some people might experience itching or redness upon touching the bark or leaves. If parts of the plant are consumed, it could potentially lead to nausea or stomach upset. It is always advisable to exercise caution and consult a professional before any direct exposure.
