Acer erianthum

What's the taxonomical classification of Acer erianthum?

Acer erianthum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it is placed in the order Sapindales and the family Sapindaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by the genus Acer and the specific species name erianthum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer erianthum has opposite, palmately lobed leaves that typically feature five to seven distinct lobes with serrated margins and a bright green color.

The tree displays a graceful, spreading habit with smooth, grayish bark that becomes more textured as the specimen matures. Its small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in clusters during the spring season to precede the foliage. During the autumn months, the leaves undergo a dramatic transformation, turning vibrant shades of red or orange.

The plant also produces winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal of its seeds.

      What are the herbal preparations of Acer erianthum?

      Acer erianthum is prepared primarily as a decoction made by boiling the bark or leaves in water to extract its medicinal compounds. This liquid extract can also be processed into concentrated tinctures using ethanol as a solvent for long-term storage. Some practitioners utilize the dried plant material to create herbal infusions, which involve steeping the parts in hot water similar to tea. Additionally, powdered versions of the dried bark can be incorporated into topical pastes or capsules for more direct administration. These various forms allow for flexible dosing depending on whether the intended use is internal or external.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Acer erianthum can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive discomfort if ingested or handled improperly. While specific clinical data on human toxicity is limited, contact with the sap or foliage may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Some people might experience itching or redness upon touching the bark or leaves. If parts of the plant are consumed, it could potentially lead to nausea or stomach upset. It is always advisable to exercise caution and consult a professional before any direct exposure.
