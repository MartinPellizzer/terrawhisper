Nageia nagi

What's the taxonomical classification of Nageia nagi?

Nageia nagi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Pinidae, following the order Pinales. As a member of the family Podocarpaceae, it is placed within the genus Nageia, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species nagi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nageia nagi has evergreen, linear leaves that are typically 10 to 25 centimeters long, leathery in texture, and possess a distinctively sharp, pungent tip.

The foliage is arranged spirally around the woody stems, often appearing densely packed to create a lush canopy. Its reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous male cones and larger, more prominent female cones that develop from ovulate scales. These female cones gradually mature into woody, somewhat globose structures that protect the developing seeds.

The overall growth habit is that of a slow-growing tree or large shrub with a conical or irregular crown.

Furthermore, its phytochemical constituents may influence antioxidant enzymes to mitigate oxidative stress at a cellular level.       What are the herbal preparations of Nageia nagi?

      Nageia nagi is prepared primarily as an essential oil extracted from the leaves or as a liquid infusion made from the foliage. These preparations are often utilized in aromatherapy to support relaxation and emotional well-being. The concentrated oil can be diluted with a carrier oil for topical application on the skin. Additionally, some traditional uses involve incorporating the plant into tea or decoctions for internal consumption. These various forms allow the plant's aromatic compounds to be used for both therapeutic and sensory purposes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Nageia nagi can cause skin irritation, digestive upset, or allergic reactions if the plant material is ingested or comes into direct contact with the skin. While the plant is not widely documented as highly toxic, the consumption of its parts may lead to nausea or stomach discomfort. Some individuals might experience dermatitis or redness upon touching the foliage or bark. It is important to exercise caution and avoid consuming any part of this species without professional guidance. Always wash your hands thoroughly after handling the plant to prevent accidental contact with your eyes or mouth.
