Albizia mollis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Albizia mollis has bipinnately compound leaves that typically measure 15 to 30 centimeters in length and feature numerous small, oblong leaflets arranged symmetrically along secondary axes. The foliage is characterized by a soft, velvety texture due to the presence of fine hairs on both the upper and lower surfaces. These plants produce large, globular flower heads that consist of numerous long, cream-colored stamens rather than traditional petals. The tree structure is generally spreading with a delicate, airy canopy formed by slender, grayish-brown branches. Small, flat, papery pods develop from the flowers and serve as the primary seed-bearing structures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad region spanning from southern China and Taiwan through Southeast Asia to the Indian subcontinent. It thrives primarily in tropical and subtropical climates where humidity is consistently high. While its natural range is concentrated in the eastern tropics, it has been introduced to many other parts of the world as an ornamental species. In these non-native areas, it is frequently planted in botanical gardens and urban landscapes due to its attractive foliage. The plant's ability to adapt to various warm environments has allowed it to establish populations in many subtropical zones globally.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Albizia mollis targets cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. The plant contains bioactive flavonoids and tannins that act as inhibitors to reduce the production of inflammatory mediators in the body. Furthermore, certain polyphenolic compounds within the species demonstrate antioxidant activity by neutralizing reactive oxygen species through the modulation of antioxidant enzymes.     What are the herbal preparations of Albizia mollis?

    Albizia mollis is prepared primarily as a decoction of the bark, a concentrated infusion of the flowers, or a topical paste made from crushed leaves. The bark is often boiled in water to extract its tannins and active compounds for internal consumption in traditional practices. Floral infusions are frequently used to create aromatic teas that are believed to support relaxation and sleep. For external applications, the leaves are ground into a fine consistency and mixed with a small amount of liquid to form a poultice for skin irritations. These various forms allow the medicinal properties of the plant to be delivered according to specific therapeutic needs.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Albizia mollis can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, itching, or rashes when people come into direct contact with its foliage or flowers. While there is limited clinical data regarding systemic toxicity, some individuals may experience respiratory irritation if they inhale high concentrations of its pollen. Potential hypersensitivity reactions can vary significantly depending on an individual's specific sensitivities to plant proteins. It is generally advised to use gloves when pruning the plant to prevent localized skin irritation. Monitoring for any unexpected bodily responses is recommended for those with known plant allergies.
