Exochorda racemosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae, eventually reaching its specific identification within the genus Exochorda and the species racemosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Exochorda racemosa has deciduous, opposite, ovate to elliptical leaves that typically measure two to four inches in length and feature finely serrated margins. The foliage is characterized by a smooth, glossy texture and a bright green color that transitions as the seasons change. During the flowering period, the plant produces small, four-petaled white blossoms that emerge in clusters along elongated racemes. These delicate flowers often appear in late spring, sometimes preceding the full development of the leaves. The stems are woody and can grow in a spreading, shrubby habit, reaching heights of several feet.

What traditional systems uses this plant?

This plant, known as the fish poison tree, has been used historically in Southeast Asia to stun fish in stagnant waters by releasing saponins from its crushed fruits into ponds.

Traditional communities in regions like Malaysia and Indonesia rely on these natural compounds to facilitate easier fishing in slow-moving rivers. Beyond its use as a piscicide, various parts of the tree have been integrated into local medicinal practices to treat skin ailments and internal discomfort.
