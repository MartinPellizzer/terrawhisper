What to know about Corchorus olitorius (jute) before using it medicinally

Corchorus olitorius, commonly known as jute, is a nutrient-rich herb that has been used for centuries to boost overall health and well-being due to its high content of vitamins A, C, and E, as well as minerals such as potassium and iron.

This versatile herb is easy to grow and can thrive in a variety of climates and soil conditions, making it a popular choice for gardeners and farmers. Botanically speaking, Corchorus olitorius is a member of the Malvaceae family and is characterized by its distinctive green leaves and tender shoots, which are often harvested for culinary and medicinal use.

With ancient roots in traditional medicine and cuisine, Corchorus olitorius has been referenced in the Ayurvedic and Unani medical systems of India and the Middle East, as well as in African and Asian folk remedies.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Corchorus olitorius.

What are the medicinal properties of Corchorus olitorius?

Corchorus olitorius helps with various medicinal applications, including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties. It is used to treat gastrointestinal issues, diarrhea, and fever. The plant has also been traditionally used to treat skin conditions and wounds.

The active constituents responsible for the medicinal properties of Corchorus olitorius are mucilages, flavonoids, and saponins. Mucilages contribute to its anti-inflammatory and soothing effects, while flavonoids exhibit antimicrobial and antioxidant activities. Saponins have been shown to have antitumor and immunomodulatory properties.

The most commonly used parts of the plant for medicinal purposes are the leaves and stems. The leaves are rich in mucilages and are used to treat digestive issues, while the stems contain flavonoids and are used to treat fever and skin conditions.

Improper use of Corchorus olitorius can lead to gastrointestinal side effects, including nausea and diarrhea. Allergic reactions, such as skin rashes and itching, may also occur in some individuals.

Precautions should be taken when using Corchorus olitorius medicinally, including pregnancy and breastfeeding warnings. Additionally, individuals with gastrointestinal issues or allergies should consult a healthcare professional before using the plant for medicinal purposes.

What are the horticulural aspects of Corchorus olitorius?

Corchorus olitorius grow in a warm and humid climate with a temperature range of 25-35°C. It thrives in well-drained soil with a pH of 5.5-6.5 and a rainfall of 500-1000 mm per annum.

To plant Corchorus olitorius, seeds are sown 1-2 cm deep in nursery beds in a row and 10-20 cm apart. The seedlings are transplanted to the main field when they are 10-15 cm tall. A spacing of 30-60 cm is maintained between plants.

Harvesting of Corchorus olitorius is done when the plants are 30-60 cm tall and the leaves are tender. The leaves are picked regularly to encourage new growth and prevent flowering. The stalks are harvested when mature, usually 3-4 months after sowing.

Common pests affecting Corchorus olitorius include the aphid, jassid, and whitefly, while diseases include leaf spot, powdery mildew, and root rot, caused by pathogens such as Cercospora, Oidium, and Pythium.

Overall, Corchorus olitorius exhibits a mix of characteristics typical of both herbaceous and woody plants, making it a unique member of the Malvaceae family.
