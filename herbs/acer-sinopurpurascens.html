Acer sinopurpurascens

What's the taxonomical classification of Acer sinopurpurascens?

Acer sinopurpurascens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Sapindales and the family Sapindaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Acer, with its specific designation being the species sinopurpurascens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer sinopurpurascens has a small to medium-sized deciduous habit characterized by spreading, rounded crowns and dark purplish-red foliage that transitions to green in summer.

The leaves are palmately lobed with three to five distinct lobes and finely serrated margins. During the spring, the tree produces small, red, upright clusters of flowers that emerge alongside the new leaf growth. The bark typically becomes grayish and develops shallow fissures as the specimen reaches maturity.

In the autumn, the foliage often shifts to vibrant shades of orange, red, or deep purple before falling.

      What are the herbal preparations of Acer sinopurpurascens?

      Acer sinopurpurascens is prepared as a liquid decoction, an infused tea, or a topical tincture derived from its bark and leaves. Practitioners often boil the bark to extract tannins and medicinal compounds for internal consumption. The leaves can be steeped in hot water to create a mild herbal tea used for various traditional purposes. Additionally, the plant material is frequently macerated in alcohol to produce a concentrated tincture for topical application. These different methods allow for the targeted delivery of the plant's specific phytochemical properties.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Acer sinopurpurascens can cause contact dermatitis, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals who touch its leaves or bark. While the plant is not considered highly toxic to humans, its sap may trigger redness or itching upon skin contact. Some people might also experience respiratory irritation if they are exposed to large amounts of pollen during the blooming season. Pet owners should exercise caution because ingestion could potentially lead to mild gastrointestinal upset in dogs or cats. Monitoring for these specific reactions is recommended when handling the plant in a garden setting.
