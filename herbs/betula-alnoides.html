Betula alnoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Betula alnoides?

Betula alnoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Fagales and the family Betulaceae, eventually reaching its specific designation within the genus Betula. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its unique specific epithet, alnoides.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Betula alnoiides has ovate to elliptical leaves with serrated margins, slender branches, and small, pendulous catkins. The foliage typically features a bright green color and fine, pointed teeth along the leaf edges. Its bark is characterized by a smooth, greyish texture that may become slightly fissured on older specimens. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that develop into winged seeds known as samaras. These structures are essential for wind dispersal across its natural woodland habitat.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds betulinic acid, oleanolic acid, ursolic acid, lupeol, and various flavonoids. These pentacyclic triterpenoids are known for providing significant anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties to the organism. Betulinic and oleanolic acids specifically contribute to the plant's potential anticancer and antiviral activities. Lupeol works alongside ursolic acid to support cellular health and promote wound healing processes. Furthermore, the presence of flavonoids enhances the overall therapeutic profile by protecting cells from oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, Betula alnoiides contains five pentacyclic triterpenoid compounds obtained from the ethanol extract of its stems. These specific medicinal compounds are identified as betulinic acid (1), betulin (2), lupeol (3), oleanolic acid (4), and ursolic acid (5). Among these, compound 2 exhibited the most potent inhibitory activity against HIV-1 IN with an IC50 value of 17.7 μM. Furthermore, all compounds demonstrated significant inhibitory effects on LPS-induced nitric oxide production with an IC50 < 68.7 μM. This research provides scientific support for the use of Betula alnoiides in traditional medicine.
