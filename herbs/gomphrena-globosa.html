Gomphrena globosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Gomphrena globosa?

Gomphrena globosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Caryophyllales. This plant is a member of the family Amaranthaceae, specifically falling within the genus Gomphrena, and is identified by its unique species name, globosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gomphrena globosa has erect, branched stems that support globose, papery flower heads composed of small, tubular florets surrounded by colorful, bract-like structures. The leaves are typically opposite, lanceolate to ovate in shape, and possess a smooth or slightly hairy texture. These plants are characterized by their vibrant, spherical inflorescences that can range in color from bright pink to deep purple. The bracts provide a long-lasting ornamental quality because they remain colorful even after the plant has dried. Small, inconspicuous flowers are nestled within these showy bracts to facilitate pollination.

What are the pharmacological activities of Gomphrena globosa?

This plant has antioxidant, antidiabetic, cholagogue, prostate-related, and stoppage-of-water pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. The plant also demonstrates antidiabetic effects by assisting in the regulation of blood glucose levels. As a cholagogue, it stimulates the secretion of bile to aid in digestion. Furthermore, it is utilized to address prostate problems and functions as a treatment for the stoppage-of-water condition.

According to a study published by "Scientific reports", the methanolic extract of Gomphrena globosa treated with combined AMF inoculation exhibited higher secondary metabolite content, including 50.11 mg GAE/g DW of total phenolics, 29.67 mg QE/g DW of flavonoids, 122.55 mg DE/g DW of saponins, 165.71 TAE/g DW of tannins, and 8.24 mg LE/g DW of terpenoids. HPTLC examination of this treatment showed the presence of kaempferol at 0.90% and benzoic acid at 5.83%. The plant also demonstrated high antioxidant activities with a DPPH IC50 of 401.39 µg/mL, an ABTS IC50 of 71.18 µg/mL, and a FRAP of 8774.73 µM Fe (II) equivalent. These bioactive compounds and activities could potentially lead to the development of novel pharmaceuticals and herbal remedies.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic skin reactions, digestive upset, or potential interactions with blood-thinning medications in sensitive individuals. While generally considered safe in moderate amounts, contact with the foliage may trigger contact dermatitis or localized rashes in people with plant sensitivities. Ingesting concentrated extracts might lead to nausea, stomach cramps, or general gastrointestinal discomfort. Some researchers suggest that the bioactive compounds could influence blood clotting processes, making caution necessary for those on anticoagulants. Because clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, individuals should consult a healthcare professional before using it medicinally.
