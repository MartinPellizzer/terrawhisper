Botanical Identification of Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica)

To identify Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Stinging Nettle, Urtica dioica, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Urticales, family Urticaceae, genus Urtica, and species Urtica dioica. This classification places it within the group of flowering plants known for their stinging hairs and medicinal properties.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Stinging Nettle is Urtica dioica, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It is also known by several common names, including stinging nettle, nettle, and urtica. Regional and historical names vary, reflecting its widespread use in traditional medicine and its distinctive stinging hairs.

Morphological Characteristics

Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica) is an upright herb with a fibrous root system that spreads horizontally. Its stems are square and covered in stinging hairs. Leaves are heart-shaped with serrated edges and a dense covering of fine hairs. The inflorescence is a dense cluster of small greenish flowers. Each flower produces a small, round fruit containing a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Stinging Nettle can be distinguished by its heart-shaped leaves with serrated edges, smooth stems with stinging hairs, and small greenish flowers in clusters. It grows in moist, disturbed soils and is often found in hedgerows or along trails. It differs from similar species like Urtica urens by its larger leaves and more robust growth habit.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Stinging Nettle is native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers moist, nutrient-rich soils and is commonly found in woodland edges, hedgerows, and disturbed areas. It grows best at low to mid-altitudes, typically between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Stinging Nettle blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is most identifiable for harvesting during its vegetative stage in spring and early summer.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica) belongs to the genus Urtica, which includes several species of stinging plants. It is closely related to Urtica urens, another stinging nettle species. Within the Urticaceae family, it shares traits like stinging hairs and opposite leaf arrangement with other members.
