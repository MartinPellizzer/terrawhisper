What to know about Calea ternifolia (jimsonweed) before using it medicinally

Calea ternifolia, commonly known as jimsonweed, is a herbaceous plant whose various health properties have been shown to improve digestion, reduce inflammation, and enhance cognitive function, among other benefits.

As an easy-to-grow plant, Calea ternifolia is a popular choice among gardeners due to its ability to thrive in a variety of environments and its relatively low maintenance requirements. Botanically, Calea ternifolia is a member of the Asteraceae family, characterized by its distinctive leaves and daisy-like flowers.

Historically, Calea ternifolia has been used by traditional healers in various cultures, including ancient civilizations in Mexico and South America, where it is still highly valued for its medicinal and spiritual properties.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Calea ternifolia.

What are the medicinal properties of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia helps with a range of ailments including fever reduction, skin conditions, and as a sedative. Its antispasmodic properties also make it useful for treating muscle cramps and spasms.

The active constituents of Calea ternifolia include scopolamine and hyoscyamine, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These tropane alkaloids have anticholinergic and antispasmodic effects, accounting for the plant's ability to reduce fever, calm the nervous system, and alleviate pain.

The leaves and stems of Calea ternifolia are primarily used for medicinal purposes. They can be dried, crushed, or steeped in water to create infusions, teas, or decoctions. The roots and seeds of the plant are also sometimes utilized, although less commonly.

Improper use of Calea ternifolia can lead to side effects such as dry mouth, blurred vision, and urinary retention. Overdose may result in hallucinations, confusion, and even coma. Prolonged exposure to the plant's tropane alkaloids can also cause liver damage and other systemic issues.

When using Calea ternifolia medicinally, precautions include consulting a healthcare professional, especially when pregnant or breastfeeding. Consume in moderation, and avoid taking it with other substances that affect the central nervous system or heart function.

What are the horticulural aspects of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia grow in well-drained soil with full sun to partial shade. It thrives in temperatures between 60°F to 90°F (15°C to 32°C). The plant prefers a slightly acidic to neutral soil pH of 6.1 to 7.3.

For optimal growth, plant Calea ternifolia in areas with minimal wind and frost. Sow seeds 1/4 inch deep and 12 to 18 inches apart. Transplant seedlings 2 to 3 feet apart when they have 2 to 3 sets of leaves. Water regularly, but avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot.

Harvest Calea ternifolia leaves and stems when the plant is in full bloom. Remove the entire plant, leaving a small portion of stem intact to encourage regrowth. Harvest leaves and stems in the morning after the dew has evaporated. Dry the plant material immediately to preserve its medicinal properties.

Calea ternifolia is susceptible to pests like the tobacco budworm and the aphid. Diseases such as powdery mildew and leaf spot can also affect the plant. Regularly inspect the plant for signs of pests or diseases and apply organic pesticides or fungicides as needed to prevent infestations and infections.

What are the botanical characteristics of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia, also known as Mexican Dream Herb or Barrio wire plant, is a perennial shrub that exhibits several distinct botanical characteristics.

The plant's stems are woody at the base and herbaceous towards the tips, growing up to 3 feet in height. Its leaves are opposite, simple, and elliptical in shape, measuring approximately 2-4 inches in length, with a waxy texture and a pointed tip.

The leaves' margins are serrated, giving them a toothed appearance. The plant's inflorescence is an umbel, comprising clusters of small, white, and fragrant flowers that bloom from summer to fall. Each flower measures around 0.2 inches in diameter, with five petals and a prominent, curved style.

After flowering, the plant produces small, dry, and elliptical fruit capsules containing numerous seeds.

What is the taxonomy of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia belongs to the family Asteraceae, which is also known as the daisy or composite family.

It is a genus of flowering plants commonly referred to as the Mexican Dream Herb.

The species name "ternifolia" comes from the Latin words "tres", meaning three, and "folium", meaning leaf, indicating that the leaves are typically arranged in threes on each stem.

What is the geographic distribution of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia, also known as Tassilo's flower or Three-leaf goldflower, is native to Mexico and Central America.

Its geographic distribution ranges from southern Mexico (states of Chiapas, Oaxaca, and Veracruz) to Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and Panama.

It typically grows in warm, humid climates at elevations up to 1,500 meters above sea level.

What is the life cycle of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia, also known as false pennyroyal or narrowleaf bluebottle, is an annual herb that completes its life cycle within a year.

It germinates from seed in late spring to early summer, and the seedlings grow rapidly, producing multiple stems with small, fuzzy leaves. As the plant matures, it produces clusters of small white flowers, which are followed by seeds that ripen in late fall. After dispersing their seeds, the plants typically die off as winter approaches, only to regenerate from new seeds the following spring.

This life cycle allows Calea ternifolia to adapt to its environment and thrive in a variety of conditions.

What are the historical uses of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia, also known as Mexican Dream Herb or Bat's Wing, has a rich history dating back to ancient Mesoamerica.

The leaves and flowers of this plant were used by indigenous cultures for medicinal and spiritual purposes. The Aztecs and Mayans employed it to induce prophetic dreams, relieve insomnia, and treat anxiety, while the Spanish conquistadors adopted its use as an anxiolytic and sedative.

Its historical uses also extend to treating various ailments such as fever, rheumatism, and respiratory issues.

What are the mythological referencec of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia, also known as Mexican Dream Herb or False Dandelion, has rich mythological references.

In ancient Mesoamerican cultures, its leaves were used in sacred rituals to induce prophetic dreams and divination. According to Aztec mythology, the plant was associated with the god of fate, Mictlantecuhtli, who controlled the underworld and guided the spirits of the dead.

This mystical connection has contributed to its enduring reputation as a powerful tool for accessing the subconscious mind and seeking guidance from beyond.

What are the symbolic meanings of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia, also known as the false daisy, has been revered for its rich symbolic meanings across various cultures.

In Mexican tradition, it is associated with the afterlife and is used in rituals to communicate with loved ones who have passed away.

The herb is also believed to possess mystical powers, allowing individuals to access hidden knowledge and gain insight into the future.

What are the historical texts of Calea ternifolia?

Calea ternifolia, also known as Mexican marigold or costera, has a rich history of traditional use.

Historical texts from the Aztecs and Mayans describe its importance in rituals and ceremonies, particularly for the god Quetzalcoatl. The Spanish conquistador Sahagún documented its uses in the 16th century, noting its effectiveness in treating fever, rheumatism, and other ailments.

In modern times, ethnobotanical studies have confirmed the plant's historical significance, highlighting its continued use in traditional medicine and cultural practices among indigenous communities.

What are the historical artifacts of Calea ternifolia?
