What is Blue Mistflower (Calea Ternifolia)?

Blue Mistflower, or Calea ternifolia, is a perennial herb native to Mexico and parts of Central America, known for its distinctive blue flowers and traditional use in herbal medicine for its purported calming and digestive properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Blue mistflower is Calea ternifolia. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Liliopsida, order Asphodelales, family Acanthaceae, genus Calea, and species Calea ternifolia. It is commonly known as Blue mistflower in North America and as Calea in parts of South America. Synonyms for this plant include Calea ternifolia, Calea mexicana, and Calea zonata.

Plant Description and Morphology

Blue mistflower is a perennial herb that grows up to 1.5 meters tall with a branching structure and a fibrous root system.

Its leaves are ovate to elliptic, with a slightly serrated margin and a glossy, dark green upper surface. The flowers are small, tubular, and arranged in dense, spike-like clusters at the ends of the stems, producing a pale blue or purple coloration. The plant has a distinct, aromatic scent reminiscent of mint, and its leaves and stems are covered in fine, hairy textures.

The overall appearance is delicate yet sturdy, with a soft, velvety feel to the foliage.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Blue Mistflower (Calea ternifolia) is typically grown in temperate climates and requires well-drained, loamy soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH.

It thrives in full sun to partial shade, though it performs best with at least 6 hours of direct sunlight daily. Propagation is most effectively done through stem cuttings taken in early spring, which root readily in moist soil. The plant reaches maturity in about 12 to 18 months and is best harvested in the early morning when the leaves are most fragrant.

Harvesting involves cutting the entire plant at the base, ensuring the roots are not damaged to preserve future growth.
