What to know about Vinca minor (periwinkle) before using it medicinally

Vinca minor, commonly known as periwinkle, is a perennial herb that has been valued for its numerous health benefits, including its ability to lower blood pressure, improve circulation, and reduce inflammation.

As a popular ornamental plant, Vinca minor is prized for its dark green, glossy foliage and vibrant blue-purple flowers, which bloom in early spring and continue through summer. From a botanical standpoint, Vinca minor is a member of the Apocynaceae family and is characterized by its heart-shaped leaves and twining stems.

The herb has been mentioned in ancient texts, including the writings of Dioscorides and Pliny the Elder, who noted its medicinal properties and its use in treating various ailments.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Vinca minor.

What are the medicinal properties of Vinca minor?

Vinca minor helps with a variety of health conditions due to its alkaloids, particularly vinblastine and vincristine, which have anti-cancer properties, as well as its antiviral and antibacterial properties.

The active constituents responsible for the medicinal properties of Vinca minor are vinblastine and vincristine, two alkaloids that inhibit microtubule formation, thereby stopping cell division and leading to apoptosis in cancer cells.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are the leaves, stems, and roots, which are harvested at different times to extract the required alkaloids and other bioactive compounds, such as flavonoids and saponins.

Possible side effects of Vinca minor include peripheral neuropathy, nausea, and vomiting, as well as bone marrow suppression and thrombocytopenia due to its inhibitory effects on microtubule formation and cell division.

When using Vinca minor medicinally, precautions include avoiding its use in pregnant or breastfeeding women due to its potential teratogenic and cytotoxic effects, as well as its use in combination with other antineoplastic agents to prevent synergistic toxicity.

What are the horticulural aspects of Vinca minor?

Vinca minor grow in USDA zones 4-8, requiring full sun to partial shade and well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. It thrives in moist soil but can tolerate some drought. Average watering needs are 1-2 inches per week.

To plant Vinca minor successfully, dig a trench 6-8 inches deep and 12-18 inches wide. Add organic matter like compost or well-rotted manure to improve soil fertility. Space plants 12-18 inches apart and water thoroughly. Mulch around plants to retain moisture and suppress weeds.

Vinca minor flowers and leaves can be harvested for medicinal purposes, but proper identification and preparation are crucial. Gather leaves and flowers in the morning after they have reached full color and fragrance. Dry or freeze them immediately to preserve potency and shelf life. Only use clean equipment and storage containers.

Common pests affecting Vinca minor include spider mites, mealybugs, and aphids. Fungal diseases like leaf spot and root rot are also prevalent. Regular monitoring and pruning can prevent infestations, while fungicides may be necessary for severe disease outbreaks. Maintain good air circulation and avoid overwatering to prevent these issues.

What are the botanical characteristics of Vinca minor?

Vinca minor, also known as periwinkle or blueberry vine, is a low-growing, evergreen perennial plant that exhibits several distinct botanical characteristics.

It has heart-shaped leaves that are dark green in color and measure between 2-5 cm in length, with a waxy texture and a distinctive reddish-purple margin. The leaves are arranged oppositely on the stem and have a pointed tip.

The stems of Vinca minor are slender, woody, and branching, growing up to 30 cm tall. In the spring, small, fragrant flowers appear in clusters at the leaf axils, which are typically blue-purple in color with five petals and measure around 1-2 cm in diameter. After flowering, the plant produces dark blue-black berries that contain a single seed each.

Overall, Vinca minor's unique combination of foliage and flower characteristics makes it a distinctive and recognizable species in gardens and landscapes.

What is the taxonomy of Vinca minor?

Vinca minor belongs to the family Apocynaceae, which is a large and diverse group of flowering plants commonly known as dogbanes or oleander families.

Within this family, V. minor is classified under the genus Vinca, which includes about 15 species of evergreen perennials native to Europe, Asia, and Africa.

The specific epithet "minor" refers to the plant's relatively small size compared to other members of the genus.
