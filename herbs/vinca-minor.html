Periwinkle (Vinca minor): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Vinca minor, commonly known as periwinkle, is a low-growing evergreen perennial vine widely used in herbal medicine for its medicinal properties and historical applications in traditional healing practices.

What is Periwinkle (Vinca Minor)?

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) is a low-growing evergreen perennial plant native to Europe, western Asia, and North Africa, commonly used in herbal medicine for its medicinal properties and historical applications.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Periwinkle is Vinca minor. This plant belongs to the family Apocynaceae, order Apocynales, class Liliopsida, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. It is commonly known as periwinkle in Europe and Vinca minor in North America. The plant is also referred to as small periwinkle in some regions and has synonyms such as Vinca major and Myoporum laetum.

Plant Description and Morphology

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) is a low-growing perennial evergreen groundcover plant characterized by its glossy dark green oval leaves and clusters of blue to purple tubular flowers that bloom from late spring to early summer.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, and is commonly found in woodlands, shaded gardens, and along rocky slopes.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, roots, and flowers of Periwinkle (Vinca minor) are used medicinally, with the roots and leaves being the most commonly utilized parts for their active alkaloid content.

Phytochemical Composition

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including alkaloids such as vincamine, vinca alkaloids, and vincorine, along with flavonoids, iridoids, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) contains alkaloids such as vincamine and vincaminine, which have been shown to exhibit cognitive-enhancing, vasodilatory, and neuroprotective properties, making it a subject of interest in herbal medicine for its potential therapeutic applications.

Mechanisms of Action

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) contains alkaloids such as vinca alkaloids that inhibit cell division by interfering with microtubule formation, thereby exerting antitumor and antiproliferative effects in various cancer cell lines.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its potential to support cardiovascular health, reduce inflammation, and promote wound healing due to its alkaloid content, particularly vincamine and vincorine.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or topical ointment, with the most common method involving maceration of the dried leaves and roots in alcohol or vinegar to extract its active compounds.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Periwinkle (Vinca minor) for herbal use typically ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried leaves per day, though specific formulations may vary depending on the preparation method and intended therapeutic purpose.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses for short-term periods, but it may cause gastrointestinal upset, drowsiness, and in some cases, more severe side effects such as irregular heartbeats, especially when combined with certain medications or in higher doses.

Drug Interactions

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) may interact with medications that affect the heart, blood pressure, or blood clotting, including anticoagulants, antiarrhythmics, and certain chemotherapy drugs, due to its potential to influence cardiac function and metabolic pathways.

Toxicity and Precautions

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal upset, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and long-term use or high doses may lead to more serious side effects, including liver damage.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) is typically cultivated in shaded, moist environments with well-drained soil, and harvested during late summer to early autumn when the leaves are mature and the plant is at its peak chemical potency.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Periwinkle (Vinca minor) typically involve harvesting the aerial parts of the plant, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using solvent-based methods such as ethanol or methanol extraction to isolate the active alkaloids, including vincamine and vinblastine.

Storage and Shelf Life

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent moisture damage, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Periwinkle (Vinca minor) has primarily focused on its alkaloid content, particularly vincamine, which has been studied for its potential effects on cognitive function and vascular health.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Periwinkle (Vinca minor) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to treat conditions such as wounds, inflammation, and even certain types of cancer, with historical records dating back to ancient Greek and Roman times.

Regulatory Status
