Cnidium officinale

What's the taxonomical classification of Cnidium officinale?

Cnidium officinale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Apiales. This plant is a representative of the family Apiaceae, which is characterized by its distinctive flowering structures. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Cnidium, and its specific biological identity is defined by the species officinale.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cnidium officinale has an erect, perennial herbaceous structure characterized by hollow, furrowed stems that can reach heights of up to 1.5 meters. The leaves are highly dissected and pinnately compound, exhibiting a lace-like appearance with serrated margins. Small, white flowers are arranged in dense, compound umbels that emerge from the terminal ends of the branches. The plant produces small, ribbed schizocarps that serve as its characteristic seeds. Its root system consists of a thick, fleshy rhizome that is often used in medicinal preparations.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a vast region encompassing much of central and southern Europe, parts of western and central Asia, and northern Africa. Its distribution follows specific ecological niches that favor well-drained soils and open landscapes. What are the pharmacological activities of Cnidium officinale?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidative, antibacterial, and antispasmodic properties. Its chemical constituents work to reduce pain perception and inhibit inflammatory responses in various tissues. The presence of bioactive compounds also allows it to combat oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals. Additionally, the plant demonstrates significant efficacy in inhibiting the growth of certain bacterial strains. Finally, its antispasmodic effects contribute to the relaxation of smooth muscle tissues throughout the body.

According to a study published by "Early human development", Cnidium officinale Makino is a perennial plant that has traditionally been used as a medicinal herb. It possesses analgesic, antiinflammatoty, antipyretic, antibacterial, antispasmodic, vasodilatory, hypertensive, and sedative effects. In the study, Cnidium root was indicated for controlling cough and cold in 54.1% of patients. Some patients took a maximal dose of 12,000 mg/day between 1 day to 12.4 weeks of gestation. Despite these properties, the study notes that there are no studies of reproductive toxicity in humans.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several bioactive medicinal compounds including osthole, various phthalides, Z-ligustilide, ligustilide, and butylidenephthalide. These specific chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's diverse pharmacological effects on the human body. The phthalides group serves as a core structural component within the plant's chemical profile. Among these, ligustilide and its specific isomer Z-ligustilide are noted for their significant biological activities. Additionally, butylidenephthalide and osthole contribute to the overall medicinal potency of the species.

According to a study published by "Biology", osthole is a natural coumarin-like compound isolated from the Fructus cnidii. This plant-derived product and its derivatives have aroused much attention for interesting biological activities including anticancer, anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, and insecticidal effects. The review summarizes recent progress on the biological activities of osthole and its derivatives from 2018 to early 2025. The research focuses on total synthesis, structural modifications, and mechanisms of action. Additionally, structure-activity relationships of osthole derivatives are presented to serve as a reference for medicinal and agricultural applications.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cough, cold, gastric ulcers, gastritis, and various skin disorders. Its medicinal properties are frequently applied to soothe respiratory discomfort and alleviate symptoms of the common cold. In digestive health, it is utilized to manage inflammation and irritation caused by gastritis and gastric ulcers. Additionally, the plant serves as a topical or internal remedy to address several different skin disorders. These diverse applications demonstrate the broad therapeutic potential of Cnidium officinale in traditional medicine.

According to a study published by "Early human development", Cnidium officinale Makino has been traditionally used as a medicinal herb for its analgesic, antiinflamatory, antipyretic, antibacterial, antispasmodic, vasodilatory, hypertensive, and sedative effects. In a study involving 111 singleton pregnant women, Cnidium root was indicated for controlling cough and cold in 54.1% of patients. These patients took a maximal dose of 12,000 mg/day between 1 day to 12.4 weeks of gestation. While 4 babies were born with major malformations in the exposed group compared to 14 in the unexposed group, there was a tenfold increase in the frequency of shorter newborns (<2SD) in the exposed group (OR = 10.1; 95% CI 1.2-87.6; p = 0.019).
