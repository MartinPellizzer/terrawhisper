Dendrobium catenatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Dendrobium catenatum?

Dendrobium catecatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asparagales. This plant is a distinguished representative of the family Orchidaceae, situated within the genus Dendrobium, and is specifically identified by the species name catecatum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dendrobium catecatum has elongated, pendulous pseudobulbs that are distinctly segmented and can reach lengths of up to one meter. These fleshy stems are often slightly compressed and possess a yellowish-green color with longitudinal ridges. The leaves are coriaceous, lanceolate, and emerge from the upper nodes of the pseudobulbs. Its inflorescences consist of multiple small, fragrant flowers that bloom in clusters along the upper portion of the stem. The petals and sepals typically feature intricate purple or magenta markings against a pale base.

Its distribution follows the warm, moist corridors of the Indo-Malayan and Australasian realms. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains dendrobine, medicinal polysaccharides, flavonoids, isoquercitrin, and phloretin. These diverse chemical constituents contribute to the various therapeutic properties associated with the species. The presence of flavonoids and isoquercitrin suggests significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory potential. Furthermore, the medicinal polysaccharides play a crucial role in supporting immune system functions. Phloretin also adds to the complex bioactive profile used in traditional medicinal applications.

According to a study published by "International journal of molecular sciences", Dendrobium catenatum contains the sesquiterpene alkaloid dendrobine, which is recognized as the main active compound and a quality control standard of medicinal orchids in the Chinese Pharmacopoeia. To enhance its medicinal value, researchers engineered the plant by stacking 7 key biosynthesis genes, which resulted in a dendrobine yield that was 2-fold higher than the empty vector control. Further investigation identified Copper Methylamine Oxidase as a candidate that, when overexpressed, increased dendrobine content by 2-fold. Additionally, the overexpression of the regulatory transcription factor gene MYB61 increased dendrobine levels by more than 2-fold in Dendrobium catenatum.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause localized skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or respiratory discomfort if its pollen or sap comes into direct contact with sensitive tissues. While there is limited clinical data regarding systemic toxicity in humans, individuals with known sensitivities to orchidaceae species may experience redness or itching. Contact with the plant's fluids should be avoided to prevent potential dermatological reactions. Some people might also encounter sneezing or minor swelling if they inhale concentrated dust or pollen from the blossoms.
