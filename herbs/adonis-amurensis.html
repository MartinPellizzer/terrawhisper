Adonis amurensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Adonis amurensis?

Adonis amurensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its hierarchical progression, it falls under the order Ranunculales and the family Ranunculaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Adonis, specifically identified as the species amurensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Adonis amurensis has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by erect stems that reach heights of approximately 10 to 30 centimeters and feature finely divided, pinnatisect leaves. The foliage consists of narrow, linear segments that create a delicate, lace-like texture throughout the plant. During the flowering season, the plant produces solitary, bright yellow blossoms that sit atop slender pedicels. Each flower typically displays eight to twelve shiny, waxy petals arranged around a central cluster of numerous stamens. These floral structures emerge from the terminal ends of the stems to attract pollinators in its native habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Adonis amurensis?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, cardiac, anticancer, anti-cardiac insufficiency, and anti-edema pharmacological activities. Its medicinal properties are primarily utilized to manage various cardiovascular conditions by strengthening heart muscle contractions. The plant also exhibits significant potential in the specialized treatment for cancer through its bioactive compounds. Additionally, its diuretic effects make it an effective component in the treatment for edema. These diverse therapeutic actions highlight the significant pharmacological value of Adonis amurensis in modern medicine.

According to a study published by "British journal of pharmacology", the cardiac glycoside convallatoxin is isolated from the traditional Chinese medicinal plant Adonis amurensis Regel et Radde. This compound inhibits IL-1β production by suppressing ZFP91-mediated pro-IL-1β K63-linked polyubiquitination and caspase-8 inflammosome activity. Convallatoxin treatment suppresses ZFP91-mediated activation of the non-canonical cysteine-requiring aspartate protease-8 (caspase-8) inflammosome and MAPK signalling pathways in macrophages. In vivo, convallatoxin ameliorated D-GalN/LPS-induced liver injury, DSS-induced colitis, and alum-induced peritonitis by down-regulating ZFP91. These findings suggest that convallatoxin-mediated inhibition of ZFP91 can prevent inappropriate inflammatory responses to maintain immune homeostasis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds, most notably a group of potent cardiac glycosides. Researchers study these bioactive molecules to better understand their potential applications in treating cardiovascular conditions. The precise concentration and interaction of these substances define the overall therapeutic potency of the plant.

According to a study published by "Natural product research", the roots of Adonis amurensis Regel et Radde contain cardiac glycosides as their core medicinal components. Through ultrasonic-assisted extraction, the extraction yield of these compounds was increased by 51.30% to reach 87.42 mg/g. Optimal extraction conditions involved using 30% ethanol, a liquid-to-solid ratio of 25:1 (mL/g), an ultrasonic time of 58 min, a temperature of 67 °C, and a power of 250 W. This method allowed for the separation of four specific cardiac glycosides. Furthermore, the contents of apocycin and convallatoxin increased 3-4 times when compared with the hot water bath method.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea upon ingestion. Its toxic glycosides also impact the cardiovascular system, potentially leading to dangerous bradycardia or irregular heart rhythms known as arrhythmia. A significant drop in blood pressure, or hypotension, is another serious risk associated with its consumption. These systemic effects can ultimately result in dizziness and other neurological complications.
