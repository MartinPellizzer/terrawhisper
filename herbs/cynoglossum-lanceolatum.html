Cynoglossum lanceolatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Cynoglossum lanceolatum?

Cynoglossum lanceolatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Boraginales and the family Boraginaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Cynoglossum, and its unique species designation, lanceolatum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cynoglossum lanceolatum has lanceolate leaves that are sessile and arranged alternately along a slender, erect stem.

The foliage typically features a smooth margin and a prominent central vein running the length of each blade. Its inflorescence consists of small, tubular flowers that are often clustered in dense cymes. The plant produces small, nut-like fruits that are characteristic of the Boraginaceae family.

These morphological traits allow the species to thrive in its specific ecological niche.

What are the herbal preparations of Cynoglossum lanceolatum?

Cynoglossum lanceolatum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste derived from its leaves and roots. Practitioners often boil the plant parts in water to extract potent alkaloids for internal medicinal use. The resulting liquid can also be used as a wash for skin irritations or minor wounds. In some traditional practices, the dried material is crushed into a fine powder to be mixed with oils or fats. These various methods allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be administered according to the specific ailment being treated.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cynoglossum lanceolatum can cause significant toxicity, including neurological impairment and potential organ damage, due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids in its tissues. These alkaloids are known to be hepatotoxic, meaning they can cause serious injury or even failure of the liver if ingested. Symptoms of poisoning may include nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain following consumption. In some cases, the chemical compounds may also affect the central nervous system, leading to altered states of consciousness. Because of these risks, the plant should be handled with extreme caution and avoided for medicinal use without professional supervision.
