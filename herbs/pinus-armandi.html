Pinus armandi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Pinidae, following the taxonomic order Pinales. As a member of the family Pinaceae, it is situated within the genus Pinus, and is specifically identified by its species name, armandi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pinus armandi has needles arranged in fascicles of three that are typically 10 to 15 centimeters long, a woody trunk with reddish-brown bark, and woody cones measuring 7 to 12 centimeters in length. The foliage is a deep green color and possesses a somewhat stiff texture. Its bark becomes deeply fissured and scaly as the tree reaches maturity. The cones are ovoid in shape and feature thick, thickened scales that protect the seeds. This species is characterized by its upright growth habit and spreading crown.

What are the pharmacological activities of Pinus armandi?

This plant has significant antibacterial and antiseptic pharmacological activities that make it a valuable subject for medicinal research. The essential oils extracted from its needles and bark demonstrate a strong ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria. These antimicrobial properties allow the plant to serve as an effective agent in preventing infection and maintaining hygiene. Researchers often investigate its chemical constituents to develop new therapeutic applications for wound care and skin treatments. Such biological actions highlight the plant's potential in the development of natural disinfectant products.

As discussed in a study, lipophilic extractive metabolites from the needles and defoliated twigs of Pinus armandi exhibit antibacterial activity. The needle extracts of Pinus armandi contain predominantly 15-O-functionalized labdane type acids known as anticopalic acid, along with fatty acids, nonacosan-10-ol, sterols, nonacosan-10-ol and sterol saponifiable esters, and acylglycerols. The defoliated twig extract of Pinus armandi contains major compounds such as abietane and isopimarane type acids, fatty acids, sterols, labdanoids (cis-abienol), cembranoids (isocembrol and 4-epi-isocembrol), saponifiable sterol esters, and acylglycerols. Both the needle and twig extracts of Pinus armandi significantly inhibited the growth of the Gram-negative bacteria Serratia marcescens with a MIC of 0.1 mg ml-1. At the same concentrations, these Pinus armandi extracts slightly stimulated the growth of the Gram-positive bacteria Bacillus subtilis. These lipophilic extractive compounds are considered prospective for the development of antiseptics against Gram-negative bacteria.

As discussed in a study, seven compounds were identified from the pine cone of Pinus armandii. These constituents included sandaracopimaric acid (I), isodextropimaric acid (II), 12-hydroxyabietic acid (III), dehydroabietic acid (IV), 15-hydroxydehydroabietic acid (V), beta-sitosterol (VI) and daucosterol (VII). The study noted that compounds I, II, III, and IV were isolated from this plant for the 1st time. All of these compounds were identified based on spectral analysis following isolation via a chromatographic method.
