Salix variegata

What's the taxonomical classification of Salix variegata?

Salix variegata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is categorized under the order Malpighiales. It is a member of the family Salicaceae, specifically situated within the genus Salix, and is identified by its unique species name, variegata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salix variegata has small, narrow, lanceolate leaves that measure approximately 2 to 5 centimeters in length and feature distinctively variegated margins with white or cream-colored edges. The leaf surfaces are typically smooth and can appear somewhat leathery depending on the specific subspecies. These leaves are arranged alternately along slender, flexible woody stems that often exhibit a reddish or brownish tint. The plant's reproductive structures consist of small, catkin-like flowers that appear on long, pendulous stalks. Overall, the shrub maintains a compact, multi-stemmed growth habit suitable for rocky or alpine environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Salix variegata contains salicin in its bark and leaves, which are the primary parts utilized for their medicinal properties. These components act as precursors to salicylic acid, a compound known for its analgesic and antipyretic effects. Traditionally, decoctions made from the bark have been employed to alleviate pain and reduce fevers. The leaves are also harvested to create infusions used for treating inflammatory conditions. Because of these bioactive substances, various parts of the plant serve as natural alternatives to modern aspirin.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, stomach pain, gastric irritation, increased bleeding risk, tinnitus, and dizziness. These adverse reactions occur because the species contains salicin, a precursor to salicylic acid similar to aspirin.
