Litsea verticillata

What's the taxonomical classification of Litsea verticillata?

Litsea verticillata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Laurales. The plant is a member of the family Lauraceae, which places it within the genus Litsea, and it is specifically identified by its species name, verticillata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Litsea verticillata has simple, evergreen leaves that are arranged in distinct whorls of three to five along its woody stems. The leaf blades are typically elliptical or oblong in shape with entire margins and acute tips. These leaves possess a leathery texture and a smooth, glossy surface that helps minimize water loss. The plant develops small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually give way to fleshy drupes. These fruits are often rounded and change color as they reach maturity.

What are the pharmacological activities of Litsea verticillata?

This plant has potent anti-HIV and antiviral pharmacological activities derived from its bioactive secondary metabolites. Research indicates that various extracts from Litsea verticillata exhibit significant inhibitory effects against several viral pathogens. Specifically, its chemical constituents demonstrate the ability to interfere with viral replication processes, making it a subject of interest for anti-HIV studies. These medicinal properties are often linked to the presence of specialized terpenoids and phenolic compounds found within its leaves and essential oils. Such findings suggest that the species holds considerable potential for the development of new therapeutic agents in virology.

According to a study published by "Planta medica", six sesquiterpenes and five butenolides were isolated from the leaves and twigs of Litsea verticillata. Among the isolates, three were new sesquiterpenes identified as isolitseanes A (1), B (2), and C (3), while three known sesquiterpenes included oxyphyllenodiol B (4), 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-2,5-dimethyl-8-(1-methylethyl)-1,2-naphthalenediol (5), and chromolaevanedione (6). The butenolides consisted of the novel litseabutenolide (9) and four known compounds, 3-epi-litsenolide D2 (7), cis-listenolide D1 (8), 4-hydroxy-2-methylbut-2-enolide (10), and hydroxydihydrobovolide (11). Isolates 2, 4, 5, 7, 9, 10, and 11 were found to inhibit HIV-1 replication in a HOG.R5 reporter cell line. These compounds demonstrated IC50 values of 38.1, 54.6, 91.0, 9.9, 40.3, 129.8, and 122.7 microM, respectively.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Epstein-Barr Virus, HIV, HIV-1, swellings caused by injury, and swellings caused by snake bites. Its medicinal properties are frequently utilized in traditional practices to combat various viral infections. Researchers have specifically investigated its potential efficacy against the Epstein-Barr Virus as well as both HIV and HIV-1. Additionally, the plant serves as a topical remedy for reducing localized inflammation. This includes managing swellings caused by injury and addressing swellings caused by snake bites.

According to a study published by "The American journal of Chinese medicine", Litsea verticillata Hance is a Chinese medicine used to treat swelling caused by injury or by snake bites. This plant was the first identified by the NIH-funded ICBG project to exhibit anti-HIV activities. Researchers discovered a class of 8 novel litseane compounds and 26 additional compounds of different structural types from this species. Two new litseanes, litseaverticillols L and M, were found to inhibit HIV-1 replication with an IC50 value of 49.6 μM. Additionally, lignan 8 and lignan 9 were shown to be active against the Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) with EC50 values of 22.0 μM and 16.2 μM, respectively.
