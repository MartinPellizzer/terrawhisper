Geum aleppicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Geum aleppicum?

Geum aleppicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant is placed in the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Geum and the species aleppicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Geum aleppium has a perennial rootstock that produces basal leaves with long petioles and deeply lobed, serrated margins. The flowering stems rise from this base and are typically hairy, supporting small, five-petaled flowers that range in color from yellow to pale pink. Each flower features five stamens and five pistils surrounded by a nectariferous disc. The resulting fruit consists of numerous small, achene-type seeds that often possess hooked styles for animal dispersal. These morphological features help distinguish the species within its natural Mediterranean and Middle Eastern habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Geum aleppicum?

This plant has potent antifungal, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities attributed to its rich profile of secondary metabolites. The antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. Its antimicrobial and antifungal actions allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungal strains effectively. Furthermore, the plant exhibits measurable cytotoxicity, which suggests its potential role in interfering with the proliferation of certain cancer cell lines. These diverse biological functions make Geum aleppium a significant subject for future pharmaceutical development.

As discussed in a study, the hydroethanolic (50:50, v/v) extract of Geum aleppicum aerial parts contains 24 identified compounds, including 2.28 mg/g of ellagic acid. The extract features a high polyphenol content of 131.45 mg GAE/g and demonstrates strong antioxidant activity with 3.82 ± 0.07 mmol Fe2+/g in FRAP assays and 106.61 ± 0.89 mg GAE/g in DPPH assays. It shows notable antifungal activity against Candida spp., specifically Candida glabrata and Candida tropicalis, with a minimum inhibitory concentration of 0.125 mg/mL. While the extract inhibited the cytopathic effect induced by Human Herpesvirus 1 in VERO cells, it did not significantly reduce viral replication. Furthermore, the extract exerted moderate and selective cytotoxicity against A549 lung cancer cells with a CC50 of 75.51 µg/mL and an SI of 9.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including ellagic acid, flavonoids, phenolic acids, polyphenols, and tannins. These bioactive substances work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. The presence of polyphenols plays a crucial role in neutralizing free radicals and protecting cells from oxidative stress. Furthermore, the specific combination of tannins and phenolic acids contributes to the plant's astringent and antimicrobial properties. Each of these chemical constituents offers unique therapeutic potential for various health applications.

As discussed in a study, a hydroethanolic (50:50, v/v) extract from the aerial parts of Geum aleppicum contains 24 identified compounds, which are predominantly phenolic acids, flavonoids, tannins, and triterpenoids. The major compound identified via RP-HPLC-DAD was ellagic acid at 2.28 mg/g dry extract. The extract possesses a high polyphenol content of 131.45 mg GAE/g and strong antioxidant activity, measured at 3.82 ± 0.07 mmol Fe2+/g in the FRAP assay and 106.61 ± 0.89 mg GAE/g in the DPPH assay. Regarding antimicrobial activity, the extract showed a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) as low as 0.125 mg/mL against Candida spp. such as C. glabrata and C. tropicalis. Furthermore, the extract exerted moderate and selective cytotoxicity against A549 lung cancer cells with a CC50 of 75.51 µg/mL and an SI of 9.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive upset in sensitive individuals. Some people might experience gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or stomach cramps if the plant is ingested. Allergic symptoms could also manifest as respiratory irritation or rashes in those with specific sensitivities to its chemical compounds. Because its full toxicity profile is not extensively documented, caution is necessary when handling the species.
