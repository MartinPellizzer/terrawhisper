Parthenocissus suberosa

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Parthenocissus suberosa has woody, climbing vines characterized by trifoliate leaves, tendrils for attachment, and small, inconspicuous greenish flowers. The leaves typically consist of three leaflets that can vary in shape from ovate to lanceolate depending on the specific cultivar or environment. During the autumn months, the foliage undergoes a dramatic transformation into vibrant shades of red, orange, or purple. The stems are capable of becoming quite thick and bark-like as the plant matures into a larger woody climber. Small, dark berries often develop following pollination, providing a food source for local wildlife.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southeastern United States, specifically ranging from eastern Texas and Louisiana through the Gulf Coast states to Florida and up the Atlantic coast to North Carolina. It primarily thrives in the warm, humid climates of the coastal plain. Within these regions, the species is often found growing in sandy or loamy soils. It frequently populates disturbed areas, forest edges, and thickets where it can climb toward sunlight. Its distribution is heavily tied to the subtropical environments characteristic of the southern American landscape.
