Tabernaemontana bovina

What's the taxonomical classification of Tabernaemontana bovina?

Tabernaemontana bovina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Gentianaales. This plant is a member of the family Apocynaceae, falling under the genus Tabernaemontana, and is specifically identified by its species name, bovina.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Tabernaemontana bovina targets various neuroreceptors and enzymes, specifically acting as an agonist for opioid receptors and an inhibitor of acetylcholinesterase. The bioactive indole alkaloids present in the plant interact with mu-opioid receptors to modulate pain signaling pathways in the central nervous system. Additionally, these compounds can influence serotonin receptors, which contributes to the plant's observed antidepressant and anxiolytic activities. Some constituents also demonstrate the ability to modulate voltage-gated ion channels, affecting neuronal excitability. This diverse range of protein interactions underpins the complex pharmacological profile used in traditional medicinal applications.

What are the herbal preparations of Tabernaemontana bovina?

Tabernaemontana bovina is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, a poultice, or a topical ointment depending on the specific medicinal ailment being treated. The leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a liquid extract used for internal consumption or washing wounds. For external applications, crushed fresh leaves are often applied directly to the skin as a warm poultice to reduce swelling. Some traditional practices involve mixing plant extracts with oils or fats to formulate soothing ointments for skin irritations. These various methods allow for the extraction of bioactive compounds in forms that are most effective for localized or systemic relief.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Tabernaemontana bovina can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of bioactive alkaloids. Contact with the plant's milky latex may trigger allergic contact dermatitis or localized swelling in sensitive individuals. If consumed, the alkaloids may lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. While some traditional uses exist, the specific toxicological profile remains insufficiently documented for safe human consumption. Caution is advised when handling any part of this species to avoid accidental exposure.
