Viscum album

What's the taxonomical classification of Viscum album?

Viscum album belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Santalales and is a member of the family Santalaceae. Finally, its taxonomic hierarchy is completed by its designation within the genus Viscum and the specific species album.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viscum album has evergreen, leathery, oblong leaves that grow in opposite pairs along a woody, branched stem. The plant lacks true roots, instead using a specialized haustorium to penetrate the host tree for nutrients. Its small, inconspicuous flowers are typically yellowish-green and are borne in clusters at the ends of branches. During the fruiting stage, the plant produces translucent, white or yellowish berries that are highly attractive to birds. These berries are held in small clusters that remain visible throughout the winter months.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad range spanning much of Europe, western Asia, and parts of northern Africa. Its presence is heavily influenced by the availability of host trees, which dictates its specific localized clusters. In many temperate regions, it can be found from the Mediterranean basin up through much of the British Isles and into Scandinavia. Certain subspecies have also established themselves in more varied climates, showing a remarkable ability to adapt to different woodland environments. Its distribution is often patchy because it relies on specific avian species for seed dispersal between isolated tree canopies.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Viscum album contains bioactive compounds primarily located within its entire green aerial part, which consists of the leaves and stems. This plant is a hemiparasite, meaning it grows on the branches of host trees and absorbs nutrients through specialized structures. The harvested plant material is often processed into extracts or tinctures to harness its specific lectins and viscotoxins. These constituents are frequently studied for their potential roles in immunomodulation and supporting the body during certain types of cancer treatments. Practitioners typically utilize the whole plant body rather than specific organs like roots or flowers to ensure a consistent concentration of active agents.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viscum album?

This plant has potent anti-tumor and anti-cancer pharmacological activities driven by its ability to trigger apoptosis induction in malignant cells. It functions as a powerful immunomodulatory agent that strengthens the body's natural defense mechanisms. One primary way it achieves this is through NK cytotoxicity enhancement, which increases the ability of natural killer cells to destroy targets. These multifaceted biological actions make Viscum album a significant subject of study in modern oncology.

As discussed in a study, Viscum album L. contains a large number of miRNAs associated with human diseases such as cancer, cardiovascular diseases, and neurological disorders. One specific miRNA, val-miR218, shows high potential anti-cancer effects against osteosarcoma by inhibiting cell growth and arresting cells in the G0/G1 phase. Research identified 61 genes as the direct targets of val-miR218, which are related to essential functions like DNA replication, cell cycle, and cell morphology. Mistletoe extracellular vesicles provide adequate protection for val-miR218 and facilitate its uptake by human cells. This study demonstrated that val-miR218 has significant potential for proliferation inhibition in various tumor cell lines in vitro and for osteosarcoma in vivo.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds viscoxin, viscum album agglutinin, mistletoe lectin I, oleanolic acid, and viscumin. Viscumin and mistletoe lectin I serve as potent proteins that can influence cellular processes through lectin activity. The plant also produces viscoxin, which acts as a toxic component often studied for its ability to disrupt cell membranes. Additionally, oleanolic acid is present and is known for its diverse pharmacological properties, including anti-inflammatory effects. These various constituents work together to define the complex chemical profile of the species.

As discussed in a study, the standardized extract Iscador Qu Spezial from Viscum album is standardized for its lectin and viscotoxin content. In a phase I/II study, 16 HIV-positive patients and 8 healthy subjects received doses of 0.01 mg, 0.1 mg, 1.0 mg, 2.0 mg, 5.0 mg, and 0.1 mg/kg. These administrations occurred twice per week subcutaneously over a period of 6 to 8 months, with each dose lasting for 2-6 weeks. While the extract caused increased urea levels and a minor decrease in albumin, no HIV-positive patients progressed in disease stage.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the adjunctive treatment of various forms of cancer, including breast cancer, leukemia, lymphoma, and melanoma. It is often administered in specialized clinical settings to help mitigate the side effects of conventional therapies like chemotherapy and radiation. Patients frequently utilize these preparations to support the immune system and improve their overall quality of life during oncological care. While it is not a standalone cure, its application focuses on strengthening the body's natural defenses against malignant growths. Research continues into how its specific components interact with different types of tumor cells.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a wide range of adverse reactions including gastrointestinal issues like nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. Some individuals may also experience systemic responses such as allergic reactions or neurological symptoms including dizziness and headache. Cardiovascular complications are a significant concern because ingestion may lead to hypotension and bradycardia. These various side effects highlight the potential toxicity associated with improper use of the species.
