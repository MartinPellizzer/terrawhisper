Viscum album

What's the taxonomical classification of Viscum album?

Viscum album belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Santalales and is a member of the family Santalaceae. Finally, its taxonomic hierarchy is completed by its designation within the genus Viscum and the specific species album.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viscum album has evergreen, leathery, oblong leaves that grow in opposite pairs along a woody, branched stem.

The plant lacks true roots, instead using a specialized haustorium to penetrate the host tree for nutrients. Its small, inconspicuous flowers are typically yellowish-green and are borne in clusters at the ends of branches. During the fruiting stage, the plant produces translucent, white or yellowish berries that are highly attractive to birds.

These berries are held in small clusters that remain visible throughout the winter months.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Viscum album contains bioactive compounds primarily located within its entire green aerial part, which consists of the leaves and stems. This plant is a hemiparasite, meaning it grows on the branches of host trees and absorbs nutrients through specialized structures. The harvested plant material is often processed into extracts or tinctures to harness its specific lectins and viscotoxins. These constituents are frequently studied for their potential roles in immunomodulation and supporting the body during certain types of cancer treatments. Practitioners typically utilize the whole plant body rather than specific organs like roots or flowers to ensure a consistent concentration of active agents.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a complex mixture of bioactive molecules including lectins, viscotoxins, flavonoids, phenolic acids, saponins, terpenoids, and alkaloids.

The lectins and viscotoxins are primarily responsible for the plant's immunomodulatory and cytotoxic effects during medical applications. Flavonoids and phenolic acids provide significant antioxidant properties that help mitigate oxidative stress in biological systems. Furthermore, the presence of saponins and terpenoids contributes to various anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activities.

These diverse alkaloids further enhance the pharmacological potential of the species in specialized therapeutic research.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Viscum album targets various proteins including tumor necrosis factor (TNF), tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL), and various interleukins to induce cancer cell apoptosis. The plant's lectins specifically bind to glycoprotein receptors on the surface of malignant cells, triggering programmed cell death pathways. These bioactive compounds also modulate the activity of enzymes involved in the inflammatory response, such as cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX). Furthermore, its constituents interact with cell surface receptors to stimulate the production of natural killer cells and T-lymphocytes. These multifaceted protein interactions contribute to the plant's observed immunomodulatory and antitumor activities.

What are the therapeutic actions of Viscum album?

Viscum album has potent antitumor properties that facilitate the destruction of malignant cells by inducing apoptosis and inhibiting tumor growth.

As a natural immunomodulator, the plant enhances the activity of natural killer cells and T-lymphocytes to strengthen the body's defense mechanisms. Its classification as an adaptogen allows it to help the organism maintain homeostasis and better manage physiological stress. Furthermore, it serves as a restorative tonic that supports overall vitality and systemic equilibrium.

This combination of effects makes it a significant subject in integrative oncology and supportive medicine.

What are the herbal preparations of Viscum album?

Viscum album is prepared as liquid extracts, standardized tinctures, homeopathic dilutions, and occasionally as topical ointments or creams. These preparations are most commonly used in the form of highly diluted homeopathic remedies to treat specific physiological conditions. In clinical settings, liquid extracts are often derived from the fresh or dried berries and leaves of the mistletoe plant. Some pharmaceutical manufacturers produce standardized oral drops that ensure a consistent concentration of active lectins and viscotoxins. These diverse delivery methods allow for both systemic administration and localized application depending on the intended therapeutic goal.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Viscum album can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness if ingested in significant amounts. These symptoms often occur because the plant contains toxic lectins and viscotoxins that irritate the digestive system. Severe poisoning may lead to more serious complications such as low blood pressure, slow heart rate, or even seizures. Because of these risks, the berries and leaves should never be consumed raw or in uncontrolled doses. It is crucial to consult a medical professional before using any preparations derived from this mistletoe species.
