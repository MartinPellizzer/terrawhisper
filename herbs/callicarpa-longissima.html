Callicarpa longissima

What's the taxonomical classification of Callicarpa longissima?

Callicarpa longissima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Lamiales and falls under the family Lamiaceae. Finally, its taxonomic position is completed by its designation within the genus Callicarpa and the specific species epithet longissima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Callicarpa longissima has opposite, lanceolate leaves that measure up to 25 centimeters in length and are characterized by finely serrated margins and prominent pinnate venation.

The stems are woody and often exhibit a slightly pubescent texture depending on the maturity of the plant. Small, tubular flowers emerge from axillary clusters, typically presenting in a dense cyme arrangement. These blossoms eventually give way to spherical, metallic purple drupes that hang in tight bunches from the branches.

The overall architecture of the plant is shrubby, reaching a height that supports its heavy clusters of fruit.

Callicarpa longissima targets enzymes and signaling proteins involved in inflammatory pathways, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX) to reduce prostaglandin synthesis. Additionally, certain compounds within the extract show potential for interacting with antioxidant enzymes to mitigate oxidative stress. This multi-target approach contributes to the plant's overall pharmacological efficacy in treating inflammatory conditions.

      What are the herbal preparations of Callicarpa longissima?

      Callicarpa longissima is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or topical poultices using its leaves and bark to treat various ailments. The leaves are often steeped in hot water to create a medicinal tea that addresses internal issues. Bark extracts can be concentrated into liquid tinctures for more potent medicinal applications. For external skin conditions, the plant material is crushed into a paste to serve as a soothing poultice. These different preparations allow the plant's active compounds to be delivered effectively through ingestion or direct application.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Callicarpa longissima can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested. While the plant is not widely documented as highly toxic, the essential oils in the leaves may trigger redness or itching upon direct contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals might experience a localized rash or swelling if they handle the foliage without protection. If consumed accidentally, the plant could potentially lead to nausea or stomach discomfort. Always exercise caution and avoid direct skin contact or ingestion to prevent these adverse reactions.
