Pandanus tectorius

What's the taxonomical classification of Pandanus tectorius?

Pandanus tectorius belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Pandanales and the family Pandanaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Pandanus, and its unique species designation, tectorius.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pandanus tectorius has a woody, tree-like habit characterized by a stout trunk, aerial prop roots for stability, and long, sword-shaped leaves arranged in tight spiral clusters at the branch tips. These leaves are leathery and feature sharp, serrated edges lined with small spines along both the margins and the midrib. The plant produces large, fragrant, cream-colored flowers that eventually develop into fleshy, aggregate fruits. Each fruit consists of many individual drupes that are often colorful and contain fibrous tissue. This distinctive structure allows the plant to thrive in coastal environments and sandy soils.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, extending from the coastal areas of India and Sri Lanka through the Indonesian archipelago to the Philippines. It primarily thrives in maritime environments such as sandy beaches, mangrove swamps, and rocky coastal cliffs. Because it is highly salt-tolerant, it can establish itself in various littoral zones across the Indo-Pacific islands. Its distribution is largely limited to low-lying tropical latitudes where humidity and warmth remain consistent. Many populations are now also found in other tropical regions due to human-assisted dispersal and ornamental planting.

What are the pharmacological activities of Pandanus tectorius?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-hyperlipidemic, antihyperglycemic, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and hypolipidemic properties. Its bioactive compounds work effectively to lower blood glucose levels and manage metabolic disorders. The plant also exhibits potent hypolipidemic and anti-hyperlipidemic actions by regulating lipid profiles in the body. Additionally, its antioxidant capabilities help mitigate oxidative stress while its antimicrobial effects provide protection against various pathogens. These diverse therapeutic attributes make Pandanus tectorius a valuable subject for medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, the polyphenol-rich Pandanus tectorius fruit extract (PTF) exerts anti-hyperlipidemic effects and modulates the gut microbiota. In a study involving male C57BL/6J mice, hyperlipidemia was induced via a high-fat diet (HFD) for 4 weeks, followed by 6 weeks of oral administration of PTF, antibiotics, PTF plus antibiotics, or a vehicle. PTF was effective in ameliorating HFD-induced hyperlipidemia and significantly changed the structure of the gut microbiota. Specifically, PTF selectively enhanced the relative abundance of Lactobacillus while decreasing the relative abundance of Bacteroides and Alistipes. Lactobacillus was negatively associated with serum lipids and glucose, whereas Bacteroides and Alistipes were positively associated with serum lipids and glucose.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including caffeoylquinic acid, various caffeoylquinic acids, polyphenols, and amino acids. These bioactive substances contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and therapeutic properties. The presence of polyphenols helps protect cells from oxidative stress and inflammation. Furthermore, the specific profile of caffeoylquinic acids provides unique health benefits through their chemical complexity. The inclusion of essential amino acids also supports the biological functionality of the plant's extract.

As discussed in a study, the fruit extract of Pandanus tectorius contains caffeoylquinic acids (CQAs). When treating diabetic db/db mice with 200 mg/kg of this CQAs-rich Pandanus tectorius fruit extract (PTF), researchers observed significant physiological changes. The PTF treatment decreased body weight and fasting glucose levels while declining the glucose area under the curve in both oral glucose tolerance and insulin tolerance tests. This extract also stimulated the phosphorylation of AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) and Akt substrate of 160 kDa (AS160). Furthermore, PTF enhanced the expression and translocation of glucose transporter type 4 (GLUT4) in skeletal muscles.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive distress if handled improperly or ingested. Direct contact with the leaves or sap may lead to contact dermatitis, resulting in redness, itching, or a localized rash on the skin. Some individuals may experience hypersensitivity symptoms such as swelling or hives upon exposure to its components. If parts of the plant are accidentally consumed, it can trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. It is important to exercise caution and consult medical professionals if any adverse reactions occur after contact.
