Rhododendron moulmainense

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rhododendron moulmainense has elliptic to oblong leaves that are typically leathery, dark green on the upper surface, and possess a distinctively revolute margin. The foliage is characterized by a somewhat coriaceous texture and a glabrous finish that aids in moisture retention. Its inflorescence consists of clusters of bell-shaped flowers that range in color from pale pink to deep rose. The stems are woody and develop a rugged bark texture as the plant matures into a shrub or small tree. These structural features collectively define the species' distinctive silhouette within its natural montane habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the high-altitude regions of the Eastern Himalayas, specifically found within parts of Nepal, Bhutan, and northeastern India. It thrives in the moist, temperate forests located at elevations typically ranging from 2,000 to 3,500 meters. The species is most frequently documented in areas characterized by acidic soil and heavy monsoon rainfall. Its distribution is closely tied to the specific microclimates provided by the rugged mountain terrain of the Himalayan foothills. These narrow ecological niches restrict its occurrence to specific longitudinal bands within the mountain range.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Rhododendron moulmainense can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and even death if ingested due to the presence of toxic grayanotoxins. These toxins interfere with the sodium channels in cell membranes, leading to a condition often referred to as mad honey disease. Common symptoms of poisoning include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and a dangerous drop in blood pressure. Victims may also experience irregular heartbeats, dizziness, and blurred vision. In extreme cases, the physiological impact can result in cardiac arrhythmia or respiratory failure.
