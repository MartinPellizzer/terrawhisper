Leucaena leucocephala

What's the taxonomical classification of Leucaena leucocephala?

Leucaena leucocephalha belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It is further organized under the order Fabales and falls within the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Leucaena and its specific species name, leucocephala.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Leucaena leucocephalha has bipinnate leaves composed of numerous small, oblong leaflets arranged along a central axis, a woody stem structure, and white, spherical flower heads. The tree typically grows to a height of several meters with a spreading canopy and a bark that becomes fissured with age. Its small, bright green leaflets are arranged oppositely on the rachis to maximize sunlight absorption. The inflorescences consist of dense clusters of tiny flowers that eventually develop into flat, brown, papery seed pods. These pods contain several small, hard seeds that are characteristic of the Fabaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Mexico, Central America, and parts of South America, but it has become widely naturalized throughout tropical and subtropical regions of the world. It is frequently found in the Caribbean islands and across much of Africa and Southeast Asia due to human introduction. Because it thrives in disturbed soils and warm climates, it often colonizes tropical dry forests and abandoned agricultural lands. Its ability to grow in diverse environments has allowed it to spread across many different continents. This wide distribution is driven by both its natural resilience and its historical use in reforestation projects.

What are the pharmacological activities of Leucaena leucocephala?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, anticancer, antioxidant, and hypoglycemic pharmacological activities that make it a subject of significant medicinal interest. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, which can prevent cellular damage and reduce oxidative stress in the body. The antibacterial effects allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms, while its anti-inflammatory actions help mitigate swelling and pain. Furthermore, research suggests the plant possesses anticancer potential by inhibiting tumor cell growth and can act as a hypoglycemic agent to help lower blood sugar levels. These diverse biological functions highlight the therapeutic versatility of Leucaena leucocephalia in pharmacological studies.

As discussed in a study, extracts from Leucaena leucocephala demonstrated significant anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting the production of NO by 97% at a concentration of 25µg/mL. This plant also exhibited strong activity against acetylcholinesterase with an IC50 value of 118µg/mL. Furthermore, high levels of phenolics and flavonoids were found in Leucaena leucocephala. These findings regarding the leaf extracts validate their use in South African traditional medicine against inflammation.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains mimosine, flavonoids, phenolics, coumarins, and terpenes which provide its diverse medicinal properties. The presence of mimosine is particularly notable for its potential biological activities in various therapeutic applications. Flavonoids and phenolics contribute significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects to the plant's chemical profile. Additionally, the inclusion of coumarins and terpenes offers further specialized pharmacological benefits. These combined secondary metabolites work together to define the plant's medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, Leucaena leucocephalia contains the compound mimosine. Animals with experience in the plant took up 1.14 g/kg0.75 of mimosine, which was significantly higher than the 0.93 g/kg0.75 and 0.92 g/kg0.75 observed in two other groups. During a 45 day period of feeding 100% Leucaena, goats with experience consumed 50.01 g/kg0.75 of dry matter compared to 44.40 g/kg0.75 and 45.63 g/kg0.75 in other groups. The Serum-Thyroxin level (T4) for the group with experience was 4.65 micrograms/100 ml, while the control group was 5.12 micrograms/100 ml and the other trial groups were 3.9 micrograms/100 ml. These measurements were all significantly different with P less than 0.05.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe health complications in animals and humans due to the presence of the toxic non-protein amino acid mimosine. Consumption of this toxin leads to significant thyroid dysfunction and goiter by interfering with iodine metabolism. It also triggers various physical ailments including hair loss, growth retardation, and serious reproductive issues. Additionally, individuals may experience systemic problems such as hypoglycemia alongside nausea and digestive distress.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Hypoglycemia
  • Toxicity (mimosine)
  • Goiter/thyroid dysfunction
  • Hair loss
  • Growth retardation
  • Reproductive issues
  • Nausea
  • Digestive distress
