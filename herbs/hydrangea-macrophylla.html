What's the taxonomical classification of Hydrangea macrophylla?

Hydrangea macrophylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Cornales and the family Hydrangeaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Hydrangea and the species macrophyllia.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and southern Japan, where it thrives in cool, moist environments. It is commonly found growing in the shaded understory of deciduous and coniferous forests. These plants often inhabit slopes and ravines where soil moisture remains consistently high throughout the growing season. While its natural range is restricted to specific Japanese islands, it has been widely distributed globally as an ornamental species in temperate gardens. What are the therapeutic actions of Hydrangea macrophylla?

Hydrangea macrophylla has diuretic, antipyretic, and astringent properties that are primarily utilized in traditional medicinal practices to manage fluid retention and fever. The plant's diuretic action helps the body eliminate excess water and sodium through increased urine production. Its antipyretic qualities assist in reducing elevated body temperatures during febrile illnesses. Additionally, the astringent components can help contract tissues and reduce secretions in various bodily systems. These combined actions make the plant a versatile subject in ethnobotanical studies.

As discussed in a study, Hydrangea macrophylla contains the bioactive dihydroisoquinolone phyllodulcin, which functions as a non-caloric, high-intensity sweetener up to 800 times sweeter than sucrose. Researchers identified 14 out of 182 different Hydrangea accessions to study the contrasting foliar concentrations of phyllodulcin and the closely related hydrangenol.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe toxicity through the ingestion of cyanogenic glycosides, leading to nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. As the poisoning progresses, a person may experience dizziness and confusion. In more critical cases, the toxins can trigger seizures and acute respiratory distress. If the exposure is sufficiently high and medical intervention is not immediate, the resulting systemic failure can lead to death.
