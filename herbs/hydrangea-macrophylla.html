Hydrangea macrophylla

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hydrangea macrophyllahas large, opposite, ovate leaves that measure up to 15 centimeters in length and feature serrated margins and prominent veins. These broad foliage structures are typically deep green and provide a lush backdrop for the plant's blooming season. The species is characterized by large, corymb-like inflorescences composed of numerous small fertile flowers surrounded by showy, sterile florets. Depending on soil acidity, these flower heads can transition between shades of blue, pink, or purple. Woody stems form a multi-stemmed shrubby habit that reaches several feet in height.

While its natural range is restricted to specific Japanese islands, it has been widely distributed globally as an ornamental species in temperate gardens. What are the therapeutic actions of Hydrangea macrophylla?

Hydrangea macrophylla has diuretic, antipyretic, and astringent properties that are primarily utilized in traditional medicinal practices to manage fluid retention and fever. Its antipyretic qualities assist in reducing elevated body temperatures during febrile illnesses. Additionally, the astringent components can help contract tissues and reduce secretions in various bodily systems. These combined actions make the plant a versatile subject in ethnobotanical studies.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Hydrangea macrophyllas can cause nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and dizziness if the plant is ingested due to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides. These compounds release cyanide when they come into contact with digestive enzymes in the human body. While accidental ingestion of a few leaves might only cause mild stomach upset, larger quantities can lead to much more severe toxic reactions. Symptoms of cyanide poisoning may include headaches, confusion, or even more critical respiratory issues in extreme cases. It is important to keep this plant away from children and pets to prevent accidental poisoning.
