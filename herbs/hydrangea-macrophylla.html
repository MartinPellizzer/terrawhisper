Hydrangea macrophylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Hydrangea macrophylla?

Hydrangea macrophylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Cornales and the family Hydrangeaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Hydrangea and the species macrophyllia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hydrangea macrophyllahas large, opposite, ovate leaves that measure up to 15 centimeters in length and feature serrated margins and prominent veins. The species is characterized by large, corymb-like inflorescences composed of numerous small fertile flowers surrounded by showy, sterile florets. What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and southern Japan, where it thrives in cool, moist environments. It is commonly found growing in the shaded understory of deciduous and coniferous forests. These plants often inhabit slopes and ravines where soil moisture remains consistently high throughout the growing season. While its natural range is restricted to specific Japanese islands, it has been widely distributed globally as an ornamental species in temperate gardens. What are the pharmacological activities of Hydrangea macrophylla?

This plant has antimicrobial, antiallergic, and anti-malarial pharmacological activities derived from its complex chemical constituents. The presence of various bioactive compounds allows the plant to effectively inhibit the growth of diverse pathogenic microorganisms. Research suggests that its extracts can modulate immune responses to mitigate allergic reactions in biological systems. Additionally, certain phytochemicals within the species demonstrate potential in combating malaria parasites. These multifaceted therapeutic properties make the species a subject of significant pharmacological interest.

As discussed in a study, the leaf-extract of Hydrangea macrophylla inhibits the parasitic growth of Plasmodium falciparum with an IC50 of 0.18 microg/ml. The IC50 to NIH 3T3-3 cells was 7.2 microg/ml, resulting in a selective toxicity of 40. In an in vivo test, Hydrangea macrophylla leaf-extract was administered orally at 3.6 mg/0.2 ml 3 times a day for 3 days. When administered 2 times a day for 3 days to 5 mice, malaria parasites did not appear in 4 of the mice but appeared in 1 mouse. Furthermore, the extract was administered 3 times a day for 3 days to mice with a parasitemia of 2.7%, where parasites disappeared on day 3 but reappeared after day 5 or 6. These results confirm that the Hydrangea macrophylla leaf extract contains an anti-malarial substance that can be administered orally.

Understanding the chemical structure of these substances helps scientists harness their medicinal benefits more effectively. Further investigation into these constituents could lead to new developments in plant-based medicine.

As discussed in a study, Hydrangea macrophylla contains the bioactive dihydroisoquinolone phyllodulcin, which functions as a non-caloric, high-intensity sweetener up to 800 times sweeter than sucrose. This compound possesses medicinal properties and assists the plant in pathogen resistance and abiotic stress tolerance. Researchers identified 14 out of 182 different Hydrangea accessions to study the contrasting foliar concentrations of phyllodulcin and the closely related hydrangenol. Accessions producing more phyllodulcin showed higher metabolite levels of phenylalanine, umbelliferone, p-coumaric acid, naringenin, resveratrol, and thunbergiol C. This chemical profile is supported by the upregulation of specific cyclase and ketoreductase genes in accessions with high concentrations of the compound.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe toxicity through the ingestion of cyanogenic glycosides, leading to nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. As the poisoning progresses, a person may experience dizziness and confusion. In more critical cases, the toxins can trigger seizures and acute respiratory distress. If the exposure is sufficiently high and medical intervention is not immediate, the resulting systemic failure can lead to death.
