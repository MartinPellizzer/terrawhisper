Bigleaf hydrangea, scientifically known as Hydrangea macrophylla, is a plant that has been revered for its numerous health benefits and versatility in various aspects of our lives.

Additionally, bigleaf hydrangea has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for centuries, with records of its use dating back to the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644) as a treatment for various ailments, solidifying its position as a treasured botanical with a rich history and cultural significance.

What are the harvesting tips of Hydrangea macrophylla?

Hydrangea macrophylla, a popular and beloved garden shrub, requires careful attention during harvest to ensure the quality and longevity of its beautiful flowers.

When harvesting hydrangea blooms, it's essential to cut stems in the morning when the dew is still present, as this helps prevent damage to the delicate petals. Use sharp pruning shears or floral scissors to snip off the stem at a 45-degree angle, just above a node (where a leaf meets the stem).

Avoid crushing or tearing the stems, which can cause them to become brittle and prone to shedding flowers. Gently grasp each stem by wrapping your fingers around the base of the flower head, taking care not to bruise or damage the delicate petals. Harvest only the outermost blooms first, as this will encourage the plant to produce more flowers for future harvests.

Finally, place harvested stems immediately into a clean container filled with cold water to keep them fresh until they're ready to be arranged in bouquets or used in floral arrangements.

What are the pest and diseases tips of Hydrangea macrophylla?

Hydrangea macrophylla, a popular ornamental shrub, is susceptible to several pests and diseases that can impact its growth and aesthetic appeal.

One common pest of hydrangeas is aphids, which feed on sap and secrete honeydew, attracting ants and promoting fungal growth. Another significant pest is the Japanese beetle, whose larvae feed on roots, causing stunted growth and reduced flowering.

Fungal diseases such as powdery mildew and leaf spot can also affect hydrangeas, caused by overwatering or poor air circulation, leading to white powdery patches or yellow lesions on leaves. Root rot, often caused by Phytophthora spp., is another major disease that can infect hydrangeas, resulting in stunted growth, yellowing foliage, and eventual plant death. Additionally, hydrangeas are also vulnerable to bacterial blight, which can cause premature defoliation and flower drop.

Proper cultural practices, such as regular watering, pruning, and fertilization, along with integrated pest management techniques, can help mitigate the impact of these pests and diseases on hydrangea macrophylla.
