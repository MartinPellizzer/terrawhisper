Quinoa

Chenopodium quinoa

Ashwagandha is a prominent medicinal herb in Ayurvedic medicine, classified as a rasayana (rejuvenator). It is primarily utilized as an adaptogen to help the body manage physical and chemical stress.

Family
Solanaceae (Nightshade)

Native Region
India, Middle East, Africa

Part Used
Root, Leaf, Berry

Botanical Identification

Quinoa is scientifically known as Chenopodium quinoa and belongs to the Amaranthaceae family. It is also called "golden grain" in some regions and is an annual plant native to the Andes. It has small green flowers and thrives in high-altitude environments. Its leaves are broad and serrated, and it has a fibrous root system. Historically, it was referred to by various names in traditional herbal texts.

Active Compounds

Quinoa contains key bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, saponins, and phenolic acids. These compounds work synergistically to provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. They contribute to the herb's therapeutic properties in herbal medicine.

  • Flavonoid
  • Saponin
  • Phenolic acid

Preparation Methods

Culinary use: Used as a nutrient-rich food source for energy and overall health.

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and inflammation due to its anti-inflammatory properties.

Infusion: Used for respiratory ailments such as coughs and colds due to its mild soothing properties.

Safety Profile

Quinoa is generally safe for most people when consumed as food. However, it may cause gastrointestinal upset in some individuals. There are no well-documented contraindications for its use as a dietary supplement, though caution is advised for those with chronic kidney disease.
