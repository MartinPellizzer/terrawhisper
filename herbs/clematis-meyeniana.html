Clematis meyeniana

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis meyeniana?

Clematis meyeniana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Ranunculales, the plant is a member of the family Ranunculaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is defined by its membership in the genus Clematis, specifically reaching the species designation of meyenianana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis meyeniana has woody, climbing stems that produce opposite, trifoliate leaves characterized by small, ovate leaflets with entire margins.

The foliage typically displays a bright green color and serves as the primary vegetative structure for the vine. Its inflorescence consists of small, bell-shaped flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These blossoms are usually pale or cream-colored and feature a delicate, tubular structure.

The plant eventually produces small, dry achenes that are attached to feathery styles for wind dispersal.

        What are the herbal preparations of Clematis meyeniana?

        Clematis meyeniana is prepared primarily as a decoction, a concentrated liquid infusion made by boiling the dried roots or stems in water to extract active compounds. This method is favored because the tough, woody parts of the plant require prolonged heat to release their medicinal properties effectively. Some traditional practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of a tincture, soaking the botanical material in high-proof alcohol to create a shelf-stable extract. Additionally, topical applications such as poultices may be fashioned from the crushed plant material for external use. These various preparations allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Clematis meyeniana can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential respiratory issues if ingested or handled improperly. The plant contains protoanemonin, a toxic chemical that triggers blistering and redness upon contact with the skin. If swallowed, the compounds may lead to intense vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. Inhalation of dust or pollen from the plant can also irritate the mucous membranes of the throat and lungs. These toxic effects necessitate cautious handling and complete avoidance of ingestion to prevent systemic poisoning.
