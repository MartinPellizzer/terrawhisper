Acanthus ilicifolius

What's the taxonomical classification of Acanthus ilicifolius?

Acanthus ilicifolius belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Acanthaceae and is specifically identified by the genus Acanthus, falling under the unique species name ilicifolius.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acanthus ilicifolius has large, leathery, dark green leaves that are deeply lobed with sharp, holly-like spines along the margins. These leaves are arranged oppositely on woody stems and often possess a glossy texture. The plant features a robust, shrubby growth habit with a branching structure that supports its heavy foliage. Small, white to pale blue flowers emerge from specialized bracts, typically appearing during the warmer seasons. The overall architecture of the plant is defined by its stiff, architectural leaves and sturdy, upright stems.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across parts of Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, and southern China. It is commonly found growing in moist, shaded environments such as lowland rainforests and coastal thickets. The species prefers areas with high humidity and consistent rainfall characteristic of equatorial climates. Within these habitats, it often occurs in understory layers where it can thrive in filtered sunlight. Its distribution is largely limited to these warm, wet territories where the soil remains consistently damp.

What are the pharmacological activities of Acanthus ilicifolius?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, antioxidant, gastroprotective, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within cellular environments. The plant also exhibits anti-inflammatory effects that can mitigate swelling and pain in various biological systems. Furthermore, research suggests it possesses anti-tumor capabilities that may inhibit the growth of certain cancer cells. Finally, its gastroprotective and hepatoprotective actions offer significant defense for the stomach lining and liver functions against chemical or environmental damage.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic extract of Acanthus ilicifolius leaves (MEAL) possesses significant anti-inflammatoty properties and exhibits anti-ulcer activities. When administered to rats in dosages of 200 mg and 400 mg/kg BW after intraperitoneal administration, MEAL showed significant protective activity against gastric lesions induced by aspirin, indomethacin, stress, ethanol, and pylorus ligation. In pylorus-ligated rats, the extract significantly decreased gastric volume, acidity, and peptic activity while restoring levels of reduced glutathione (GSH) and antioxidant enzymes including superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), and glutathione peroxidase (GPX). MEAL also provided significant inhibition of both lipid peroxidation and myeloperoxidase (MPO) activity in pylorus-ligated animals and ethanol-induced ulceration models. Furthermore, MEAL pre-treatment inhibited cold-restraint stress-induced (CRS) ulceration and improved the status of antioxidant enzymes while reducing lipid peroxide levels.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds acteoside, apiigenin-7-O-beta-D-glucuronide, luteolin 7-O-beta-D-glucuronide, methylapiigenin-7-O-beta-D-glucuronate, and penexanthone A. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the diverse therapeutic profile found within the plant's biological structure. The presence of these flavonoids and phenylethanoid glycosides suggests significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory potential. Researchers study these molecules to understand how they interact with human cellular pathways. Such detailed chemical compositions are essential for developing targeted herbal treatments.

As discussed in a study, Acanthus ilicifolius contains the bioactive phenylethanoid glycoside known as acteoside. This specific compound acts as a potent natural GS inhibitor. Through its interaction with GS, acteoside helps restore the GLU-GSH-GPX4 antioxidant axis to suppress lipid peroxidation and ferroptosis. The study demonstrates that this medicinal compound can significantly attenuate DOX-induced cardiac dysfunction, fibrosis, and myocardial atrophy. Consequently, acteoside is established as a promising natural lead compound for the management of DIC.
