Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the plant is Sideritis syriaca, a species within the Lamiaceae family. It was first described by the botanist Johann Friedrich Klotzsch in 1840. This name reflects its native region, Syria, and is used in botanical literature to distinguish it from closely related species.

Morphological Characteristics

Sideritis syriaca is a perennial herb with a taproot system. It has upright, branched stems that are hairy. Its leaves are opposite, ovate, and hairy. The plant produces whorled inflorescences with pink to purple flowers. The fruit is a capsule containing small, oblong seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Sideritis syriaca can be distinguished by its slender, hairy stems and lance-shaped leaves with prominent veins. The flowers are pale blue to violet, arranged in whorls, and the plant has a distinctive aromatic scent. It is often found in rocky or arid soils, which helps differentiate it from similar species with different habitat preferences.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Sideritis syriaca is native to Syria and surrounding regions. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It is commonly found between 500 and 1,500 meters in altitude.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Sideritis syriaca blooms from June to August, producing pink to purple flowers. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested during its flowering period to ensure accurate species recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Sideritis syriaca is closely related to species within the Sideritis genus, particularly those found in the Mediterranean region.
