What's the taxonomical classification of Spirodela polyrhiza?

Spirodela polyrhiza belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Alismatales and the family Araceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Spirodela, and its unique species designation, polyrhiza.

What are the pharmacological activities of Spirodela polyrhiza?

This plant has potent anticoagulating, antithrombotic, and fibrinolytic pharmacological activities that influence blood coagulation processes. Its chemical constituents work to prevent the formation of unwanted blood clots within the vascular system. By inhibiting specific clotting factors, the plant exhibits a strong antithrombotic effect to reduce the risk of thrombosis. Furthermore, its fibrinolytic properties help in the breakdown of existing fibrin strands to dissolve clots. These combined actions make the species a significant subject for research in cardiovascular pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, a fibrinolytic protease was purified from Spirodela polyrhiza that has a molecular mass of 145 kDa in gel filtration and 70 kDa in SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, implying it is a dimer. This enzyme has an optimum pH of 4.5-5.0 and remains stable below 42 degrees C and after lyophilization. The protease hydrolyzes fibrin and fibrinogen by cleaving A-alpha and B-beta chains while preferentially cleaving the peptide bond of Arg or Lys at the P1 position. It exhibits anticoagulating activity by delaying APTT, TT, and PT two times at concentrations of 36, 39, and 128 nM, respectively. These activities were significantly inhibited by leupeptin and aprotinin or drastically reduced after heat treatment.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apiagenin glycosides, cyanidin-3-O-glucoside, kaempferol, luteolin, and quercetin. These diverse phytochemicals are primarily categorized as flavonoids, which are well-known for their antioxidant properties. Each specific molecule contributes to the plant's ability to combat oxidative stress within biological systems. For example, the presence of quercetin and kaempferol often supports anti-inflammatory responses in cellular environments. Together, these compounds form a complex chemical profile that provides various therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, the Spirodela polyrhiza 6068 mutant named 68-red shows significant medicinal potential through increased medicinal compound levels. In this mutant, anthocyanins and proanthocyanidins increased by 563.47% and 231.19% compared to the wild type. A total of 734 metabolites were identified, including significantly accumulated cyanidin-3-O-glucoside, cyanidin-3-O-galactoside, pelargoniidin-3-O-glucoside, and pelargoniidin-3-O-(6″-O-malonyl)glucoside. The mutant also shows significant accumulation of proanthocyanidins (B1, B2, B3, B4, C1, C2), 11 luteolin and its glycosides, 14 quercetin and its glycosides, 14 kaempferol and its glycosides, and 2 apigenin glycosides. Additionally, cynaroside and orientin content were significantly increased in the 68-red mutant.
