Artemisia phaeolepis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artemisia phaeolepis has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, woody stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 80 centimeters. The foliage consists of small, narrow, linear leaves that are often densely covered in fine, grayish-white hairs. These leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and possess a distinctively grayish-green coloration. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flower heads that are arranged in many-flowered panicles. This morphological structure allows the species to thrive in arid, sandy, or rocky environments.

      What are the herbal preparations of Artemisia phaeolepis?

      Artemisia phaeolepis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the specific ailment being treated. When used internally for digestive issues, the dried leaves are often boiled in water to extract their active bitter compounds. For skin irritations or wound healing, a concentrated liquid extract can be applied directly to the affected area.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Artemisia phaeolepis can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential toxicity if ingested in large quantities. Contact with the plant's leaves or stems may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Inhalation of airborne particles or dust from the plant can lead to respiratory discomfort or sneezing. If consumed, the chemical compounds within the plant may cause gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or vomiting. These adverse effects are typically linked to the specific bioactive compounds found within the species.
