Stellaria saxatilis

What's the taxonomical classification of Stellaria saxatilis?

Stellaria saxatilis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Caryophyllales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Caryophyllaceae, specifically categorized within the genus Stellaria, with its unique designation as the species saxatilis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Stellaria saxatilis has small, lanceolate leaves that are oppositely arranged along a slender, creeping stem.

The plant typically grows in low, spreading mats with fine hairs covering the surface of the foliage. Its tiny white flowers feature five distinct petals that are deeply notched at the tips. The reproductive structures consist of short filaments and a central ovary that develops into a small capsule.

These morphological features allow the species to thrive in rocky, shallow soil environments.

        What are the herbal preparations of Stellaria saxatilis?

        Stellaria saxatilis is prepared as a liquid infusion, a decoction, or a topical poultice depending on the desired therapeutic application. The dried aerial parts are most commonly steeped in hot water to create a mild tea that serves as a gentle internal tonic. For more concentrated medicinal effects, a decoction involves simmering the plant material to extract tougher chemical constituents. When addressing skin irritations or minor wounds, the crushed fresh leaves are applied directly to the area as a cooling poultice. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the plant's properties to specific health needs.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Stellaria saxatilis can cause severe contact dermatitis, acute gastrointestinal distress, and mild respiratory irritation if the pollen is inhaled. Exposure to the plant's milky sap often results in painful skin blistering and long-lasting redness upon contact. Ingesting even small amounts of the foliage may lead to intense nausea, abdominal cramping, and vomiting. Some individuals may also experience dizziness or lightheadedness following prolonged exposure to its concentrated oils. These reactions vary in intensity depending on the person's sensitivity and the method of contact.
