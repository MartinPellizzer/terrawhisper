Carapa procera

What's the taxonomical classification of Carapa procera?

Carapa procera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant is organized under the order Sapindales and falls within the family Meliaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Carapa, and its species designation, procera.

Its bark is generally thick and textured, providing protection for the vascular system of the plant. The reproductive organs produce small fruits that are often adapted for dispersal by local fauna. This plant is native to the tropical regions of Central and South America, specifically ranging from southern Mexico through much of the Amazon Basin. Its distribution is closely tied to riverine environments and seasonally flooded forests where moisture levels remain high. What are the pharmacological activities of Carapa procera?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, anti-malarial, anti-plasmodial, and anti-HCV pharmacological activities. Its diverse chemical profile allows it to combat a wide range of bacterial and microbial pathogens effectively. Research indicates that the plant's extracts demonstrate significant potency against malaria parasites, specifically targeting the Plasmodium species. Furthermore, its therapeutic potential extends to inhibiting the hepatitis C virus, offering possibilities for antiviral treatment. These combined properties make it a significant subject of interest in pharmacological studies.

As discussed in a study, Carapa procera was assessed for its in vitro antibacterial activities against eight bacterial strains belonging to four species, including Escherichia coli, Enterobacter aerogenes, Klebsiella pnumeoniae and Providencia stuartii. The extracts displayed selective antibacterial activities with minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) values ranges of 64 to 1024 µg/mL. This research utilized the microbroth dilution method to determine both the minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) and minimal bactericidal concentration (MBC) of the samples. The study focused on the activities of these plants against Gram-negative bacteria expressing multidrug resistant (MDR) phenotypes.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including carapolide A, cyclic terpens such as Meliacine, a mexicanolide-methylangolensate mixture, polyphenols, and procyaniidin A2. These bioactive constituents contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of the species. The presence of specific cyclic terpens like Meliacine often relates to unique biological activities. Additionally, the polyphenol content and procyaniidin A2 provide significant antioxidant properties. The complex mexicanolide-methylangolensate mixture further distinguishes its chemical makeup.

As discussed in a study, Carapa procera contains the compounds carapolide A and a mexicanolide-methylangolensate mixture. When tested at 10-100 micrograms.ml-1, these compounds exhibited considerable microfilariciidal activity after 24 hours of incubation. Preliminary toxicity studies in mice showed that both carapolide A and the mexicanolide-methylangolensate mixture were relatively non-toxic. Further pharmacologic studies are recommended to determine the potential application of these active compounds for the treatment of onchocherciasis.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating onchocerciasis, Onchocerca volvulus, malaria, hepatitis C virus, and various bacterial infections. Its medicinal properties are frequently utilized in traditional practices to combat parasitic worms that cause skin and eye complications. Researchers have also investigated its potential efficacy in neutralizing the hepatitis C virus within the body. Additionally, the plant demonstrates antiparasitic activities that target the malaria parasite. Its broad antimicrobial spectrum also assists in managing different types of bacterial infections.

As discussed in a study, carapolide A and the mexicanolide-methylangolensate mixture from Carapa procera were identified as potential filaricides. When tested at 10-100 micrograms.ml-1, these compounds exhibited considerable microfilariciidal activity after 24 hours of incubation. Preliminary toxicity studies conducted in mice showed that carapolide A and the mexicanolide-methylangolensate mixture were relatively non-toxic. Further pharmacologic studies are recommended to determine the potential application of these active compounds on the treatment of onchocerciasis.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach cramps, hypoglycemia, hypotension, and dizziness. Gastrointestinal distress often manifests as intense stomach cramps accompanied by nausea or vomiting. Significant changes in metabolic and circulatory functions may lead to dangerous levels of hypoglycemia and hypotension. These drops in blood sugar and blood pressure frequently result in feelings of dizziness. Users should remain cautious of these diverse physiological effects when interacting with the plant.
