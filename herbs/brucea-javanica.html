Brucea javanica

What's the taxonomical classification of Brucea javanica?

Brucea javanica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed under the order Sapindales. The plant is further categorized into the family Simaroubaceae, within which it falls under the genus Brucea. Finally, its specific taxonomic designation is completed by the species name javanica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Brucea javanica has an erect, woody shrub habit characterized by simple, alternate, lanceolate leaves that measure approximately 10 to 25 centimeters in length and possess entire margins. The leaf blades are typically dark green with an acute apex and a slightly cordate base. Small, inconspicuous yellowish-green flowers are produced in axillary clusters or panicles. These flowers transition into distinctive, elongated, capsule-like fruits that turn from green to a reddish-brown color upon maturity. Inside these capsules, small, smooth, dark seeds are contained which are dispersed once the fruit dehisces.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across countries like Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, and parts of southern China. It is primarily found in lowland areas where the climate remains warm and humid throughout the year. Within these territories, the species often inhabits secondary forests, scrublands, and disturbed sites near riverbanks. Its range is closely tied to the monsoon patterns that define the seasonal moisture levels of these equatorial environments. This specific distribution patterns suggest a preference for well-drained soils found in these lowland tropical ecosystems.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Brucea javanica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its seeds, though the leaves and roots are also utilized in traditional applications. The seeds are the most potent part of the plant and are frequently used to treat intestinal parasites and various skin diseases. Extracts from the leaves are sometimes applied topically to address inflammatory conditions or fungal infections. What are the pharmacological activities of Brucea javanica?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-parasitic, anti-proliferative, anti-malarial, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities derived from its bioactive compounds. Its complex limonoids contribute significantly to its ability to inhibit the growth of various malignant cell lines. These chemical constituents also demonstrate potent anti-proliferative effects that disrupt cellular division processes. Furthermore, the plant serves as a natural source for treating malaria and other parasitic infections through its specific medicinal properties. Research also highlights its capacity to modulate immune responses to provide anti-inflammatory benefits.

As discussed in a study, the anti-cancer herb medicine Yadanzi, derived from Brucea javaica, was selected for a mechanistic study. A total of 7,119 ingredient-target interactions were identified for thirteen active ingredients of the plant. Approximately 29.5% of these interactions were estimated to possess a better binding affinity than the corresponding marketing drug-target interactions. Bioinformatics analyses suggest that the plant works against non-small cell lung cancers through the simultaneous manipulation of multiple proteins in the MAPK signaling pathway and the phosphorylation process of anti-apoptosis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds Bruceine A, Bruceine B, Bruceine C, Bruceanin, and various Quassinoids. These specialized alkaloids and bitter principles are primary components found within the plant's chemical structure. Researchers often study these substances to understand their potential pharmacological activities and biological effects. The presence of Bruceine A, B, and C highlights the complex chemical profile unique to this species. Furthermore, the combination of Bruceanin and Quassinoids contributes to the overall medicinal properties identified in scientific literature.

As discussed in a study, fifteen C-20 quassinoids were isolated from the fruits of Brucea javanica. Among these, Bruceine A, bruceantinol, bruceine C, brusatol, and bruceine B demonstrated strong antitrypanoosmal activities with IC50 values ranging from 2.9-17.8nM. These values compared well to the standard drugs diminazene aceturate with an IC50 of 8.8nM and suramin with an IC50 of 43.2nM. In contrast, dehydrobruceine A, dehydrobruceine B, and dehydrobrusatol were 2100, 900, and 1200 times less active than bruceine A, bruceine B, and brusatol, respectively.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating malaria, cancer, dysentery, babesiosis, and various parasitic infections. Its medicinal properties are primarily derived from bitter compounds that exhibit significant antiprotozoal activity. Researchers have investigated its ability to combat blood-borne pathogens like those causing babesiosis and malaria. Additionally, studies suggest that its chemical constituents may play a role in inhibiting certain types of cancer cells. The plant also serves as a traditional remedy for gastrointestinal issues such as dysentery.

As discussed in a study, Brucea javanica is a medicinal plant from which the natural quassinoid compound bruceine A is extracted from its dried fruits. Natural quassinoid compounds from this plant have been used as traditional medicines for the treatment of various ailments including malaria. In vitro, bruceine A killed parasites within 24 hr at a concentration of 25 nM. Oral administration of bruceine A at a dosage of 6.4 mg/kg/day for 5 days resulted in no clinical findings in a dog with normal hematological and biochemical blood values. Two dogs treated with bruceine A at a dosage of 6.4 mg/kg/day for 6 days from day 5 post-infection maintained healthy conditions throughout a 4 week experimental period.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain due to its highly irritating chemical constituents. Ingesting the plant may lead to more serious conditions such as gastritis or significant dizziness. There is also a profound risk of hepatotoxicity, which involves damage to the liver cells. These toxic effects underscore the danger of consuming the plant without professional medical supervision.
