Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the plant is Cleome gynandra, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It is also known by several synonyms, including Cleome hassleriana and Cleome tenuifolia. Common names such as spider plant and mallow weed are widely used, while regional and historical names reflect its traditional uses in various cultures.

Morphological Characteristics

Cleome gynandra is an annual herb with a branching stem that grows upright. Its root system is taproot-based, supporting the plant's structure. The leaves are alternate, simple, and ovate with serrated edges. The inflorescence is a cluster of green, leaf-like bracts, with small yellow flowers emerging from the axils. The fruit is a capsule containing small, flat, winged seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cleome gynandra can be distinguished by its opposite, ovate leaves with serrated edges and yellow flowers in clusters. It has a distinctive cylindrical stem with spines, and its fruit is a capsule that splits into three parts. These features help differentiate it from similar species like Cleome hassleriana.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cleome gynandra is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa. It thrives in warm climates with high humidity. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It is commonly found at low to moderate altitudes, typically below 1,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cleome gynandra blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn, around August to October. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during its flowering stage, which is ideal for collecting leaves and flowers for herbal use.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cleome gynandra belongs to the Cleome genus within the Malvaceae family. It is most closely related to species like Cleome hassleri and Cleome viscosa. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, including hairy stems and capsule fruits. The genus relations highlight its placement within the Malvaceae family, characterized by sympetalous flowers and fibrous roots.
