Mentha haplocalyx

What's the taxonomical classification of Mentha haplocalyx?

Mentha haplocalyx belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Lamiales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Lamiaceae, which places it within the genus Mentha, ultimately identifying it as the specific species Mentha haplocalyx.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mentha haplocalyx has an erect, herbaceous structure characterized by square stems, opposite leaves, and small, tubular flowers arranged in dense terminal verticillasters. The leaves are typically ovate or lanceolate with serrated margins and a distinct aromatic quality. Its calyx is notably small and possesses a single-lobed appearance, which distinguishes it from many other mint species. The inflorescence consists of compact clusters that emerge from the axils or the tip of the stem. Tiny, bilabiate flowers emerge from these clusters to facilitate pollination.

What are the pharmacological activities of Mentha haplocalyx?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, α-glucosidase inhibitory, and various cytokine-modulating activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. It exerts its anti-inflammatory effects through the reduction of IL-6 and TNF-α production. Additionally, the plant aids in the suppression of CCL2 and TNF-α production to manage immune responses. It further demonstrates biological utility through the suppression of nitric oxide production. Finally, its α-glucosidase inhibitory activity suggests a potential role in regulating glucose metabolism.

As discussed in a study, Mentha haplocalyx has shown beneficial effects on atopic dermatitis. In vitro tests using concentrations of 0.125-1 mg/ml showed that Herb herba Menthae significantly reduced the production of IL-6 and tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α in HMC-1 cells. This plant also suppressed the lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced nitric oxide (NO) production in RAW 264.7 cells. Furthermore, Mentha haplocalyx significantly suppressed CCL2 and TNF-α production in LPS-induced RAW 264.7 cells.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds emodin, flavonoids, menthol, menthone, and oleanolic acid. The presence of menthol and menthone provides characteristic cooling sensations often used in topical applications. Flavonoids within the plant contribute significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Emodin serves as a powerful anthraquinone known for its potential laxative and antimicrobial effects. Additionally, oleanolic acid offers therapeutic benefits through its role in supporting liver health and reducing cellular inflammation.

As discussed in a study, nine compounds were isolated and purified from the herb of Mentha haplocalyx using column chromatography. The chemical constituents identified include emodin (I), chrysophanol (II), physcione (III), benzoic acid (IV), trans-cinnamic acid (V), beta-sitosterol (VI), aloe-emodin (VII), ursolic acid (VIII) and daucosterol (IX). Specifically, compounds I, II, III, V, and VII were first isolated from Mentha haplocalyx. Spectral analysis was utilized to elucidate the structures of these nine identified substances.
