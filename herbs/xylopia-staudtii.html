Xylopia staudtii

What's the taxonomical classification of Xylopia staudtii?

Xylopia staudtii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Magnoliales and is a member of the family Annonaceae. Specifically, this plant is identified by the genus Xylopia, with its unique designation being the species staudtii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Xylopia staudtii has an evergreen woody structure characterized by simple, alternate leaves with entire margins and an obtuse apex. The foliage typically displays a coriaceous texture and a deep green color on the upper surface. Its reproductive morphology involves small, inconspicuous flowers that are borne on the branches. The fruit develops into a woody follicle that contains numerous seeds. These morphological features are typical of many species within the Annonaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of Central and West Africa, specifically ranging from Sierra Leone and Liberia through to Cameroon and Gabon. It primarily thrives within the humid lowland forests that characterize the Guineo-Congolian biogeographical region. Its distribution is closely linked to areas with high annual rainfall and consistent warm temperatures found in these equatorial zones. Within these habitats, the species is often found in the dense understory or mid-canopy layers of primary forest ecosystems. What are the pharmacological activities of Xylopia staudtii?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antimicrobial, antishigellosis, and bactericidal pharmacological activities. These biological properties allow the species to combat various infectious pathogens and reduce swelling in tissues. Its antimicrobial nature makes it effective against a broad spectrum of microorganisms that cause disease. Specifically, the plant exhibits strong bactericidal effects that can directly kill harmful bacteria. Furthermore, its antishigellosis potential offers a therapeutic way to manage infections caused by Shigella bacteria.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Xylopia staudtii possesses antibacterial and antishigellosist properties, specifically showing a bactericidal effect against Shigella and a bacteriostatic effect against Escherichia coli. In mice treated with the extract at doses of 100, 200, and 400 mg/kg, the rate of deaths was brought to zero (0) in animals treated at 400 mg/kg. At this 400 mg/kg dosage, the bacteria load in faeces was reduced by 100%. The extract also elicited anti-inflammatory properties by reducing MPO activity and Lcn2 intestinal levels while preventing damages in the intestinal tissue and shortening of the colon. Furthermore, the serum levels of ASAT, ALAT, bilirubin, urea, and creatinine in treated animals remained similar to those of normal animals.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating bacillary dysentery, dysenteric diarrhoea, dysentery, Shigella infection, and shigellosis. The medicinal properties of Xylopia staudtii are specifically applied to combat these severe intestinal ailments. Practitioners utilize the plant to manage the symptoms associated with various forms of dysentery. It is particularly effective in addressing infections caused by the Shigella bacteria. By targeting these gastrointestinal conditions, the plant serves as a traditional remedy for dysenteric outbreaks.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", the fruits of Xylopia staudtii are used in western Cameroon for their abdominal cramp relieving properties. The bark of this plant is used in the treatment of dysentery in Mont Cameroun localities. In studies involving mice treated with 100, 200 and 400 mg/kg of the extract, the rate of deaths was brought to zero (0) in animals treated at 400 mg/kg. Furthermore, the bacteria load in faeces was reduced by 100% in animals treated at 400 mg/kg. These activities support the usage of Xylopia staudtii as an antimicrobial against bacillary dysentery.
