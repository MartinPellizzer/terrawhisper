Celtis sinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Celtis sinensis?

Celtis sinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized under the order Rosales and falls within the family Cannabaceae. Finally, it is categorized under the genus Celtis, with its specific identification being the species sinensis.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Celtis sinensis contains medicinal properties found primarily in its bark, leaves, and fruit. The bark is frequently utilized in traditional practices to treat digestive ailments and skin inflammations. Decocting the leaves can provide relief for respiratory issues and fever reduction. Additionally, the fruit is often consumed to support overall health and alleviate certain internal discomforts. These various components work together to offer a wide range of therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, triterpenoids, alkaloids, phenolic acids, and steroids.

These specific chemical constituents contribute to the various pharmacological activities observed in traditional medicine. For instance, the presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The triterpenoids and steroids within the plant tissues are often associated with antimicrobial and anti-tumor effects.

Additionally, the alkaloids play a crucial role in the plant's complex physiological interactions and medicinal potential.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Celtis sinensis targets various enzymes and signaling proteins including cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various proinflammatory cytokines to mediate its pharmacological activities. The bioactive compounds within the plant, such as flavonoids and triterpenoids, interact with these specific proteins to suppress inflammatory cascades. These molecular interactions primarily inhibit the production of prostaglandins and nitric oxide, which helps reduce systemic inflammation. Furthermore, the plant's constituents can modulate apoptosis-related proteins and antioxidant enzymes to protect cells from oxidative stress. By engaging these diverse protein pathways, the plant exerts its documented anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and neuroprotective effects.

What are the therapeutic actions of Celtis sinensis?

Celtis sinensis has therapeutic properties that primarily involve treating digestive ailments, skin inflammation, and respiratory issues through the use of its bark, leaves, and fruit. The bark is often decocted to address symptoms such as diarrhea and dysentery due to its astringent qualities. Additionally, the leaves can be applied topically or ingested to reduce swelling and soothe skin irritations. The fruit is frequently consumed to provide nutritional support and aid in overall digestive regularity. These various parts work together to provide a broad spectrum of traditional medicinal applications.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions, skin ailments, digestive issues, and respiratory ailments. The bark is often employed in traditional medicine to reduce swelling and soothe skin irritations such as eczema or rashes. Decoctions made from its components are frequently consumed to alleviate symptoms of coughs, bronchitis, and other lung-related discomforts. Additionally, certain preparations are used to address gastrointestinal distress and promote overall digestive health. These therapeutic applications rely on the plant's diverse chemical properties to target specific bodily systems.

What are the herbal preparations of Celtis sinensis?

Celtris sinensis is prepared as a decoction of the dried fruit, a powder from the bark or leaves, or as a topical paste for skin ailments. The dried fruit is most commonly simmered in water to create a sweet tonic used to nourish the yin and moisten the intestines. Practitioners may also grind the bark into a fine powder to be encapsulated or mixed into liquid mediums for internal consumption. For external applications, the crushed leaves are often fashioned into a poultice to treat inflammatory conditions or sores. These diverse methods allow the plant's medicinal properties to be utilized for both systemic nourishment and localized healing.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Celtis sinensis can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, itching, or swelling if the sap or bark comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. While the plant is used in traditional medicine, its safety profile for internal consumption remains insufficiently documented in modern clinical studies. Potential side effects from ingestion may include gastrointestinal distress or unpredictable reactions due to unknown chemical concentrations in different parts of the tree. Individuals with pre-existing allergies to related species should exercise extreme caution when handling any part of this plant. Consult a medical professional before attempting any herbal applications to avoid unexpected adverse outcomes.
