Botanical Identification of Bearberry (Arctostaphylos uva-ursi)

To identify Bearberry (Arctostaphylos uva-ursi) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Bearberry, or Arctostaphylos uva-ursi, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Ericales, family Ericaceae, genus Arctostaphylos, and species uva-ursi. These classifications help identify its botanical relationships and place it within the broader context of plant life.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Bearberry is Arctostaphylos uva-ursi, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Arctostaphylos uva-ursi (L.) S. Watson and Pyrrocoma uva-ursi. Common names include Bearberry, Stonecrop, and Foxberry, with regional variations such as Bear's Wort and Redberry. Historically, it was referred to as Uva ursi, reflecting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Bearberry is a low-growing evergreen shrub with a dense, mat-forming growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly in rocky or sandy soils. Its stems are prostrate, often rooting at the nodes, and covered in short, stiff hairs. Leaves are oval, leathery, and glossy, with a reddish tint in winter. The inflorescence is a cluster of small, reddish-white flowers that bloom in early spring. The fruit is a red, bladder-like berry containing one or two seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Bearberry can be distinguished by its evergreen leaves with white undersides, red berries in fall, and small, leathery leaves that remain throughout winter. It grows low to the ground with dense foliage, and its stems are prostrate or trailing. It is often found in rocky, acidic soils, which helps differentiate it from similar shrubs.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Bearberry is native to the northern hemisphere, including Europe, Asia, and North America. It thrives in cool, temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers acidic, well-drained soils and is commonly found in rocky, woodland, or alpine areas. It grows at altitudes ranging from 0 to 2,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Bearberry blooms in early spring with small white or pink flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer, following the flowering period. For identification purposes, harvest is best done in late summer to early autumn when the leaves are fully developed and the plant is most identifiable.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Bearberry belongs to the Ericaceae family, closely related to other shrubs like blueberries and cranberries. It is most closely related to species within the Arctostaphylos genus, sharing similar morphological and chemical characteristics. These relationships highlight its role in the heath plant group, which includes many medicinal and ecologically significant species.
