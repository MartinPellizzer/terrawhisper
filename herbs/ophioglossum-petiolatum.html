Ophioglossum petiolatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Ophioglossum petiolatum?

Ophioglossum petiolatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Ophioglossidae. Following its taxonomic descent, it falls under the order Ophioglossales and is a member of the family Ophioglossaceae. Ultimately, this specific plant is defined by its placement within the genus Ophioglossum, specifically identifying it as the species petiolatum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ophioglossum petiolatum has a single, fleshy, undivided leaf consisting of a long, slender stalk called a petiole and a terminal, lanceolate fertile spike. The basal portion of the plant features a small, scale-like sterile leaf that is significantly reduced in size. This sterile component is attached to the base of the petiole and serves to protect the emerging growth. The fertile spike is densely packed with small, brownish sporangia that release spores for reproduction. The entire structure emerges directly from an underground rhizome, making the plant appear as a solitary, upright stalk.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ophioglossum petiolatum?

This plant has anti-HBV surface antigen activity, anti-inflammatory properties, and antioxidant capabilities. Research indicates that extracts from Ophioglossum petiolatum can effectively reduce the presence of the hepatitis B virus surface antigen in biological models. Beyond its antiviral potential, the plant contains bioactive compounds that help mitigate oxidative stress within cellular environments. These phytochemicals also work to suppress inflammatory responses by regulating specific chemical mediators in the body. Consequently, the plant serves as a significant subject for developing new therapeutic agents against viral and inflammatory conditions.

As discussed in a study, Ophioglossum petiolatum contains six homoflavonoids identified as ophioglonin (1), ophioglonin 7-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (2), ophioglonol (3), ophioglonol prenyl ether (4), ophioglonol 4'-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (5), and isoophioglonin 7-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (6). The plant also yielded five known compounds including quercetin, luteolin, kaempferol, 3,5,7,3',4'-pentahydroxy-8-prenylflavone, and quercetin 3-O-methyl ether. Regarding pharmacological activities, compounds 1 and quercetin 3-O-methyl ether demonstrated slight anti-HBV surface antigen activity. This specific activity was observed at a concentration of 25 microM.
