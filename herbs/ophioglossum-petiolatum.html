Ophioglossum petiolatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Ophioglossum petiolatum?

Ophioglossum petiolatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Ophioglossidae. Following its taxonomic descent, it falls under the order Ophioglossales and is a member of the family Ophioglossaceae. Ultimately, this specific plant is defined by its placement within the genus Ophioglossum, specifically identifying it as the species petiolatum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ophioglossum petiolatum has a single, fleshy, undivided leaf consisting of a long, slender stalk called a petiole and a terminal, lanceolate fertile spike. The basal portion of the plant features a small, scale-like sterile leaf that is significantly reduced in size. This sterile component is attached to the base of the petiole and serves to protect the emerging growth. The fertile spike is densely packed with small, brownish sporangia that release spores for reproduction. The entire structure emerges directly from an underground rhizome, making the plant appear as a solitary, upright stalk.

How is this plant cultivated?

What are the pharmacological activities of Ophioglossum petiolatum?

This plant has demonstrated significant anti-HBV surface antigen activity in pharmacological studies. Researchers have identified specific bioactive compounds within Ophioglossum petiolatum that help inhibit the replication of the hepatitis B virus. These substances work by targeting the production of viral proteins, which can reduce the presence of the surface antigen in the bloodstream. Beyond its antiviral properties, the plant is often investigated for its broader potential in treating various inflammatory conditions. Continued investigation into its chemical constituents aims to fully understand its therapeutic mechanisms.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", Ophioglossum petiolatum contains six homoflavonoids, which are ophioglonin (1), ophioglonin 7-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (2), ophioglonol (3), ophioglonol prenyl ether (4), ophioglonol 4'-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (5), and isoophioglonin 7-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (6). The plant also yields five known compounds, specifically quercetin, luteolin, kaempferol, 3,5,7,3',4'-pentahydroxy-8-prenylflavone, and quercetin 3-O-methyl ether. Regarding pharmacological activities, compounds 1 and quercetin 3-O-methyl ether demonstrated slight anti-HBV surface antigen activity. This specific activity was observed at a concentration of 25 microM.
