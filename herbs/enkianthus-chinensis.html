Enkianthus chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Enkianthus chinensis?

Enkianthus chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down its taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Ericales and is a member of the family Ericaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Enkianthus, and its specific species epithet, chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Enkianthus chinensis has erect, perennial herbaceous stems that reach heights of up to 1 meter and bear opposite, lanceolate to oblong leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically bright green and possesses a somewhat fleshy texture. Its inflorescence consists of terminal, many-flowered cymes featuring salverform corollas that are predominantly white or pale pink. Each individual flower displays a narrow tube that expands into five spreading lobes. The plant also produces small, capsule-like fruits that contain numerous tiny seeds.

The species thrives in temperate to subtropical climates characterized by seasonal variations in moisture and temperature. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains oleanane-type pentacyclic triterpenoids, taraxerane-type triterpenoids, and ursane-type triterpenoids which function as potent medicinal compounds. These diverse chemical structures contribute to a wide range of pharmacological activities, including anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. The presence of oleanane-type pentacyclic triterpenoids is particularly noted for supporting liver health and cellular protection. Additionally, the taraxerane-type and ursane-type compounds play significant roles in modulating immune responses within the body. Together, these specific secondary metabolites define the therapeutic potential of the plant's chemical profile.

As discussed in a study, thirty new pentacyclic triterpenoids were isolated from the stems and branches of Enkianthus chinensis. These include five oleanane-type (1-5), twenty-three ursane-type (9-23, 26-33), and two taraxerane-type (24 and 25) compounds, alongside fourteen known triterpenoids. Among these, sixteen compounds (1-5, 9-13, 20, 22, 32, 34-36) contain a gem-hydroxymethyl group at C-4. In vitro biological activity evaluations showed that compounds 8, 9, 12-14, 17, 24, and 44 exhibited potent hepatoprotective effects at 10 μM. Additionally, compound 25 demonstrated latent activity against HSV-1 with an IC50 value of 6.4 μM.
