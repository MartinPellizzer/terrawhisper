Enkianthus chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Enkianthus chinensis?

Enkianthus chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down its taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Ericales and is a member of the family Ericaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Enkianthus, and its specific species epithet, chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Enkianthus chinensis has erect, perennial herbaceous stems that reach heights of up to 1 meter and bear opposite, lanceolate to oblong leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically bright green and possesses a somewhat fleshy texture. Its inflorescence consists of terminal, many-flowered cymes featuring salverform corollas that are predominantly white or pale pink. Each individual flower displays a narrow tube that expands into five spreading lobes. The plant also produces small, capsule-like fruits that contain numerous tiny seeds.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains various medicinal compounds including oleanane-type pentacyclic triterpenoids, taraxerane-type triterpenoids, and ursane-type triterpenoids. These specific chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the pharmacological activities observed in the species. Research indicates that these triterpenoids contribute to the plant's anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. The diversity of these chemical structures allows the plant to interact with various biological pathways in the human body. Scientists continue to study these specific molecular frameworks to develop new therapeutic applications.

According to a study published by "Bioorganic chemistry", thirty new pentacyclic triterpenoids were isolated from the stems and branches of Enkianthus chinensis. These include five oleanane-type (1-5), twenty-three ursane-type (9-23, 26-33) and two taraxerane-type (24 and 25) compounds, alongside fourteen known triterpenoids. Sixteen compounds (1-5, 9-13, 20, 22, 32, 34-36) bearing a gem-hydroxymethyl group at C-4 were identified. During in vitro biological activity evaluation, compounds 8, 9, 12-14, 17, 24, and 44 showed potent hepatoprotective effects at 10 μM. Additionally, compound 25 demonstrated latent activity against HSV-1 with an IC50 value of 6.4 μM.
