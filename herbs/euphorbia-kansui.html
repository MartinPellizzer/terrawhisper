Euphorbia kansui

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia kansui?

Euphorbia kansui belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malpighiales. The plant is further categorized into the family Euphorbiaceae, which contains the genus Euphorbia, and is specifically identified by its species name, kansui.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia kansui has a perennial, herbaceous growth habit characterized by upright, fleshy stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 100 centimeters. The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and possess an elliptical to lanceolate shape with smooth margins. These plants produce small, inconspicuous flowers organized into specialized structures called cyathia, which are clustered in terminal umbel-like cymes. A distinguishing morphological feature is the presence of a milky white latex that flows from the stems when injured. The root system is generally shallow but robust enough to support its upright structure.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical and temperate regions of East Asia, specifically spanning across parts of China, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. It thrives in various habitats ranging from coastal areas to mountain slopes where light is abundant. Within these regions, it is frequently found growing in sandy soils or rocky crevices. Its distribution is closely tied to mild climates that provide sufficient moisture and warmth. While its primary range is concentrated in these specific Asian territories, its ecological niche remains relatively localized.

What are the pharmacological activities of Euphorbia kansui?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anticancer, antitumor, cytotoxicity, and laxative properties. Its chemical constituents exert cytotoxic effects that allow it to inhibit the growth of various malignant cells. These anticancer and antitumor actions make the species a subject of interest in modern oncology research. Additionally, the plant possesses potent anti-inflammatory properties that help reduce swelling and immune responses. Due to its natural chemical composition, it is also frequently utilized for its strong laxative effects.

As discussed in a study, Euphorbia kansui stir-fried with vinegar (V-kansui) possesses promising biological activities for treating malignant ascites with reduced toxicity compared to crude kansui. The study identified 17 correlated compounds as potential active ingredients in V-kansui. Through metabolomics, 16 significant metabolites were screened out, which are mainly involved in arachidonic acid metabolism, steroid hormone biosynthesis, and primary bile acid to reduce inflammation and modulate the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. Key targets identified for the treatment of malignant ascites include HSP90AA1, ANXA2, PRDX6, PCNA, SOD2, and ALB. These results suggest that V-kansui could be a promising safe and therapeutic medicine for the excretion of ascites.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds ingenol, kansenoine, kansuinin A, euphol, and paeonal. These specific diterpenoids and phytochemicals are recognized for their diverse biological activities in pharmacological research. Ingenol and kansenoine are frequently studied for their potential roles in modulating cellular responses. Additionally, the presence of kansuinin A, euphol, and paeonal contributes to the complex chemical profile used in various therapeutic investigations. Each of these substances provides unique structural characteristics that interest medicinal chemists.

As discussed in a study, Euphorbia kansui contains the major diterpenoid 3-O-(2'E,4'Z-decadienoyl)-20-O-acetylingenol, also known as 3-O-EZ. This compound can convert into ingenol after the plant is processed with vinegar. In the study, 3-O-EZ and ingenol were administered to an H22 mouse hepatoma ascites model at levels of 110.14, 50.07, and 27.54 mg/kg over seven days. When Euphorbia kansui is stir-fried with vinegar, the decreasing rate of 3-O-EZ is 19.14%, while the increasing rate of ingenol is 92.31%. Both 3-O-EZ and ingenol possess significant effects in treating malignant ascites effusion.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cancer, tumors, malignant pleural effusion, ascites, and edema. Its medicinal properties are traditionally applied to help reduce the size of various types of tumors. Doctors and herbalists also utilize it to manage the accumulation of fluid in the abdomen known as ascites. Furthermore, the plant helps alleviate edema by addressing systemic swelling. It is specifically noted for its role in managing malignant pleural effusion by reducing fluid buildup around the lungs.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the roots in water, a tincture by extracting its properties in alcohol, a powder through drying and grinding, or an external application such as a poultice. Tinctures provide a concentrated form that allows for precise dosing in liquid preparations. The dried powder can be easily incorporated into capsules or topical salves. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation and blistering, intense eye irritation, respiratory irritation, and gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. Contact with the milky sap often leads to localized inflammation and painful skin lesions. If the sap enters the eyes, it may cause significant redness and irritation. Ingestion of the plant typically triggers intense abdominal pain accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Some individuals may also experience dizziness following exposure or accidental ingestion.
