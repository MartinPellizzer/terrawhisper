Grewia mollis

What's the taxonomical classification of Grewia mollis?

Grewia mollis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent through the order Malvales, it is placed in the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Grewia and is specifically designated as the species mollis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Grewia mollis contains bioactive compounds including saponins, tannins, and various flavonoids that contribute to its pharmacological potential. These secondary metabolites are primarily concentrated within the plant's leaves and bark. Research suggests that the presence of these polyphenolic substances may provide antioxidant and antimicrobial properties. Some studies indicate that the plant's chemical profile could support anti-inflammatory activities in biological systems. Further investigation is often required to isolate specific molecules for precise therapeutic applications.

What are the therapeutic actions of Grewia mollis?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin conditions, digestive issues, and inflammatory ailments. Its medicinal properties are often utilized to manage dermatological problems such as wounds or skin irritations. Traditional applications also include addressing gastrointestinal discomfort through its bioactive compounds. Some studies suggest its extracts may assist in reducing systemic inflammation within the body. Local communities frequently rely on its bark or leaves for these specific therapeutic purposes.

What are the herbal preparations of Grewia mollis?

Grewia mollis is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion derived from its bark, leaves, or roots for medicinal use. These liquid extracts are often concentrated to enhance the potency of their bioactive compounds. Some traditional practitioners may also process the plant material into topical ointments or poultices to treat external ailments. The powdered form of the dried plant parts can be used in specific herbal mixtures or capsules. Such diverse preparations allow for both internal consumption and localized dermatological applications.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Grewia mollis can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of unidentified secondary metabolites. While specific clinical studies on human side effects are limited, traditional knowledge suggests caution when handling the plant's tissues. Contact with the sap or leaves may trigger localized dermatitis or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of any part of the plant could lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Therefore, it is essential to avoid direct contact and prevent accidental consumption of this species.
