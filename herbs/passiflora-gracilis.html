Passiflora gracilis

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora gracilis?

Passiflora gracilis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Malpighiales. This plant is a member of the family Passifloraceae, specifically situated within the genus Passiflora, and is identified by its unique species designation, gracilis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Passiflora gracilis has slender, climbing stems characterized by thin, cylindrical tendrils used for anchoring to surrounding vegetation.

The leaves are typically simple or deeply lobed with smooth margins and a glossy texture on the upper surface. Its flowers feature a complex structure including a distinct corona of fine filaments and a prominent central reproductive apparatus. The fruit develops into a small, fleshy berry that typically transitions in color as it reaches maturity.

These morphological traits distinguish it from other members of the Passiflora genus found in similar tropical habitats.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Passiflora gracilis targets gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) receptors, adenosine receptors, and specific monoamine oxidase enzymes to exert its sedative and anxiolytic effects. The bioactive alkaloids and flavonoids within the plant act as modulators of the central nervous system by enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission. By interacting with GABAergic pathways, these compounds help reduce neuronal excitability and alleviate anxiety. Additionally, the inhibition of monoamine oxidase may influence the levels of neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine, contributing to its antidepressant potential. These multi-target interactions explain the complex pharmacological profile observed in its traditional medicinal use.

        What are the herbal preparations of Passiflora gracilis?

        Passiflora gracilis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice to utilize its bioactive compounds. The dried aerial parts are most commonly boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid extract for internal consumption. Alternatively, steeping the plant material in hot water allows for a gentler infusion that preserves more volatile components. For external applications, the crushed plant matter is applied directly to the skin in the form of a moist poultice. These various methods ensure that the specific therapeutic properties of the species are effectively extracted and delivered.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Passiflora gracilis can cause drowsiness, dizziness, and low blood pressure due to its sedative properties. Some individuals may experience mild gastrointestinal issues such as nausea or stomach discomfort after consumption. There is also a potential risk of interactions with pharmaceutical medications, particularly those designed to treat anxiety or insomnia. Because it may lower blood sugar levels, people with diabetes should monitor their glucose closely. It is generally advised to avoid use during pregnancy because of its potential effects on uterine contractions.
