Lycium barbarum

What's the taxonomical classification of Lycium barbarum?

Lycium barbarum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this botanical lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its hierarchical descent, the plant is organized under the order Solanales and falls within the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Lycium and the species barbarum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lycium barbarum has perennial, woody, deciduous shrub stems that typically reach heights of two to three meters and feature small, ovate to lanceolate leaves arranged alternately along the branches. These leaves are generally bright green and measure between ten and twenty-five millimeters in length. The plant produces small, bell-shaped flowers that are typically violet or purplish with darker veins. Following pollination, it develops elongated, red, fleshy berries that are the primary harvestable fruit. The stems often exhibit sharp, small spines at the nodes, which is a characteristic feature of the species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and semi-arid regions of northern and western China, specifically within the Ningxia, Gansu, and Xinjiang provinces. It thrives in dry, sandy soils where sunlight is abundant and temperatures are relatively high. While its primary cultivation is centered in these East Asian territories, it has been introduced to various other parts of the world for medicinal and agricultural use. Some populations can now be found in parts of North America and Europe due to human intervention. These global distributions are typically limited to climates that mimic its original steppe and desert habitats.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Lycium barbarum contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its dried fleshy fruits, commonly known as goji berries. These berries are harvested and dried to preserve high levels of antioxidants like polysaccharides and carotenoids. While the fruit is the most widely utilized part, certain traditional practices also involve using the plant's roots or leaves for specific decoctions. The fruit is most frequently consumed in various forms to support immune function and eye health. Research continues to explore how these specific components interact with human biological systems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Lycium barbarum?

This plant has potent pharmacological activities including anti-oxidative, anti-inflammatory, hepatoprotective, immunomodulatory, and anti-diabetic properties. The presence of polysaccharides and carotenoids helps neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage. Its anti-inflammatory effects work by reducing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines within the body. Furthermore, the plant supports liver health through hepatoprotective mechanisms and regulates the immune system via immunomodulatory actions. Finally, its anti-diabetic compounds assist in managing blood glucose levels and improving insulin sensitivity.

As discussed in a study, Lycium barbarum, specifically in the form of goji berry leaf, has been used for medicinal foods due to its pharmacological effects including anti-oxidative and anti-obesity activities. The standardized roasted extract of this plant is rich in kaempferol-3-O-sophoroside-7-O-glucosides and shows 0.7-2.1% of analytical standard variance. Regarding safety, the lethal dose of the roasted extract was determined to be ≥ 2000 mg/kg during single dose toxicity testing. Repeated doses of 0-1000 mg/kg per day for 14 days did not show any toxicity signs or gross pathological abnormalities. No genotoxic signs appeared via bacterial reverse mutation up to 5000 μg/plate, and chromosomal aberrations were not significantly increased with a p > 0.05. Furthermore, chromosomal aberrations were not induced at 2000 mg/kg in the in vivo bone marrow micronucleus test with a p > 0.05.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains Lycium barbarum polysaccharides, Lyciumamides L-N, Kaempferol, Luteolin, and Zeaxanthin dipalmitate. These medicinal compounds work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the body. The polysaccharides and Lyciumamides are specifically noted for their potential to support immune function and cellular health. Additionally, the presence of Kaempferol and Luteolin contributes to the plant's ability to mitigate oxidative stress. Zeaxanthin dipalmitate further enhances these effects by protecting ocular health and overall biological stability.

As discussed in a study, Lycium barbarum polysaccharides were evaluated regarding their immunological effects. Pure LBPs, referred to as LBPs-X, were divided into different doses for comparison. Lower doses of LBPs-X ranging from 5-20 mg/kg.d demonstrated a remarkable effect on immunological enhancement. Specifically, LBPs-X at 10 mg/kg.d showed a highly significance difference compared with crude LBPs on immune indices in mice. Lycium barbarum polysaccharides achieve the best pharmacological effect when administered at the best dose.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating age-related macular degeneration, diabetes, liver diseases, neurodegenerative disease, and oxidative stress. Its high antioxidant content helps mitigate cellular damage caused by oxidative stress throughout the body. For ocular health, the berries provide essential nutrients that may slow the progression of age-related macular degeneration. The compounds in Lycium barbarum also assist in regulating blood glucose levels for those managing diabetes. Furthermore, regular consumption is linked to improved metabolic functions that support the management of liver diseases and the protection of neurons against neurodegenerative disease.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as dried fruit known as goji berries, a liquid decoction, a concentrated extract, a fine powder, or an alcohol-based tincture. The dried berries are the most common form used in culinary and traditional applications. For medicinal purposes, a decoction is made by simmering the fruit in water to release its soluble components. Concentrated extracts and powders offer more potent doses of active polysaccharides for specific health goals. Finally, a tincture provides a shelf-stable way to ingest the plant's properties through a concentrated liquid medium.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, hypotension, blood thinning effects, nausea, diarrhea, abdominal discomfort, dizziness, and dry mouth. Consuming Lycium barbarum may lead to significant drops in blood sugar levels or a decrease in blood pressure. Some individuals might experience gastrointestinal issues such as nausea, diarrhea, or general abdominal discomfort. There is also a risk of experiencing dizziness or a sensation of dry mouth after use. Furthermore, the plant's potential blood thinning effects could interact with certain medications or affect clotting.

  • Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)
  • Hypotension (low blood pressure)
  • Blood thinning effects
