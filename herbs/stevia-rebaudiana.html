Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Stevia rebaudiana is a perennial shrub native to South America, known for its sweet-tasting leaves that are widely used as a natural, zero-calorie sweetener in herbal medicine and alternative health practices.

What is Stevia (Stevia Rebaudiana)?

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) is a perennial plant native to Paraguay and Brazil, belonging to the sunflower family, and is widely cultivated for its leaves, which contain natural sweeteners known as steviol glycosides.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Stevia is Stevia rebaudiana. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Stevia, and species Stevia rebaudiana. It is commonly known as sweetleaf in North America and perilla in parts of Asia. The plant is also referred to by botanical synonyms such as Stevia rebaudiana Bertoni and regional names like kuuma in Japan.

Plant Description and Morphology

Stevia rebaudiana is a perennial shrub native to Paraguay and Brazil, characterized by its upright growth habit, lance-shaped leaves with serrated edges, and small, greenish-white flowers that bloom in clusters.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Stevia rebaudiana is native to the tropical regions of South America, specifically Paraguay and Brazil, where it grows in shaded, humid environments such as forests and along stream banks.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves of Stevia rebaudiana are the primary plant part used medicinally, containing steviol glycosides which are responsible for its intense sweet taste and various pharmacological effects.

Phytochemical Composition

Stevia rebaudiana contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including steviol glycosides, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and potential health benefits.

Pharmacological Properties

Stevia rebaudiana contains high concentrations of steviol glycosides, which are responsible for its intense sweetening properties and have been shown to exert various pharmacological effects including antidiabetic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant activities.

Mechanisms of Action

Stevia rebaudiana exerts its effects primarily through the stimulation of sweet taste receptors on the tongue, the modulation of glucose metabolism, and the inhibition of enzymes involved in carbohydrate digestion.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) is primarily used in herbal medicine for its intense, natural sweetness and as a potential alternative to sugar, with some studies suggesting it may support blood sugar regulation and have anti-inflammatory properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Stevia is commonly prepared as a dried leaf or extract, with the leaves typically used to make a tea, infused into beverages, or processed into a powdered form for sweetening purposes.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of stevia typically ranges from 100 to 500 milligrams per day, with the exact amount depending on the form of stevia used, such as leaf extract, powder, or liquid, and individual health needs.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Stevia rebaudiana is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a sweetener, with minimal risk of adverse effects for most people, although high doses may cause gastrointestinal discomfort or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Drug Interactions

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the cardiovascular system, such as ACE inhibitors and diuretics, due to its potential effects on blood pressure and potassium levels.

Toxicity and Precautions

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, with minimal toxicity reported in most populations, though high doses may cause gastrointestinal discomfort or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Stevia rebaudiana is typically cultivated in warm climates with well-drained soil, requiring minimal water and regular pruning to promote the growth of high-quality leaves used for extraction of stevioside.

Processing and Extraction

Stevia rebaudiana is processed and extracted through methods such as aqueous extraction, ethanol extraction, or supercritical CO2 extraction to isolate steviol glycosides, the sweet compounds responsible for its intense sweetness.

Storage and Shelf Life

Stevia leaves and extracts should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dark, and dry place to maintain their potency and shelf life, which is typically 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of Stevia rebaudiana in herbal medicine has shown its potential as a natural sweetener with low caloric content and possible metabolic benefits.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Stevia rebaudiana has been traditionally used for centuries by indigenous peoples of South America, particularly the Guarani and Guaraní communities, as a natural sweetener and medicinal plant, valued for its intense sweetness and various therapeutic properties.

Regulatory Status

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for use as a food additive and sweetener, and is approved as a food ingredient in the European Union and other regions, with varying regulations on its use in medicinal contexts.
