Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Stevia rebaudiana is a perennial shrub native to South America, known for its sweet-tasting leaves that are widely used as a natural, zero-calorie sweetener in herbal medicine and alternative health practices.

What is Stevia (Stevia Rebaudiana)?

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) is a perennial plant native to Paraguay and Brazil, belonging to the sunflower family, and is widely cultivated for its leaves, which contain natural sweeteners known as steviol glycosides.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Stevia is Stevia rebaudiana. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Stevia, and species Stevia rebaudiana. It is commonly known as sweetleaf in North America and perilla in parts of Asia. The plant is also referred to by botanical synonyms such as Stevia rebaudiana Bertoni and regional names like kuuma in Japan.

Plant Description and Morphology

Stevia is a perennial shrub that typically grows to a height of 30-60 cm with a compact, upright structure.

Its leaves are lanceolate, with a pointed tip and a slightly serrated edge, and they are arranged alternately along the stems. The small, greenish-white flowers are clustered in dense, rounded inflorescences and bloom in late summer. The plant has a fibrous root system that is shallow and spreads horizontally near the soil surface.

The leaves are glossy, slightly hairy, and have a mild, sweet scent, while the texture is firm and slightly waxy.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Stevia is native to tropical regions of South America, specifically Paraguay and Brazil, where it grows in the wild.

Its geographic distribution includes parts of Argentina, Uruguay, and northern Chile. The plant thrives in warm, humid climates with mild winters and abundant sunlight. It prefers well-drained soils and moderate to high rainfall.

Stevia is typically found in moist, shaded environments such as riverbanks and forest understories.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves of Stevia rebaudiana are the primary part used medicinally.

They contain steviol glycosides, which are responsible for the plant's intense sweet taste. These compounds are extracted and used as a natural sweetener. The leaves are also used in traditional medicine for their purported health benefits.

The roots and stems are occasionally used, but the leaves are the most significant medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Stevia rebaudiana contains a variety of active compounds, primarily glycosides known as steviol glycosides, which are responsible for its intense sweet taste.

These include stevioside, rebaudioside A, and rebaudioside C, among others. Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, and terpenoids also contribute to its pharmacological properties. Chemical markers like steviol and its derivatives are used to authenticate the quality and purity of stevia products.

These compounds are responsible for both the sweetening effect and the potential health benefits associated with stevia.

Pharmacological Properties

Stevia rebaudiana exhibits pharmacological properties that make it a valuable component in herbal medicine, primarily due to its active compounds such as steviol glycosides.

Its therapeutic actions include antidiabetic, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects, which contribute to its use in managing metabolic disorders and infections. Biologically, stevia influences glucose metabolism and may help regulate blood sugar levels by enhancing insulin sensitivity. Pharmacodynamically, it acts by modulating key enzymes and receptors involved in metabolic pathways.

System-level effects of stevia include potential cardiovascular benefits and gastrointestinal support, though further research is needed to fully understand its mechanisms.

Mechanisms of Action

Stevia rebaudiana exerts its effects through multiple physiological mechanisms, primarily by interacting with specific receptors and metabolic pathways in the body.

At the cellular level, its sweet compounds, such as steviol glycosides, bind to sweet taste receptors on the tongue, triggering neural signals that signal sweetness without caloric intake. Biochemically, these compounds are metabolized in the liver into steviol, which may influence glucose metabolism and insulin response through interactions with key enzymes and transporters. The target systems include the gastrointestinal tract, where they modulate gut microbiota and reduce glucose absorption, and the central nervous system, where they may affect appetite regulation.

These mechanisms collectively contribute to its use in herbal medicine for metabolic support and flavor enhancement.

Therapeutic Uses

Stevia rebaudiana is used therapeutically for its potential to support blood sugar regulation and reduce calorie intake.

It is commonly employed to manage mild hypertension by promoting vasodilation. The plant has been studied for its ability to aid in weight management due to its zero-calorie sweetening properties. It may also help alleviate symptoms of metabolic syndrome by improving insulin sensitivity.

Additionally, it is used to treat respiratory conditions such as coughs and sore throats due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Stevia rebaudiana is commonly used in various forms, including dried leaves, extracts, and powders.

The most popular preparation is the dried leaves, which are often used as a natural sweetener. Extracts are concentrated forms of the plant, typically containing stevioside, the main sweetening compound. These extracts can be found in liquid or powder form for easy incorporation into food or beverages.

Additionally, the plant is processed into specialized sweetener products for commercial use.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of stevia for general use is typically 100 to 200 milligrams per day, depending on the form and concentration of the product.

Standard doses are usually based on extracts containing 10% to 20% stevioside, the primary sweetening compound. Stevia should be taken 2 to 3 times daily, spaced evenly throughout the day. The duration of use should not exceed 12 weeks without consulting a healthcare professional.

To avoid overuse, it is important to follow the manufacturer's instructions and consult a healthcare provider for personalized guidance.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Stevia rebaudiana is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a sweetener, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort or a bitter aftertaste in some individuals.

Side effects are typically rare and mild, though excessive consumption might lead to nausea or headaches in sensitive people. Contraindications include individuals with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family, as cross-reactivity is possible. While limited research suggests it may be safe during pregnancy, healthcare providers often recommend caution due to insufficient long-term data.

Allergic reactions, though uncommon, can include symptoms like skin rashes, itching, or respiratory issues, and should be avoided by those with a history of similar allergies.

Drug Interactions

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) may interact with certain pharmaceuticals and supplements, potentially affecting their efficacy or increasing the risk of side effects.

It can interfere with medications that regulate blood sugar, such as insulin or oral hypoglycemics, by enhancing their effects. Stevia may also interact with blood pressure medications, as it has mild hypotensive properties. Some studies suggest that it could reduce the absorption of certain supplements, particularly fat-soluble vitamins like vitamin A and D.

It is important to consult a healthcare provider before using stevia if you are on any medication or supplement regimen.

Toxicity and Precautions

The toxicity of Stevia rebaudiana is generally low when consumed in typical amounts used as a sweetener, but excessive intake may lead to adverse effects.

The toxic dose for humans is not well established, though some studies suggest that consuming more than 1,000 mg of stevioside per day may cause gastrointestinal discomfort. Long-term safety studies indicate that regular use of stevia within recommended limits is considered safe for most individuals, including pregnant women and children. However, there are concerns about potential risks for individuals with specific health conditions, such as diabetes or hypertension, due to its effects on blood sugar and blood pressure.

Poisoning from stevia is rare but possible with extremely high doses, leading to symptoms like nausea, vomiting, and dizziness.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Stevia is typically grown in temperate climates and requires well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5.

It thrives in full sunlight, needing at least 6 to 8 hours of direct sun daily for optimal growth. The plant is drought-tolerant but requires consistent moisture during the growing season, with watering reduced after flowering. Propagation is usually done through stem cuttings, which root easily in a moist, warm environment. Stevia reaches maturity in about 6 to 8 months, with the best harvest time occurring when the leaves are fully developed but before flowering.

Harvesting involves cutting the upper stems just above the leaf nodes to encourage new growth and maximize leaf yield.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and Extraction of Stevia involves several key steps to isolate its sweet compounds.

The first step is drying the leaves, which removes moisture and prepares the plant material for further processing. After drying, the leaves are ground into a fine powder to increase surface area for efficient extraction. Extraction is typically done using water or alcohol to dissolve the steviol glycosides, the main sweetening compounds.

Distillation may then be employed to concentrate the extracted compounds and remove solvents, resulting in a purified extract.

Storage and Shelf Life

Stevia leaves and extracts should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain potency and quality.

The shelf life of dried stevia leaves is typically 1 to 2 years when stored properly. Exposure to light can degrade the active compounds, reducing effectiveness over time. To prevent moisture damage, keep stevia in airtight containers or sealed jars.

Ideal storage conditions include temperatures below 70°F (21°C) and low humidity levels.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of Stevia rebaudiana in herbal medicine has shown its potential as a natural sweetener with low caloric content and possible metabolic benefits.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Stevia rebaudiana has been traditionally used for centuries by indigenous peoples of South America, particularly the Guarani and Guaraní communities, as a natural sweetener and medicinal plant, valued for its intense sweetness and various therapeutic properties.

Regulatory Status

Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for use as a food additive and sweetener, and is approved as a food ingredient in the European Union and other regions, with varying regulations on its use in medicinal contexts.
