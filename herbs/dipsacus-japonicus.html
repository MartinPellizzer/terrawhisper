Dipsacus japonicus

What's the taxonomical classification of Dipsacus japonicus?

Dipsacus japonicus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Dipsacales and the family Caprifoliaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Dipsacus, and its unique species designation, japonicus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dipsacus japonicus has an erect, perennial herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 10 to 60 centimeters and features opposite, lanceolate to ovate leaves with serrated margins. The foliage is characterized by a decussate arrangement where each pair of leaves is positioned at a right angle to the pair above or below it. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal heads composed of numerous small, tubular, pale purple or white flowers. Each individual floret is zygomorphic and is subtended by several prominent, leaf-like bracts that surround the flower cluster. The plant also develops a woody base or caudex from which the annual stems emerge each growing season.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Dipsacus japonicus targets inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. What health problems are treated using this plant?

This plant is used for treating bone-associated diseases, reducing inflammation, and addressing various types of pain. It is frequently applied in traditional medicine to alleviate symptoms related to joint stiffness and aching bones. The plant's properties are also utilized to manage swelling and promote blood circulation throughout the body. Some practitioners use it to help soothe muscular discomfort and systemic heat. This botanical specimen remains a significant component in herbal remedies targeting musculoskeletal issues.

As discussed in a study, Dipsacus japonicus root frequently contaminates Dipsaci Radix in Korean herbal markets. The effects of root extracts on osteoclastic bone resorption and TRAP-positive cell formations were evaluated using bone marrow-derived macrophages from 5 week old male ICR mice. All tested extracts, including those from Dipsacus japonicus, decreased bone resorption and inhibited TRAP-positive cell formations in a dose-dependent manner. However, only the Dipsacus japonicus extract demonstrated toxicity. Consequently, processing methods that reduce this toxicity must be discovered for its use in the Korean Pharmacopoeia.
