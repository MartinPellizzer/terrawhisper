Zea mays

What's the taxonomical classification of Zea mays?

Zea mays belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it falls under the order Poales and the family Poaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Zea, and its species name, mays.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Zea mays has a fibrous root system, an erect stem consisting of multiple nodes and internodes, and long, narrow, parallel-veined leaves that wrap around the stem. The plant reaches significant heights through its sturdy, hollow stalk which provides structural support for heavy ears of grain. Reproductive structures include separate male and female flowers, with pollen-producing tassels at the top and female silk-covered ears located in the leaf axils. Each leaf features a prominent midrib and a sheath that protects the developing nodes. The entire morphology is specialized to support rapid growth and the efficient production of large quantities of starch-rich kernels.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to central Mexico and has been cultivated extensively across nearly every continent due to its role as a primary global staple crop. While its ancestral origins lie in the diverse climates of Mesoamerica, human migration and agricultural expansion spread it to North and South America. Today, it is grown on a massive scale across the vast plains of the United States, China, and Brazil. Its ability to adapt to various environments allows it to thrive in diverse regions ranging from temperate grasslands to tropical zones. This wide distribution ensures that it remains one of the most widely planted cereal crops in the world.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Zea mays contains medicinal properties primarily found in its silk, kernels, and various plant extracts. The fine, thread-like silk is frequently utilized in traditional remedies to treat urinary tract infections and reduce inflammation. Corn kernels are processed to create corn oil, which provides essential fatty acids used in many therapeutic applications. Additionally, the plant's stover and husks can be utilized in specialized treatments to manage cholesterol levels. These diverse components allow for a wide range of applications in both nutritional and pharmaceutical contexts.

What are the therapeutic actions of Zea mays?

Zea mays has therapeutic properties derived from its mucilaginous content, which provides nutritive support and acts as a potent demulcent for irritated mucous membranes. These soothing compounds help to coat the digestive tract and alleviate discomfort from inflammation. The plant also functions as a mild diuretic, helping to increase urine output and support renal function. By reducing swelling and irritation, its anti-inflammatory effects contribute to overall systemic relief. These diverse actions make it a versatile botanical choice for various digestive and urinary concerns.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including anthocyanins, flavonoids, phenolic acids, polyphenols, and terpenoids. These polyphenols serve as potent antioxidants that help neutralize harmful free radicals within the body. The specific presence of anthocyanins and flavonoids provides significant anti-inflammatory and cardiovascular benefits. Additionally, the phenolic acids contribute to the plant's overall antimicrobial and protective properties. Finally, the terpenoids offer unique therapeutic effects that can support various physiological functions.

As discussed in a study, Zea mays contains a chemical arsenal of flavonoids that are essential for survival. The flavonoid pathway in maize involves a total of twenty-one genes. Within this pathway, the first 3 genes participate in the general phenylpropanoid pathway. Additionally, 4 genes act as common biosynthetic early genes for flavonoids, while 14 genes are specific to the flavonoid subgroups of anthocyanins and flavone C-glycosides. The major maize anthocyanin, C3G, is noted for having antica ncer, antidiabetic, and antioxidant effects.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat various agricultural diseases that impact its yield and health. Common rust, caused by Puccinia sorghi, creates reddish-brown pustules on the leaves. Gray leaf spot, triggered by Cercospora zeae-maydis, results in necrotic rectangular lesions. Northern corn leaf blight, driven by Exserohilum turcicum, produces long, cigar-shaped spots. Corn smut is caused by Ustilago maydis and produces large, fleshy galls on the kernels. Finally, maize dwarf mosaic virus can stunt growth and cause mosaic patterns on the foliage.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Common rust (puccinia sorghi)
  • Gray leaf spot (cercospora zeae-maydis)
  • Northern corn leaf blight (exserohilum turcicum)
  • Corn smut (ustilago maydis)
  • Maize dwarf mosaic virus

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing diabetes, hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, hypertension, and urinary infections. Extracts from the silk or kernels can assist in regulating blood glucose levels to prevent spikes in sugar. It also aids in lowering elevated lipid profiles to improve overall cardiovascular health. The plant's diuretic properties make it effective for treating various urinary infections by increasing fluid output. Additionally, its components help in reducing high blood pressure through natural metabolic support.

As discussed in a study, Zea mays provides corn stigma (CS) derived from the stigma and style of the plant. This substance has shown potential in relieving hyperglycemia associated with diabetes and can improve blood glucose levels in both GK rats and diabetic zebrafish. In rats, CS partially restored glucose and lipid metabolism indicators such as GHb, AST, ALT, U-Glu, UA, U-TP, U-ALB, and ACR. The treatment works by suppressing the expression of genes fdft1, lss, and cyp51 which are concerned with the steroid biosynthesis pathway. Through these mechanisms, Zea mays effectively improves blood glucose levels and ameliorates hyperglycemia.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a corn silk infusion, a corn silk tincture, or a corn silk extract to utilize its diuretic and anti-inflammatory properties. A corn silk infusion is typically made by steeping the dried stigmas in hot water to create a soothing tea. For a more concentrated form, a corn silk tincture uses alcohol to pull out specific phytochemicals over several weeks. Manufacturers also produce a corn silk extract through specialized processes to ensure consistent potency in standardized doses. These various preparations allow for different methods of administering the plant's therapeutic compounds.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions, digestive distress, and potential respiratory issues in sensitive individuals. While corn is a staple food, some people experience hives, itching, or swelling after consuming it. High consumption of corn-based products can lead to bloating or stomach discomfort due to its fiber content. Additionally, inhaling corn dust or pollen may trigger asthma attacks or seasonal allergy symptoms in certain populations. Those with known corn allergies should exercise caution to avoid severe systemic responses.
