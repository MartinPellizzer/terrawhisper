Zea mays

What's the taxonomical classification of Zea mays?

Zea mays belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it falls under the order Poales and the family Poaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Zea, and its species name, mays.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Zea mays contains bioactive compounds such as ferulic acid, p-coumaric acid, and various carotenoids that provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. These phenolic acids are particularly concentrated in the corn bran and germ, where they help neutralize free radicals in the body. The plant also features lutein and zeaxanthin, which are essential pigments known to support ocular health and protect against macular degeneration. Additionally, certain phytochemicals found in corn silk have been studied for their potential diuretic and hypoglycemic effects. These diverse chemical constituents contribute to the plant's growing importance in nutritional pharmacology.

What are the therapeutic actions of Zea mays?

Zea mays has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and diuretic properties that are primarily utilized through its silk and various grain extracts. The bioactive compounds found in the plant help reduce oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals within the body. Its diuretic effects assist in increasing urine production, which can help alleviate symptoms of urinary tract infections and edema. Additionally, the presence of specific flavonoids and phenolic acids contributes to its ability to mitigate systemic inflammation. These therapeutic actions make the plant a significant subject in both traditional medicine and modern nutritional studies.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including inflammation, urinary tract infections, and digestive issues through its medicinal properties. The kernels and silk can be processed into decoctions or teas to soothe gastrointestinal distress. Some traditional practices also utilize the plant to manage high blood pressure and reduce fever. While primarily known as a food staple, its bioactive compounds offer potential support for kidney health. These applications rely on the plant's natural antioxidant and diuretic qualities.
