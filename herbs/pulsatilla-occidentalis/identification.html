Botanical Identification of Pulsatilla (Pulsatilla occidentalis)

To identify Pulsatilla (Pulsatilla occidentalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Pulsatilla occidentalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Ranunculales, family Ranunculaceae, genus Pulsatilla, and species Pulsatilla occidentalis. These classifications place it within the broader group of flowering plants and the buttercup family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the plant is Pulsatilla occidentalis, a member of the Ranunculaceae family. It was first described by Carl von Linne in 1753. Synonyms include Anemone occidentalis and Pulsatilla vulgaris, though the latter is now considered invalid. Common names include Pasqueflower and Western Pasqueflower, with regional variations such as "Pulsatilla" in some parts of Europe.

Morphological Characteristics

Pulsatilla occidentalis is a herbaceous perennial with a short, stout root system. It has a single, upright stem that is hairy and usually less than 30 cm tall. The leaves are basal, alternate, and have a distinctive waxy coating. The plant produces a single, showy, yellow-centered flower with purple petals. The fruit is a follicle containing numerous small, dark seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Pulsatilla occidentalis can be distinguished by its hairy, blue-green stems and large, showy white or pale purple flowers with a yellow center. It has a distinctive, rounded, and often sticky fruit that sets it apart from similar species like Pulsatilla patens. The leaves are spoon-shaped and hairy, further aiding in identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Pulsatilla occidentalis is native to western North America, particularly the Pacific Northwest. It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and mild winters. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found at elevations between 1,000 and 3,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Pulsatilla occidentalis blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and June. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August. For identification purposes, harvest timing is best during the blooming period when the plant is most distinct and easily recognizable.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Pulsatilla occidentalis is most closely related to Pulsatilla patens and Pulsatilla vulgaris. It belongs to the genus Pulsatilla within the family Ranunculaceae. The genus shares traits like showy flowers and milky sap with other members of the family.
