Phaseolus coccineus

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Phaseolus coccineus has climbing or trailing stems that emerge from a taproot system and support trifoliate leaves with ovate to lanceolate leaflets. The foliage typically exhibits a deep green color and can vary in size depending on the specific cultivar. Its reproductive structures consist of bright red or orange tubular flowers that are characteristic of the Fabaceae family. Once pollinated, the plant produces elongated, flattened seed pods that contain multiple small, hard-coated seeds. These seeds often feature striking patterns or colors such as red, black, or mottled cream.

    What are the herbal preparations of Phaseolus coccineus?

    Phaseolus coccineus is prepared primarily as a decoction of the dried roots, a concentrated infusion of the leaves, or a topical poultice made from crushed fresh plant matter. These various preparations are utilized to extract specific bioactive compounds for different therapeutic purposes. For example, boiling the woody roots in water creates a potent liquid used to treat internal ailments. Alternatively, steeping the tender leaves in hot water allows for a gentler tea that may soothe digestive issues. The fresh leaves can also be mashed into a paste and applied directly to the skin to reduce inflammation or irritation.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Phaseolus coccineus can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, and abdominal cramping if the seeds are consumed before being properly cooked. These symptoms occur because the raw seeds contain high concentrations of lectins and phytates that are toxic to humans. To mitigate these risks, the plant must undergo thorough boiling or pressure cooking to neutralize the harmful proteins. Ingesting even small amounts of untreated seeds can lead to significant physical discomfort or more serious illness. Proper preparation is therefore essential to ensure the plant is safe for human consumption.
