Clematis nannophylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis nannophylla?

Clematis nannophylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Ranunculales. This plant is a member of the family Ranunculaceae, falling under the genus Clematis, and is specifically identified by its species name, nannophylla.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis nannophylla has opposite, trifoliate leaves characterized by small, ovate leaflets that are typically less than 3 centimeters in length.

The foliage is relatively sparse compared to other species in the genus, giving the plant a delicate appearance. Its woody stems act as climbing vines that use petioles to grasp surrounding structures for support. The flowers are small and often feature pale or cream-colored tepals that bloom during the warmer seasons.

This plant is specifically adapted to rocky or mountainous environments where its compact growth habit is advantageous.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Clematis nannophylla targets various inflammatory enzymes and signaling pathways, specifically inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and the nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) protein complex. These interactions are primarily mediated by bioactive triterpenoid saponins and flavonoids present within the plant's chemical profile. By suppressing the activation of the NF-κB pathway, the plant effectively reduces the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-alpha and IL-6. Additionally, the inhibition of COX-2 and iNOS enzymes prevents the synthesis of prostaglandins and nitric oxide, which are key mediators of pain and swelling. These molecular mechanisms collectively contribute to the plant's observed anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.

        What are the herbal preparations of Clematis nannophylla?

        Clematis nannophylla is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice derived from its leaves and stems. The plant's parts are often simmered in water to create a concentrated liquid extract used in traditional medicinal practices. These infusions can be consumed orally to harness the botanical properties contained within the plant's tissues. Additionally, the crushed material is frequently applied directly to the skin as a poultice for localized treatment. Such diverse preparation methods allow for the different ways the plant's chemical constituents interact with the body.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Clematis nannophylla can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of protoanemonin. Contact with the plant's sap often leads to contact dermatitis, manifesting as redness, itching, or blistering on the skin. If any part of the plant is swallowed, it may trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. These adverse reactions occur because the plant contains chemical compounds that act as irritants to mucous membranes and sensitive tissues. It is important to wear gloves when handling the stems to avoid these uncomfortable physical symptoms.
