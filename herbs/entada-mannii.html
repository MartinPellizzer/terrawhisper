Entada mannii

What's the taxonomical classification of Entada mannii?

Entada mannii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized as Equisetopsida, falling under the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is further organized into the order Fabales and is a member of the family Fabaceae. Within this botanical hierarchy, it is specifically assigned to the genus Entada, with its unique designation being the species mannii.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Entada mannii contains various bioactive phytochemicals including saponins, tannins, flavonoids, and alkaloids that contribute to its therapeutic properties. These secondary metabolites are primarily extracted from the roots and bark to treat diverse ailments in traditional medicine. Saponins within the plant are known for their potential anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activities. The presence of tannins provides astringent qualities that assist in wound healing and digestive regulation. Researchers continue to investigate these specific compounds to better understand their pharmacological potential in modern medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Entada mannii?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, stomach ailments, respiratory issues, and various types of physical pain. The various parts of the plant, including the roots and bark, are prepared into decoctions or pastes to address these conditions. For skin issues, topical applications are often employed to soothe irritations or infections. Additionally, the plant's medicinal properties are utilized to calm digestive distress and alleviate discomfort from coughs or colds. Local healers frequently rely on these traditional methods to manage a range of common illnesses.

What are the herbal preparations of Entada mannii?

Entada mannii is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a juice depending on the specific ailment being treated. The bark and roots are frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal infusion used for various systemic conditions. In some traditional practices, the dried plant material is ground into a fine powder to be mixed with water or other substances. The liquid extract or juice can also be applied topically or ingested to address specific health concerns. These diverse preparation methods allow different parts of the plant to be utilized effectively for therapeutic purposes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Entada mannii can cause unpredictable adverse reactions including gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, or allergic responses due to its complex chemical composition. While many parts of the plant are studied for medicinal properties, the specific toxicological profile for human consumption remains insufficiently documented in clinical literature. Potential side effects may include nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping if improper parts of the plant are ingested. There is also a risk of contact dermatitis when handling certain tissues without protective measures. Caution is necessary because the exact concentration of bioactive compounds can vary significantly between individual specimens.
