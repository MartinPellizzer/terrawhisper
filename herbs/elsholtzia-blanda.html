What's the taxonomical classification of Elsholtzia blanda?

Elsholtzia blanda belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is positioned in the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Lamiaceae and is specifically identified within the genus Elsholtzia, where it is designated as the species blanda.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Elsholtzia blanda has a deciduous tree structure characterized by pinnately compound leaves with 7 to 15 leaflets and small, inconspicuous greenish flowers. The leaflets are typically ovate or lanceolate with serrated margins and a smooth, somewhat leathery texture. Its bark matures into a grayish color with shallow longitudinal fissures as the tree ages. The fruit consists of small, winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal from the branches. During the autumn season, the foliage undergoes a distinct color change to shades of yellow or orange before falling.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apigenin, luteolin, luteolin glycoside, luteolin-7-galactoside(IV), and luteolin-3'-glucoside(III). These specific flavonoids are known for providing various antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the organism. The presence of luteolin-7-galactoside(IV) and luteolin-3'-glucoside(III) represents specialized glycosylated forms of the core luteolin structure. Such chemical diversity allows the plant to engage in complex biological interactions within medicinal applications. Each of these constituents contributes to the overall therapeutic profile of the species.

As discussed in a study, Elsholtzia blanda contains three flavonoid aglycones including luteolin, apigenin, and 5-hydroxy-6,7-dimethoxyflavone. The analysis of these compounds was conducted using a mobile phase of methanol-0.2% phosphoric acid at a 70:30 v/v ratio and a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min. Standard curves for these medicinal components were linear over concentration ranges of 8.54-85.4 microg/mL for luteolin, 1.2-12 microg/mL for apigenin, and 9.2-92 microg/mL for 5-hydroxy-6,7-dimethoxyflavone. Average recoveries for these substances were measured at 96.8%, 98.0%, and 100.5%, respectively. Optimal extraction through hydrolysis requires 3.0M HCl, 70% methanol, a temperature of 85 degrees C, and a time of 3 h.
