Senna siamea

What's the taxonomical classification of Senna siamea?

Senna siamea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Fabales. This plant is a member of the family Fabaceae, which places it among the legumes, and it is specifically identified within the genus Sennna under the species name siamea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Senna siamea has pinnately compound leaves consisting of numerous small, oblong leaflets arranged along a central rachis, bright yellow flowers with five petals, and elongated, cylindrical green to brown seed pods. The plant typically grows as a perennial shrub or small tree reaching heights of several meters. Each leaf features a distinct petiole and a terminal leaflet that helps define its lush, green appearance. The inflorescence consists of small clusters of flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. Once pollination occurs, the woody pods develop and eventually split to release small, dark seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, spanning across countries like India, Myanmar, and Thailand. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland habitats and can often be found growing along riverbanks or in seasonally flooded areas. Due to its adaptable nature, it has naturalized in several other tropical territories throughout the Indo-Pacific region. Its distribution is closely tied to warm climates with significant rainfall patterns. In some locations, it has even spread into parts of Africa and Oceania through human movement or environmental transport.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Senna siamea contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its dried leaves and pods which function as potent laxatives. These plant parts are harvested and processed to extract anthraquinone glycosides that stimulate intestinal contractions. The leaves are frequently used in traditional preparations to treat constipation and assist with bowel regularity. Additionally, the pods can be utilized in various herbal decoctions to address digestive ailments. Practitioners often rely on these specific components to manage gastrointestinal motility through controlled dosing.

What are the pharmacological activities of Senna siamea?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-malarial, anti-ulcer, antibacterial, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals, providing a strong antioxidant defense within biological systems. Research indicates that the plant's extracts possess potent antimicrobial and antibacterial qualities capable of inhibiting various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, its medicinal potential extends to treating gastrointestinal issues through its observed anti-ulcer effects. The specific chemical constituents also contribute to its efficacy in combating malaria parasites.

As discussed in a study, Senna siamea, also known as shekutoure, was used by 18% of herb users to treat symptoms of malaria. This research involved interviewing 810 randomly-sampled rural and urban adult residents of Bo, Sierra Leone, in December 2013 and January 2014. Among the participants, 55% reported taking one or more of seven herbs to treat suspected malaria. The study found that 37% of herb users utilized combination herbal therapy by taking two or more herbs together.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds cassiarin A, kaempferol, luteolin, naringenin, and quercetin. These specific phytochemicals are categorized as flavonoids and serve various biological roles within the organism. Each of these substances has been studied for its potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties in human health. The presence of such diverse compounds contributes to the overall therapeutic profile of the plant. Researchers continue to investigate how these chemical constituents interact to provide medicinal benefits.

As discussed in a study, Senna siamea leaves contain various phytochemical constituents including saponins, flavonoids, phenolics, steroids, and alkaloids. The plant contains 13.25 ± 0.03 mg GAE/g total phenolics and 3.99 ± 0.01 mg QE/g flavonoid contents. Specific medicinal compounds identified in the leaves are chlorogenic acid, p-coumaric acid, friedelin, quercetin, emodin, cassiarin A, and kaempferol. Antioxidant properties are demonstrated by IC50 values of 206.01 µg/mL for DPPH, 347.66 µg/mL for NO radicals, 394.92 µg/mL for TBARS, and 145.01 µg/mL for FRAP. Antimicrobial activity at 100 mg/mL showed average zones of inhibition of 18 and 16 mm for ethyl acetate and 15 and 12 mm for methanol, with minimum inhibitory concentrations of 25 and 50 mg/mL.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating malaria, skin diseases, and ulcers. The medicinal properties of the leaves are often applied topically to soothe various skin diseases. When prepared as a decoction, it can be administered to help manage symptoms associated with malaria. Additionally, certain extracts are utilized in traditional practices to address the discomfort of an ulcer. These applications demonstrate the diverse therapeutic potential found within the plant.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including abdominal cramps, abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Prolonged or excessive use often leads to dehydration and a dangerous electrolyte imbalance. A significant clinical concern is hypokalemia, which is a low level of potassium in the blood. In extreme cases of gastrointestinal bleeding, a person may experience melena.
