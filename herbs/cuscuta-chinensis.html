What to know about Cuscuta chinensis (dodder) before using it medicinally

Cuscuta chinensis, commonly known as dodder, is a herb that has been valued for its diverse health benefits, including its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties, which can improve cardiovascular health, boost the immune system, and support the treatment of various diseases.

From a horticultural perspective, this parasitic plant is known to thrive in a variety of environments, and can be found climbing on other plants in regions with temperate climates. Botanically, Cuscuta chinensis is a type of flowering plant that belongs to the Convolvulaceae family, characterized by its thread-like stems and small, yellow flowers.

Historically, this herb has been mentioned in traditional Chinese medicine, where it has been used for centuries to treat conditions such as fever, rheumatism, and respiratory disorders.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Cuscuta chinensis.

What are the medicinal properties of Cuscuta chinensis?

Cuscuta chinensis helps with various health conditions, including fever reduction, pain relief, and digestive issues. Its medicinal properties are attributed to its anti-inflammatory and antibacterial activities. It is also used to treat rheumatism and skin conditions.

The active constituents responsible for its medicinal properties are phenolic compounds, flavonoids, and saponins. These compounds exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities, which contribute to the plant's therapeutic effects. Research has shown that these compounds are responsible for the plant's anti-hyperlipidemic and anti-hyperglycemic activities.

The most commonly used parts of the plant for medicinal purposes are the seeds and leaves. The seeds are rich in phenolic compounds and are used to treat fever and rheumatism. The leaves are used to treat digestive issues and skin conditions. The roots and stems are also used, but to a lesser extent.

Improper use of Cuscuta chinensis can lead to side effects such as allergic reactions, stomach upset, and interact with other medications. Prolonged use can also cause liver and kidney damage. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using the plant due to its potential estrogenic effects.

When using Cuscuta chinensis medicinally, it is essential to follow proper precautions. Consume the plant in moderation, and avoid using it with other medications that may interact with its active constituents. Consult a healthcare professional before using the plant, especially if you have any underlying health conditions.

What are the horticulural aspects of Cuscuta chinensis?

Cuscuta chinensis grow well in temperate climates with mild winters and hot summers. They thrive in full sun to partial shade conditions, with optimal growth at 20-30°C (68-86°F) and relative humidity above 50%.

Planting tips for Cuscuta chinensis involve attaching the stems to host plants through small hooks or by cutting the host's stem to allow the dodder to twine. They require a large host plant with a sturdy stem to support their extensive growth.

Harvesting Cuscuta chinensis typically involves cutting the plant above the node where it attaches to the host, allowing for regrowth and multiple harvests. Harvest timing is usually during the morning, after the dew has evaporated, to prevent water loss.

Common pests and diseases affecting Cuscuta chinensis include aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites, as well as root rot and leaf spot caused by fungi such as Phoma and Colletotrichum. Regular monitoring and control measures can help mitigate these issues.
