What's the taxonomical classification of Dorstenia psilurus?

Dorstenia psilurus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the taxonomic hierarchy, it is positioned in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Rosales and falls within the family Moraceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Dorstenia and the species psilurus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dorstenia psilurus has a prostrate growth habit characterized by fleshy, creeping stems and relatively small, ovate to elliptical leaves that emerge from a central rhizome. The foliage typically displays a smooth texture with entire margins and can vary in color from light green to deeper shades depending on light exposure. Its most distinctive morphological feature is the specialized inflorescence, which consists of a flattened, disc-like receptacle known as a hypanthodium. This structure hosts minute, inconspicuous flowers embedded within the surface of the disc. The plant lacks traditional large petals, relying instead on these complex reproductive platforms to facilitate pollination.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the unique medicinal compounds dorsilurin C, dorsilurin F, dorsilurin K, the group known as dorsilurins F-K, and artelasticin. These specific chemical constituents are primarily isolated from the plant to study their potential biological activities. Researchers have focused on the structural diversity found within the dorsilurins F-K series to understand their chemical properties. Additionally, the presence of artelasticin provides another layer of complexity to the plant's pharmacological profile. Investigating these substances helps scientists identify new leads for drug development.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", six new unusual C-4'-prenylated flavonols, dorsilurins F-K (1-6), were isolated from the roots of Dorstenia psilurus. These six new compounds were found together with six known compounds from the same plant. The isolated compounds exhibited moderate to low alpha-glucosidase inhibitory activity. Dorsilurin F (1), which contains three unmodified prenyl groups, was the most active compound identified. In contrast, dorsilurin K (6), featuring only 1 unmodified prenyl group, was the least active. Additionally, NMR data for dorsilurin C (7), which was isolated from the plant some years ago, have been revised.
