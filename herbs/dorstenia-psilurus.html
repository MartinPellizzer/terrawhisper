What's the taxonomical classification of Dorstenia psilurus?

Dorstenia psilurus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the taxonomic hierarchy, it is positioned in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Rosales and falls within the family Moraceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Dorstenia and the species psilurus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dorstenia psilurus has a prostrate growth habit characterized by fleshy, creeping stems and relatively small, ovate to elliptical leaves that emerge from a central rhizome. The foliage typically displays a smooth texture with entire margins and can vary in color from light green to deeper shades depending on light exposure. Its most distinctive morphological feature is the specialized inflorescence, which consists of a flattened, disc-like receptacle known as a hypanthodium. This structure hosts minute, inconspicuous flowers embedded within the surface of the disc. The plant lacks traditional large petals, relying instead on these complex reproductive platforms to facilitate pollination.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds dorsilurin C, dorsilurin F, dorsilurin K, the group of dorsilurins F-K, and artelasticin. These specific chemical constituents are recognized for their unique biological properties and therapeutic potential. Researchers study these substances to understand how they interact with various cellular pathways in the human body. The presence of the dorsilurin series suggests a complex chemical profile within the plant's tissue. Furthermore, the inclusion of artelasticin adds to the diversity of the plant's bioactive profile.

As discussed in a study, six new unusual C-4'-prenylated flavonols named dorsilurins F-K (1-6) were isolated from the roots of Dorstenia psilurus. These six new compounds were identified alongside six known compounds through spectroscopic evidence. The isolated substances demonstrated moderate to low alpha-glucosidase inhibitory activity. Among the findings, dorsilurin F (1), which contains three unmodified prenyl groups, was identified as the most active. Conversely, dorsilurin K (6), featuring only one unmodified prenyl group, was the least active compound. Additionally, the NMR data for dorsilurin C (7), which was isolated from the same plant some years ago, underwent revision.
