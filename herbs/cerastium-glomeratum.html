Cerastium glomeratum

What's the taxonomical classification of Cerastium glomeratum?

Cerastium glomeratum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Caryophyllales and the family Caryophyllaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Cerastium, specifically as the species glomeratum.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cerastium glomeratum?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions and skin irritations such as eczema and psoriasis. It contains specific phytochemical properties that may help mitigate localized swelling and redness. Traditional applications often focus on soothing topical ailments through its natural chemical compounds. Some studies suggest its bioactive constituents may play a role in managing minor infections. Researchers continue to investigate its potential efficacy in broader dermatological therapies.

What are the herbal preparations of Cerastium glomeratum?

Cerastium glomeratum is prepared primarily as an aqueous decoction or an infusion using the dried aerial parts of the plant. Practitioners often utilize these liquid extractions to concentrate the plant's active phytochemical constituents for medicinal use. These preparations can also be processed into tinctures by using ethanol as a solvent to ensure higher potency and longer shelf life. Some traditional applications involve topical applications of plant poultices made from crushed fresh leaves. Each form is selected based on the specific therapeutic goal and the desired speed of absorption into the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cerastium glomeratum can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals upon direct contact with its tissues. While it is not classified as a highly toxic plant, some people may experience localized redness, itching, or a mild rash. These dermatological reactions typically occur when the plant's sap or physical structure touches the skin. There is currently very little scientific data regarding the internal effects of consuming this species. Therefore, any ingestion should be avoided to prevent unknown gastrointestinal distress or systemic complications.
