Eomecon chionantha

What's the taxonomical classification of Eomecon chionantha?

Eomecon chionantha belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant further falls under the order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Papaveraceae. Taxonomically, it is identified by the genus Eomecon, with its specific designation being the species chionantha.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eomecon chionantha has large, spreading, deeply lobed leaves that are typically 10 to 25 centimeters wide and characterized by a distinctively bluish-green or glaucous color. The plant produces showy, cup-shaped flowers that range in color from brilliant blue to deep purple, often featuring a bright yellow center. Each flower is supported by a sturdy, upright stem that can reach heights of up to 40 centimeters. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the isoquinoline alkaloids alpha-allocryptopine, chelerythrine, protopine, and sanguinarine. These specific medicinal compounds are often studied for their diverse biological activities and potential pharmacological applications. Sanguinarine and chelerythrine are particularly noted for their potent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Protopine contributes to the chemical profile by offering distinct sedative and antispasmodic effects. Together, these alkaloids form a complex chemical matrix that defines the plant's therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Eomecon chionantha collected from April to August in 1996 contain four alkaloids: sanguinarine, chelerythrine, protopine, and alpha-allocryptopine. These contents were determined by the capillary electrophoretic method. The results showed that the contents of sanguinarine and chelerythrine were lower in June than in the other months. Additionally, the contents of chelerythrine were higher than those of sanguinarine. The concentration of alpha-allocryptopine was found to be higher than protopine.
