Antennaria dioica

What's the taxonomical classification of Antennaria dioica?

Antennaria dioica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant falls under the order Asterales. It is further categorized into the family Asteraceae, which is characterized by its distinctive composite flower heads. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Anteennaria, and its specific identity is defined by the species name dioica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Antennaria dioica has a low-growing, prostrate growth habit characterized by basal rosettes of small, spoon-shaped leaves that are covered in fine, silvery-white hairs. These leaves are typically evergreen and form a dense mat close to the ground to conserve moisture. The plant is dioecious, meaning individual plants are either male or female, which influences the structure of the flowering stalks. Male plants produce small, inconspicuous flower heads on short stalks, while female plants produce larger, more prominent heads that develop into feathery, wind-dispersed seeds. The presence of dense pubescence across the foliage and stems provides a characteristic woolly appearance to the entire plant.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as harebell, thrives in well-drained, sandy, or rocky soils under full sun to partial shade within temperate climates.

It requires a period of winter dormancy to maintain its natural growth cycle and health. Because it is a slow-growing perennial, it is best cultivated in areas where it will not be disturbed by heavy foot traffic. Regular watering is necessary during dry spells, but the substrate must never remain waterlogged to prevent root rot.

Once established, it can spread via underground rhizomes to form small, silvery clumps.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Antennaria dioica presented moderate to high bioactivity during the investigation of twelve Norwegian medicinal plants. The study utilized accelerated solvent extraction to obtain dichloromethane and 80 % ethanol extracts for testing. These extracts were assessed for their ability to inhibit nitric oxide (NO) and TNF-α cytokine production. Furthermore, the research evaluated the protection of the intestinal epithelial barrier against damage induced by indomethacin using differentiated Cac0-2 cells. Antennaria dioica was identified as one of the plants showing these anti-inflammatory properties.
