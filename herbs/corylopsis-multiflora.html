Corylopsis multiflora

What's the taxonomical classification of Corylopsis multiflora?

Corylopsis multiflora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Saxifragales and is a member of the family Hamamelidaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Corylopsis, and its species designation, multiflora.

What are the therapeutic actions of Corylopsis multiflora?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, inflammation, and various digestive ailments. Its medicinal properties are often harnessed to soothe topical irritations and promote healing in wounded tissues. Some traditional practices also utilize extracts from the plant to alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort. The specific chemical compounds within the species contribute to its overall therapeutic potential. These applications are primarily documented in regional folk medicine traditions.

What are the herbal preparations of Corylopsis multiflora?

Corylopsis multiflora is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion made from its dried aerial parts to extract its bioactive compounds. The plant material is typically simmered in water to create a concentrated liquid medicine used in traditional applications. Some practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of a tincture by soaking the dried components in high-proof alcohol. This method allows for a more potent and shelf-stable concentration of the plant's medicinal properties. These various preparations serve as the standard methods for administering the herbal benefits of the species.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Corylopsis multiflora can cause localized skin irritation, redness, and itching upon direct contact with its foliage or sap. Individuals with sensitive skin or known botanical allergies may experience heightened dermatological reactions such as contact dermatitis. Some users might also report mild gastrointestinal distress if any part of the plant is ingested accidentally. It is important to handle the specimen with gloves to prevent any immediate inflammatory response. Always consult a medical professional if a rash or systemic reaction develops after exposure.
