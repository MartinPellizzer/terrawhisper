Aerva javanica

What's the taxonomical classification of Aerva javanica?

Aerva javanica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant is organized under the order Caryophyllales and placed within the family Amaranthaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Aerva and the species javanica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aerva javanica has an erect, herbaceous, and branched stem that reaches heights of up to 60 centimeters. The leaves are simple, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and feature entire margins with an acute apex. Small, white, five-petaled flowers are arranged in dense, terminal cymes. The fruit is a small, globose capsule that typically contains one to several seeds. These botanical characteristics distinguish it from other members of the Hydrophyllaceae family.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including alkaloids, flavonoids, gallic acid, rutin, and saponins. The presence of alkaloids provides significant pharmacological activity that can influence various physiological systems. Flavonoids such as rutin serve as potent antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress. Additionally, the inclusion of gallic acid offers strong anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Finally, the saponins within the plant contribute to its overall therapeutic profile by aiding in various biological processes.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical screening of the total ethanol extract of Aerva javanica aerial parts revealed the presence of alkaloids, flavonoids, tannins, sterols, and saponins. Within these compounds, the antioxidant flavonoid rutin was specifically identified. Testing on DCFH-injured cells showed that a dose of 100 µg/ml recovered cells to approximately 56.7%, while a 200 µg/ml dose resulted in hepatocyte recovery of approximately 90.2%. The extract also demonstrated strong free-radical scavenging ability in 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl and β-carotene assays. Oral administration of 100 and 200 mg/kg.bw/day significantly normalized various serum biochemical markers and tissue levels in injured rats.
