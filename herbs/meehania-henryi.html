Meehania henryi

What's the taxonomical classification of Meehania henryi?

Meehania henryi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Lamiales. It is a member of the family Lamiaceae, specifically falling within the genus Meehania, with its unique designation as the species henryi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Meehania henryi has an erect, perennial herbaceous habit characterized by oppositely arranged, ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins and a distinctly pubescent texture.

The plant typically reaches heights of up to 60 centimeters, featuring stems that are often covered in fine hairs. Its inflorescence consists of small, tubular, bilabiate flowers that are predominantly pale blue or lavender in color. These flowers are borne in dense clusters, often appearing in the leaf axils or at the terminal ends of the stems.

The reproductive structures are tucked within the corolla, reflecting its placement within the Lamiaceae family.

      What are the herbal preparations of Meehania henryi?

      Meehania henryi is prepared primarily as a decoction or a tea by boiling the dried leaves and stems in water to extract its bioactive compounds. This liquid extract is frequently consumed to address various ailments related to the respiratory and digestive systems. Some practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of a tincture, where the herb is macerated in alcohol for extended periods to create a more concentrated solution. Additionally, topical applications using poultices made from crushed fresh leaves are sometimes employed for skin-related issues. These diverse methods allow for different absorption rates and therapeutic benefits depending on the intended use.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Meehania henryi can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive upset if consumed or touched improperly. While often used in traditional medicine, the plant may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Some users report mild nausea or abdominal discomfort when ingesting preparations containing the herb. There is limited clinical data regarding long-term toxicity or systemic side effects in humans. Caution is advised when handling the plant to avoid localized rashes or sensitivities.
