Dioscorea hemsleyi

What's the taxonomical classification of Dioscorea hemsleyi?

Dioscorea hemsleyi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Dioscoreales. As a member of the family Dioscoreaceae, it is categorized under the genus Dioscorea, with its specific identification being the species hemsleyi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dioscorea hemsleyi has a tuberous rhizome with heart-shaped leaves that feature long petioles and a climbing, herbaceous stem structure. The foliage typically displays a distinct venation pattern on the leaf surface to aid in photosynthesis. Its climbing vines use specialized twining mechanisms to ascend surrounding vegetation in its natural habitat. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves during the growing season. The plant's overall morphology is adapted for life in shaded, forest environments where vertical growth is advantageous.

Its distribution is largely concentrated within the central and eastern mountainous territories of the country. What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating diabetic vascular complications, hindlimb ischemia, and type 2 diabetic hindlimb ischemia. Researchers have focused on its ability to improve blood flow and promote vessel regeneration in models of metabolic disease. It specifically targets the physiological damage caused by chronic high blood sugar levels in the extremities. By addressing these issues, the plant helps mitigate the severe risks associated with reduced circulation in diabetic patients. This medicinal application highlights its potential role in managing complex cardiovascular issues related to diabetes.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Dioscorea hemsleyi is one of the seven traditional medicinal plants included in the BuZangTongLuo formula (BZTLF). This formula is potentially effective against diabetic vascular complications. Specifically, the study investigated if the formula could prevent the occurrence of type 2 diabetic (T2D) hindlimb ischemia in mice. The treatment involved gavaging mice with BZTLF at a dosage of 5 g [raw materials]/kg/d for three weeks. The research demonstrated that BZTLF treatment remarkably restored blood flow and capillary density in models of diabetic hindlimb ischemia.
