Atriplex sibirica

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Atriplex sibirica has erect, perennial stems that reach heights of 30 to 100 centimeters and bear alternate, ovate-oblong leaves measuring 2 to 7 centimeters in length. The leaf margins are typically entire or slightly wavy, while the surfaces exhibit a distinct mealy or scurfy texture due to small bladder-like hairs. These leaves are often succulent in appearance and can range in color from bright green to a reddish hue. The inflorescence consists of small, inconspicuous flowers arranged in dense clusters along the upper parts of the stems. The plant produces small, triangular fruits that are characteristic of the Amaranthaceae family.

The plant contains bioactive secondary metabolites like flavonoids and phenolic acids that interfere with the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. What side-effects this plant can have?

Atriplex sibirica can cause gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, or potential toxicity if consumed in excessive amounts due to its high salt content. The plant is known for accumulating significant quantities of sodium from the soil, which can lead to electrolyte imbalances in sensitive individuals. Direct contact with the foliage may also trigger localized allergic reactions or dermatitis in some people. Because its chemical composition can vary based on the growing environment, the severity of these side effects is often unpredictable. It is generally advised to exercise caution and avoid large-scale ingestion without proper identification and preparation.
