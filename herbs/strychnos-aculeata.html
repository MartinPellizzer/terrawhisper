Strychnos aculeata

What's the taxonomical classification of Strychnos aculeata?

Strychnos aculeaata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Gentianales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Loganiaceae, specifically categorized under the genus Strychnos, with its unique designation being the species aculeaata.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Strychnos aculeata contains various bioactive alkaloids, most notably strychnine, brucine, and various emetine-type compounds. These secondary metabolites are primarily concentrated within the seeds and bark of the plant. The presence of these indole alkaloids provides the species with potent pharmacological properties, including neurotoxic and emetic effects. Researchers study these specific chemical constituents to understand their potential applications in developing central nervous system stimulants or anti-parasitic agents. However, the high toxicity of these compounds requires careful extraction and dosage control during medicinal research.

What are the therapeutic actions of Strychnos aculeata?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin conditions, digestive ailments, and inflammatory disorders. Local medicinal practitioners often utilize its bark and roots to manage wounds and skin infections. It is also frequently applied in traditional remedies to alleviate stomach pains and intestinal disturbances. Additionally, the plant's components are sometimes employed to reduce swelling and treat systemic inflammation. These applications vary significantly depending on the specific part of the plant used and the preparation method.

What are the herbal preparations of Strychnos aculeata?

Strychnos aculeata is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a leaf infusion, or a crushed pulp used for topical applications. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used in traditional medicinal practices. Leaves are often steeped in hot water to produce a tea intended for internal consumption. In certain regional practices, the plant material is pounded into a paste and applied directly to the skin for its local effects. These various forms allow different parts of the plant to be utilized according to specific therapeutic needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Strychnos aculeaata can cause severe neurotoxicity, muscular convulsions, and respiratory failure due to the presence of potent alkaloids like strychnine. If ingested, these toxic compounds act on the spinal cord to induce violent, uncontrolled muscle spasms. These contractions can be so intense that they lead to physical injury or permanent muscle damage. As the nervous system becomes overwhelmed, the ability to breathe effectively diminishes significantly. In extreme cases, the resulting asphyxiation can lead to death.
