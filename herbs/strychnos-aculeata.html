Strychnos aculeata

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Strychnos aculeata has woody climbing vines or lianas characterized by simple, alternate, and elliptical leaves with entire margins and prominent pinnate venation. The stems are typically robust and often feature small prickles or scars from fallen branchlets. Its flowers are small, inconspicuous, and greenish-white, appearing in axillary clusters. The plant produces hard, woody, globose fruits that contain several large, dark brown seeds. These seeds are often surrounded by a fleshy aril which attracts various forest fauna.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of sub-Saharan Africa, ranging from Senegal in the west to Ethiopia in the east and extending south to South Africa. It is commonly found inhabiting lowland forests, riverine thickets, and woodland areas across much of the continent. The species thrives in humid environments where seasonal rainfall supports dense vegetation. Its distribution is primarily tied to the presence of tropical forest ecosystems and moist coastal belts. Because it prefers warm climates, it remains absent from the arid deserts and high-altitude mountain ranges of Africa.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Strychnos aculeata contains various bioactive alkaloids, most notably strychnine, brucine, and various emetine-type compounds. These secondary metabolites are primarily concentrated within the seeds and bark of the plant. The presence of these indole alkaloids provides the species with potent pharmacological properties, including neurotoxic and emetic effects. Researchers study these specific chemical constituents to understand their potential applications in developing central nervous system stimulants or anti-parasitic agents. However, the high toxicity of these compounds requires careful extraction and dosage control during medicinal research.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Strychnos aculeata targets voltage-gated ion channels and specific neurotransmitter receptors, primarily through the action of indole alkaloids like strychnine and related compounds. These alkaloids act as potent antagonists at glycine receptors within the spinal cord and brainstem, which disrupts inhibitory neurotransmission. By blocking these receptors, the plant's compounds can induce excitatory effects that influence neuromuscular signaling. Some secondary metabolites also show potential interaction with acetylcholinesterase enzymes, which regulates the availability of acetylcholine at synaptic clefts. These diverse molecular interactions contribute to the plant's complex pharmacological profile used in traditional medicinal practices.

It can also function as an emetic to induce vomiting when clearing the digestive tract is necessary. Additionally, the plant exhibits antipyretic properties that help in reducing elevated body temperatures during a fever.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause severe poisoning characterized by nausea, vomiting, dizziness, and intense muscle spasms. As the toxicity progresses, patients may experience extreme muscle rigidity and violent convulsions. These physical reactions often escalate into life-threatening seizures. Ultimately, the neurological impact can lead to fatal respiratory depression.
