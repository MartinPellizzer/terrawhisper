Peregrine Marrubium

Marrubium peregrinum

Peregrine Marrubium (Marrubium peregrinum) is a medicinal plant native to certain regions of Europe, known for its historical use in treating respiratory conditions such as coughs and bronchitis.

Peregrine Marrubium (Marrubium peregrinum) is a species belonging to the genus Marrubium in the family Fabaceae, classified within the order Fabales, class Fabales, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae.

Morphology

Peregrine Marrubium (Marrubium peregrinum) is a herbaceous perennial plant characterized by its erect, branched stems, lanceolate leaves with rough, hairy surfaces, and clusters of small, blue to purple flowers that bloom in late spring to early summer.

Distribution

Peregrine Marrubium (Marrubium peregrinum) is native to the mountainous regions of southern Europe, including parts of Spain, Italy, and the Balkans, and is also found in some isolated regions of North Africa.

Traditional Uses

Peregrine Marrubium (Marrubium peregrinum) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its antispasmodic, expectorant, and mild sedative properties, primarily to treat respiratory conditions such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma, as well as to alleviate digestive spasms and promote relaxation.

Medicinal Systems

In traditional medicinal systems such as Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, and Native American herbal practices, Peregrine Marrubium has been historically used for its purported therapeutic properties.

Phytochemistry

The phytochemistry of Peregrine Marrubium (Marrubium peregrinum) is characterized by the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, saponins, and mucilage, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Pharmacology

Peregrine Marrubium (Marrubium peregrinum) exhibits pharmacological properties characterized by antispasmodic, anti-inflammatory, and mild sedative effects, primarily due to its ability to modulate smooth muscle activity and reduce nerve excitability.
