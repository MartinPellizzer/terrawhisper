Ficus retusa

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus retusa?

Ficus retusa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Moraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Ficus, and its unique species designation, retusa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus retusa has elliptical or obovate leaves that are thick, leathery, and glossy with entire margins and a blunt or rounded apex. The foliage typically presents a deep green color on the upper surface and often features a lighter green underside. These leaves are arranged in an alternate pattern along woody, branching stems that can become quite gnarled with age. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced within specialized structures called syconia, which are technically fleshy inflorescences. The plant's root system is highly adaptable, often developing aerial roots that can descend from branches to anchor the tree.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, spanning from India and Sri Lanka through much of Indochina to Indonesia and the Philippines. It thrives primarily in humid, low-altitude environments where temperatures remain consistently warm. Because it is highly adaptable to various soil types and moisture levels, it has been widely introduced to other tropical zones around the world. Many specimens can now be found in ornamental gardens and urban landscapes across Africa and the Pacific Islands. Its ability to tolerate diverse climates has made it a common sight in botanical collections globally.

      This plant can cause skin irritation or contact dermatitis, eye irritation, and gastrointestinal distress if its sap or parts are mishandled. The milky white latex contained within the leaves and stems is the primary source of these adverse reactions. When the sap touches the skin, it may lead to redness, itching, or a painful rash. Furthermore, accidental ingestion of any part of the plant can trigger gastrointestinal distress, including nausea or vomiting.
