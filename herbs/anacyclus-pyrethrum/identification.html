Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Pyrethrum is Anacyclus pyrethrum, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Pyrethrum vulgare and Helenium pyrethrum. Common names such as pyrethrum flower and golden marigold reflect its historical use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Pyrethrum (Anacyclus pyrethrum) is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and can grow up to 30 cm tall. The leaves are basal, alternate, and have a hairy, lobed appearance. The inflorescence consists of a dense cluster of yellow flowers. Each flower has five petals and produces a small, dry fruit containing several seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Pyrethrum (Anacyclus pyrethrum) can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow flowers with a purple eye, basal rosette of gray-green leaves, and hairy stems. It differs from similar species like Anthemis by having fewer flower heads and a more compact growth habit. The leaves are also more elongated and less lobed compared to related plants.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Anacyclus pyrethrum is native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters and warm summers. It prefers well-drained soils and is typically found at altitudes between 500 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Pyrethrum (Anacyclus pyrethrum) typically blooms from late spring to early summer, producing yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested when the flowers are fully open and the seed heads are developing, usually around mid to late summer.
