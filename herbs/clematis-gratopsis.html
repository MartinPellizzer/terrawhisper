Clematis gratopsis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis gratopsis has opposite, trifoliate leaves characterized by ovate to lanceolate leaflets with entire or slightly wavy margins and a dull green surface. The stems are typically slender and woody, often featuring fine hairs that aid in climbing through surrounding vegetation. Its flowers are actinomorphic and solitary, presenting four to five petaloid sepals that range in color from pale cream to yellowish-white. The reproductive structures consist of numerous central stamens and pistils that later develop into feathery, achene-bearing styles. These specialized seed heads facilitate wind dispersal once the plant reaches maturity.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Clematis gratopsis can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential neurological symptoms if ingested or handled improperly. The plant contains protoanemonin, a chemical compound that triggers contact dermatitis when the sap touches human skin. This reaction typically manifests as redness, swelling, or blistering at the site of contact. If any part of the plant is swallowed, it may induce vomiting, abdominal pain, and inflammation of the digestive tract. In some cases, high doses of the toxins found in this species may lead to more serious systemic effects.
