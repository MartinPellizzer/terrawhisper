Aerangis biloba

What's the taxonomical classification of Aerangis biloba?

Aerangis biloba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic progression continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, eventually placing it in the order Asparagales. As a member of the family Orchidaceae, this plant is situated within the genus Aerangiis, with its specific identity defined by the species name biloba.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Aerangis biloba targets cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha to exert its medicinal effects. By inhibiting the cyclooxygenase-2 enzyme, the plant effectively reduces the synthesis of prostaglandins that drive pain and inflammation. Its interaction with nitric oxide synthase helps regulate vascular tone and mitigates oxidative stress within cellular environments. Additionally, the modulation of cytokine signaling pathways assists in dampening systemic inflammatory responses. These multi-protein interactions collectively contribute to the plant's potential as an anti-inflammatory and analgesic agent.

    Aerangis biloba has no documented therapeutic actions or medicinal uses in human or veterinary pharmacology.     What are the herbal preparations of Aerangis biloba?

    Aerangis biloba is prepared as a concentrated decoction, a fine powdered extract, or a topical infused oil to utilize its active compounds. The decoction involves simmering the dried root in water for an extended period to extract soluble minerals. For oral consumption, the powdered form is often encapsulated or stirred into warm liquids to ensure consistent dosage. The infused oil is created by steeping the plant material in a carrier oil to facilitate transdermal absorption. These various methods allow for different rates of bioavailability depending on the intended therapeutic use.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Aerangis biloba can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if the plant material is ingested or touched without protection. While specific toxicological data is limited, many orchid species contain alkaloids or organic compounds that may trigger hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. Contact with the sap or leaves can result in redness, itching, or localized swelling on the skin. If swallowed, the plant might lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain. It is generally recommended to wear gloves when handling the specimen to prevent these adverse reactions.
