Scientific and Botanical Profile

Chlorophytum Borivilianum, with botanical name chlorophytum borivilianum, is a member of the Asparagaceae family, commonly known as Shatavari, Kewra Plant, Asparagus Fern, Kewra Grass, Brahma Kamal, Blue Vervain, Borivalia Grass, Kanakakamal, Kasai Grass, Borivalia Nutgrass, Indian Vervain, Asparagus Racemosus, Blue False Indigo, Common Asparagus Fern, Borivilla Nutgrass, Turmeric Grass, Indian Asparagus, Indian Wheatear, and Asparagus Setaceus.

Native to regions such as India, Nepal, Bhutan, Pakistan, the Himalayan Regions, Eastern Himalayas, Western India, Tibet, and Southern Nepal, this plant is widely distributed across these areas. Morphologically, it is a perennial herb with slender, wiry stems and linear, grass-like leaves that are often blue-green in color, giving it a distinctive appearance.

It produces small, inconspicuous white or greenish flowers, followed by small, berry-like fruits, and is valued for its medicinal properties in traditional systems of medicine.

History and Cultural Relevance

Its enduring presence in both historical and contemporary contexts underscores its deep-rooted cultural relevance and therapeutic value across generations.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Chlorophytum borivilianum can be considered relatively safe when used as directed, but it is important to be aware of its potential side effects, which may include gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, and in some cases, interactions with other medications.

It is generally not recommended for use during pregnancy or breastfeeding due to limited research on its effects on fetal development and infant health. Individuals with chronic illnesses, such as diabetes or hypertension, should consult a healthcare provider before use, as it may interact with medications or affect existing conditions. To ensure safe use, always follow recommended dosages, consult with a healthcare professional, and avoid combining it with other herbs or medications without guidance.

Additionally, individuals with known allergies to plants in the Asparagaceae family should exercise caution when using chlorophytum borivilianum.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Chlorophytum borivilianum grows best in well-drained loamy soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5, in a sunny to partially shaded location with adequate air circulation.

It requires regular watering to keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged, especially during the growing season, while allowing the soil to dry slightly between waterings. To maintain healthy growth, it is important to fertilize with a balanced, slow-release fertilizer every few months and to prune any dead or diseased foliage to promote new growth. Harvesting is best done during the late summer to early autumn when the plant has matured and the roots are most potent, using a spade to carefully dig up the entire plant, ensuring minimal damage to the root system.

After harvesting, the roots should be washed, sliced, and dried in a cool, shaded area or using a dehydrator to preserve their potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties.
