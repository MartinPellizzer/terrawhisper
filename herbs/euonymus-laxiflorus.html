Euonymus laxiflorus has opposite, simple leaves that are typically elliptic to ovate in shape with finely serrated margins and acute tips. Following pollination, the species produces small, globose capsules that eventually split open to reveal brightly colored seeds.       Euonymus laxiflorus is prepared primarily as a decoction made by boiling the dried leaves or stems in water to extract its active chemical constituents.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Euonymus laxiflorus can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of alkaloids and glycosides. Direct contact with the plant's leaves or stems may trigger contact dermatitis, characterized by redness, itching, or localized swelling. If any part of the plant is consumed, individuals might experience nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, or diarrhea. The specific chemical compounds within the species can affect the central nervous system if high doses are absorbed. Because of these risks, careful avoidance of ingestion and skin contact is necessary when handling this plant.
