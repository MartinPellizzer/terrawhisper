What's the taxonomical classification of Phaius delavayi?

Phaius delaavayi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Asparagales, it is further categorized into the family Orchidaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by the genus Phaius and is specifically designated as the species delaavayi.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

The plant contains bioactive compounds like phenanthrenes and flavonoids that modulate these protein pathways to reduce oxidative stress. By inhibiting the cyclooxygenase pathway, its constituents help decrease the production of prostaglandins responsible for pain and inflammation. This multi-target approach allows the plant to demonstrate significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities in biological systems.

      What are the herbal preparations of Phaius delavayi?

      Phaius delavayi is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a topical paste depending on the specific traditional medicinal application required. The rhizomes are most frequently dried and ground into a fine powder to be mixed with water or other liquids for oral consumption. When used to treat skin irritations, the plant material is crushed into a fresh paste to be applied directly to the affected area. Some practitioners also utilize a concentrated decoction made by boiling the plant parts to extract active compounds for therapeutic use. These various forms allow for the versatile administration of the plant's properties in different healing contexts.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Phaius delavayi can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive distress if ingested or handled improperly. While primarily grown as an ornamental plant, its sap or contact with its tissues may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Ingesting parts of the plant could lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramps due to unknown toxic compounds. There is limited clinical research regarding its systemic toxicity, so caution is necessary when using it in any medicinal or culinary context. Always wear gloves when handling the plant to prevent potential dermatological issues.
