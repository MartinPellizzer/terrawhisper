Aquilegia vulgaris

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aquilegia vulgaris has compound, lobed leaves that are divided into three leaflets, each further subdivided into smaller, delicate segments. The stems are typically erect and can reach heights of up to one meter in length. Its most distinctive feature is the spurred, bell-shaped flower that displays a range of colors from deep blue to violet with prominent yellow centers. These complex blossoms are held aloft on slender pedicels above the foliage. The plant's overall structure is characterized by a graceful, airy appearance due to its fine-textured leaves and pendulous blooms.

    What are the herbal preparations of Aquilegia vulgaris?

    Aquilegia vulgaris is prepared as a tincture, an infusion, or a decoction using the aerial parts of the plant. The dried flowering tops are most commonly used to create alcoholic tinctures for long-term storage and concentrated dosing. Infusions made from the leaves and flowers are often utilized in tea form for immediate consumption. Decoctions may also be prepared by boiling the tougher plant materials to extract specific chemical constituents. These various liquid preparations allow for different absorption rates and therapeutic applications in traditional herbalism.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Aquilegia vulgaris can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested due to the presence of protoanemonin. Contact with the plant's sap often leads to redness, itching, or blistering on sensitive skin areas. When swallowed, the toxins may induce symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Some individuals might also experience more severe systemic reactions depending on the quantity consumed. Caution is necessary as these effects can vary significantly between different people.
