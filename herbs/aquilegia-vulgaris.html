Aquilegia vulgaris

What's the taxonomical classification of Aquilegia vulgaris?

Aquilegia vulgaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its evolutionary path continues through the order Ranunculales and the family Ranunculaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by its specific genus, Aquilegia, and its species designation, vulgaris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aquilegia vulgaris has compound, lobed leaves that are divided into three leaflets, each further subdivided into smaller, delicate segments. The stems are typically erect and can reach heights of up to one meter in length. Its most distinctive feature is the spurred, bell-shaped flower that displays a range of colors from deep blue to violet with prominent yellow centers. These complex blossoms are held aloft on slender pedicels above the foliage. The plant's overall structure is characterized by a graceful, airy appearance due to its fine-textured leaves and pendulous blooms.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

While its core distribution remains centered in the European continent, it can occasionally be found in localized pockets of North America due to human introduction. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe physiological distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, dizziness, confusion, hallucinations, seizures, convulsions, tachycardia, and respiratory depression. Ingesting the toxic alkaloids found in the species can trigger significant gastrointestinal upset and neurological instability. The central nervous system may react with intense disorientation or visual disturbances that escalate into violent physical movements. Cardiovascular symptoms like an abnormally rapid heart rate often accompany these neurological shifts. Ultimately, the most life-threatening consequences involve a dangerous slowing of breathing and loss of consciousness.
