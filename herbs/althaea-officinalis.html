What to know about marshmallow before using it medicinally

Marshmallow, scientifically known as Althaea officinalis, is a herb that has been used for centuries for its numerous health benefits.

It contains mucilage, a substance that becomes gel-like when mixed with water, which can help soothe and protect irritated tissues in the digestive tract, respiratory system, and urinary tract. In cooking, marshmallow is mainly used as a thickening agent and natural sweetener in desserts, beverages, and candies. It is a hardy perennial that grows well in full sun and moist, well-drained soil.

The plant can reach up to 4 feet tall and has soft, velvety leaves and pinkish-white flowers. Botanically, marshmallow is a member of the Malvaceae family, which also includes hibiscus and okra. Historically, marshmallow has been used in traditional medicine in many cultures, including ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome.

It was used to treat a variety of ailments, such as sore throats, coughs, and skin irritations.

What are the side effects of marshmallow when used improperly?

When used improperly, marshmallow increases the chances of experiencing side effects, such as digestive discomfort, allergic reactions, and potential drug interactions.

Overuse of marshmallow in medicinal forms, such as teas, powders, or capsules, can lead to bloating, diarrhea, and stomach pain due to its high mucilage content, which can interfere with the normal digestion process. Additionally, people who are allergic to marshmallow or other members of the Malvaceae family may experience symptoms like skin rashes, hives, or difficulty breathing. Moreover, marshmallow can interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners, diabetes drugs, and medications that need acidic environments to be absorbed, potentially reducing their effectiveness or increasing the risk of adverse effects.

It is crucial to consult with a healthcare professional before incorporating marshmallow into a medicinal routine to ensure safe and appropriate use.
