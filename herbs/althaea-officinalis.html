Althaea officinalis

What's the taxonomical classification of Althaea officinalis?

Althaea officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Malvales, it is placed in the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Althaea, and its specific species designation, officinalis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Althaea officinalis has a perennial, rhizomatous rootstock and erect, woody stems that can reach heights of up to 1.5 meters. The leaves are alternate, heart-shaped to ovate, and possess long petioles with crenate margins and a soft, pubescent texture. Its flowers are pale pink to white, featuring five petals and a prominent central column of stamens that characterizes the Malvaceae family. The plant is notable for its dense covering of fine, silky hairs across its entire surface, which provides a velvety feel. Each flowering season produces numerous small, kidney-shaped seed capsules that eventually release dark, brownish seeds.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Althaea officinalis contains high concentrations of mucilage in its roots and leaves that are utilized for their soothing medicinal properties. The dried roots are most commonly harvested to create syrups, decoctions, and teas used to treat respiratory irritation and sore throats. These plant parts work by coating the mucous membranes, which helps alleviate coughing and inflammation. The leaves can also be used in topical applications to soothe skin irritations or minor wounds. Overall, the plant's ability to provide a protective layer makes its various parts essential in traditional herbal remedies.

What are the pharmacological activities of Althaea officinalis?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antitussive, expectorant, soothing, and wound healing pharmacological activities. Its mucilage content provides a protective layer that works to soothe the oral and throat mucosa during irritation. The plant acts as an antitussive to suppress coughing and functions as an expectorant to help clear mucus from the airways. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory properties help reduce swelling and pain in various tissues. These diverse medicinal qualities also support the wound healing process by promoting tissue repair.

As discussed in a study, the root extract of Althaeaa officiinaliis L. exerts anti-inflammatory and anti-oxidative properties and improves the migratory capacity of endothelial cells in vitro. When human umbilical vein endothelial cells are pretreated for 24 h with the root extract, it inhibits the release of IL6 activated by lipopolysaccharides after 3 h of incubation. The root extract also inhibits cytotoxicity and intracellular reactive oxygen species production induced by hydrogen peroxide after 1 h. Furthermore, the root extract and Phytohustil significantly stimulate wound closure compared to the control. These pharmacological activities may contribute to the healing characteristics of the plant extract in the treatment of irritated oral mucosa.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a complex profile of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, alkaloids, caffeic acid, chicoric acid, and coumarins. These bioactive substances contribute to the plant's diverse therapeutic properties and biological activities. The presence of flavonoids and alkaloids often provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects within the body. Additionally, caffeic acid and chicoric acid serve as potent phenolic compounds that support cellular health. Finally, the inclusion of coumarins adds to the chemical complexity and potential medicinal utility of the plant's extract.

As discussed in a study, three colors of petals of Althaea officinalis flowers, which include pink, reddish pink, and white, were examined for their medicinal potential. The reddish pink flowers of A. officinalis demonstrated more antioxidant activity than the pink and white flowers. Specifically, the power of antioxidant activity followed the order of reddish pink > pink > white. In contrast, the flavonoid content was found to be highest in the white flower. These findings suggest that the dark color can serve as an indicator of antioxidant content, while light color flowers may be considered for medicinal uses due to flavonoid levels.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating respiratory disorders, sore throat, dry cough, and inflamed buccal mucosal membranes. Its mucilaginous properties provide a soothing coating that helps alleviate the irritation associated with a persistent dry cough. This soothing effect is particularly beneficial for calming an aching sore throat during illness. Furthermore, the plant helps protect and heal inflamed buccal mucosal membranes within the mouth and throat. These combined actions make it a common remedy for various minor respiratory disorders.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion, decoction, tincture, syrup, or mucilage to utilize its soothing properties. The roots are commonly boiled in water to create a decoction or steeped in hot water as an infusion to extract beneficial compounds. Because the plant is rich in mucilage, these liquid preparations provide a protective coating for irritated mucous membranes. A tincture made with alcohol provides a more concentrated dose for long-term storage. Additionally, the mucilage can be turned into a sweet syrup to make the medicinal taste more palatable for patients.
