Botanical Identification of Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba)

To identify Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Ginkgo biloba belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Ginkgophyta, class Ginkgoopsida, order Ginkgoales, family Ginkgoaceae, genus Ginkgo, and species Ginkgo biloba. These classifications highlight its unique position as a gymnosperm with ancient evolutionary origins.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Ginkgo is Ginkgo biloba, first described by Linnaeus in 1771. It belongs to the family Ginkgoaceae and is the only living species in its genus. Common names include maidenhair tree and Chinese silver apricot. It is also known as the "living fossil" due to its ancient lineage.

Morphological Characteristics

Ginkgo biloba is a deciduous tree with a conical shape when young, becoming broad and rounded with age. Its roots are taproots with lateral branches, providing strong anchorage. The stem is smooth, straight, and often fissured, with a distinctive crown. Leaves are fan-shaped, bipinnate, and have a unique dichotomous venation pattern. Inflorescences are terminal, with male flowers producing two pollen sacs and female flowers having a single ovule. The fruit is a pendulous, fleshy sphere that turns yellowish when mature, enclosing a hard, edible seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Ginkgo biloba can be distinguished by its fan-shaped leaves with a distinct dichotomy, smooth bark, and unique fan-like fruits. It is often mistaken for other gymnosperms but lacks the needle-like leaves of pines and the scale-like leaves of cycads. Its distinct odor and leaf shape help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Ginkgo biloba is native to eastern Asia, particularly China. It thrives in temperate climates with cold winters and warm summers. It prefers well-drained, slightly acidic to neutral soils. It typically grows at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Ginkgo biloba blooms in early spring with yellowish-green catkins. Seed formation occurs in autumn, with mature seeds developing by late summer. For identification purposes, harvest timing is typically in late summer to early autumn when seeds are fully mature and ready for collection.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Ginkgo biloba is most closely related to the genus Ginkgo, which is monotypic, containing only this species. It belongs to the family Ginkgoaceae, distinguished by its unique fan-shaped leaves and naked seeds. The family exhibits distinct traits such as a single cotyledon and a short, conical trunk, setting it apart from other gymnosperms.
