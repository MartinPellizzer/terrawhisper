Guava

Psidium guajava

Ashwagandha is a prominent medicinal herb in Ayurvedic medicine, classified as a rasayana (rejuvenator). It is primarily utilized as an adaptogen to help the body manage physical and chemical stress.

Family
Solanaceae (Nightshade)

Native Region
India, Middle East, Africa

Part Used
Root, Leaf, Berry

Botanical Identification

Guava (Psidium guajava) is a species in the Myrtaceae family. Common names include "guayaba" in Spanish and "kumara" in some South Asian regions. It is a tropical evergreen tree native to the Americas. The plant has smooth bark, thick leaves, and fragrant white flowers. It is also known as "java apple" in some herbal texts.

Active Compounds

Guava contains key bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and phenolic acids. These compounds work synergistically to provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Their combined action enhances the herb's therapeutic potential in herbal medicine.

  • Flavonoid
  • Tannin
  • Phenolic acid

Therapeutic Indications

System Condidtion Action
Respiratory Cough, bad breath, oral infections Expectorant, antimicrobial, immunostimulant, antiviral
Integumentary Skin infections, wound healing, wounds Antimicrobial, antioxidant, antifungal
Digestive Diarrhea, gastrointestinal infections, gastrointestinal discomfort, stomach upset, gastritis Antimicrobial, antispasmodic, anti-inflammatory

Preparation Methods

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and respiratory ailments.

Infusion: Used for colds and flu due to its antiviral properties.

Extract: Used for managing diabetes and inflammation.

Safety Profile

Guava is generally safe when consumed in typical food amounts. However, it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals. It is contraindicated for those with known allergies to the fruit or its derivatives. Always consult a healthcare provider if you have existing health conditions or are taking medications.
