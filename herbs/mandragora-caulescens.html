Mandragora caulescens

What's the taxonomical classification of Mandragora caulescens?

Mandragora caulescens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Solanales and is a member of the family Solanaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by its classification within the genus Mandragora, with the specific epithet cauleescens.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Mandragora caulescens contains high concentrations of tropane alkaloids, specifically hyoscyamine, scopolamine, and atropine, within its roots and leaves. These chemical compounds act as potent anticholinergic agents that affect the central and peripheral nervous systems. Historically, these substances were utilized in small doses to induce sedation, alleviate pain, or serve as anesthetics during surgical procedures. However, the narrow therapeutic window of these alkaloids makes them extremely dangerous, as slight increases in dosage can cause severe hallucinations, tachycardia, and respiratory failure. Modern pharmacological study focuses on isolating these specific molecules for controlled medical applications.

What are the therapeutic actions of Mandragora caulescens?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat insomnia, severe pain, and various neurological disorders through its potent sedative and analgesic properties. The presence of alkaloids like scopolamine and hyoscyamine allows it to function as a powerful anesthetic in historical medicinal practices. It was frequently administered to induce sleep or to calm patients suffering from extreme agitation and mania. Because of its high toxicity, it was often used in very small, carefully controlled doses to manage intense physical suffering. Modern pharmacology recognizes these chemical compounds as precursors to many contemporary treatments for similar symptoms.
