Pastinaca sativa

What's the taxonomical classification of Pastinaca sativa?

Pastinaca sativa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Apiales and into the family Apiaceae, which is characteristic of many flowering plants with umbrella-like inflorescences. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Pastinaca, with its specific designation being the species sativa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pastinaca sativa has a stout, fleshy taproot and a basal rosette of pinnately compound leaves that feature serrated leaflets. The stems are hollow, branched, and can reach heights of up to two meters in late summer. Small, yellowish-green flowers grow in dense, flat-topped compound umbels characteristic of the Apiaceae family. As the plant matures, it produces numerous small, oblong seeds that are easily dispersed by wind or water. The overall structure transitions from a compact low-growing herb to a tall, spreading biennial.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa, but it has become naturalized across much of North America and other temperate regions worldwide. It spreads rapidly through disturbed soils and roadside habitats where sunlight is abundant. Many populations are found in agricultural fields and along riverbanks in the United States and Canada. Its ability to thrive in diverse climates allows it to expand into various temperate ecosystems across the globe. The seeds are often transported by water or human activity, facilitating its wide geographic reach.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Pastinaca sativa contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its taproot, which is the most frequently utilized part of the plant for therapeutic purposes. The root is harvested for its high concentration of coumarins and essential oils that possess various biological activities. While the seeds and foliage also contain chemical constituents, they are less commonly used in traditional herbal preparations due to their potential toxicity. Some historical applications involved using the root to stimulate digestive processes or as a topical agent. However, caution is necessary because contact with the plant's juices can cause photosensitivity in the skin.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a complex profile of medicinal compounds including coumarins, furanocoumarins, isopimpinellin, polyacetylenes, and essential oils. The presence of furanocoumarins is particularly significant because these substances can cause photosensitivity in the skin upon exposure to sunlight. Isopimpinellin serves as a specific type of coumarin found within the plant's chemical structure. Additionally, the polyacetylenes and essential oils contribute to the unique bioactive properties of the species. These diverse chemical constituents determine the plant's pharmacological interactions and potential toxicity.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Pastinaca sativa targets various enzymes and receptors through its primary bioactive constituents, specifically furanocoumarins like xanthotoxin and bergapten, which modulate cytochrome P450 monooxygenases in the liver. These compounds exert significant influence on the metabolic pathways of drugs by inhibiting specific CYP450 isoenzymes, thereby altering the rate of drug oxidation and clearance. Additionally, certain phytochemicals in the plant interact with cellular signaling proteins and can influence calcium channels to affect muscle contraction or nerve impulse transmission. The presence of various polyphenols also allows for the modulation of inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase and lipoxygenase enzymes. These multifaceted interactions with protein targets contribute to the plant's complex pharmacological profile and potential therapeutic applications.

What are the therapeutic actions of Pastinaca sativa?

Pastinaca sativa has therapeutic actions that include being a bitter, carminative, stomachic, diaphoric, expectorant, and alterative. Its bitter properties stimulate the appetite and prepare the digestive system for food. As a carminative and stomachic, it helps relieve intestinal gas and improves overall gastric function. The plant also acts as a diaphoretic to promote perspiration and an expectorant to help clear mucus from the respiratory tract. Finally, its role as an alterative helps to gradually restore healthy metabolic processes within the body.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe phototoxicity, skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea in humans. If the sap touches the skin and is subsequently exposed to sunlight, it can trigger painful blistering or burning sensations. This inflammatory reaction is known as phytophotodermatitis and may leave long-lasting marks. Additionally, ingestion of the plant parts can lead to gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. These systemic effects make the plant potentially dangerous if consumed.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Phototoxicity (skin blistering/burning upon sun exposure)
  • Skin irritation
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
