Nymphoides peltata

What's the taxonomical classification of Nymphoides peltata?

Nymphoides peltata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the order Asterales and falls under the family Menyanthaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Nymphoides, and its specific species designation, peltata.

Nymphoides peltata contains various secondary metabolites including flavonoids, tannins, and phenolic compounds that contribute to its biological activity. What are the therapeutic actions of Nymphoides peltata?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, digestive issues, and various inflammatory conditions through its traditional medicinal applications. The leaves and roots contain bioactive compounds that help combat bacterial and fungal pathogens on the skin. In some herbal practices, decoctions made from the plant are consumed to alleviate internal discomfort and promote healing. Research into its chemical properties suggests potential benefits for regulating inflammatory responses in the body. While primarily used in folk medicine, its specific therapeutic uses are often localized to regional traditional practices.

What are the herbal preparations of Nymphoides peltata?

Nymphoides peltata is prepared primarily as a liquid infusion or decoction using the leaves and flowering tops of the plant. These aqueous extracts are often used in traditional topical applications to soothe skin irritations. Some practitioners also utilize dried plant material to create concentrated tinctures through alcohol extraction. These preparations are valued for their specific chemical profiles when processed in these traditional ways. Each method allows for different levels of bioavailability for the plant's active constituents.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Nymphoides peltata can cause ecological harm by displacing native aquatic vegetation and disrupting local biodiversity when introduced into non-native waterways. This invasive species forms dense floating mats that block sunlight from reaching submerged plants. These thick layers also deplete dissolved oxygen levels in the water, which can lead to fish kills and other aquatic mortality. Furthermore, the rapid growth of the plant can obstruct navigation in canals and clog drainage systems. Such environmental shifts can fundamentally alter the stability of the entire wetland ecosystem.
