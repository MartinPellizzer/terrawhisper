Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Chinese Angelica, or Angelica sinensis, is a perennial plant native to the high altitude regions of the Tibetan Plateau and other mountainous areas of China, known for its rich pharmacological properties and traditional use in Chinese medicine.

What is Chinese Angelica (Angelica Sinensis)?

Chinese Angelica, or Angelica sinensis, is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of China, known for its medicinal roots that have been used in traditional Chinese medicine for centuries to treat a variety of ailments.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Chinese Angelica is Angelica sinensis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Angelica, and species Angelica sinensis. It is commonly known as Chinese Angelica in China, and as Dong Quai in North America. It is also known as 1000-year root in some parts of China. The synonyms for this plant include Angelica acutiloba, Angelica morii, and Angelica gigas.

Plant Description and Morphology

Chinese Angelica is a biennial or perennial herb that grows up to 1 meter tall, with a stout, reddish-brown rootstock and a straight, hollow stem.

Its leaves are alternate, pinnately compound, with serrated leaflets that are lanceolate to ovate in shape. The flowers are small, clustered in loose panicles, with white or pale pink petals and yellow stamens, blooming from June to August. The root system consists of a thick, fleshy main root with numerous lateral roots, which is the primary medicinal part.

The root has a dark brown exterior with a yellowish interior, a strong earthy aroma, and a rough, fibrous texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chinese Angelica is native to the mountainous regions of China, particularly in Sichuan, Yunnan, Qinghai, and Gansu provinces.

It thrives in temperate to alpine climates with cool, moist conditions and well-drained soil. The plant prefers elevations between 1,500 and 3,600 meters where temperatures range from 5°C to 20°C. It grows in rocky or loamy soils, often in forested or semi-shaded areas.

Its natural habitat includes meadows, grasslands, and mountain slopes with adequate sunlight and moisture.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots and rhizomes of Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) are the primary parts used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) contains a complex array of phytochemical compounds, including lignans, terpenoids, coumarins, alkaloids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic effects.

Pharmacological Properties

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, immunomodulatory, and hematinic effects, primarily attributed to its bioactive compounds such as ligustilide, senkyunolide, and ferulic acid.

Mechanisms of Action

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through the modulation of inflammatory pathways, enhancement of antioxidant defense systems, and regulation of immune responses, with its active compounds such as ligustilide and ferulic acid contributing to these mechanisms.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat blood deficiency, anemia, menstrual disorders, and inflammatory conditions due to its ability to invigorate blood, regulate menstruation, and nourish the body.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Chinese Angelica is typically prepared as a dried root, often sliced or ground into powder, and used in decoctions, tinctures, or as a component in herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) varies depending on the form of the herb, with typical oral dosages ranging from 3 to 10 grams per day in decoction form, and 0.5 to 2 grams in pill or powder form, though it is essential to consult a qualified practitioner for personalized guidance.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) is generally considered safe, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by individuals with bleeding disorders.

Drug Interactions

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) may interact with anticoagulant medications, such as warfarin, by increasing the risk of bleeding, and may also affect the metabolism of certain drugs due to its potential influence on liver enzymes.

Toxicity and Precautions

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, so it should be used with caution and under professional guidance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chinese Angelica is typically cultivated in high-altitude regions with cool climates, requiring well-drained soil and a long growing season, and is harvested primarily during late autumn when the roots are most mature and medicinally potent.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Chinese Angelica involve methods such as cutting, drying, and sometimes soaking in alcohol to enhance its medicinal properties and extract active compounds like ferulic acid and ligustilic acid.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) in traditional medicine have been increasingly documented, with studies highlighting its pharmacological effects such as anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and hemostatic properties, as well as its role in improving blood circulation and treating conditions like anemia and menstrual disorders.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chinese Angelica has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for centuries to treat conditions such as blood deficiency, anemia, menstrual disorders, and inflammation, with its roots traceable to ancient texts like the Shennong Ben Cao Jing.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Chinese Angelica is regulated as a herbal medicine with specific guidelines for quality control, safety, and labeling, though regulatory frameworks may vary depending on the region and the intended use of the product.
