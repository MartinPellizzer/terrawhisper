Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Chinese Angelica, or Angelica sinensis, is a perennial plant native to the high altitude regions of the Tibetan Plateau and other mountainous areas of China, known for its rich pharmacological properties and traditional use in Chinese medicine.

What is Chinese Angelica (Angelica Sinensis)?

Chinese Angelica, or Angelica sinensis, is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of China, known for its medicinal roots that have been used in traditional Chinese medicine for centuries to treat a variety of ailments.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Chinese Angelica is Angelica sinensis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Angelica, and species Angelica sinensis. It is commonly known as Chinese Angelica in China, and as Dong Quai in North America. It is also known as 1000-year root in some parts of China. The synonyms for this plant include Angelica acutiloba, Angelica morii, and Angelica gigas.

Plant Description and Morphology

Chinese Angelica is a biennial or perennial herb that grows up to 1 meter tall, with a stout, reddish-brown rootstock and a straight, hollow stem.

Its leaves are alternate, pinnately compound, with serrated leaflets that are lanceolate to ovate in shape. The flowers are small, clustered in loose panicles, with white or pale pink petals and yellow stamens, blooming from June to August. The root system consists of a thick, fleshy main root with numerous lateral roots, which is the primary medicinal part.

The root has a dark brown exterior with a yellowish interior, a strong earthy aroma, and a rough, fibrous texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Chinese Angelica is native to the mountainous regions of China, particularly in Sichuan, Yunnan, Qinghai, and Gansu provinces.

It thrives in temperate to alpine climates with cool, moist conditions and well-drained soil. The plant prefers elevations between 1,500 and 3,600 meters where temperatures range from 5°C to 20°C. It grows in rocky or loamy soils, often in forested or semi-shaded areas.

Its natural habitat includes meadows, grasslands, and mountain slopes with adequate sunlight and moisture.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary medicinal parts of Chinese Angelica are the roots and rhizomes.

These are harvested during the autumn season when the plant has matured. The roots are typically dried and processed to preserve their active compounds. The rhizomes, which are the underground stems, are also used in traditional preparations.

Both parts contain essential oils, polysaccharides, and other bioactive substances that contribute to their medicinal properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) contains a variety of active compounds, including essential oils, alkaloids, and coumarins, which contribute to its pharmacological properties.

Secondary metabolites such as lignans and phenolic acids are also present and play a significant role in its therapeutic effects. The plant is known for its chemical markers like ligustilide and butylphthalide, which are responsible for many of its biological activities. These compounds are often analyzed to ensure the quality and authenticity of the herb.

The phytochemical profile of Chinese Angelica is well-documented and supports its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that underpin its use in traditional herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, and antioxidant effects, which contribute to its wide therapeutic applications. The biological activity of Chinese Angelica is attributed to its rich composition of bioactive compounds such as ligustilide, ferulic acid, and adenosine. Pharmacodynamically, these compounds interact with various physiological systems to modulate immune responses and reduce oxidative stress.

System-level effects include the regulation of the cardiovascular and nervous systems, enhancing overall physiological balance.

Mechanisms of Action

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) exerts its therapeutic effects through multiple mechanisms involving cellular, biochemical, and systemic interactions.

At the cellular level, it modulates immune responses by enhancing macrophage activity and promoting cytokine production. Biochemically, it activates pathways such as Nrf2 and NF-κB, which regulate antioxidant defense and inflammatory responses. These effects are mediated through bioactive compounds like ligustilide and ferulic acid.

Target systems include the circulatory, nervous, and reproductive systems, where it influences vascular tone, neurotransmitter balance, and hormone regulation.

Therapeutic Uses

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) is widely used in traditional Chinese medicine for its diverse therapeutic properties.

It is particularly effective in treating blood deficiency, menstrual disorders, and anemia due to its hemostatic and circulatory benefits. The herb is commonly prescribed for conditions such as dysmenorrhea, irregular menstruation, and postpartum recovery. It also supports the treatment of respiratory ailments like asthma and coughs by improving lung function.

Additionally, it is used to alleviate symptoms of fatigue, migraines, and inflammatory conditions.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Chinese Angelica involve various forms in which the plant is utilized.

It is most commonly used in the form of dried root, which is sliced and processed for medicinal use. The dried root can be ground into a fine powder or made into a decoction by boiling in water. Additionally, it is often incorporated into herbal formulations as part of a combination formula.

It may also be used in the form of oil extracts or alcohol tinctures for specific therapeutic applications.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of Chinese Angelica typically ranges from 3 to 10 grams per day, depending on the individual's condition and the form of the herb used.

For most applications, a concentration of 1:5 to 1:10 extract is considered appropriate, though this may vary based on the specific preparation. It is generally recommended to take the herb 1 to 3 times daily, with intervals that allow the body to process the dose effectively. The duration of treatment should be limited to 4 to 8 weeks unless otherwise directed by a healthcare professional.

Due to its potent nature, it is crucial to follow recommended guidelines closely to avoid potential adverse effects from overuse.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Chinese Angelica is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Prolonged or excessive use can lead to liver toxicity, particularly in individuals with pre-existing liver conditions. It is contraindicated for people with known hypersensitivity to the plant or its derivatives. Allergic reactions may include skin rashes, itching, or respiratory symptoms, and should be avoided in those with a history of similar allergies.

During pregnancy, it is considered unsafe due to potential risks to fetal development, and its use should be avoided unless specifically advised by a healthcare professional.

Drug Interactions

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) may interact with anticoagulant medications, such as warfarin, by increasing the risk of bleeding, and may also affect the metabolism of certain drugs due to its potential influence on liver enzymes.

Toxicity and Precautions

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, so it should be used with caution and under professional guidance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Chinese Angelica is typically cultivated in high-altitude regions with cool climates, requiring well-drained soil and a long growing season, and is harvested primarily during late autumn when the roots are most mature and medicinally potent.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Chinese Angelica involve methods such as cutting, drying, and sometimes soaking in alcohol to enhance its medicinal properties and extract active compounds like ferulic acid and ligustilic acid.

Storage and Shelf Life

Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of Chinese Angelica (Angelica sinensis) in traditional medicine have been increasingly documented, with studies highlighting its pharmacological effects such as anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and hemostatic properties, as well as its role in improving blood circulation and treating conditions like anemia and menstrual disorders.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Chinese Angelica has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for centuries to treat conditions such as blood deficiency, anemia, menstrual disorders, and inflammation, with its roots traceable to ancient texts like the Shennong Ben Cao Jing.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Chinese Angelica is regulated as a herbal medicine with specific guidelines for quality control, safety, and labeling, though regulatory frameworks may vary depending on the region and the intended use of the product.
