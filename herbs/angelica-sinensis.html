Angelica sinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica sinensis?

Angelica sinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Apiales and the family Apiaceae, which is characterized by its distinctive umbel-shaped inflorescences. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Angelica and is specifically designated as the species sinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica sinensis has a thick, fleshy taproot that is typically dark brown or blackish on the outside and yellowish-white on the inside. The plant reaches heights of up to two meters and features large, compound leaves with serrated margins. These leaves are arranged alternately along a hollow, grooved stem that is often purplish in color. Small, white flowers are arranged in dense, umbrella-shaped umbels that bloom during the summer months. The plant produces small, oval-shaped fruits that contain several tiny seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude mountainous regions of China, specifically within provinces such as Sichuan, Yunnan, Guizhou, and Gansu. It thrives in cool, moist environments typically found at elevations ranging from 1,500 to 3,000 meters above sea level. The species prefers well-drained soils located along mountain slopes and near forest edges where sunlight is partially filtered. Because of its specific preference for temperate, high-elevation climates, its natural range is quite restricted compared to more widespread botanical species. This localized distribution makes it a significant component of the traditional medicinal flora found in those specific Chinese highlands.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant Angelica sinensis requires cool, moist climates and well-drained, fertile loamy soil to thrive during its two-year growth cycle.

Farmers typically sow the seeds in the autumn to allow for natural stratification during the winter months. During the first year, the plant focuses on developing a robust rosette of leaves and a strong root system. In the second year, the plant produces a flowering umbel before the roots reach their optimal medicinal potency.

Harvesting must occur in the late autumn of the second year when the plant's chemical constituents are most concentrated.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Angelica sinensis contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its thick, fleshy roots which are harvested and dried for therapeutic use. These roots are the most significant part of the plant because they serve as the foundation for traditional herbal formulations. The rhizomes are processed to extract essential oils and polysaccharides that are believed to support blood circulation and hormonal balance. While the roots are the primary medicinal component, other parts like the seeds are occasionally utilized in specific preparations. Careful cultivation and drying of the root system are essential to ensure the potency of its bioactive constituents.

What are the pharmacological activities of Angelica sinensis?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including blood activating, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, promoting blood circulation, and nourishing the blood. Its unique chemical constituents allow it to effectively mitigate oxidative stress and reduce systemic inflammation within the body. By enhancing the movement of blood through the vessels, it serves as a potent agent to promote blood circulation. Additionally, the plant is traditionally used to nourish the blood to support overall hematological health. These combined effects make it a significant subject in both traditional medicine and modern pharmacological research.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Angelica sinensis was identified as the most frequently used core herb among 11 herbs found in 98 collected postpartum lochia discharge formulations. These formulations used a total of 60 medicinal materials, and the 11 core herbs formed 136 drug combinations consisting of 2-7 herbs each. Angelica sinensis was positioned in the center of the network and jointly appeared with Ligusticum striatum in 92.8% of the analyzed formulations. The medicinal materials used, which include Angelica sinensis, predominantly focused on the traditional functions of qi tonifying and blood activating.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several bioactive medicinal compounds including ligustilide, senkyunolide A, ferulic acid, n-butylidenephthalide, and Angelica sineensis polysaccharides. Ligustilide and n-butylidenephthalide are key phthalides responsible for many of the plant's cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory effects. Senkyunolide A serves as another important component that contributes to its diverse pharmacological activities. Ferulic acid provides significant antioxidant properties that help protect cells from oxidative stress. Finally, Angelica sineensis polysaccharides play a crucial role in modulating immune responses within the body.

According to a study published by "Scientific reports", Ligustilide (LIG) is the main component of Angelica sinensis and is considered to be the most effective biologically active ingredient in this plant. This major bioactive component promotes bone formation via the GPR30/EGFR pathway. Ligustilide has been found to have multiple pharmacological activities, such as anti-atherosclerosis, neuroprotection, anticancer, anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects. The study showed that LIG significantly ameliorated inhibition of bone formation in zebrafish caused by prednisolone. Furthermore, LIG promoted osteoblast differentiation, including that of the pre-osteoblastic cell line MC3T3-E1 and bone marrow mesenchymal stem cells. LIG also greatly improved the viability of MC3T3-E1 cells exposed to H2O2 and attenuated H2O2-induced apoptosis.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for improving blood circulation, resolving blood stasis, treating various gynecological disorders, alleviating menopause symptoms, and nourishing the blood. By promoting the movement of blood, it helps to clear blockages and reduce pain associated with stagnation. Its ability to nourish the blood makes it particularly effective for addressing deficiencies that lead to weakness or fatigue. In women's health, it is frequently utilized to regulate menstrual cycles and ease the discomfort of reproductive issues. Furthermore, it serves as a natural support for managing the hormonal shifts and physical symptoms experienced during menopause.

According to a study published by "Wound repair and regeneration : official publication of the Wound Healing Society [and] the European Tissue Repair Society", Angelica sinensis was selected for investigation due to its benefits for blood circulation. A purified low-molecular-weight fraction from the plant, named SBD.4A, demonstrated strong wound healing activity in the diabetic mouse wound-healing model and the human/severe combined immunodeficiency mouse chimera wound-healing model. In both of these models, SBD.4A compared favorably with the Food and Drug Administration-approved wound-healing drug becaplermin. A hydrophilic fraction of SBD.4A strongly stimulated endothelial cell proliferation, tridimensional endothelial cell network formation, human dermal fibroblast proliferation, and type I collagen secretion. These processes suggest that this botanical product could be a valuable treatment for difficult-to-heal wounds.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause various adverse reactions including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, increased bleeding risk, photosensitivity, and dizziness. Gastrointestinal distress such as stomach cramps or digestive upset is a common occurrence for some users. Because it may affect blood clotting, there is a heightened danger of excessive bleeding during injury or surgery. Some individuals might experience skin sensitivity to sunlight, leading to increased risk of burns. Neurological symptoms like dizziness can also manifest following consumption.
