Angelica sinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica sinensis?

Angelica sinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Apiales and the family Apiaceae, which is characterized by its distinctive umbel-shaped inflorescences. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Angelica and is specifically designated as the species sinensis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Angelica sinensis contains a complex mixture of bioactive compounds including coumarins like ferulic acid, ligustilide, phthalides, and various essential oils. These chemical constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's diverse pharmacological effects on the human body. Ligustilide, in particular, is noted for its significant role in regulating blood flow and providing neuroprotective benefits. The presence of ferulic acid contributes antioxidant properties that help combat oxidative stress at a cellular level. Together, these compounds work synergistically to support cardiovascular health and alleviate menstrual discomfort in traditional medicinal practices.

What are the therapeutic actions of Angelica sinensis?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat blood deficiency, menstrual irregularities, abdominal pain caused by coldness, and various forms of anemia. It is primarily utilized in traditional medicine to promote blood circulation and regulate the menstrual cycle in women. Practitioners often prescribe it to alleviate symptoms of dysmenorrhea and postpartum bleeding issues. Additionally, its properties are leveraged to address stagnation in the liver and improve overall vitality. The herb is frequently combined with other botanicals to enhance its efficacy in managing gynecological and circulatory disorders.

What are the herbal preparations of Angelica sinensis?

Angelica sinensis is prepared primarily as a decoction, a concentrated liquid extract made by boiling the dried roots in water, though it is also processed into tinctures, powders, and topical oils. The most common traditional method involves simmering the sliced roots to extract active volatile oils and glycosides for oral consumption. In modern pharmacy, the dried root is often ground into a fine powder that can be encapsulated or mixed into granules for easier dosing. Tinctures provide a more concentrated alcohol-based alternative that preserves specific aromatic compounds. These various forms allow practitioners to tailor the dosage and delivery method based on the intended therapeutic effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Angelica sinensis can cause adverse reactions such as gastrointestinal upset, skin irritation, photosensitivity, and potential interference with blood clotting or hormonal balance. Some individuals may experience nausea, abdominal pain, or diarrhea shortly after consumption. Because it contains coumarins, it may increase the risk of bleeding, especially if taken alongside anticoagulant medications. Topical application can lead to dermatitis or increased sensitivity to sunlight in some users. It is also important to note that its estrogenic effects may impact people with hormone-sensitive conditions.
