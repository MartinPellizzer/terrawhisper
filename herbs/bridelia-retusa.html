Bridelia retusa

What's the taxonomical classification of Bridelia retusa?

Bridelia retusa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malpighiales. This plant is further categorized into the family Phyllanthaceae, within which it is placed in the genus Bridelia. Finally, its specific identification is completed by its species name, retusa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bridelia retusa has opposite, simple leaves that are elliptic to obovate in shape, measuring approximately 5 to 15 centimeters in length with entire margins and acute to acuminate apices. The leaf texture is typically coriaceous, often featuring a glabrous upper surface and a slightly pubescent underside. Its woody stems are characterized by a somewhat rough bark and a branching pattern that supports a small to medium-sized shrub or tree habit. The inflorescences consist of small, inconspicuous flowers arranged in dense axillary clusters or panicles. These flowers eventually develop into small, globose drupes that change color as they mature.

What are the pharmacological activities of Bridelia retusa?

This plant has antibacterial, anti-biofilm, and beta-lactamase inhibitor activities that make it a significant subject in pharmacological research. Its phytochemical constituents allow it to effectively suppress the growth of various pathogenic bacterial strains. Beyond direct killing, the plant can disrupt the protective matrices that bacteria use to form resilient biofilms. It also functions as a beta-lactamase inhibitor, which helps prevent bacteria from breaking down certain antibiotic medications. These combined properties suggest that the plant could play a vital role in combating multidrug-resistant infections.

As discussed in a study, the leaf extracts of Bridelia retusa contain phytoconstituent components including flavonoids, phenolic compounds, phytosterols, and glycosides. These plant extracts act as strong reducing agents for the continuous synthesis of biocompatible silver nanoparticles. The synthesized nanoparticles were screened for antibacterial and anti-biofilm activity against 3 human pathogens, specifically Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Escherichia coli, and Staphylococcus aureus. The study employed UV-vis spectroscopic analysis, Attenuated total reflectance-Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy, and dynamic light scattering spectroscopy to characterize these particles. Morphological examination of the nanoparticles was also conducted through scanning electron microscopy.
