Dicliptera chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Dicliptera chinensis?

Dicliptera chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Lamiales. This plant is a representative of the family Acanthaceae, specifically situated within the genus Dicliptera, and is identified by the unique species name chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dicliptera chinensis has an erect herbaceous habit characterized by opposite, lanceolate to ovate leaves with entire margins and a woody base. The stems are typically slender and can reach heights of up to one meter in length. Its flowers are small, zygomorphic, and emerge from the axils of the leaves in a tubular structure. The inflorescence often features a distinct bilabiate corolla that transitions from white to light purple or blue hues. After pollination, the plant develops small, rounded capsules that contain numerous tiny seeds.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dicliptera chinensis?

This plant has anti-fibrotic pharmacological activity that helps prevent the excessive accumulation of connective tissue in organs. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can mitigate the fibrotic processes that lead to organ dysfunction. By modulating specific cellular pathways, the plant may reduce the scarring effects caused by chronic inflammation. These medicinal properties make it a subject of interest for developing treatments related to tissue repair. Further studies are necessary to fully understand the molecular mechanisms behind this effect.

As discussed in a study, Dicliptera chinensis polysaccharide (DCP) alleviates dimethylnitrosamine-induced liver fibrosis in rats through the suppression of the TGF-β/Smad pathway. In vivo experiments involved administering DCP at concentrations of 0, 100, and 300 mg/kg via intraperitoneal injection. These treatments significantly down-regulated the expression of TGF-β1, pSmad2/3, and α-SMA in liver tissue in a dose-dependent way. In vitro, DCP was applied at concentrations of 0, 25, 50, 100, 200, and 400 µg/ml to HSC-T6 cells stimulated with 1 ng/mL of recombinant rat TGF-β1. This intervention inhibited the proliferation and activation of the cells while significantly down-regulating α-SMA and pSmad2/3 expression.

As discussed in a study, Dicliptera chinensis polysaccharide (DCP) targets the TGF-β/Smad pathway to inhibit hepatic stellate cell activation. In rats, DCP was administered via intraperitoneal injection at concentrations of 0, 100, and 300 mg/kg. To simulate an activating model in vitro, HSC-T6 cells were stimulated by recombinant rat TGF-β1 at 1 ng/mL. These cells were then interfered with DCP at concentrations of 0, 25, 50, 100, 200, and 400 µg/ml. The results showed that DCP significantly down-regulated the expression of TGF-β1, pSmad2/3, and α-SMA in a dose-dependent way. This botanical polysaccharide shows potential for the management of liver fibrosis.

Ultimately, the study revealed that DCP attenuates the progression of liver fibrosis by suppressing the TGF-β/Smad pathway.
