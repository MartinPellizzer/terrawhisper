Dicliptera chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Dicliptera chinensis?

Dicliptera chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Lamiales. This plant is a representative of the family Acanthaceae, specifically situated within the genus Dicliptera, and is identified by the unique species name chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dicliptera chinensis has an erect herbaceous habit characterized by opposite, lanceolate to ovate leaves with entire margins and a woody base. The stems are typically slender and can reach heights of up to one meter in length. Its flowers are small, zygomorphic, and emerge from the axils of the leaves in a tubular structure. The inflorescence often features a distinct bilabiate corolla that transitions from white to light purple or blue hues. After pollination, the plant develops small, rounded capsules that contain numerous tiny seeds.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

What health problems are treated using this plant?

This plant is used for treating hepatic fibrosis, inflammatory conditions, and various skin ailments. It possesses significant anti-inflammatory properties that help mitigate swelling and tissue damage in the body. Researchers have specifically studied its ability to slow the progression of liver scarring through its protective effects on hepatic cells. Additionally, its medicinal extracts are often applied to address topical infections and dermatological issues. These diverse therapeutic applications stem from the plant's unique bioactive compounds.

As discussed in a study, Dicliptera chinensis polysaccharide (DCP) was administered to rats at concentrations of 0, 100, and 300 mg/kg to alleviate DMN-induced liver fibrosis. In vitro, DCP was tested at concentrations of 0, 25, 50, 100, 200, and 400 µg/ml against HSC-T6 cells stimulated by 1 ng/mL of recombinant rat TGF-β1. The results demonstrated that DCP significantly down-regulated TGF-β1 expression, pSmad2/3, and α-SMA in liver tissue in a dose-dependent way. Furthermore, DCP inhibited the proliferation and activation of TGF-β1-stimulated HSC-T6 cells and significantly down-regulated α-SMA and pSmad2/3 expression. Ultimately, the study revealed that DCP attenuates the progression of liver fibrosis by suppressing the TGF-β/Smad pathway.
