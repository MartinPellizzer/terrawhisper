Notoginseng (Panax notoginseng): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Notoginseng, also known as Panax notoginseng, is a revered medicinal herb native to the mountainous regions of China, traditionally used in Chinese medicine for its purported ability to promote blood circulation, reduce inflammation, and enhance recovery from injuries.

What is Notoginseng (Panax Notoginseng)?

Notoginseng, also known as Panax notoginseng, is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of China, primarily found in Yunnan Province, and is widely used in traditional Chinese medicine for its purported ability to promote blood circulation, reduce inflammation, and aid in wound healing.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Notoginseng is Panax notoginseng. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Araliaceae, genus Panax, and species Panax notoginseng. It is commonly known as Notoginseng in China, and as Tienchi or Sanqi in other regions of East Asia. The synonyms for this plant include Panax pseudoginseng and Panax qinlingensis, which are botanical and regional variations.

Plant Description and Morphology

Notoginseng is a perennial herb that grows up to 1 meter tall, featuring a straight, branching stem covered in soft, gray-green hairs.

Its leaves are alternate, lanceolate to ovate in shape, with a smooth margin and a pointed tip, typically measuring 5-15 cm in length. The flowers are small, greenish-yellow, and clustered in loose inflorescences, with five petals and stamens that are shorter than the petals. The root system consists of a central rhizome with numerous fleshy, fibrous roots that are typically pale yellow to light brown in color.

The plant has a faint, earthy aroma, with a slightly bitter and woody texture when handled.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Notoginseng is native to the mountainous regions of China, particularly the provinces of Yunnan, Sichuan, and Guizhou, where it grows in the subtropical and temperate climates of the Hengduan Mountains and other highland areas.

Its geographic distribution is primarily confined to these regions, with limited cultivation in neighboring countries such as Vietnam and Myanmar. The plant thrives in cool, moist environments with well-drained soil, typically found at elevations between 1,500 and 2,500 meters above sea level. It requires a humid climate with mild winters and warm, rainy summers to support its growth cycle.

The ecological conditions of its native habitat include dense forest cover, partial shade, and a rich understory that provides the necessary microclimate for its survival.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in Notoginseng (Panax notoginseng) are the root and rhizome.

These components are harvested after several years of growth and undergo careful processing. The root is typically sliced and dried to produce the dried root, which is the most commonly used form. The rhizome, often referred to as the "red root," is also utilized in traditional formulations.

Both parts are valued for their pharmacological properties and are integral to the preparation of various herbal remedies.

Phytochemical Composition

Notoginseng contains a variety of active compounds, including saponins, which are responsible for its medicinal properties.

The most well-known saponins in notoginseng are notoginsenosides, a group of protopanaxadiol-type triterpenoid saponins. Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids and polyphenols contribute to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. These compounds also include ginsenosides, which are structurally similar to other ginseng compounds but have distinct biological activities.

Chemical markers like notoginseng saponins and flavonoids are used to authenticate the quality and purity of notoginseng products.

Pharmacological Properties

Notoginseng exhibits a range of pharmacological activities that support its use in traditional medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, anticoagulant, and cardioprotective effects. The biological activity of notoginseng is attributed to compounds such as ginsenosides and notoginsenosides. Pharmacodynamically, it influences platelet aggregation and vascular tone.

System-level effects involve improved circulation and reduced oxidative stress in multiple organs.

Mechanisms of Action

Notoginseng exerts its therapeutic effects through multiple mechanisms involving cellular, biochemical, and systemic interactions.

At the cellular level, it modulates signaling pathways and reduces oxidative stress by enhancing the activity of antioxidant enzymes. Biochemically, it contains saponins and flavonoids that influence inflammatory and immune responses by inhibiting pro-inflammatory cytokines. These compounds also interact with various receptors and enzymes, such as cyclooxygenase and phosphodiesterase, to exert their effects.

Target systems include the cardiovascular, nervous, and immune systems, where Notoginseng promotes tissue repair and reduces pathological inflammation.

Therapeutic Uses

Notoginseng is widely used in traditional Chinese medicine for its purported ability to promote blood circulation, reduce inflammation, and enhance recovery from injuries.

It is commonly employed to treat conditions such as traumatic injuries, bleeding disorders, and cardiovascular issues. The herb is also believed to support the healing of wounds and fractures by improving tissue repair and reducing swelling. Additionally, it is used to address symptoms like dizziness, fatigue, and palpitations, which are often linked to blood deficiency or stagnation.

Its therapeutic effects are attributed to its rich content of saponins and other bioactive compounds.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Notoginseng vary depending on its intended use, with common forms including raw herb, processed powder, and extracts.

It is frequently used in the form of dried root slices, which are either sliced thinly or ground into a fine powder. The powdered form is often encapsulated or made into tablets for convenient consumption. Additionally, it can be brewed as a decoction by simmering the root in water.

Extracts are also available in concentrated liquid or softgel form, offering a more potent dosage.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Notoginseng (Panax notoginseng) varies depending on the form of the herb, with typical oral doses ranging from 1 to 3 grams per day in divided doses, though specific formulations may require professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Notoginseng is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause mild side effects such as stomach discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and it should be avoided by those with bleeding disorders or during pregnancy without medical supervision.

Drug Interactions

Notoginseng may interact with anticoagulant medications, antiplatelet drugs, and certain cardiovascular medications due to its potential to affect blood clotting and cardiovascular function.

Toxicity and Precautions

Notoginseng is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by individuals with bleeding disorders due to its potential anticoagulant properties.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Notoginseng is typically cultivated in shaded, well-drained mountainous regions with a humid climate, and it is harvested after 5 to 7 years of growth, primarily during the late autumn or early winter when the roots reach their mature size.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Notoginseng involve methods such as drying, slicing, and using solvents to isolate active compounds like ginsenosides, which are critical for its medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Proper storage of Notoginseng (Panax notoginseng) in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight is essential to maintain its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of 2 to 5 years when stored correctly.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence on Notoginseng (Panax notoginseng) have increasingly focused on its pharmacological properties, including its potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and cardioprotective effects, with studies supporting its traditional use in promoting wound healing and cardiovascular health.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Notoginseng has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for centuries to promote wound healing, reduce inflammation, and enhance circulation, with historical records dating back to the Ming Dynasty.

Regulatory Status

Notoginseng (Panax notoginseng) is classified as a traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) herb and is regulated under the Chinese Pharmacopoeia, with specific guidelines for its cultivation, processing, and quality control.
