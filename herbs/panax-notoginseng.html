What to know about Panax notoginseng (tienchi) before using it medicinally

Panax notoginseng, commonly known as tienchi, is a medicinal herb that has been prized for its health-promoting properties for centuries, including its ability to improve cardiovascular health, reduce inflammation, and boost immune function.

Cultivation of this herb requires specific conditions, including well-drained acidic soil and high altitudes, which can limit its geographical distribution. From a botanical perspective, Panax notoginseng is a perennials that belongs to the Panax genus, characterized by its unique, forked roots.

The herb has been mentioned in traditional Chinese medicine for over 1,000 years, with ancient texts such as the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing referencing its medicinal uses.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Panax notoginseng.

What are the medicinal properties of Panax notoginseng?

Panax notoginseng helps with cardiovascular health, reducing blood pressure, and improving circulation. It is also used to alleviate symptoms of anxiety and depression. Additionally, it has anti-inflammatory properties, reducing swelling and pain.

The active constituents of Panax notoginseng include ginsenosides, phenolic acids, and polysaccharides, which are responsible for its medicinal properties. Ginsenosides are the primary active compounds, comprising 70-80% of the total saponin content.

The roots and rhizomes of Panax notoginseng are most commonly used for medicinal purposes. These parts are harvested in the autumn and dried to preserve their potency.

Improper use of Panax notoginseng may lead to gastrointestinal upset, such as nausea and diarrhea. Additionally, high doses may cause headaches and dizziness. Long-term use may also cause interactions with other medications.

Precautions when using Panax notoginseng include avoiding high doses, especially in individuals with bleeding disorders or taking anticoagulant medications. Additionally, pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult their healthcare provider before using this herb medicinally.

What are the horticulural aspects of Panax notoginseng?

Panax notoginseng grow in subtropical and tropical regions of Asia, requiring a temperature range of 15-30°C (59-86°F) and annual rainfall of 1,500-2,000 mm (59-79 in). Well-draining acidic soils with pH 5.5-6.5 are preferred for optimal growth.

Proper planting of Panax notoginseng involves selecting a location with partial shade and well-draining soil. Seeds or seedlings are planted in spring or autumn, 10-15 cm (3.9-5.9 in) deep and 30-40 cm (11.8-15.7 in) apart, with adequate watering and fertilization.

Harvesting of Panax notoginseng typically occurs after 4-6 years of cultivation, when the roots reach a diameter of 2-5 cm (0.79-1.97 in). Harvesting is done by digging the roots carefully to avoid damaging them, and then cleaning and drying them for storage and processing.

Common pests affecting Panax notoginseng include the root-knot nematode (Meloidogyne spp.) and the spider mite (Tetranychus spp.), while diseases such as root rot (Phytophthora spp.), leaf blight (Pseudomonas spp.), and damping off (Pythium spp.) can also occur.

What are the botanical characteristics of Panax notoginseng?

Panax notoginseng, also known as San Qi or Tien Qi, is a perennial plant species in the Araliaceae family.

The botanical characteristics of Panax notoginseng are notable for its unique morphology. The plant grows up to 1-2 meters tall with multiple stems and leaves that are alternate, petiolate, and palmately compound. Each leaf consists of five to seven leaflets that are ovate or elliptical in shape, measuring 10-20 cm long and 5-15 cm wide.

The flowers of Panax notoginseng are small and greenish-yellow in color, arranged in a terminal umbel-like inflorescence that can reach up to 30 cm in diameter. The fruit is an aggregate berry, consisting of several smaller berries that are reddish-purple in color when mature. The rhizome of Panax notoginseng is the part used medicinally and is characterized by its thick, fleshy, and branching nature.

It is rich in saponins, flavonoids, and other bioactive compounds, which contribute to its reported medicinal properties.

What are the variants of Panax notoginseng?

Panax notoginseng has variants, such as P. n. chinense and P. n. koreanum, which are considered to be subspecies or geographical forms of the species.

The main differences between these variants lie in their morphological characteristics, growth habits, and chemical compositions. For example, P. n. chinense is characterized by its smaller root size and higher content of ginsenosides Rb1 and Rc, while P. n. koreanum has a larger root size and higher content of ginsenoside Rd.

These variants may have different therapeutic properties and uses in traditional Chinese medicine, although more research is needed to fully understand their differences.

What is the geographic distribution of Panax notoginseng?

Panax notoginseng, also known as Chinese ginseng or Sanqi, is native to southern China and Southeast Asia.

Its geographic distribution includes the provinces of Guangxi, Guizhou, Hunan, Yunnan, and Xishuangbanna in China, as well as parts of Myanmar, Laos, Vietnam, and Thailand. The plant typically grows at high elevations between 1,000 to 2,500 meters above sea level in mountainous regions with mild temperatures, rich soil, and adequate rainfall.

Its specific habitats include subtropical forests, mountain meadows, and scrublands.
