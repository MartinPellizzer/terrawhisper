Panax notoginseng

What's the taxonomical classification of Panax notoginseng?

Panax notoginseng belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Apiales. This plant is further categorized under the family Araliaceae, which places it within the genus Panax, ultimately identifying it by its specific species name, notoginseng.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Panax notoginseng has a fleshy, spindle-shaped taproot that is typically light yellow to white in color and possesses a characteristic pungent aroma. The aerial portion consists of a perennial herbaceous structure featuring large, palmate leaves that often grow in clusters of three to five leaflets. These leaves are dark green and possess serrated margins along their edges. During the flowering stage, the plant produces small, white, star-shaped flowers arranged in umbels on a central stalk. After pollination, the plant develops small, round berries that transition from green to a bright red color when fully ripe.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of the Korean Peninsula and parts of northeastern China. It thrives primarily in temperate forest ecosystems characterized by high humidity and cool climates. Its natural habitat is typically restricted to shaded areas with well-drained, organic-rich soil found at specific elevations. Due to its specialized environmental requirements, the species is often confined to protected woodland zones. Wild populations are increasingly rare as natural habitats face pressure from human activity and climate shifts.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Panax notoginseng contains bioactive saponins and polysaccharides primarily concentrated in its fleshy taproot. This underground root is harvested and processed to extract the medicinal compounds used in traditional and modern pharmacology. While the root is the principal part used for therapeutic purposes, some studies also investigate the potential benefits of the rhizomes. The specific ginsenosides found within these root tissues are credited with supporting cardiovascular health and cognitive function. Consequently, the entire root structure is carefully cultivated to ensure a high concentration of these valuable medicinal elements.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several medicinal ginsenosides including Ginsenoside Rg1, Ginsenoside Rb1, Ginsenoside Rd, Ginsenoside Re, and Ginsenoside Rg3. These bioactive saponins are the primary functional components responsible for the herb's adaptogenic properties. Ginsenoside Rg1 and Ginsenoside Re are often noted for their potential effects on the central nervous system and cognitive function. Meanwhile, Ginsenoside Rb1 and Ginsenoside Rd contribute to various metabolic and physiological regulatory processes. The presence of Ginsenoside Rg3 is particularly significant due to its studied roles in supporting immune health and cellular protection.

As discussed in a study, Panax notoginseng contains the saponin Ginsenoside Rg1, which is a natural active ingredient derived from its roots or stems. This compound exerts neurotrophic and neuroprotective effects through 4 sets of mechanisms. These include anti-oxidant activity involving pathways such as Akt, Nrf2/HO-1, PPARγ/HO-1, ERK, p38, and c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK). The plant's medicinal activity also involves anti-inflammatory activities via MAPK pathways including JNK1/2 and ERK1/2. Furthermore, it influences neurological cell cycle, proliferation, differentiation, and regeneration through pathways like PI3K-Akt/mTOR, PKB/Akt, and HIF-1α/VEGF.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be processed into various medicinal forms including decoction, tincture, powder, extract, and alcoholic preparation. A decoction is typically made by simmering the dried roots in water to draw out active ginsenosides. For more concentrated applications, a tincture or an alcoholic preparation is created by soaking the plant material in ethanol. The dried root can also be finely ground into a powder for direct consumption or mixed into food. Additionally, a standardized extract provides a potent and measured dose of the plant's primary therapeutic compounds.

What side effects this plant can have?

Stimulant-like effects can lead to cardiovascular issues like hypertension, tachycardia, and palpitations. Neurological and psychological symptoms often manifest as headache, dizziness, agitation, or irritability. Additionally, the herb is known to cause significant changes in sleep pattern and persistent insomnia.
