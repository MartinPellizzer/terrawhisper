Quassia amara

Ashwagandha is a prominent medicinal herb in Ayurvedic medicine, classified as a rasayana (rejuvenator). It is primarily utilized as an adaptogen to help the body manage physical and chemical stress.

Family
Solanaceae (Nightshade)

Native Region
India, Middle East, Africa

Part Used
Root, Leaf, Berry

Botanical Identification

Quassia amara is a species of evergreen shrub in the family Simaroubaceae. It is also known as quassia or bitter wood, native to tropical regions of South America. The plant has dark green, oval leaves and yellow flowers, with a distinctive aromatic, bitter sap. It is sometimes referred to as Quassia indica or Quassia cordifolia in older herbal texts.

Active Compounds

Quassia contains key bioactive compounds such as alkaloids, saponins, and flavonoids. These compounds work synergistically to enhance its medicinal properties. Alkaloids contribute to its bitter taste and potential therapeutic effects, while saponins and flavonoids support anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activities.

  • Alkaloid
  • Saponin
  • Flavonoid

Therapeutic Indications

System Condidtion Action
Respiratory Tuberculosis, tonsillitis, respiratory infections, cough Antimicrobial, expectorant, antitussive
Musculoskeletal Gout, arthritis, joint pain, inflammatory joint pain Anti-inflammatory
Immune Malaria, fever, influenza, inflammatory conditions Antimicrobial, antipyretic, immunostimulant, anti-inflammatory

Preparation Methods

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and as an antipyretic

Extract: Used for treating fever and as an antiseptic

Powder: Used for respiratory infections and as a digestive aid

Safety Profile

Quassia amara is generally safe when used in recommended doses. However, it should not be taken by pregnant or breastfeeding women due to potential risks. Long-term use may cause gastrointestinal irritation, so it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before use.
