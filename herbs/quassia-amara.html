Forms, Preparation and Usage

Quassia amara has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare quassia amara, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. An infusion can also be made by soaking the powdered form in warm water, and topical applications are common for skin conditions. For adults, a typical dosage is 1-2 grams of dried leaves or 1-2 capsules per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision if safe.

It is recommended to use quassia amara sparingly, with a maximum duration of 2-4 weeks, to avoid potential toxicity and ensure safe long-term use.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Quassia amara can be used as a medicinal plant for its antipyretic, antimalarial, and digestive properties, but it requires careful consideration due to its potential risks.

While generally safe when used in appropriate doses, quassia amara may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, and in higher doses, it may lead to more severe symptoms like liver damage. It may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and antidiabetic drugs, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Special populations, such as pregnant or breastfeeding women, should avoid quassia amara due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic liver or kidney disease should use it with caution.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a healthcare professional before use, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if any adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Quassia amara grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a pH range of 5.5 to 7.5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain moist but not waterlogged conditions.

It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates and benefits from mulching to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. For optimal growth, regular pruning of dead or diseased branches and monitoring for pests like scale insects are essential. The leaves and bark are harvested during the dry season when their medicinal compounds are most concentrated, using clean, sharp tools to cut the branches or strip the bark without damaging the plant.

After harvesting, the plant parts should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area to preserve potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties.
