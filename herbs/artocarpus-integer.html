Artocarpus integer

What's the taxonomical classification of Artocarpus integer?

Artocarpus integer belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant further falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Moraceae. Ultimately, it is identified by the genus Artocarpus and the specific species designation integer.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artocarpus integer has large, coriaceous leaves that are elliptic to oblong in shape, possessing an entire margin and an acute to acuminate apex. The foliage is typically dark green on the upper surface and paler beneath, with a distinctively smooth or slightly leathery texture. The plant features a woody trunk that can reach significant heights, often supporting a dense, spreading canopy. Its reproductive structures consist of fleshy, syncarpous fruits that are covered in small, soft protuberances. The flowers are small and arranged in axillary inflorescences, characteristic of the Moraceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand, and parts of the Philippines. It typically thrives in the humid, lowland rainforest environments characteristic of these equatorial territories. The species is most commonly found in primary forests where high rainfall and consistent warmth are available. Some populations may also extend into southern China depending on local climate conditions. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of dense, evergreen canopy cover.

What are the pharmacological activities of Artocarpus integer?

This plant has antimicrobial, antimalarial, and antityrosinase pharmacological activities derived from its diverse bioactive compounds. Studies indicate that extracts from Artocarpus integer demonstrate significant inhibitory effects against various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Its chemical constituents also exhibit potent properties that can combat malaria-causing parasites. Furthermore, the plant shows promising antityrosinase activity, which suggests potential applications in treating skin hyperpigmentation. These multifaceted biological actions highlight the therapeutic potential of the species in medicinal pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, an anti-malarial activity-guided study of the aerial parts of Artocarpus integer led to the isolation of the prenylated stilbene, trans-4-(3-methyl-E-but-1-enyl)-3,5,2',4'-tetrahydroxystilbene. This specific compound demonstrated an EC50 of 1.7 micrograms/ml against Plasmodium falciparum in culture. Additionally, the known stilbenes trans-4-isopentenyl-3,5,2',4'-tetrahydroxystilbene and 4-methoxy-2,2-dimethyl-6-(2-(2,4-dihydroxy)phenyl-trans-ethenyl)chromene were also isolated from the plant. The structures of these isolated compounds were deduced based on their spectral data.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds artocarpanone, artocarpine, cudraflavone C, flavone 12, and various geranylated xanthones. These specific phytochemicals are known for contributing to the biological activities and therapeutic potential of the species. The presence of artocarpanone and artocarpine suggests significant bioactive properties within the plant tissues. Additionally, the inclusion of cudraflavone C and flavone 12 provides a diverse chemical profile often studied for medicinal purposes. Geranylated xanthones further enhance the complexity of its chemical composition.

As discussed in a study, Artocarpus integer root extract demonstrated a tyrosinase inhibition of 90.57 ± 2.93 %. The plant contains three pure compounds isolated as artocarpin, cudraflavone C, and artocarpanone. Artocarpanone exhibited an anti-tyrosinase effect, while artocarpin and cudraflavone C showed antibacterial activity against S. aureus, S. epidermidis, and P. acnes. These compounds showed a MIC at 2, 4, and 2 μg/ml, respectively, and a MBC at 32 μg/ml for these bacteria. The root extract also showed antimicrobial activity against S. aureus, S. epidermidis, P. acnes, and Trichophyton mentagophytes with inhibition zones of 9.10 ± 0.00, 10.67 ± 0.09, 15.25 ± 0.05, and 6.60 ± 0.17 mm, respectively.

What health problems is this plant used for?

The plant also serves as a natural remedy for skin issues caused by bacteria like Propionibacterium acne and Staphylococcus aureus. Additionally, it effectively manages infections involving Staphylococcus epidermidis to prevent a widespread microbial skin infection.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause allergic skin reactions, digestive upset, or gastrointestinal distress if consumed in excessive amounts. While the fruit is generally considered safe for consumption, some individuals may experience localized itching or rashes upon contact with the latex. There is limited clinical data regarding its systemic toxicity, but consuming unripe specimens might lead to stomach discomfort. People with known sensitivities to the Moraceae family should exercise caution when handling or eating the fruit.
