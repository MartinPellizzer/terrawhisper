What to know about Centaurea cyanus (cornflower) before using it medicinally

Centaurea cyanus, commonly known as cornflower, is a herb that has been valued for its numerous health benefits, which range from relieving digestive issues and reducing inflammation to promoting antioxidant and anti-cancer properties.

As a popular ornamental plant, it is highly prized for its vibrant blue flowers, which bloom in profusion and are a magnet for pollinators, making it an attractive addition to any garden. From a botanical standpoint, Cornflower is an annual herb that belongs to the Asteraceae family and exhibits a unique combination of characteristics, including a taproot system and a leafy stem covered in fine hairs.

Its historical references date back to ancient times, with mentions in the works of the Greek physician Hippocrates and the Roman naturalist Pliny the Elder, who touted its medicinal properties and beauty.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Centaurea cyanus.

What are the medicinal properties of Centaurea cyanus?

Centaurea cyanus helps with anxiety, insomnia, and restlessness due to its sedative and calming effects. It has been traditionally used to treat digestive issues, such as diarrhea and constipation, and as an anti-inflammatory agent. Its antispasmodic properties help to relieve menstrual cramps and muscle spasms. Additionally, it has been used to treat skin conditions, including eczema and acne.

The active constituents of Centaurea cyanus that contribute to its medicinal properties include flavonoids, such as quercetin and kaempferol, and terpenes like limonene and pinene. These compounds have been shown to exhibit anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and sedative activities, which are responsible for the plant's medicinal effects.

The most commonly used parts of the plant for medicinal purposes are the flowers, leaves, and roots. The flowers are used to treat anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues, while the leaves and roots are used to treat skin conditions, including eczema and acne. The flowers and leaves are typically made into infusions or tinctures, while the roots are used to prepare decoctions.

When used improperly, Centaurea cyanus can cause adverse effects, including nausea, dizziness, and allergic reactions. Excessive consumption can lead to over-sedation, and interactions with other medications can exacerbate these effects. Additionally, the plant's antispasmodic properties can interact with other medications, such as antacids and laxatives.

To use Centaurea cyanus medicinally safely, it is essential to follow proper dosages and preparation methods. It is recommended to consult with a healthcare professional before using the plant, especially if you have underlying medical conditions or are taking other medications.

What are the horticulural aspects of Centaurea cyanus?

Centaurea cyanus grow best in full sun to partial shade and well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. It requires a minimum of 12 hours of direct sunlight and can tolerate temperatures between 65°F to 75°F (18°C to 24°C).

To plant cornflower, sow seeds directly into the ground in the spring or fall, 1-2 inches deep and 12-18 inches apart. The soil should be loosened to a depth of 8-10 inches to allow for proper root growth. Sowing in clusters of 3-5 seeds can help ensure successful germination.

Cornflower flowers can be harvested in mid-summer, about 60-70 days after sowing, when the blooms are fully open and the color has reached its peak intensity. The stems should be cut off at the base, leaving about an inch of stem attached to the flower. Harvesting in the morning, after dew has evaporated, can help prevent moisture from accumulating and reducing the flower's vase life.

Cornflower is susceptible to several pests, including aphids, caterpillars, and root-knot nematodes. It is also vulnerable to diseases such as powdery mildew, leaf spot, and downy mildew, which can be caused by fungal pathogens like Podosphaera xanthii and Mycosphaerella tassiana.

What are the botanical aspects of Centaurea cyanus?

Centaurea cyanus is an annual herbaceous plant, reaching 1-3 ft in height, with simple, alternate leaves and a solitary flower head with tubular florets and ligulate florets.

Centaurea cyanus belongs to the family Asteraceae, subfamily Carduoideae, tribe Cardueae, subtribe Centaureinae, with approximately 600-700 species in the genus Centaurea. Its botanical name is derived from the Latin "centaurea," meaning "heavenly."

Several variants of Centaurea cyanus exist, including "Jubilee Blue," "Snowtop," and "Frost and Flame," which exhibit variations in flower color and petal shape. These variants have been developed through selective breeding for desirable traits.

Centaurea cyanus has a widespread geographical distribution across Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in dry, sandy areas, and can tolerate a range of soil types and conditions.
