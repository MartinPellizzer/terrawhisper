Durio zibethinus

What's the taxonomical classification of Durio zibethinus?

Durio zibethinus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Malvales. It is a member of the family Malvaceae, which falls under the genus Durio, and is specifically identified by the species name zibethinus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Durio zibethinus has large, woody, evergreen trees characterized by a conical crown, a thick trunk covered in greyish-brown bark, and pinnately compound leaves that are oblong or elliptic in shape. The foliage typically features a dark green upper surface and a more glaucous, silvery underside. Its reproductive morphology includes large, fleshy flowers that emerge from the trunk or older branches, often displaying a pale yellow or cream color. The most distinctive feature is the large, oval fruit protected by a thick rind covered in sharp, conical spines. Inside this protective shell, the seeds are surrounded by a rich, custard-like aril that varies in color from pale yellow to deep orange.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically within the Malay Archipelago which includes Malaysia, Indonesia, and the Philippines. Its natural habitat is primarily concentrated in the lowlands and foothills of these humid, equatorial islands. While it originates in these wild forest environments, it has been widely introduced to other tropical zones through cultivation. You can now find organized plantations and orchards in various parts of mainland Southeast Asia and parts of Oceania. This widespread distribution is driven by the plant's requirement for consistent warmth and high rainfall.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (+)-(R)-de-O-methyllasiodiplodin, fraxidin, methyl 27-O-cis-caffeoylcylicodiscate, methyl 27-O-trans-caffeoylcylicodiscate, and threo-carolignan E. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their unique biological activities and structural properties. The presence of these various metabolites can influence the overall pharmacological potential of the plant species. Researchers analyze these complex molecules to better understand their potential therapeutic applications in medicine. Detailed chemical profiling of these substances provides insight into the plant's natural defense mechanisms and biochemical makeup.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical exploration of a wood bark extract from Durio zibethinus afforded two new triterpenoids, methyl 27-O-trans-caffeoylcylicodiscate (1) and methyl 27-O-cis-caffeoylcylicodiscate (2). The analysis also revealed a new phenolic, 1,2-diarylpropane-3-ol (3). Furthermore, the extract contained seven known compounds, including fraxidin, eucryphin, boehmenan, threo-carolignan E, (-)-(3R,4S)-4-hydroxymellein, methyl protocatechuate, and (+)-(R)-de-O-methyllasiodiplodin (4). The structures of these compounds were determined using spectroscopic data.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat various fungal diseases including phytophthora palmivoa which causes foot rot, rhizopus serious fruit rot, and pythium fruit rot. These pathogens primarily attack the root systems and the developing fruit of the tree. Additionally, the plant must be protected from durian leaf blight caused by colletotrichum. Cercospora leaf spot also represents a common ailment that affects the health of the foliage. Managing these diverse infections is essential for maintaining a productive durian crop.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Phytophthora palmivora (phytophthora foot rot)
  • Rhizopus serious fruit rot
  • Pythium fruit rot
  • Durian leaf blight (colletotrichum)
  • Cercospora leaf spot

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause gastrointestinal distress including nausea, bloating, gas, indigestion, heartburn, and weight gain. The high sugar and fat content in the fruit often triggers these digestive issues when consumed in large quantities. Many people experience significant bloating and gas shortly after eating the flesh. Frequent consumption may also lead to persistent indigestion or heartburn due to the fruit's richness. Additionally, the high caloric density of the fruit can contribute to unintentional weight gain if eaten regularly.
