Pteridium aquilinum

What's the taxonomical classification of Pteridium aquilinum?

Pteridium aquilinum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Polypodiinae. Following the order Polypodiales, the plant is further categorized into the family Dennstaedtiaceae. Finally, its taxonomic identity is completed by its assignment to the genus Pteridium and its specific designation as the species aquilinum.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Pteridium aquilinum contains various bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, tannins, phenolic acids, and various terpenoids. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's diverse biological activities, such as antioxidant and antimicrobial properties. Research has identified several specific compounds like quercetin and kaempferol that may influence cellular health. However, the plant also produces ptaquiloside, a potent norsesquiterpene glycoside that is known for its carcinogenic and cytotoxic effects. Because of this high concentration of toxic compounds, careful consideration must be given to its potential medicinal use.

What are the therapeutic actions of Pteridium aquilinum?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat intestinal parasites, specifically tapeworms, roundworms, and various other helminths. The active compounds in the fern act as an anthelmintic to expel worms from the digestive tract. Historically, it was a common remedy for many different types of parasitic infections in humans and animals. However, the plant contains thiaminase, an enzyme that can destroy vitamin B1 in the body if consumed in large amounts. Due to this toxicity, modern medical practice rarely recommends its use for treating disease.
