To identify Arabian Coffee (Coffea arabica) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Coffea arabica is the scientific name for Arabian Coffee, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by various synonyms such as Coffea arabica L. and Coffea canephora, though the latter refers to a different species. Common names include Arabica coffee, true coffee, and African coffee. Regional and historical names reflect its cultural significance across the Arabian Peninsula and beyond.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Arabian Coffee (Coffea arabica) can be distinguished by its elliptical, glossy leaves with prominent veins, opposite arrangement, and red or purple flowers. Its fruit is a smooth, red berry containing two seeds. These features differentiate it from similar species like Coffea canephora, which has smaller, less glossy leaves and a more rugged fruit surface.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Arabian Coffee, or Coffea arabica, is native to the highlands of Ethiopia and Sudan. It thrives in tropical climates with high humidity and consistent rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils and is typically found between 500 and 2,000 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Arabian Coffee (Coffea arabica) blooms during the spring months, typically between March and May. Seed formation occurs in the summer, around June to August. Harvesting takes place in the fall, from September to November, when the cherries turn fully red, indicating maturity for collection.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Arabian Coffee (Coffea arabica) is closely related to other Coffea species within the Rubiaceae family. It shares key morphological and genetic traits with species like Coffea canephora and Coffea liberica. Its phylogenetic position highlights its evolutionary ties to other tropical coffee species.
