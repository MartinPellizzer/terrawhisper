Crataegus sanguinea

What's the taxonomical classification of Crataegus sanguinea?

Crataegus sanguinea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Rosales, it is a member of the family Rosaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Crataegus, and its specific species name, sanguinea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crataegus sanguinea has a deciduous shrub or small tree form characterized by densely branched, thorny stems and small, oval-shaped leaves with serrated margins.

The foliage is typically dark green during the growing season and turns a reddish hue in the autumn. Small, white, five-petaled flowers emerge in clusters during the spring months to attract pollinators. These blossoms eventually give way to small, fleshy red drupes that serve as a food source for various bird species.

The bark on older branches becomes rough and develops a fissured texture over time.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Crataegus sanguinea contains bioactive compounds in its leaves, flowers, and fruits that are utilized for their medicinal properties. The berries are often processed into syrups or tinctures to support cardiovascular health and improve circulation. Extracts from the leaves and flowers are frequently studied for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. These various plant components are traditionally used to manage symptoms related to heart palpitations and blood pressure. Scientific interest continues to grow regarding the specific flavonoids found within these different tissues.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive substances including flavonoids, proanthocyanidins, oligomeric procyanidins, flavonols, triterpenoids, and alkaloids.

These chemical constituents work together to provide various pharmacological benefits, particularly regarding cardiovascular support. The proanthocyanidins and oligomeric procyanidins are highly valued for their potent antioxidant properties. Additionally, the presence of specific flavonols and triterpenoids contributes to the plant's ability to mitigate oxidative stress in tissues.

Alkaloids also play a role in the complex chemical profile that defines the medicinal potential of this species.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Crataegus sanguinea targets phosphodiesterase enzymes, voltage-gated calcium channels, and various adrenergic receptors to exert its cardiovascular effects. The bioactive flavonoids and oligomeric procyanidins in the plant inhibit phosphodiesterase, which increases intracellular cyclic AMP levels to promote vasodilation. Additionally, the extract interacts with L-type calcium channels to modulate myocardial contractility and vascular tone. These polyphenolic compounds also show affinity for alpha-adrenergic receptors, contributing to improved coronary blood flow. By modulating these specific protein structures, the plant helps reduce peripheral resistance and improves overall cardiac efficiency.

What are the therapeutic actions of Crataegus sanguinea?

Crataegus sanguinea has cardiotonic, vasodilator, antispasmodic, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and astringent therapeutic actions that primarily support cardiovascular and digestive health.

Its cardiotonic properties help strengthen heart muscle contractions, while its vasodilator effects improve blood flow by relaxing vessel walls. The plant also functions as an antispasmodic to reduce muscle spasms and provides antioxidant protection against cellular oxidative stress. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory capabilities help mitigate systemic swelling and discomfort.

Finally, its astringent qualities assist in tightening tissues and managing digestive secretions.

What are the herbal preparations of Crataegus sanguinea?

Crataegus sanguinea is prepared as dried leaf and flower tinctures, standardized extracts, infusions, decoctions, and various topical ointments. These herbal preparations are most commonly administered through oral tinctures to ensure the efficient absorption of bioactive flavonoids and oligomeric procyanidins. Liquid extracts and standardized capsules are frequently utilized in clinical settings to maintain consistent dosages of active compounds. Teas or infusions made from the dried plant material offer a milder method for daily consumption in traditional practices. Additionally, the plant may be incorporated into topical formulations to utilize its specific phytochemical properties for external applications.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Crataegus sanguinea can cause side effects such as dizziness, nausea, palpitations, and potential interactions with heart medications like digoxin or blood pressure drugs. While generally considered safe for many, some individuals may experience lightheadedness or gastrointestinal discomfort after consumption. It is particularly important to monitor blood pressure, as the plant may enhance the effects of antihypertensive treatments. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid use due to a lack of sufficient clinical safety data. Consulting a healthcare professional is necessary to prevent dangerous drops in heart rate or blood pressure.
