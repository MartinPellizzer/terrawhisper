Paullinia pinnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Paullinia pinnata?

Paullinia pinnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical order of Sapindales, it is a member of the family Sapindaceae. The plant is specifically identified by its genus, Paullinia, and its species designation, pinnata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Paullinia pinnata has pinnately compound leaves characterized by multiple pairs of leaflets arranged along a central rachis. Each individual leaflet is typically elliptical or oblong in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. The plant exhibits a woody structure with stems that support the complex foliage arrangement. Small flowers are produced in clusters, often emerging from the leaf axils or terminal ends. The overall architecture of the foliage provides a dense, textured appearance to the shrub or small tree.

What are the pharmacological activities of Paullinia pinnata?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce pain and suppress inflammatory responses in various biological models. The plant also demonstrates significant potential in inhibiting the growth of specific bacterial strains. Additionally, its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage. Finally, certain extracts exhibit cytotoxic effects that suggest a capacity for targeting cancer cells.

As discussed in a study, aqueous (AEPP) and methanol (MEPP) extracts of Paulliniia pinnata leaves demonstrate significant anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects. When administered at 100, 200, and 300 mg/kg/day, these extracts reduced inflammation and pain sensation in the ankle and paw by 20-98% (p < 0.001). In vitro, extracts tested at 10, 30, and 100 μg/ml reduced nitric oxide production in stimulated macrophages by 47-88%, while also inhibiting TNF-α by 35-68% and IL-1β by 31-36%. AEPP significantly increased glutathione levels (p < 0.05) and both extracts reduced MDA production in serum and spinal cord (p < 0.001). Furthermore, MEPP showed a concentration-dependent affinity for Sigma 2 receptors with an IC50 of 50 μg/ml. These extracts were also evaluated against more than 45 molecular targets in competitive binding assays.
