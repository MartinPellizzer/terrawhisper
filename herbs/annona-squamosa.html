Annona squamosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Annona squamosa?

Annona squamosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls within the order Magnoliales. The plant is a member of the family Annonaceae and is further classified into the genus Annona, with its specific designation being the species squamosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Annona squamosa has a small to medium-sized tree structure characterized by a spreading crown, woody branches, and elliptical, dull green leaves that measure approximately 5 to 15 centimeters in length. The leaves are arranged alternately along the stems and possess a smooth, entire margin. Its flowers are typically solitary and emerge from the axils, displaying fleshy, yellowish-green petals. The fruit is a large, globose aggregate berry covered in distinctive, knobby protuberances that resemble scales. These scales separate as the fruit matures to reveal a white, pulpy interior filled with numerous black seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to tropical regions of the Americas, specifically ranging from Mexico through Central America and into parts of northern South America. It has since been widely naturalized across many other tropical and subtropical zones throughout the world. Current distributions include significant populations in South and Southeast Asia, as well as various islands in the Pacific and Indian Oceans. Because it thrives in warm, humid climates, it is commonly cultivated in many countries within the tropical belt. The species continues to spread into new areas where frost is minimal and consistent warmth is available.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Annona squamosa contains medicinal compounds found in its leaves, seeds, bark, roots, and fruit. The leaves are frequently used in decoctions to treat skin diseases, fever, and inflammation. Seeds are often crushed to create extracts that function as potent anthelmintics to expel intestinal worms. The bark and roots are utilized in various traditional practices to manage digestive issues and alleviate pain. Additionally, the fruit is consumed for its nutritional benefits and to support overall digestive health.

What are the pharmacological activities of Annona squamosa?

This plant has anti-tumor, anti-oxidant, anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, and anti-microbial pharmacological activities. Its unique phytochemical profile allows it to inhibit the growth of various cancer cell lines through induced apoptosis. The presence of bioactive compounds also helps neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within the body. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates an ability to regulate blood glucose levels and mitigate systemic inflammation. These properties, combined with its ability to combat bacterial and fungal pathogens, make it a significant subject for medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, eleven compounds were obtained from the 95% EtOH extract of Annona squamosa to evaluate their anti-tumor activity using the SRB method. The isolated structures included annosquamosin C(1), 15, 16-epoxy-17-hydroxy-ent-kau-ran-19-oiic acid(2), 16,17-dihydroxy-ent-kau-ran-19-oiic acid(3), annosquamosin A(4), ent-kau-r-16-en-19-oiic acid(5), 19-nor-ent-kauran4-ol-17-oiic acid(6), 16-hydroxy ent-kau ran-19-oiic acid(7), ent-15beta-hydroxy-kau-r-16-en-19-oiic acid(8), annosquamosin B(9), ent-16beta, 17-dihydroxykauran-19-al(10), and 16, 17-dihydroxy-ent-kauran-19-oiic acid methyl ester(11). Compounds 1,2,3,5, and 9 demonstrated inhibitory activities against 95-D lung cancer cells, with compound 5 showing the strongest effect at an IC50 value of 7.78 micromol x L(-1). Additionally, compounds 2, 5, and 9 showed inhibitory activities against A2780 ovarian cancer cells. Within this group, compounds 2 and 9 exhibited strong effects with IC50 values of 0.89 and 3.10 micromol x L(-1), respectively.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains anno nacceous acetogenins (ACGs), anonaine, bullatacin, charantin, and flavonoids which provide various therapeutic benefits. The presence of ACGs is particularly significant due to their potent cytotoxic properties against certain cancer cell lines. Within this chemical profile, anonaine and bullatacin contribute to the plant's complex biological activities and neuroprotective potential. Additionally, the compound charantin is well-known for its hypoglycemic effects in managing blood sugar levels. Finally, the diverse array of flavonoids offers antioxidant support that helps protect cells from oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, Annona squamosa seed oil (ASSO) displays good antitumoer activity against a variety of tumor cells. When prepared as nanoparticles (ASSO-NPs) with a uniform size of less than 200 nm, the efficacy is significantly enhanced. In 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, the ASSO-NPs group at 15 mg/kg achieved a tumor inhibition rate (TIR) of 69.8%. This was greater than the ASSO solution which showed a 52.7% TIR at 135 mg/kg (p < 0.05). Furthermore, the relative tumor-targeting index (RTTI) of (ACGs + ASSO)-NPs was 1.47-fold that of ACGs delivered alone. These ASSO-NPs also showed good storage stability at 25 ± 2 °C.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cancer, diabetes, malaria, peptic ulcer, and various infectious diseases through its diverse bioactive compounds. Researchers have investigated its ability to inhibit tumor growth and help regulate blood glucose levels in diabetic patients. The leaves and fruit also contain properties that may combat malaria parasites and reduce inflammation associated with peptic ulcers. Additionally, its antimicrobial qualities assist in managing several types of infectious diseases. These medicinal applications rely heavily on the presence of acetogenins and other phytochemicals found within the plant.

As discussed in a study, Annona squamosa L. is an evergreen plant located in tropical and subtropical regions that has been found to have anticancer activities. Phytochemistry investigations on the species have identified main constituents including 88 ACGs, 33 DITs, 19 ALKs, and 13 CPs. These specific constituents consist of annonaceous acetogenins, diterpenes, alkaloids, and cyclopeptide. Research has explored various medicinal uses for the different portions of Annona squamosa, including its role as a tonic, apophlegmatisant, cool medicine, abortient, and heart sedative. This plant is also noted for having anti-oxidant, antidiaabetic, antihypertensive, hepatoprotective, antiparasitic, antimalarial, insecticidal, microbiocidal, and molluscicidal activities.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the bark and roots, an infusion by steeping the leaves in hot water, a tincture through alcohol extraction, a powder from dried fruit or leaves, a juice from the fresh pulp, or a concentrated extract. Each method utilizes different parts of the plant to target specific medicinal purposes. A decoction is often used for its more robust woody components, while an infusion provides a gentler way to consume leaf properties. Tinctures and extracts offer highly concentrated forms of the plant's active compounds for long-term storage. The powder and juice provide more direct ways to ingest the nutrients and alkaloids found within the fruit and foliage.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe adverse reactions including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dizziness, neurotoxicity, and abdominal pain if ingested improperly. The presence of certain alkaloids and acetogenins within the fruit and seeds may trigger significant gastrointestinal distress. Individuals who consume large quantities or specific parts of the plant often report intense abdominal pain and persistent vomiting. Furthermore, the chemical compounds in Annona squamosa are linked to neurotoxicity, which can impact the central nervous system. Such neurological complications may manifest as dizziness or more serious cognitive impairments.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Dizziness
  • Neurotoxicity
  • Abdominal pain
