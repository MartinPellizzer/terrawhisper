Ficus altissima

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus altissima?

Ficus altissima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its classification through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed under the order Rosales. The plant is a member of the family Moraceae and is further identified by its genus, Ficus, and its specific species, altissima.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Ficus altissima contains various bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, tannins, saponins, and alkaloids that serve as the basis for its medicinal properties. These chemical constituents are primarily concentrated within the leaves and latex of the plant. The presence of tannins provides significant astringent and antimicrobial qualities used in traditional topical treatments. Additionally, the alkaloids and saponins contribute to its potential anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activities. Researchers study these specific compounds to understand how they might interact with human physiological processes.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ficus altissima?

Ficus altissima has the ability to improve indoor air quality by absorbing various airborne toxins such as formaldehyde, benzene, and toluene through its large leaves. This plant functions as a natural air purifier that helps to mitigate the presence of volatile organic compounds commonly found in household environments. By releasing moisture into the surrounding atmosphere through transpiration, it also assists in increasing local humidity levels. This natural humidification process can provide relief for individuals suffering from dry respiratory passages or skin irritation. Consequently, keeping this plant in a living space supports a healthier breathing environment.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin conditions such as eczema, psoriasis, and various types of fungal infections. The milky latex extracted from the leaves and stems contains bioactive compounds that serve as natural anti-inflammatory agents. These medicinal properties allow the sap to soothe irritated skin and reduce redness associated with dermatological issues. Some traditional practices also involve using extracts to manage wound healing and minor bacterial ailments. Researchers continue to study these botanical elements to understand their specific biochemical impact on human tissue.

What are the herbal preparations of Ficus altissima?

Ficus altissima is prepared primarily as a liquid decoction or a topical poultice utilizing the plant's latex and leaf extracts. The milky sap, known as latex, is often applied directly to the skin to treat dermatological issues or fungal infections. Leaves can be boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal cleansing or to alleviate digestive discomfort. Some traditional practices involve crushing the fresh foliage into a paste for use as a cooling compress on inflamed areas. These various preparations rely on the plant's concentrated phytochemicals to deliver therapeutic benefits.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Ficus altissima can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive distress if the milky sap is ingested or touches the body. The white latex released from broken leaves or stems contains proteolytic enzymes that may trigger dermatitis or itching upon contact with the skin. If a person accidentally swallows this sap, it can lead to nausea, vomiting, or irritation of the mouth and throat. Pets that chew on the plant are also at risk for significant gastrointestinal upset and inflammation. While the plant is generally safe to display, careful handling is necessary to avoid these physical complications.
