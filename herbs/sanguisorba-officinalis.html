Scientific and Botanical Profile

Sanguisorba, with botanical name Sanguisorba officinalis, is a member of the Rosaceae family, commonly known as the rose family.

Native to Europe, Asia, and North America, this plant is also referred to as Bloodwort, Blusher, Bleeding Heart, Bloodroot, Redroot, Common Sanguisorba, Lungwort, Common Burnet, Blushweed, Sangisorba, Sangusorba, Red Baneberry, and Official Sanguisorba. Morphologically, it is a perennial herb with erect, branched stems that are often reddish or purplish in color, and it produces dense clusters of small, pinkish-white flowers arranged in a panicle.

The plant has pinnately compound leaves with serrated margins, and its roots are characterized by their reddish hue and medicinal properties, which have contributed to its historical use in herbal medicine.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Sanguisorba officinalis has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms including fresh plant, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves or roots in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the roots for a longer period. It can also be used topically as an infusion or poultice for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried herb per cup of tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

For optimal results, it is advised to use it no more than 3 times daily for a short duration, staying conservative to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Sanguisorba officinalis can be considered safe for short-term use in adults when used as directed, but it may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals.

It is generally not recommended for long-term use due to its potential to cause liver toxicity, especially when consumed in large quantities or over extended periods. There are limited studies on its interactions with medications, but it is advisable to avoid combining it with anticoagulants or drugs that affect liver function due to the risk of enhanced effects or toxicity. During pregnancy and breastfeeding, sanguisorba officinalis is not recommended due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, always follow recommended dosages, consult a qualified healthcare professional, and discontinue use if any adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Sanguisorba officinalis grows best in well-drained, loamy soil that is rich in organic matter, preferring full sun to partial shade for optimal growth.

It thrives in moist environments but requires good drainage to prevent root rot, making it suitable for woodland gardens or wet meadows. Regular watering during the growing season is essential, though it becomes more drought-tolerant once established. For care and maintenance, regular weeding and occasional pruning can promote healthy growth and prevent overcrowding, while mulching helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Harvesting should occur in late summer to early autumn when the plant's leaves and roots are most potent, using a sharp knife or shears to cut the stems and roots cleanly.

To preserve its medicinal potency, the harvested material should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain its active compounds.
