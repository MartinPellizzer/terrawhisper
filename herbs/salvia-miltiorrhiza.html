Salvia miltiorrhiza

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvial miltiorrhiza has a perennial rootstock that produces upright, square-shaped stems reaching heights of 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are primarily basal and heart-shaped with serrated margins, while the upper leaves are smaller and more lanceolate. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal spikes featuring tubular, two-lipped flowers that typically range in color from pale blue to lavender. The calyx is often hairy and serves to protect the developing floral structures throughout the growing season. These morphological traits collectively distinguish the species within the Lamiaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a specific range within the southeastern United States, primarily concentrated in the coastal plain regions of Georgia, South Carolina, North Carolina, and Florida. It thrives in the sandy soils and open woodlands characteristic of these subtropical environments. Within these states, it is often found in pine flatwoods and along the edges of maritime forests. The species shows a preference for habitats that receive significant sunlight and maintain moderate moisture levels.
