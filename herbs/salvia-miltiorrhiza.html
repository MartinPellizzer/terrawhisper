Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Red Sage, or Salvia miltiorrhiza, is a perennial herb native to China and widely used in traditional Chinese medicine for its purported ability to improve circulation, reduce inflammation, and support cardiovascular health.

What is Red Sage (Salvia Miltiorrhiza)?

Red Sage, or Salvia miltiorrhiza, is a traditional Chinese herb native to Asia, known for its roots that are widely used in herbal medicine for their purported cardiovascular, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Red sage is Salvia miltiorrhiza. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Salvia, and species Salvia miltiorrhiza. It is commonly known as Red sage in China, and as Korean red sage in Korea. It is also known as Dan Shen in traditional Chinese medicine. The synonyms for this plant include Salvia miltiorrhiza, Salvia haematodes, and Salvia miltiorrhiza var. miltiorrhiza.

Plant Description and Morphology

Red sage is a perennial herb that grows to a height of 30-100 cm, with a spreading, upright stem that is usually hairy.

Its leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate in shape, with serrated edges and a dark green upper surface. The flowers are small, clustered in whorls along the stem, and have a distinctive purple-red color with a lighter pinkish base. The root system consists of a thick, fleshy taproot that is the main source of the medicinal material.

The plant has a mild, earthy aroma, with a slightly bitter taste and a rough, fibrous texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) is native to China, particularly thriving in the mountainous regions of eastern and central China, and is also found in parts of Korea and Japan.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots and leaves of Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) are the primary plant parts used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Red Sage contains active compounds such as tanshinones, lucidin, and salvianolic acids, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic effects.

Pharmacological Properties

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and cardioprotective effects, primarily due to its active compounds such as tanshinone I and II, and salvianolic acid.

Mechanisms of Action

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) exerts its pharmacological effects primarily through the activation of multiple signaling pathways, including the regulation of oxidative stress, inflammation, and apoptosis, as well as the modulation of various enzymes and transcription factors involved in cellular processes.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to promote blood circulation, reduce inflammation, and support cardiovascular health, with indications including menstrual disorders, cardiovascular diseases, and neurodegenerative conditions.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) is commonly prepared as a dried root, used in decoctions, powdered form, or as an extract, with specific methods varying based on traditional Chinese medicine practices and modern pharmaceutical applications.

Dosage and Administration

The typical recommended dosage of Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) for general use ranges from 3 to 9 grams per day, typically divided into two to three doses, with the exact amount depending on the form of the herb (e.g., dried root, tincture, or extract) and the individual's health condition.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) is generally considered safe, but it may cause gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by individuals with bleeding disorders.

Drug Interactions

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) may interact with anticoagulant medications, such as warfarin, by enhancing their effects and increasing the risk of bleeding, and it may also affect the metabolism of certain drugs through its influence on liver enzymes.

Toxicity and Precautions

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interactions with certain medications, and should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) is typically cultivated in well-drained soil with partial shade, requiring consistent moisture and moderate temperatures, and is harvested primarily from its roots after 2-3 years of growth.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Red Sage involve methods such as soil washing, alcohol extraction, and water extraction to obtain active compounds like tanshinone and salvianolic acid, which are critical for its medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Red Sage (Salvia miltiorrhiza) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has demonstrated that Red Sage contains compounds such as tanshinone IIA and salvianolic acid B, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and cardiovascular protective effects, supporting its traditional use in promoting blood circulation and reducing oxidative stress.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Red Sage has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for centuries to promote blood circulation, treat menstrual disorders, and support cardiovascular health.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Red Sage is classified as a traditional herbal medicine and is subject to varying regulatory frameworks, with some regions requiring specific licensing or quality control standards for its production and sale.
