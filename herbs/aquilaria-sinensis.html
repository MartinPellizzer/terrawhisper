Aquilaria sinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Aquilaria sinensis?

Aquilaria sinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Malvales and is a member of the family Thymelaeaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Aquilaria, and its unique species name, sinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aquilaria sinensis has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and features opposite, ovate-lanceolate leaves with serrated margins. The foliage is characterized by a bright green color and a slightly fleshy texture. Small, yellowish-white flowers emerge in terminal clusters, possessing a tubular structure that opens into five distinct lobes. These blossoms are accompanied by small, inconspicuous bracts located near the base of the inflorescence. The plant eventually produces small, dry fruits that facilitate seed dispersal.

What are the pharmacological activities of Aquilaria sinensis?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antitumor pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms and neutralize harmful free radicals in the body. The plant also functions to reduce swelling and pain by modulating inflammatory responses at a cellular level. Furthermore, research suggests that its chemical constituents possess significant potential for suppressing tumor progression and cell proliferation. These diverse therapeutic properties make the species a subject of interest for natural drug development.

As discussed in a study, Aquilaria sineensis (Lour.) Gilg serves as a traditional medicinal herb and valuable natural spice in China with many significant pharmacological effects. The resinous heartwood acquired from wounded A. sineensis trees is known as agarwood and is widely used in pharmaceuticals due to its excellent medicinal value. Antioxidant tests demonstrated that essential oils from agarwood and A. sineensis possessed antioxidant capacities when compared with butylated hydroxytoluene and vitamin E. Additionally, an antibacterial activity test showed that the inhibition effect of the essential oil was better against Gram-positive bacteria than against Gram-negative bacteria.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds beta-sitosterol, cedrol, citral, geraniol, and nerolidol. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's diverse therapeutic properties and aromatic profile. Beta-sitosterol is frequently recognized for its potential anti-inflammatory and cholesterol-lowering effects. The terpenoids citral, geraniol, and nerolidol provide significant antimicrobial and antioxidant activities. Additionally, the presence of cedrol offers calming properties that can assist in reducing stress. Together, these compounds form a complex bioactive matrix used in various traditional and modern medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, the leaves of Aquilaria sinensis were analyzed to identify its chemical constituents through solvent extraction and chromatographic techniques. Eleven compounds were successfully obtained and identified from the plant. These specific compounds include beta-sitosterol (1), hexacosaic acid (2), cryptotanshinone (3), 2alpha-hydroxyursane (4), dihydrotanshinone I (5), tanshinone I (6), tanshinone II(A) (7), 2alpha-hydroxyursolic acid (8), p-hydroxybenzoic acid (9), hydroquinone (10), and daucosterol (11). All of these identified compounds except for compound 9 were isolated from the leaves of A. sinensis for the first time.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach pain, and liver toxicity in individuals who ingest it. These gastrointestinal issues often manifest as significant discomfort following consumption. Beyond digestive distress, the chemical constituents of the plant pose a serious risk to hepatic health. Severe cases of exposure may lead to measurable liver toxicity and organ damage. Monitoring for these specific symptoms is essential when considering the risks associated with this species.
