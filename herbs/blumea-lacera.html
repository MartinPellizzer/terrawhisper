Blumea lacera

What's the taxonomical classification of Blumea lacera?

Blumea lacera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant follows the biological lineage of the order Asterales. It is further categorized under the family Asteraceae, a large group of flowering plants, and specifically falls within the genus Blumea. Ultimately, its unique taxonomic identity is defined by its specific designation as the species lacera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Blumea lacera has lanceolate to ovate leaves with deeply serrated or lacerate margins, a woody stem, and small, yellowish-white flower heads arranged in terminal corymbs. The leaf surfaces are often covered in a dense layer of fine, white tomentum that gives the foliage a fuzzy appearance. Its stems exhibit a branching habit that supports multiple inflorescences throughout the growing season. The plant typically features a perennial life cycle with a robust root system to support its upright structure. Small, tubular florets compose the individual heads, characteristic of the Asteraceae family.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds α-solanine, α-solasonine, solasodine, tomatidine HCl, and kaempferol. These specific alkaloids and flavonoids are secondary metabolites that play a role in the plant's chemical defense systems. While compounds like α-solanine and α-solasonine are known steroidal glycoalkaloids, solasodine serves as a foundational structural component within them. The presence of tomatidine HCl further characterizes the unique chemical profile of the species. Additionally, the flavonoid kaempferol contributes antioxidant properties to the plant's overall composition.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extract of Blumea lacera led to the isolation of a new steroidal glycalkaloid known as SGA 1. This compound is structurally identified as (25R)-3β-{O-β-D-glucopyranosyl-(1 → 4)-O-α-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1 → 4)-[O-α-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1 → 2)]-α-L-rhamnopyranosyl}-22αN-spirosol-5-ene. SGA 1 demonstrated significant cytotoxic activity against human cancer cell lines, specifically showing an IC50 of 2.62 µM against MCF-7 cells. Furthermore, the compound displayed the highest apoptotic potential with a value of 32% AV+/PI- on MCF-7 cells. The study also noted that SGA 1 has a slight but significant cell cycle arresting effect.
