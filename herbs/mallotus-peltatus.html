Mallotus peltatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Mallotus peltatus?

Mallotus peltatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Malpighiales. The plant is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae, specifically positioned within the genus Mallotus, and is identified by the species name peltatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mallotus peltatus has woody stems with simple, alternate leaves that are characterized by a peltate attachment where the petiole connects to the underside of the leaf blade rather than the margin. The leaf blades are typically ovate or elliptic in shape and often feature a somewhat leathery texture. The margins of the foliage are generally entire, though they may occasionally exhibit slight undulations. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in clusters, eventually developing into small, fleshy fruits. These morphological traits distinguish the species within its specific ecological niche.

Localized populations are often determined by the availability of consistent rainfall and specific soil moisture levels found in these equatorial climates. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains ursolic acid, which is a potent pentacyclic triterpenoid known for its diverse therapeutic properties. Beyond this primary compound, the plant also houses various flavonoids and phenolic acids that contribute to its overall medicinal profile. These bioactive substances work synergistically to provide significant anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects within the body. Research suggests that these chemical constituents may also support metabolic health and help protect cells from oxidative stress. Consequently, the complex chemical makeup of the plant makes it a valuable subject for pharmacological study.

As discussed in a study, Mallotus peltatus contains an active fraction A and isolated ursolic acid that exhibit potent antiherpesevirus activity. Against HSV-1, fraction A and ursolic acid showed an EC(50) of 7.8 and 5.5 μg/ml with an SI of 22.3 and 20, respectively. For HSV-2, the EC(50) values were 8.2 and 5.8 μg/ml with SI values of 21.2 and 18.97. Both fraction A and 10 μg/ml of isolated ursolic acid inhibited plaque formation of HSV-1 and HSV-2 at more than 80% levels. The anti-HSV activity of these compounds is highest at 2-5 h post-infection, likely by inhibiting multiplication during the 0-5 h post-infection period.

    What health problems is this plant used for?

    This plant is used for treating infections caused by the herpes simplex virus, specifically targeting both HSV-1 and HSV-2. Researchers have investigated its bioactive compounds for their potential antiviral properties against these specific strains. By applying extracts from the plant, it is possible to inhibit the replication of the virus responsible for oral and genital lesions. The phytochemical profile of Mallotus peltatus suggests it may offer a natural approach to managing the symptoms of these viral outbreaks. This makes the species a subject of interest in the search for new therapeutic agents against herpes simplex virus.

    As discussed in a study, the methanolic crude extract of Mallotus peltatus possessed weak anti-HSV activity, whereas active fraction A and isolated ursolic acid from fraction A exhibited potent activity against HSV-1 with EC(50) values of 7.8 and 5.5 μg/ml and SI values of 22.3 and 20. These components also showed activity against HSV-2 with EC(50) values of 8.2 and 5.8 μg/ml and SI values of 21.2 and 18.97. Both fraction A and isolated ursolic acid at 10 μg/ml inhibited plaque formation of HSV-1 and HSV-2 at more than 80% levels. The anti-HSV activity of fraction A and isolated ursolic acid is highest at 2-5 h post-infection. This study suggests that the bioactive fraction A and isolated ursolic acid of Mallotus peltatus has good anti-HSV activity, likely by inhibiting the early stage of multiplication during 0-5 h post-infection.
