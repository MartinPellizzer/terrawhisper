Piper mullesua

What's the taxonomical classification of Piper mullesua?

Piper mullesua belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Piperales. It is further categorized into the family Piperaceae and the genus Piper, eventually reaching its specific identification as the species mullesua.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Piper mullesua has woody, perennial stems that feature a climbing or scrambling growth habit with nodes and internodes clearly defined. The leaves are simple, ovate to elliptic in shape, and possess an entire margin with an acute apex. A distinct characteristic of this species is the prominent venation pattern that extends from the midrib to the leaf edges. The inflorescence consists of small, spike-like structures that typically emerge from the leaf axils. These floral spikes bear numerous tiny, inconspicuous flowers arranged along a central axis.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically occurring in parts of India, Sri Lanka, and various Southeast Asian countries. It thrives primarily in moist, shaded forest environments characterized by high humidity. Its distribution is closely tied to the lowland and sub-montane tropical rainforest ecosystems of the Indo-Malayan realm. Because it prefers warm, wet climates, it is rarely found in arid or temperate zones. Localized populations are often concentrated along riverbanks and within dense jungle understories.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds sesamin, asarinin, and fargesin. These specific lignans are known for their diverse biological activities within the human body. Sesamin is frequently studied for its potential antioxidant and cholesterol-lowering properties. Asarinin and fargesin also contribute to the plant's complex chemical profile and therapeutic potential. Together, these bioactive substances make the species a subject of interest in pharmacological research.

According to a study published by "Phytotherapy research : PTR", Piper mullesua of Manipur origin contains the major lignan sesamin. This potent antifeedant principle exhibited significant antifeedant activity and moderate growth inhibition towards 4th instar larvae of Spilarctia obliqua. No larval toxicity of sesamin could be established in topical bioassay experiments. The effective dose for 50% feeding deterrence (ED(50)) was found to be 3856 ppm. Additionally, the growth inhibition (GI(50)) was found to be 6212 ppm.
