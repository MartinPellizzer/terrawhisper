Trivalvaria costata

What's the taxonomical classification of Trivalvaria costata?

Trivalvaria costata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Magnoliales and the family Annonaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Trivalvaria, and its species name, costata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Trivalvaria costata has a creeping rhizomatous stem with narrow, lanceolate leaves that are typically arranged in a distichous pattern. The leaves are characterized by their parallel venation and acute tips. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters. The plant develops small, fleshy fruits that contain numerous tiny seeds. This species typically thrives in moist, shaded forest environments where its low-growing structure allows it to spread across the ground cover.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Bidebilines A, Bidebilines B, trivalcostaisoquinoline, trivalcostatines A-J, and urabaine. These specific secondary metabolites are identified through chemical analysis of the species. The trivalcostatines group consists of ten distinct substances labeled from A through J. Additionally, the presence of unique alkaloids like urabaine and the isoquinoline derivative trivalcostaisoquinoline characterizes its chemical profile. Researchers study these diverse molecules to understand their potential pharmacological applications.

According to a study published by "Phytochemistry", a phytochemical investigation of the twig extract of Trivalvaria costata identified ten undescribed dimeric apo rphine alkaloids known as trivalcostatines A-J. The study also identified one undescribed isoquinoline alkaloid named trivalcostaisoquinoline along with four known apo rphine alkaloids. Three of the dimeric apo rphine structures were confirmed through single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. Regarding medicinal properties, Bidebilines A and B, heteropsine, and urabaine showed α-glucosidase inhibitory activities. These specific compounds exhibited IC50 values within the range of 4.1-11 μM.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating dengue virus infection and managing diabetes through its alpha-glucosidase inhibitory activities. The extracts from the plant demonstrate significant potential in suppressing the replication of the dengue virus in biological models. Additionally, its ability to inhibit the alpha-glucosidase enzyme helps in regulating postprandial blood glucose levels. These dual pharmacological properties make it a subject of interest in traditional and modern medicine. Researchers continue to study its bioactive compounds to better understand these therapeutic mechanisms.

According to a study published by "Biochemical and biophysical research communications", the dimeric apoarpine alkaloids bidebiline A and heteropsine are isolated from Trivalvaria costata. These compounds were investigated for their antiviral activity against dengue virus (DENV) infection in Huh7 liver cells. Heteropsine was found to inhibit DENV production and infection more effectively than bidebiline A, while also exerting antiviral activity in endothelial and lung cell lines. The study shows that heteropsine suppresses DENV-induced immunopathogenesis by downregulating the expression of 4 cytokine/chemokine genes, specifically TNF-α, IL-6, RANTES, and IP-10. Additionally, heteropsine attenuates the nuclear translocation of the p65 subunit of the nuclear factor-kappaB transcription factor.
