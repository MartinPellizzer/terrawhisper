Macaranga denticulata

What's the taxonomical classification of Macaranga denticulata?

Macaranga denticulata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Macaranga and the species denticulata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Macaranga denticulata has ovate to elliptical leaves with serrated margins and a distinctively textured, somewhat rugose surface. The leaves are arranged oppositely along the woody stems and feature prominent venation patterns. The plant produces small, actinomorphic flowers that are typically white or cream-colored and clustered in inflorescences. Its fruit consists of small, fleshy drupes that develop from the ovary after successful pollination. The overall structure of the shrub is characterized by its branching habit and relatively thin, flexible stems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Macaranga denticulata?

This plant has pharmacological activities that include Zika virus inhibitory activity, anticholinesterase activity, antioxidant properties, nitric oxide inhibition, and tyrosinase inhibition. Its antioxidant capacity allows it to neutralize free radicals and mitigate oxidative stress within biological systems. The ability to inhibit nitric oxide helps in managing inflammatory responses, while its anticholinesterase activity suggests potential roles in neurological health. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates tyrosinase inhibition, which can be utilized for skin lightening or regulating melanin production. These diverse mechanisms highlight its significant potential for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, Macaranga denticulata Müll. Arg. contains six undescribed geranylated flavonoid derivatives labeled 1-6 and two known compounds 7-8. Among these, compound 4 demonstrated potent Zika virus inhibitory activity with an EC50 value of 8.72 μM. Additionally, compound 4 showed low cytotoxicity to Vero cells, as evidenced by a CC50 value exceeding 100 μM. Due to these properties, compound 4 may be developed as potential anti-ZIKV agents.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 2α-hydroxyaleuritolic acid 3-p-hydroxybenzoate, aleuritolic acid-3-acetate, maslinic acid, scopleton, and β-sitosterol. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of the species. Maslinic acid and its derivatives are often studied for their potent biological activities. Additionally, the presence of scopleton and β-sitosterol provides further therapeutic potential. Together, these compounds form a complex phytochemical makeup that characterizes the plant's medicinal properties.

As discussed in a study, nine compounds were isolated and identified from the Macaranga denticulata root. These nine compounds include gheddic acid (1), aleuritolic acid-3-acetate (2), β-sitosterol (3), stigmast-4-en-6β-ol-3-one (4), 2α-hydroxyaleuritolic acid 3-p-hydroxybenzoate (5), scopoletin (6), daucosterol (7), 2, 6-dimethoxy-1,4-benzoquinone (8) and maslinic acid (9). The isolation and purification of these constituents were achieved through silica-gel column chromatography and recrystallization. The study confirms that compounds 1-9 are obtained from this plant for the first time.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause intense skin irritation, severe stinging sensations, and localized swelling due to the presence of irritant hairs on its leaves. Upon contact, the tiny trichomes penetrate the skin and inject chemical irritants that trigger an immediate inflammatory response. This reaction often manifests as redness, itching, and persistent burning that can last for several hours or even days. In some sensitive individuals, the contact may lead to blistering or more significant dermatological distress. Careful avoidance of the foliage is necessary to prevent these painful cutaneous symptoms.
