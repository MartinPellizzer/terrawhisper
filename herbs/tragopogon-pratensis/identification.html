Botanical Identification of Common Cudweed (Tragopogon pratensis)

To identify Common Cudweed (Tragopogon pratensis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Common Cudweed, or Tragopogon pratensis, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of flowering plants, class of eudicots, order of asterales, family of asteraceae, genus tragopogon, and species pratensis. This classification places it within the daisy family, known for its composite flower heads and medicinal uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Common Cudweed is Tragopogon pratensis, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Anagallis arvensis and Taraxacum pratense. Common names such as cudweed, meadow cudweed, and yellow flowered grass are frequently used in herbal medicine. Regional and historical names vary, reflecting its widespread use in traditional practices.

Morphological Characteristics

Common Cudweed is a tufted perennial herb with a fibrous root system. It has upright, branching stems that grow up to 30 cm tall. Its basal leaves are lance-shaped with a hairy surface, while the upper leaves are narrower. The plant bears a loose, spherical inflorescence with yellow flowers that open sequentially. The fruits are small, hairless achenes with a pappus for wind dispersal.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Common Cudweed can be distinguished by its upright, leafy stems and blue-green leaves with a slightly hairy texture. It has a flowering head with yellow flowers that turn into fluffy seeds. It lacks the basal rosette of leaves seen in other similar species, and its leaves are more elongated and less lobed compared to related weeds.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Common Cudweed is native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained soils, often found in meadows and grasslands. It typically grows between 100 and 1,500 meters in elevation.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Common Cudweed blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering stage, which is ideal for harvesting its leaves and flowers for herbal use.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Common Cudweed (Tragopogon pratensis) is closely related to other members of the Asteraceae family, particularly species within the genus Tragopogon. It shares key family traits such as composite flower heads and alternate leaf arrangement. Its closest relatives include other cudweeds and goat's-beards, which are also found in similar habitats.
