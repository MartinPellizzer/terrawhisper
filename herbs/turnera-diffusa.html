Turnera diffusa

Turnera diffusa

Turnera Diffusa (Turnera diffusa) is a flowering plant native to Central and South America, commonly known as "Palo de Santa Rosa" or "Chamomile of the Andes," and it is traditionally used for its potential health benefits, particularly in improving digestive and respiratory conditions.

Botanical Identification

Turnera diffusa, also known as "Yellow Wood Sorrel," is a perennial plant native to South America. It belongs to the Malvaceae family and is characterized by heart-shaped leaves and yellow flowers. Common names include "Cup-of-Gold" and "Brazillian Sorrel," with synonyms in herbal literature often referring to it as "Turnera ulmifolia."

The following list summarize the taxonomy of plant:

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Eudicots
  • Order: Sapindales
  • Family: Rutaceae
  • Genus: Turnera
  • Species: Turnera diffusa

Active Compounds

Turnera diffusa contains key bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, coumarins, and phenolic acids. These compounds work synergistically to exert their therapeutic effects, primarily through antioxidant and anti-inflammatory mechanisms. The presence of multiple active constituents enhances its medicinal potency and supports its use in herbal medicine.

  • Flavonoid
  • Coumarin
  • Phenolic acid

Turnera diffusa Actions.

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for respiratory issues and inflammation due to its anti-inflammatory properties.

Tincture: Used for treating coughs and respiratory infections due to its expectorant properties.

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and as a general tonic due to its warming and digestive properties.

Safety Profile

Turnera diffusa is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses. However, it should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential risks. Long-term use may cause gastrointestinal issues, so it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before use.
