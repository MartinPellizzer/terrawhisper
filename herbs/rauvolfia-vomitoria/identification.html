Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Vomitoria Root is Rauvolfia vomitoria, first described by the botanist Rauwolf in the 18th century. It has several synonyms, including Strychnos vomitoria, and is known by various common names such as Vomitoria Root, Indian Snakeroot, and Kali Mandaar. It is also referred to by regional names in different parts of India and has historical names linked to its traditional uses in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Vomitoria Root (Rauvolfia vomitoria) is a climbing shrub with a fibrous root system. Its stems are woody and twining, while leaves are alternate, ovate, and have a rough texture. The inflorescence is a panicle, bearing small, white flowers with five petals. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous elliptical, dark brown seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Vomitoria Root, or Rauvolfia vomitoria, can be distinguished by its distinctive red, tubular roots and heart-shaped leaves. It is often confused with Rauvolfia serpentina, but it has a more intense red color and shorter, more compact stems. The presence of a strong, bitter odor and the specific leaf shape also aid in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Rauvolfia vomitoria is native to tropical regions of Africa, particularly West and Central Africa. It thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained, loamy soils. The plant is typically found at elevations between 200 and 800 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Vomitoria Root (Rauvolfia vomitoria) blooms from April to June, producing small white flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to September. Harvesting is typically done during the dry season, from November to February, when the roots are most developed and potent for identification and medicinal use.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Rauvolfia vomitoria is closely related to other species within the Rauvolfia genus. It shares key morphological and genetic traits with these species, reflecting its placement within the Rubiaceae family. These relationships highlight its evolutionary connections and aid in accurate botanical identification.
