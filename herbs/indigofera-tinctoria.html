Indigofera tinctoria

What's the taxonomical classification of Indigofera tinctoria?

Indigofera tinctoria belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized as Equisetopsida, which falls under the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its lineage through the order Fabales, it is a member of the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Indigofera, and its specific species name, tinctoria.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Indigofera tinctoria has pinnately compound leaves consisting of several pairs of small, oval-shaped leaflets and small, fragrant, pale yellow or white pea-like flowers arranged in axillary racemes. The plant typically grows as a perennial shrub or subshrub reaching heights of up to one meter. Its stems are woody at the base and often feature fine, spreading hairs. The leaf stalks are slender, supporting leaflets that are arranged symmetrically along the rachis. Small, oblong seed pods develop after flowering, eventually splitting to release the seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, specifically spanning across parts of India, China, and Southeast Asia. It thrives in warm climates where consistent sunlight and well-drained soils are available. While its origins are concentrated in these eastern territories, it has been introduced to various other tropical zones globally for dye production. The species typically populates lowland areas and coastal plains that match its requirement for high temperatures. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including the sesquiterpene lactone eupalitin, the flavanone hesperetin, various flavonoids, furano-flavones 1-3, and flavonol glycoside 4. These chemical constituents contribute to the plant's complex pharmacological profile. The presence of specific flavonoids and furano-flavones suggests significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory potential. Furthermore, the inclusion of hesperetin and flavonol glycoside 4 highlights its role in traditional medicinal applications. These specialized molecules work synergistically to provide therapeutic benefits.

As discussed in a study, Indigofera tinctoria provides Sumatrol as a phytochemical for therapeutic potential. Molecular docking and MD simulations showed that Sumatrol interacts with PIK3CA with a binding energy of -8.9 kcal/mol. This compound maintained stable hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions for over 30% of a 25 ns simulation. These findings support the use of such bioactive plant-derived compounds to modulate estrogen signaling.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Plasmodium falciparum infections, gastrointestinal nematodes, liver damage, liver injury, and psoriasis. Its medicinal properties are often utilized to combat malaria caused by the specific Plasmodium falciparum parasite. Research also highlights its efficacy in addressing various gastrointestinal nematodes that infect the digestive tract. Additionally, the plant contains compounds that help mitigate both liver damage and acute liver injury. Some traditional applications also focus on managing the skin symptoms associated with psoriasis.

As discussed in a study, Indigofera tinctoria showed moderate antiplasmodial activity against Plasmodium falciparum. This activity was specifically observed using the leaf ethyl acetate extract (EAE). The recorded IC50 value for the Pf 3D7 strain was between 40 and 60 μg/mL. These findings provide validity to the traditional medicinal usage of the plant.
