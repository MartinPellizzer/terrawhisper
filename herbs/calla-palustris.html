Calla palustris

What's the taxonomical classification of Calla palustris?

Calla palustris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Aliasmaetales, it is categorized under the family Araceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Calla, and its unique species designation, palustris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Calla palustris has large, arrow-shaped leaves that are dark green, glossy, and can reach up to 30 centimeters in length with prominent veining. These leaves emerge from a thick, fleshy rhizome that grows horizontally through wetland substrates. The plant produces a distinctive inflorescence consisting of a white, waxy spathe surrounding a central spadix covered in tiny yellowish flowers. This spadix is often fragrant and serves to attract various insect pollinators. During the fruiting stage, the spadix transitions into a cluster of bright red berries.

European populations range from the British Isles across to Russia and into parts of Scandinavia. Its presence in Asia extends through Siberia and reaches into parts of East Asia. How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires constant moisture and thrives in boggy, acidic soils or shallow standing water to ensure healthy growth.

You should plant it in a dedicated water garden or a container with no drainage holes to mimic its natural wetland habitat. Providing a location with full sun to partial shade will encourage the development of its characteristic white spathes. Because it spreads aggressively via rhizomes, it is best to keep the specimen contained within a lined pond or a specific garden zone.

Regular monitoring of water levels is essential to prevent the soil from drying out completely.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Calla palustris contains medicinal properties primarily located within its rhizomes and roots. These underground structures are harvested to extract compounds used in traditional folk medicine. Historically, decoctions made from the roots have been applied to treat various ailments such as skin irritations and internal inflammation. However, the plant contains high concentrations of calcium oxalate crystals which can cause severe irritation if not prepared correctly. Careful processing is essential to neutralize these toxins before any medicinal application can occur safely.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe oral irritation, a burning sensation of the mouth and throat, mucous membrane irritation, swelling of the lips and tongue, skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Ingesting any part of the plant often leads to intense discomfort in the digestive tract and mouth due to its chemical makeup. Contact with the skin may result in localized redness or itching. If the plant is swallowed, the most common reactions include significant swelling and a painful stinging feeling in the throat.
