The scientific name of Tripartite Tickseed is Bidens tripartita, first described by the botanist Linnaeus in 1753. It has no widely recognized synonyms, though it is sometimes referred to by its common name, Tickseed, due to its small, tick-like seeds. Morphological Characteristics

Tripartite Tickseed is an annual herb with a fibrous root system. Its stems are upright, branched, and covered in fine hairs. Leaves are alternate, heart-shaped, and have a rough texture. The inflorescence consists of clusters of yellow flowers with three petals. Each flower produces a three-lobed, hairy seed capsule that splits open to release small, smooth seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Tripartite Tickseed can be distinguished by its three-lobed leaves and yellow flowers with five ray florets. It differs from similar species like Bidens frondosa by having smaller, more upright flower heads and narrower leaves. The plant also has a distinctive seed structure with three prongs, aiding in identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Tripartite Tickseed is native to the southeastern United States, particularly Florida and parts of the Gulf Coast. It thrives in warm, humid climates and prefers well-drained sandy or loamy soils. It commonly grows in low-lying areas and is typically found at elevations below 200 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Tripartite Tickseed blooms from June to August, producing yellow flowers with three ray florets. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early fall, with mature seeds dispersing by wind by mid-autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering stage in late summer.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Tripartite Tickseed belongs to the genus Bidens in the family Asteraceae. Its closest relatives include other Bidens species, such as Bidens pilosa and Bidens frondosa.
