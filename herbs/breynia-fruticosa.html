Breynia fruticosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Breynia fruticosa?

Breynia fruticosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its evolutionary placement continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Phyllanthaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Breynia, and its unique species designation, fruticosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Breynia fruticosa has a woody, multi-stemmed shrubby habit characterized by small, elliptical leaves and tiny, inconspicuous flowers that lack petals. The foliage is typically dark green and arranged alternately along slender, branching stems. Small, fleshy white berries develop from the flowers and serve as a primary reproductive feature of the plant. The bark on older stems becomes somewhat rough and grayish as the plant matures. These morphological traits collectively define its compact and bushy growth form.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds arborinone, breyniin B, epibreyniin B, isoarborinol, and sulfur-containing spiroketal glycosides. These diverse chemical constituents are primarily extracted from the various parts of the shrub for pharmacological study. The presence of these unique molecules contributes to the plant's recognized bioactive properties. Researchers often examine these specific substances to understand their potential therapeutic applications in medicine. The structural complexity of these compounds makes them a significant subject in phytochemical analysis.

According to a study published by "Zhongguo Zhong yao za zhi = Zhongguo zhongyao zazhi = China journal of Chinese materia medica", seven compounds were obtained and identified from the aerial parts of Breynia fruticosa. These constituents include aviculin [(+)-isolariciresinol-9'-rhamno-pyranoside], friedelan-3beta-ol, friedelin, arborinoe, isoarborinol, 5-hydroxy-7,8,4'-trimethoxy flavone, and 2,4-dihydroxy-6-methoxy-3-methyl-acetophenone. The study utilized various chromatographic techniques for the isolation and purification of these substances. Through chemical evidence and spectral methods, the structures of these compounds were elucidated. Notably, aviculin was isolated from the Euphorbiaceae family for the first time.
