Bauhinia delavayi

What's the taxonomical classification of Bauhinia delavayi?

Bauhinia delavayi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Fabales and is a member of the family Fabaceae. Specifically, it is identified by the genus Bauhinia and is classified as the species delaayi.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, digestive issues, and respiratory ailments. Its extracts are frequently applied topically to address dermatological conditions such as inflammation or fungal growth. In traditional medicine, decoctions of the plant are consumed to alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort and stomach pains. Some practitioners also utilize the plant to manage symptoms related to coughing or bronchitis. These medicinal applications rely on the specific bioactive compounds found within its leaves and bark.

What are the herbal preparations of Bauhinia delavayi?

Bauhinia delavayi is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste derived from its various plant parts. The leaves and flowers are commonly boiled in water to create a medicinal tea for internal consumption. For external applications, the crushed plant material is often formulated into a thick paste to treat skin conditions. Some traditional practices also involve using the extract as a wash for cleansing specific areas. These different methods allow for the extraction of active compounds depending on the desired therapeutic effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Bauhinia delavayi can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive distress if ingested or handled without protection. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many species within the Bauhinia genus contain bioactive compounds that may trigger sensitivities. Contact with the plant's sap or leaves might result in localized redness or itching for individuals with sensitive skin. Ingesting parts of the plant could potentially lead to stomach upset or other gastrointestinal discomfort. It is important to consult a medical professional if any adverse symptoms occur after exposure.
