Allium ascalonicum

What's the taxonomical classification of Allium ascalonicum?

Allium ascalonicum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Asparagales and falls under the family Amaryllidaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Allium and the species name ascalonicum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Allium ascalonicum has bulbous roots topped by slender, cylindrical leaves that grow in a basal rosette. The plant produces a single, upright flowering stalk known as a scape that emerges from the center of the foliage. This scape supports a dense, spherical umbel composed of numerous small, star-shaped white or pale pink flowers. The individual tepals of the flowers are delicate and often feature a subtle translucent quality. Each bulb is typically small and characterized by a thin, papery tunic that protects the internal layers.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the western regions of North America, ranging from British Columbia in Canada down through the Pacific Northwest into California. It primarily inhabits various open habitats such as grasslands, sagebrush steppes, and rocky slopes. Within these ecosystems, it is frequently found in well-drained soils and areas with significant sunlight exposure. Its distribution is often concentrated in the interior valleys and foothills of the Cascade and Sierra Nevada mountain ranges. Small populations may also appear in higher elevations depending on local moisture availability.

What are the pharmacological activities of Allium ascalonicum?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and hypoglycemic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals, which provides significant antioxidant protection to cells. The specimen also exhibits potent antibacterial and antimicrobial properties that help inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory actions assist in reducing swelling and pain throughout the body. The hypoglycemic effects of the plant are particularly useful for managing elevated blood glucose levels.

As discussed in a study, the shallot bulb Allium ascalonicum is a bioactive component used in a Hair Rise microemulsion formulated at concentrations of 50%, 75%, and 100% v/v. This formulation significantly enhanced the proliferation of human hair follicle dermal papilla cells compared to minoxidil. It upregulates critical hair growth signaling pathways, including Wnt/β-catenin (CTNNB1), Sonic Hedgehog (SHH, SMO, GLI1), and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). The microemulsion also demonstrates potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties by reducing nitric oxide production and oxidative stress. Furthermore, it inhibits 5α-reductase (types 1-3) in both human prostate cancer cells (DU-145) and HFDPCs to promote hair growth.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of medicinal compounds including alkaloids, flavonoids, saponins, tannins, and terpenoids. The alkaloids present in the tissues often act as potent bioactive agents that interact with the nervous system. Flavonoids provide significant antioxidant properties that help protect cells from oxidative stress. Saponins and tannins contribute to the plant's ability to reduce inflammation and act as natural antimicrobial agents. Finally, the terpenoids offer additional therapeutic benefits through their various anti-inflammatory and sedative effects.

As discussed in a study, Allium ascalonicum contains a rich phytochemical profile including flavonoids, alkaloids, tannins, saponins, and terpenoids. GC-MS analysis identified 34 compounds in the ethyl acetate fraction, with major constituents being phenol, 2-methoxy-4-vinyl- at 17.77% and 2-cyclohexene-1-one at 7.99%. The essential oil revealed 10 major compounds, such as hexadecanoic acid, methyl ester at 30.77% and caryophyllene oxide at 10.82%. This essential oil demonstrated potent inhibitory activity with IC50 values of 7.93, 7.74, and 17.86 μg/mL for α-amylase, DPP-4, and PTP-1B. Additionally, both the ethyl acetate fraction and essential oil reduced fasting blood glucose by over 40% in vivo. No adverse effects were observed in acute toxicity studies conducted up to 2000 mg/kg.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating allergic rhininitis, hair loss, various bacterial infections, diabetes, and type II diabetes mellitus. Its medicinal properties help reduce inflammation in the nasal passages to soothe allergic rhininitis symptoms. The extracts are also applied topically to the scalp to address hair loss and promote growth. Furthermore, the plant possesses antimicrobial qualities effective against certain types of bacteria. Finally, its bioactive compounds assist in managing blood sugar levels associated with diabetes and type II diabetes mellitus.

    What side effects this plant can have?
