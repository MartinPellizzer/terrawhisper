Circaea mollis

What's the taxonomical classification of Circaea mollis?

Circaea mollis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Myrtyleales and into the family Onagraceae. Finally, the plant is identified within the genus Circaea, specifically categorized as the species mollis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Circaea mollis has soft, ovate to lanceolate leaves with long petioles and small, four-petaled white flowers arranged in terminal racemes. The foliage is characterized by a distinctly pubescent texture, making the leaves feel velvety to the touch. Its stems are slender and upright, often bearing several small, inconspicuous flowers that bloom in late summer. The plant produces small, cylindrical capsules that eventually develop fine, bristly hairs to aid in wind dispersal. These morphological traits distinguish it from other members of the Onagraceae family.

What are the pharmacological activities of Circaea mollis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its potent antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, anti-arthritic, and anticancer activities. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds help neutralize harmful free radicals to prevent cellular damage. These properties also allow it to mitigate systemic inflammation and reduce the painful symptoms associated with arthritic conditions. Furthermore, certain extracts from the species show promise in inhibiting the growth and progression of various cancer cell lines. Such diverse therapeutic effects highlight the importance of studying its chemical constituents for future medicinal development.

As discussed in a study, ethanol extracts of Circaea mollis (EEC) demonstrated potential therapeutic effects against arthritis in rodents. The EEC inhibited inflammatory swelling and increased the heat-induced pain threshold in mice. In FCA-induced rats, EEC significantly alleviated paw swelling and the arthritis index while decreasing the spleen index and thymus index. The extract also down-regulated serum TNF-α and IL-1β and increased the production of serum IL-10. Histopathological examination showed that EEC can effectively relieve synovial hyperplasia, control inflammatory infiltration, and protect cartilage from destruction. These activities are attributed to modulating pro-inflammatory cytokines TNF-α, IL-1β, and anti-inflammatory factor IL-10, potentially driven by flavonoids and polyphenols.
