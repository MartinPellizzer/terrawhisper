Euphorbia prostrata

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia prostrata?

Euphorbia prostrata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its lineage through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is situated under the order Malpighiales. The plant is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae and falls under the genus Euphorbia, ultimately reaching its specific classification as the species prostrata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia prostrata has a prostrate, spreading growth habit characterized by slender, cylindrical stems that often exhibit a reddish or purplish hue. The leaves are small, elliptical to obovate in shape, and arranged in an alternate pattern along the stem. These plants produce tiny, inconspicuous yellowish flowers clustered in small umbels called cyathia. A defining morphological feature is the presence of milky white latex that exudes from the stems when they are damaged. The root system is typically shallow and fibrous, helping the plant colonize disturbed ground quickly.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia prostrata contains medicinal properties found primarily in its entire vegetative structure, including the leaves, stems, and the milky latex sap. What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Leishmania donovani, diabetes mellitus, acute inflammation, and various skin and wound infections. Its medicinal properties are particularly noted for their ability to combat visceral leishmaniasis caused by protozoan parasites. Researchers have studied its chemical constituents to understand how they mitigate acute inflammation within the body. Additionally, the plant shows promise in managing blood sugar levels related to diabetes mellitus. Topical applications are frequently employed to accelerate the healing of skin and wound infections.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, mucous membrane irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain if contact or ingestion occurs. The milky latex produced by the plant is known to provoke redness and discomfort upon touching the skin. If the sap comes into contact with the eyes or mouth, it can result in significant irritation to the mucous membranes. Ingesting any part of the plant may trigger severe gastrointestinal distress, including nausea and vomiting. Furthermore, individuals may suffer from prolonged diarrhea and intense abdominal pain following consumption.
