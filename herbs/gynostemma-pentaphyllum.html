Scientific and Botanical Profile

Jiaogulan, with botanical name Gynostemma pentaphyllum, is a climbing herbaceous plant belonging to the Cucurbitaceae family, commonly known as the gourd or cucumber family.

Native to regions such as China, Korea, Japan, Taiwan, Vietnam, Indochina, the Eastern Himalayas, Southern China, and North-East India, it is also referred to by various common names including Chinese Ginseng, Five-Leaf Ginseng, Five-Leaf Gynostemma, Gynostemma, Southern Ginseng, Chinese Immortality Herb, Swingle's Ginseng, Chinese Gymnast's Club, and Teara. Morphologically, it is a perennial vine with heart-shaped leaves that are typically five-lobed, giving it the name Five-Leaf Ginseng, and small yellowish-green flowers that develop into capsule fruits containing oval seeds.

This plant is celebrated for its potential adaptogenic and antioxidant properties, making it a subject of interest in both traditional and modern botanical research.

In rural communities, it was traditionally harvested during specific lunar cycles and used in family remedies to treat ailments such as fatigue, respiratory issues, and stress. The plant remains culturally significant, symbolizing resilience and harmony with nature in both traditional and contemporary wellness practices.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Gynostemma pentaphyllum has been traditionally used as a medicinal plant known for its adaptogenic properties, available in various forms such as fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. Topical applications include using the powder or essential oil for skin conditions, while the powder can also be incorporated into food or supplements. For adults, a typical dosage is 1 to 3 grams of dried leaves or 100 to 200 mg of standardized extract per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision.

Due to its potent effects, it is recommended to use gynostemma pentaphyllum sparingly, with a duration of no more than 4 to 6 weeks, unless advised otherwise by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Gynostemma pentaphyllum can be considered relatively safe when used in moderate amounts for short periods, but it is not without potential risks.

Common side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea or diarrhea, and in some cases, allergic reactions. It may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the liver or blood pressure, and should be used cautiously with other herbs like ginseng or St. John's wort. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid gynostemma pentaphyllum due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, always start with a low dose, monitor for adverse effects, and consult a healthcare professional, especially if taking other medications or supplements.
