What's the taxonomical classification of Cascabela thevetia?

Cascabela thevetia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its evolutionary path continues through the order Gentianales and the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific taxonomic placement within the genus Cascabela and the species thevetia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cascabela thevetia has an evergreen shrub or small tree morphology characterized by lanceolate, leathery leaves that are arranged alternately along dark green, woody stems. The foliage typically measures between 10 and 20 centimeters in length and features entire margins with acute tips. It produces terminal clusters of funnel-shaped flowers that vary in color from bright yellow to orange. These blossoms are accompanied by elongated, narrow drupes that contain a single hard seed. The entire plant structure is robust, often reaching heights of up to 6 meters in tropical environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cascabela thevetia contains potent cardiac glycosides primarily located in its seeds, leaves, and bark. The seeds are the most concentrated source of these active compounds, which can influence heart rate and muscle contraction. Extracts from the leaves are also frequently utilized in traditional medicine to prepare topical applications or internal remedies. Due to the high toxicity of these components, precise measurements are critical to avoid severe poisoning. Practitioners must handle all parts of the plant with extreme caution because even small amounts can be lethal to humans and animals.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Cascabela thevetia targets cardiac glycoside-sensitive proteins, specifically the Na+/K+-ATPase enzyme, which is the primary mechanism for its physiological actions. By inhibiting this sodium-potassium pump, the plant increases intracellular sodium levels, which subsequently leads to an increase in intracellular calcium through the sodium-calcium exchanger. This modulation of ion concentration enhances the force of myocardial contraction, explaining its potent cardiotonic properties. Additionally, certain compounds within the plant may interact with various signaling pathways and membrane-bound receptors to exert sedative or emetic effects. These complex molecular interactions underscore both the therapeutic potential and the high toxicity of the plant's chemical constituents.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cascabela thevetia?

Cascabela thevetia has potent cardiotonic properties due to the presence of cardiac glycosides that increase the force of heart muscle contractions. While these chemical compounds can be used to treat certain cardiac conditions, the plant is highly toxic if ingested in uncontrolled amounts. If consumed, the plant acts as a powerful emetic that induces rapid vomiting in humans and animals. It also functions as a strong purgative by causing significant intestinal irritation and diarrhea. Careful medical supervision is required because the line between a therapeutic dose and a lethal dose is extremely narrow.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe toxicity characterized by nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, dizziness, and seizures. Ingesting its seeds or leaves often leads to critical cardiovascular complications such as arrhythmia and bradycardia. These heart rhythm disturbances can cause a dangerous drop in blood pressure known as hypotension. In the most extreme cases of poisoning, the toxic compounds may trigger a fatal cardiac arrest.
