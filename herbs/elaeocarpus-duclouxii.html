What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeocarpus duclouxii?

Elaeocarpus duclouxii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the taxonomic lineage of the order Oxalidales. This specific plant is situated within the family Elaeocarpaceae and is characterized by its placement in the genus Elaeocarpus, ultimately reaching its specific identification as the species duclouxii.

What are the therapeutic actions of Elaeocarpus duclouxii?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, wounds, stomach aches, and various types of intestinal parasites. The bark and leaves are often prepared as decoctions or topical applications to address these specific ailments. In traditional medicinal practices, the plant serves as a remedy for inflammatory skin conditions and minor abrasions. It is also utilized to alleviate gastrointestinal distress and promote digestive health. These therapeutic properties make it a significant resource in local ethnobotanical studies.

What are the herbal preparations of Elaeocarpus duclouxii?

Elaeocarpus duclouxii is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a juice extracted from the leaves, or a topical paste made from crushed plant matter. These various preparations are used by traditional healers to treat a range of ailments including skin infections and digestive issues. The bark is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid for internal consumption. In some regions, the leaves are macerated to produce a medicinal liquid applied directly to wounds. These diverse forms allow practitioners to tailor the treatment to the specific needs of the patient.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Elaeocarpus duclouxii can cause skin irritation, dermatitis, or allergic reactions when the plant's sap or tissues come into direct contact with human skin. The chemical compounds within the plant may trigger redness, itching, or swelling in sensitive individuals. Ingesting any part of the plant can lead to gastrointestinal distress, including nausea or vomiting. Caution should be exercised when handling the plant to avoid accidental exposure to its bioactive substances. Proper protective gear is recommended for those working closely with its foliage or bark.
