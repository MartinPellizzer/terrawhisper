Solanum surattense

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum surattense?

Solanum surattense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its lineage through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed under the order Solanales. This species is a member of the family Solanaceae, falling under the widely recognized genus Solanum, and is specifically identified by its unique species name, surattense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum surattense has an erect, perennial shrubby habit characterized by woody stems and densely pubescent foliage. The leaves are typically ovate to lanceolate with entire or slightly wavy margins and a velvety texture caused by fine hairs. Small, actinomorphic flowers emerge in clusters, featuring five distinct petals and prominent yellow anthers. The fruit develops as a fleshy berry that transitions in color as it matures. These morphological traits distinguish it within its specific taxonomic group in tropical regions.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains a diverse array of steroidal glycoalkaloids including solamargine, solanidine, solasoadine, solasonine, and tomatidine. These specific medicinal compounds serve as natural defense mechanisms for the plant while offering unique biological activities. Solanidine and solasoadine act as the aglycone bases for several of the other complex glycosides present. Solasonine and tomatidine are particularly notable for their potent chemical structures within the plant's secondary metabolism. The presence of solamargine further contributes to the complex chemical profile found in these tissues.

    As discussed in a study, Solanum surattense contains a total of 338 metabolites of various chemical classes. This includes 137 (40.53%) terpenoids, 56 (16.56%) phenol derivatives, and 52 (15.38%) lipids. Among these metabolites, 51 were identified and biologically tested, showing antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antitumoal activities. The most biologically active metabolites with high potency are mostly steroidal alkaloids and triterpenoids, such as solamargine, solanidine, solasodine, solasonine, tomatidine, xanthosaponin A-B, dioscin, lupeol, and stigmasterol. These compounds contribute to the plant's potential as a source of herbal medicines.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause severe physiological and neurological reactions including nausea, vomiting, dizziness, abdominal pain, diarrhea, hallucinations, confusion, and tachycardia. Ingestion of the toxic components often leads to acute gastrointestinal distress characterized by intense abdominal pain and frequent diarrhea. The chemical compounds may also trigger central nervous system disturbances such as disorientation and vivid hallucinations. Furthermore, the heart rate can increase significantly, resulting in dangerous episodes of tachycardia. These combined symptoms indicate a serious risk of poisoning if any part of the plant is consumed.
