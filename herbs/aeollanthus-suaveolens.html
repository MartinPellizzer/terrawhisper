Aeollanthus suaveolens

What's the taxonomical classification of Aeollanthus suaveolens?

Aeollanthus suaveolens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is placed in the order Lamiales and belongs to the family Lamiaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Aeollanthus, and its species designation, suaveolens.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Aeollanthus suaveolens targets enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to mediate its pharmacological activities. The plant contains bioactive secondary metabolites like alkaloids and flavonoids that interfere with the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. These compounds specifically inhibit the production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes, which are key mediators of pain and inflammation. Additionally, its phytochemical constituents interact with oxidative stress-related proteins to scavenge free radicals and reduce cellular damage. Through these multi-target interactions, the plant exerts significant antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory effects.

What are the herbal preparations of Aeollanthus suaveolens?

Aeollanthus suaveolens is prepared as a decoction, infusion, or topical paste using its leaves, roots, or bark depending on the intended medicinal application. The leaves are often steeped in hot water to create a tea used for internal cleansing or digestive support. For external ailments such as skin irritations or wounds, the plant material is crushed into a fresh paste and applied directly to the affected area. Roots are typically boiled for extended periods to extract potent alkaloids for stronger decoctions. These various preparations allow for the targeted delivery of the plant's bioactive compounds to different parts of the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Aeollanthus suaveolens can cause severe cardiac arrhythmia, vomiting, abdominal pain, and potentially fatal respiratory failure if ingested. The plant contains potent cardiac glycosides that disrupt the normal electrical activity of the heart muscle. Symptoms of poisoning often manifest as intense nausea and gastrointestinal distress shortly after consumption. As the toxins circulate, patients may experience blurred vision, irregular heartbeats, or extreme dizziness. In extreme cases, the physiological stress can lead to seizures or complete cardiac arrest.
