Baphicacanthus cusia

What's the taxonomical classification of Baphicacanthus cusia?

Baphicacanthus cusia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Acanthaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Baphicacanthus, and its species name, cusia.

What are the pharmacological activities of Baphicacanthus cusia?

This plant has significant anti-HCC and antitumor activities that demonstrate its potential for cancer therapy. Research indicates that extracts from Baehicacanthus cusia can inhibit the proliferation of various cancer cell lines. Specifically, its anti-HCC properties target hepatocellular carcinoma cells to prevent further tumor development. These medicinal effects are attributed to a diverse profile of bioactive phytochemicals present within the plant. Further investigation into these pharmacological mechanisms continues to support its use in oncology research.

As discussed in a study, paxilline derived from an endophytic fungus of the leaves of Baphicacanthus cusia (Nees) Bremek alleviates hepatocellular carcinoma through autophagy-mediated apoptosis. This paxilline inhibits the proliferation of HepG2 cells and arrests HepG2 cells in the S phase. The substance inhibits HepG2 cell migration and markedly downregulates the mRNA expression of Cyclin D1, CDK4, LC3B, mTOR, Parkin, and PINK1. Paxilline treatment significantly increases the expression of LC3B II/I, bax, cleaved-PARP, and cleaved-caspase 3 while significantly decreasing the expression of bcl-2. Furthermore, paxilline may have synergistic effects with Rapa to jointly promote cellular apoptosis and the expression of proapoptotic proteins.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 7-methoxy-13-dehydroxypaxilliine, paixilliine, tryptanthrin, indirubin, and indigo. These specific alkaloids and pigments are responsible for much of the plant's unique chemical profile. Tryptanthrin and the various forms of indirubin are often studied for their potential biological activities. The presence of indigo provides distinct coloring properties alongside these bioactive molecules. Together, these substances define the pharmacological complexity found within the plant's tissues.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal plant Baphicacanthus cusia contains bioactive compounds that can be extracted using optimized conditions. The optimal extraction process for Baphicacanthus cusia leaves involved a lactic acid/L-menthol ratio of 5:2 (mol/mol), a solid-liquid ratio of 80.0 mL/g, and a temperature of 60.5 °C. Through this method, the extraction yields for specific compounds were measured. Tryptanthrin reached a yield of 0.356 mg/g, indigo reached 1.744 mg/g, and indirubin reached 0.562 mg/g. These results demonstrate the efficiency of using deep eutectic solvents for these extractions.
