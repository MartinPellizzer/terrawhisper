Lilium speciosum

What's the taxonomical classification of Lilium speciosum?

Lilium speciosum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized under the order Liliales and falls within the family Liliaceae. Finally, its specific biological identification is defined by the genus Lilium and the species name speciosum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lilium speciosum has erect, unbranched stems that typically reach heights of 60 to 120 centimeters and bear whorled, lanceolate leaves arranged in several tiers. The foliage consists of bright green, linear leaves that are often slightly curved or recurved toward the ground. Its most prominent feature is the large, trumpet-shaped flowers that emerge from the upper stem in shades of pink, red, or white with dark spots. These blossoms possess six distinct tepals and long, protruding stamens that facilitate pollination. The plant also develops bulbous underground structures which serve as its primary nutrient storage organs.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of the eastern Himalayas, specifically spanning through parts of India, Nepal, Bhutan, and southwestern China. It typically thrives in subalpine zones where it inhabits open forests and grassy slopes. These populations are often found at elevations ranging from 2,500 to 4,000 meters above sea level. The species prefers cool, moist environments provided by mountain climates. Its specific distribution is closely tied to the unique ecological niches found within these rugged mountain ranges.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause severe kidney failure and fatal toxicity if ingested by cats. Even a small amount of the plant material, such as a single petal or a sip of water from a vase, can trigger rapid organ damage. Symptoms of poisoning often include vomiting, lethargy, and a complete loss of appetite. If left untreated, the damage progresses to total renal shutdown within a few days. Owners should be extremely cautious and keep all parts of the plant out of reach of feline companions.
