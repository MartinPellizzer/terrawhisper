Cinchona calisaya

What's the taxonomical classification of Cinchona calisaya?

Cinchona calisaya belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Gentianales, the plant is a member of the family Rubiaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identification is established within the genus Cinchona, with the distinct species name calisaya.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cinconona calisaya has an erect, woody habit characterized by slender branches and elliptical to lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The leaves are typically arranged in an opposite pattern along the stem and feature prominent pinnate venation. Small, tubular flowers emerge in clusters from the axils of the leaves or at the terminal ends of the branches. These blossoms are often white or pale pink and possess a delicate structure designed for specific pollinators. The plant produces small, fleshy fruits that contain numerous seeds used for propagation.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cinchona calisaya?

This plant has potent anti-malarial, antimicrobial, and antioxidant pharmacological activities derived from its complex alkaloid content. The presence of quinine and related alkaloids allows it to effectively combat Plasmodium parasites responsible for malaria. Beyond its medicinal use against malaria, the plant exhibits significant inhibitory effects against various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Researchers have also noted its capacity to neutralize free radicals, contributing to its overall therapeutic potential. These diverse biological properties make Cinchona calisaya a valuable subject for pharmaceutical development.

According to a study published by "Current topics in medicinal chemistry", Cinchonine is obtained in much lower quantities from Cinchona calisaya. This alkaloid is used as an anti-malarial agent and possesses biological properties including anti-cancer, anti-obesity, anti-inflammatory, anti-parasitic, antimicrobial, anti-platelet aggregation, and anti-osteoclast differentiation. It induces anti-cancer activity through the activation of caspase-3 and PARP-1 while triggering the endoplasmic reticulum stress response. The compound up-regulates GRP78 and promotes the phosphorylation of PERK and ETIF-2α. Additionally, it inhibits osteoclastogenesis by inhibiting TAK1 activation and suppressing NFATc1 expression through the regulation of AP-1 and NF-κB.

Beyond its antimalarial properties, the species is traditionally utilized to manage high fevers and inflammatory conditions. The medicinal components found in its bark serve as a natural foundation for several pharmaceutical applications. Researchers continue to study its chemical profile to better understand its systemic health benefits.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Cinchona calisaya Wedd. is a species native from Peru that contains the antimalarial alkaloid quinine. This plant is categorized as a Brazilian quina used in traditional medicine. The study utilized 28 samples of barks acquired from a popular market to assess authenticity. Within these samples, 23 had their DNA extracted successfully for analysis. Researchers amplified and sequenced the matK and rbcl regions for 15 and 23 samples, respectively. Biological assays were conducted through antimalarial tests in vitro to evaluate the species.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a wide range of serious adverse reactions including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, dizziness, headache, blurred vision, and confusion. Consumption may lead to sensory disturbances such as tinnitus, impaired hearing, or permanent hearing loss. Severe neurological and cardiovascular complications can occur, manifesting as hyperactivity, convulsions, cardiac arrhythmias, and methemoglobinemia. These multifaceted side effects demonstrate the significant toxicity associated with its chemical constituents.
