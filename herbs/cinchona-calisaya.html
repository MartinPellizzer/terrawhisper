What to know about Cinchona calisaya (jesuit's bark) before using it medicinally

Cinchona calisaya, commonly known as Jesuit's bark, is a highly valued herb renowned for its extraordinary health benefits, particularly in reducing fever and treating malaria, which have made it a trusted companion for many for centuries.

As a tropical plant, Cinchona calisaya thrives in high-altitude regions and requires a humid climate with plenty of rainfall, making it a challenging but rewarding crop for growers to cultivate. Botanically, Cinchona calisaya belongs to the family Rubiaceae and is characterized by its large, elliptical leaves and vibrant green color.

The herb's historical significance can be traced back to the 17th century, when Spanish and Portuguese missionaries first introduced it to the West, leading to its widespread adoption as a medicinal herb in traditional medicine.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Cinchona calisaya.

What are the medicinal properties of Cinchona calisaya?

Cinchona calisaya, also known as Jesuit's bark, helps with reducing fever and inflammation due to its antipyretic, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties. It has been used to treat malaria, rheumatism, and other conditions characterized by pain and swelling.

The active constituents of Cinchona calisaya include alkaloids such as quinine, cinchonine, and cinchonidine, which are responsible for its medicinal properties. These alkaloids have a strong antimalarial effect, as well as anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects.

The bark of Cinchona calisaya is the most commonly used part for medicinal purposes. It can be obtained from the tree through a process of tapping, where a notch is cut into the bark to allow the latex to flow out.

When used improperly, Cinchona calisaya can cause side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and headache. Prolonged use of the plant can also lead to cinchonism, a condition characterized by symptoms such as dizziness, ringing in the ears, and vision problems.

To use Cinchona calisaya safely, precautions should be taken to avoid side effects. Dosing should be done under medical supervision, and the plant should be used in combination with other antimalarial medications to avoid toxicity. Pregnant women and children should not use Cinchona calisaya without consulting a healthcare professional.

What are the horticulural aspects of Cinchona calisaya?

Cinchona calisaya, also known as Jesuit's bark, grow best in subtropical regions with high altitudes, tolerating temperatures between 15°C to 25°C. It requires well-draining acidic soils with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. Moderate humidity and filtered sunlight are ideal conditions for this plant.

For optimal growth, plant Cinchona calisaya in shaded areas with regular watering, but ensuring the soil does not become waterlogged. Fertilize with an acidic fertilizer (pH 4.0-5.0) every 2-3 months during the growing season. Mulching helps retain moisture and suppress weeds.

Cinchona calisaya bark is ready for harvesting when it turns dark red to yellow, and the bark is 1-2 cm in thickness. Harvesting usually occurs between 3-5 years after planting, and the plant is left to continue growing. Harvesting can be done manually by skilled labor, with care to avoid damaging the remaining bark.

Pests commonly affecting Cinchona calisaya include scale insects, mealybugs, and aphids. Leaf spot diseases like Cercospora, Colletotrichum, and Phyllosticta are prevalent, as well as root rot caused by Phytophthora and Pythium. Regular monitoring and maintenance help mitigate these issues.

What are the botanical characteristics of Cinchona calisaya?

Cinchona calisaya is a deciduous shrub or small tree that belongs to the family Rubiaceae.

It is native to the Andes mountain range in South America, specifically in Bolivia and Peru. Botanically, C. calisaya is characterized by its evergreen leaves that are oval-shaped, elliptical, and have a length of 10-15 cm, with a pointed tip and serrated margins.

The leaves are dark green on the upper surface and lighter green on the lower surface, with fine pubescence along the midrib and veins. The plant produces small, white to cream-colored flowers in dense spikes, typically 5-7 cm long, which are richly scented and bloom from June to October. Cinchona calisaya also bears small, globose fruit that is 1-2 cm in diameter, containing numerous seeds.

The bark of the plant is thick and rough, with a reddish-brown color, and is highly valued for its medicinal properties, particularly for the production of quinine, a valuable antimalarial drug.

What are the historical uses of Cinchona calisaya?

Cinchona calisaya, also known as Jesuit's bark or Peruvian bark, has a rich history dating back to ancient times.

For centuries, its bark was used in traditional medicine to treat fever and malaria, particularly in South America. The Spanish conquistadors and European physicians adopted this remedy, and by the 17th century, cinchona calisaya became a vital component of quinine-based treatments for malaria.

Its extracts were also used to treat other ailments, such as rheumatism and gout.
