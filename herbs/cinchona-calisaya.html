Scientific and Botanical Profile

Calisaya Cinchona, with botanical name Cinchona calisaya, is a member of the Rubiaceae family and is native to regions across South America, including Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia, the Amazon Basin, the Andes Mountains, Central America, and the Madre De Dios Region. Commonly known as Cinchona Calisaya, Calisaya Tree, Peruvian Bark, Caliquina, Yellow Cinchona, Calisaya Quina, and Calyx Cinchona, it is renowned for its medicinal properties, particularly its quinine content. Morphologically, it is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree, typically reaching heights of 6-12 meters, with elliptical to ovate leaves that are dark green on the upper surface and lighter on the underside. Its flowers are clustered in cymes and are typically yellow or white, while the fruit is a capsule containing small, yellowish seeds.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Cinchona calisaya has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, with various forms available including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule. For internal use, preparations such as tea, decoction, and infusion can be made by steeping the dried leaves in hot water, while topical applications involve using the essential oil or powder directly on the skin. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried leaves per cup of tea, taken up to three times a day, while children should only use it under medical supervision. It is advisable to use cinchona calisaya for short durations, no longer than a week, to avoid potential side effects and to monitor for any adverse reactions.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Cinchona calisaya can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential therapeutic properties, primarily due to its quinine content, which has historically been used for malaria treatment. However, it is important to note that cinchona calisaya may cause side effects such as nausea, vomiting, dizziness, and in severe cases, hearing loss or tinnitus due to its effects on the inner ear and nervous system. It may also interact with certain medications, including antihistamines, antidepressants, and other antimalarial drugs, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Special populations such as pregnant women, breastfeeding mothers, and individuals with chronic illnesses should avoid its use without medical supervision, as it may pose risks to fetal development or exacerbate existing conditions. To ensure safe use, it is advisable to consult a healthcare professional before taking cinchona calisaya, follow recommended dosages, and monitor for any unusual symptoms that may indicate toxicity.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Cinchona calisaya grows in tropical and subtropical regions, preferring well-drained, fertile soil with a pH between 5.5 and 6.5, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in warm climates with consistent moisture, needing regular watering but avoiding waterlogged conditions, and benefits from mulching to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. For care and maintenance, regular pruning of dead or diseased branches promotes air circulation and prevents pest infestations, while monitoring for common pests like aphids and scale insects is essential. The best time to harvest the bark is during the dry season, typically between October and March, using a hand saw or knife to carefully strip the outer bark without damaging the inner layers. After harvesting, the bark should be dried in a cool, shaded area to preserve its potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain its medicinal properties.
