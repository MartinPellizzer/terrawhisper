Buddleja cordata

What's the taxonomical classification of Buddleja cordata?

Buddleja cordata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Lamiales and the family Scrophulariaceae. Finally, it is defined by its specific genus, Buddlejia, and its species designation, cordata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Buddlejia cordata has heart-shaped, opposite leaves that measure between 5 and 15 centimeters in length and are covered in fine, soft hairs on both surfaces. These leaves feature prominent veins and slightly serrated margins that contribute to their distinctive shape. The plant typically grows as a deciduous shrub, reaching heights of up to 2 meters with multiple woody stems. During the flowering season, it produces dense, terminal panicles of small, tubular flowers that are usually light purple or lilac in color. This morphological structure attracts various pollinators, making the plant a significant feature in many garden ecosystems.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a specific range in East Asia, spanning across parts of China, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. The species is often found at moderate elevations where it can access sufficient sunlight. As discussed in a study, Buddlejia cordata contains the medicinal compound verbascoside. This specific compound was evaluated against Staphylococcus aureus using killing kinetics and incorporation of precursors methods. The research shows that verbascoside induced a lethal effect on the S. aureus bacteria. This action was achieved by affecting protein synthesis. Furthermore, the substance worked by inhibiting leucine incorporation.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating skin burns, sores, arthritis, rheumatic pains, and infections caused by Candida spp. The leaves and other parts of the plant are often applied topically to promote healing in damaged skin tissues. Its medicinal properties help reduce inflammation associated with joint discomfort and chronic rheumatic pains. Additionally, it serves as an agent to combat fungal issues related to Candida spp. through its antimicrobial qualities. These diverse applications make it a versatile resource in traditional herbal medicine.

As discussed in a study, the antifungal activity of the methanol extracts of Buddlejia cordata was evaluated against clinical isolates of different Candida species including C. albicans, C. parapsilosis, C. tropicalis, C. krusei, and C. glabrata. This evaluation followed the micro dilution assay described in the CLSI M27-A protocol. The methanol extracts of Buddlejia cordata showed MIC values < 31.25μg/mL against at least one of the strains used. These results are noted as being very interesting because they were crude extracts.
