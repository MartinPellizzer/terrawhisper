Wrightia arborea

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Wrightia arborea has a small to medium-sized woody tree characterized by a slender trunk, smooth greyish bark, and spreading branches with elliptic, glossy green leaves. The leaves are arranged oppositely or in whorls of three, possessing an acute apex and a slightly wavy margin. During its flowering season, the tree produces clusters of fragrant, white, tubular flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These blossoms feature five spreading lobes and often appear in profuse numbers against the dark foliage. After pollination, the tree develops small, globose, fleshy berries that transition from green to a dark color upon ripening.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, including India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of southern China. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland areas and is commonly found growing in forest edges or along riverbanks. Due to its ornamental value, it has been widely introduced to many other tropical climates around the world for landscaping. The species prefers warm environments with seasonal rainfall and is rarely found in temperate zones. Its distribution is largely dictated by its need for high humidity and consistent warmth.

These molecular interactions contribute to its traditional use in treating skin ailments and fever.     What are the herbal preparations of Wrightia arborea?

    Wrightia arborea is prepared as a decoction from the bark, a juice expressed from the leaves, or a topical paste made from crushed plant parts. In traditional medicine, the bark is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for internal consumption. The fresh leaves can be squeezed to obtain a juice that serves as a remedy for various ailments. Additionally, the plant matter is frequently ground into a fine paste to be applied directly to the skin for localized treatment. These different methods allow practitioners to target specific physiological needs using the various components of the tree.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Wrightia arborea can cause skin irritation and allergic reactions if the milky sap comes into direct contact with the body. This sap contains various chemical compounds that may lead to redness, itching, or dermatitis in sensitive individuals. If the plant material is ingested, it may result in gastrointestinal distress such as stomach aches or vomiting. There is also a potential for toxicity if significant amounts are consumed by pets or livestock. It is generally advised to wear gloves when pruning the plant to avoid these adverse effects.
