Wrightia arborea

What's the taxonomical classification of Wrightia arborea?

Wrightia arborea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Gentianales, the plant is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Wrightia and the species arborea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Wrightia arborea has a small to medium-sized woody tree characterized by a slender trunk, smooth greyish bark, and spreading branches with elliptic, glossy green leaves. The leaves are arranged oppositely or in whorls of three, possessing an acute apex and a slightly wavy margin. During its flowering season, the tree produces clusters of fragrant, white, tubular flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These blossoms feature five spreading lobes and often appear in profuse numbers against the dark foliage. After pollination, the tree develops small, globose, fleshy berries that transition from green to a dark color upon ripening.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, including India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of southern China. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland areas and is commonly found growing in forest edges or along riverbanks. Due to its ornamental value, it has been widely introduced to many other tropical climates around the world for landscaping. The species prefers warm environments with seasonal rainfall and is rarely found in temperate zones. Its distribution is largely dictated by its need for high humidity and consistent warmth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Wrightia arborea?

This plant has documented pharmacological activities that include analgesic, anti-inflammatory, anti-nociceptive, anticancer, and antiproliferative properties. The analgesic and anti-nociceptive effects suggest its potential for reducing pain sensitivity and managing discomfort. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory actions indicate an ability to mitigate swelling and immune responses in biological systems. The anticancer and antiproliferative capabilities demonstrate that the plant can inhibit the growth and spread of malignant cells. These diverse biological activities highlight the medicinal importance of Wrightia arborea in therapeutic research.

As discussed in a study, methanolic extract of Wrightia arborea (MEWA) showed no mortality in rats even after 14 days of observation at oral doses up to 2000 mg kg(-1) b.wt. MEWA at 100 and 200 mg kg(-1) b.wt. demonstrated highly significant anti-nociceptive activity against chemically induced pain (p < 0.001) and thermally induced pain (p < 0.05 and p < 0.001). In the formalin test, doses of 100 and 200 mg kg(-1) significantly prevented paw edema volume increases in both neurogenic and inflammatory phases (p < 0.05 and p < 0.01). Additionally, MEWA at 200 and 400 mg kg(-1) p.o. significantly prevented the increase in paw edema volume in the Carrageenan test (p < 0.05 and p < 0.001). These results suggest that MEWA possesses significant analgesic and anti-inflammatory potential.

As discussed in a study, Wrightia arborea is an Indian Ayurvedic medicinal plant used traditionally to treat various ailments. The chloroform extract, known as WAC, selectively killed K562 cells without affecting MCF-7, Hep G2 cells, or normal human peripheral blood lymphocytes. This WAC extract induced DNA damage, cell cycle perturbation, and potent G2 abrogations. Cell death was characterized by increased Ca2+ and ROS, phosphatidyl serine externalization, mitochondrial membrane depolarization, and DNA laddering. The study concluded that Wrightia arborea leaf extract WAC is capable of selectively killing leukemic cancer cells through DNA damage and G2 abrogation leading to mitochondrial apoptosis.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of milky latex in its stems and leaves. Contact with the sap often leads to redness, itching, or even chemical burns on sensitive skin areas. Some individuals may experience swelling or dermatitis after accidental exposure to the plant's fluids. If the plant material is swallowed, it may induce gastrointestinal distress or more severe poisoning depending on the amount consumed. Always use gloves when pruning this species to avoid these adverse physical reactions.
