What's the taxonomical classification of Palhinhaea cernua?

Palhinhaea cernua belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Lycopodiidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Lycopodiales. The plant is further categorized under the family Lycopodiaceae, falling within the genus Palhinhaea, and is specifically identified by its species name, cernua.

What are the pharmacological activities of Palhinhaea cernua?

This plant has pharmacological activities that include acting as a xanthine oxidase inhibitor, demonstrating antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties. Research indicates that the bioactive compounds within Palhinhaea cernua can effectively mitigate oxidative stress in biological systems. Its ability to inhibit xanthine oxidase suggests potential therapeutic value in managing conditions like hyperuricemia and gout. Furthermore, the plant's phytochemical profile supports its use in reducing inflammation and combating various pathogenic microorganisms. These diverse biological functions highlight its significance in ethnopharmacological studies.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", fractionation of the ethanol extract of Palhinhaea cernua yielded a new compound 1, which is apigenin-4'-O-(2' '-O-p-coumaroyl)-beta-D-glucopyranoside. This specific glycoside acts as a xanthine oxidase inhibitor with an IC50 value of 23.95 +/- 0.43 microM. The inhibition occurs in a competitive-noncompetitive manner, characterized by K(i) and K(I) values of 14.35 and 93.68 microM, respectively. While compound 1 shows activity, the two known p-coumaroylated apiigenin glycosides 2 and 3 from the plant were found to be inactive.

This mechanism is essential for preventing the painful accumulation of crystals in the joints associated with gouty arthritis. Additionally, its antioxidant properties may support overall kidney function and systemic inflammation management. These therapeutic effects make it a significant subject in ethnopharmacological studies focused on purine metabolism.
