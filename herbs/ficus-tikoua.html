Ficus tikoua

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus tikoua?

Ficus tikoua belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Rosales. Further taxonomic refinement places this plant within the family Moraceae, specifically within the genus Ficus, with its unique identity defined by the species name tikoua.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus tikoua has an arborescent growth habit characterized by woody stems, large elliptic to oblong leaves with entire margins, and small, inconspicuous pale green flowers.

The leaves typically feature a coriaceous texture and a prominent midrib that extends toward the apex. Its bark is generally smooth and grayish, providing a stark contrast to the dense, dark green foliage. As a member of the Moraceae family, the plant produces small syconia that function as specialized inflorescences.

These structures are essential for the plant's reproductive cycle and are often found tucked within the leaf axils.

        What are the herbal preparations of Ficus tikoua?

        Ficus tikoua is prepared as a decoction by boiling the bark or leaves in water, a tincture made with alcohol, or a topical poultice applied directly to the skin. These various methods allow for the extraction of different active compounds depending on the intended therapeutic use. Decoctions are most commonly used to create medicinal teas for internal consumption to treat digestive or inflammatory issues. Tinctures provide a more concentrated dose that can be stored for long periods and administered in small liquid quantities. For external ailments such as skin irritations or wounds, the crushed leaves are often used in a fresh paste or poultice.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Ficus tikoua can cause skin irritation, respiratory distress, and digestive upset if the milky sap or leaves come into contact with a person. The plant contains proteolytic enzymes and latex that often lead to contact dermatitis or redness upon touching the skin. Inhaling particles from the plant may trigger coughing or inflammation in the lungs of sensitive individuals. If ingested, the toxic compounds can result in nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Proper precautions should be taken when handling this species to avoid these adverse reactions.
