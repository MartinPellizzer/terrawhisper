Ficus tikoua

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus tikoua?

Ficus tikoua belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Rosales. Further taxonomic refinement places this plant within the family Moraceae, specifically within the genus Ficus, with its unique identity defined by the species name tikoua.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus tikoua has an arborescent growth habit characterized by woody stems, large elliptic to oblong leaves with entire margins, and small, inconspicuous pale green flowers. The leaves typically feature a coriaceous texture and a prominent midrib that extends toward the apex. Its bark is generally smooth and grayish, providing a stark contrast to the dense, dark green foliage. As a member of the Moraceae family, the plant produces small syconia that function as specialized inflorescences. These structures are essential for the plant's reproductive cycle and are often found tucked within the leaf axils.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the tropical forests of the Tikoua region within the Department of Chaco in Paraguay. It grows specifically in localized pockets where the soil conditions and humidity levels meet its unique biological requirements. Its range is highly restricted, meaning it is not found in surrounding South American countries or even other parts of Paraguay. Because of this narrow distribution, the species is considered geographically rare. Conservation efforts often focus on these specific habitats to prevent local extinction.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ficus tikoua?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antidiabetic, antifibrotic, antihepatic fibrosis, and antioxidant properties. Its antioxidant capabilities help mitigate oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals within biological systems. The plant also exhibits potent anti-inflammatory effects that can reduce swelling and pain in various tissues. Furthermore, its antidiabetic potential suggests a role in regulating glucose levels and managing metabolic dysfunction. Finally, the specific antifibrotic and antihepatic fibrosis actions work to prevent or slow the progression of scarring in vital organs, particularly the liver.

As discussed in a study, a 75% ethanol extract of Ficus tikoua, known as FT-D12, demonstrates dose-dependent effects in decreasing the expression of fibrosis markers including FN, Collagen I, and α-SMA. In mice, the application of FT-D12 significantly lowered serum levels of AST, ALT, and HYP, along with hepatic levels of PC-III, COL IV, and HA. UPLC-MS/MS analysis of the FT-D12 extract identified 39 components, which included 30 flavonoids. Among the potential antifibrotic candidates identified through network pharmacology, quercetin exhibited the strongest inhibitory effect on Collagen I and FN. These results indicate that the antifibrotic mechanism of Ficus tikoua may involve the modulation of the EGFR-AKT pathway.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains various medicinal flavonoids including quercetin, rutin, apihenin-7-O-glucoside, and isorhamnetin-3-O-glucoside. These specific chemical constituents are known for providing potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the body. The presence of quercetin and rutin helps to stabilize cell membranes and neutralize free radicals. Additionally, the glycosides apihenin-7-O-glucoside and isorhamnetin-3-O-glucoside contribute to the complex therapeutic profile of the species. Together, these bioactive compounds support overall cellular health through diverse biochemical pathways.

As discussed in a study, a 95% ethanol extract of Ficus tikoua Bur. yielded a new hemiacetal chromone racemate named (±)-ficunomone (1) along with twenty-two known flavonoids (2-23). Among these isolated compounds, seven flavonoids demonstrated inhibitory activities against yeast α-glucosidase. Compounds 22 and 23 exhibited the highest inhibitory activity with IC50 values at 5.12 ± 0.10 and 3.43 ± 0.15 μM respectively. Kinetic analysis showed that both compounds 22 and 23 act as uncompetitive inhibitors. Molecular docking studies further revealed that these two compounds bind to amino acid residues of the α-glucosidase activity pocket.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if the sap or parts of the plant are ingested. The milky white latex contains irritating compounds that may trigger redness, itching, or blistering upon contact with the skin. If these fluids enter the eyes, they can result in intense pain and temporary vision impairment. Ingesting any portion of the plant may lead to vomiting, stomach cramps, or more serious internal inflammation. Proper protective gear should be used when handling this species to avoid these adverse reactions.
