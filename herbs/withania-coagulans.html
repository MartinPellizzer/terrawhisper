Withania Coagulans, scientifically known as Withania coagulans, is a medicinal herb native to the Indian subcontinent, often referred to as the "Indian Winter Cherry" or "Ashwagandha" in some regions, though it is distinct from the more widely known Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera).

This adaptogen is valued for its ability to help the body resist stress and promote overall vitality, with primary benefits including enhanced energy levels, improved cognitive function, and support for immune health. Traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine, it has been employed to treat ailments ranging from fatigue and anxiety to digestive disorders and respiratory issues. In modern wellness practices, it is increasingly incorporated into supplements and herbal formulations aimed at boosting resilience, reducing oxidative stress, and supporting mental clarity.

Notably, Withania coagulans contains unique bioactive compounds such as withanolides, which contribute to its therapeutic properties, and it has a distinct, slightly bitter flavor that sets it apart from other adaptogenic herbs.

Scientific and Botanical Profile

Withania Coagulans, with botanical name Withania coagulans, is a species belonging to the Solanaceae family, commonly known as the nightshade family.

Native to regions across the Indian subcontinent, including India, Nepal, and parts of Southeast Asia, this plant has also been introduced to other tropical and subtropical areas. Morphologically, it is a shrub or small tree that can grow up to 3 meters tall, featuring smooth, glabrous stems and alternate, ovate to lanceolate leaves with a waxy surface. The plant is known by various common names such as Indian Nightshade, Indian Devil's Lettuce, Indian Soursop, Black Nightshade, Ashwagandha, Cherokee Nightshade, Wild Lettuce, Sateda, Cape Gooseberry, Madhurima, Cape Jasmine, Banyan Tree, Coagulant Plant, Hindu's Banyan, and others, reflecting its widespread use in traditional medicine and culinary practices.

Its fruit, which resembles a small, yellowish berry, is often used as a coagulant in the processing of dairy products and has been historically valued for its medicinal properties.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Withania coagulans has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms such as fresh plant, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare the plant, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant material for a longer period. An infusion may also be prepared by soaking the dried herb in water, and topical applications include using the powder or essential oil directly on the skin. For adults, a typical dosage is 250-500 mg of powdered extract in capsule form twice daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision due to limited safety data.

It is recommended to use Withania coagulans for short durations, typically up to 4-6 weeks, and to consult a healthcare provider before long-term use.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Withania coagulans can be used as a medicinal plant with potential therapeutic benefits, but it also carries important safety considerations.

While it may exhibit anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, it can cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea in some individuals. Additionally, it may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and antiplatelet drugs, increasing the risk of bleeding. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and it should be used with caution in individuals with chronic illnesses or those undergoing medical treatments.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a healthcare provider before use, start with low doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Withania coagulans grows in well-drained, sandy or loamy soil that is slightly acidic to neutral, and thrives in full sun to partial shade, requiring at least 6 hours of direct sunlight daily.

It prefers moderate watering, with the soil allowed to dry slightly between waterings to prevent root rot, and benefits from regular fertilization during the growing season. To ensure optimal growth, it should be planted in early spring, and regular weeding and pest monitoring are essential to maintain plant health. Harvesting is best done during the late summer to early autumn when the fruits are fully mature and turn red, using clean pruning shears to cut the stems just below the fruiting nodes.

After harvesting, the fruits should be dried in a cool, shaded area to preserve their potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to extend shelf life.
