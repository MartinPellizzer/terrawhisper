Aspidistra elatior

What's the taxonomical classification of Aspidistra elatior?

Aspidistra elatior belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic descent, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As a member of the order Asparagales, it falls under the family Asparagaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Aspidistra, with its specific designation being the species elatior.

What are the therapeutic actions of Aspidistra elatior?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including fever, malaria, and skin infections through its traditional medicinal applications. In certain regions, the leaves are prepared into decoctions to help reduce high body temperatures. The plant also possesses properties that are believed to assist in managing stomach-related issues and digestive discomfort. Local healers sometimes apply crushed foliage directly to wounds or skin irritations to promote healing. These uses are deeply rooted in regional folk medicine rather than standardized clinical pharmacology.

What are the herbal preparations of Aspidistra elatior?

Aspidistra elatior is prepared as a decoction of its leaves or as a poultice applied topically to the skin. The leaves are often boiled in water to extract their active constituents for medicinal use. In some traditional practices, the plant material is crushed into a paste to soothe local inflammation. These preparations are primarily used to address specific skin conditions or internal ailments depending on the culture. This versatility allows practitioners to utilize the plant in various therapeutic contexts.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Aspidistra elatior can cause mild skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis if its sap comes into contact with sensitive human skin. While the plant is generally considered non-toxic to humans, some individuals may experience localized redness or itching upon handling the foliage. There is limited clinical research regarding the systemic effects of ingestion, but caution is advised to avoid consuming any part of the plant. Pets may also experience gastrointestinal discomfort if they attempt to eat the leaves. Maintaining proper hygiene after gardening with this species can help mitigate these minor risks.
