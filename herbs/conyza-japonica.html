What's the taxonomical classification of Conyza japonica?

Conyza japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae, falling into the genus Conyza, and is specifically identified by its species name, japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Conyza japonica has an erect, herbaceous annual habit characterized by square stems that reach heights of up to 1 meter and are covered in fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are oppositely arranged, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and feature serrated margins with distinctively long-acuminate tips. Small, white or pale lavender flowers are clustered in dense, terminal spikes that emerge from the upper leaf axils. Each flower possesses five petals and is supported by a tubular calyx that becomes more prominent during fruiting. The plant eventually produces small, dry achenes that are essential for its dispersal in disturbed habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Conyza japonica?

This plant has inhibitory activity against nitric oxide production. This specific pharmacological effect suggests that Conyza japonica may play a role in modulating inflammatory responses within biological systems. By reducing the levels of nitric oxide, the plant extracts can potentially limit the overproduction of reactive species associated with various diseases. Researchers often investigate these properties to determine the potential for developing new anti-inflammatory therapeutic agents. Such studies help clarify how the chemical constituents of the plant interact with cellular signaling pathways.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", four new sesquiterpenoids named conyterpenol A-D (1-4) and nineteen known analogues (5-23) were isolated from the aerial parts of Conyza japonica. Compounds 3, 4, and 12 demonstrated moderate inhibition against LPS-induced nitric oxide production in RAW264.7 macrophages with IC50 values ranging from 26.4 to 33.6 μM. These compounds were also evaluated for cytotoxicity against the HepG2 and MCF-7 cancer cell lines. All 23 compounds showed no obvious cytotoxicity against these two cell lines at 100 μM. Conyterpenol D (4) was identified as a new type of sesquiterpenoid featuring a seven-membered lactone ring.
