Heracleum dissectifolium

What's the taxonomical classification of Heracleum dissectifolium?

Heracleum dissectifolium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls within the order Apiales. The plant is a member of the family Apiaceae, which is further narrowed down to the genus Heracleum, ultimately identifying it as the specific species dissectifolium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Heracleum dissectifolium has large, deeply divided leaves that are pinnately dissected into narrow, lanceolate segments with serrated margins. These complex foliage structures are arranged on thick, hollow stems that can reach significant heights. The plant features large, compound umbels composed of numerous small, white flowers clustered together. The undersides of the leaves and the stems often exhibit fine pubescence or slight hairiness. Its root system is typically composed of a sturdy taproot that supports the vigorous growth of the plant.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, burns, erythema, and phototoxicity upon contact with human tissue. The primary danger arises from furanocoumarins within the sap, which react violently when the affected area is exposed to sunlight. This chemical reaction leads to intense erythema and painful, red inflammation. If the exposure is significant, the damaged skin may develop large blisters. These burns can persist for weeks and may leave permanent scarring or pigmentation changes.
