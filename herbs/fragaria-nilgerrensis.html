Fragaria nilgerrensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Fragaria nilgerrensis?

Fragaria nilgerrensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its placement in the order Rosales, the plant is a member of the family Rosaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Fragaria and the species nilgerrensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Fragaria nilgerrensis has a perennial growth habit characterized by stoloniferous spreading, trifoliate leaves with serrated margins, and small white flowers with five petals. The leaves typically feature long petioles and a distinctively textured surface with prominent venation. During the flowering stage, the plant produces numerous delicate blossoms that emerge from the base of the foliage. These flowers eventually transition into small, aggregate fruits that develop from the receptacle. The overall structure allows the plant to colonize ground space effectively through its creeping runners.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of West and Central Africa, specifically spanning across countries like Nigeria, Cameroon, and Ghana. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland environments where humidity levels remain consistently high. Within these habitats, it is often found growing in forest clearings or along the margins of riverbanks. The distribution is closely tied to the presence of seasonal rainfall patterns characteristic of the Guineo-Congolian region. This specific ecological niche allows the species to spread throughout the dense vegetation of the African tropics.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, localized swelling, and intense itching if the sap or fruit comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals may experience gastrointestinal distress including nausea or stomach cramps if the plant parts are ingested accidentally. There is also a potential risk of systemic allergic responses in people with known sensitivities to the Rosaceae family. Monitoring for redness or hives is recommended after any physical contact with the vegetation. Consult a medical professional if any severe swelling or breathing difficulties occur after exposure.
