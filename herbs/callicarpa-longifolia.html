Callicarpa longifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Callicarpa longifolia?

Callicarpa longifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows a lineage that places it under the order Lamiales. This specific plant is a part of the family Lamiaceae, and its taxonomic identity is further defined by its placement in the genus Callicarpa, with its unique designation as the species longifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Callicarpa longifolia has elongated, lanceolate leaves that measure between 10 and 25 centimeters in length and are arranged in opposite pairs along its woody stems.

The leaf margins are finely serrated, while the upper surfaces exhibit a smooth, lustrous texture. Small, tubular flowers emerge in dense, axillary clusters that form around the stem during the blooming season. These floral structures eventually give way to distinctive, spherical drupes that turn a vibrant purple color as they ripen.

The overall architecture of the plant consists of upright, branching shrubs that can reach significant heights in their natural habitat.

      What are the herbal preparations of Callicarpa longifolia?

      Callicarpa longifolia is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using the dried leaves and fruits. The leaves are often simmered in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal ailments. When applied externally, the plant matter is crushed into a poultice to treat skin irritations or localized inflammation. These various methods allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be delivered effectively depending on the intended therapeutic use.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Callicarpa longifolia can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested by humans or animals. While the plant is not documented as highly toxic, direct contact with the foliage or berries may trigger redness or itching in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of the plant parts can lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramps. There is limited scientific research available regarding long-term systemic effects or specific chemical toxicity in humans. Therefore, cautious handling is advised to prevent unexpected physical reactions.
