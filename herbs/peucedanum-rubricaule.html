Peucedanum rubricaule

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Peucedanum rubricaule has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by deeply divided, pinnate leaves and small, yellowish-green flowers arranged in compound umbels. The stems are typically erect and may exhibit reddish pigmentation near the base or along the nodes. Leaflets are narrow and often display a finely serrated or lobed margin that contributes to a feathery appearance. The inflorescence consists of multiple smaller umbels held atop a common peduncle, supported by bracts at the base. Fruit production results in small, schizocarpic structures that are characteristic of the Apiaceae family.

      Peucedanum rubricaule is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture derived from its dried aerial parts.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Peucedanum rubricaule can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and potential systemic toxicity if ingested due to the presence of furanocoumarins. These specific compounds are known to induce phytophototoxicity, which occurs when sunlight reacts with the plant oils on the skin to cause blistering. Contact with the sap may lead to redness, swelling, or painful rashes that persist for several days. If the plant is consumed, it can disrupt digestive processes and lead to more serious internal complications. It is essential to avoid all direct contact with the foliage and stems to prevent these adverse reactions.
