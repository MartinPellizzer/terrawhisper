What to know about Nelumbo nucifera (lotus) before using it medicinally

Nelumbo nucifera, commonly known as lotus, is a revered herb that has been prized for its remarkable health properties, which include reducing inflammation, improving digestion, and supporting weight loss.

As a versatile horticultural specimen, it is grown in a variety of water-based environments, including ponds and lakes, and is prized for its stunning flowers and foliage. Botanically speaking, Nelumbo nucifera is a member of the Nelumbonaceae family and is characterized by its unique rhizome and flower morphology.

Throughout history, the lotus has been celebrated in cultures such as ancient Egypt and India, where it was revered for its spiritual and medicinal significance.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Nelumbo nucifera.

What are the medicinal properties of Nelumbo nucifera?

Nelumbo nucifera helps with various health conditions such as diarrhea, dysentery, and skin allergies due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. It has been used in traditional medicine for its cooling and detoxifying effects. Lotus has been used to treat fever, rheumatism, and skin conditions.

The active constituents of Nelumbo nucifera include alkaloids, flavonoids, and saponins, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds exhibit anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial activities, which are responsible for the plant's therapeutic effects. The exact composition of the plant can vary depending on the region and growing conditions.

The rhizome, leaves, and flowers of Nelumbo nucifera are most commonly used for medicinal purposes. The rhizome contains the highest concentration of bioactive compounds and is often used to make decoctions or infusions. The leaves and flowers are also used to prepare infusions or are dried for later use.

Improper use of Nelumbo nucifera can cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and interactions with other medications. The plant's high saponin content can cause digestive problems if taken in excessive amounts. It is essential to consult with a healthcare professional before using the plant medicinally.

Precautions when using Nelumbo nucifera medicinally include consulting with a healthcare professional, especially for pregnant or breastfeeding women. It is also essential to follow proper dosages and preparation methods to avoid adverse effects. The plant should not be used in cases of allergies or sensitivities.

What are the horticulural aspects of Nelumbo nucifera?

Nelumbo nucifera grow in subtropical and tropical climates, preferring full sun to partial shade and still or slow-moving water temperatures between 15°C to 30°C.

Plant Nelumbo nucifera tubers 10-20 cm deep in late spring or early summer, after the danger of frost has passed, and water the soil consistently to prevent drought stress. The rhizome grows up to 30 cm deep into the soil.

Harvest Nelumbo nucifera flowers, leaves, and rhizomes when mature, typically in late summer or early fall. Flowers and leaves can be harvested continuously, while rhizomes should be dug up every 2-3 years to promote new growth.

Common pests of Nelumbo nucifera include aquatic snails, water striders, and insects such as water lilies beetles and nematodes. Common diseases include fungal infections such as root rot, crown rot, and leaf spot caused by pathogens like Phytophthora and Pythium.

What are the botanical characteristics of Nelumbo nucifera?

Nelumbo nucifera, also known as the sacred lotus or Indian lotus, is a deciduous aquatic plant that exhibits several distinctive botanical characteristics.

The plant's stems, which can grow up to 2 meters in height, are thick and fleshy, with a reddish-brown coloration. Its leaves are large, typically measuring between 20-50 cm in length, and are deeply lobed with three to five lobes that resemble the fingers of a hand. The leaves are also slightly waxy and have a velvety texture.

Nelumbo nucifera's flowers are perhaps its most striking feature, consisting of showy, fragrant blooms that can reach up to 15 cm in diameter. The flower's petals are white or yellowish-white, with a distinctive cup-shaped structure at the base. The plant's fruit is an oblong capsule containing numerous seeds.

Additionally, Nelumbo nucifera has a unique ability to absorb oxygen from both the air and water, allowing it to thrive in shallow waters with low oxygen levels.

What is the taxonomy of Nelumbo nucifera?

Nelumbo nucifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae, class Magnoliopsida, order Nymphaeales, family Nelumbonaceae, and genus Nelumbo.

Specifically, it is a monocotyledonous flowering plant commonly known as the sacred lotus or Indian lotus.

What is the geographic distribution of Nelumbo nucifera?

Nelumbo nucifera, commonly known as the sacred lotus, is native to tropical Asia and Southeastern Europe.

Its geographic distribution spans across countries such as China, India, Japan, Korea, and Southeast Asian nations including Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, and Thailand. It has also been introduced and naturalized in various parts of the world, including Africa, North America, and South America.

The sacred lotus is typically found in shallow waters with muddy or sandy bottoms, where it thrives in temperate climates with warm summers and mild winters.

What is the life cycle of Nelumbo nucifera?

Nelumbo nucifera, commonly known as the sacred lotus, has a unique and complex life cycle.

The plant starts its life cycle as a rhizome, which is a type of underground stem that produces new shoots and roots. In late spring to early summer, the rhizome begins to grow above ground, producing leaves and flowers.

The flowers bloom for several weeks in mid-summer, after which they produce seeds. The seeds are dispersed by wind or water, and germinate into new plants when conditions are favorable.

After about 30-40 days, the seedlings develop into mature plants that can live for up to 100 years or more in ideal conditions.
