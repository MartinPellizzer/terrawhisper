Lonicera modesta

What's the taxonomical classification of Lonicera modesta?

Lonicera modesta belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Dipsacales. This plant is a representative of the family Caprifoliaceae, which is further categorized into the genus Lonicera, ultimately reaching its specific identification at the species level as Lonicera modesta.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat respiratory ailments such as coughs, sore throats, and congestion, as well as various skin irritations and digestive disturbances. The berries and bark contain phytochemicals that possess antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. These natural compounds help soothe inflamed mucous membranes in the throat and lungs. When applied topically, extracts from the plant may assist in healing minor wounds or skin abrasions. Traditional medicine practitioners often utilize different parts of the shrub to target specific inflammatory responses within the body.

What are the herbal preparations of Lonicera modesta?

Lonicera modesty is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical tincture derived from its dried stems, leaves, and flowers. Practitioners often boil the woody stems for an extended period to extract the medicinal compounds necessary for a potent decoction. For more delicate parts like the flowers, a shorter steeping process in hot water creates a milder infusion. Tinctures made with alcohol serve as a concentrated method for long-term storage and standardized dosing. These various preparations allow the plant's properties to be utilized for both internal consumption and external applications.

What side-effects this plant can have?

LoniceraModesta can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the plant's stems or leaves may trigger redness, itching, or swelling in sensitive individuals. If any part of the shrub is eaten, it can lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. While specific toxicity levels are not extensively documented for humans, caution is advised when handling the plant. Always wash hands thoroughly after touching the vegetation to prevent accidental ingestion or irritation.
