Lemon Myrtle, scientifically known as Backhousia citriodora, is a medicinal herb native to Australia, valued for its distinct lemon-like aroma and potential health benefits.

This adaptogen and spice is renowned for its rich content of citral, a rare active compound with antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Traditionally used by Indigenous Australians for digestive ailments and as a natural remedy for respiratory and skin conditions, it has also been incorporated into cultural rituals and culinary practices. Scientific and Botanical Profile

Lemon Myrtle, with botanical name backhousia citriodora, is a small evergreen shrub belonging to the Myrtaceae family, known for its distinct aromatic properties.

Native to the eastern regions of Australia, particularly Queensland and New South Wales, this plant thrives in subtropical climates. Morphologically, it features narrow, lance-shaped leaves with a glossy surface and a strong lemony scent, while its small white flowers are followed by spherical, blue-black berries. Commonly referred to as Citron Myrtle, Backhousia Lemon, Citrus Myrtle, Citronella Bush, and Citriodora Backhousia, it is valued for its essential oils and culinary uses.

Its botanical profile highlights its unique combination of fragrance, medicinal potential, and ornamental appeal.

History and Cultural Relevance

Lemon Myrtle was used by Indigenous Australian communities for centuries, valued not only for its distinctive citrus scent but also for its medicinal and spiritual significance.

The plant has deep roots in Aboriginal culture, where it was traditionally used in healing practices, as a source of food, and in ceremonial contexts. Its essential oils were incorporated into rituals and used to treat ailments such as digestive issues, skin conditions, and respiratory infections, reflecting its importance in traditional medicine systems. Today, Lemon Myrtle continues to be valued for its antifungal, antibacterial, and antioxidant properties, with modern applications in natural remedies for conditions like acne, fungal infections, and even as a flavoring agent in organic products.

Its enduring presence in both historical and contemporary contexts highlights its cultural relevance and the ongoing respect for its therapeutic potential.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Lemon Myrtle contains a rich array of bioactive compounds, including high concentrations of citral, a monoterpene that contributes to its distinctive lemon scent and offers antimicrobial properties.

It is also a source of flavonoids such as quercetin and kaempferol, which exhibit antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects, along with essential oils containing limonene and geraniol. Nutritionally, Lemon Myrtle is low in calories but provides trace amounts of vitamins like vitamin C and some minerals such as potassium and magnesium, though it is not a significant source of macronutrients.

The active compounds in Lemon Myrtle work synergistically to support immune function, reduce oxidative stress, and potentially inhibit the growth of harmful bacteria and fungi by disrupting cell membranes and interfering with microbial metabolic processes.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Backhousia citriodora has a wide range of medicinal properties that benefit multiple body systems, including the respiratory, immune, and digestive systems.

Its essential oil, rich in citral and other terpenes, exhibits strong antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, making it useful for treating respiratory infections, reducing inflammation, and supporting overall immune function. Compared to similar herbs like lemon grass or lemongrass (Cymbopogon), backhousia citriodora offers a more potent and consistent concentration of active compounds, particularly citral, which contributes to its stronger antimicrobial properties. It is also valued for its calming effects on the nervous system, helping to reduce stress and promote mental clarity, which sets it apart from many other aromatic herbs.

Overall, its versatility and potency make it a valuable plant in both traditional and modern herbal medicine.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Backhousia citriodora has a variety of forms available, including fresh leaves, dried material, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules, each offering different methods of preparation and application.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period, while an infusion involves soaking the leaves in cold or warm water. For topical use, the essential oil or powder can be applied directly to the skin, often diluted with a carrier oil, to treat minor skin conditions or for aromatherapy. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision, with a lower dose if safe.

It is advised to use backhousia citriodora sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to avoid potential side effects and ensure safety.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Backhousia citriodora can be used as a medicinal plant with potential therapeutic benefits, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, and skin irritation in some individuals. There is limited research on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with anticoagulants or sedatives due to possible synergistic effects. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient data on safety during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with small doses, monitor for adverse reactions, and seek professional guidance when incorporating backhousia citriodora into a health regimen.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Backhousia citriodora grows best in well-drained, fertile soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, in a sunny to partially shaded location with consistent moisture.

It thrives in temperate climates and requires regular watering, especially during dry periods, but should not be overwatered to avoid root rot. To ensure healthy growth, prune dead or diseased branches annually and apply mulch to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Harvesting should occur in late spring to early autumn when the leaves contain the highest concentration of essential oils, using sharp scissors to cut the foliage without damaging the plant.

For optimal potency, the harvested leaves should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers away from light and heat.
