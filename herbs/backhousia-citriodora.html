Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is a small evergreen shrub native to Australia, known for its strong lemon-scented leaves and potential health benefits, particularly in supporting digestive health and reducing inflammation.

What is Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia Citriodora)?

Lemon Myrtle, or Backhousia citriodora, is a small evergreen tree native to northeastern Australia, known for its highly aromatic leaves that emit a distinct lemon-like fragrance and are widely used in herbal medicine for their antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Lemon Myrtle, or Backhousia citriodora, is a species of flowering plant in the myrtle family (Myrtaceae), native to the eastern coast of Australia, particularly Queensland and New South Wales.

Common Names and Synonyms

Lemon Myrtle, scientifically known as Backhousia citriodora, is also referred to by several common names including Lemon Myrtle, Lemon Tree, and Citron Myrtle, and is native to the tropical regions of Australia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is a small evergreen tree or shrub characterized by its narrow, lance-shaped leaves that are glossy green on the upper surface and pale grey-white on the underside, with a strong lemony fragrance.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is native to the eastern coast of Australia, specifically found in the tropical and subtropical regions of Queensland and New South Wales, growing in wet sclerophyll forests and rainforests.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves and flowers of Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) are primarily used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly harvested part due to their high concentration of essential oils containing citral and other bioactive compounds.

Phytochemical Composition

Lemon myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) contains a high concentration of citral, a monoterpene ketone responsible for its distinctive lemon-like aroma and many of its pharmacological properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Lemon myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) exhibits significant pharmacological properties, including potent antioxidant, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antifungal activities, primarily due to its high concentration of citral, a key bioactive compound.

Mechanisms of Action

The essential oil of Lemon Myrtle contains high concentrations of citral, which exhibits antimicrobial, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory properties by disrupting microbial cell membranes and modulating inflammatory pathways in the body.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Lemon Myrtle is used to treat respiratory infections, digestive issues, skin conditions, anxiety, and inflammation.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is typically prepared as a powdered form, essential oil, or infused into topical applications, with its concentrated essential oil being the most commonly used form in herbal medicine.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) for herbal use ranges from 500 to 1,000 milligrams per day, typically divided into two to three doses, with the exact amount depending on the intended therapeutic effect and individual response.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Lemon Myrtle is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, some individuals may experience mild gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or skin irritation, particularly with excessive or prolonged use.

Drug Interactions

Lemon myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) may interact with certain medications due to its potential effects on liver enzymes and its antimicrobial properties, which could influence the metabolism or efficacy of drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Lemon myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and for children has not been fully established.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is typically cultivated in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil, often in regions of Australia where it thrives in full sun and requires minimal watering once established.

Processing and Extraction

Lemon myrtle is typically processed by drying the leaves and then grinding them into a fine powder or using them fresh in various herbal preparations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to preserve its potency and extend its shelf life, which is typically up to 12 months when properly stored.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) has shown promising evidence for its antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties, with studies indicating potential applications in herbal medicine for treating infections, skin conditions, and digestive issues.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) has been traditionally used by Indigenous Australian communities for its antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties, with applications in treating respiratory infections, skin conditions, and digestive ailments.

Regulatory Status

Lemon Myrtle (Backhousia citriodora) is not approved as a medicinal herb in the United States or Europe, but it is recognized in Australian and New Zealand traditional medicine and is subject to specific regulations regarding its use in food and cosmetic products.
