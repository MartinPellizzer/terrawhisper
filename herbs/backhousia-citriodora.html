Scientific and Botanical Profile

Lemon Myrtle, with botanical name Backhousia citriodora, is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree native to various regions of Australia, including Queensland, the Northern Territory, Northern New South Wales, and the Tropical Rainforests of Australia. It belongs to the family Myrtaceae and is commonly known by several names such as Backhousia Lemon, Citron Myrtle, Citrus Myrtle, Citriodora Backhousia, Citriodora Myrtle, Citronella Myrtle, Citronella Tree, Australian Lemon Tree, and Citronella Tree. Morphologically, it is characterized by its aromatic, lemon-scented leaves that are narrow, lance-shaped, and have a rough, leathery texture, with small white flowers that bloom in clusters. This versatile plant is also found in coastal regions of northern Australia, tropical areas of Queensland, mangrove swamps of northern Australia, coastal areas of the Northern Territory, and tropical savannas of northern Australia, where it thrives in humid and warm climates.

History and Cultural Relevance

Lemon Myrtle was used by Indigenous Australian communities for thousands of years as a valuable plant with a wide range of applications in daily life, traditional medicine, and spiritual practices. The plant, native to the eastern coast of Australia, was deeply embedded in the cultural and medicinal knowledge of Aboriginal peoples, who utilized its aromatic leaves for treating ailments such as digestive issues, respiratory infections, and skin conditions. In traditional rituals, Lemon Myrtle was often used in ceremonies to purify spaces, mark significant life events, or as an offering to ancestral spirits, reflecting its sacred status within Indigenous communities. Today, its antibacterial and antioxidant properties continue to make it a popular ingredient in natural remedies, including teas, topical balms, and essential oils, with its distinctive lemon scent still valued for its therapeutic and culinary uses. The plant's enduring presence in both historical and contemporary contexts highlights its deep cultural significance and ongoing relevance in holistic health practices.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Lemon Myrtle contains a rich array of bioactive compounds, including high concentrations of citral, a monoterpene that contributes to its distinctive lemon scent and numerous therapeutic properties. It is also a source of flavonoids such as quercetin and kaempferol, which possess strong antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Additionally, Lemon Myrtle contains essential oils, alkaloids, and various terpenes that contribute to its antimicrobial and antifungal activities. Nutritionally, it is low in calories but provides trace amounts of vitamins such as vitamin C and some B-complex vitamins, along with minerals like potassium and calcium. These compounds work synergistically in the body to support immune function, reduce oxidative stress, and potentially offer protective effects against chronic diseases.

To ensure safe use, it is advisable to follow recommended dosages, consult with a qualified practitioner, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Backhousia citriodora grows best in well-drained, sandy or loamy soils with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, in full sun to partial shade, and requires consistent moisture without waterlogging. It thrives in warm, tropical to subtropical climates and benefits from regular watering during dry periods, though it is relatively drought-tolerant once established. For optimal growth, regular pruning to encourage bushy growth and pest monitoring are essential, along with mulching to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. The plant is typically harvested when the leaves and flowers are in full bloom, usually between late spring and early autumn, by cutting the stems just above a node to ensure continued growth. To preserve its potency, the harvested parts should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain its aromatic and medicinal properties.
