What's the taxonomical classification of Daphniphyllum calycinum?

Daphniphyllum calycinum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Saxifragales and is a member of the family Daphniphyllaceae. Finally, its taxonomic hierarchy is completed by its placement in the genus Daphniphyllum, identifying its specific species as calycinum.

These combined biological actions suggest that the species could be useful in developing new therapeutic treatments. Researchers continue to study its chemical constituents to better understand these specific mechanisms.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", an ethyl acetate extract of Daphniphyllum calycinum leaves showed significant activity in a 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) free-radical assay. Two specific compounds were isolated from the leaves, identified as the novel flavonoid diglycoside 5,6,7,4'-tetrahydroxyflavonol 3-O-rutinoside (1) and the previously known kaempferol 3-O-neohesperidoside (2). Both compounds 1 and 2 were found to be moderately active as antioxidants in the DPPH assay.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating viral infections, specifically targeting Enterovirus 71 to alleviate related symptoms. Researchers have investigated its pharmacological properties to determine how it might inhibit viral replication in human cells. The bioactive compounds within the plant demonstrate potential in managing inflammatory responses caused by such pathogens. These medicinal applications are often studied within the context of traditional herbal medicine and modern pharmacology. Further clinical exploration is necessary to fully understand its efficacy in human subjects.

According to a study published by "Phytochemistry", Daphniphyllum calycinum Benth. is used to treat Enterovirus 71 (EV71), which is a major causative agent of hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD). The leaves and stems of the plant contain cyanodaphcalycines A-C (1-3) and daphmanidin G (4). In biological evaluations using human malignant embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma (RD) cells, compounds 3 and 4 showed potent antiviral effects against EV71. Specifically, compound 3 had an EC50 value of 3.78 ± 0.23 μg/mL, while compound 4 had an EC50 value of 6.87 ± 0.30 μg/mL. These results demonstrate superior efficacy compared to ribavirin, which had an EC50 of 65.77 ± 0.11 μg/mL.
