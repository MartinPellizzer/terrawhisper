Botanical Identification of Burdock (Arctium lappa)

To identify Burdock (Arctium lappa) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Burdock, or Arctium lappa, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Arctium, and species Arctium lappa. These classifications place it within the flowering plants, specifically in the carrot family, and highlight its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name for Burdock is Arctium lappa, first described by the Swedish botanist Carl von Linné in 1753. It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Arctium tomentosum and Arctium minus. Common names such as Burdock root, Great burdock, and Gromwell reflect its widespread use in herbal medicine. Regional and historical names vary, but the scientific name remains the most reliable identifier for this plant.

Morphological Characteristics

Burdock is an annual herb with a thick, fleshy taproot. Its stem is erect, hairy, and branched. Leaves are large, heart-shaped, and lobed, with a rough texture. The plant produces a spiny, globular flower head, and its fruit is a small, brown achene with a hooked pappus.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Burdock can be distinguished by its large, heart-shaped leaves with rough, hairy surfaces and thick, purple-black stems. Its unique feature is the spherical, spiny bur with hooked bracts that cling to clothing. These characteristics help differentiate it from other similar plants in the burdock family. The leaves are usually larger and more robust compared to related species.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Burdock is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and cold winters. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found at low to mid-elevation areas, typically between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Burdock blooms from June to August, producing purple to pinkish flowers. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. For identification, the plant is best harvested in early summer when the leaves are fully developed but before flowering begins.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Burdock (Arctium lappa) is closely related to species within the same genus, Arctium. It shares family traits with the Asteraceae family, such as composite flowers and alternate leaves. Its closest relatives include other burdock species like Arctium minus, which exhibit similar morphological and ecological characteristics.
