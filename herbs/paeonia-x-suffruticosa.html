Tree Peony, scientifically known as Paeonia x suffruticosa, is a flowering plant widely recognized for its medicinal properties and historical significance in traditional medicine.

This perennial shrub, native to China, has been used for centuries as a medicinal herb and adaptogen, valued for its ability to support physical and emotional resilience. Scientific and Botanical Profile

Tree Peony, with botanical name Paeonia x suffruticosa, is a hybrid shrub belonging to the family Ranunculaceae, known for its ornamental value and long blooming period.

Native to China and Japan, it is commonly referred to as Chinese Tree Peony, Japanese Tree Peony, or Shrub Peony, reflecting its widespread cultivation in both regions. Morphologically, it is a deciduous shrub with multiple stems, woody at the base, and broad, palmate leaves that are dark green and leathery in texture. The flowers are large, showy, and typically have five to seven petals, with a rich array of colors including red, pink, white, and yellow, often accompanied by a fragrant aroma.

This plant is also known as Chinese Peony, Japanese Peony, or simply Tree Peony, and is celebrated for its hardiness and ability to thrive in a variety of garden settings.

History and Cultural Relevance

Tree Peony was used for centuries in various cultures for its medicinal properties, ornamental beauty, and symbolic meaning, with roots tracing back to ancient China, where it was revered as a symbol of prosperity and good fortune.

In traditional Chinese medicine, the petals and roots of the Tree Peony were employed to treat ailments such as inflammation, fever, and skin conditions, with its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties still recognized today in herbal remedies. The plant also played a significant role in cultural rituals, including weddings and festivals, where its blossoms were used in decorations and as offerings to deities. In folk traditions, Tree Peony was believed to bring luck and protect against evil spirits, and its cultivation was often associated with spiritual practices and healing ceremonies.

Today, the Tree Peony remains an important part of both horticulture and traditional medicine, bridging ancient wisdom with modern applications in natural health practices.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Tree Peony contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including alkaloids, flavonoids, essential oils, and terpenes, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

These compounds are responsible for its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and neuroprotective effects. In terms of nutritional content, Tree Peony is a good source of vitamins such as vitamin C and some B-complex vitamins, along with minerals like potassium and magnesium. It also contains a range of antioxidants that help neutralize free radicals and support immune function.

The mechanism of action involves these active compounds interacting with cellular pathways to reduce oxidative stress and modulate inflammatory responses in the body.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Paeonia x suffruticosa has been widely recognized for its potent medicinal properties and diverse health benefits, particularly in supporting the cardiovascular, respiratory, and nervous systems.

It is known to improve circulation, reduce inflammation, and alleviate symptoms of anxiety and insomnia, making it a valuable herb for stress-related disorders. Compared to other peony species like Paeonia lactiflora, Paeonia x suffruticosa offers a more balanced profile of compounds, including higher concentrations of paeoniflorin, which enhances its anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects. Its unique combination of alkaloids and flavonoids also contributes to its ability to support immune function and promote skin health, setting it apart from other traditional herbs.

This plant is often preferred in herbal formulations for its superior potency and broader therapeutic range, making it a versatile choice for holistic health practices.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Paeonia x suffruticosa has a variety of forms available, including fresh roots, dried roots, tinctures, powders, essential oils, and capsules, each offering different methods of preparation and application.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried roots in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the roots for a longer period to extract more active compounds. For topical use, the powdered form or essential oil can be applied directly to the skin, often for pain relief or skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried root per cup of tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision, with lower dosages.

Due to its potency, it is advised to use paeonia x suffruticosa sparingly, with a maximum duration of use not exceeding two weeks unless guided by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Paeonia x suffruticosa can be used medicinally for its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and sedative properties, but it must be used with caution due to its potential toxicity.

Side effects may include gastrointestinal upset, drowsiness, and in severe cases, liver damage or toxicity, especially with long-term or high-dose use. It may interact with medications such as anticoagulants, antidepressants, and sedatives, increasing the risk of adverse effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid paeonia x suffruticosa due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic liver disease or other serious illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, always consult a qualified healthcare professional, use it in recommended doses, and avoid prolonged use without medical supervision.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Paeonia x suffruticosa grows best in well-drained, fertile soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, thriving in full sun to partial shade.

It requires regular watering during the growing season, though it is relatively drought-tolerant once established, and benefits from mulching to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. For optimal growth, regular pruning of dead or damaged wood in early spring and fertilization with a balanced fertilizer in late winter or early spring are recommended. Harvesting should occur in late summer to early autumn when the flowers are fully open but before they begin to wilt, using sharp scissors or pruning shears to cut the stems just below the flower buds.

To preserve its medicinal potency, the harvested flowers should be dried in a cool, dark, well-ventilated area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain their active compounds.
