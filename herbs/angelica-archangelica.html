What to know about Angelica archangelica (angelica) before using it medicinally

Angelica archangelica, commonly known as angelica, is a herb that boasts a range of health benefits, including reducing inflammation and improving digestion, thereby alleviating symptoms of conditions such as irritable bowel syndrome.

As a versatile and hardy perennial, angelica is a popular choice for gardeners due to its ability to thrive in a variety of conditions and its ornamental value. From a botanical perspective, angelica belongs to the parsley family and is characterized by its umbels of small, white, or pale yellow flowers and its distinctive, fleshy stem.

In traditional medicine and folklore, angelica has been referenced in ancient texts, such as the Swedish herbalist Linnaeus' writings, and was even used by medieval physicians to treat a range of ailments.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Angelica archangelica.

What are the medicinal properties of Angelica archangelica?

Angelica archangelica helps with various health issues, including digestive problems, fever reduction, and respiratory issues. Its extracts have been used to treat sore throats, bronchitis, and asthma. It also exhibits anti-inflammatory properties.

The active constituents of angelica archangelica include volatile oils, flavonoids, and phenolic acids. These compounds are responsible for its medicinal properties, including antimicrobial and antifungal activities. The plant's essential oil also contains compounds like angelic acid and isoangelic acid.

The parts of the plant used for medicinal purposes are the root, leaf, and stem. The root is the most commonly used part, containing high concentrations of bioactive compounds. The leaves and stems are also used, but to a lesser extent.

Using angelica archangelica improperly can cause side effects such as skin irritation, allergic reactions, and interactions with other medications. Prolonged use of the plant can also lead to gastrointestinal issues, including nausea and diarrhea.

When using angelica archangelica medicinally, it is essential to follow proper guidelines and precautions. These include consulting a healthcare professional before use, especially if pregnant or breastfeeding. Additionally, monitor blood sugar levels and blood pressure, as the plant may interact with certain medications.

What are the horticulural aspects of Angelica archangelica?

Angelica archangelica grow in full sun to partial shade, preferring moist, rich soil with a pH range of 6.0-7.0. It thrives in areas with moderate temperatures between 15-20°C (59-68°F) and high humidity.

To plant angelica, choose a location with well-draining soil and space plants 1-2 feet apart. Plant seeds or divisions in spring or fall, about 1-2 inches deep. Water regularly, keeping the soil consistently moist during the first year. Fertilize lightly after the first growing season.

For optimal harvest, wait until the stems are 2-3 feet tall and the umbels are fully developed. Cut the stems near the base, leaving a few inches of stem attached to the crown. Harvest roots in fall, after the foliage has died back. Use scissors to trim the leaves to within an inch of the base of the stem.

Pests such as aphids, slugs, and snails can infest angelica. Fungal diseases like powdery mildew, root rot, and leaf spot can also affect the plant. Regularly inspect the plant for signs of pests or diseases and take action promptly to prevent further damage.

What are the botanical characteristics of Angelica archangelica?

Angelica archangelica, also known as wild celery or Archangel's Root, is a perennial herbaceous plant that belongs to the carrot family (Apiaceae).

Its botanical characteristics are quite distinct. The plant grows up to 2 meters tall, with a large, hollow stem that is typically green and hairless. The leaves are tripinnate, meaning they have three levels of pinnae, which are long and narrow, with a pointed tip.

The leaves are also aromatic and can be used as a flavoring agent in cooking. The plant produces umbels of small, yellowish-green flowers that are arranged on a stalk-like structure called an umbelicle. The flowers are followed by small, oval-shaped fruits that contain numerous seeds. The root of the plant is thick and fleshy, with a sweet, slightly bitter taste, which has been used for centuries in traditional medicine and as a flavoring agent in food and beverages.

Overall, Angelica archangelica's botanical characteristics make it a striking and unique plant, both visually and aromatically.

What is the geographic distribution of Angelica archangelica?

Angelica archangelica, also known as wild angelica or giant angelica, is native to northern Europe and Asia.

Its geographic distribution includes the British Isles, Norway, Sweden, Finland, Russia, Poland, and parts of Eastern Europe. It can be found growing in damp meadows, woodland edges, and along riversides at elevations up to 1,500 meters above sea level.

The plant is widely distributed across Europe, but its range has been reduced in recent centuries due to habitat destruction and over-harvesting for medicinal purposes.

What are the historical uses of Angelica archangelica?

Angelica archangelica, also known as wild celery, has been used for centuries in traditional medicine and cooking.

In medieval times, it was believed to have angelic properties, hence its name. The root of the plant was used to treat a range of ailments, including indigestion, flatulence, and rheumatism. It was also used to flavor drinks, such as ale and beer, and to add depth to soups and stews.

In Scandinavian cuisine, it is still used today in dishes like pickled angelica and angelica jelly.

Medieval herbals further linked angelica to St. Michael's sword, symbolizing protection against disease and evil.
