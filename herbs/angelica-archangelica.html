Archangelica (Angelica archangelica): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Archangelica, or Angelica archangelica, is a biennial herb native to the northern regions of Europe and Asia, known for its medicinal properties and historical use in traditional herbal medicine.

What is Archangelica (Angelica Archangelica)?

Archangelica, also known as Angelica archangelica, is a biennial herb native to cool, moist climates of northern Europe and Asia, characterized by its tall, hollow stems and large, umbrella-shaped flowers.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Archangelica is Angelica archangelica. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Angelica, and species Angelica archangelica. It is commonly known as angelica in Europe and archangelica in North America. It is also referred to as wild angelica in some regions and has botanical synonyms such as Angelica sylvestris and Angelica officinalis.

Plant Description and Morphology

Archangelica, or Angelica archangelica, is a biennial or perennial herb native to northern Europe and parts of Asia, characterized by its tall, slender stem that can reach up to 2 meters in height, large compound leaves with a distinctive serrated edge, and clusters of small, greenish-yellow flowers that bloom in late summer.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) is native to the cool, moist regions of northern Europe, northern Africa, and western Asia, primarily growing in mountainous areas and shaded woodlands.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, and seeds of Angelica archangelica are used medicinally, with the roots being the primary part harvested for their aromatic and medicinal properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Archangelica contains a diverse array of phytochemicals, including volatile oils such as methyl eugenol, limonene, and alpha-pinene, as well as flavonoids like apigenin and luteolin, alkaloids, and various phenolic compounds that contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) exhibits pharmacological properties including antispasmodic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, primarily attributed to its high concentration of volatile oils, coumarins, and flavonoids.

Mechanisms of Action

Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) exerts its effects through various mechanisms, including its ability to stimulate the production of digestive enzymes, enhance bile secretion, and possess mild antispasmodic properties that help alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support digestive health, alleviate respiratory symptoms, and promote circulation due to its high concentration of volatile oils and bioactive compounds.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Archangelica is typically prepared as a tincture, tea, or extract, with the most common method involving maceration of the dried root in alcohol or vinegar to extract its active compounds.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) for herbal use ranges from 250 to 500 mg of standardized extract, taken two to three times daily, or as directed by a qualified healthcare practitioner.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, headaches, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and it is contraindicated during pregnancy, lactation, and for those with bleeding disorders due to its potential to increase bleeding risk.

Drug Interactions

Archangelica may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, sedatives, and antidepressants, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding, drowsiness, or serotonin syndrome due to its mild anticoagulant and sedative properties.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or skin irritation in some individuals, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with bleeding disorders due to its potential anticoagulant properties.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Archangelica is typically cultivated in cool, moist climates with well-drained soil, and harvested primarily during late summer to early autumn when the plant's roots and leaves contain the highest concentration of essential oils.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Archangelica involves harvesting the fresh or dried root and aerial parts, followed by methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain essential oils, tinctures, or powdered forms for medicinal use.

Storage and Shelf Life

Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) has explored its potential pharmacological properties, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects, though more rigorous clinical studies are needed to confirm its efficacy in human health applications.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Archangelica has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its digestive, respiratory, and circulatory benefits, with historical records dating back to ancient Greek and Roman times for treating ailments such as indigestion, coughs, and fever.

Regulatory Status

In the United States, Archangelica (Angelica archangelica) is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the FDA when used as a food or dietary supplement, but it is not approved as a drug, and its use as a medicinal herb is regulated under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA).
