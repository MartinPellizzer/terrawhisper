Botanical Identification of Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa)

To identify Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Parsnip, or Pastinaca sativa, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Pastinaca, and species sativa. These classifications help identify its botanical relationships and place it within the broader plant kingdom.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Parsnip is Pastinaca sativa, first described by the Swedish botanist Carl von Linné in 1753. This name is widely accepted in botanical literature. It is also known by several synonyms, including Sium sativa and Pastinaca sativa var. sativa. Common names such as parsnip, wild carrot, and earth apple are also used in various regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa) is a biennial herb with a taproot system. It has a stout, cylindrical root that is typically white or yellowish. The stem is usually short and stout, emerging from the base of the plant. The leaves are alternate, heart-shaped, and have a rough texture. The inflorescence is a complex umbel, with small yellow flowers that bloom in late summer. The fruit is a small, dry schizocarp, containing two one-seeded mericarps.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa) can be distinguished by its long, tapering root with a pale yellow to brown skin and white, crunchy flesh. It has alternate, heart-shaped leaves and yellow flowers in umbels. It is often confused with celery and parsnip root, but it has a more robust root and a distinct, sweet, nutty flavor.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa) is native to Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and mild winters. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Parsnip blooms from June to August, producing yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around September. For identification, the plant is most easily recognized in its mature stage, typically from late fall to early winter when the roots are fully developed.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa) belongs to the Apiaceae family, closely related to species like carrot (Daucus carota) and parsley (Petroselinum sativum). It shares key morphological and genetic traits with other members of the genus Pastinaca, distinguishing it from related genera within the family. Its phylogenetic position reflects evolutionary ties to other umbelliferous plants.
