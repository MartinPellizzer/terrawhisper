Buddleja crispa

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Buddlejia crispa has an erect, woody shrub habit characterized by lanceolate, serrated leaves and dense, terminal panicles of small, tubular, pale lilac to white flowers. The foliage is notably rugose with a textured surface and a distinctively wrinkled appearance. Its stems are often branched and can become somewhat woody at the base as the plant matures. The inflorescences consist of numerous tiny florets tightly packed along elongated stalks. These flowers are typically accompanied by small, globose fruit that develop after successful pollination.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude mountainous regions of western China, specifically within the provinces of Sichuan, Yunnan, and Gansu. It typically thrives in subalpine environments where the terrain is rocky and well-drained. These botanical specimens are often found growing along mountain slopes and in forest margins at elevations ranging from 2,500 to 4,000 meters. The specific microclimates of these eastern Himalayan foothills provide the necessary moisture and temperate conditions for its survival. Because of its specialized habitat requirements, its natural range remains relatively localized to these specific Asian highlands.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Buddlejia crispia can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, redness, and itching when the plant's sap or foliage comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals may experience swelling or localized irritation if they handle the leaves or stems without protective gear. Ingestion of the plant may lead to gastrointestinal distress, including nausea or stomach discomfort. While severe toxicity is not widely documented, caution is advised to prevent accidental exposure to children or pets.
