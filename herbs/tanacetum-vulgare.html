Tanacetum vulgare

What's the taxonomical classification of Tanacetum vulgare?

Tanacetum vulgare belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. The plant is a prominent representative of the family Asteraceae, which is characterized by its composite flower heads. Within this family, it is categorized under the genus Tanacetum, and its specific biological identity is defined by the species name vulgare.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Tanacetum vulgare contains parthenolide, flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and various essential oils that provide its primary therapeutic properties. Parthenolide acts as a key bioactive compound known for its ability to inhibit inflammatory responses and reduce muscle spasms. The presence of flavonoids contributes to its antioxidant activity, which helps protect cells from oxidative stress. Additionally, the volatile oils within the plant possess antimicrobial and insecticidal qualities used in traditional medicine. These combined chemical constituents make the plant a subject of study for managing migraines and inflammatory conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Tanacetum vulgare?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions, digestive ailments, skin irritations, and parasitic infections. Historically, it has been applied topically to soothe skin diseases like eczema and to reduce swelling from insect bites. Its bitter compounds were often utilized in traditional medicine to stimulate digestion and combat intestinal worms. Some herbal practices also suggest its use in managing menstrual irregularities and certain types of headaches. However, caution is required because its chemical components can be toxic if used improperly.

What are the herbal preparations of Tanacetum vulgare?

Tanacetum vulgarle is prepared as a dried infusion, a decoction, a tincture, or an external topical ointment. The dried flowering tops are most frequently steeped in hot water to create an infusion for internal consumption. For more concentrated therapeutic effects, the plant material is macerated in alcohol to produce a potent tincture. Topical preparations such as salves or oils are often derived from the plant to treat skin irritations or localized aches. These various forms allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended medicinal use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Tanacetum vulgare can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential toxicity if ingested in large quantities. Contact with the plant may lead to contact dermatitis or localized swelling in sensitive individuals. Consuming the herb can result in nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Long-term or excessive use is particularly dangerous for pregnant women as it may induce uterine contractions and lead to miscarriage. Some people may also experience dizziness or headaches following consumption.
