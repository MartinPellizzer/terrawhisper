Tanacetum vulgare

What's the taxonomical classification of Tanacetum vulgare?

Tanacetum vulgare belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. The plant is a prominent representative of the family Asteraceae, which is characterized by its composite flower heads. Within this family, it is categorized under the genus Tanacetum, and its specific biological identity is defined by the species name vulgare.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tanacetum vulgarle has upright, branched stems reaching up to 1 meter in height, bearing deeply pinnatisect, fern-like leaves that are dark green on both sides and possess a strong, aromatic scent. The foliage consists of narrow, lobed segments arranged alternately along the stem. At the terminal ends of these branches, the plant produces solitary, bright yellow flower heads characterized by a central disc of tiny florets surrounded by a ring of ray florets. These composite flower heads measure approximately 1 to 2 centimeters in diameter. The overall structure is bushy and robust, typical of many members of the Asteraceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa, but it has become widely naturalized throughout much of North America and other temperate regions. It thrives in a variety of habitats including roadsides, meadows, and disturbed soils where sunlight is abundant. Because it is highly adaptable, it has successfully expanded its range into many different climates across the globe. Its ability to spread through both seeds and rhizomes allows it to colonize new territories rapidly. Consequently, it is frequently encountered as a common weed in many parts of the world.

What are the pharmacological activities of Tanacetum vulgare?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antispasmodic, and antibacterial properties. Its antioxidant capabilities help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. The anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic effects make it useful for managing pain and soothing muscle contractions. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates strong antimicrobial and antibacterial actions against various pathogenic microorganisms. These diverse biological functions contribute to its traditional use in treating various ailments.

As discussed in a study, Tanacetum vulgare presented moderate to high bioactivity during the investigation of twelve Norwegian medicinal plants. The research involved the use of accelerated solvent extraction to produce dichloromethane and 80 % ethanol extracts for testing. These extracts were assessed for their ability to inhibit nitric oxide (NO) and TNF-α cytokine production. Additionally, the study evaluated protection against indomethacin induced damage of the intestinal epithelial barrier using differentiated Cac0-2 cells. The dichloromethane extracts showed a stronger effect regarding NO and TNF-α inhibition, while the 80 % ethanol extracts demonstrated greater protection of barrier integrity.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including apigenin, luteolins, rosmarinic acid, parthenolide, and various flavonoids. These bioactive molecules work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. The presence of flavonoids is particularly notable for their ability to combat oxidative stress at a cellular level. Additionally, compounds like rosmarinic acid and apigenin contribute to the plant's overall therapeutic profile. Parthenolide further enhances these medicinal properties by offering specific biological activities.

As discussed in a study, the surface flavonoids of Tanacetum vulgare are methyl ethers of the flavones scutellarein and 6-hydroxyluteolin. The vacuolar flavonoids of the plant include apiigenin and luteolin 7-glucuronides. Additionally, the uncommon 6-hydroxyluteolin 7-glucoside was found to be present in T. vulgare. These compounds were tested pharmacologically for their ability to inhibit major pathways of arachidonate metabolism in leukocytes. Specifically, the tansy 6-hydroxyflavones were found to be less active than feverfew 6-hydroxyflavonols as inhibitors of cyclo-oxygenase and 5-lipoxygenase.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating intestinal parasites, inflammation, inflammatory conditions, digestive problems, and diarrhea. Its medicinal properties have historically been applied to expel worms from the gastrointestinal tract. The plant also serves as an agent to soothe various inflammatory conditions within the body. Additionally, it is utilized to alleviate common digestive problems such as upset stomachs. Regular application in traditional medicine can also help manage episodes of diarrhea.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions, nausea, vomiting, skin irritation, abdominal pain, diarrhea, uterine contractions, dizziness, and seizures. Exposure to the plant may lead to gastrointestinal distress such as abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea. Topical contact with the leaves or flowers often results in localized skin irritation. Ingestion can trigger neurological issues including dizziness and even seizures in some cases. Additionally, the chemical components of the plant are known to stimulate uterine contractions.
