Tanacetum vulgare

What's the taxonomical classification of Tanacetum vulgare?

Tanacetum vulgare belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. The plant is a prominent representative of the family Asteraceae, which is characterized by its composite flower heads. Within this family, it is categorized under the genus Tanacetum, and its specific biological identity is defined by the species name vulgare.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Tanacetum vulgare contains medicinal compounds located primarily in its aerial parts, which include the leaves, flowers, and flowering stems. The essential oils and bitter principles found in these green structures are the most frequently utilized components in traditional remedies. Practitioners often harvest the plant during its flowering stage to ensure a high concentration of active volatile oils. These extracted elements are traditionally used to treat various ailments ranging from digestive issues to inflammatory conditions. Careful dosage is required because the plant's potency can lead to toxicity if used improperly.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of bioactive secondary metabolites including terpenoids, flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, coumarins, and phenolic acids.

The sesquiterpene lactones are particularly notable for providing the plant with its characteristic bitter taste and potential anti-inflammatory properties. Flavonoids and phenolic acids contribute to the overall antioxidant capacity of the extract by scavenging free radicals. Additionally, the presence of various terpenoids and coumarins supports the plant's complex chemical defense mechanisms.

Together, these specific compounds form the basis for the various pharmacological activities attributed to Tanacetum vulgare.

  • Terpenoids
  • Flavonoids
  • Sesquiterpene lactones
  • Coumarins
  • Phenolic acids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Tanacetum vulgare targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, specifically COX-1 and COX-2, as well as various pro-inflammatory cytokines to mediate its medicinal effects. The plant's primary bioactive constituents, such as parthenolide, act by inhibiting the NF-kappaB signaling pathway, which prevents the transcription of inflammatory genes. By suppressing these specific molecular cascades, the plant effectively reduces the production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes responsible for pain and swelling. Additionally, its sesquiterpene lactones can influence microtubule assembly within cells, contributing to its potential cytotoxic properties. This multifaceted interaction with inflammatory proteins and intracellular signaling molecules explains its traditional use in treating rheumatic conditions and migraines.

What are the therapeutic actions of Tanacetum vulgare?

Tanacetum vulgare has antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antispasmodic properties that are primarily driven by its high concentration of essential oils and sesquiterpene lactones. These bioactive compounds work to inhibit the growth of various bacteria and fungi, making the plant useful in traditional topical applications. The presence of bitter principles also stimulates digestive secretions, which helps to alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort and promote healthy digestion. Additionally, its ability to relax smooth muscle tissue provides relief from various types of cramping and spasms. While these therapeutic actions are significant, the plant must be used with caution due to its potential toxicity in high doses.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat parasitic worms, rheumatism, skin inflammations, menstrual irregularities, and various digestive issues.

Its bitter properties make it an effective remedy for addressing various digestive issues and promoting gastrointestinal health. Historically, herbalists applied the plant to soothe skin inflammations and reduce the discomfort associated with rheumatism. It has also been utilized in traditional medicine to regulate menstrual irregularities and balance hormonal cycles.

Additionally, its anthelmintic qualities provide a natural method for expelling parasitic worms from the body.

The main common ailment treated using this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Parasitic worms
  • Rheumatism
  • Skin inflammations
  • Menstrual irregularities
  • Digestive issues

What are the herbal preparations of Tanacetum vulgare?

Tanacetum vulgarle is prepared as a dried infusion, a decoction, a tincture, or an external topical ointment. The dried flowering tops are most frequently steeped in hot water to create an infusion for internal consumption. For more concentrated therapeutic effects, the plant material is macerated in alcohol to produce a potent tincture. Topical preparations such as salves or oils are often derived from the plant to treat skin irritations or localized aches. These various forms allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended medicinal use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Tanacetum vulgare can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential toxicity if ingested in large quantities. Contact with the plant may lead to contact dermatitis or localized swelling in sensitive individuals. Consuming the herb can result in nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Long-term or excessive use is particularly dangerous for pregnant women as it may induce uterine contractions and lead to miscarriage. Some people may also experience dizziness or headaches following consumption.
