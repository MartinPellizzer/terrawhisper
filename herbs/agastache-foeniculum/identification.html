Botanical Identification of Anise Hyssop (Agastache foeniculum)

To identify Anise Hyssop (Agastache foeniculum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Anise Hyssop, Agastache foeniculum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Agastache, and species foeniculum. This classification places it within the mint family, known for its aromatic properties and medicinal uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Agastache foeniculum is the scientific name for Anise Hyssop. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Synonyms include Agastache scrophularifolia and Hyssopus foeniculum. Common names include Anise Hyssop, Licorice Hyssop, and Blue Hyssop. It is also known by regional and historical names such as Wild Hyssop and Herba Agastachi.

Morphological Characteristics

Anise Hyssop is a perennial herb with a fibrous root system. It has upright, square stems that are hairy and often branching. Its leaves are opposite, lance-shaped, and have serrated edges. The inflorescence is a dense spike of tubular flowers, typically violet to blue. Each flower has five petals and produces small, oval fruits containing tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Anise Hyssop can be distinguished by its tall spikes of pale blue to purple flowers, opposite heart-shaped leaves with scalloped edges, and a strong anise-like scent. It grows up to 3 feet tall with square stems and is often found in moist soils. It is not to be confused with other hyssops or mint relatives due to its unique floral structure and fragrance.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Anise Hyssop is native to North America, particularly the central and eastern United States. It thrives in temperate climates with full sun. It prefers well-drained soils and is commonly found at elevations between 600 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Anise Hyssop blooms from mid-summer to early fall, typically between July and September. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to October. For identification purposes, harvest timing is best during the early flowering stage to ensure accurate species recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Anise Hyssop (Agastache foeniculum) is closely related to other members of the Lamiaceae family, particularly species in the Agastache genus. It shares key characteristics such as square stems, opposite leaves, and aromatic foliage with its relatives. These traits help distinguish it from other genera within the same family. Its phylogenetic position reflects its evolutionary ties to other aromatic herbs.
