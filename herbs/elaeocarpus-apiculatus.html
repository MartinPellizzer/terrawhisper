Elaeocarpus apiculatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeocarpus apiculatus?

Elaeocarpus apiculatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Oxalidales. This plant is a member of the family Elaeocarpaceae and is characterized by its placement within the genus Elaeocarpus, specifically identifying as the species apiiculatus.

What are the therapeutic actions of Elaeocarpus apiculatus?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin ailments, wounds, and internal digestive issues. The medicinal properties of the plant are often applied topically to promote healing in damaged tissues. In some traditional practices, extracts from the plant are utilized to manage symptoms of stomach discomfort. Local healers may also employ the plant to address specific inflammatory conditions. These therapeutic applications rely on the unique chemical compounds found within the species.

What are the herbal preparations of Elaeocarpus apiculatus?

Elaeocarpus apiculatus is prepared primarily as a decoction of the bark or as a topical poultice made from crushed leaves. These herbal preparations are traditionally used to address various skin ailments and inflammatory conditions. The bark is often boiled in water to extract its active compounds for internal or external use. Additionally, the leaves can be macerated to create medicinal washes for cleansing wounds. These diverse methods ensure that the plant's phytochemical properties are effectively delivered to the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Elaeocarpus apiculatus can cause skin irritation, dermatitis, or allergic reactions if the plant's sap comes into direct contact with the skin. Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or swelling at the site of contact. Ingestion of the plant is generally advised against as it may lead to gastrointestinal distress or other systemic toxicities. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the presence of certain phytochemicals suggests caution is necessary when handling the specimen. Always use protective gloves to minimize the risk of accidental exposure during pruning or cultivation.
