Mimusops elengi

What's the taxonomical classification of Mimusops elengi?

Mimusops elengi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Ericales. Further taxonomic refinement places this plant in the family Sapotaceae, specifically within the genus Mimusops, with its unique identity defined by the species elengi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mimusops elengi has an evergreen habit characterized by dense, elliptical leaves that are glossy green on the upper surface and paler underneath. The foliage is arranged in an opposite pattern along sturdy, woody branches. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the leaf axils, typically appearing in clusters. These blossoms eventually give rise to fleshy, globose fruits that transition from green to a dark color upon maturity. The overall structure of the plant is compact and bushy, making it suitable for various landscape uses.

What are the pharmacological activities of Mimusops elengi?

This plant has demonstrated potent pharmacological activities including anti-HIV-1 integrase activity, antibacterial, antifungal, antimicrobial, and antineoplastic properties. Its chemical constituents show significant potential in inhibiting the enzymes necessary for HIV-1 replication. Furthermore, the plant exhibits broad-spectrum antimicrobial effects that combat various bacterial and fungal pathogens. Research also indicates that its bioactive compounds possess antineoplastic qualities useful in cancer studies. These diverse medicinal properties make Mimusops elengi a subject of intense pharmacological interest.

As discussed in a study, the ethanol leaf extract of Mimusops elengi L. exhibited the strongest anti-HIV-1 IN activity among eight Thai medicinal plant extracts evaluated at concentrations of 10-100 µg/mL. This leaf extract demonstrated an IC50 value of 62.1 µg/mL. Through a bioassay-guided isolation process, a mixture of gallocatechin and epigallocatechin was isolated from the M. elengi leaf extract. This specific mixture showed satisfactory anti-HIV-1 IN activity with an IC50 value of 35.0 µM. Additionally, the flavanol glycoside mearnsitrin was isolated from the plant but remained inactive at a concentration of 100 µM.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 16 alpha-hydroxy Mi-saponin A, Mi-saponin A, lupeol, myricitrin, and ursolic acid. These specific chemical constituents are responsible for many of the biological activities associated with the species. The presence of triterpenoids like lupeol and ursolic acid often contributes to anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Additionally, the saponins Mi-saponin A and its alpha-hydroxy derivative play significant roles in the plant's chemical profile. Myriciﬂavone-related compounds such as myrici­trin further enhance its complex therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, Mimusops elengi seeds contain a novel minor triterpenoid saponin called mimusin with the chemical structure 3-O-[beta-D-glucopyranosyl-(1-->6)-beta-D-glucopyranosyl]-2 beta,3 beta,6 beta,23-tetrahydroxyolean-12-en-28-oiic acid 28-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1-->3)-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-(1-->4)-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1-->2)-alpha-L-arabinopyranoside. The plant also contains two known triterpenoid saponins identified as Mi-saponin A and 16 alpha-hydroxy Mi-saponin A. Researchers established the structure of the minor saponin by comparing its 13C NMR and LS-MS linked-scan and ESI-MS data with FAB-MS of the mimusopsin isolated from the same source.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe skin irritation, allergic reactions, and internal poisoning if ingested due to the presence of toxic alkaloids. Contact with the sap may result in painful dermatitis or redness on the skin. If the plant is swallowed, it can lead to gastrointestinal distress and more serious systemic toxicity. It is essential to avoid direct skin contact and ensure children do not consume any part of the foliage. Always use gloves when handling this species to prevent accidental exposure.
