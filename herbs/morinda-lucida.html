Morinda lucida

What's the taxonomical classification of Morinda lucida?

Morinda lucida belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Gentianales and is a member of the family Rubiaceae. Specifically, the plant is categorized within the genus Morinda, with its unique designation being the species lucida.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Morinda lucida has an evergreen shrub or small tree characterized by large, glossy, elliptical leaves with prominent venation and a woody stem. The foliage is typically dark green and feels somewhat leathery to the touch. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the leaf axils in clusters. These flowers eventually develop into fleshy, aggregate fruits that turn from green to a deep red or yellowish color when ripe. The plant structure is often dense, providing significant shade through its broad canopy.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically ranging across parts of China, Vietnam, Thailand, and Malaysia. It thrives primarily in lowland areas where the climate remains consistently warm and humid throughout the year. Within these regions, it is frequently found growing in secondary forests or along the edges of well-watered ravines. The species prefers moist, well-drained soils that are characteristic of its natural forest habitats. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of monsoon rainfall patterns that support its growth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Morinda lucida?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anti-malarial properties. Its antioxidant effects help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. The anti-inflammatory and anti-diabetic actions suggest potential applications in managing chronic metabolic conditions and systemic swelling. Additionally, its antimicrobial capabilities allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms effectively. Research into its anti-malarial properties further highlights its importance in treating parasitic infections.

As discussed in a study, a methanol extract of Morinda lucida Benth. leaves demonstrates significant (P < 0.05) dose-dependent hypoglycaemic activity in normal rats within 4 h after oral administration. At 4 h, a dose of 400 mg kg(-1) resulted in a plasma glucose level of 42.5 +/- 0.4 mg/100 mL compared to a control of 67.4 +/- 1.2 mg/100 mL. After 12 h, plasma glucose levels for doses of 50, 100, 200, or 400 mg kg(-1) fell to 51.9 +/- 1.2, 47.3 +/- 0.8, 43.1 +/- 0.4, and 40.0 +/- 0.5 mg/100 mL, respectively. In hyperglycaemic rats, the extract produced a significant (P < 0.05) anti-diabetic effect from day 3, where animals treated with 400 mg kg(-1) had a plasma glucose level of 248.7 +/- 5.3 mg/100 mL. This was compared to animals treated with 10 mg kg(-1) of glibenclamide, which had a plasma glucose level of 251.5 +/- 5.8 mg/100 mL.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds such as chlorogenic acid, daidzein, genistein, rutin, and various flavonoids. These bioactive constituents contribute to the plant's diverse therapeutic potential and pharmacological activities. Chlorogenic acid is known for its antioxidant and metabolic properties, while the isoflavones daidzein and genistein may offer estrogenic benefits. Rutin acts as a powerful antioxidant that supports vascular health and circulation. Collectively, these flavonoids work to protect cells from oxidative stress and inflammation.

As discussed in a study, Morinda lucida contains polyphenol compounds such as chlorogenic acid, epicatechin, daidzein, rutin, naringin, quercetin, naringenin, and genistein. The methanolic extract of M. lucida demonstrated the lowest IC50 for DPPH scavenging activity at 0.51 ± 0.01 mg/mL. Additionally, the dichloromethane extract of M. lucida showed the highest inhibitory capacity of β-glucosidase activity at 82.11 ± 2.15%. These findings regarding the medicinal compounds and inhibitory activities help justify the traditional uses of the plant.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions such as nausea, hypoglycemia, hypotension, dizziness, diarrhea, and stomach discomfort. Users may experience gastrointestinal issues like diarrhea and general stomach discomfort after consumption. Low blood sugar, known as hypoglycemia, is a potential risk that might lead to feelings of dizziness. Additionally, the plant may cause hypotension, which refers to a significant drop in blood pressure. These physiological changes can vary in intensity depending on the individual and the dosage used.
