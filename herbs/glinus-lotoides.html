Glinus lotoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Glinus lotoides?

Glinus lotoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Caryophyllales. This specific plant is a representative of the family Molluginaceae, falling under the genus Glinus, with its unique biological identity defined by its species name, lotoides.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Glinus lotoides has a perennial growth habit characterized by creeping rhizomes and leaves that are broadly ovate to cordate in shape. The foliage typically features prominent venation and a smooth or slightly textured margin. Its flowering structure consists of small, inconspicuous blossoms that emerge from the leaf axils. The plant often develops a dense cluster of stems as it spreads through its natural habitat. These morphological traits allow the species to colonize moist, shaded environments effectively.

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across parts of Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, and the Philippines. Its distribution is closely tied to high-moisture environments where the soil remains consistently damp. What are the pharmacological activities of Glinus lotoides?

This plant has potent anticancer, antioxidant, antitumour, and chemopreventive pharmacological activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Its antioxidant properties allow it to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. These mechanisms play a crucial role in its chemopreventive capabilities by preventing the cellular damage that often leads to malignancy. Additionally, the plant demonstrates significant antitumour effects by inhibiting the growth and spread of abnormal cell populations. Through these combined actions, Glinus lotoides serves as a promising subject for developing new strategies against various forms of cancer.

According to a study published by "Journal of nutritional science and vitaminology", the methanol and n-hexane extracts of Glinus lotoides seeds showed differential growth inhibitory responses in carcinoma cell lines. Specifically, the Calu-3 IC(50) values were 29.7 and 79.8 µg/mL, while the Caco-2 IC(50) values were 69.7 and 74.6 µg/mL. These extracts were compared to normal cell lines with MDCK IC(50) values of 106.1 and 131.1 µg/mL and IEC-6 IC(50) values of 134.0 and 128.5 µg/mL. Furthermore, the extracts induced significant apoptosis in cancer cells at 100 µg/mL with a p<0.05 significance level. The plant also demonstrated antioxidant activity with a hydrophilic oxygen radical absorption capacity (ORAC) value of 123 µM Trolox Equiv./g.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds glinusides F, glinusides G, glinusides H, glinusides I, and hopane-type saponins. These specific glycosides are primary bioactive constituents identified through chemical analysis of the species. The presence of hopane-type saponins suggests potential pharmacological activities often associated with such triterpenoid structures. Researchers study these individual glinusides to understand their unique chemical profiles and biological properties. Such detailed composition provides a foundation for exploring the therapeutic potential of the plant.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", the seeds of Glinus lotoides growing in Ethiopia contain four new hopane-type saponins named glinusides F, G, H, and I, labeled 1-4. These seeds also yield the known succulentoside B, which is identified as 5. Additionally, two known flavones were isolated from the plant, specifically 5,7,4'-trihydroxyflavone-6,8-di-C-glucoside (vicenin-2) and 5,7,4'-trihydroxyflavone-8-C-sophoroside (vitexin-2''-O-glucoside). The new structures were characterized using 2D NMR and HRESIMS as 3beta-O-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-6alpha-O-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-16beta-O-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-22-hydroxyhopane (1), 3beta-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1-->2)-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-6alpha,16beta-dihydroxy-22-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosylhopane (2), 3beta-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1-->2)-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-6alpha-O-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-16beta-hydroxy-22-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosylhopane (3), and 3beta-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranosyl-(1-->2)-beta-d-xylopyranosyl-6alpha-O-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-16beta-O-beta-D-xylopyranosyl-22-hopane (4).

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment of Dalton's ascites lymphoma, various forms of cancer, and carcinoma. Researchers have investigated its bioactive compounds for their potential to inhibit tumor growth in experimental models. These medicinal properties are often studied to understand how the plant affects malignant cell proliferation. Such studies provide insight into its possible role in managing complex oncological conditions. The specific mechanisms by which it targets these diseases remain a subject of ongoing scientific inquiry.

According to a study published by "Biological & pharmaceutical bulletin", the methanolic extract of Glinus lotoides (MGL) was evaluated for its antitumeur activity against Dalton's ascitic lymphoma (DAL) in Swiss albino mice. A significant enhancement of mean survival time of tumour bearing mice and peritoneal cell count in normal mice was observed compared to the control group. When animals treated with MGL underwent i.p. inoculation with DAL cells, tumour cell growth was found to be inhibited. After 14 d of inoculation, MGL is able to reverse the changes in the haemotoological parameters, protein and packed cellular volume consequent to tumour inoculation.
