White Clover (Trifolium repens): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is a low-growing perennial herb commonly found in lawns and pastures, known for its trifoliate leaves and small white flowers, and it has been traditionally used to support digestive health and alleviate symptoms of conditions such as irritable bowel syndrome.

What is White Clover (Trifolium Repens)?

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is a low-growing, perennial herb native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa, commonly found in lawns, meadows, and along roadsides, and has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its mild diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is a low-growing perennial herbaceous plant belonging to the legume family Fabaceae, characterized by its trifoliate leaves and small white flowers.

Common Names and Synonyms

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is commonly known by several names including Oxeye, Milkweed, and Queen Anne's Lace, though these names are often used for other plants and may lead to confusion.

Plant Description and Morphology

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is a low-growing perennial herb with trifoliate leaves composed of three oval-shaped leaflets, often with a white or pale-colored V-shaped marking, and small, white to pinkish flowers that bloom in clusters during the spring and early summer.

Native Habitat and Distribution

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northwestern Africa, and it has been widely naturalized in temperate regions around the world, including North America and parts of Australia.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and roots of White Clover (Trifolium repens) are the primary plant parts used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly harvested for their high concentration of bioactive compounds.

Phytochemical Composition

White Clover (Trifolium repens) contains a variety of phytochemicals including flavonoids such as quercetin and rutin, phenolic acids like caffeic acid and ferulic acid, and volatile oils containing compounds such as methyl salicylate and linalool.

Pharmacological Properties

White Clover (Trifolium repens) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects, primarily due to its bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, saponins, and volatile oils.

Mechanisms of Action

White Clover (Trifolium repens) exerts its effects primarily through the presence of flavonoids, saponins, and volatile oils, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and mild diuretic properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

White Clover is used to treat digestive issues, stress-related ailments, urinary tract infections, skin irritations, and as a general tonic to support overall health.

Preparation Methods and Forms

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is typically prepared as a dried herb for use in teas, tinctures, capsules, or poultices, with the most common form being a dried herb used to make herbal infusions.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of White Clover for herbal use is 2 to 4 grams of dried herb per day, usually prepared as a tea by steeping 1 to 2 teaspoons in hot water for 10 to 15 minutes, taken 2 to 3 times daily.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While white clover is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea or stomach upset, in some individuals, and excessive use may lead to allergic reactions in those sensitive to plants in the legume family.

Drug Interactions

White Clover (Trifolium repens) may interact with medications that affect the liver or blood clotting due to its potential to influence platelet aggregation and coagulation factors.

Toxicity and Precautions

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals and should be avoided by those with known allergies to the Asteraceae family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil, preferring partial to full sunlight and requiring regular watering during its growing season.

Processing and Extraction

White Clover (Trifolium repens) is typically processed by drying the fresh herb, which is then harvested during its flowering stage to preserve its active constituents.

Storage and Shelf Life

White Clover (Trifolium repens) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 1 to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on White Clover (Trifolium repens) has shown potential benefits in herbal medicine, particularly in its use as a mild diuretic, its role in supporting digestive health, and its potential anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

Traditional and Historical Uses

White Clover (Trifolium repens) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to support digestive health, reduce inflammation, and promote wound healing, with historical records dating back to ancient Greek and Roman herbal practices.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, White Clover is considered a common weed and is not regulated as a medicinal herb, though some regions may have specific guidelines for its use in herbal preparations.
