Trifolium repens

What's the taxonomical classification of Trifolium repens?

Trifolium repens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, eventually reaching the order Fabales. As a member of the family Fabaceae, it is further categorized under the genus Trifolium, with its specific identification being the species repens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Trifolium repens has trifoliate leaves composed of three oval leaflets, a creeping stoloniferous growth habit, and small white or pinkish flower heads. The leaflets are typically 1 to 3 centimeters long and feature a distinct white V-shaped mark near the base. Its stems consist of horizontal runners that root at the nodes to facilitate rapid spreading across the ground. The inflorescence is a dense, globular cluster of pea-like flowers held atop a slender peduncle. Small, serrated margins may occasionally be visible on the leaf edges.

Within its range, the species commonly inhabits diverse environments ranging from moist meadows to coastal dunes and disturbed soils. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds isoflavones, kaempferol, quercetin, betulinic acid, and biochanin A. These specific phytochemicals are known for providing various antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. Within the chemical profile, the presence of kaempferol and quercetin contributes to significant cellular protection. Additionally, the isoflavones biochanin A and other related structures play crucial roles in hormonal regulation. The inclusion of betulinic acid further enhances the plant's potential therapeutic value in modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, the plant Trifolium repens, also known as red clover, belongs to the group of legumes that produce isoflavones. These medicinal compounds are produced almost exclusively in legumes to serve natural roles in plant defense and root nodulation. The biosynthesis of these isoflavones occurs through a branch of the phenylpropanoid pathway. This pathway is catalyzed by the enzyme isoflavone synthase, which performs the first committed step in the creation of these compounds. Identifying the specific gene encoding this enzyme allows for the potential manipulation of the pathway for nutritional purposes.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat soil depletion and a lack of nutritional support by restoring essential nitrogen levels to the earth. As a nitrogen fixer, Trifolium repens converts atmospheric nitrogen into a form that other plants can readily absorb through their roots. This process effectively heals ground that has become exhausted from intensive farming or continuous cropping. By replenishing the organic matter in the substrate, the clover prevents the long-term degradation of land quality. Consequently, it serves as a biological remedy for landscapes suffering from nutrient deficiencies.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe swelling of the mouth, throat, and digestive tract due to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides that release toxic hydrogen cyanide upon ingestion. Consuming large quantities may lead to acute cyanide poisoning, which manifests through symptoms such as dizziness, headache, and rapid heart rate. In more extreme cases, the toxicity can progress to respiratory failure or even death if not treated immediately. Individuals with sensitivities to legumes should also be cautious of potential allergic reactions to its proteins. Because of these significant risks, it is important to avoid consuming the plant in any form.
