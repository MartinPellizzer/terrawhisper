Botanical Identification of Lousewort (Ledum palustre)

To identify Lousewort (Ledum palustre) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Lousewort, or Ledum palustre, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Ericales, family Ericaceae, genus Ledum, and species Ledum palustre. These classifications outline its place in the plant kingdom, from broad categories like kingdom and division down to the specific species level.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Lousewort is Ledum palustre, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It belongs to the family Ericaceae and has several synonyms, including Ledum groenlandicum and Ledum alpinum. Common names such as Lousewort, Bog Rosemary, and Frostweed reflect its appearance and habitat. Regional and historical names vary, with some ancient references noting its use in traditional medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Lousewort is a low-growing perennial herb with a woody root system. Its stems are erect, often reddish, and branched. Leaves are opposite, linear, and covered in white hairs. The inflorescence is a dense, terminal spike. Flowers are small, pink to purple, and clustered. The fruit is a capsule that releases tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Lousewort can be distinguished by its purple to pink flowers in clusters, hairy stems, and opposite, oval leaves with toothed edges. It grows in wet, acidic soils and is often found in bogs. It is not to be confused with other heath plants like blueberry or heather, which have different flower colors and growth habits.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Lousewort is native to northern regions of Europe, Asia, and North America. It prefers cool, moist climates and grows in acidic, sandy or peaty soils. It is commonly found in wetlands, bogs, and heathlands. It typically thrives at low to mid-altitude ranges, usually between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Lousewort blooms from June to August, producing clusters of purple flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, harvest timing is best during the flowering period when the plant is most visually distinct.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Lousewort belongs to the Ericaceae family, sharing characteristics with other heath plants. It is closely related to species like Ledum gmelinii and Kalmia species. These relationships are evident in its morphology and chemical composition, reflecting its placement within the genus Ledum.
