Garuga pinnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Garuga pinnata?

Garuga pinnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the taxonomic order Sapindales. This plant is specifically situated within the family Burseraceae, belonging to the genus Garuga, with the species name pinnata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Garuga pinnata has pinnately compound leaves consisting of several pairs of oblong to lanceolate leaflets arranged along a central rachis. The leaflets are typically leathery in texture and feature entire margins with acute tips. This plant is characterized by its woody structure, often forming small to medium-sized trees with distinct branching patterns. The flowers are small and usually appear in axillary clusters, while the fruit develops into a woody capsule. The overall morphology supports its adaptation to tropical and subtropical forest environments.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains specific bioactive phytochemicals including 6'-hydroxygaruganin V and 9'-desmethylgarugamblin I. These unique chemical structures belong to the class of garuganin derivatives found within the species. Researchers study these specific compounds to understand their potential pharmacological properties and therapeutic applications. The presence of such specialized molecules contributes to the medicinal profile of the plant in traditional practices. Such detailed chemical analysis helps in the identification of new natural products for drug development.

As discussed in a study, three macrocyclic diarylheptanoids were isolated from the petroleum ether and dichloromethane extracts of the stem bark of Garuga pinnata. These specific medicinal compounds include 6'-hydroxygaruganin V (1), 9'-desmethylgarugamblin I (2), and 1,9'-didesmethylgaruganin III (3). The structures of these 3 compounds were established through extensive spectroscopic studies. These methods of identification involved high field NMR and MS measurements.
