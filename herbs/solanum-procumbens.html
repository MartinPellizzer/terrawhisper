Solanum procumbens

What's the taxonomical classification of Solanum procumbens?

Solanum procumbens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Solanales. The plant is further categorized into the family Solanaceae, which houses its specific genus, Solanum, and is ultimately identified by its unique species designation, procumbens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Solanum procumbens has a prostrate or decumbent growth habit characterized by woody stems that sprawl along the ground and small, ovate, green leaves with entire to slightly wavy margins. The foliage is typically covered in fine, spreading hairs that give the surface a somewhat pubescent texture. Its inflorescence consists of small, pale yellow to white flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These flowers feature a prominent central cone of anthers surrounding a small style. Following pollination, the plant produces small, globose berries that transition from green to a dark purple or black color when mature.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and potential toxicity due to the presence of alkaloids like solanine. There is currently insufficient human clinical data to establish a comprehensive profile of its safety or long-term physiological effects. Because many members of the Solanum genus contain glycoalkaloids, ingestion may lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Topical contact with certain parts of the plant might also trigger localized allergic reactions or dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Given the lack of rigorous clinical trials, cautious handling and avoidance of ingestion are strongly advised.
