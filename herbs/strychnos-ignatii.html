Strychnos ignatii

What's the taxonomical classification of Strychnos ignatii?

Strychnos ignatii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Gentianaales. This plant is a member of the family Loganiaceae and is specifically categorized within the genus Strychnos, with its precise scientific designation being the species ignatii.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Strychnos ignatii contains high concentrations of indole alkaloids, specifically strychnine, brucine, and physostigmine. These potent compounds are primarily found within the seeds of the plant. Strychnine and brucine act as powerful central nervous system stimulants that can cause intense muscular contractions. Physostigmine is a notable acetylcholinesterase inhibitor used in pharmacological research to study neurotransmitter regulation. Because of their extreme toxicity, these medicinal compounds must be handled with extreme caution and precise dosage.

What are the therapeutic actions of Strychnos ignatii?

Strychnos ignatii has therapeutic actions primarily involving its use as a powerful central nervous system stimulant and tonic through the administration of its alkaloids, strychnine and brucine. These compounds act by antagonizing glycine receptors in the spinal cord, which increases the excitability of motor neurons. In controlled, minute doses, these properties have been historically utilized to treat conditions such as paralysis, muscular weakness, and digestive sluggishness. The plant also serves as a potent stimulant for the respiratory and circulatory systems. However, the therapeutic window is extremely narrow, as any increase in dosage can rapidly lead to severe neurotoxicity and convulsions.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including nervous disorders, epilepsy, and various forms of paralysis. The seeds contain potent alkaloids such as strychnine and brucine which act on the central nervous system. Historically, these substances were applied in extremely small doses to stimulate the respiratory system or counteract certain types of physical weakness. However, the high toxicity of the plant means that any medicinal application requires precise measurement to avoid fatal convulsions. Modern medical practices have largely replaced these traditional uses with safer, more controlled pharmaceutical alternatives.

What are the herbal preparations of Strychnos ignatii?

Strychnos ignatii is prepared primarily as a concentrated powder derived from the dried seeds to facilitate controlled dosing of its potent alkaloids. These powdered seeds are often encapsulated or incorporated into specialized tinctures to ensure precise measurements during medicinal application. In some traditional contexts, the plant material may be infused into oils or ointments for topical use, though oral administration remains the most common method. Because the strychnine content is extremely high, these preparations require rigorous standardization to prevent fatal toxicity. Practitioners must exercise extreme caution when handling any form of this botanical extract due to its narrow therapeutic index.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Strychnos ignatii can cause severe neurotoxicity, muscle spasms, convulsions, and respiratory failure due to the presence of strychnine and brucine. These alkaloids act as potent stimulants to the central nervous system by blocking inhibitory neurotransmitters. As the toxins take effect, an individual may experience intense muscular contractions and painful tetanic spasms. These seizures can be triggered by even minor sensory stimuli like light or sound. In many cases, death occurs because the respiratory muscles become paralyzed, leading to asphyxiation.
