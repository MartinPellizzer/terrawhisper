Phlomis likiangensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Phlomis likiangensis?

Phlomis likiangensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its progression through the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Lamiaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Phlomis, and its unique species designation, likiangensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Phlomis likiangensis has a woody, perennial structure characterized by erect, square-shaped stems and large, rugose, ovate leaves that are covered in dense, velvety hairs. The foliage is typically grayish-green and possesses a prominent reticulate venation pattern on the surface. During its flowering period, the plant produces dense, verticillaster inflorescences that form distinct whorls along the upper portions of the stems. These tubular, bilabiate flowers are often yellowish or pale in color and emerge from protective, leafy bracts. The overall habit of the plant is bushy and robust, allowing it to thrive in rocky, Mediterranean-style habitats.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including iridoid glucosides, alkaloids, and various polyphenolic antioxidants. The iridoid glucosides found within its tissues are particularly valued for their potent anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. These specific chemical structures interact with biological systems to help modulate immune responses and reduce oxidative stress. In addition to these glycosides, the plant produces secondary metabolites that contribute to its overall therapeutic profile. Researchers continue to study these complex chemical constituents to better understand their potential applications in modern pharmacology.

    As discussed in a study, Phlomis likiangensis is traditionally used in China to activate collaterals and protect the cardiovascular system. Researchers isolated twelve known iridoid glucosides, labeled 1-12, from Phlomis likiangensis. These iridoid glucosides 1-12 were investigated for their vasodilatory effects and underlying mechanisms on rat aortic rings. The study demonstrated that these iridoid glucosides significantly increased vasodilatation in the vessels. This effect was abolished by removing the endothelium or by eliminating the generation of nitric oxide. These findings provide the first evidence that the iridoid glucosides of Phlomis likiangensis may be the pharmacodynamic basis for its traditional efficacy.
