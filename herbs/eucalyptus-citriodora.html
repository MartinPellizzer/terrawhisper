Eucalyptus citriodora

What's the taxonomical classification of Eucalyptus citriodora?

Eucalyptus citriodora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the order Myrtales and belongs to the family Myrtaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Eucalyptus, and its specific species name, citriodora.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eucalyptus citriodora has lanceolate, bright green leaves that measure up to 15 centimeters in length and are characterized by a distinct lemon-scented oil. The foliage is typically arranged oppositely on the branches during its juvenile stage before transitioning to an alternate pattern as the tree matures. Its bark is smooth and light in color, often shedding in thin flakes to reveal a pale surface underneath. The tree produces small, white, clustered flowers that emerge from woody buds. These floral structures eventually give way to hard, cup-shaped fruits known as capsules.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the eastern coastal regions of Australia, specifically spanning from the northern parts of Queensland down through New South Wales. It thrives primarily in subtropical and temperate climates where moisture is relatively consistent. Within these areas, the species is frequently found in well-drained soils along forest margins and creek lines. While its natural range is restricted to these specific Australian states, it has been widely introduced to other parts of the world for its fragrant essential oils. Consequently, cultivated populations can now be found in various Mediterranean-style climates across different continents.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Eucalyptus citriodora contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its aromatic leaves and essential oils. The leaves are harvested to extract citronellal, which serves as the main active constituent for therapeutic applications. This oil is frequently used in aromatherapy and topical treatments to alleviate muscle aches and respiratory congestion. Additionally, the plant's foliage can be prepared as an infusion to help reduce fever or soothe digestive issues. The potency of these medicinal properties depends heavily on the concentration of oils found in the fresh or dried foliage.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds flavonoids, kaempferol, quercetin-3-O-β-D-glucopyranoside, eugenol, and citronellal. The flavonoids present in its structure contribute significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the organism. Specifically, the presence of kaempferol and quercetin-3-O-β-D-glucopyranoside supports cellular protection against oxidative stress. Additionally, the aromatic compounds eugenol and citronellal provide antimicrobial and insect-repellent qualities. These diverse chemical constituents work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, Eucalyptus citriodoras leaf extracts showed promising values against Staphylococcuus aureus with halos varying from 13-20 mm. The analysis of the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) values revealed inhibitory activity of S. aureus with concentrations varying from 3.12-12.5 mg/mL. This result is considered significant because S. aureus is one of the main causes of hospital infections. The crude extracts investigated are promising and their antibacterial and antioxidant actions should be thoroughly studied.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an essential oil, an infusion, a tincture, or through steam inhalation to utilize its high citronellal content. The essential oil is frequently extracted through steam distillation to create potent aromatherapy blends and topical applications. An infusion is made by steeping the leaves in hot water to produce a soothing herbal tea. Tinctures are created by soaking the plant material in alcohol to concentrate its medicinal properties for long-term storage. Steam inhalation involves adding crushed leaves to boiling water to help clear respiratory passages.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reaction, nausea, vomiting, stomach cramps, dizziness, confusion, and seizures if ingested or applied improperly. Topical exposure to the essential oils may lead to redness or localized rashes on sensitive areas. When swallowed, the plant often triggers gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, and intense stomach cramps. Systemic toxicity can also impact the central nervous system, resulting in dizziness and mental confusion. In severe cases of poisoning, individuals may experience dangerous neurological symptoms including seizures.
