Tetrastigma hemsleyanum

What's the taxonomical classification of Tetrastigma hemsleyanum?

Tetrastigma hemsleyanum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Vitales and is a member of the family Vitaeceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Tetrastigma, and its unique species designation, hemsleyanum.

Research on the plant mainly focuses on the discovery of new compounds and pharmacology. The resveratrol biosynthesis pathway in Tetrastigma hemsleyanum involves four key enzymes, ThPAL, ThC4H, Th4CL, and ThRS. These enzymes contain open reading frames of 2139 bp, 1518 bp, 1716 bp, and 1035 bp, which encode 712, 505, 571, and 344 amino acids respectively. All four genes were expressed in all tested tissues, though expression was more stable in root tubers.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (+)-catechin, (-)-epicatechin, hesperetin-7-O-glucoside, kaempferol, and rutin. These specific flavonoids and glycosides are known to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. The presence of catechins specifically supports cardiovascular health by protecting cells from oxidative stress. Additionally, compounds like kaempferol and rutin contribute to the plant's overall therapeutic profile through their bioactive properties. Together, these substances form a complex chemical matrix that promotes various physiological wellness functions.

As discussed in a study, the flavonoids in Tetrastigma hemsleyanum Diels et Gilg have high medicinal value. The contents of six flavonoids were profiled, including (+) -catechin at 692.63 ± 127.24 μg/g dry weight (DW), (-) -epicatechin at 163.34 ± 31.86 μg/g DW, neochlorogenic acid at 45.95 ± 3.46 μg/g DW, luteolin-6-C-glucoside at 209.68 ± 6.03 μg/g DW, and orientin at 56.82 ± 4.75 μg/g DW in 30-day-old hairy roots. These concentrations in the hairy roots were higher than those found in 3-year-old true roots. The hairy roots also demonstrated stronger antioxidant activity than the true roots.
