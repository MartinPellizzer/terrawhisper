Cotoneaster moupinensis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cotoneaster moupinensis has an arching, deciduous shrub habit characterized by slender, greyish-brown branches and small, ovate, dark green leaves that measure approximately 1 to 2 centimeters in length. The foliage typically features finely serrated margins and a slightly leathery texture. During the flowering season, small white blossoms emerge from clusters of inconspicuous stamens. These flowers eventually develop into small, globose pomes that transition from green to a bright red color upon ripening. The overall structure of the plant allows it to form dense, spreading thickets in its natural habitat.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Cotoneaster moupinensis can cause gastrointestinal distress and potential poisoning if any part of the plant, particularly its red berries, is ingested by humans or pets. The small fruits contain cyanogenic glycosides which may lead to symptoms such as vomiting, abdominal pain, or dizziness upon consumption. While the plant is primarily used for ornamental purposes, accidental ingestion poses a significant risk to small children and animals. It is important to keep these berries away from areas where they could be easily mistaken for edible fruit. Proper identification and supervision are necessary to prevent any adverse health reactions.
