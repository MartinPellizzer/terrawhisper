Botanical Identification of White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album)

To identify White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

White Dead-Nettle, Lamium album, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Lamium, and species album. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the mint family, which includes many medicinal and aromatic plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Lamium album is the scientific name for White Dead-Nettle. It was first described by Carl Linnaeus in 1753. The species has several synonyms, including Veronica hirsuta and Limonium album. It is known by various common names such as White Dead-Nettle, Dead-Nettle, and White Nettle.

Morphological Characteristics

White Dead-Nettle is an annual herb with a branched root system. It has square, erect stems that are hairy and slightly tetragonal. Its opposite, heart-shaped leaves are hairy and have a serrated edge. The inflorescence consists of a spike of small, two-lipped flowers that are pinkish-white with a purple tinge. The fruit is a dry, capsule containing small, dark seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

White Dead-Nettle can be distinguished by its smooth, hairy leaves with a reddish stem, and its small, pale pink flowers. It lacks the purple markings on the leaves seen in Lamium purpureum. The leaves are heart-shaped with a pointed tip, and the plant grows in moist, shaded areas.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

White Dead-Nettle is native to Europe, western Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in temperate climates and prefers moist, shaded environments. It grows in well-drained loamy or sandy soils. It is commonly found at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

White Dead-Nettle blooms from April to June, producing violet flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer, typically between July and September. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering period, making mid-spring to early summer the optimal time for accurate botanical identification.

Phylogenetic Relationships

White Dead-Nettle belongs to the genus Lamium in the mint family, Lamiaceae. It is closely related to species like Lamium purpureum and Lamium amplexicaule. These species share similar leaf structures and flowering patterns, but differ in color and habitat preferences. The genus Lamium is part of the tribe Lamieae, which includes other herbaceous plants with opposite leaves and square stems.
