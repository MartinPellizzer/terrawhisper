Corydalis parviflora

What's the taxonomical classification of Corydalis parviflora?

Corydalis parviflora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further categorized under the order Ranunculales. It is a member of the family Papaveraceae, specifically situated within the genus Corydalis, and is identified by the specific epithet parviflora.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Corydalis parviflora has delicate, pinnately compound leaves with small, ovate leaflets and slender, erect stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 25 centimeters. The plant produces small, tubular flowers that are primarily pale yellow or white, often featuring subtle markings. These blossoms are borne on thin pedicels emerging from the leaf axils. The root system consists of a small, tuberous rhizome that stores nutrients for the plant. Its overall structure is diminutive and ephemeral, reflecting its specialized life cycle in woodland environments.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause nausea, dizziness, sedation, respiratory depression, and gastrointestinal distress in individuals who ingest it. These adverse reactions often manifest as a significant slowing of the central nervous system. Users may experience intense drowsiness or a feeling of lightheadedness shortly after consumption. More severe complications include an irregular digestive tract leading to gastrointestinal distress. Most critically, high doses can lead to life-threatening respiratory depression where breathing becomes dangerously shallow or stops entirely.
