Caragana acanthophylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Caragana acanthophylla?

Caragana acanthophylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it in the order Fabales. As a member of the family Fabaceae, this plant is categorized under the genus Caragana, with the specific epithet acanthophylla denoting its unique species identity.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Caragana acanthophylla has a woody, spreading shrub habit characterized by stiff, spine-tipped branchlets and small, pinnate leaves.

The leaves are composed of several pairs of leaflets that are often reduced to sharp, needle-like spines to minimize water loss. These modified leaves serve as a defense mechanism against herbivores while the plant occupies arid environments. The flowers are typically bright red and pea-like, appearing in clusters among the thorny foliage.

This combination of spiny morphology and vibrant blooms allows the species to thrive in harsh, scrubland habitats.

      Caragana acanthophylla is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the intended therapeutic application.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Caragana acanthophylla can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and physical injury from its sharp, thorny branches. The plant features stiff, spine-like leaves that can puncture skin or clothing upon contact. If these thorns penetrate the flesh, they may lead to localized swelling, redness, or minor infections. Individuals with sensitive skin might experience more severe contact dermatitis after touching the foliage. Careful handling is necessary to avoid these mechanical and dermatological complications.
