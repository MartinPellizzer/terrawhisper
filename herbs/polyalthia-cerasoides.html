Polyalthia cerasoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Polyalthia cerasoides?

Polyalthia cerasoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is part of the order Magnoliales. This plant is a member of the family Annonaceae, specifically situated within the genus Polyalthia, and is identified by its unique species name, cerasoides.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Polyalthia cerasoides contains bioactive compounds primarily located in its leaves, bark, and roots that are utilized for their various medicinal properties. These different plant parts work together to provide a diverse range of phytochemicals beneficial to human health.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Polyalthia cerasoides targets various enzymes and signaling proteins, most notably cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and several proinflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha. The presence of bioactive alkaloids and acetogenins in the plant allows it to interfere with the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway to reduce inflammation. These secondary metabolites also demonstrate an ability to inhibit specific protein kinases that are involved in cell cycle regulation and tumor progression. Furthermore, the plant's constituents may interact with acetylcholinesterase, suggesting potential neuroprotective activities through the modulation of neurotransmitter levels. By binding to these distinct molecular sites, the plant exerts its observed antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and cytotoxic effects.

    What are the herbal preparations of Polyalthia cerasoides?

    Polyalthia cerasoides is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using its leaves and bark to address various ailments.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Polyalthia cerasoides can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled improperly. The specific toxic compounds within the plant may trigger contact dermatitis in individuals with sensitive skin. If any part of the foliage is consumed, it could lead to nausea, vomiting, or more severe internal discomfort. While comprehensive human clinical studies are limited, caution is advised when working with its leaves or berries. Always wear gloves and avoid ingestion to prevent potential adverse reactions.
