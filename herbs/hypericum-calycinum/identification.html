Botanical Identification of Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum)

To identify Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Golden St. John'S Wort (Hypericum calycinum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Golden St. John's Wort, Hypericum calycinum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Garryales, family Clusiaceae, genus Hypericum, and species Hypericum calycinum. These classifications reflect its evolutionary relationships and botanical characteristics within the plant kingdom.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Golden St. John's Wort is Hypericum calycinum, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Hypericum album and Hypericum luteum, reflecting historical taxonomic changes. Common names such as Golden St. John's Wort and Everlasting are used across regions, while historical names often reference its medicinal use in ancient herbal practices.

Morphological Characteristics

Golden St. John's Wort is a low-growing, mat-forming perennial with a fibrous root system. Its stems are prostrate to ascending, covered in short, glandular hairs. Leaves are opposite, oval to elliptic, with a waxy surface and prominent veins. The inflorescence is a terminal cluster of bright yellow flowers with red-orange glands. Each flower has five petals and five stamens, while the fruit is a small, three-lobed capsule containing numerous tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Golden St. John's Wort can be distinguished by its opposite, lance-shaped leaves with a slightly serrated edge and its bright orange-yellow flowers with five petals. It typically grows in shaded, moist environments and has a trailing habit, differentiating it from taller, more upright species like Hypericum patulum. Its stems are often reddish and covered in fine hairs, aiding in visual identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Golden St. John's Wort is native to Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, moist soils and is commonly found at low to mid-elevation areas.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Golden St. John's Wort blooms from late spring to early summer, typically June to July. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August. For identification purposes, harvest timing is crucial between June and August when the plant is most identifiable and the active compounds are concentrated.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Hypericum calycinum is closely related to other Hypericum species, particularly Hypericum perforatum. It shares characteristics with the genus Hypericum, such as yellow flowers and orange berries. Within the Clusiaceae family, it exhibits traits like opposite leaves and clustered flowers, which help distinguish it from other genera.
