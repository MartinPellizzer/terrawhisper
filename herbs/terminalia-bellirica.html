Scientific and Botanical Profile

Belliric Myrobalan, with botanical name Terminalia bellirica, is a member of the Combretaceae family and is native to regions spanning India, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan, Bangladesh, Myanmar, Cambodia, Thailand, Vietnam, Indochina, Southern China, and Eastern India.

Commonly known as Indian Date Palm, Chebulic Myrobalan, Bael Tree, Bael Fruit, Indian Myrobalan, Belleric Myrobalan, Bahera, Bael Fruit Tree, Tara Tree, Indian Date Palm, Indian Bdellium, and Terminalia Myrobalan, it is a large deciduous tree that can grow up to 20 meters tall, featuring a broad, umbrella-like canopy. Its trunk is smooth and grayish-brown, with thick, fibrous roots, and it produces yellowish-green flowers that develop into large, spherical, indehiscent fruits.

The fruit, known as Bael Fruit, has a thick, leathery outer layer and contains a sweet, aromatic pulp that is widely used in traditional medicine and culinary practices.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Terminalia bellirica has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms including fresh fruit, dried fruit, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

The preparation methods vary, with common approaches including making a tea by boiling the dried fruit, creating a decoction for more potent extraction, or using an infusion for gentler preparation. It can also be applied topically for skin conditions or used in aromatherapy with its essential oil. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-3 grams of the powder or 1-2 capsules daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

Due to its potency, it is advisable to use Terminalia bellirica sparingly, with a short duration of use unless advised otherwise by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Terminalia bellirica can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential health benefits, but it is important to be aware of its safety profile.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals. It may also interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and diabetes medications, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of adverse effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using Terminalia bellirica due to limited safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is advisable to follow recommended dosages, consult a healthcare professional, and monitor for any unusual side effects.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Terminalia bellirica grows in tropical and subtropical regions, preferring well-drained, loamy soils with a pH between 5.

5 and 7. 5, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in warm climates with consistent moisture, needing regular watering during the dry season but avoiding waterlogging. Regular pruning and mulching help maintain plant health and encourage fruiting, while monitoring for pests like fruit flies and leaf beetles is essential for care and maintenance. The fruit is best harvested when it turns dark red or black, typically during the monsoon season, using a sharp knife or pruning shears to cut the fruit from the tree without damaging the branches.

After harvesting, the fruit should be dried in the shade to preserve its potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to extend shelf life and maintain medicinal properties.
