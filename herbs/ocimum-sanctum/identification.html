Botanical Identification of Tulsi (Ocimum sanctum)

To identify Tulsi (Ocimum sanctum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Tulsi (Ocimum sanctum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Tulsi, or Ocimum sanctum, is classified within the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Ocimum, and species Ocimum sanctum. This classification highlights its position as a flowering plant in the mint family, commonly used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Ocimum sanctum is the scientific name of Tulsi, also known as holy basil. It was first described by the botanist R.A. Stewart in 1835. The plant has numerous synonyms, including Ocimum tenuiflorum and Ocimum americanum. It is widely recognized by common names such as tulsi, holy basil, and sacred basil across different regions.

Morphological Characteristics

Tulsi is an aromatic herb with a compact, bushy growth habit. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, supporting its upright stems. Leaves are opposite, ovate, and slightly hairy, with a strong, spicy aroma. The inflorescence is a terminal spike, bearing small, tubular flowers. The fruit is a dry capsule containing tiny, black seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Tulsi can be distinguished by its opposite, oval to lance-shaped leaves with slightly serrated edges, a strong aromatic scent, and small blue or purple flowers. It is often found in gardens or near water sources, and its stem is square and hairy, unlike similar species with smooth stems. The leaves have a distinct basil-like aroma when crushed.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Tulsi (Ocimum sanctum) is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly the eastern and southern regions. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates with high humidity. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is commonly found at altitudes between 300 and 1,200 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Tulsi typically blooms during the summer months, from May to September. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn, around August to October. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the flowering stage, when leaves are fully developed and aromatic.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Tulsi, or Ocimum sanctum, belongs to the Lamiaceae family. Its closest relatives include other Ocimum species like O. basilicum and O. americanum. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, such as square stems and aromatic leaves, which are characteristic of the genus Ocimum.
