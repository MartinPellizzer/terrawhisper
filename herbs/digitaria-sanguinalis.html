Digitaria sanguinalis

What's the taxonomical classification of Digitaria sanguinalis?

Digitaria sanguinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Poales. This plant is a part of the family Poaceae, falling under the genus Digitaria, and is specifically identified by its species name, sanguinalis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Digitaria sanguinalis has an annual growth habit characterized by erect or decumbent stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 50 centimeters. The leaves are linear and lanceolate, featuring a prominent midrib and scabrous margins that feel rough to the touch. Its inflorescence consists of a dense, cylindrical spikelet arrangement that can appear reddish or purplish in color. The plant produces fibrous roots that allow it to colonize disturbed soils effectively. Small, compressed spikelets are arranged closely along the rachis to form the distinctive seed head.

What are the pharmacological activities of Digitaria sanguinalis?

This plant has antibacterial, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities. Its extracts demonstrate a significant capacity to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Research indicates that the bioactive compounds within the plant can effectively disrupt microbial cell membranes. These medicinal properties suggest potential applications in treating infectious diseases and managing localized inflammation. Ongoing studies continue to explore the specific mechanisms behind these therapeutic effects.

According to a study published by "Cellular and molecular biology (Noisy-le-Grand, France)", the methanolic leaf extracts of Digitaria sanguinalis (L.) Scop. were evaluated for their effects on a panel of 177 S. aureus isolates, 92 (51.97%) of which were methicillin-resistant. All MRSA isolates were sensitive to the extracts, which demonstrated dose-dependent inhibition of the tested microorganisms. These plants showed maximum inhibition zones at a concentration of 300 mg/L. The minimum inhibitory concentrations on MRSA isolates for D. sanguinalis were 388.4 ± 0.2 mg/L.
