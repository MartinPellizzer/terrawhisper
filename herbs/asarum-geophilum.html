Asarum geophilum

What's the taxonomical classification of Asarum geophilum?

Asarum geophilum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae, following the evolutionary lineage of the order Piperales. This plant is a member of the family Aristolochiaceae, which places it within the genus Asarum, and is specifically identified by its unique species designation, geophilum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Asarum geophilum has kidney-shaped, dark green leaves that are typically cordate or reniform in form with entire margins and a somewhat leathery texture.

The plant is a low-growing perennial herb characterized by its creeping rhizomes that spread along the forest floor. Its flowers are small and inconspicuous, often appearing near the ground to facilitate pollination by ground-dwelling insects. The foliage is arranged in a basal rosette, which helps the plant shade its own root system in moist environments.

These morphological traits collectively allow the species to thrive in the shaded understory of its native habitat.

        What are the herbal preparations of Asarum geophilum?

        Asarum geophilum is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using its dried rhizomes and roots. Practitioners often boil the woody parts in water to extract active alkaloids for internal consumption. These liquid preparations are frequently combined with honey or other sweeteners to mask the bitter flavor profile of the plant. This versatility allows the plant to be utilized in both oral and external medicinal traditions.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Asarum geophilum can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, liver toxicity, and potential neurological impairments if ingested. These adverse effects primarily stem from the presence of aristolochic acids, which are known to be highly nephrotoxic and carcinogenic. Consumption of plants within this genus may lead to kidney damage or even fatal outcomes due to these toxic compounds. Individuals should avoid any internal use of this species to prevent permanent organ damage. Careful identification is necessary because many species in this group share similar dangerous chemical profiles.
