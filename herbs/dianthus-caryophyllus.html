Dianthus caryophyllus

What's the taxonomical classification of Dianthus caryophyllus?

Dianthus caryophyllus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is positioned in the order Caryophyllales. The plant is a member of the family Caryophyllaceae and is further categorized under the genus Dianthus, eventually reaching its specific identification as the species caryophyllus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dianthus caryophyllus has upright, branching stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bear linear, lanceolate leaves arranged in opposite pairs. The foliage is characterized by a glaucous, bluish-green color and a smooth, slightly fleshy texture. Its most prominent morphological feature is the large, actinomorphic flower consisting of five broad, overlapping petals that exhibit ruffled or serrated edges. These blossoms are produced in terminal cymes and can display a wide array of colors, ranging from pure white to deep crimson. The base of each flower features a long, cylindrical calyx that encloses the ovary.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and southern Europe, extending from the Pyrenees through the Alps to the Balkans. It thrives primarily in rocky, limestone-rich habitats at various elevations. While its natural range is centered in the Mediterranean and temperate European zones, it has been widely naturalized in other parts of the world through cultivation. Many gardeners in North America and other temperate climates now grow it as an ornamental species. Its ability to tolerate diverse soil types allows it to spread beyond its original ecological niche.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dianthus caryophyllus?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including antioxidant, antibacterial, anti-tumor, anti-influenza virus, and neuraminidase inhibitory activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, while its antibacterial effects combat various pathogenic microorganisms. Research also indicates that its chemical constituents possess anti-tumor potential by inhibiting the growth of certain cancer cells. Furthermore, the plant exhibits anti-influenza virus activity specifically through its neuraminidase inhibitory activity, which prevents the spread of the virus within the body. These multifaceted biological actions highlight its potential importance in medicinal applications.

According to a study published by "BMC complementary and alternative medicine", the extract obtained from Flos Caryophylli showed potent inhibitory activity against neuraminidase with IC50 values ranging from 4.1 to 9.6 μg/mL. This extract was among the most bioactive extracts that protected MDCK cells from A (H1N1) influenza virus infection. The EC50 values for these extracts ranged from 1.8 to 14.1 μg/mL, while CC50 values ranged from 97.0 to 779.2 μg/mL. Additionally, the selectivity index (SI) values ranged from 14 to 438. Quantitative RT-PCR analysis further showed that these extracts inhibited viral RNA synthesis in a dose-dependent manner.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds isoorientin 2"-O-rhamnoside, kurarinone, luteolin-4'-O-glucoside, methyl ferulate, and rhein-8-O-glucoside. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the diverse biological activities associated with Dianthus caryophyllus. The presence of various flavonoids and phenolic acids suggests potential therapeutic properties such as antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Researchers study these constituents to better understand how the plant interacts with human physiological systems. Each identified molecule plays a unique role in the chemical profile of the species.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in plant science", the flowers and roots of Dianthus caryophyllus exhibit superior antioxidant capacity due to an abundance of polyphenols, flavonoids, and antioxidants. The flowers contain specific substances including methyl ferulate and luteolin-4'-O-glucoside. In the roots, the presence of the anthraquinone compound rhein-8-O-glucoside may contribute to enhanced antioxidant properties. Ten distinct compounds were isolated and recognized in the flowers of the plant. Among these, Isoorientin 2"-O-rhamnoside and Kurarinone demonstrated notable antioxidant activity and binding affinity to SOD1 and SOD3.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating gum infections, throat infections, and various skin wounds due to its antimicrobial properties. Its extracts demonstrate significant inhibitory activity against both G(+) bacteria and G(-) bacteria. By suppressing these diverse microbial groups, the plant helps prevent further inflammation in oral and respiratory tissues. These therapeutic applications make it a valuable natural remedy for managing localized bacterial colonization.

According to a study published by "Phytomedicine : international journal of phytotherapy and phytopharmacology", the flower buds of Dianthus caryophyllus L. have been traditionally implemented by the indigenous people of northern Iraq for the treatment of wounds, throat and gum infections and gastro-intestinal disorder. The study involved the isolation of antibacterial compounds from these flower buds. The research assessed antibacterial activities against 7 selected pathogenic bacteria, which included Staphylococcuus aureus, Bacillus cereus, Listeria monocytogenes, Proteus mirabilis, Escherichia coli, Klebsiella pneumoniae and Pseudomonas aeruginosa. The isolation of these compounds validates the use of this species in the treatment of throat and gum infections, wound-healing and gastro-intestinal disorder.

  • G(-) bacteria

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Some individuals may experience contact dermatitis characterized by redness or itching upon touching the foliage or petals. Ingestion of the plant can lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain in sensitive subjects. While generally considered non-toxic to many, concentrated exposure should be avoided to prevent adverse bodily reactions. Always consult a medical professional if symptoms arise after contact with this species.
