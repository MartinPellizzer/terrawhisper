Dianthus caryophyllus

What's the taxonomical classification of Dianthus caryophyllus?

Dianthus caryophyllus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is positioned in the order Caryophyllales. The plant is a member of the family Caryophyllaceae and is further categorized under the genus Dianthus, eventually reaching its specific identification as the species caryophyllus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dianthus caryophyllus has upright, branching stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bear linear, lanceolate leaves arranged in opposite pairs. The foliage is characterized by a glaucous, bluish-green color and a smooth, slightly fleshy texture. Its most prominent morphological feature is the large, actinomorphic flower consisting of five broad, overlapping petals that exhibit ruffled or serrated edges. These blossoms are produced in terminal cymes and can display a wide array of colors, ranging from pure white to deep crimson. The base of each flower features a long, cylindrical calyx that encloses the ovary.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and southern Europe, extending from the Pyrenees through the Alps to the Balkans. It thrives primarily in rocky, limestone-rich habitats at various elevations. While its natural range is centered in the Mediterranean and temperate European zones, it has been widely naturalized in other parts of the world through cultivation. Many gardeners in North America and other temperate climates now grow it as an ornamental species. Its ability to tolerate diverse soil types allows it to spread beyond its original ecological niche.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dianthus caryophyllus?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including antibacterial, antioxidant, anti-tumor, anti-influenza virus, and neuraminidase inhibitory activities. Its diverse chemical profile allows it to combat various bacterial pathogens and mitigate oxidative stress within biological systems. Researchers have identified its potential to inhibit tumor growth, making it a subject of interest for oncological studies. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates specific anti-influenza virus activity by acting through neuraminidase inhibitory activity to prevent viral replication. These multifaceted properties highlight the therapeutic potential of Dianthus caryophyllus in modern medicine.

As discussed in a study, the extract Flos Caryophylli showed potent inhibitory activity against neuraminiadse with IC50 values ranging from 4.1 to 9.6 μg/mL. These bioactive extracts were found to protect MDCK cells from A (H1N1) influenza virus infection with very low cytotoxicity to the host cells. The EC50 values for these extracts ranged from 1.8 to 14.1 μg/mL, while the CC50 values ranged from 97.0 to 779.2 μg/mL. Additionally, the selectivity index (SI) values for these extracts ranged from 14 to 438. Quantitative RT-PCR analysis also showed that these extracts inhibited viral RNA synthesis in a dose-dependent manner.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds eugenol, thymol, rhein-8-O-glucoside, luteolin-4'-O-glucoside, and methyl ferulate. These specific bioactive molecules contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile and therapeutic potential. For instance, eugenol and thymol are well-known for their potent antimicrobial and antiseptic properties. The presence of rhein-8-O-glucoside and luteolin-4'-O-glucoside suggests significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities within the organism. Additionally, methyl ferulate provides further chemical complexity that may assist in protecting cells from oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the flower buds of Dianthus caryophyllus L. have been traditionally used to treat wounds, throat and gum infections, and gastro-intestinal disorder. Two known antibacterial compounds, thymol and eugenol, were isolated from the extracted essential oils of the plant. The study assessed the biological activity of these compounds against 7 selected pathogenic bacteria using 96 multi-well microtiter plates. For thymol, MIC values ranged from 15.6 to 250.0 microg/ml, with B. cereus being the most sensitive pathogen at 15.6 microg/ml. Eugenol achieved a best MIC value of 15.6 microg/ml against B. cereus, L. monocytogenes, and K. pneuemoniae, while S. aureus, P. mira bilis, and E. coli were inhibited at 31.2 microg/ml.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Some individuals may experience contact dermatitis characterized by redness or itching upon touching the foliage or petals. Ingestion of the plant can lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain in sensitive subjects. While generally considered non-toxic to many, concentrated exposure should be avoided to prevent adverse bodily reactions. Always consult a medical professional if symptoms arise after contact with this species.
