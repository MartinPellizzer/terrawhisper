Heracleum hemsleyanum

What's the taxonomical classification of Heracleum hemsleyanum?

Heracleum hemsleyanum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls under the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Apiales. As a member of the family Apiaceae, it is further categorized into the genus Heracleum, specifically identified by the species name hemsleyanum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Heracleum hemsleyanum has large, deeply pinnatifid leaves that are arranged in a basal rosette and feature prominent, coarse teeth along their margins. The stems are robust and hollow, typically growing upright to support heavy, compound umbels of small white flowers. These inflorescences consist of numerous tiny florets clustered in flat-topped structures that can reach considerable diameters. The plant also possesses a thick, fleshy root system that provides stability and nutrient storage. Its overall structure is characteristic of many members of the Apiaceae family, emphasizing expansive foliage and intricate flowering patterns.

        What side effects this plant can have?

        This plant can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and chemical burns upon contact with the skin due to the presence of photosensitizing furanocoumarins. When the plant's sap touches the skin and is subsequently exposed to sunlight, it triggers a phototoxic reaction known as phytophotodermatitis. This reaction often results in painful redness, swelling, and the formation of large fluid-filled blisters. Once the initial irritation subsides, the affected area may develop long-lasting dark pigmentation or scarring. These dermatological symptoms can be particularly intense if the sap is accidentally transferred to the eyes or mucous membranes.
