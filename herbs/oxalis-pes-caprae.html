Oxalis pes-caprae

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxalis pes-caprae has a perennial growth habit characterized by underground bulbils, trifoliate leaves with obcordate leaflets, and bright yellow actinomorphic flowers. The leaves consist of three heart-shaped leaflets that exhibit nyctinastic movements by closing at night. Its flowering stems emerge from the soil to support small, five-petaled blossoms that typically appear in clusters. The plant spreads aggressively through the production of small, dark bulbils located at the base of the stem or near the roots. These morphological features allow the species to thrive in various disturbed environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

Because it spreads easily through underground bulbs, it often invades coastal regions and grassy habitats where climates remain mild. Its ability to thrive in diverse soil types has allowed it to establish itself on nearly every continent except Antarctica. This widespread dispersal makes it a common sight in many urban and agricultural landscapes globally.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Oxalis pes-caprae can cause significant harm to humans and livestock through the ingestion of high concentrations of oxalic acid. This organic acid acts as a potent toxin by binding with calcium in the bloodstream to form insoluble calcium oxalate crystals. These crystals can precipitate in the kidneys, potentially leading to acute renal failure or permanent kidney damage. In animals, consumption often results in severe gastrointestinal distress, including vomiting and abdominal pain. Prolonged exposure or large doses can lead to systemic electrolyte imbalances and even death.
