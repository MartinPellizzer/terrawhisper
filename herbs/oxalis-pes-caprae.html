Oxalis pes-caprae

What's the taxonomical classification of Oxalis pes-caprae?

Oxalis pes-caprae belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, following the evolutionary lineage of the order Oxalidales. At the family level, it is a member of the Oxalidaceae, which contains various species characterized by their distinctive leaf structures. Within this family, the plant is categorized under the genus Oxalis, and its specific biological identity is defined by the species pes-caprae.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxalis pes-caprae has a perennial growth habit characterized by underground bulbils, trifoliate leaves with obcordate leaflets, and bright yellow actinomorphic flowers. The leaves consist of three heart-shaped leaflets that exhibit nyctinastic movements by closing at night. Its flowering stems emerge from the soil to support small, five-petaled blossoms that typically appear in clusters. The plant spreads aggressively through the production of small, dark bulbils located at the base of the stem or near the roots. These morphological features allow the species to thrive in various disturbed environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region and parts of South Africa, but it has become widely naturalized across much of the world's subtropical and temperate zones. It is frequently found in disturbed areas, gardens, and lawns throughout Australia, the southwestern United States, and various parts of South America. Because it spreads easily through underground bulbs, it often invades coastal regions and grassy habitats where climates remain mild. Its ability to thrive in diverse soil types has allowed it to establish itself on nearly every continent except Antarctica. This widespread dispersal makes it a common sight in many urban and agricultural landscapes globally.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains cinnamic acid ester, noroxyneolignan, flavonoids, phenolic compounds, and phenyl cinnamate derivatives. These specific chemical constituents are responsible for the biological activity observed in the species. The presence of various phenolic compounds provides significant antioxidant properties to the plant matter. Furthermore, the combination of flavonoids and phenyl cinnamate derivatives contributes to its diverse medicinal potential. These bioactive molecules work together to offer various therapeutic benefits.

As discussed in a study, Oxalis pes-caprae contains a cinnamic acid ester, two dihydrocinnamic acid esters, a noroxyneolignan, and a dibenzyl ether derivative. The extracts of fresh leaves and twigs also contain common aromatic compounds and flavonoids. The effects of these isolated aromatic compounds were studied at concentrations ranging from 10(-4) to 10(-7) M. Some of these compounds exhibited activities that were higher than that of the commercial pre-emergence herbicide pendimethalin.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause kidney stones, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and calcium deficiency due to its high concentration of oxalic acid. When ingested, the acid interferes with the body's ability to absorb essential minerals. This process often leads to a calcium deficiency in the bloodstream. The accumulation of calcium oxalate crystals in the urinary tract is a primary cause of painful kidney stones. Consuming the plant may also trigger gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, and intense abdominal pain.
