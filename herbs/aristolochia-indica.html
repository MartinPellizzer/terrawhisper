Aristolochia indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Aristolochia indica?

Aristolochia indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Piperales and falls within the family Aristolochiaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Aristolochia and the species indica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aristolochia indica has ovate to lanceolate leaves with entire margins and a cordate base, climbing stems, and tubular, brownish-purple flowers arranged in clusters. The foliage is typically dark green and grows in an opposite arrangement along the slender, twining vines. Its reproductive structures consist of a perianth that forms a distinct tube leading to a swollen chamber. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils to facilitate pollination by specific insect visitors. The plant often produces small, capsule-like fruits that contain numerous tiny seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of East and Southeast Asia, spanning from China and Japan through Taiwan to parts of Southeast Asia. It is commonly found in moist, shaded habitats such as forest edges and dense shrublands where humidity remains high. Within its native range, the species thrives in various elevations ranging from coastal lowlands to hilly terrain. Its presence is often closely tied to specific microclimates that support its climbing growth habit. Detailed botanical records indicate that its distribution is most concentrated in the warm, temperate zones of the Eastern Palearctic and Oriental realms.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Aristolochia indica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its whole plant, including the roots, leaves, and stems. Historically, various parts of the herb have been utilized in traditional medicine to treat ailments such as inflammation, skin conditions, and digestive issues. The roots are often processed to create decoctions intended to reduce swelling or fever. However, modern scientific research has identified the presence of aristolochic acids within these tissues, which are highly nephrotoxic and carcinogenic. Because of these potent toxins, the medicinal use of the plant is strictly cautioned against or prohibited in many regions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Aristolochia indica?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antifungal, antimicrobial, and antioxidant pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals, which provides significant antioxidant protection for cells. The extract also demonstrates a broad spectrum of antimicrobial properties by inhibiting the growth of various harmful pathogens. Specifically, it exhibits targeted antibacterial and antifungal actions against diverse bacterial and fungal strains. These combined medicinal properties suggest that Aristolochia indica holds potential for developing various therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, Aristolochia indica (AI) was evaluated for its antioxidant activity, phytochemical composition, antibacterial, antifungal, cytotoxic, and anti-inflammarotory potential. In the ranking of cytotoxicity for the tested extracts, AI was positioned between TTF and CP. Regarding the in vitro anti-inflammarotory potential at a concentration of 200 μg/mL, the ranking placed AI after CP, TTL, and TTF. The study investigated these activities through various methods including the brine shrimp assay and albumin denaturation, membrane stabilization, and proteinase inhibitory assays.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Aristolochic acid-I, Aristolochic acid, Aristolochic acid II, Magnoflorine, and Savinin. These specific alkaloids and organic acids are distributed throughout various parts of the plant tissue. Aristolochic acid and its derivatives are particularly noted for their potent biological activity and potential toxicity. Magnoflorine serves as another significant alkaloid within the chemical profile of the species. Additionally, the presence of Savinin contributes to the overall complex chemical composition of the plant.

As discussed in a study, Aristolochia indica roots contain various compounds including savinin (1) isolated from fraction D and (12S)-7,12-secoishwaran-12-ol (3) isolated from fraction A. The plant contains aristolochic acid-I (2) isolated from fraction E, which was found to be toxic when administered postcoitally to hamsters. Additional constituents include aristolic acid (4) prepared from aristolochic acid-I (2) and methyl aristolate (5) prepared by methylating aristolic acid (4). The study also identified seven previously reported compounds, a new naphthoquinone called aristolindiquinone (6) from fraction E, and magnoflorine from fraction C. Other substances tested included cis- and trans-p-coumaric acid.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating fever, infections, inflammation, and the effects of snake venom following snake bites. Traditional practitioners often prepare decoctions from the roots or leaves to reduce body temperature and combat systemic infections. The plant's chemical properties are also utilized to mitigate localized inflammation at the site of a wound. When dealing with snake bites, it is applied to help neutralize or manage the symptoms caused by snake venom. This medicinal application remains a significant part of various folk medicine practices.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the roots or stems in water to extract concentrated medicinal compounds. A tincture is also commonly made by soaking the dried plant parts in alcohol to create a shelf-stable liquid extract. For topical applications, the crushed leaves or roots are applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat localized inflammation. These different methods allow for varying levels of potency and absorption depending on the intended use. Practitioners select these specific preparations based on whether the treatment is meant for internal consumption or external healing.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe nephrototoxicity leading to kidney damage, carcinogenicities that trigger cancer, and uropathy resulting from the ingestion of aristolochic acids. These toxic compounds are known to damage the renal tubules and the lining of the urinary tract. Individuals consuming the plant often experience acute gastrointestinal distress, including nausea and vomiting. Abdominal pain is another common symptom associated with the ingestion of its harmful chemicals. Long-term exposure significantly increases the risk of developing upper urinary tract cancers.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Nephrotoxicity (kidney damage)
  • Carcinogenicity (cancer)
  • Uropathy
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Abdominal pain
