Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Java Tea (Orthosiphon Stamineus)?

Java Tea, also known as Orthosiphon stamineus, is a flowering plant native to Southeast Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Java tea is Orthosiphon stamineus. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Orthosiphon, and species Orthosiphon stamineus. It is commonly known as Java tea in Southeast Asia, and as Kencur or Kandur in Indonesia. It is also known as Java tea in Malaysia and Singapore. The synonyms for this plant include Orthosiphon aristatus, Orthosiphon pubescens, and regional names such as Kencur in Indonesia and Kandur in parts of Malaysia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Java tea is a perennial shrub that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters, with a compact, upright structure and a woody stem that becomes more branched with age.

Its leaves are elliptical to ovate in shape, with a smooth or slightly serrated margin, and are arranged alternately along the stem. The flowers are small, white or pale pink, and clustered in dense inflorescences at the ends of the branches, producing a mild, sweet aroma. The root system is fibrous and extensively branched, anchoring the plant firmly in the soil and supporting its growth in various soil types.

The plant has a distinctively bitter taste, with a rough, slightly hairy texture on the leaves and stems, and a pale green to grayish-green coloration.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Java Tea, or Orthosiphon stamineus, is native to Southeast Asia, particularly Indonesia, Malaysia, and the Philippines, and is commonly found in tropical and subtropical regions with high humidity and well-drained soils.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves and stems of Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) are primarily used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Java Tea, derived from Orthosiphon stamineus, contains a variety of bioactive compounds including iridoid glycosides, flavonoids, tannins, saponins, and essential minerals such as potassium, magnesium, and calcium.

Pharmacological Properties

Java Tea, or Orthosiphon stamineus, exhibits pharmacological properties primarily attributed to its rich content of antioxidants, flavonoids, and saponins, which contribute to its diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) involve its active compounds, such as saponins, flavonoids, and phenolic acids, which exhibit diuretic, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties, contributing to its traditional use in supporting kidney function, reducing inflammation, and promoting urinary health.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Java Tea, also known as Orthosiphon stamineus, is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support kidney function, reduce uric acid levels, and promote urinary health by increasing urine production and aiding in the elimination of toxins and excess minerals from the body.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Java Tea, or Orthosiphon stamineus, is typically prepared as a herbal tea by steeping the dried leaves in hot water, though it is also available in capsule, powder, and tincture forms for various consumption methods.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) for general use ranges from 3 to 5 grams of dried leaves per cup of water, prepared as a tea, with 2 to 3 cups consumed daily, though specific dosing may vary based on individual health conditions and professional guidance.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) is generally considered safe when consumed in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea or diarrhea, in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and for children has not been fully established.

Drug Interactions

Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) may interact with medications such as diuretics, antihypertensives, and blood thinners due to its potential to enhance their effects, leading to increased risk of dehydration, low blood pressure, or bleeding complications.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals, and excessive consumption could lead to dehydration due to its diuretic properties. It is advisable to consult a healthcare professional before use, especially for pregnant women, nursing mothers, and individuals with kidney disease or hypertension.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Java Tea, or Orthosiphon stamineus, is primarily cultivated in tropical regions such as Southeast Asia, where it thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil, and is typically harvested by hand during its peak growth season to ensure optimal potency and quality.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) typically involve harvesting the leaves, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using solvent-based methods such as water or ethanol extraction to isolate active compounds like saponins, flavonoids, and essential oils.

Storage and Shelf Life

Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dry, and dark place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and prevent spoilage, with a typical shelf life of 1 to 2 years when properly stored.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of Java Tea (Orthosiphon stamineus) as a herbal medicine primarily focus on its diuretic properties, anti-inflammatory effects, and potential role in managing conditions such as hypertension and kidney disorders.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Java Tea, also known as Orthosiphon stamineus, has been traditionally used in Southeast Asian countries for its diuretic properties and as a remedy for urinary tract infections, kidney stones, and hypertension.
