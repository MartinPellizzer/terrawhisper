Vitex peduncularis

What's the taxonomical classification of Vitex peduncularis?

Vitex peduncularis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Lamiales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Lamiaceae, where it is placed within the genus Vitex, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species peduncularis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Vitex peduncularis has opposite, decussate leaves that are typically lanceolate to oblong in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins and a pubescent underside. The plant exhibits a woody structure characterized by slender stems and a branching habit common to many shrubs in the Lamiaceae family. Its reproductive morphology features small, tubular flowers that are borne on long, slender pedicels, which gives the species its specific epithet. These inflorescences are often arranged in terminal panicles or clusters that emerge from the axils of the foliage. The resulting fruit is a small, fleshy drupe that serves as a primary component of its morphological profile.

What are the pharmacological activities of Vitex peduncularis?

This plant has significant antibacterial and antimalarial pharmacological activities that demonstrate its therapeutic potential. Research indicates that its chemical constituents can inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria. Furthermore, the plant exhibits potent effects against malaria-causing parasites, making it a subject of interest for drug development. These biological properties are attributed to the specific secondary metabolites found within its tissues. Such medicinal qualities highlight the importance of investigating this species for future pharmaceutical applications.

According to a study published by "Asian Pacific journal of tropical medicine", Vitex peduncularis possesses a broad spectrum of antibacterial activity and showed the highest activity against all the screened microorganisms. The plant produced a zone of inhibition ranging between (11.000 ± 0.577) and (22.670 ± 0.667) mm. Its minimum inhibitory concentrations (MIC) were measured from 62.5 to 1 000.0 μg/mL. Additionally, the minimum bactericidal concentrations (MBC) for this species were found to be between 125.0 and 2 000.0 μg/mL. Consequently, it is recommended for the isolation of antibacterial molecules responsible for activity against the tested human pathogenic bacterial strains.
