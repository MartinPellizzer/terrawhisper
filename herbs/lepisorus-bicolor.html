Lepisorus bicolor

What's the taxonomical classification of Lepisorus bicolor?

Lepisorus bicolor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Polypodiinae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Polypodiales and the family Polypodiaceae, eventually reaching its specific classification within the genus Lepisorus and the species bicolor.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lepisorus bicolor has pinnate fronds characterized by a distinct bicolor appearance where the upper surface of the pinnules is green and the underside is covered in dense, silvery-white scales or hairs.

These scales are most prominent on the abaxial surface, providing a striking contrast to the verdant dorsal side. The stipes are typically dark and slender, supporting the delicate, spreading foliage. Small sori are located on the underside of the segments, often tucked near the midvein or margins.

This combination of scale density and color variation is a primary diagnostic feature for identifying the species.

        What are the herbal preparations of Lepisorus bicolor?

        Lepisorus bicolor is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion through the boiling or steeping of its leafy biomass in water. This aqueous extraction method allows the soluble phytochemicals within the plant tissue to be released into a liquid medium for consumption. Some traditional applications involve crushing the fresh leaves to create a topical paste applied directly to the skin. These various preparations are utilized to harness the specific medicinal properties inherent in the plant's organic compounds. The choice of preparation form typically depends on whether the intended therapeutic effect is systemic or localized.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Lepisorus bicolor can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or localized rashes if the plant's sap comes into direct contact with human skin. While specific toxicological data is limited, many ferns in similar environments contain compounds that may trigger sensitivity in certain individuals. Contacting the foliage can lead to redness, itching, or swelling at the site of exposure. It is generally advisable to wear gloves when handling the plant to prevent these dermatological reactions. Always wash any skin that may have touched the vegetation to minimize the risk of a reaction.
