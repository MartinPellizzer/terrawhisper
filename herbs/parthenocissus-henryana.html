Parthenocissus henryana

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Parthenocissus henryana has palmately compound leaves consisting of five to seven leaflets that are deeply lobed and display a dark, glossy green color. The individual leaflets are typically ovate or lanceolate in shape with finely serrated margins. This climbing vine utilizes specialized adhesive disks located on its tendrils to cling firmly to various surfaces. During the autumn season, the foliage undergoes a dramatic transformation, turning brilliant shades of red or purple. The plant also produces small, inconspicuous flowers followed by dark berries that attract local wildlife.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Parthenocissus henryana can cause skin irritation, redness, and blistering upon contact due to the presence of urushiol, the same allergenic oil found in poison ivy. This organic compound can trigger a delayed hypersensitivity reaction known as contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Symptoms typically manifest as an itchy, bumpy rash that may persist for several days or weeks. While the risk is lower than that of poison ivy, direct handling of the leaves or stems should still be avoided to prevent an outbreak. People with known allergies to toxicodendron species should exercise extreme caution around this climbing vine.
