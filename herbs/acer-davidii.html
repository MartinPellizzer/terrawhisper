Acer davidii

What's the taxonomical classification of Acer davidii?

Acer davidii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of its order, Sapindales, it falls into the family Sapindaceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Acer, specifically identified by its unique species name, davidii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer davidii has opposite, palmately lobed leaves that typically feature three to five distinct lobes and serrated margins.

The leaf blades are often ovate or broadly triangular in shape, measuring several centimeters in length. Its bark is characterized by a smooth, greyish texture that becomes more fissured as the tree matures. The plant produces small, winged samaras that develop in clusters from inconspicuous flowers.

These fruit structures facilitate wind dispersal, which is a key trait of the maple genus.

        What are the herbal preparations of Acer davidii?

        Acer davidii is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical tincture using its bark, leaves, or roots. Practitioners often boil the dried plant material in water to extract its active medicinal compounds for oral consumption. Some preparations involve steeping the leaves in hot water to create a milder tea for gentler therapeutic effects. Additionally, the plant can be processed into concentrated liquid extracts used for external applications on the skin. These various methods allow for the precise delivery of the plant's specific chemical constituents depending on the intended use.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Acer davidii can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals due to the presence of natural compounds in its sap and foliage. While generally considered safe for ornamental landscaping, direct contact with the tree's juices may trigger redness, itching, or a localized rash. There is limited scientific research regarding the systemic toxicity of this specific maple species if ingested. However, people with known allergies to various woody plants should exercise caution when handling its leaves or bark. Always wash hands thoroughly after pruning or touching the vegetation to minimize any potential reaction.
