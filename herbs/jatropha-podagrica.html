Jatropha podagrica

What's the taxonomical classification of Jatropha podagrica?

Jatrophia podagrica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is positioned in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its higher taxonomic ranks, the plant falls under the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae. Finally, it is categorized under the genus Jatropha and is identified by its specific species name, podaigrica.

What are the pharmacological activities of Jatropha podagrica?

This plant has demonstrated significant antibacterial, antimicrobial, antiprototozoal, antioxidant, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities in various scientific studies. Its chemical constituents provide potent defense mechanisms against a wide range of pathogenic bacteria and microbes. Research indicates that specific extracts can effectively inhibit the growth of protozoa, making it a subject of interest for treating parasitic infections. Furthermore, the plant possesses strong antioxidant properties that help neutralize harmful free radicals within biological systems. Studies also highlight its cytotoxic potential, suggesting that certain compounds may play a role in targeting and destroying cancer cells.

As discussed in a study, the ethyl acetate extract of the stem bark of Jatropha podagrica exhibited antioxidant activity with IC50 values of 46.7 for DPPH, 66.0 for ABTS, and 492.6 for FRAP. The mixture C2 showed high antioxidant properties with an IC50 for DPPH and ABTS of 2.5 µg/mL, while mixture C3 had an IC50 for FRAP of 381 µg/mL. Fraction C4 acted as a potential agent in the growth inhibition of six bacterial strains with MIC values of 5, 20, 30, 20, 25, and 20 mg/mL. Additionally, the mixture C3 performed maximal inhibition on shoot growth at an IC50 of 49.4 µg/mL and root growth at an IC50 of 47.1 µg/mL. These findings suggest that the isolated constituents of J. podagrica possess antioxidant, antibacterial, and growth inhibitory potentials.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds corilagin, ellagic acid, gallic acid, jatropaggine A, and jatropaggine B. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the diverse biological activities observed in the species. Gallic acid and ellagic acid are well-known for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of corilagin further enhances the plant's therapeutic potential through its polyphenolic structure. Additionally, the alkaloids jatropaggine A and jatropaggine B provide unique pharmacological characteristics that distinguish this plant from others in its family.

As discussed in a study, the fruit of the medicinal plant Jatrophas podagrica was analyzed to identify 46 compounds through a high-resolution LC-MS strategy. During the identification process, 10 isomers were completely distinguished from each other. The 46 compounds were then simultaneously quantified using an authentic standard comparison method. To characterize their antioxidant capacities, the compounds were subjected to a 2,2'-azinobisis(3-ethylbenzothiazolin-6-sulfonic acid) radical (ABTS+) scavenging assay. Corilagin, gallic acid, ellagic acid, and phillygenin were identified as the four top antioxidant contributors. These four specific compounds are recommended to build up a quality-markers (Q-markers) system for Jatrophas podagrica fruits.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating infections caused by Trypano Soma evansi, combating breast cancer, inhibiting various tumor cell lines, and neutralizing venomous snake bites. Scientific investigations have highlighted its significant potential in pharmacological applications related to oncology. Researchers have specifically observed its ability to interfere with the growth of certain tumor cell lines in laboratory settings. Furthermore, its bioactive compounds are being studied for their efficacy against parasitic infections like Trypano Soma evansi. The plant also shows promise in traditional and experimental medicine for managing the effects of venomous snake bites.

As discussed in a study, the extracts of Jatropha podagrica demonstrated remarkable antitrypano somal activity against trypomastigotes of Trypano Soma eva nsi. These extracts exhibited IC50 values within the range of 27.2-52.6 microg/ml. Additionally, the selectivity indices for Jatropha podagrica were recorded in the range of 11.4-15.1.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and gastritis. These intense digestive symptoms can quickly lead to dangerous levels of dehydration if not treated. Some individuals may also experience dizziness as a secondary effect of these systemic reactions. Contact with the plant may further result in localized skin irritation.
