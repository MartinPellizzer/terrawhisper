Pinus densata

What's the taxonomical classification of Pinus densata?

Pinus densata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida, the subclass Pinidae, and the order Pinales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Pinaceae and falls under the genus Pinus, ultimately being identified by its specific species name, densata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pinus densata has needles that are typically two per fascicle, measuring 3 to 7 centimeters in length, and are characterized by a dense, dark green color with a slightly twisted appearance. The bark on mature trees becomes thick, scaly, and develops deep fissures with a grayish-brown hue. Its cones are relatively small and ovoid, often featuring thick, woody scales that protect the seeds. The tree typically exhibits a conical to irregular crown shape depending on its growth environment. This species often grows in stunted, multi-stemmed forms when located in harsh, high-altitude conditions.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of western Canada, specifically spanning from the Yukon and British Columbia through Alberta to parts of Saskatchewan. It thrives primarily within the subalpine zones of the Rocky Mountains and the Coast Mountains. The species is typically found at high elevations where rocky, well-drained soils are prevalent. Within these habitats, it often forms part of the forest canopy or exists as scattered individuals in open montane areas. Its range is closely tied to the specific climatic conditions provided by the interior and coastal mountain ranges of the Northwest.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, Pinus densata, requires well-draining, acidic soil and full sunlight to thrive in temperate or montane environments.

It is best cultivated in rocky or sandy substrates that prevent water from pooling around the root system. Regular watering is necessary during the establishment phase, though the mature tree is notably drought-tolerant once its root structure is deep. Providing ample space for its spreading canopy ensures proper air circulation and prevents fungal issues.

Careful monitoring of soil pH is essential to maintain the specific acidic conditions this species prefers.
