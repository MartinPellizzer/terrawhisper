Red Silk Cotton Tree (Butea superba) Uses

The Red Silk Cotton Tree is used to treat inflammation, infertility, and menstrual cramps. It addresses ovarian and uterine issues, as well as bacterial vaginosis and sexually transmitted infections. It also helps with menstrual pain and male infertility, supporting reproductive health.

Red Silk Cotton Tree (Butea superba) Monograph

1. Inflammation

Red Silk Cotton Tree (Butea superba) is used to reduce inflammation due to its anti-inflammatory compounds. It helps alleviate symptoms in conditions like arthritis and skin inflammation. The active ingredients work by suppressing inflammatory responses in the body. Its traditional use supports its role in managing inflammatory disorders.

2. Infertility

Butea superba is used in traditional medicine to support fertility by enhancing reproductive health. It is believed to stimulate hormonal balance and improve ovarian function. Its active compounds may increase egg quality and promote uterine blood flow. It is often incorporated into herbal formulations for women's reproductive wellness.

3. Menstrual cramps

Butea superba is used to alleviate menstrual cramps due to its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. It helps reduce uterine contractions and soothe pain during menstruation. Traditionally, it is prepared as a decoction or powder and taken internally. Its efficacy is supported by traditional Thai medicine and modern pharmacological studies.

4. Ovarian issues

Butea superba is used in traditional herbal medicine to support hormonal balance and address ovarian issues. It is believed to enhance ovarian function and regulate menstrual cycles. Its active compounds may help reduce inflammation and improve reproductive health. It is often incorporated into formulations for women's health support.

5. Uterine inflammation

Butea superba is used in traditional herbal medicine to treat uterine inflammation due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. It helps reduce swelling and infection in the reproductive system. Often combined with other herbs, it supports healing and balances hormonal imbalances associated with the condition.

6. Ovarian inflammation

Butea superba is used in traditional herbal medicine to treat ovarian inflammation due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. It helps reduce swelling and pain associated with the condition. The active compounds in the plant support immune response and tissue repair, making it a valued remedy in herbal treatments for gynecological disorders.

7. Sexually transmitted infections

Butea superba has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat sexually transmitted infections due to its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. It is believed to support the body's natural defenses against bacterial and viral pathogens. The tree's bark and flowers are often prepared as decoctions or tinctures for this purpose. Its efficacy is supported by both historical use and modern scientific research.

8. Menstrual pain

Butea superba is used in traditional Thai medicine to alleviate menstrual pain due to its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. It helps reduce cramping and discomfort associated with dysmenorrhea by regulating uterine contractions and reducing prostaglandin activity. Often prepared as a decoction or capsule, it is a key herb in herbal formulations for menstrual disorders.

9. Bacterial vaginosis

Butea superba is used in traditional herbal medicine to treat bacterial vaginosis due to its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. It helps restore the vaginal flora balance and reduce harmful bacterial overgrowth. The active compounds in the plant support natural healing and may alleviate symptoms associated with the condition.

10. Male infertility

Butea superba is used in traditional Thai medicine to support male fertility by enhancing sperm production and motility. It contains compounds that may improve hormonal balance and reduce oxidative stress, contributing to better reproductive health. Its role in treating infertility is supported by historical use and some modern studies.
