Kalanchoe pinnata

What's the taxonomical classification of Kalanchoe pinnata?

Kalanchoe pinnata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is organized under the order Saxifragales and falls within the family Crasculaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Kalanchoe, and its specific species designation, pinnata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Kalanchoe pinnata has succulent, fleshy leaves that are typically oblong or lanceolate in shape and possess distinctly serrated or scalloped margins. The leaf surfaces are smooth and can range in color from a bright green to a deep reddish-purple hue depending on light exposure. These thick leaves serve as specialized storage organs for water, allowing the plant to thrive in arid environments. The plant also features small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from upright stems during its blooming period. Its growth habit is often spreading, with stems that can become somewhat woody as the plant matures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Madagascar and has since spread across many tropical and subtropical regions including parts of Africa, Asia, Australia, and the Americas. It thrives in various environments ranging from coastal areas to inland tropical forests. Due to its ability to reproduce through leaf cuttings, it has become naturalized in many gardens and wild landscapes globally. The species is particularly successful in warm, humid climates where it can easily colonize disturbed soils. This widespread distribution is largely attributed to its resilience and ease of propagation.

What are the pharmacological activities of Kalanchoe pinnata?

This plant has potent pharmacological activities including anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, and anticancer properties. Its bioactive compounds work to regulate blood glucose levels, making it a subject of interest for managing diabetes. The presence of specific phytochemicals also helps reduce inflammation and fight off various bacterial infections. Furthermore, its high antioxidant capacity protects cells from oxidative stress, which may contribute to its observed anticancer effects. These diverse biological actions highlight the significant therapeutic potential of Kalanchoe pinnata in modern medicine.

As discussed in a study, Kalanchoe pinnata has been researched for its potential anti-type-2 diabetes mellitus mechanism. The methanolic extract of Kalanchoe pinnata shows alpha-amylase inhibitory activity with an IC50 of 29.50 ± 0.04 μg/ml. It also demonstrates alpha-glucosidase inhibitory activity with an IC50 of 32.04 ± 0.35 μg/ml, which is similar to the standard drug acarbose with an IC50 of 35.82 ± 0.14 μg/ml. Bioactive compounds like luteolin, kaempferol, alpha amyrin, and stigmasterol target core proteins including AMY2A, NOX4, RPS6KA3, ADRA2A, CHRM5, and IL2. Additionally, molecular simulation of the alpha-amylase-friedelin complex was performed for a 200 ns duration to depict stability.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds bryophyllin A, bryophyllin C, kaempferol, luteolin, and quercetin. These specific bioactive molecules contribute to the various therapeutic properties observed in the species. The bryophyllins are specialized compounds often linked to unique biological activities within the plant. Additionally, the presence of flavonoids like kaempferol, luteolin, and quercetin provides significant antioxidant benefits. Together, these diverse chemical constituents form a complex profile of phytochemicals.

As discussed in a study, five bufadienolides (1-5) were isolated from the leaves of Kalanchoe pinnata. These compounds were examined for their inhibitory effects on Epstein-Barr virus early antigen (EBV-EA) activation in Raji cells. Among the tested compounds, bryophyllin A (1) exhibited the most marked inhibition with an IC50 of 0.4 microM. Bryophyllin C (2), a reduction analogue of 1, and bersaldegenin-3-acetate (3) were found to be less active. These results suggest that the bufadienolides found in the plant are potential cancer chemopreventive agents.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a fresh leaf extract, a topical poultice, an infusion, or a decoction to treat various ailments. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and stomach cramps in humans who ingest its parts, and it is toxic to pets if consumed. When animals eat the leaves, they may experience significant gastrointestinal distress or more severe reactions. People should avoid ingesting any part of the plant to prevent these painful digestive symptoms. Pet owners must keep this succulent out of reach of dogs and cats to ensure their safety. Always consult a professional if accidental ingestion occurs.
