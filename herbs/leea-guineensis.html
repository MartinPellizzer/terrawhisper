Leea guineensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Leea guineensis?

Leea guineensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent through the order Vitales, the plant is a member of the family Vitaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Leea, with the individual species identified as guineensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Leea guineensis has opposite, simple, ovate to elliptic leaves with entire margins and an acute apex, growing on woody, climbing or scrambling stems. The foliage often exhibits a deep green color on the upper surface with paler undersides. Small, clustered flowers typically emerge from the leaf axils in terminal or axillary inflorescences. These blossoms give way to small, fleshy berries that serve as the primary fruit structure. The stems can develop woody textures as the plant matures within its tropical forest habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of West and Central Africa, spanning from Guinea and Sierra Leone eastward through Nigeria to the Congo Basin. It primarily thrives in the humid, low-altitude evergreen forests characteristic of these equatorial zones. Within these habitats, the species is often found in shaded understory environments where moisture levels remain consistently high. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of dense canopy cover and stable tropical climates. Consequently, its range is restricted to specific ecological niches within the African tropical belt.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant thrives when grown in moist, well-draining soil under bright, indirect sunlight and temperatures between 65 and 80 degrees Fahrenheit.

It requires consistent watering to maintain high humidity levels, which prevents the foliage from drying out. Providing regular fertilization during the growing season helps support its vibrant red flower clusters. Because it is native to tropical regions, it should be protected from frost and harsh, direct midday sun.

Proper drainage is essential to prevent root rot in these moisture-loving shrubs.
