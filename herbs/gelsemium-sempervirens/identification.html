Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Gelsemium sempervirens is the scientific name for the yellow wildflower commonly used in herbal medicine. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Synonyms include Datura candida and Cassia sempervirens. Common names include yellow wood, crown of the kings, and wild jasmine.

Morphological Characteristics

Gelsemium sempervirens is a woody vine with a climbing growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it to supports. The stem is smooth, green, and slightly hairy. Leaves are opposite, oval, and have a smooth margin. The inflorescence is a loose cluster of yellow flowers. Flowers are trumpet-shaped with yellow petals and a long, narrow corona. The fruit is a capsule that releases small, flat seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Gelsemium sempervirens can be distinguished by its yellow, clustered flowers and opposite, simple, dark green leaves. It has a woody stem with smooth, light-colored bark. It lacks the bright red or orange flowers of similar species like Tripterygium wilfordii. Its scent is faint and not overpowering compared to other herbs.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Gelsemium sempervirens is native to the southeastern United States, particularly Florida and the Gulf Coast. It thrives in humid, subtropical climates. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It typically grows at low to mid-elevation, often in wooded areas or along stream banks.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Gelsemium sempervirens blooms from late winter to early spring, typically between February and April, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during its flowering period for accurate botanical recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Gelsemium sempervirens is closely related to species within the genus Gelsemium, which belongs to the family Loganiaceae. It shares key morphological and genetic traits with its closest relatives, such as Gelsemium elegans, including similar floral structures and alkaloid profiles.
