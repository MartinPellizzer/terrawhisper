Dipterocarpus turbinatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Dipterocarpus turbinatus?

Dipterocarpus turbinatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malvales. This plant is further categorized into the family Dipterocarpaceae and is situated within the genus Dipterocarpus, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species turbinatus.

What are the therapeutic actions of Dipterocarpus turbinatus?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, stomach aches, and various inflammatory conditions. The medicinal properties of its leaves and bark are often utilized to soothe irritated skin or accelerate the healing of open cuts. Some traditional practices also involve using extracts to alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort and digestive distress. These applications rely on the plant's natural bioactive compounds to combat pathogens and reduce swelling. Local healers frequently prepare specific decoctions to address these diverse ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Dipterocarpus turbinatus?

Dipterocarpus turbinatus is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a topical paste depending on the intended therapeutic use. The plant material is typically dried and ground into a fine powder to facilitate easier ingestion or mixing. For internal ailments, the dried parts are boiled in water to create a potent liquid extract. The resulting powder can also be mixed with honey or other carriers to improve palatability. In some traditional practices, the leaves are crushed into a thick paste for application directly onto the skin.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Dipterocarpus turbinatus can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if its chemical compounds are ingested or touched without protection. While specific toxicological studies on this exact species are limited, many plants in related genera contain secondary metabolites that act as irritants to human tissue. Contact with the sap or foliage may trigger localized redness, itching, or swelling in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of untreated plant material could potentially lead to nausea or stomach cramping due to bioactive alkaloids. It is essential to exercise caution and avoid direct exposure to prevent these adverse physiological reactions.
