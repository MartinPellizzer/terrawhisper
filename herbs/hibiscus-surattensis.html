Hibiscus surattensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hibiscus surattensis has woody, perennial stems with ovate, serrated leaves and large, trumpet-shaped flowers that typically feature five petals in shades of red, pink, or yellow. Its reproductive structures include a prominent central column where the numerous stamens are fused around the style. The flowers are supported by long pedicels and emerge from the leaf axils or terminal positions. The root system is robust and woody, providing stability for its shrubby growth habit.

This plant has potent antiplasmodial and dermatological activities that make it valuable for medicinal applications. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate a significant ability to inhibit the growth of Plasmodium parasites, which are responsible for malaria. Furthermore, the plant exhibits strong antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties that effectively treat various dermatological infections. These therapeutic effects are often attributed to the presence of specific phytochemicals like flavonoids and tannins found in its leaves and flowers. Researchers continue to study these properties to develop more effective natural treatments for skin ailments and parasitic diseases.

As discussed in a study, Hibiscus surattensis L. is one of 5 ethanolic plant extracts tested for its antiplasmodial activity against asexual stage parasites of a culture-adapted strain of P. falciparum. This extract showed inhibitory activity against asexual P. falciparum and selected new allelic variants of the Msp1 and Msp2 genes compared to unexposed parasites. The newly selected Msp1 gene variants include K1_100bp and RO33_300bp. For the Msp2 gene, the selected variants are FC27_150bp, FC27_300bp, FC27_400bp, and FC27_600bp. Despite these effects, there was no significant difference in the multiplicity of infection (MOI) between drug-exposed and unexposed parasites.
