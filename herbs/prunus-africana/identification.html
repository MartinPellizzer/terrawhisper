Botanical Identification of African Cherry (Prunus africana)

To identify African Cherry (Prunus africana) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

African Cherry (Prunus africana) Monograph

Scientific Classification

The African Cherry, or Prunus africana, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rosales, family Rosaceae, genus Prunus, and species Prunus africana. This classification places it within the rose family, highlighting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Prunus africana is the scientific name for African Cherry, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It belongs to the Rosaceae family and is widely recognized in herbal medicine for its medicinal properties. The name reflects its native African origin and its use in traditional healing practices.

Morphological Characteristics

African Cherry is a medium to large evergreen tree with a rounded crown. It has a deep taproot system and extensive lateral roots, providing stability. The stem is smooth, grey-barked, and can grow up to 20 meters tall. Leaves are alternate, simple, and elliptical with a leathery texture. Inflorescences are racemes, bearing small, white, fragrant flowers. The fruit is a drupe, red when mature, containing a single stone with one seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Prunus africana can be distinguished by its elliptical leaves with a short petiole, dense clusters of white flowers, and spherical, dark blue berries. It is often confused with other Prunus species, but its unique leaf shape and clustered flowering pattern help in accurate identification. The bark is thick and fissured, further aiding in differentiation from similar plants.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Prunus africana is native to tropical Africa, particularly in regions like West and Central Africa. It thrives in humid climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils. It is typically found at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

African Cherry blooms from February to April, producing white to pink flowers. Seed formation occurs between May and July, with seeds developing inside hard capsules. For identification purposes, the best time to collect plant material is during the flowering period, as this is when the plant is most easily recognized.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Prunus africana belongs to the Rosaceae family and is closely related to other Prunus species. It shares characteristics with cherries and plums, such as similar fruit structure and leaf morphology. Its closest relatives include Prunus africana var. africana and Prunus lusitanica, which are also native to Africa. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary history and medicinal properties.
