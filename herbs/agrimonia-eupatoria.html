Agrimonia eupatoria

What's the taxonomical classification of Agrimonia eupatoria?

Agrimonia eupatoria belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic journey continues through the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae, eventually reaching its specific designation within the genus Agrimonia and the species eupatoria.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Agrimonia eupatoria has an erect, branched stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 70 centimeters and features leaves that are deeply palmately lobed into several narrow segments. The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and possess serrated margins. Small, yellow, five-petaled flowers grow in dense, terminal clusters known as corymbs. These flowers are supported by long pedicels and are characteristic of the Rosaceae family. The plant also produces small, dry fruits called achenes that are often covered in minute hooked hairs to assist in seed dispersal.

What are the therapeutic actions of Agrimonia eupatoria?

Agrimonia eupatoria has therapeutic actions as a bitter, diaphoric, februgifuge, astringent, tonic, emmenagogue, expectorant, and diuretic. Its bitter properties stimulate digestion and act as a general tonic to restore vigor to the body. As a februgifuge and diaphoric, it helps reduce fever by inducing perspiration. The plant serves as an astringent to tighten tissues and an expectorant to help clear mucus from the respiratory tract. Additionally, it functions as a diuretic to promote kidney function and an emmenagogue to regulate menstrual cycles.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several medicinal compounds including apiagenin, flavonoids, isovitexin, luteolin, and vitexin. These bioactive molecules are categorized as flavonoids, which are known for their diverse therapeutic properties. Within this group, specific flavones such as apiagenin and luteolin contribute to the plant's biological activity. Additionally, the presence of C-glycosyl flavones like isovitexin and vitexin adds to its complex chemical profile. Together, these compounds work to provide the medicinal benefits associated with the species.

As discussed in a study, the aerial parts of Agrimonia eupatoria L. contain various bioactive natural compounds including polyphenols such as agrimoniin and flavonoids. The polyphenolic content of Agrimonia eupatoria L. was determined to be 1.3% in the dry matter of leaves, 0.3% in stems, 0.9% in fruits, 0.6% in seeds, and 0.5% in hypanthia. Agrimoniin served as the main polyphenolic compound in the aerial parts of this species. This plant is also a valuable source of flavonoid glycosides, specifically apigenin, luteolin and quercetin. These findings indicate that Agrimonia eupatoria L. may serve as a medicinal raw material or a valuable material for food use.
