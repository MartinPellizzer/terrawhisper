Madagascar Haronga (Haronga madagascariensis) belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the flowering plants. It is classified under the order Fabales, family Bignoniaceae, genus Haronga, and species madagascariensis. Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Madagascar Haronga is Haronga madagascariensis, first described by the French botanist Jean-Baptiste A. P. Delile in 1825. It has no widely recognized synonyms, though it is known by various common names, including "Haronga" and "Madagascar Haronga," reflecting its native region and traditional use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Haronga madagascariensis is a shrub with a woody stem and a fibrous root system. Its leaves are elliptical, dark green, and have a rough texture. The plant produces small, clustered inflorescences with white to pale yellow flowers. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, flat seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Haronga madagascariensis can be distinguished by its unique combination of features, including its lanceolate leaves with serrated edges, pale yellow flowers with five petals, and the presence of small, clustered fruits. It differs from similar species through its leaf shape, flower color, and fruit structure, which are key diagnostic traits in botanical identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Haronga madagascariensis is native to Madagascar, specifically the eastern and southern regions. It thrives in tropical climates with high humidity and rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 500 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Haronga madagascariensis blooms between October and December, producing yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs from January to March. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested during its flowering period, from October to December, when its distinguishing features are most visible.

Haronga madagascariensis is most closely related to species within the genus Haronga, sharing key morphological and genetic traits.
