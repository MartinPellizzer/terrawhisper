Cyclea gracillima

What's the taxonomical classification of Cyclea gracillima?

Cyclea gracillima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Menispermaceae. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Cyclea, with the binomial designation of the species gracillima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cyclea gracillima has woody climbing vines characterized by slender stems and trifoliate leaves consisting of three distinct leaflets. Each leaflet is typically ovate or elliptical in shape with smooth or slightly wavy margins and a pointed tip. The foliage is arranged alternately along the climbing stems to maximize sunlight absorption in forest undergrowth. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils to facilitate reproduction. The plant's structure is adapted for climbing through surrounding vegetation using its flexible stems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cyclea gracillima?

This plant has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities that contribute to its medicinal potential. The antioxidant properties of Cyclea gracillima help neutralize free radicals and prevent oxidative stress within biological systems. Research also indicates that its extracts possess the ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, the plant exhibits anti-inflammatory effects by modulating chemical pathways associated with swelling and pain. These diverse biological actions suggest that the species could serve as a valuable source for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, Cyclea gracillima Diels is a Taiwanese native medicinal herb whose antioxidant attributes were assayed using in vitro models. The extracts were tested for 2,2-diphenyl-1-(2,4,6-trinitrophenyl)-hydrazyl radical scavenging, Trolox equivalent antioxidant capacity, reducing power, and chelating ferrous ions. These extracts also showed inhibition of human low density lipoprotein oxidation, inhibition of human erythrocyte hemolysis, and scavenging oxygen radicals in human blood. The analysis revealed that the extracts contained 6 phenolic acids, mainly ferulic acid, sinapic acid, and syringic acid. Additionally, 12 flavonoids were identified, including mainly naringenin, myricetin, naringin, and apigenin. These extracts with high polyphenol levels, especially those extracted with ethanol, presented remarkable antioxidant effects in all assays.
