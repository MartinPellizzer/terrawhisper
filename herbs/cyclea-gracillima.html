Cyclea gracillima

What's the taxonomical classification of Cyclea gracillima?

Cyclea gracillima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Menispermaceae. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Cyclea, with the binomial designation of the species gracillima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cyclea gracillima has woody climbing vines characterized by slender stems and trifoliate leaves consisting of three distinct leaflets.

Each leaflet is typically ovate or elliptical in shape with smooth or slightly wavy margins and a pointed tip. The foliage is arranged alternately along the climbing stems to maximize sunlight absorption in forest undergrowth. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils to facilitate reproduction.

The plant's structure is adapted for climbing through surrounding vegetation using its flexible stems.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Cyclea gracillima targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX), along with various pro-inflammatory cytokines and signaling pathways. The bioactive alkaloids and polyphenols present in the plant act by inhibiting the expression of nitric oxide synthase (NOS) to reduce oxidative stress. These compounds also interact with nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) to suppress the transcription of genes responsible for the inflammatory response. Additionally, certain constituents may modulate adenosine receptors or interact with specific kinase pathways to exert neuroprotective and antimicrobial effects. These multifaceted protein interactions underpin the plant's documented traditional uses in treating swelling, pain, and infections.

      What are the herbal preparations of Cyclea gracillima?

      Cyclea gracillima is prepared as a decoction by boiling the fresh leaves in water, as a topical poultice applied directly to the skin, or as a juice extracted through crushing the plant material. The decoction is most commonly consumed orally to treat various internal ailments and digestive issues. For skin conditions such as wounds or inflammation, the leaves are crushed into a paste and applied as a warm poultice. Some traditional practices also involve using the expressed juice of the leaves for medicinal purposes. These different methods allow for both systemic absorption and localized treatment depending on the specific ailment being addressed.
