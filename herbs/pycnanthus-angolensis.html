Pycnanthus angolensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Pycnanthus angolensis?

Pycnanthus angolensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Magnoliales. Further taxonomic refinement identifies it as a member of the family Myristicaceae, belonging to the genus Pycnanthus, with the specific epithet angolenis defining its species designation.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pycnanthus angolenensis has large, leathery, evergreen leaves that are elliptical to oblong in shape with entire margins and a glossy dark green surface. These fruits are typically characterized by a bright red or orange exocarp that surrounds a dark pulp. What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of West and Central Africa, stretching from Guinea and Sierra Leone in the west to the Democratic Republic of the Congo in the east. It thrives primarily within the Guineo-Congolian lowland forest biome where rainfall is abundant. Populations are frequently found in countries such as Nigeria, Cameroon, Gabon, and Equatorial Guinea. The species favors humid environments characterized by dense canopy cover and stable tropical temperatures. Its distribution is closely tied to the availability of moist, nutrient-rich soils found throughout these equatorial belts.

What are the pharmacological activities of Pycnanthus angolensis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including AChE inhibition, BChE inhibition, antihyperglycemic, antimalarial, and cytotoxicity properties. Its ability to inhibit acetylcholinesterase and butyrylcholinesterase suggests potential therapeutic applications for managing neurodegenerative disorders like Alzheimer's disease. The plant also exhibits antihyperglycemic effects that may assist in regulating blood glucose levels in diabetic models. Furthermore, its documented antimalarial properties provide a natural basis for combating parasitic infections. Finally, the observed cytotoxicity of its extracts indicates a capacity to target and destroy specific cell lines, which is a critical factor in cancer research.

According to a study published by "Journal of pharmaceutical and biomedical analysis", Pycnanthus angolensis displayed high DPPH and ABTS scavenging, ferric and cupric reducing, and total antioxidant capacity in the phosphomolybdenum assay. This plant showed similar results for AChE, BChE, and tyrosinase inhibition, as well as a comparable α-amylase inhibitory effect. The methanol extract of the stem bark appeared to be the most bioactive among the three solvents tested. Pycnanthus angolensis was found to be the richest in TFC with values ranging from 7.68-12.48 mg RE/g. Additionally, genistein (m/z 271.06), procyanidin B (m/z 577.13), and C (m/z 865.19) isomers were identified in the stem bark extracts. Consequently, the plant can be considered a potential pharmaceutical agent or functional food component to reduce the risks of oxidative stress-related disorders.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several bioactive medicinal compounds including Pycnanthuquinone A, Pycnanthuquinone B, Pycnanthuquinone C, Formononetin, and Prunetin. These specific quinones are recognized for their unique chemical structures and potential therapeutic properties. Additionally, the presence of the lignans Formononetin and Prunetin contributes to the plant's complex phytochemical profile. Researchers study these individual substances to understand their specific biological activities and medicinal applications. This diverse array of secondary metabolites makes the plant a significant subject in pharmacological investigation.

According to a study published by "Structural chemistry", molecular docking was performed on 84 selected phytochemicals to identify potential molecules. After assessing the pharmacokinetics and pharmacological abilities of 15 compounds with low binding energy values, only 2 compounds showed suitable pharmacological properties for human administration. Based on molecular dynamics simulations and reactivity studies, only Pycnanthuquinone C (17) and Pycnanthuquinone A (18) extracted from Pycnanthus angolensis were considered candidate inhibitors. These 2 specific compounds from Pycnanthus angolensis are expected to show excellent in vitro and in vivo activity against SARS-CoV-2.

According to a study published by "Structural chemistry", Pycnanthus angolensis is investigated for its potential to fight against COVID-19. From an initial set of 84 selected phytochemicals, 15 compounds showed low binding energy values during molecular docking with the SARS-CoV-2 main protease (PDB ID: 6lu7) protein. After assessing ADMET properties, only 2 compounds were shortlisted for having suitable pharmacological properties for human administration. Specifically, Pycnanthuquinone C (17) and Pycnanthuquinone A (18) extracted from Pycnanthus angolensis were identified as candidate inhibitors through molecular dynamics simulations and reactivity studies. It is expected that these 2 compounds could show excellent in vitro and in vivo activity against SARS-CoV-2.
