Corydalis polygalina

What's the taxonomical classification of Corydalis polygalina?

Corydalis polygalinina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Ranunculales. The plant is a specific representative of the family Papaveraceae, falling under the genus Corydalis, and is ultimately identified by its unique species designation, polygalina.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Corydalis polygalinina has a basal rosette of compound, pinnately divided leaves with small, ovate leaflets and slender, erect stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters. The foliage is characterized by a delicate, fern-like texture due to the deeply lobed structure of each leaf. During the flowering period, the plant produces small, zygomorphic flowers that are often pale yellow or cream-colored with distinct spurred petals. These blooms emerge from the stem and are supported by thin pedicels. The overall architecture is relatively low-growing and herbaceous, making it well-suited for woodland understories.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Corydalis polygalina contains a variety of bioactive isoquinoline alkaloids, primarily including protopine, corydaline, and various derivatives of benzylisoquinoline. These specific chemical compounds are known to exhibit significant pharmacological properties such as analgesic, sedative, and antitussive effects. Researchers study these alkaloids because they interact with various neurotransmitter systems in the central nervous system to alleviate pain. The plant's chemical profile also shows potential for anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activities in laboratory settings. What are the molecular targets of this plant?
