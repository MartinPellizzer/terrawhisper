Colocasia fallax

What's the taxonomical classification of Colocasia fallax?

Colocasia fallax belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this classification, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Alismatales and the family Araceae. Finally, it is classified under the genus Colocasia, with its specific designation being the species fallax.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Colocasia fallax contains medicinal properties primarily found in its edible rhizomes, leaves, and stems. The underground rhizomes are often processed to extract nutrients used in traditional remedies for digestive ailments. Its leaves are frequently utilized in topical applications or cooked to serve as a source of essential minerals. The fleshy stalks also provide dietary fiber and various phytochemicals that contribute to overall wellness. Careful preparation is essential because these plant parts contain calcium oxalate crystals that can cause irritation if not handled correctly.

      What are the herbal preparations of Colocasia fallax?

      Colocasia fallax is prepared as a topical paste, a decoction, or a poultice depending on the specific ailment being treated. The leaves are often crushed into a raw paste to be applied directly to skin irritations or swellings. For internal systemic issues, the plant material is boiled in water to create a medicinal decoction. Poultices are frequently constructed by wrapping the softened plant parts in cloth to maintain heat against the body. These various methods allow practitioners to extract the active compounds effectively for different therapeutic purposes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Colocasia fallax can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested due to the presence of calcium oxalate crystals. These microscopic, needle-shaped crystals penetrate the soft tissues of the mouth and throat upon contact. Contact with the plant's sap can lead to intense itching, redness, and swelling of the skin. If a person swallows the plant, they may experience immediate swelling of the tongue and throat, which can obstruct breathing. Such symptoms require prompt medical attention to prevent long-term tissue damage or respiratory failure.
