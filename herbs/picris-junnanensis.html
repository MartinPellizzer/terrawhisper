Picris junnanensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Picris junnanensis?

Picris junnanensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized into the order Asterales. It is a member of the family Asteraceae, which places it among the diverse group of composite flowers. Specifically, its taxonomic hierarchy concludes with its placement in the genus Picris, where it is identified as the species junnanensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Picris junnanensis has a perennial habit characterized by an erect stem that typically reaches heights of 20 to 50 centimeters and features leaves that vary from basal rosettes to smaller, lanceolate cauline leaves.

The foliage is generally entire or slightly dentate along the margins and possesses a distinctively textured surface. Its inflorescence consists of several small, yellow flower heads arranged in a corymbose cyme. Each individual head contains numerous disc florets and lacks prominent ray florets.

The pappus is composed of fine, white bristles that facilitate wind dispersal of the seeds.

        What are the herbal preparations of Picris junnanensis?

        Picris junnanensis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the intended medicinal application. The dried aerial parts of the plant are typically simmered in water to extract bitter principles for oral consumption. Infusions are often used when a milder strength is required to soothe digestive discomfort. For external ailments such as skin inflammation, the crushed leaves are applied directly to the affected area as a moist compress. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the concentration of active compounds to specific therapeutic needs.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Picris junnanensis can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or digestive upset if ingested or touched directly. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, members of the Asteraceae family often contain sesquiterpene lactones that trigger contact dermatitis. These compounds can lead to redness, itching, or swelling upon contact with sensitive skin. If consumed accidentally, the plant may cause gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or abdominal pain. Caution should be exercised when handling this species to avoid potential hypersensitivity.
