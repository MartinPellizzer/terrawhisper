Alangium chinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Alangium chinense?

Aliangium chinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Cornales and is a member of the family Cornaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific classification within the genus Aliangium and the species chinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Alangium chinense has an evergreen shrub or small tree structure characterized by opposite, elliptical leaves with entire margins and a glossy texture. The foliage typically exhibits a deep green color on the upper surface and a slightly paler underside. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters. These flowers eventually develop into small, fleshy drupes that serve as the plant's fruit. The woody stems are sturdy and contribute to the compact, bushy habit of the species.

What are the pharmacological activities of Alangium chinense?

This plant has pharmacological activities that include alleviating pain, anti-inflammatory, anti-rheumatic, breaking up blood stagnation, and cytotoxicity. Its medicinal properties are frequently utilized to reduce swelling and mitigate the discomfort associated with inflammatory conditions. Specifically, the plant's anti-rheumatic effects help manage chronic joint issues by targeting systemic inflammation. In traditional practices, it is also valued for its ability to promote circulation by breaking up blood stagnation. Furthermore, research into its cytotoxicity suggests potential applications in evaluating its ability to affect cell viability.

As discussed in a study, Alangium chinense is traditionally used to dispel wind, reduce dampness, break up blood stagnation, and alleviate pain. Research utilizing UPLC-MS/MS analysis revealed 75 blood-absorbed components in Alangium chinense, with 18 significantly linked to liver injury factors. Network pharmacology identified 123 potential targets associated with the hepatotoxicity induced by the plant. KEGG pathway analysis further revealed 247 signaling pathways associated with these common targets, emphasizing the PI3K-Akt, lipid and atherosclerosis, and chemical carcinogenesis-receptor activation signaling pathways. In vivo experiments showed that Alangium chinense increased serum levels of AST and ALT, with the water extract of the fibrous root causing the greatest damage. Additionally, in vitro experiments confirmed that Anabasine and the alkaloids of the fibrous root and root significantly increased AST and ALT levels in mouse AML-12 cells.
