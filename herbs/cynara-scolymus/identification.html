Botanical Identification of Artichoke (Cynara scolymus)

To identify Artichoke (Cynara scolymus) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

The Artichoke, Cynara scolymus, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically within the flowering plants. It is classified under the order Asterales and family Asteraceae, which includes many familiar plants like daisies and sunflowers. The genus Cynara contains several species, with Cynara scolymus being the most commonly cultivated for its edible leaves and flower buds.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Artichoke is Cynara scolymus, first described by the Swedish botanist Carl von Linné in 1753. It has several synonyms, including Cynara cardunculus var. scolymus, and is known by common names such as globe artichoke and heart of artichoke. Regional and historical names vary, reflecting its long use in traditional medicine and cuisine across different cultures.

Morphological Characteristics

Artichoke (Cynara scolymus) is a thistle-like plant with a rosette growth habit. It has a fibrous root system and a thick, ridged stem. Its leaves are large, spiny, and lobed, forming a dense basal rosette. The inflorescence is a purple flower head with dense, spiky bracts. Flowers are small and yellow, while the fruit is a spherical achene with a pappus for wind dispersal.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Artichoke can be distinguished by its purple, spiky flower heads and large, heart-shaped leaves with a fuzzy texture. It has a robust stem and produces edible buds. Unlike cardoon, it has fewer leaves, and unlike globe artichoke, it lacks the dense, layered leaves. Its leaves are more upright and less curled.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Artichoke, or Cynara scolymus, is native to the Mediterranean region, including southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with mild winters and warm summers. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Artichoke (Cynara scolymus) typically blooms in late spring to early summer, around May to June, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, from August to September. Harvesting of the edible portion, the flower buds, is usually done in early to mid-summer when the buds are tight and firm.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Artichoke belongs to the genus Cynara in the family Asteraceae. Its closest relatives include Cynara cardunculus and Cynara humilis. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, reflecting their close phylogenetic relationship within the genus.
