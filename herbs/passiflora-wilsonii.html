Passiflora wilsonii

What's the taxonomical classification of Passiflora wilsonii?

Passiflora wilsonii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Passifloraceae. Finally, it is identified within the genus Passiflora, specifically as the species wilsonii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Passiflora wilsonii has climbing, twining stems that produce deeply three-lobed leaves with serrated margins and distinctively long petioles.

The foliage is typically dark green and displays a somewhat coarse texture across its surface. Its flowers are characterized by complex structures including a prominent corona of fine filaments and a central reproductive column. Small, globose fruits develop from the ovaries following successful pollination.

These botanical features distinguish this specific passionflower species from its close relatives within the genus.

    What are the molecular targets of this plant?

    Passiflora wilsonii targets gamma-aminobutyric acid type A receptors, serotonin receptors, and various monoamine oxidase enzymes to exert its sedative and anxiolytic effects. The bioactive flavonoids and alkaloids within the plant act as modulators of the central nervous system by enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission. Specifically, these compounds interact with GABAergic pathways to reduce neuronal excitability and alleviate anxiety. They also demonstrate potential inhibition of enzymes involved in neurotransmitter degradation, which helps maintain higher levels of calming chemical signals in the brain. This multifaceted interaction with neurological protein structures accounts for the plant's traditional use in treating insomnia and restlessness.

    What are the therapeutic actions of Passiflora wilsonii?

    Passiflora wilsonii has sedative, anxiolytic, and mild hypnotic properties that primarily function by modulating GABAergic neurotransmission in the central nervous system.

The plant is often utilized to improve sleep quality and combat mild insomnia through its calming effect on the brain. Additionally, its phytochemical constituents may assist in soothing psychosomatic symptoms related to stress and restlessness.

        What are the herbal preparations of Passiflora wilsonii?

        Passiflora wilsonii is prepared primarily as a dried herbal infusion, a concentrated tincture, or a topical poultice derived from its leaves and stems. The dried plant material is often steeped in hot water to create a soothing tea used for its calming properties. For a more potent medicinal application, the botanical parts are macerated in alcohol to produce a liquid tincture. Some traditional practices involve crushing the fresh plant matter into a paste for direct application to the skin. These various forms allow for different absorption rates and methods of administration depending on the intended therapeutic use.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Passiflora wilsonii can cause adverse reactions such as drowsiness, dizziness, and gastrointestinal upset when ingested. These side effects primarily stem from the plant's sedative properties which may impair cognitive function or motor coordination. Individuals may also experience mild nausea or abdominal discomfort after consumption. Because the specific chemical profile of this species is not extensively documented in clinical literature, the full scope of potential toxicity remains uncertain. It is important to avoid use if you are pregnant, nursing, or taking sedative medications to prevent dangerous interactions.
