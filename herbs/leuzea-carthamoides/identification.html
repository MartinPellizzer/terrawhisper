Leuzea carthamoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Leuzea, and species Leuzea carthamoides. Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of the plant is Leuzea carthamoides, first described by the botanist Linnaeus. It has several synonyms, including Hemistepta carthamoides and Leuce carthamoides. Common names include "devil's weed" and "milkweed," while regional names vary by location. Historically, it was also known as "false dandelion" in some areas.

Morphological Characteristics

Leuzea carthamoides is an upright, herbaceous perennial with a taproot system. Its stems are slender, branched, and covered in fine hairs. The leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate, with hairy surfaces and serrated edges. The plant produces a panicle inflorescence with yellow flowers arranged in clusters. The fruit is a small, hairy achene containing a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Leuzea carthamoides can be distinguished by its robust, hairy stems and large, heart-shaped leaves with rough texture. The flowers are yellow and clustered in heads, often with a slight greenish tinge. It is typically found in moist habitats, which helps differentiate it from similar species with different growth preferences. Its distinct leaf shape and stem characteristics are key identifiers.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Leuzea carthamoides is native to Central Asia, particularly regions like Kazakhstan and southern Russia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained soils, often found in dry grasslands and steppe areas. It typically grows at low to mid-altitude ranges, between 500 and 1500 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Leuzea carthamoides blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn, around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering stage, which is optimal for accurate botanical identification.
