Anemone rivularis

What's the taxonomical classification of Anemone rivularis?

Anemone rivularis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Ranunculales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Ranunculaceae, falling under the genus Anemone, and is specifically identified by the species name rivularis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anemone rivularis has basal leaves that are deeply divided into three to five narrow, linear segments and terminal flowers featuring five to seven white petals with yellow centers.

The plant typically grows to a height of about 10 to 25 centimeters, supported by a slender, hairy stem. Its foliage consists of both basal leaves and smaller, less complex leaves located further up the stem. The flowers are characterized by their delicate, star-like appearance and occur during the late spring or early summer months.

This species often develops from a creeping rhizome that allows it to spread through moist, riparian habitats.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

        What are the herbal preparations of Anemone rivularis?

        Anemone rivularis is prepared primarily as a topical application such as a crushed poultice, an infused oil, or a diluted tincture for external use. Because the plant contains protoanemonin, which is highly irritating to the skin and mucous membranes, it is rarely used in oral forms. Practitioners typically use the fresh or dried plant material to create ointments intended to soothe localized external irritations. These preparations must be carefully controlled to prevent chemical burns or intense dermatological reactions. Such topical preparations are generally limited to specific traditional applications rather than widespread medicinal use.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Anemone rivularis can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and intense stinging sensations upon contact with human tissue. This plant contains potent irritants that react immediately when the sap or physical structure touches the skin. Contact may lead to localized redness, swelling, and the formation of painful blisters. If the toxins enter the eyes or mucous membranes, they can cause significant inflammation and temporary vision impairment. Prolonged exposure might result in lingering dermatitis or hypersensitivity in certain individuals.
