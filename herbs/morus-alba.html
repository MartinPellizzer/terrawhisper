White Mulberry, scientifically known as Morus alba, is a deciduous tree native to Asia and widely cultivated for its medicinal and nutritional properties.

It is recognized as a medicinal herb and adaptogen, valued for its ability to support overall health and resilience. The fruit and leaves of the tree are rich in nutrients like vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants, offering primary benefits such as improved digestion, blood sugar regulation, and cardiovascular health. Scientific and Botanical Profile

White Mulberry, with botanical name Morus alba, is a deciduous tree belonging to the family Moraceae, commonly known as the mulberry family.

It is also referred to as Common Mulberry, Morus, Silk Cotton Tree, Morus Tree, Silk Mulberry, and Chinese Mulberry, reflecting its widespread use and cultural significance. Native to regions of Asia, including China, India, and the Middle East, this species has been cultivated for thousands of years for its fruit, bark, and leaves. Morphologically, Morus alba is a tall tree with smooth, gray bark, alternate leaves that are ovate to broadly ovate with a serrated margin, and small, aggregate fruits that turn from green to white or pink when mature.

Its versatile nature has made it an important plant in both ecological and economic contexts.

History and Cultural Relevance

White Mulberry was used extensively in ancient civilizations such as China, India, and the Middle East for its medicinal, nutritional, and cultural significance.

In traditional Chinese medicine, it has been valued for centuries for its ability to support digestive health, detoxify the body, and promote skin health, with leaves and fruits commonly used in herbal formulations. The plant also holds a special place in cultural rituals, such as being part of traditional festivals and offerings in some Asian communities, where it symbolizes prosperity and longevity. Today, White Mulberry remains relevant in modern herbal practices, with its leaves still used to make tea that aids in weight management and blood sugar regulation.

Its enduring presence across cultures highlights its deep-rooted importance in both health and tradition.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

White Mulberry contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, alkaloids, terpenes, and essential oils, which contribute to its therapeutic properties.

The leaves and fruits are rich in vitamins such as vitamin A, C, and E, as well as minerals like calcium, iron, and potassium, making it a nutrient-dense plant. It also contains significant amounts of antioxidants, including polyphenols and anthocyanins, which help neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress. These compounds work synergistically to support immune function, improve cardiovascular health, and regulate blood sugar levels by enhancing insulin sensitivity.

Additionally, the presence of certain alkaloids and terpenes may influence metabolic processes and have anti-inflammatory effects in the body.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Morus alba has been widely recognized for its medicinal properties and numerous health benefits, particularly in supporting the immune system, digestive health, and skin condition.

It is known to promote detoxification by aiding the liver and kidneys, while also providing antioxidants that help reduce inflammation and oxidative stress throughout the body. Compared to similar herbs like mulberry (Morus nigra), Morus alba is often considered more potent in its ability to support metabolic functions and improve blood sugar regulation. Its unique advantage lies in its high concentration of flavonoids and dietary fiber, which contribute to better cardiovascular health and gastrointestinal wellness.

Overall, Morus alba stands out as a versatile herbal remedy with a broad spectrum of benefits across multiple body systems.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Morus alba has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its various therapeutic properties, and it is available in several forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

For internal use, the most common preparations include making a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, a decoction by boiling the leaves for a longer period, or an infusion using fresh leaves. Topical applications involve using the leaves as a poultice or applying a diluted tincture directly to the skin. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-3 grams of dried leaves per cup of tea, while for children, it should be used only under medical supervision.

It is advised to use morus alba sparingly, with a frequency of no more than 2-3 times per week and a duration not exceeding two weeks to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Morus alba can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential health benefits, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and immunomodulatory properties.

However, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and hypoglycemic effects, particularly when consumed in large quantities. It may interact with medications like antidiabetics, anticoagulants, and immunosuppressants, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid morus alba due to limited safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a healthcare professional, start with small doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Morus alba grows best in well-drained, fertile soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering during dry periods. It thrives in temperate climates and benefits from annual pruning to promote healthy growth and increase leaf production. For optimal care, mulching around the base of the tree helps retain moisture and suppress weeds, while regular fertilization during the growing season ensures robust development. Harvesting should occur in late spring to early autumn when leaves are fully mature, using clean shears to cut leaves or branches for maximum yield and quality.

To preserve potency, harvested leaves should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area or in a dehydrator, then stored in airtight containers away from light and moisture, or refrigerated for extended shelf life.
