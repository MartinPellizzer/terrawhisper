What to know about Morus alba (mulberry) before using it medicinally

Morus alba, commonly known as mulberry, is a herb that has been revered for its numerous health properties, including its ability to reduce inflammation, improve cardiovascular health, and support the immune system.

As a versatile horticultural plant, Morus alba is widely cultivated for its fruit, leaves, and bark, which are all used in various herbal remedies and traditional medicine practices. From a botanical perspective, Morus alba is a deciduous tree that belongs to the Moraceae family, characterized by its distinctive white bark and broad, ovate leaves.

Throughout history, Morus alba has been mentioned in ancient texts, including those from China, where it was used to treat various ailments and was also valued for its nutritional and medicinal properties.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Morus alba.

What are the medicinal properties of Morus alba?

Morus alba helps with various health issues, including diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and inflammation, due to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. It also exhibits antimicrobial, antiviral, and antifungal activities. The plant has been used in traditional medicine for centuries.

The medicinal properties of Morus alba are attributed to its active constituents, including flavonoids (quercetin, kaempferol), phenolic acids (gallic acid), and anthocyanins (cyanidin, delphinidin). These compounds contribute to the plant's antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities.

The parts of Morus alba most used for medicinal purposes are the bark, leaves, and fruit. The bark contains a higher concentration of flavonoids and phenolic acids, making it a valuable source of antioxidants. The leaves and fruit also exhibit significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities.

Improper use of Morus alba may lead to gastrointestinal side effects, including diarrhea and stomach upset, due to the plant's high anthocyanin content. Excessive consumption of the plant may also interact with medications, such as anticoagulants and diabetes medications.

When using Morus alba medicinally, precautions include consuming the plant in moderation and under medical supervision. The plant should not be used by individuals with bleeding disorders or those taking anticoagulant medications. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should also consult a healthcare professional before using Morus alba.

What are the horticulural aspects of Morus alba?

Morus alba grow best in USDA zones 5-9, requiring full sun to partial shade and well-draining soil with a pH between 6.0-8.0. They thrive in temperatures between 35°F and 95°F, but can tolerate short periods of frost.

Proper planting involves selecting a location with adequate air circulation and mulching to retain moisture. Plant mulberry trees in early spring or fall, spacing them 15-20 feet apart to allow for mature growth. Dig a hole twice as wide and as deep as the root ball, mixing in compost and organic matter.

Harvest mulberry fruit in mid-to-late summer, when berries are fully ripe and dark in color. Collect fruit gently to avoid bruising, and use for eating fresh or in recipes. Leave some fruit on the tree for birds and other wildlife to enjoy.

Common pests affecting mulberry trees include aphids, spider mites, and caterpillars, while fungal diseases like anthracnose, root rot, and powdery mildew also occur. Regularly inspect plants for signs of infestation or disease, and implement integrated pest management strategies to minimize damage.

What are the botanical characteristics of Morus alba?

Morus alba, also known as white mulberry, is a deciduous tree species that exhibits several distinctive botanical characteristics.

Its bark is typically grayish-brown in color and is smooth on younger stems, becoming darker and more rugged with age. The leaves of M. alba are large, ranging from 10-20 cm in length, with a pointed tip and serrated edges.

They are alternate, simple, and have a soft pubescence (hairiness) on the underside, which aids in photosynthesis by trapping sunlight. The tree's inflorescences are cymose, meaning that they grow in a branched pattern, with male flowers (called catkins) appearing first and then giving way to female flowers. Each flower is small, typically less than 1 cm in diameter, and has five or six petals that are white or creamy in color.

The fruit of M. alba is an aggregate of drupelets, with each drupelet containing a single seed surrounded by juicy pulp.

What is the taxonomy of Morus alba?

Morus alba belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Malpighiales, family Moraceae, and genus Morus.

Specifically, it is classified as Morus alba L., where "L." denotes Linnaeus, the botanist who first described the species in 1753.

Morus alba has variants, such as M. alba var.

pyramidalis, which has a more upright growth habit and produces clusters of fruit that resemble a pyramid. Other variants include M. alba var. sericea, with silvery-gray stems and branches, and M. alba var. sinensis, found in China and known for its small, ovate leaves.

These variants demonstrate the diversity within the species and have been used in breeding programs to develop new cultivars with desirable traits.

What is the geographic distribution of Morus alba?

Morus alba, also known as white mulberry, is native to Asia and has been widely naturalized in other parts of the world.

Its geographic distribution spans across China, Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia. It was introduced to Europe through trade with Asia and has since become naturalized in many countries, including Mediterranean regions, the Middle East, and parts of Africa.

In North America, it is commonly found in the southeastern United States, particularly in urban areas where it can grow as an invasive species.

What is the life cycle of Morus alba?

Morus alba, also known as the White Mulberry tree, has a life cycle that begins with seed germination.

The seeds typically take 1-3 years to sprout and grow into a small sapling. After about 5-10 years, the sapling reaches maturity and starts producing leaves and flowers.

The flowers are pollinated by insects and develop into fruit, which ripens in late summer to early fall. The fruit is an aggregate of multiple drupes that contain seeds. The tree can live for up to 100 years, although the average lifespan is around 50-70 years.

With proper care and conditions, Morus alba trees can continue to produce fruit and thrive for many decades.

What are the historical uses of Morus alba?

Morus alba, also known as white mulberry, has a rich history dating back thousands of years.

Its silkworm cocoons were highly prized for silk production in ancient China, with records of sericulture (silk farming) dating to the Shang Dynasty (16th-11th centuries BCE). The plant was later introduced to Europe and Asia by the Silk Road traders, where it was used for food, medicine, and as a dye plant.

In traditional Chinese medicine, Morus alba is still used today to treat various ailments.

What are the mythological referencec of Morus alba?

Morus alba, also known as white mulberry, has rich mythological references.

In Chinese mythology, it is associated with longevity and immortality, said to have been consumed by the Eight Immortals to attain eternal life. In Japanese Buddhism, the tree is considered sacred and its wood is used in temples and shrines.

Additionally, Morus alba is mentioned in ancient Indian texts, such as the Mahabharata, where it is associated with the god Indra and symbolizes good luck and prosperity.

Morus alba, also known as white mulberry, has a rich history of symbolic meanings across various cultures.

What are the historical texts of Morus alba?

Morus alba, also known as white mulberry, has a rich history dating back thousands of years.

Historical texts describe its cultivation and uses in ancient China, where it was considered a symbol of longevity and immortality.

The classic Chinese text "Shennong Ben Cao Jing" (Divine Farmer's Herb-Root Classic) mentions mulberry leaves for silk production, while the "Ben Cao Gang Mu" (Compendium of Materia Medica) by Li Shizhen records its medicinal properties.

What are the historical artifacts of Morus alba?

Morus alba, also known as white mulberry, has been a significant plant in human history for thousands of years.

Historical artifacts related to this tree include ancient Chinese silk production tools, such as wooden frames and combs, which were used to rear silkworms that fed on its leaves. Additionally, Mulberry wood was often used in the construction of furniture, utensils, and other everyday items during ancient times.

These artifacts provide valuable insight into the cultural and economic significance of Morus alba in various civilizations.
