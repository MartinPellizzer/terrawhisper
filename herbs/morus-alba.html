Morus alba

What's the taxonomical classification of Morus alba?

Morus alba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the evolutionary hierarchy, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its specific lineage, the plant is a member of the order Rosales and the family Moraceae. Finally, it is identified by its genus, Morus, and its specific species, alba.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Morus alba contains a diverse array of bioactive compounds including flavonoids, alkaloids, sterols, and phenolic acids that provide various therapeutic benefits. The presence of mulberry-derived flavonoids like rutin and quercetin offers significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the plant is well known for containing 1-deoxynojirimycin, an alkaloid that helps regulate blood glucose levels by inhibiting alpha-glucosidase enzymes. These chemical constituents work together to support cardiovascular health and metabolic stability. Research also indicates that certain extracts possess antimicrobial and hypoglycemic activities useful in traditional and modern medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Morus alba?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and various inflammatory conditions. The leaves contain bioactive compounds like 1-deoxynojirimycin which help regulate blood sugar levels by inhibiting glucose absorption. Its antioxidant properties are often leveraged to combat oxidative stress and reduce systemic inflammation. Additionally, extracts are frequently studied for their potential to lower cholesterol and manage high blood pressure. Traditional medicine also utilizes the plant to address respiratory issues and digestive ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Morus alba?

Morus alba is prepared as decoctions from the dried root bark, infusions from the leaves, powders from the dried fruit, and tinctures using the various plant parts in alcohol. The root bark is most commonly boiled into a strong decoction to extract its therapeutic constituents for medicinal use. Leaf infusions are frequently used in teas to support metabolic health and blood sugar management. Dried fruit can be ground into a fine powder or consumed whole as a nutritional supplement. Additionally, concentrated tinctures allow for a more potent and shelf-stable way to administer the plant's properties.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Morus alba can cause allergic reactions such as skin rashes, itching, or swelling when direct contact occurs with the plant or its sap. Some individuals may experience gastrointestinal distress, including nausea or diarrhea, if they consume large quantities of the fruit or leaves. The plant's ability to lower blood sugar levels may lead to hypoglycemia in people already taking diabetes medications. It is also possible for the plant to interact with blood-thinning drugs by affecting how the body processes clotting factors. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should exercise caution due to a lack of extensive clinical studies on the plant's safety during these stages.
