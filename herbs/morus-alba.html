White Mulberry (Morus alba): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is a deciduous tree native to Asia, known for its heart-shaped leaves and small, white to yellowish fruits, and it has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to support blood sugar regulation and improve skin health.

What is White Mulberry (Morus Alba)?

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) is a deciduous tree native to Asia, widely cultivated for its edible fruit and its use in traditional herbal medicine.

Botanical Identity and Classification

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is a deciduous tree native to China, belonging to the Moraceae family, and is characterized by its heart-shaped leaves, white or pale pink flowers, and edible berries.

Common Names and Synonyms

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is commonly known by various names such as Chinese White Mulberry, Indian Mulberry, and Persian Mulberry, with regional variations including Morus alba, Morus indica, and Morus ficifolia.

Plant Description and Morphology

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) is a deciduous tree characterized by its smooth, gray-brown bark, heart-shaped leaves with serrated edges, and clusters of small, greenish-white flowers that appear in early spring, followed by small, round, black fruits known as mulberries.

Native Habitat and Distribution

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is native to China, where it has been cultivated for thousands of years, and is now widely distributed across temperate regions of Asia, Europe, and North America.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, bark, roots, and fruits of the White Mulberry (Morus alba) are used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly utilized part for traditional herbal remedies.

Phytochemical Composition

The phytochemical composition of White Mulberry (Morus alba) includes a variety of bioactive compounds such as alkaloids, flavonoids, terpenoids, and polyphenols, with notable constituents including morusin, 1,3-dihydroxy-2-(4-hydroxyphenyl)propan-1-one, and various sugars and polysaccharides.

Pharmacological Properties

White Mulberry (Morus alba) exhibits various pharmacological properties including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, hypoglycemic, and immunomodulatory effects, primarily attributed to the presence of compounds such as morusin, 1,4-dihydroxy-2-naphthoic acid, and polysaccharides.

Mechanisms of Action

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) exerts its effects through various mechanisms, including the presence of bioactive compounds such as alkaloids, flavonoids, and polysaccharides, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and immunomodulatory properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

White Mulberry is used to treat diabetes, high cholesterol, hypertension, skin disorders, inflammation, and as a general tonic for improving health and vitality.

Preparation Methods and Forms

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is commonly prepared as a decoction, powder, or tincture, with the leaves and bark being the primary parts used, typically harvested in early summer for optimal medicinal potency.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of White Mulberry (Morus alba) varies depending on the form of the preparation, with typical oral dosages ranging from 500 to 1,500 mg of standardized extract per day, divided into two to three doses, while dried leaves or bark may be used in amounts of 3 to 10 grams per day, though specific guidance should be sought from a qualified healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While white mulberry (Morus alba) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, excessive consumption may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, including nausea, diarrhea, and stomach cramps, and in some cases, allergic reactions such as skin rashes or itching.

Drug Interactions

White Mulberry (Morus alba) may interact with medications that affect blood sugar, blood pressure, or cholesterol levels, and may also interfere with the metabolism of certain drugs due to its effects on liver enzymes.

Toxicity and Precautions

While White Mulberry (Morus alba) is generally considered safe when used in typical herbal doses, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals, and there is limited research on its long-term safety, particularly during pregnancy and lactation.

Cultivation and Harvesting

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, requiring regular watering and pruning to promote healthy growth and fruit production.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of White Mulberry (Morus alba) typically involves harvesting the leaves, stems, and fruits, followed by drying, grinding, or extracting to create various formulations used in herbal medicine.

Storage and Shelf Life

White Mulberry (Morus alba) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence have increasingly supported the use of White Mulberry (Morus alba) in traditional herbal medicine for its potential health benefits, including anti-diabetic, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties.

Traditional and Historical Uses

White Mulberry (Morus alba) has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for centuries to support liver health, promote longevity, and treat ailments such as diabetes and skin conditions.

Regulatory Status

The regulatory status of White Mulberry (Morus alba) varies by country and region, with some regions recognizing it as a traditional herbal medicine and others requiring specific licensing or restrictions for its use in dietary supplements or pharmaceutical products.
