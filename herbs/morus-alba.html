White Mulberry (Morus alba): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) is a small, deciduous tree native to Asia, widely cultivated for its edible fruit and historically valued in traditional medicine for its diverse therapeutic properties.

What is White Mulberry (Morus Alba)?

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) is a deciduous tree native to Asia, widely cultivated for its edible fruit and its use in traditional herbal medicine for its purported health benefits.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of White Mulberry is Morus alba. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Urticales, family Moraceae, genus Morus, and species Morus alba. Common names for this plant include White Mulberry in North America and Chinese Mulberry in East Asia. It is also known as Mulberry tree in Europe and the Middle East. Synonyms for this plant include Morus alba subsp. alba, Morus alba var. alba, and regional names such as Pāṉdān in China and Mālān in India.

Plant Description and Morphology

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) is a deciduous tree or shrub that typically grows to a height of 5-15 meters, featuring smooth, gray-brown bark, opposite leaves with serrated edges, and clusters of small, greenish-white flowers that develop into fleshy, amber-colored berries.

Native Habitat and Distribution

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is native to Asia, particularly China, and has been widely cultivated across temperate regions of the world including Europe, North America, and parts of Africa for its fruit and medicinal properties.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, bark, roots, and fruits of the White Mulberry (Morus alba) are used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly utilized part in traditional herbal remedies.

Phytochemical Composition

The phytochemical composition of White Mulberry (Morus alba) includes a variety of bioactive compounds such as polysaccharides, flavonoids, alkaloids, triterpenoids, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic potential.

Pharmacological Properties

White Mulberry (Morus alba) exhibits pharmacological properties including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and hypoglycemic effects, primarily attributed to the presence of compounds such as morusin, 1,4-benzenediol, and various polysaccharides.

Mechanisms of Action

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its bioactive compounds, including alkaloids, flavonoids, and polysaccharides, which modulate immune function, reduce oxidative stress, and exhibit anti-inflammatory and antidiabetic properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support blood sugar regulation, promote skin health, and enhance overall vitality through its rich content of antioxidants, polysaccharides, and other bioactive compounds.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The White Mulberry (Morus alba) is commonly prepared as a decoction, tincture, powder, or dried fruit, with each method influencing its medicinal properties and bioavailability.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of White Mulberry (Morus alba) for herbal use ranges from 3 to 10 grams per day, usually prepared as a decoction or tincture, with the exact amount depending on the specific preparation and individual health conditions.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, White Mulberry (Morus alba) is generally considered safe, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

Drug Interactions

White Mulberry (Morus alba) may interact with certain medications, including diabetes drugs, anticoagulants, and hypolipidemic agents, due to its potential effects on blood sugar, blood clotting, and lipid metabolism.

Toxicity and Precautions

While White Mulberry (Morus alba) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy, lactation, and for children has not been fully established.

Cultivation and Harvesting

White Mulberry (Morus alba) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sunlight, requiring regular watering and pruning to promote healthy growth and optimal fruit production.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of White Mulberry (Morus alba) involve harvesting the leaves, which are then dried and either used fresh or processed into extracts through methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent-based extraction to isolate bioactive compounds like morusin and 1-deoxynojirimycin.

Storage and Shelf Life

White Mulberry (Morus alba) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence have increasingly supported the use of White Mulberry (Morus alba) in traditional medicine, particularly for its potential roles in managing diabetes, improving skin health, and supporting cardiovascular function through compounds like moruside and polysaccharides.

Traditional and Historical Uses

White Mulberry (Morus alba) has been traditionally used in Chinese and Ayurvedic medicine for centuries to support digestive health, enhance vitality, and treat conditions such as diabetes and skin disorders.

Regulatory Status

The regulatory status of White Mulberry (Morus alba) varies by region, with some countries classifying it as a food supplement, while others regulate it as a medicinal herb, requiring specific licensing for commercial use.
