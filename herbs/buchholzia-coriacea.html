Buchholzia coriacea

What's the taxonomical classification of Buchholzia coriacea?

Buchholzia coriacea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Brassicales and is a member of the family Capparaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Buchholzia and the species coriacea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Buchholzia coriacea has a pinnate leaf structure consisting of long, leathery leaflets arranged along a central rachis that can reach heights of several meters. The individual leaflets are linear to lanceolate in shape and possess a distinctively thick, waxy texture. These leaves emerge from a large, fleshy rhizome that allows the plant to form dense colonies in swampy environments. The plant also produces large, compound inflorescences that bear numerous small, white flowers. These flowering structures are supported by sturdy stalks that rise significantly above the surrounding foliage.

What are the pharmacological activities of Buchholzia coriacea?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including immunoModulatory and immunostimulatory properties that affect the immune system's response to pathogens. Research indicates that the extracts can both enhance and regulate immune cell functions to maintain biological balance. These activities are primarily attributed to the presence of specific bioactive secondary metabolites found within its tissues. By stimulating the production and activity of white blood cells, the plant helps bolster the body's natural defense mechanisms. Such diverse effects make it a subject of interest for developing natural therapeutic agents.

According to a study published by "Pharmaceuctical biology", the seeds of Buchholzia coriacea are used in Eastern Nigeria to treat malaria, sleeping sickness, and feverish conditions. In an experiment using 75 mice, the seed methanol extract was tested at doses of 250, 500, and 1000 mg/kg to assess its immunostimulatory activity. The extract inhibited delayed hypersensitivity reaction by 20.9% at 250 mg/kg and 20.8% at 500 mg/kg, while at 1000 mg/kg, the paw size increased by -101.9% compared to the control. Furthermore, the extract elevated the antibody titre from 1.60 ± 0.40 in the control group to 8.00 ± 3.58 for the 500 mg/kg group. No clinical signs or death occurred within 24 h post treatment during acute toxicity tests at all tested doses.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and allergic contact dermatitis upon contact with its sap or crushed leaves. The caustic substances within the plant act as a potent irritant to human tissue, often resulting in redness, swelling, and blistering. If the sap accidentally enters the eyes, it may lead to intense pain and potential corneal damage. Prolonged exposure to the plant's compounds can sensitize the skin, making future contact even more reactive. Because the specific toxicological profile is not extensively documented in medical literature, caution should always be exercised when handling this species.
