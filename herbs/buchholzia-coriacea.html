Buchholzia coriacea

What's the taxonomical classification of Buchholzia coriacea?

Buchholzia coriacea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Brassicales and is a member of the family Capparaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Buchholzia and the species coriacea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Buchholzia coriacea has a pinnate leaf structure consisting of long, leathery leaflets arranged along a central rachis that can reach heights of several meters.

The individual leaflets are linear to lanceolate in shape and possess a distinctively thick, waxy texture. These leaves emerge from a large, fleshy rhizome that allows the plant to form dense colonies in swampy environments. The plant also produces large, compound inflorescences that bear numerous small, white flowers.

These flowering structures are supported by sturdy stalks that rise significantly above the surrounding foliage.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Buchholzia coriacea targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, lipoxygenase proteins, and various proinflammatory cytokines to mediate its pharmacological activities. The plant contains bioactive secondary metabolites that inhibit the synthesis of prostaglandins by disrupting the arachidonic acid cascade. These interactions help suppress the inflammatory response by reducing the expression of tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6. Furthermore, its chemical constituents may modulate nitric oxide synthase activity to exert antioxidant effects. This multi-target approach contributes to the species' traditional use in treating inflammation and pain.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Buchholzia coriacea can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled improperly. The plant contains chemical compounds that may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals upon skin exposure. When consumed, the organic constituents might lead to nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramping. There is also limited scientific documentation regarding long-term systemic toxicity in humans. Therefore, caution should be exercised when working with or consuming any part of this species.
