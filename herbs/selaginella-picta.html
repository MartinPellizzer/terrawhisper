Selaginella picta

What's the taxonomical classification of Selaginella picta?

Selaginella picta belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It is further categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Lycopodiidae. Within its lineage, it falls under the order Selaginellales and the family Selaginellaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Selaginella and the species epithet picta.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Selaginella picta targets cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), lipoxygenase (LOX), and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. The plant contains bioactive flavonoids and polyphenols that act as potent inhibitors of these specific enzymatic pathways. By suppressing the expression of nitric oxide synthase and interleukin-6, its constituents effectively reduce systemic inflammation. These phytochemical interactions also modulate oxidative stress by targeting superoxide dismutase and catalase activities within cells. Such molecular engagements contribute to the plant's documented medicinal potential in treating inflammatory and degenerative conditions.

    What are the herbal preparations of Selaginella picta?

    Selaginella picta is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste derived from its dried aerial parts. The plant material is often simmered in water to extract its active phytochemicals for internal consumption. In some traditional practices, the crushed leaves are applied directly to the skin in a paste form to treat localized ailments. These preparations are valued for their specific bioactive compounds that are released through heat or mechanical breakdown. The choice of method depends heavily on whether the intended use is systemic or external.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Selaginella picta can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or localized rashes if the plant's fluids come into direct contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals may experience itching or redness after touching the fronds. If the plant is ingested, it could potentially cause gastrointestinal distress or more severe toxic reactions depending on the concentration of alkaloids present. While specific human toxicity studies are limited, caution should be taken when handling the foliage. Always wash your hands thoroughly after any physical interaction with the plant to mitigate these risks.
