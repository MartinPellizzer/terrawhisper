Phoenix canariensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Phoenix canariensis?

Phoenix canariensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage that leads to the order Arecales. Within this order, the plant is situated in the family Arecaceae and is further categorized under the genus Phoenix, specifically identifying as the species canariensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Phoenix canariensis has a single, thick, woody trunk that can reach heights of up to 10 meters and is characterized by a dense pattern of diamond-shaped leaf scars. The crown consists of a massive rosette of pinnate leaves that extend up to 3 meters in length with stiff, dark green leaflets. These leaflets are arranged in multiple planes along the rachis, giving the fronds a lush, three-dimensional appearance. At the base of the leaf stalks, small spines are often present to protect the developing growth. The plant also produces large, heavy clusters of bright orange or yellow fruits that hang beneath the foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Canary Islands, an archipelago located in the Atlantic Ocean off the coast of northwest Africa. Within this island chain, it is primarily found in the subtropical zones of islands such as Tenerife, Gran Canaria, and La Palma. It thrives in coastal areas and ravines where moisture is relatively consistent. While its natural range is strictly limited to these volcanic islands, it has been widely introduced to other warm climates globally. In regions like Southern California and parts of Australia, it is frequently cultivated as an ornamental landscape species.

According to a study published by "Nutricion hospitalaria", the sap of the Phoenix canariensis palm tree is used for medicinal uses in homeopathic medicine. An analysis of 35 syrup samples from five different producing regions in La Gomera island was conducted to determine its composition. The major carbohydrates found in the syrup are sucrose at 37.8%, glucose at 9.50%, and fructose at 4.80%.
