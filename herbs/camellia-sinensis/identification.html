Botanical Identification of Camellia (Camellia sinensis)

To identify Camellia (Camellia sinensis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Camellia sinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Theales, family Theaceae, genus Camellia, and species Camellia sinensis. This classification system organizes the plant within the broader taxonomy of flowering plants, highlighting its relationship to other related species.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Camellia sinensis is the scientific name for the plant commonly known as the tea plant. It was first described by the botanist Carl Peter Thunberg in 1788. This name is widely accepted and used in botanical literature, though it has several synonyms and regional variations in naming.

Morphological Characteristics

Camellia sinensis is an evergreen shrub or small tree with a dense, branched root system. Its stems are sturdy and woody, often with a smooth, grayish-brown bark. Leaves are alternate, elliptic to ovate, with a glossy dark green upper surface and a lighter underside. Inflorescences are axillary, bearing clusters of small, white or pink flowers. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release two elongated, flat seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Camellia sinensis can be distinguished by its elliptical, glossy dark green leaves with serrated margins and a woody, branched stem. It produces white, campanulate flowers and ellipsoid, hairy berries. These features differentiate it from similar species like Camellia oleifera and Camellia taliensis.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Camellia sinensis is native to eastern Asia, particularly China, India, and Southeast Asia. It thrives in tropical and subtropical climates with high humidity and moderate temperatures. It prefers well-drained, acidic soils and is typically found at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Camellia sinensis blooms between late winter and early spring, producing white or pink flowers. Seed formation occurs in the summer months following pollination. Harvesting leaves is typically done in spring and summer, depending on climate, and is critical for identifying the plant in its vegetative state.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Camellia sinensis belongs to the genus Camellia, which is part of the family Theaceae. Its closest relatives include species like Camellia sasanqua and Camellia oleifera. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, such as leaf structure and flower morphology, which aid in their classification within the genus.
