Bassia dasyphylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Bassia dasyphylla?

Bassia dasyphylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic classification continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Caryophyllales. Further identification places the plant in the family Amaranthaceae, specifically within the genus Bassia, and it is ultimately identified by its unique species name, dasyphylla.

    What are the herbal preparations of Bassia dasyphylla?

    Bassia dasyphylla is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion of its dried aerial parts to extract medicinal compounds. The plant material is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for internal consumption. In some traditional practices, the crushed roots are utilized to make topical pastes or poultices applied directly to the skin. These different methods allow for the targeted release of specific phytochemicals depending on the intended therapeutic use. Such preparations are common in regional folk medicine for managing various ailments.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Bassia dasyphylla can cause gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and potential toxicity if ingested in large quantities by humans or livestock. While it is a common component of many desert diets, the plant contains various secondary metabolites that may trigger adverse reactions. Consuming excessive amounts can lead to stomach upset or digestive irregularities. Topical contact with the plant's parts may also result in localized redness or itching for sensitive individuals. Monitoring the amount consumed is essential to avoid these physiological complications.
