Bassia dasyphylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Bassia dasyphylla?

Bassia dasyphylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic classification continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Caryophyllales. Further identification places the plant in the family Amaranthaceae, specifically within the genus Bassia, and it is ultimately identified by its unique species name, dasyphylla.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bassia dasyphylla has erect, branched stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 100 centimeters and are covered in dense, fine hairs. The leaves are small, linear to lanceolate in shape, and possess a grayish-green hue due to a thick coating of trichomes. These hairs serve as a protective layer against moisture loss in arid environments. The inflorescence consists of dense, many-flowered spikes that develop into fleshy, berry-like fruits. Each fruit is encased in a thick, succulent perianth that provides a specialized structure for seed protection.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds O-methylherbarin, Phaeosphspirone, and herbarin. These specific chemical constituents are responsible for much of the plant's biological activity. Researchers study herbarin to understand its unique molecular structure and potential health benefits. The presence of O-methylherbarin suggests a complex metabolic pathway within the plant tissue. Additionally, Phaeosphspirone contributes to the overall medicinal profile of the species.

As discussed in a study, the desert plant Bassia dasyphylla hosts the endophytic fungus Phaeosphaeriacea sp. which produces specific medicinal compounds. These compounds include the undescribed polyketide phaeosphspirone, which possesses a unique 6/5/5/6-fused tetracyclic system. The study also identified two known analogues, herbarin and O-methylherbarin, purified from the fungus. Both herbarin and O-methylherbarin exhibited moderate cytotoxicity against 3 cancer cell lines. Phaeosphspirone exists as a pair of unique polyketide enantiomers identified as 1/1'.
