Dog Rose (Rosa canina): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Dog Rose, or Rosa canina, is a shrub native to Europe and parts of Asia, widely used in herbal medicine for its nourishing and protective properties.

What is Dog Rose (Rosa Canina)?

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) is a shrub native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa, known for its red-orange flowers and hips that are used in traditional herbal medicine for their high content of vitamin C, antioxidants, and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Dog rose is Rosa canina. This plant belongs to the family Rosaceae, genus Rosa, and species Rosa canina, within the order Rosales, class Eudicots, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. Common names for this plant include "Dog Rose" in Europe and "Wild Rose" in North America. It is also known as "Rose Dog" in some regional contexts. Synonyms for Dog rose include Rosa sylvestris, Rosa alpina, and Rosa eburnea, which are botanical and regional variations.

Plant Description and Morphology

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) is a thorny shrub typically growing to a height of 1-3 meters, with arching stems covered in reddish-brown bark and dense, sharp prickles, bearing pinnate leaves with 5-7 leaflets and pink to pale yellow flowers that develop into red, hairy fruits called hips.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northwestern Africa, typically growing in open woodlands, heathlands, and along coastal areas.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The petals, leaves, and hips (the fruit) of the Dog Rose are the primary plant parts used medicinally in herbal medicine.

Phytochemical Composition

The phytochemical composition of Dog Rose (Rosa canina) includes a variety of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, proanthocyanidins, tannins, ellagitannins, vitamins (including vitamin C), minerals, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) possesses anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine for treating respiratory and digestive ailments.

Mechanisms of Action

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and immune-modulating properties, which are attributed to its high content of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, proanthocyanidins, and vitamin C.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to support respiratory health, alleviate digestive discomfort, and address skin conditions due to its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and astringent properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Dog Rose (Rosa canina) in herbal medicine typically involve using the dried flowers, which are harvested in late summer and early autumn, and can be made into tinctures, teas, syrups, or capsules for various therapeutic applications.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Dog Rose (Rosa canina) for herbal use ranges from 2 to 4 grams of dried herb per day, often divided into two or three doses, with specific adjustments based on the intended therapeutic purpose and individual patient needs.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in appropriate doses, Dog Rose (Rosa canina) is generally considered safe for most people, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with allergies to plants in the Rosaceae family.

Drug Interactions

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) may interact with medications that affect blood clotting, such as anticoagulants and antiplatelet drugs, due to its potential antithrombotic properties, and may also interact with diabetes medications as it has mild hypoglycemic effects.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Dog Rose (Rosa canina) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and pregnant or breastfeeding women should consult a healthcare provider before use.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, harvested primarily from the hips, which are collected in late summer to early autumn when they are fully mature and dry.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Dog Rose (Rosa canina) typically involve harvesting the flowers and hips, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain the active compounds for use in herbal preparations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain its potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Rosa canina has shown its potential in supporting immune function, reducing inflammation, and promoting cardiovascular health, with studies indicating its rich content of antioxidants, flavonoids, and vitamin C contributes to these therapeutic effects.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Dog Rose (Rosa canina) has been historically used in herbal medicine for its astringent, anti-inflammatory, and tonic properties, particularly to treat digestive issues, wounds, and respiratory conditions.

Regulatory Status

The regulatory status of Dog Rose (Rosa canina) as an herbal medicine varies by country, with some regions recognizing it as a traditional herbal medicine under specific legislation, while others require additional clinical evidence for approval as a pharmaceutical product.
