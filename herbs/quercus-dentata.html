Quercus dentata

What's the taxonomical classification of Quercus dentata?

Quercus dentata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Fagales and is a member of the family Fagaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Quercus, specifically identified by the species name dentata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Quercus dentata has an upright, spreading crown with leaves that are oblong-obovate, leathery, and characterized by prominent, coarse, serrated teeth along the margins. The leaf texture is distinctly scabrous, meaning it feels rough to the touch, and the undersides often possess a slightly pubescent or hairy quality. The bark of mature trees is thick, deeply furrowed, and dark gray in color. During the growing season, the tree produces small, inconspicuous catkins that eventually develop into acorns with shallow, rounded caps. These acorns are typically ovoid and possess a relatively smooth surface compared to other oak species.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as the sawtooth oak, thrives in well-drained, acidic soils within USDA hardiness zones 5 through 9.

It requires full sun to partial shade to facilitate optimal growth and strong structural development. When cultivating this species, ensure the soil remains moist but never waterlogged to prevent root rot. Young saplings benefit significantly from regular watering and organic mulch to maintain consistent soil moisture levels.

As the tree matures, it becomes increasingly drought-tolerant and requires minimal supplemental care.
