What is Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera Longifolia)?

The Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia) is a carnivorous plant native to southern Africa, known for its sticky, glandular leaves that trap and digest insects, and historically used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory and wound-healing properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Long-leaved Sundew is Drosera longifolia. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division unknown, class unknown, order unknown, family Sarraceniaceae, genus Drosera, and species Drosera longifolia. It is commonly known as Long-leaved Sundew in North America and as Sundew in parts of Europe. Synonyms for this plant include Drosera longifolia subsp. longifolia and Drosera longifolia var. longifolia.

Plant Description and Morphology

The Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia) is a small, herbaceous plant characterized by its prostrate or upright growth habit, with slender, often reddish stems that bear numerous glandular hairs, and its leaves arranged in a rosette or whorled pattern, each leaf tipped with sticky, hair-like structures that secrete a mucilaginous substance to trap insects.

Native Habitat and Distribution

The Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia) is native to the temperate regions of southern Africa, particularly South Africa, where it thrives in moist, nutrient-poor soils of fynbos vegetation and coastal areas.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves of the Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia) are the primary plant parts used medicinally, containing the sticky, glandular secretions that are harvested and utilized in traditional herbal remedies.

Long-Leaved Sundew (Drosera longifolia) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat respiratory conditions such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma due to its expectorant and anti-inflammatory properties.
