Botanical Identification of Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac)

To identify Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Oleales, family Oleaceae, genus Jasminum, and species Jasminum sambac. These classifications reflect its botanical relationships and place within the plant kingdom.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Jasminum sambac is the scientific name for Common Jasmine. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. The plant has several synonyms, including Jasminum officinale and Jasminum grandiflorum. It is also known by common names such as Arabian jasmine and night jasmine, with regional variations in its naming across different cultures.

Morphological Characteristics

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a shrub with a compact growth habit. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, supporting its upright stem. Leaves are simple, opposite, and ovate with a fragrant aroma. The inflorescence consists of terminal clusters of fragrant white flowers. Each flower has five petals and a tubular corolla. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous small, flat seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) can be distinguished by its fragrant, white, star-shaped flowers with five petals, opposite leaves, and a woody vine structure. It differs from similar species like Jasminum multiflorum by having smaller, more numerous flowers and a distinct, sweet aroma. The leaves are also more oval and have a slightly rough texture compared to other jasmine varieties.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is native to South and Southeast Asia, including regions like India, Sri Lanka, and Indonesia. It thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained, loamy soil. It typically grows at low altitudes, often near coastal areas or in tropical and subtropical regions.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn, around August to September. For identification purposes, the most notable harvest timing is during the blooming period when the flowers are fully open and aromatic.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Jasminum sambac belongs to the genus Jasminum within the Oleaceae family. Its closest relatives include other Jasminum species such as J. officinale and J. nudiflorum. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, reflecting their close phylogenetic relationships. The genus exhibits distinct family characteristics, such as opposite leaves and fragrant flowers, which aid in identification.
