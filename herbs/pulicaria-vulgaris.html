Pulicaria vulgaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a representative of the family Asteraceae, a diverse group of flowering plants, and is specifically categorized under the genus Pulicaria, with its specific designation being the species vulgaris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pulicaria vulgaris has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 20 to 60 centimeters and features a densely hairy surface. The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem, appearing lanceolate to linear in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. At the apex of the plant, the inflorescence consists of small, discoid flower heads clustered into dense, terminal heads. These flower heads are characterized by their yellowish to brownish-pink tubular florets that lack ray petals. The overall texture of the foliage and stems is often described as somewhat sticky or pubescent due to the presence of fine hairs.

What are the pharmacological activities of Pulicaria vulgaris?

This plant has antibacterial, anticholinesterase, antityrosinase, antiviral, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities. Research indicates that its chemical constituents can inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and viruses. The extracts also demonstrate the ability to regulate enzyme functions, specifically by inhibiting anticholinesterase and antityrosinase activities. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant cytotoxic properties that may be useful in evaluating potential anticancer applications. These diverse biological effects highlight the medicinal potential of Pulicaria vulgaris in various therapeutic contexts.

As discussed in a study, the essential oils extracted from Pulicaria vulgaris Gaertn. via the hydrodistillation method displayed high inhibitory effects against ESBL-producing E. coli strains. These E. coli strains were identified from 103 out of 295 isolates, with 97 or 90.65% showing TEM gene distributions. The antibacterial activity of the Pulicaria vulgaris Gaertn. essential oil was evaluated using the microdilution method to determine the minimum inhibitory concentration. This investigation focused on strains isolated from urine samples of patients with urinary tract infections at Shiraz hospital, Iran. The findings suggest that the essential oil from this plant may propose a good treatment option against resistant infectious bacteria.
