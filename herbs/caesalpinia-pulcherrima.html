Caesalpinia pulcherrima

What's the taxonomical classification of Caesalpinia pulcherrima?

Caesalpinia pulcherimma belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic journey continues through the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae, which characterizes its relationship to other legumes. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Caesalpinia, and its unique species designation, pulcherrima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Caesalpinia pulcherrima has bipinnately compound leaves with numerous small leaflets, bright orange or red flowers, and long, spreading, woody stems. The foliage consists of multiple pairs of leaflets that create a delicate, fern-like appearance. Its showy blossoms feature prominent, protruding stamens that extend significantly beyond the colorful petals. The plant often develops small prickles along its branches to provide structural defense. Each flower typically grows in clusters at the ends of the stems, adding to the overall density of the canopy.

What are the pharmacological activities of Caesalpinia pulcherrima?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its documented anti-cancer, anti-plasmodial, antibacterial, antimicrobial, and antioxidant activities. The presence of bioactive phytochemicals allows it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and microorganisms. Research also indicates that its extracts possess the capacity to combat malaria parasites by exerting anti-plasmodial effects. Furthermore, its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, protecting cells from oxidative damage. These diverse biological functions suggest that the plant could serve as a valuable source for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the secondary metabolites of Caesalpinia pulcherrima were screened to identify potential inhibitors of the β-catenin-Tcf/Lef interaction. The diterpene metabolite 6β-Cinnamoyl-7β-hydroxyvouacapen-5α-ol (6βCHV) was identified as a potent inhibitor of the Wnt/β-catenin pathway. This compound demonstrated anti-proliferative activity against NTERA-2 cells with an IC50 of 8.92 µM and breast cancer stem cells (bCSCs) with an IC50 of 49.18 µM. It also showed activity against gastric adenocarcinoma (IC50 = 1.90 µM), hepatocellular carcinoma (IC50 = 5.96 µM), and ovarian carcinoma (IC50 = 7.66 µM). By upregulating the p53 gene and downregulating Cyclin D1 and CD44, 6βCHV leads to apoptosis in bCSCs.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds bonducelliin, dihydrobonducelliin, isobonducelliin, 2'-methoxybonducelliin, and various flavonoids. These specific chemical constituents are primary markers used to identify the plant's bioactive properties. The presence of these unique bonducelliin derivatives suggests significant therapeutic potential within the species. Additionally, the flavonoids contribute to the overall antioxidant profile of the plant. Researchers study these combined elements to better understand how they interact with biological systems.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical analysis of Caesalpinia pulcherrimma pods revealed the presence of tannins, flavonoids, steroids, and alkaloids. The methanolic extract contained a total phenolic content of 38.04% w/w equivalent to gallic acid. In cytotoxicity testing using the Brine Shrimp Lethality bioassay, LC50 values were 750 microg mL(-1) for petroleum ether, 800 microg mL(-1) for chloroform, and 900 microg mL(-1) for methanol extracts. Regarding DPPH radical scavenging, the IC50 values for petroleum ether, chloroform, and methanol extracts were 124.75, 112.08, and 54.34 microg mL(-1) respectively. Furthermore, nitric oxide radical scavenging showed IC50 values of 93.32 microg mL(-1) for petroleum ether, 65.12 microg mL(-1) for chloroform, and 54.83 microg mL(-1) for the ethyl acetate soluble fraction of methanolic extracts.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe toxicity if ingested improperly because it contains compounds that irritate the digestive system. Consuming parts of the plant may lead to nausea and frequent vomiting. Individuals who swallow the plant often experience bouts of diarrhea as the body attempts to expel the irritant. Intense stomach pain is another common side effect resulting from the ingestion of its toxic elements. These symptoms typically manifest shortly after the plant is consumed.
