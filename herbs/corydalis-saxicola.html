Corydalis saxicola

What's the taxonomical classification of Corydalis saxicola?

Corydalis saxicola belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Papaveraceae. Finally, it is identified under the genus Corydalis, with its specific designation being the species saxicola.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Corydalis saxicola has a rhizomatous root system, pinnately dissected leaves with ovate or lanceolate lobes, and zygomorphic flowers characterized by a long, slender nectar spur. The basal leaves are typically divided into several segments that vary in depth depending on the specific environment. Its flowering stems are erect and bear flowers that can range in color from pale yellow to deep violet. Each flower possesses a distinct spurred structure that serves as a primary diagnostic feature for the species. The plant often exhibits a delicate, herbaceous texture throughout its entire structure.

What are the pharmacological activities of Corydalis saxicola?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, anticancer, antioxidant, and antiviral properties. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals, which provides a potent antioxidant effect for the body. Research indicates that these chemical constituents can inhibit the growth of various pathogens through antibacterial and antiviral mechanisms. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates the ability to reduce systemic inflammation and suppress the development of malignant cells. These diverse therapeutic actions make the species a subject of interest for pharmaceutical development.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in pharmacology", the total alkaloids of Corydalis saxicola Bunting, known as YTH, can inhibit diarrhea and alleviate bleeding. These alkaloids were introduced to DSS-induced colitis mice in 3 graded doses for 7 days to evaluate their anti-colitis effect. The YTH alleviated weight loss, diarrhea, and hematochezia while reducing inflammation and oxidative stress. Metabolomics profiling of the colon, serum, and feces showed that YTH restored disrupted metabolite profiles, specifically highlighting linoleic acid. Additionally, YTH ameliorated gut microbiota imbalance by significantly increasing the abundance of Lactobacillus.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (S)-tetrahydroberberine, corydalisin C, corydalisine D-K, dehyrdoapocavidine, and dehyrdoisoapocavidine. These specific alkaloids are secondary metabolites that contribute to the biological activity of the species. Researchers study these chemical constituents to understand their potential pharmacological applications in treating various conditions. The presence of these complex structures highlights the chemical diversity found within the Corydalis genus. Such detailed profiling of the plant's chemical makeup is essential for drug discovery and development.

According to a study published by "Molecular biology reports", Corydalis saxicola contains a (S)-THB gene, designated as Cs(S)-THBO with Genbank accession No. HQ393909, which is part of the benzylisoquinoline alkaloids pathway. This gene has a full-length cDNA of 1127 bp and an open reading frame of 699 bp that predicts a 232-amino acid polypeptide with a molecular mass of 25.20 kDa. The enzyme encoded by this gene catalyzes the dehydrogenation of four hydrogen atoms of (S)-tetrahydroberberine to produce the medicinal compound berbermin. Real-time quantitative PCR analysis showed that Cs(S)-THBO is constitutively expressed in the roots, stems, leaves, and flowers of the plant, with the highest expression level found in the roots.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating abdominal pain, hepatitis, inflammation, intestinal barrier impairment, and ulcerative colitis. Researchers have studied its bioactive compounds for their potential to soothe gastrointestinal distress. Specifically, the plant demonstrates properties that may help repair the intestinal barrier and reduce chronic inflammation. These therapeutic effects make it a subject of interest for managing the symptoms of ulcerative colitis. Its ability to mitigate liver issues also suggests a role in addressing hepatitis.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in plant science", the endangered medicinal plant Corydalis saxicola is widely used in Traditional Chinese Medicine.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause gastrointestinal distress, dizziness, and potential neurological disturbances due to the presence of isoquinoline alkaloids. These bioactive compounds interact with the central nervous system and can lead to unexpected sedation or altered states of consciousness. If ingested in large quantities, the toxic alkaloids may provoke nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Some individuals might also experience localized skin irritation or allergic reactions upon direct physical contact with the foliage. Careful monitoring is necessary because the specific physiological impacts can vary significantly between different people.
