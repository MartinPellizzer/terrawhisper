Rabdosia japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Rabdosia japonica?

Rabdosia japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Lamiales. Its taxonomic journey continues through the family Lamiaceae, where it is placed within the genus Rabdosia. Ultimately, the specific identity of this plant is defined by its species name, japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rabdosia japonica has an erect, herbaceous habit characterized by square-shaped stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are arranged in opposite pairs along the stem and possess an ovate to lanceolate shape with serrated margins. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying a pale purple or white coloration. These blossoms are often grouped in dense clusters that appear during the peak growing season. The plant also develops a rhizomatous root system that allows it to spread effectively through the soil.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rabdosia japonica?

This plant has antitumor, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and antipyretic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to inhibit the growth of various cancer cell lines by inducing apoptosis. Research also indicates that the plant can effectively reduce inflammation and combat several types of bacterial infections. Additionally, it serves as a natural agent to help lower body temperature during febrile conditions. These diverse properties make it a significant subject for modern medicinal studies.

According to a study published by "Advanced biology", the bioactive diterpenoid Glaucocalyxin A (GLA) from the medicinal plant Rabdosia japonica demonstrates potent antitumor activity. GLA triggers cytotoxicity in renal cell carcinoma (RCC) cells through the overaccumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS). This surge in ROS activates autophagy, which acts as a pro-death effector in this context. Through activity-based protein profiling (ABPPP) and proteomic analysis, peroxiredoxins PRDX1 and PRDX2 were identified as the direct covalent targets of GLA. Ultimately, the findings delineate a ROS-autophagy-apoptosis axis driven by PRDX1/2 targeting.

What side effects this plant can have?

The onset of such symptoms can lead to severe dehydration if the vomiting and diarrhea are not managed properly.
