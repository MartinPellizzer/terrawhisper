Rabdosia japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Rabdosia japonica?

Rabdosia japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Lamiales. Its taxonomic journey continues through the family Lamiaceae, where it is placed within the genus Rabdosia. Ultimately, the specific identity of this plant is defined by its species name, japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rabdosia japonica has an erect, herbaceous habit characterized by square-shaped stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are arranged in opposite pairs along the stem and possess an ovate to lanceolate shape with serrated margins. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying a pale purple or white coloration. These blossoms are often grouped in dense clusters that appear during the peak growing season. The plant also develops a rhizomatous root system that allows it to spread effectively through the soil.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rabdosia japonica?

This plant has antitumoor activities that allow it to inhibit the growth and spread of various cancer cells. Researchers have studied its specific bioactive compounds to understand how they induce apoptosis in malignant tissues. These chemical constituents work by disrupting the cell cycle of tumor cells to prevent further proliferation. Additionally, the plant's potential in oncology is being explored through various in vitro and in vivo experimental models. Such studies aim to clarify the precise mechanisms behind its ability to suppress tumor development.

As discussed in a study, the bioactive diterpenoid Glaucocalyxin A (GLA) is derived from the medicinal plant Rabdosia japonica and demonstrates potent antitumor activity. GLA triggers cytotoxicity in renal cell carcinoma (RCC) cells through the overaccumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS). This ROS surge activates autophagy, which acts as a pro-death effector in the context of GLA-induced cell death. Through proteomic analysis, peroxiredoxins PRDX1 and PRDX2 were identified as the direct covalent targets of GLA. Furthermore, the overexpression of PRDX1/2 was found to mitigate the apoptosis mediated by GLA.

What side effects this plant can have?
