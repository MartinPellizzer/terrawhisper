Scientific and Botanical Profile

Ceylon Trichopus, with botanical name Trichopus zeylanicus, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Araliaceae, commonly known as the ginseng or ivy family.

Native to the tropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, including India, Sri Lanka, Indonesia, and parts of Africa, this plant is widely distributed in humid, shaded environments. Morphologically, it is a perennial herb with a short, stout rhizome and a rosette of large, leathery, heart-shaped leaves that are often covered in a waxy cuticle. The plant produces small, greenish-yellow flowers in umbellate clusters, followed by small, round berries that are edible and have a mild, spinach-like flavor.

Commonly known by various names such as Zeylanic Trichopus, Blue Banyan, Bamboo Fern, and Indian Spinach, it is also referred to as Kerala Spinach, Java Spinach, and Tropical Spinach, reflecting its widespread use in local cuisines and traditional medicine across its native regions.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Trichopus zeylanicus has a variety of forms available, including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules, allowing for versatile use depending on the intended application.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by simmering them for a longer period to extract more active compounds. For topical use, the essential oil or powder can be applied directly to the skin, often diluted with a carrier oil for sensitive areas. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried powder or 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision due to limited safety data.

Usage should be conservative, with a short duration of 1-2 weeks unless advised otherwise by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Trichopus zeylanicus can be used in traditional medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties, but its safety profile requires careful consideration.

While generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea in some individuals. There is limited scientific evidence on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with anticoagulants or anti-diabetic medications due to potential synergistic effects. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient data on fetal or neonatal safety, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, always follow recommended dosages, consult with a qualified healthcare professional, and discontinue use if adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Trichopus zeylanicus grows best in tropical and subtropical climates with well-drained, fertile soil that is rich in organic matter and slightly acidic to neutral in pH.

It thrives in partial to full sun, requiring at least 4-6 hours of direct sunlight daily, and benefits from regular watering during the growing season without becoming waterlogged. To ensure healthy growth, it is important to maintain consistent moisture levels, apply balanced fertilizers during the growing season, and prune any dead or diseased foliage to promote air circulation. Harvesting is most effective during the dry season, when the leaves are mature and the plant's active compounds are most concentrated; the leaves should be cut with clean, sharp shears to avoid damaging the plant.

After harvesting, the leaves should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation to preserve their potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties for extended periods.
