Euphorbia lathyris

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia lathyris?

Euphorbia lathyris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is organized under the order Malpighiales and placed within the family Euphorbiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Euphorbia, and its specific species epithet, lathyris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia lathyris has an erect, herbaceous perennial habit characterized by thick, succulent stems that reach heights of up to two meters and lack true branches. The leaves are arranged in an alternate pattern along the stem and feature a distinctively oblong or lanceolate shape with entire margins. These leaves are typically a bright, yellowish-green color and possess a somewhat fleshy texture. The plant produces small, inconspicuous yellowish flowers held within specialized structures called cyathia at the ends of the stems. A defining morphological feature is the presence of a milky white latex that exudes from the stem and leaves when the plant tissue is damaged.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia lathyris contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its milky latex, which is extracted from the plant's stems and leaves. This caustic sap has historically been utilized as a topical treatment for various skin conditions such as warts and ringworm. When applied carefully, the latex acts as a powerful irritant to help remove abnormal skin growths. Beyond topical applications, the plant's juices were sometimes used in traditional medicine to treat internal ailments, though this requires extreme caution due to its toxicity. Because the sap can cause severe inflammation and irritation to the eyes and skin, it must be handled with great care.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe physiological distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and intense abdominal pain if ingested. Contact with the milky sap often results in painful skin irritation and the formation of blisters. If the sap reaches the eyes, it can cause significant eye irritation and permanent eye damage. Some individuals may also experience dizziness following exposure to the plant. These adverse reactions underscore the need for extreme caution when handling any part of Euphorbia lathyris.
