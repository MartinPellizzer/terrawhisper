Atalantia henryi

What's the taxonomical classification of Atalantia henryi?

Atalantia henryi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized into the order Sapindales. It is a member of the family Rutaceae, falling specifically within the genus Atalantia, and is identified by its unique species designation, henryi.

What are the therapeutic actions of Atalantia henryi?

Atalantia henryi has antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties derived from its diverse secondary metabolites such as alkaloids and flavonoids. What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat stomach aches, diarrhea, dysentery, and various types of fever. The leaves and roots are frequently prepared in medicinal decoctions to soothe gastrointestinal distress. It also possesses properties that help alleviate inflammatory conditions within the body. Some traditional practices utilize the plant to manage symptoms related to respiratory ailments as well. This species remains a significant component in local herbal remedies for addressing diverse physical discomforts.

What are the herbal preparations of Atalantia henryi?

Atalantia henryi is prepared primarily as a decoction, a concentrated liquid extract made by boiling the plant's parts in water to extract its medicinal compounds. This traditional method is often used to harness the bioactive constituents found within the roots and leaves. Beyond decoctions, the plant material may be processed into infusions where hot water is poured over it without prolonged boiling. Some practitioners also utilize the dried components to create powders that can be mixed into other substances. These various forms allow for different absorption rates and concentrations of the plant's active ingredients.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Atalantia henryi can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, or allergic reactions if ingested or applied topically. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many plants in the Rutaceae family contain volatile oils that may act as irritants. Contact with the plant's leaves or fruit could potentially lead to dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of unknown botanical substances often results in nausea, vomiting, or abdominal discomfort. It is essential to exercise caution as the full profile of its potential adverse effects remains largely undocumented in medical literature.
