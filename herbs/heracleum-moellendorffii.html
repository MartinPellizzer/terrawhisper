What's the taxonomical classification of Heracleum moellendorffii?

Heracleum moellendorffii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Apiales and falls under the family Apiaceae. Finally, it is defined by its specific classification within the genus Heracleum, with its unique identity established at the species level as Heracleum moellendorffii.

What are the pharmacological activities of Heracleum moellendorffii?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, immune-enhancing, and immunostiimulatory pharmacological activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Its chemical constituents work to reduce swelling and inhibit the inflammatory response within biological systems. By modulating the body's natural defense mechanisms, the plant serves as an effective immunostimulatory agent. These properties collectively help to strengthen the host's resistance against various pathogens. Such diverse biological actions make it a significant subject for medicinal research.

According to a study published by "BMC complementary and alternative medicine", Heracleum moellendorffii roots (HM-R) exhibit anti-inflammatory activity by dose-dependently blocking LPS-induced NO and PGE2 production. These roots inhibit the LPS-induced overexpression of iNOS, COX-2, IL-1β, and IL-6 in RAW264.7 cells. The pharmacological mechanism involves the inhibition of NF-κB signaling through blocking IκB-α degradation and p65 nuclear accumulation, as well as the inhibition of MAPK signaling by attenuating the phosphorylation of ERK1/2, p38, and JNK. Additionally, HM-R increases the nuclear accumulation of Nrf2 and HO-1 expression through the activation of ROS/Nrf2/HO-1 signaling. HPLC analysis detected falcarinol from HM-R as an anti-inflammatory compound.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe phototoxicity, skin irritation, blistering, nausea, and dizziness upon contact or ingestion. When the sap touches the skin and is subsequently exposed to sunlight, it triggers a chemical reaction known as phytophotodermatitis. This reaction often results in painful redness and the development of large, fluid-filled blisters. If parts of the plant are consumed, individuals may experience internal distress such as nausea and dizziness. These physiological responses highlight the significant toxicity associated with handling this species.
