Polygala caudata

What's the taxonomical classification of Polygala caudata?

Polygala caudata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its evolutionary lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. At the ordinal level, it is placed in Fabales, followed by its membership in the family Polygalaceae. Finally, the plant is identified under the genus Polygala, specifically as the species caudata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Polygala caudata has erect, woody stems that support small, lanceolate leaves arranged in an alternate pattern along the branches. The foliage consists of narrow, green leaves that often exhibit a slightly thickened texture. Its inflorescence features small, zygomorphic flowers characterized by a prominent keel petal and delicate pink or purplish hues. These blossoms are typically borne in terminal racemes that emerge from the upper portions of the stems. The plant's overall structure is compact and shrub-like, adapting it to rocky or sandy environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Polygala caudata?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-neoplastic, anti-tumour, cytotoxic, and neurite growth-stimulating pharmacological activities. Its potent anti-neoplastic and anti-tumour properties allow it to effectively inhibit the progression of various malignant cell lines. The plant demonstrates significant cytotoxic effects by inducing programmed cell death in targeted cancer cells. Beyond its ability to fight malignancy, it also exhibits unique neuroprotective potential through its neurite growth-stimulating capabilities. These diverse biological actions make it a subject of intense interest for both oncology and neurology research.

According to a study published by "Cell biochemistry and function", the medicinal plant Polygala caudata contains an active ingredient called euxanthone which exhibits a selective cytotoxic effect in tumour cells. This ingredient impairs cell viability and induces apoptosis via the intrinsic pathway in a concentration-dependent fashion in both SKOV3 and A2780 epithelial ovarian cancer cell lines. Euxanthone also causes inhibition of glycolysis through the downregulation of HK2, which results from STAT3 inactivation. In vivo experiments involving SKOV3 cells injected into mice models showed that euxanthone can exert anti-cancer activities without general toxicity. These findings demonstrate that euxanthone triggers mitochondrial apoptosis and inhibits glycolysis in epithelial ovarian cancer cells.
