Polygonum amphibium

What's the taxonomical classification of Polygonum amphibium?

Polygonum amphibium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Caryophyllales. Within this order, the plant is a member of the family Polygonaceae and the genus Polygonum, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species amphibium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Polygonum amphibium has erect to ascending stems that reach up to 40 centimeters in height, characterized by cylindrical shapes and reddish-green pigmentation. The leaves are primarily lanceolate or narrowly elliptical, featuring smooth margins and an alternate arrangement along the stem. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary clusters, often appearing in small groups near the leaf nodes. These flowers possess white to pinkish tepals and are typically much smaller than the surrounding foliage. The plant also develops small, ovoid fruits that turn from green to a reddish-brown color upon maturity.

What are the pharmacological activities of Polygonum amphibium?

This plant has antimicrobial pharmacological activity that allows it to inhibit the growth of various pathogens. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively combat specific bacterial and fungal strains. These medicinal properties are often attributed to the presence of unique secondary metabolites within its tissues. Such biological actions suggest that the species could serve as a valuable source for developing new therapeutic agents. Scientists continue to investigate the precise mechanisms through which these antimicrobial effects are exerted.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", the antimicrobial activity of methanol and acetone extracts of Polygonum amphibium was assessed against three Gram-positive, five Gram-negative bacteria and one fungus. The methanol extracts of the plant did not exhibit any inhibitory activity against any of the tested microorganisms. However, the acetone extracts showed significant activity against Bacillus subtilis ATC: 6633. For this specific activity, the inhibition zones were measured in the 7-16 mm range. Additionally, the minimal inhibitory concentration values for the acetone extracts were found in the 0.49-12.50 mg/mL range.
