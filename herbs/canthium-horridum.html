Canthium horridum

What's the taxonomical classification of Canthium horridum?

Canthium horridum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Gentianaales. This plant is a member of the family Rubiaceae, which is further categorized into the genus Canthium, ultimately identifying it at the species level as Canthium horridum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canthium horridum has a prostrate to ascending growth habit characterized by slender, woody stems that are densely covered in sharp, spreading spines. The leaves are small, lanceolate to linear, and arranged oppositely along the branchlets. Tiny, inconspicuous flowers appear in small clusters within the axils of the leaves. The plant typically develops a tangled, mat-like structure as it spreads across sandy or rocky substrates. These morphological adaptations allow the species to thrive in harsh, arid environments.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains the medicinal compounds scocharone, scopletein, syringic acid, vanilliic acid 4-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside, and 3'-methoxy-4'-hydroxy-trans-cinnamaldehye. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the biological activities and therapeutic potential of the species. The presence of various phenolic acids and coumarins suggests significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Researchers often study these individual constituents to understand their precise pharmacological mechanisms. Each molecule plays a unique role in the chemical profile that defines the plant's medicinal value.

    As discussed in a study, bioassay-guided isolation studies of the Canthium horridum Bl. stem extract led to the isolation of 10 compounds. These medicinal compounds include (+) -syringaresinol (1), scoparone (2), scopoletin (3), 3'-methoxy-4'-hydroxy-trans-cinnamaldehye (4), sinapic aldehyde (5), syringic acid (6), mannitol (7), vanillic acid 4-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (8), beta-daucosterol (9), and beta-sitosterol (10). Among these, compound 6 had the highest activity against Bacillus subtilis, while compound 1 showed good activity against Escherichia coli, Bacillus subtilis, and Staphylococcus aureus. Compounds 2, 4, and 6 also inhibited the growth of these 3 bacteria. None of the 10 compounds demonstrated inhibitory activity against Aspergillus niger.
