Canthium horridum

What's the taxonomical classification of Canthium horridum?

Canthium horridum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Gentianaales. This plant is a member of the family Rubiaceae, which is further categorized into the genus Canthium, ultimately identifying it at the species level as Canthium horridum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canthium horridum has a prostrate to ascending growth habit characterized by slender, woody stems that are densely covered in sharp, spreading spines. The leaves are small, lanceolate to linear, and arranged oppositely along the branchlets. Tiny, inconspicuous flowers appear in small clusters within the axils of the leaves. The plant typically develops a tangled, mat-like structure as it spreads across sandy or rocky substrates. These morphological adaptations allow the species to thrive in harsh, arid environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and semi-arid regions of Southern Africa, specifically spanning parts of South Africa, Namibia, and Botswana. The species thrives in environments where seasonal rainfall is limited and temperature fluctuations are significant. Its distribution is often dictated by the availability of well-drained substrates and specific microclimates found in these dry biomes. How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-draining, sandy soil and full sunlight to thrive in a temperate or Mediterranean climate.

Growers must ensure that the watering schedule allows the substrate to dry out completely between applications to prevent root rot. Because it is a hardy species, it can tolerate periods of drought once its root system is established. Providing ample space for lateral growth is also necessary to accommodate its spreading habit.

Regular pruning can help manage its density and maintain a healthy shape throughout the growing season.

What are the pharmacological activities of Canthium horridum?

This plant has antimicrobial, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic pharmacological activities. These diverse therapeutic properties are primarily attributed to the presence of secondary metabolites like alkaloids, flavonoids, and tannins found within its tissues. Research indicates that its antimicrobial action helps combat various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates significant potential in reducing oxidative stress and mitigating inflammatory responses in biological systems. Its ability to relieve pain and reduce fever further highlights its medicinal importance in traditional healing practices.

As discussed in a study, bioassay-guided isolation of the Canthium horridum Bl. stem extract led to the isolation of 10 compounds, which include (+)-syringaresinol (1), scoparone (2), scopoletin (3), 3'-methoxy-4'-hydroxy-trans-cinnamaldehde (4), sinapic aldehyde (5), syringic acid (6), mannitol (7), vanillic acid 4-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (8), beta-daucosterol (9), and beta-sitosterol (10). Among these, compound 6 demonstrated the highest activity against Bacillus subtilis. Compound 1 showed good activity against Escherichia coli, Bacillus subtilis, and Staphylococcus aureus. Additionally, compounds 2, 4, and 6 inhibited the growth of these 3 bacteria. However, none of the 10 compounds demonstrated inhibitory activity against Aspergillus niger.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds scopolamine, scopolin, syringic acid, vanillic acid 4-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside, and 3'-methoxy-4'-hydroxy-trans-cinnamic aldehyde. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the complex therapeutic profile observed in the species. According to a study published by "Natural product communications", bioassay-guided isolation studies of the Canthium horridum Bl. stem extract led to the isolation of 10 compounds. These compounds are (+) -syringaresinol (1), scoparone (2), scopoletin (3), 3'-methoxy-4'-hydroxy-trans-cinnamaldehye (4), sinapic aldehyde (5), syringic acid (6), mannitol (7), vanillic acid 4-O-beta-D-glucopyranoside (8), beta-daucosterol (9), and beta-sitosterol (10). Compounds 1, 4, 5, 6, and 8 were reported from the genus for the first time, while all 10 were reported for the first time from this species. Regarding antimicrobial activity, compound 6 had the highest activity against Bacillus subtilis, while compound 1 showed good activity against Escherichia coli, Bacillus subtilis, and Staphylococcus aureus. Compounds 2, 4, and 6 also inhibited the growth of these 3 bacteria, but none of the 10 compounds demonstrated inhibitory activity against Aspergillus niger.
