What to know about Myrtus communis (myrtle) before using it medicinally

Myrtus communis, commonly known as myrtle, is a herb renowned for its multifaceted health benefits, including its ability to lower blood pressure, improve digestion, and boost the immune system.

Cultivated for its ornamental value and versatile uses, myrtle is a hardy evergreen shrub that thrives in well-drained soil and partial shade. Botanically, the herb is characterized by its fragrant, white flowers and glossy, dark green leaves, which are rich in essential oils and antioxidants.

With its ancient origins dating back to ancient Greece and Rome, myrtle has been prized for its medicinal and spiritual properties for centuries, with references to its use appearing in the writings of Hippocrates and Galen.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Myrtus communis.

What are the medicinal properties of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis helps with a range of health issues, including digestive problems, respiratory issues, and skin conditions, due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Its leaves, bark, and berries are commonly used for medicinal purposes.

The active constituents of Myrtus communis include essential oils, terpenes, and flavonoids, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities, making the plant a valuable resource for traditional medicine.

The leaves, bark, and berries of Myrtus communis are the most commonly used parts for medicinal purposes. The leaves are used to make infusions and decoctions for digestive issues, while the bark is used for respiratory problems. The berries are used to treat skin conditions.

Improper use of Myrtus communis can cause allergic reactions, including skin irritation and respiratory issues. Ingestion of large quantities can lead to gastrointestinal problems, such as diarrhea and nausea. Long-term use can also cause an imbalance of electrolytes and potassium.

When using Myrtus communis medicinally, it is essential to take precautions, including consulting a healthcare professional before use, especially if pregnant or breastfeeding. The plant's essential oils can cause skin irritation, so topical applications should be diluted with a carrier oil.

What are the horticulural aspects of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis grow best in well-draining soil and full sun to partial shade, requiring a pH range of 6.0-8.0 and 600-1000 mm of annual rainfall. It is a Mediterranean native and tolerates heat and drought, but young plants are sensitive to frost.

For optimal growth, plant myrtle in a location with good air circulation, spacing individual plants 3-4 meters apart to prevent overcrowding. Water regularly during the first year after planting, then reduce watering to once or twice a week, depending on soil moisture. Mulch around the base to retain moisture and suppress weeds.

Harvest myrtle leaves and flowers at the peak of their fragrance and color, typically in late spring to early summer. Use scissors or pruning shears to cut off the stems, leaving about 2-3 cm of stem intact. Harvest only what is needed, as excessive cutting can stress the plant and reduce future yields.

Common pests affecting myrtle include the myrtle spider mite, scales, and root aphids. Diseases such as root rot, leaf spot, and powdery mildew can also occur, often caused by overwatering, poor air circulation, or fungal spores. Regularly inspect the plant for signs of infestation or disease and take corrective action promptly.

What are the botanical characteristics of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis, also known as common myrtle or true myrtle, is a small to medium-sized evergreen shrub that exhibits several distinct botanical characteristics.

The plant has an upright growth habit, typically reaching heights of 1-3 meters, with a spreading canopy and slender branches covered in fine, glossy leaves that are dark green on the upper surface and pale green beneath. The leaves themselves are lance-shaped, measuring around 5-10 cm in length, with a pointed tip and wavy margins.

The leaves also possess a distinctive, pungent aroma when crushed or bruised. Myrtus communis produces small, yellowish-white flowers that are borne in clusters at the tips of the branches, typically between May and August; these flowers are followed by small, edible berries that are an important food source for various birds and insects. The plant's bark is smooth and grey, with a reddish-brown coloration when young.

Overall, Myrtus communis is characterized by its attractive foliage, fragrant leaves, and showy flowers, making it a popular ornamental plant in Mediterranean landscapes.

What is the taxonomy of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Myrtales, family Myrtaceae, genus Myrtus, and species M. communis.

It is commonly known as common myrtle or true myrtle, and is a small evergreen tree native to the Mediterranean region.

What are the variants of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis has variants, such as Myrtus communis 'Compacta', which is a dwarf cultivar that grows up to 3-4 feet tall and is often used in hedges or topiaries.

Another variant is Myrtus communis 'Microphylla', which has smaller leaves and is commonly used in rock gardens or borders. Additionally, there are also several natural hybrids and forms of M. communis, such as the Italian myrtle (Myrtus communis subsp. italica) and the Spanish myrtle (Myrtus communis subsp.

hispanica), which have different leaf shapes and sizes, but retain the same characteristic fragrance and evergreen foliage.

What is the geographic distribution of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis, also known as common myrtle or sweet myrtle, is native to the Mediterranean region and western Asia.

Its geographic distribution extends from Spain and Portugal in the west to Turkey and Iran in the east, and from Italy and Greece in the north to Israel and Lebanon in the south. It is commonly found in scrublands, forests, and rocky areas at elevations up to 1,000 meters above sea level.

The plant has also been naturalized or introduced to other parts of Europe, Africa, Asia, and Australia, where it can be cultivated as an ornamental or for its culinary uses.

What is the life cycle of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis, also known as common myrtle, has a relatively long life cycle.

It is a perennial shrub that can live for up to 50 years or more in ideal conditions. The plant typically begins its life cycle by germinating from seed in the spring, after which it produces small, ovate leaves and eventually white flowers in the summer.

Following pollination, the flowers develop into purple-black berries that contain seeds. As the seasons change, the plant goes dormant during the winter months, only to begin anew the following spring.

With proper care and protection from extreme weather conditions, Myrtus communis can thrive for many years, eventually producing new stems and roots to continue its life cycle.

What are the historical uses of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis, also known as myrtle, has a long history of use dating back thousands of years.

In ancient Greece and Rome, its aromatic leaves and berries were used for perfumes, wreaths, and garlands. The plant was also valued for its medicinal properties, with the Greeks using it to treat a variety of ailments including headaches and diarrhea.

Additionally, myrtle was considered sacred by the Egyptians and was often used in rituals and ceremonies.

What are the mythological referencec of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis, also known as common myrtle, has been imbued with mythological significance across various cultures.

In Greek mythology, Aphrodite and Apollo were said to have fallen in love beneath a myrtle tree. The plant was also associated with Venus, the Roman goddess of love and beauty, who was often depicted wearing garlands of myrtle leaves.

Additionally, the myrtle was considered a symbol of fertility and marriage in ancient Greece and Rome.

What are the symbolic meanings of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis, also known as common myrtle, has been imbued with symbolic meanings across various cultures and traditions.

In Greek mythology, it was associated with love, beauty, and fertility, while in Christianity, it symbolized devotion, purity, and the Virgin Mary. In alchemy, myrtle represented spiritual growth and illumination, and in ancient Egypt, it symbolized eternity and rebirth.

Its sweet fragrance is often linked to emotional balance, clarity, and protection.

What are the historical texts of Myrtus communis?

Myrtus communis, also known as common myrtle, has been documented in various historical texts throughout its long history.

The Greek physician Dioscorides mentioned it in his 1st-century CE book "De Materia Medica" for its medicinal properties. In the 15th century, Leonhart Fuchs described it in his herbal "De Historia Stirpium", highlighting its use as a flavoring agent and perfume ingredient.

Similarly, the Swedish botanist Carolus Clusius wrote about it in his 1601 book "Rariorum Plantarum Historia".
