Myrtle, scientifically known as Myrtus communis, is a flowering shrub native to the Mediterranean region, valued for its aromatic leaves and berries, and widely used as a medicinal herb and adaptogen.

It is renowned for its primary benefits, including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antiseptic properties, which support respiratory health, skin conditions, and digestive wellness. Historically, myrtle has been a key component in traditional Mediterranean and Middle Eastern medicine, used to treat coughs, sore throats, and as a tonic for vitality. In modern wellness practices, it is incorporated into herbal teas, topical balms, and aromatherapy to promote relaxation and respiratory relief.

Scientific and Botanical Profile

Myrtle, with botanical name Myrtus communis, is a small evergreen shrub belonging to the family Myrtaceae, known for its aromatic foliage and medicinal properties.

Native to the Mediterranean Basin, it thrives in Southern Europe, Northern Africa, Western Asia, and regions such as the Iberian Peninsula, Balearic Islands, Corsica, Sardinia, Italy, Gibraltar, and Malta. The plant features glossy, dark green leaves that are opposite and emit a sweet, fragrant scent, while its small white or pale pink flowers bloom in spring, giving way to edible, berry-like fruits. Common names for this versatile species include Myrtle, Common Myrtle, Sweet Myrtle, and European Myrtle, reflecting its widespread cultivation and cultural significance.

Its morphological characteristics and adaptability have made it a valued plant in both traditional medicine and ornamental horticulture across its native range.

History and Cultural Relevance

Myrtle was used by ancient civilizations such as the Greeks, Romans, and Egyptians for its aromatic leaves and symbolic significance, often associated with love, fertility, and purification.

In traditional medicine systems like Greek and Ayurvedic practices, myrtle has been valued for its antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, and respiratory benefits, with leaves and essential oils used to treat coughs, infections, and skin ailments. The plant played a central role in religious and cultural rituals, such as Jewish Passover ceremonies and Roman funerary rites, where its fragrant branches were burned or used in purification processes. In Mediterranean folklore, myrtle is believed to bring good fortune and is often planted near homes or used in wedding ceremonies to symbolize love and prosperity.

Today, myrtle continues to be used in herbal remedies for respiratory support and skin care, with its essential oil still prized in aromatherapy and natural medicine.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Myrtle contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including essential oils rich in monoterpenes such as pinene and limonene, along with flavonoids like quercetin and myricetin, which contribute to its aromatic and therapeutic properties.

The plant is also a source of alkaloids, though in smaller amounts, and contains phenolic compounds that act as powerful antioxidants. Nutritionally, Myrtle is low in calories but provides trace amounts of vitamins such as vitamin C and some B-complex vitamins, as well as minerals like iron and calcium. Its antioxidant profile helps neutralize free radicals, reducing oxidative stress and supporting immune function.

The combination of these compounds may contribute to its traditional use in treating respiratory and digestive ailments by exerting anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects within the body.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Myrtus communis has been traditionally used for its diverse medicinal properties, offering benefits to multiple body systems including the respiratory, digestive, and circulatory systems.

It is known for its antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, making it effective in treating conditions such as coughs, sore throats, and gastrointestinal disorders. Compared to similar herbs like thyme or oregano, myrtus communis possesses a unique combination of essential oils, including cineole and myrtenol, which enhance its potency in respiratory support and antimicrobial activity. Its ability to support immune function and reduce inflammation sets it apart, particularly in chronic conditions where long-term use is beneficial.

Additionally, its mild nature allows for broader application in both traditional and modern herbal medicine, offering a gentler yet effective alternative to stronger pharmaceutical treatments.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Myrtus communis has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties in various forms, including fresh leaves, dried leaves, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or as a decoction by simmering the leaves for a longer period. For topical use, the essential oil or powdered form can be applied directly to the skin, often diluted with a carrier oil. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 cups of tea per day, while children may require lower doses, depending on age and safety guidelines.

It is advisable to use myrtus communis sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Myrtus communis can be considered relatively safe when used in moderate amounts for short-term periods, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals.

It is generally not known to interact significantly with most medications, but caution is advised when combining it with anticoagulants or sedatives due to potential additive effects. Special populations such as pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with small doses and monitor for any adverse reactions, and to always consult a healthcare professional before incorporating myrtus communis into a treatment regimen.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Myrtus communis grows best in well-drained, slightly acidic to neutral soils that are rich in organic matter, and it thrives in full sun to partial shade, depending on the climate.

It requires moderate watering, allowing the soil to dry slightly between waterings to prevent root rot, and it benefits from regular pruning to maintain its shape and promote new growth. For optimal care, mulching around the base of the plant helps retain moisture and suppress weeds, while annual fertilization with a balanced organic fertilizer supports healthy development. Harvesting is best done in late summer to early autumn when the berries are fully mature and have a deep blue-black color, using sharp pruning shears to cut the stems just below the berries to avoid damaging the plant.

To preserve its potency, the harvested berries should be dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties for longer.
