Armeniaca vulgaris

What's the taxonomical classification of Armeniaca vulgaris?

Armeniaca vulgaris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls into the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, the plant is situated in the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Armeniaca and the species vulgaris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Armeniaca vulgaris has a woody perennial structure characterized by spreading branches, lanceolate leaves with serrated margins, and pinkish-white five-petaled flowers. The bark on older trees becomes deeply fissured and develops a rough texture over time. Leaves are arranged alternately along the stems and typically feature long petioles. During the flowering stage, the blossoms emerge from the wood before the foliage fully develops. The plant eventually produces fleshy drupes that serve as the fruit.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, spanning across southern Europe, northern Africa, and western Asia. It thrives in coastal areas and dry, rocky terrains where sunlight is abundant. Due to human activity and its ability to adapt, it has been introduced to many other parts of the world, including parts of North America and Australia. The species often colonizes disturbed sites and coastal cliffs in these new environments. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. These symptoms often arise from toxicity caused by amygdalin, which releases cyanide when ingested. Such chemical reactions may also lead to dangerous drops in blood sugar known as hypoglycemia. Furthermore, individuals may experience hypotension and dizziness as their vital signs stabilize or fluctuate. These side effects underscore the significant health risks associated with consuming the plant.
