Nigella sativa

What's the taxonomical classification of Nigella sativa?

Nigella sativa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is positioned in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Ranunculales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Ranunculaceae, specifically situated within the genus Nigella, with its precise biological designation being the species sativa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nigella sativa has an erect, branched annual herb structure characterized by slender stems, pinnatifid leaves, and small white to blue flowers surrounded by prominent involucral bracts. The leaves are finely divided into narrow segments that create a feathery appearance throughout the plant. Its flowers typically feature five petals and numerous stamens, often appearing in clusters at the leaf axils. Following pollination, the plant produces distinctive, inflated capsule-like fruits that contain numerous small, black, angular seeds. These seeds are the primary morphological feature used for identification and commercial harvest.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant Nigella sativa is cultivated by sowing small, black seeds directly into well-drained, loamy soil during the spring or early autumn in temperate climates.

It requires full sun exposure and moderate watering to ensure optimal growth and seed development. Farmers often plant the crop in large fields where it can thrive in diverse soil types, provided the drainage is sufficient to prevent root rot. As the plant matures, it produces delicate blue flowers followed by distinctive, inflated seed capsules.

The final harvest occurs once these capsules turn brown and dry, allowing the nutritious seeds to be collected for medicinal and culinary use.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Nigella sativa contains therapeutic compounds primarily located in its small, black, oil-rich seeds. These seeds are the most significant medicinal component and are frequently used to extract black cumin oil. This oil is valued for its high concentration of thymoquinone, which possesses antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. While the seeds are the main focus, the plant's oil is often applied topically or ingested to support various health functions. The various parts of the plant are rarely used, as the chemical potency is most concentrated within the seed itself.

What traditional systems uses this plant?

This plant Nigella sativa has been utilized for millennia across the Middle East, North Africa, and South Asia as both a culinary spice and a fundamental medicinal remedy.

Ancient Egyptian records indicate that the seeds were included in burials to provide healing properties in the afterlife. In traditional Ayurvedic medicine, the seeds are frequently employed to treat respiratory ailments, digestive issues, and skin inflammation. Many historical practitioners also used the oil extracted from the seeds to soothe various systemic discomforts and bolster immune function.

These longstanding cultural practices have established the plant as a cornerstone of folk medicine in numerous global traditions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Nigella sativa?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, antidiabetic, and antihyperglycemic properties. The antioxidant components help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. Its antimicrobial effects allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms effectively. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates potent antidiabetic and antihyperglycemic actions by helping to regulate blood glucose levels. These diverse biological functions make Nigella sativa a valuable subject for therapeutic research.

According to a study published by "Pharmaceutical biology", Nigella sativa L. is an annual flowering plant used traditionally as a multipurpose medicinal agent and natural food supplement. Research covering databases such as Web of Science (ISI), PubMed, Scopus, and Google Scholar from 1990 up to February 2021 indicates the plant and its constituent thymoquinone possess antiviral, anti-inflamatory, and immunomodulatory properties. Specifically, Nigella sativa and thymoquinone reduce the levels of pro-inflamatory mediators including IL-2, IL-4, IL-6, and IL-12 while enhancing IFN-gamma. The plant also increases serum levels of IgG1 and IgG2a and provides bronchodilatory effects that improve pulmonary function tests in restrictive respiratory disorders. These findings from molecular docking, animal, and clinical studies suggest Nigella sativa may be beneficial for the treatment or control of COVID-19.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several bioactive medicinal compounds including thymoquinone, nigellidine, nigellimine, nigellannoid, and thymohydroquinone. Thymoquinone serves as the primary constituent responsible for much of the plant's antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of nigellidine and nigellimine contributes to the diverse pharmacological profile of the seeds. Additionally, nigellannoid structures and thymohydroquinone play significant roles in the plant's therapeutic potential. These specific chemical elements work together to provide various health benefits.

According to a study published by "Journal of cardiovascular pharmacology", Nigella sativa is known as the black seed. The principal phytochemical compound found in the volatile oil of this plant is thymoquinone. This specific compound has been shown to improve aging-related impairment of endothelium-dependent relaxations in animal models. The study explores the effects of these natural products and discusses the molecular mechanisms behind their potential therapeutic effects.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, hypotension, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and skin rash. Consuming Nigella sativa may lead to dangerously low blood sugar levels or a significant drop in blood pressure. Digestive issues such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain are also frequently reported side effects. Some individuals might experience dermatological reactions like a skin rash after contact or ingestion. These adverse reactions vary in severity depending on the dosage and the individual's sensitivity.
