Nigella sativa

What's the taxonomical classification of Nigella sativa?

Nigella sativa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is positioned in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Ranunculales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Ranunculaceae, specifically situated within the genus Nigella, with its precise biological designation being the species sativa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nigella sativa has an erect, branched annual herb structure characterized by slender stems, pinnatifid leaves, and small white to blue flowers surrounded by prominent involucral bracts. The leaves are finely divided into narrow segments that create a feathery appearance throughout the plant. Its flowers typically feature five petals and numerous stamens, often appearing in clusters at the leaf axils. Following pollination, the plant produces distinctive, inflated capsule-like fruits that contain numerous small, black, angular seeds. These seeds are the primary morphological feature used for identification and commercial harvest.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Nigella sativa contains therapeutic compounds primarily located in its small, black, oil-rich seeds. These seeds are the most significant medicinal component and are frequently used to extract black cumin oil. This oil is valued for its high concentration of thymoquinone, which possesses antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. While the seeds are the main focus, the plant's oil is often applied topically or ingested to support various health functions. The various parts of the plant are rarely used, as the chemical potency is most concentrated within the seed itself.

What are the pharmacological activities of Nigella sativa?

This plant has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antidiabetic, and analgesic pharmacological activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative stress and damage. The anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects allow it to reduce swelling and alleviate various types of pain. Furthermore, its antimicrobial capabilities enable it to combat diverse bacterial and fungal pathogens effectively. Finally, the antidiabetic activity supports blood glucose regulation, making it a subject of significant medicinal interest.

As discussed in a study, Nigella sativa seeds extracts (NSSE) demonstrate potential protective effects against carbendazim (CBZ) toxicity in male mice. The study utilized 6 groups of mice to investigate how NSSE can mitigate changes induced by CBZ. In mice exposed only to CBZ, there were significant declines in RBC, HB, Hct, WBC, total protein, FSH, LH, testosterone, T4, T3, CAT, and SOD. Furthermore, CBZ exposure significantly enhanced levels of ALT, AST, ALP, total bilirubin, creatinine, BUN, uric acid, glucose, cholesterol, CK, LDH, MDA, and GSH. Treatment with NSSE showed attenuation effects against these CBZ induced hematological and biochemical changes. These results suggest that the attenuation effects of NSSE are attributed to its antioxidant properties.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds thymoquinoine, nigellidine, nigellanoid, alpha-hederin, and nigella mines A(1). These diverse phytochemicals contribute to the extensive therapeutic potential observed in its biological applications. Specifically, the presence of thymoquinoine is often linked to significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Other specialized components like nigellidine and nigellanoid play critical roles in the plant's unique chemical profile. Furthermore, the combination of alpha-hederin and nigella mines A(1) provides a complex array of bioactive properties used in various medicinal studies.

As discussed in a study, Nigella sativa is also known as black seed. The principal phytochemical compound found in the volatile oil of this plant is thymoquinone. This specific compound has been shown to improve aging-related impairment of endothelium-dependent relaxations in animal models.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, hypotension, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and skin rash. Consuming Nigella sativa may lead to dangerously low blood sugar levels or a significant drop in blood pressure. Digestive issues such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain are also frequently reported side effects. Some individuals might experience dermatological reactions like a skin rash after contact or ingestion. These adverse reactions vary in severity depending on the dosage and the individual's sensitivity.
