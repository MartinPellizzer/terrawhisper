Cumin

Trachyspermum ammi

Cumin (Trachyspermum ammi) is a flowering plant native to the Mediterranean region and widely used in traditional medicine for its digestive benefits, particularly in improving conditions such as indigestion and bloating.

Botanical Identification

Cumin is scientifically known as Trachyspermum ammi and belongs to the Apiaceae family. It is also called jeera in India and zaatar in some Middle Eastern regions. An annual herb native to southern Europe and southwestern Asia, it has feathery leaves, yellow flowers, and aromatic seeds. Synonyms include Cuminum cyminum in older botanical literature.

The following list summarize the taxonomy of plant:

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Magnoliopsida
  • Order: Apiales
  • Family: Apiaceae
  • Genus: Trachyspermum
  • Species: Trachyspermum ammi

Cumin Identification.

Active Compounds

Cumin contains essential oil, coumarin, and terpenoids as its main bioactive compounds. These compounds work together to enhance the herb's medicinal properties. The essential oil contributes to its aromatic and digestive benefits, while coumarin and terpenoids support its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects.

  • Essential oil
  • Coumarin
  • Terpenoid

Preparation Methods

Powder: Used for digestive issues and as a carminative.

Infusion: Used for respiratory ailments like coughs and colds.

Essential oil: Used for treating skin infections and as an antiseptic.

Safety Profile

Cumin (Trachyspermum ammi) is generally safe when used in typical culinary amounts. However, it may cause digestive upset in some individuals. Large doses or prolonged use can lead to side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or allergic reactions. It is contraindicated in pregnancy and should be avoided by those with known allergies to the Apiaceae family.
