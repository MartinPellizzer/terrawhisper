Tilia miqueliana

What's the taxonomical classification of Tilia miqueliana?

Tilia miqueliana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its hierarchical descent, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Malvales, it is categorized under the family Malvaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Tilia, and its unique species designation, miqueliana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tilia miqueliana has heart-shaped leaves with serrated margins, woody stems, and small, yellowish-white flowers clustered in axillary cymes. The foliage is characterized by a dense arrangement of veins that radiate from the base of the leaf blade toward the edges. Its bark typically displays a textured surface that becomes increasingly fissured as the tree reaches maturity. The reproductive structures are accompanied by a specialized leaf-like bract that facilitates seed dispersal by wind. These morphological traits distinguish the species from other members of the Malvaceae family.

What are the pharmacological activities of Tilia miqueliana?

This plant has demonstrated potent pharmacological activities including analgesic, anticancer, antidepressant, and antioxidant properties. The antioxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within the body. Research indicates its analgesic effects can effectively mitigate pain sensations in various biological models. Furthermore, the plant exhibits anticancer potential by inhibiting the growth and proliferation of specific malignant cells. Its antidepressant qualities also contribute to regulating mood and neurological stability.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in plant science", Tilia miqueliana Maxim. contains bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, coumarins, and other secondary metabolites. These substances possess antioxidant, anticancer, antidepressant, and analgesic effects. The study identified 1971 metabolites through widely targeted metabolomic analysis and Illumina transcriptomic sequencing. Flavonoids, amino acids and their derivatives, and phenolic acids accounted for nearly half of the total metabolites. Active compounds including tiliroside, scopoletin, naringenin, dihydrokaempferol, apiigenin, luteolin, quercetin, kaempferol, and rutin represent secondary metabolites with potential medicinal value in the leaves.
