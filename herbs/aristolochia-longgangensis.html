Aristolochia longgangensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Aristolochia longgangensis?

Aristolochia longgangensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows a taxonomic lineage that places it in the order Piperales. Within this order, the plant is a representative of the family Aristolochiaceae. It is further categorized under the genus Aristolochia, with its specific identification being the species longgangensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aristolochia longgangensis has climbing stems with opposite, cordate-shaped leaves that possess entire margins and distinctively long petioles. The foliage typically features a smooth texture and a vibrant green color on the upper surface. Its inflorescences consist of tubular, somewhat asymmetrical flowers that often exhibit brownish or purplish hues. These specialized floral structures are designed to attract specific insect pollinators through their unique shape and scent. The overall growth habit allows the plant to scramble effectively over surrounding vegetation in its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the island of Luzon in the Philippines, specifically documented within the province of Longganay. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Aristolochia longgangenis contains various bioactive secondary metabolites including aristolochic acids, alkaloids, flavonoids, and terpenoids. The presence of aristolochic acids is the most significant chemical feature, though these compounds are known for their potent nephrotoxicity and carcinogenicity. Beyond these acids, the plant's alkaloid profile contributes to its complex pharmacological potential in traditional medicine. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Aristolochia longgangensis targets various enzymes and signaling proteins, most notably inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 and modulating tumor necrosis factor-alpha to produce anti-inflammatory effects. The bioactive aristolochic acids within the plant interact with aryl hydrocarbon receptors to influence gene expression related to cellular detoxification and immune responses. These compounds also show affinity for specific kinases that regulate cell proliferation and apoptosis in various cancer cell lines. By modulating these protein pathways, the plant exhibits potential pharmacological properties including analgesic and anti-tumor activities. However, the interaction with DNA-binding proteins can also lead to mutagenic effects and nephrotoxicity.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe nephrotoxicity, nephropathy, uropathy, carcinogenicities, and renal failure due to the presence of aristolochic acids. These toxic compounds target the urinary system, often leading to permanent damage to the kidney tissues. The resulting nephropathy can progress rapidly toward acute or chronic renal failure. Furthermore, the plant is known for its significant carcinogenicity, which is frequently linked to the development of upper urinary tract cancers. This combination of uropathy and cellular mutation poses a grave risk to human health.
