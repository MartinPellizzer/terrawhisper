Lilium pumilum

What's the taxonomical classification of Lilium pumilum?

Lilium pumilum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Liliales and is a member of the family Liliaceae. Ultimately, its scientific designation is defined by the genus Lilium and the specific species pumilum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lilium pumilum has slender, upright stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters and bear several lanceolate, bright green leaves arranged in whorls or spirals. The flowers are relatively small and nodding, featuring six tepals that exhibit a pale yellowish to creamy white coloration often marked with small reddish-brown spots. These blooms possess delicate, protruding stamens and a central pistil that are characteristic of the genus. The bulb serves as the underground storage organ, consisting of fleshy scales that allow the plant to survive dormant periods. Overall, its compact stature and subtle floral markings distinguish it from larger lily species.

What are the pharmacological activities of Lilium pumilum?

This plant has choleretic, diuretic, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities that influence various physiological functions. The choleretic properties specifically stimulate the production and secretion of bile by the liver to aid digestion. Additionally, its diuretic effects help increase the rate of urine production to assist in fluid regulation. These biological actions suggest that the plant can be utilized in traditional medicine to support metabolic and excretory processes. Careful study of its chemical constituents is necessary to fully understand these therapeutic mechanisms.

As discussed in a study, aqueous and methanolic extracts of Lilium pumilum stimulate bile flow in an isolated perfused rat liver model. These extracts increase the bile flow dose-dependently by between 9% and 30%. Testing was conducted using aqueous extracts at three different concentrations of 100, 250, and 500 mg/L, both in the absence and presence of 10 μM taurocholic acid. The aqueous extract of Lilium pumilum has a total flavonoid content of 1.04%. Detected flavonoids in the extract include rutoside, kaempferol-3-O-rutinoside, and isorrhamnetin-3-O-rutinoside. No hepatotoxic effect was seen even during longer perfusions.
