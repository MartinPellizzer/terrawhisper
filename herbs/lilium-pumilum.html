Lilium pumilum

What's the taxonomical classification of Lilium pumilum?

Lilium pumilum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Liliales and is a member of the family Liliaceae. Ultimately, its scientific designation is defined by the genus Lilium and the specific species pumilum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lilium pumilum has slender, upright stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 30 centimeters and bear several lanceolate, bright green leaves arranged in whorls or spirals. The flowers are relatively small and nodding, featuring six tepals that exhibit a pale yellowish to creamy white coloration often marked with small reddish-brown spots. These blooms possess delicate, protruding stamens and a central pistil that are characteristic of the genus. The bulb serves as the underground storage organ, consisting of fleshy scales that allow the plant to survive dormant periods. Overall, its compact stature and subtle floral markings distinguish it from larger lily species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

The species prefers environments where well-drained soil and cool temperatures are consistent with its natural mountain ecosystem. What are the pharmacological activities of Lilium pumilum?

This plant has choleretic, digestive, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Its choleretic properties specifically enhance the production and flow of bile from the liver to assist in digestion. Beyond its effect on bile, the plant contains various bioactive compounds that support overall gastrointestinal health. These phytochemicals work together to reduce inflammation within the digestive tract and alleviate discomfort. Consequently, these diverse actions make it a subject of interest for natural medicinal applications.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", the aqueous and methanolic extracts of Lilium pumilum increase bile flow dose-dependently by between 9% and 30%. This stimulation of bile secretion is comparable in the presence and absence of 10 μM taurocholic acid. The aqueous extract of Lilium pumilum has a total flavonoid content of 1.04%. Within this extract, the flavonoids rutoside, kaempferol-3-O-rutinoside, and isorrhamnetin-3-O-rutinoside were detected. No hepatotoxic effect was seen during longer perfusions of the plant extracts.
