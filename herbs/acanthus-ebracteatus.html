Acanthus ebracteatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Acanthus ebracteatus?

Acanthus ebracteatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Lamiales and belongs to the family Acanthaceae. Specifically, this plant is identified by its genus, Acanthus, and its unique species designation, ebracteatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acanthus ebracteatous has deeply lobed, sessile leaves with spiny margins and small, inconspicuous white to pale pink flowers that lack bracts.

The foliage typically exhibits a dark green color and a rugged, architectural texture common to the Acanthaceae family. Its stems are erect and somewhat woody at the base, supporting the heavy, serrated leaf structure. The inflorescence is relatively subtle compared to its close relatives, appearing as small clusters rather than large, showy spikes.

These morphological traits allow the plant to thrive in shaded understory environments where its broad leaves can capture minimal light.

      Acanthus ebracteatus is prepared as a decoction, a concentrated infusion, or a topical paste using the plant's dried leaves and stems.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Acanthus ebracteatus can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled without protection. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many species within the Acanthaceae family contain bioactive compounds that may trigger dermatitis upon contact. Some individuals might experience redness, itching, or swelling if the plant's sap touches sensitive areas of the skin. Ingestion of unprocessed plant material could potentially lead to nausea or stomach discomfort. It is therefore advisable to use gloves when pruning and to avoid consuming any part of the plant.
