Acanthus ebracteatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Acanthus ebracteatus?

Acanthus ebracteatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Lamiales and belongs to the family Acanthaceae. Specifically, this plant is identified by its genus, Acanthus, and its unique species designation, ebracteatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acanthus ebracteatous has deeply lobed, sessile leaves with spiny margins and small, inconspicuous white to pale pink flowers that lack bracts. The foliage typically exhibits a dark green color and a rugged, architectural texture common to the Acanthaceae family. Its stems are erect and somewhat woody at the base, supporting the heavy, serrated leaf structure. The inflorescence is relatively subtle compared to its close relatives, appearing as small clusters rather than large, showy spikes. These morphological traits allow the plant to thrive in shaded understory environments where its broad leaves can capture minimal light.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains ebracteatoside A, ebracteatosides B-D, benzoxazinoid glycosides, megastigmane, and a significant total phenolic content. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse biological activities observed in the species. The presence of unique ebracteatosides characterizes the distinct chemical profile of this medicinal herb. Furthermore, the benzoxazinoid glycosides and megastigmane play important roles in its therapeutic potential. High levels of total phenolic content further support its medicinal value through antioxidant properties.

According to a study published by "Phytochemistry", the aerial part of Acanthus ebracteatus contains a megastigmane glycoside known as ebracteatoside A. Researchers also isolated 3 aliphaic alcohol glycosides, specifically ebracteatosides B-D, from the plant. Additionally, the compound 7-chloro-(2R)-2-O-beta-D-glucopyranosyl-4-hydroxy-2H-1,4-benzoxaazin-3(4H)-one, also called 7-Cl-DIBOA-Glc, was identified. These specific substances were isolated along with 22 known compounds. All structural elucidations for these components were performed using spectroscopic data analyses.

According to a study published by "BMC complementary medicine and therapies", Acanthus ebracteatus is used for its antibacterial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties to treat acne vulgaris. This skin condition is characterized by inflammation and is frequently linked to the bacteria Cutibacterium acnes and Staphylococcus spp. In a combination treatment with a 15:5:10 GM:CC:AE ratio, the mixture displayed high selectivity indices of 26.87 against Staphylococcus epidermidis and 4.99 against C. acnes. The study suggests this approach is effective for controlling acne vulgaris by inhibiting the bacterial enzyme topoisomerase II alpha and regulating the human nuclear factor kappa B subunit 1.
