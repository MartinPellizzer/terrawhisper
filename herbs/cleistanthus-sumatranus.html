Cleistanthus sumatranus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cleistanthus sumatranus?

Cleistanthus sumatranus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a member of the family Phyllanthaceae, specifically categorized within the genus Cleistanthus, and is identified by its specific species name, sumatranus.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Cleistanthus sumatranus contains bioactive secondary metabolites including alkaloids, flavonoids, tannins, and saponins that contribute to its therapeutic properties. These chemical constituents work synergistically to provide significant antioxidant and antimicrobial activities. Research indicates that the plant's extracts are often utilized in traditional medicine for treating inflammatory conditions and skin ailments. The specific concentration of these compounds can vary depending on the geographical location and the part of the plant harvested. What are the therapeutic actions of Cleistanthus sumatranus?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, gastrointestinal issues, and various inflammatory conditions. Local healers often apply the crushed leaves directly to the skin to accelerate the healing of cuts and sores. It is also frequently decocted into a liquid used to soothe stomach aches and digestive discomfort. Some traditional practices utilize the plant to manage symptoms associated with fever and internal aches. These medicinal applications rely on the plant's specific chemical properties to combat pathogens and reduce swelling.

What are the herbal preparations of Cleistanthus sumatranus?

Cleistanthus sumatranus is prepared as a decoction, a tea, or a topical paste depending on the specific ailment being treated. The leaves and stems are often boiled in water to create a medicinal infusion used for internal consumption. For skin-related issues, the plant material is crushed into a fine paste and applied directly to the affected area. Some traditional practices also involve using the plant in poultices to reduce swelling or inflammation. These various methods allow the active compounds within the plant to be effectively utilized by the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cleistanthus sumatranus can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or digestive distress if consumed or handled without precautions. While specific toxicological studies on humans are limited, many plants in this family contain secondary metabolites that may act as irritants to sensitive tissues. Contact with the leaves or stems could potentially trigger redness or itching in individuals with topical sensitivities. Ingesting parts of the plant might lead to stomach discomfort or nausea depending on the concentration of its chemical compounds. It is therefore important to exercise caution when using this species in traditional or medicinal applications.
