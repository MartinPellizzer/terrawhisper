Caragana sinica

What's the taxonomical classification of Caragana sinica?

Caragana sinica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Fabales, it is a member of the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Caragana, and its unique species designation, sinica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Caragana sinica has pinnately compound leaves consisting of several pairs of leaflets, woody stems with a distinct bark texture, and small, inconspicuous flowers arranged in clusters. The leaflets are typically elliptic or lanceolate in shape with entire margins and a smooth surface. Its woody structure supports a shrubby or small tree habit, depending on the specific environmental conditions of its habitat. The reproductive structures consist of small petals and stamens that facilitate pollination within its native range. These morphological features collectively define the plant's physical identity and its adaptation to its ecological niche.

What are the pharmacological activities of Caragana sinica?

This plant has anti-hyperuricemia, antioxidant, immune-regulatory, and neuraminidase inhibitory activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent oxidative stress within cellular structures. The immune-regulatory effects allow the plant to modulate various aspects of the body's natural defense mechanisms. Furthermore, its anti-hyperuricemia capability aids in managing uric acid levels to prevent conditions like gout. Finally, the neuraminidase inhibitory activity suggests potential applications in inhibiting viral replication processes.

As discussed in a study, alpha-viniferin is the major constituent of the roots of Caragana sinica (Buc'hoz) Rehder with a trimeric resveratrol oligosstilbenoid skeleton. This constituent possesses a strong inhibitory effect on xanthine oxidase in vitro, suggesting it is a potential anti-hyperuricemia agent. In hyperuricemia mice, alpha-viniferin treatment significantly reduced serum uric acid levels and mitigated kidney injury without showing obvious toxicity. The treatment repressed 54 differentially expressed genes with criteria of log2 FPKM >= 1.5 and p <= 0.01 in the kidney. Its protective effect on renal injury involved the downregulation of S100A9 in the IL-17 pathway, CCR5 and PIK3R5 in the chemokine signaling pathway, and TLR2, ITGA4, and PIK3R5 in the PI3K-AKT signaling pathway.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (+)-alpha-viniferin, alpha-viniferin, α-viniferin, caragasinins D, and caragasinins E. These specific stilbenoids are significant for their potential bioactive properties in various biological studies. The presence of these chemical constituents, particularly the different forms of alpha-viniferin, highlights the complex phytochemical profile of the species. Additionally, the caragasinins D and caragasinins E compounds contribute to the unique medicinal potential found within the plant tissues. Researchers often investigate these particular molecules to understand their therapeutic applications in medicine.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Caragana sinica contain one alkaloid known as hypaphorine and four oligomeric stilbenes, which are pallidol, kobophenol A, miyabenol C, and (+)-alpha-viniferin. These five analytes can be separated within 12 min using a 40 cm length capillary at a separation voltage of 12 kV in a 100 mmol/L borate buffer with a pH of 10.0. The response for all investigated analytes is linear over about 3 orders of magnitude. Detection limits for these constituents, based on an S/N of 3, range from 0.0385 to 0.111 mg/L.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cough, fever, hyperuricemia, hyperuricemia-induced kidney injury, and liver damage. It possesses significant medicinal properties that help alleviate respiratory symptoms like a persistent cough and reduce body temperature during a fever. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively lower uric acid levels to manage hyperuricemia. Furthermore, the plant provides protective effects against kidney injury caused by high uric acid concentrations. It also serves as a therapeutic agent to mitigate various forms of liver damage.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a wide range of anticholinergic side effects including nausea, dizziness, sedation, confusion, hallucinations, ataxia, cognitive impairment, dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, and constipation. These symptoms often arise from the plant's potent alkaloids that interfere with the nervous system. Users may experience significant mental disturbances such as disorientation or sensory distortions. Physical impairments like loss of coordination and vision changes can also occur. Furthermore, the physiological impact can lead to digestive and urinary complications.
