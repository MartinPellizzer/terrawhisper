Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Indian Frankincense, scientifically known as Boswellia serrata, is a resinous substance derived from the sap of the tree native to the arid regions of India, Africa, and the Middle East, and has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.

What is Indian Frankincense (Boswellia Serrata)?

Indian Frankincense, or Boswellia serrata, is a tree native to the Indian subcontinent that produces a resinous sap used in traditional herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Indian frankincense is Boswellia serrata. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Burserales, family Burseraceae, genus Boswellia, and species Boswellia serrata. It is known as frankincense in the Middle East and India, and as olibanum in the Mediterranean region. Common synonyms include Boswellia carterii and Boswellia sacra, which are botanical and regional variations.

Plant Description and Morphology

Indian frankincense is a small to medium-sized tree or shrub that typically grows up to 5 meters tall, with a dense, spreading crown and a straight, slender trunk.

Its leaves are simple, alternate, and oval-shaped with a pointed tip, measuring around 2-6 centimeters in length. The flowers are small, greenish-yellow, and clustered in panicles, with stamens that are prominently visible and often covered in fine hairs. The root system is shallow and fibrous, allowing the plant to thrive in arid and semi-arid conditions.

The bark is dark brown to gray, the leaves are glossy and leathery, and the resin has a strong, aromatic scent with a balsamic and slightly sweet undertone.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Indian frankincense is native to the dry, arid regions of the Indian subcontinent, particularly in India, Pakistan, and parts of Sri Lanka.

Its geographic distribution extends to the Arabian Peninsula, where it thrives in rocky, well-drained soils. The plant prefers a hot, semi-arid climate with low rainfall and high temperatures. It grows best in areas with good sunlight exposure and minimal humidity.

Ecological conditions such as well-drained soil, rocky terrain, and sparse vegetation are essential for its natural growth.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The resinous sap, known as frankincense, is primarily used medicinally, harvested from the bark and branches of the Boswellia serrata tree.

Phytochemical Composition

Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) contains a complex array of phytochemical compounds, including boswellic acids, terpenoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and various flavonoids, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antioxidant properties.

Pharmacological Properties

The pharmacological properties of Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) include anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antioxidant effects, primarily due to the presence of boswellic acids, which inhibit inflammatory enzymes such as 5-lipoxygenase.

Mechanisms of Action

Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through the inhibition of 5-lipoxygenase, an enzyme involved in the production of pro-inflammatory leukotrienes, and by modulating the activity of cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), thereby reducing inflammation and oxidative stress.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Indian Frankincense, derived from the resin of Boswellia serrata, is traditionally used in herbal medicine to reduce inflammation, alleviate joint pain, and support respiratory and digestive health due to its active boswellic acids.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) is typically prepared as a resinous extract, often in the form of capsules, tablets, or topical ointments, with the resin being harvested by making incisions in the bark of the tree and collecting the exuded sap.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) for general use ranges from 200 to 500 mg per day, divided into two or three doses, with standardized extracts containing at least 30% boswellic acids being recommended for efficacy.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) is generally considered safe, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal side effects such as stomach upset, nausea, or diarrhea, and it should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and in individuals with known allergies to the plant.

Drug Interactions

Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) may interact with anticoagulant medications, anti-inflammatory drugs, and immunosuppressants, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or reducing the effectiveness of these treatments.

Toxicity and Precautions

Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, so it should be used with caution and under professional guidance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Indian Frankincense, or Boswellia serrata, is cultivated primarily in the arid regions of India, Somalia, and Oman, where it thrives in dry, well-drained soils and requires minimal water, with harvesting typically occurring 5 to 7 years after planting.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) typically involve harvesting the resinous sap from the bark of the tree, which is then purified through methods such as steam distillation or solvent extraction to isolate the active compounds, including boswellic acids.

Storage and Shelf Life

Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Extensive scientific research has demonstrated that Indian Frankincense, or Boswellia serrata, contains active compounds such as boswellic acids that exhibit anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and immunomodulatory properties, supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine for conditions like arthritis and inflammatory disorders.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Indian Frankincense, derived from the resin of Boswellia serrata, has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine for centuries to treat inflammatory conditions, digestive disorders, and respiratory ailments.

Regulatory Status

In the United States, Indian Frankincense (Boswellia serrata) is considered a dietary supplement and is regulated by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994, which means it is not subject to pre-market approval but is held to safety standards and labeling requirements.
