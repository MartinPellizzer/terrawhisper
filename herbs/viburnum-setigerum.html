Viburnum setigerum

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum setigerum?

Viburnum setigerum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Dipsacales and falls within the family Viburnaceaae. Finally, its taxonomic placement is completed by its designation within the genus Viburnum, specifically as the species setigerum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum setigerum has an upright, multi-stemmed shrub habit characterized by opposite, elliptical leaves that measure approximately 3 to 6 centimeters in length and possess finely serrated margins.

The foliage is typically a deep green color with a smooth, somewhat lustrous upper surface. During the flowering season, the plant produces small, creamy white blossoms arranged in dense, flat-topped cymes. These flowers eventually give way to small, globose drupes that transition from green to a dark blue or black hue upon ripening.

The woody stems are relatively slender and contribute to a dense, bushy growth form.

        What are the herbal preparations of Viburnum setigerum?

        Viburnum setigerum is prepared as decoctions from the dried berries, infusions from the leaves, or tinctures derived from the bark and roots. The berries are often simmered in water to extract their soluble compounds for traditional tea preparations. Leaf infusions are commonly used to harness the plant's specific phytochemical profile in a milder liquid form. Tinctures provide a concentrated way to preserve the medicinal properties of the woody parts for long-term storage. These various methods allow for the targeted extraction of different active constituents depending on the desired therapeutic application.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Viburnum setigerum can cause gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea if the berries are consumed in large quantities by humans. While these berries are a vital food source for many bird species, they are not recommended for direct human consumption. Some individuals may also experience mild skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis when handling the plant's foliage or stems. There is limited clinical research regarding long-term toxicity, so caution is advised for those with sensitive systems. It is best to avoid ingesting any part of the plant unless it has been identified by a professional.
