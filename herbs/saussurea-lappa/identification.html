Scientific Classification

Gao Ben, or Saussurea lappa, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Asterales, family Asteraceae, genus Saussurea, and species Saussurea lappa. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the sunflower family.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Saussurea lappa is the scientific name for Gao Ben. It was first described by the botanist Jean Baptiste Saussure. The plant is also known by several synonyms, including Himalayan wormwood and Tibetan saffron. It is commonly referred to by its Chinese name, Gao Ben, and has various regional and historical names used in traditional medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Gao Ben, or Saussurea lappa, is a perennial herb with a thick, fleshy root system. It has a short, woody stem that branches near the base. Its leaves are alternate, lance-shaped, and have a rough texture. The plant produces a composite inflorescence with yellow flowers. The fruit is a achene, and the seeds are small and dark.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Gao Ben, or Saussurea lappa, can be distinguished by its thick, aromatic rhizome with a dark brown outer layer and pale yellow interior. It has dense, fibrous roots and a distinctive, slightly woody texture. The leaves are alternate, with a hairy surface and narrow, linear blades. Its overall appearance and aroma help differentiate it from similar species in the same family.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Gao Ben, or Saussurea lappa, is native to the mountainous regions of Asia, including the Himalayas and parts of China. It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and cold winters. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It is typically found at elevations between 1,500 and 3,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Gao Ben blooms from June to July, producing yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs from August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested in early autumn, around September, when the roots are most developed and contain the highest concentration of active compounds.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Saussurea lappa belongs to the genus Saussurea in the family Asteraceae. Its closest species include S. medusa and S. obvallata. It shares key morphological and genetic traits with other members of the genus, such as capitulum structure and chromosome number. These relationships aid in accurate identification and classification within the family.
