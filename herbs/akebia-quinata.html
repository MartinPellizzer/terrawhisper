Akebia quinata

What's the taxonomical classification of Akebia quinata?

Akebia quinata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its hierarchical progression, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Ranunculales and falls under the family Lardizabalaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Akebia and the species quinata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Akebia quinata has woody, climbing vines that feature deciduous, palmately compound leaves typically consisting of five leaflets arranged around a central point. The leaflets are ovate to elliptical in shape with serrated margins and a glossy green upper surface. During the flowering season, small, pendulous, purplish-brown flowers emerge from the leaf axils. These flowers eventually develop into large, elongated, sausage-shaped fruits that transition from green to a deep purple color when ripe. The fruit possesses a thick skin that splits open to reveal a white, fleshy pulp containing numerous small seeds.

What are the pharmacological activities of Akebia quinata?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, anticancer, antioxidant, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals, which demonstrates significant antioxidant potential. Research indicates that these substances can inhibit the growth of malignant cells through various mechanisms of cytotoxicity. These properties contribute to its broader anticancer and anti-tumor effects in laboratory studies. Furthermore, the plant exhibits anti-inflammatory actions that help reduce biological markers of swelling and irritation.

As discussed in a study, an exopoly heteropolysaccharide designated as AQP70-2B was isolated from Akebia quinata. The monosaccharide composition of AQP70-2B includes rhamnose, glucose, galactose, and arabinose. The backbone of this polysaccharide consists of specific residues including →1)-l-Araf, →3)-l-Araf-(1→, →5)-l-Araf-(1→, →3,5)-l-Araf-(1→, →2,5)-l-Araf-(1→, →4)-d-Glcp-(1→, →6)-d-Galp-(1→, and →1)-d-Rhap. When AQP70-2B was modified with selenium to create Se-AQP70-2B, its anti-tumor activity was improved. Using zebrafish xenografts and anti-angiogenesis experiments, the study showed that Se-AQP70-2B inhibits tumor cell proliferation and migration while blocking angiogenesis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds hederagenin, oleanolic acid, kalopanaxsaponin A, mutongsaponin B, and mutongsaponin C. These specific triterpenoid saponins are primarily responsible for the plant's diverse biological activities. Researchers study these chemical constituents to understand their potential anti-inflammatory and antitumor properties. The presence of these molecules suggests significant pharmacological value in various therapeutic applications. Understanding the concentration of these compounds is essential for developing standardized herbal extracts.

As discussed in a study, the stem of Akebiia quinata contains medicinal components such as a methanol extract, a BuOH fraction, and specific compounds like kalopanaxsaponin A, oleanolic acid, and hederagenin. The methanol extract of the stem showed antinociceptive and anti-inflammatory effects when administered orally at doses of 100 and 250 mg/kg. Additionally, the sapogenins oleanolic acid and hederagenin produced significant effects in rats when treated perorally at 10 and 30 mg/kg. These results suggest that the antinociceptive and anti-inflammatory properties of the Akebiia quinata stem are attributed to these sapogenins.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Aβ42 induced fibrillogenesis, HTLV-1 infection, various bacteria, breast cancer, and tumors. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate a significant ability to inhibit the formation of amyloid-beta fibrils associated with neurodegenerative conditions. Research also indicates that its extracts possess potent antiviral properties effective against HTLV-1 infection. Furthermore, the plant contains phytochemicals that exhibit strong antibacterial actions against diverse bacteria. Studies suggest these components can also disrupt the growth of tumors and suppress breast cancer cell progression.
