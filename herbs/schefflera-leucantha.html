Schefflerera leucantha belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Apiales, this plant is a member of the family Araliaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identification is defined by the genus Schefflera and the species name leucantha.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Schefflera leucantha has large, elliptical to oblong leaves that typically measure between 10 and 25 centimeters in length and feature a glossy, dark green surface with prominent midribs.

The leaf margins are entire and smooth, while the texture feels somewhat leathery to the touch. These leaves are arranged alternately along woody, upright stems that can reach significant heights in their natural habitat. The plant is characterized by its dense, bushy foliage which provides a lush appearance.

Small, white flowers emerge in terminal clusters, adding a delicate contrast to the heavy greenery.

      What are the herbal preparations of Schefflera leucantha?

      Schefflera leucantha is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste derived from its various plant parts. The leaves and bark are often boiled in water to extract soluble compounds for internal consumption. Alternatively, the plant material can be steeped in hot water to create a milder tea known as an infusion. For external applications, the crushed leaves are frequently formed into a thick paste to treat skin irritations. These diverse methods allow the medicinal properties of the plant to be utilized for different therapeutic purposes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Schefflera leucantha can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested by humans or pets. The plant contains compounds that may trigger contact dermatitis or rashes upon touching its leaves or sap. If swallowed, it often leads to symptoms such as vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. Pets like cats and dogs are particularly susceptible to these adverse reactions if they chew on the foliage. While it is generally considered a decorative indoor plant, cautious handling is necessary to avoid these physical complications.
