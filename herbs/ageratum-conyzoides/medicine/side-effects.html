10 Possible Side Effects Of Ageratum Conyzoides

Ageratum conyzoides has some side effects when used improperly, such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea.

These side effects occur when the herb is ingested in large quantities or for extended periods. For instance, consuming too much blueweed can lead to severe stomach cramps, which can hinder daily activities and worsen the quality of life.

Furthermore, excessive vomiting can lead to dehydration, making people more susceptible to infections and other complications.

1. Causes allergic reactions

Ageratum conyzoides causes allergic reactions due to the presence of sesquiterpene lactones and polyacetylenes in its leaves, stems, and flowers.

These compounds can trigger an immune response in some individuals, leading to symptoms such as skin irritation, hives, and respiratory issues like wheezing and coughing.

The exact mechanism behind this allergic reaction is still not fully understood, but it is thought to be related to the plant's ability to release histamine and other chemical mediators that stimulate an immune response in susceptible people.

2. Triggers respiratory issues

Ageratum conyzoides triggers respiratory issues due to its high content of volatile oils, which can cause allergic reactions and irritation in the lungs.

When inhaled, these oils may lead to symptoms such as coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath, making it difficult for individuals to breathe normally.

Additionally, the plant's allergenic properties can exacerbate existing respiratory conditions like asthma or bronchitis.

3. Increases blood pressure

Ageratum conyzoides increases blood pressure due to its inherent vasopressor properties.

The plant contains compounds such as flavonoids and phenolic acids that stimulate the cardiovascular system, leading to an increase in blood pressure.

This effect is likely attributed to the ability of these compounds to cause vasoconstriction, which reduces blood flow and increases blood pressure.

4. Induces gastrointestinal upset

Ageratum conyzoides induces gastrointestinal upset when used.

The compound's saponins and flavonoids are thought to be responsible for this effect, irritating the mucous membranes of the stomach and intestines. This can lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain in some individuals who ingest the plant.

The exact mechanism behind these adverse reactions is unclear, but it is believed that the bioactive compounds interact with gut flora, altering the normal balance of intestinal microorganisms.

5. Aggravates skin conditions

Ageratum conyzoides aggravates skin conditions due to its allergenic properties.

The plant's leaves and stems contain a high concentration of sesquiterpenoids, which can cause an allergic reaction in some individuals.

This reaction can lead to inflammation, redness, and itching on the skin, potentially exacerbating existing conditions such as eczema or dermatitis.

6. Provokes nervous system problems

Ageratum conyzoides provokes nervous system problems in some individuals due to its high content of alkaloids, particularly ageratamine and eupolauridine.

These compounds can bind to receptors in the brain, causing a disruption in normal neurotransmitter function, leading to symptoms such as anxiety, agitation, and tremors.

Additionally, the plant's flavonoids may contribute to its potential neurotoxic effects by altering blood-brain barrier permeability and affecting the activity of certain enzymes.

7. Enhances cardiovascular risk

Ageratum conyzoides enhances cardiovascular risk due to its ability to raise blood pressure and heart rate through stimulation of the sympathetic nervous system.

This can lead to cardiac arrhythmias and increased cardiac workload, potentially exacerbating underlying conditions such as hypertension and coronary artery disease.

Furthermore, Ageratum conyzoides' effects on blood vessels may also contribute to increased peripheral resistance, further compounding cardiovascular risk.

8. Produces kidney damage

Ageratum conyzoides produces kidney damage due to its toxic properties.

The plant contains a compound called ageratumin, which can cause nephrotoxicity, leading to inflammation and scarring of the kidneys.

Additionally, blueweed's high concentration of flavonoids and terpenoids may exacerbate this effect, ultimately resulting in permanent damage to the kidney tissue.

9. Stimulates liver enzymes

Ageratum conyzoides stimulates liver enzymes.

This may occur due to the plant's ability to induce the production of certain enzymes, such as cytochrome P450, which play a crucial role in metabolizing xenobiotics and toxic substances. As a result, individuals using Ageratum conyzoides may experience altered metabolism rates, potentially leading to increased clearance of medications or other substances from their system.

The exact mechanisms behind this effect are still being researched, but it is believed to be related to the plant's unique chemical composition and its ability to interact with cellular enzymes.

10. Interferes with fertility

Ageratum conyzoides interferes with fertility by affecting hormone levels in the body.

The plant contains compounds that can alter estrogen and progesterone production, leading to irregular menstrual cycles, reduced libido, and difficulty getting pregnant.

Additionally, its estrogen-mimicking properties may cause an imbalance of sex hormones, resulting in fertility issues for both men and women who use it.
