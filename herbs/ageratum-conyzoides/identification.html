Whiteweed is an annual herb with a branched stem that grows upright or sprawling. Its roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in soil. Leaves are opposite, ovate to lanceolate, with hairy surfaces and serrated edges. The inflorescence is a terminal or axillary cluster of small, white to pale blue flowers. Each flower has five petals and is surrounded by bracts. The fruit is a small, dry capsule containing numerous tiny seeds.

Ageratum conyzoides can be distinguished by its opposite, ovate leaves with serrated edges and a hairy stem. It produces clusters of small, violet-blue flowers with white centers. Unlike similar species, it lacks a strong scent and has a fibrous root system. Its leaves are often covered in fine hairs, making it easily identifiable in the field.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Whiteweed (Ageratum conyzoides) is native to tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas. It thrives in warm, humid climates and prefers well-drained sandy or loamy soils. It is commonly found at low to moderate altitudes, often in disturbed areas like roadsides and fields.
