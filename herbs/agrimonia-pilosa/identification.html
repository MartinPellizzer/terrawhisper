Botanical Identification of Common Agrimony (Agrimonia pilosa)

To identify Common Agrimony (Agrimonia pilosa) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Common Agrimony, Agrimonia pilosa, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the flowering plants, eudicots, and the rose order. It is part of the rose family, genus Agrimonia, and the species Agrimonia pilosa is recognized for its medicinal properties in herbal medicine.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Common Agrimony is Agrimonia pilosa, first described by the Swedish botanist Carl Peter Thunberg in 1794. It is also known by several synonyms, including Agrimonia eupatoria and Eupatorium pilosum, reflecting historical taxonomic changes. Common names include Agrimony, St. John's Wort, and Burnet, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Common Agrimony is a herbaceous perennial plant with a branched root system. It has erect, slightly hairy stems that grow up to 1 meter tall. Its leaves are alternate, ovate, and serrated, with a rough texture. The plant bears a branched inflorescence of yellow flowers, each with five petals and numerous stamens. The fruit is a small, dry capsule containing several tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Common Agrimony can be distinguished by its branched, erect stems with rough hairs, serrated leaves with a central vein, and bright yellow flowers in clusters. It differs from similar species like St. John's Wort by its leaf shape and the absence of glandular dots on the stems. The seed capsules are also distinctive, being hairy and ribbed.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Common Agrimony is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained soils and is commonly found at low to mid-altitudes, often in open woodlands or along roadsides.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Common Agrimony blooms from June to August, producing bright yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is most identifiable during its flowering period, making late summer to early autumn the optimal time for accurate botanical recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Common Agrimony (Agrimonia pilosa) belongs to the genus Agrimonia in the family Rosaceae. It is closely related to species like Agrimonia eupatoria and Agrimonia sphaerocephala. Within the genus, it shares key characteristics such as stipitate stipules and persistent calyx lobes. These traits align with family-wide features of the Rosaceae, including rose-like flowers and pinnately compound leaves.
