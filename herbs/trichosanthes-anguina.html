Trichosanthes anguina

What's the taxonomical classification of Trichosanthes anguina?

Trichosanthes anguina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Taxonomically, it is a member of the order Cucurbitales and the family Cucurbitaceae. Within this family, it is identified by the genus Trichosanthes and is specifically categorized as the species anguina.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Trichosanthes anguina has a prostrate, climbing growth habit characterized by slender, herbaceous stems that can reach several meters in length through the use of tendrils. The leaves are typically cordate or kidney-shaped with smooth margins and long petioles that aid in climbing. Small, greenish-white flowers emerge from the leaf axils in clusters, often appearing inconspicuous to the casual observer. The plant produces elongated, cylindrical fruits that can become fleshy and somewhat tuberous as they mature. These botanical features collectively facilitate its rapid spreading across soil surfaces and other vegetation.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, spanning from India and China to Southeast Asia and parts of the Middle East. It thrives in warm, humid climates where seasonal rainfall is abundant. Many populations have been introduced to other tropical zones, including parts of Africa and the Americas, due to its use in agriculture. The species is frequently found in disturbed soils, garden plots, and cultivated fields. Because it is an annual climber, its presence is often tied to areas with sufficient sunlight and moisture.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including flavonoids, alkaloids, and various terpenoids. These flavonoids serve as potent antioxidants that help neutralize harmful free radicals within the body. Additionally, specific alkaloids present in the tissues may provide significant analgesic or anti-inflammatory effects. The presence of terpenoids further enhances the plant's ability to support immune system functions. Together, these chemical constituents contribute to the complex therapeutic profile of the species.

As discussed in a study, Trichosanthes anguina contains bioactive xanthine oxidase inhibitors within its ethanol extract, which demonstrated an IC50 of 383.50 ± 11.98 μg/mL. A ligand-fished fraction from the plant showed significantly enhanced inhibition with an IC50 of 77.17 ± 6.80 μg/mL. UPLC-Q-TOF-MS/MS analysis identified eleven flavonoids within the plant, six of which serve as potential xanthine oxidase inhibitors. Molecular docking of these compounds showed strong binding affinities ranging from -6.6 to -8.3 kcal/mol. These medicinal compounds from Trichosanthes anguina effectively reduce uric acid production in HK-2 cell-based models.
