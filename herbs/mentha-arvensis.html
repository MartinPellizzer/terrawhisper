Mentha arvensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Mentha arvensis?

Mentha arvensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Lamiaceae. At the most specific levels of its biological classification, it is identified as being within the genus Mentha, with the specific epithet arvensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Mentha arvensis has square, herbaceous stems that typically reach heights of 10 to 60 centimeters and bear opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves with serrated margins. The leaves are characterized by a dull green color and a slightly rugose surface texture. Small, tubular flowers in shades of pale pink or white emerge from compact, terminal clusters known as verticillasters. These flowers are supported by a calyx that is distinctly ribbed and possesses teeth of varying lengths. The plant spreads extensively through the production of underground runners called rhizomes.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Europe and parts of temperate Asia, extending across much of the continent from the British Isles to western Russia and into regions of North Africa. It commonly inhabits moist, disturbed soils throughout these territories, often colonizing riverbanks, damp meadows, and agricultural fields. While its primary range is centered in the Palearctic realm, certain populations have been introduced to other regions through human activity. What are the pharmacological activities of Mentha arvensis?

This plant has antibacterial, antifungal, antioxidant, antispasmodic, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities driven by its diverse phytochemical profile. The presence of essential oils like menthone and limonene contributes significantly to its ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. Additionally, the plant exhibits antispasmodic effects that can help relax smooth muscles and alleviate gastrointestinal discomfort. These combined therapeutic actions make Mentha arvensis a subject of interest for developing natural medicinal treatments.

According to a study published by "New microbes and new infections", Mentha arvensis was evaluated for its antibacterial activity against Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) causing pathogens including Staphylococcus spp., Proteus spp., Pseudomonas spp., Escherichia coli and Enterobacter spp. The research utilized the solvent extraction method, conventional cell culture technique, minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) assay, and disc diffusion method to analyze the plant. While ethanol and hot water extracts exhibited minimum inhibitory effects, the methanol extract showed very potent antibacterial activity with an inhibitory zone diameter up to 14 mm. When combined with commercially available antibiotics, the zone diameter increased up to 26 mm. This significant improvement suggests that combining the natural agent with a synthetic one is more efficient than a single monoterapia.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the essential oils menthol, menthone, and methy acetate along with the phenolic acid rosmarinic acid and various flavonoids. The presence of menthol and menthone provides the characteristic cooling sensation and aromatic profile used in many therapeutic applications. Menthy acetate further contributes to the complex chemical composition of the plant's volatile oils. Beyond these oils, rosmarinic acid serves as a potent antioxidant within the plant's structure. Additionally, the diverse group of flavonoids offers significant anti-inflammatory and protective biological activities.

According to a study published by "Journal of environmental management", Mentha arvensis is an aromatic crop of great economic importance due to the essential oil present in its leaf. This plant contains phytochemicals such as menthol at 12.08%, menthone at 40.07%, and menthyl acetate at 33.63%. Exposure to simulated acid rain at pH 3.0 significantly reduced these phytochemical levels. At this acidity level, antioxidant activities increased, including CAT at 144.71%, POX at 260.37%, SOD at 255.15%, and APX at 213.51%. Other increased components at pH 3.0 included MDA content at 193.97%, proline content at 607.18%, H2O2 content at 163.69%, O2•- content at 185.82%, and EL percentage at 57.23%.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for various gastrointestinal disorders including abdominal colic, gastritis, gastric ulcer, and peptic ulcer. Its therapeutic properties help soothe the digestive tract and alleviate discomfort associated with stomach inflammation. By reducing spasms, it effectively manages the cramping pain often found in abdominal colic. Furthermore, the herb provides relief for patients suffering from erosions in the stomach lining like gastric and peptic ulcers. Regular use can assist in stabilizing the digestive system against these specific ailments.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Mentha arvensis is used to treat gastrointestinal disorders associated with Helicobacter pylori, such as gastritis, peptic ulcer, and gastric cancer. The study evaluated the anti-inflammatory and cytoprotective effects of the plant on gastric epithelial cells. Extracts of Mentha arvensis exhibited strong inhibitory activity against IL-8 secretion at a concentration of 100μg/ml. These findings may lead to the use of the plant as a candidate for chemoprevention against peptic ulcer or gastric cancer.
