Ficus simplicissima

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus simplicissima?

Ficus simplicissima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Rosales. The plant is further categorized into the family Moraeceae and is situated within the genus Ficus, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species simplicissima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus simplicissima has simple, elliptical to oblong leaves that are typically 5 to 15 centimeters long with entire margins and an acute apex.

The foliage is characterized by a glossy, dark green upper surface and a paler underside. These leaves are arranged alternately along woody, branching stems that form a dense, shrubby or small tree-like structure. The plant produces small, inconspicuous syconia, which are the specialized inflorescences typical of the genus.

These fruit-like structures develop directly from the leaf axils or on specialized branches.

        What are the herbal preparations of Ficus simplicissima?

        Ficus simplicissima is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste derived from its leaves and bark. These liquid preparations are typically brewed by boiling the plant material in water to extract its active chemical constituents. Infusions are often used for milder therapeutic purposes where gentle extraction is required. The plant material can also be processed into a concentrated paste for direct application to the skin. These various forms allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended medicinal use.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Ficus simplicissima can cause skin irritation, respiratory distress, and digestive upset due to the presence of irritating milky sap known as latex. When the sap comes into direct contact with the skin, it often triggers contact dermatitis characterized by redness, itching, or blistering. If the plant's sap is accidentally ingested, it may lead to severe gastrointestinal discomfort or inflammation of the mucous membranes. Inhaling fine particles or dust from the plant can also cause allergic reactions or irritation within the respiratory tract. Therefore, caution should be exercised when pruning or handling this species to avoid these physiological reactions.
