Indian madder

Rubia cordifolia

Ashwagandha is a prominent medicinal herb in Ayurvedic medicine, classified as a rasayana (rejuvenator). It is primarily utilized as an adaptogen to help the body manage physical and chemical stress.

Family
Solanaceae (Nightshade)

Native Region
India, Middle East, Africa

Part Used
Root, Leaf, Berry

Botanical Identification

Indian Madder, scientifically known as Rubia cordifolia, belongs to the Rubiaceae family. Commonly called Kshudra in India and Red Sandal in parts of Southeast Asia, it is a perennial herb native to the Himalayas and South Asia. The plant has heart-shaped leaves, red flowers, and produces red-orange roots used in traditional medicine. It is also known as Rubia tinctorum in some herbal texts.

Active Compounds

Indian Madder contains key bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds work synergistically to provide anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, supporting its use in traditional herbal medicine.

  • Flavonoid
  • Tannin
  • Phenolic acid

Therapeutic Indications

System Condidtion Action

Respiratory Respiratory infections, cough, respiratory tract infections, bronchitis, asthma Antimicrobial, expectorant
Muscular Inflammation Anti-inflammatory

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for digestive issues and as a mild sedative.

Decoction: Used for treating skin conditions and respiratory issues.

Tincture: Used for pain relief and as an antiseptic.

Safety Profile

Indian Madder is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses. However, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals. Prolonged use or high doses could lead to skin irritation or other adverse effects, so caution is advised.
