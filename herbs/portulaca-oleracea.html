Portulaca oleracea

What's the taxonomical classification of Portulaca oleracea?

Portulaca oleracea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Caryophyllales. This plant is a representative of the family Portulacaceae, specifically situated within the genus Portulaca, with its unique biological identity defined by the species name oleracea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Portulaca oleracea has succulent, cylindrical to spatulate leaves that are fleshy, smooth, and arranged alternately along its prostrate or ascending stems. These stems are typically reddish or green and exhibit a highly branched growth habit that allows the plant to spread across the soil surface. The leaves serve as primary water storage organs, making the plant highly adapted to arid environments. During the flowering stage, the plant produces small, solitary flowers that feature five bright yellow petals. These blooms are often ephemeral, opening in response to sunlight and closing during overcast weather or at night.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region and parts of North Africa, but it has since become a widespread cosmopolitan species found on every continent except Antarctica. It thrives in various climates ranging from temperate to tropical, often colonizing disturbed soils and coastal areas. Due to its high tolerance for drought and salinity, it frequently establishes itself in sandy environments and wasteland habitats. Many populations are now considered invasive in many parts of the world, including North America and Australia. Its ability to spread through both seed and vegetative fragments allows it to inhabit a diverse array of global ecosystems.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Portulaca oleracea contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its succulent leaves, fleshy stems, and entire aerial portions of the plant. The leaves are frequently utilized in topical applications to treat skin inflammations and minor wounds due to their mucilaginous properties. Additionally, the stems and leaves are consumed or processed into extracts to harness their high concentrations of omega-3 fatty acids and antioxidants. These various parts are often prepared as poultices, juices, or decoctions to address digestive issues and systemic inflammation. What are the therapeutic actions of Portulaca oleracea?

Portulaca oleracea has anti-inflammatory, astringent, diuretic, mucilaginous, demulcent, and emollient therapeutic actions that make it useful for treating skin irritations and internal inflammation. Its mucilaginous properties allow it to act as a soothing demulcent, coating irritated mucous membranes to provide relief. The plant also functions as an emollient, softening the skin and restoring moisture to dry or damaged tissues. Because of its astringent qualities, it helps to tone and tighten the skin while reducing excessive secretions. Additionally, its diuretic effects support the body by promoting the excretion of fluids through the renal system.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds berberine, apiapigenin, kaempferol, rutin, and various flavonoids. These specific substances work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic benefits to the human body. Berberine is often recognized for its potent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of apiapigenin and kaempferol contributes to antioxidant activities that protect cells from oxidative stress. Additionally, rutin and other flavonoids support vascular health by strengthening capillary walls and improving circulation.

As discussed in a study, the petroleum ether extract of Portulaça oleracea contains active ingredients characterized by LC-MS including α-linolenic acid, linoleic acid, palmitic acid, β-sitosterol, and alkaloids such as berberine and magnoflorine. In vitro tests showed promising anticancer effects in A540 cell lines with an IC50 of 14.3 μg/mL, which is close to the reference drug doxorubicin at 13.8 μg/mL. An oral dose of 500 mg/kg/day for 20 weeks reduced the lung cancer biomarker ICAM-1 by about 18.75% compared to the cancer control. This extract demonstrated anti-proliferation by reducing Ki67 levels by 20.42% and anti-angiogenesis by decreasing MMP-9 expression by 79%. Furthermore, it showed anti-inflammation through a 62% decline in IGF-1 expression and a pro-apoptotic effect via a 262% activation in FOXO1 expression.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, diarrhea, stomach cramps, and vomiting if consumed in excessive amounts or by sensitive individuals. These gastrointestinal issues are the most commonly reported adverse reactions to the plant's ingestion. Some people may experience these digestive disturbances shortly after eating the succulent leaves or flowers. While often used in culinary contexts, the potential for such physical discomfort remains a documented concern. Monitoring your reaction to the plant is essential to avoid severe stomach distress.
