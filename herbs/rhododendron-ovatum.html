Rhododendron ovatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Rhododendron ovatum?

Rhododendron ovatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Ericales, it is a member of the family Ericaceae. Finally, it is categorized under the genus Rhododendron, with its specific designation being the species ovatum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rhododendron ovatum has elliptical to ovate leaves that are leathery, dark green, and approximately 5 to 12 centimeters in length. The plant is a woody evergreen shrub that typically reaches a height of up to 2 meters. Its flowers are funnel-shaped and vary in color from pale pink to deep rose, often featuring distinct spots on the upper petals. The inflorescence consists of small clusters of blooms that emerge from terminal buds during the spring season. Small, woody stems support these leaves and flowers, providing a dense, bushy structure to the plant.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several bioactive medicinal compounds including beta-sitosterol, betulinic acid, quercetin, quercetin-3-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranoside, and taraxerol. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the various pharmacological properties attributed to the Rhododendron ovatum species. The presence of these sterols and flavonoids suggests significant potential for antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Researchers often study these constituents to understand how they interact with biological systems. Identifying these precise chemical markers helps in evaluating the plant's overall therapeutic value.

According to a study published by "Yao xue xue bao = Acta pharmaceutica Sinica", seven compounds were isolated and purified from Rhododendron ovatum Planch. using silica gel column chromatography. These chemical constituents include 3,5,7-trihydroxylchromone 3-O-beta-D-xylopyranoside (I), taraxerol (II), beta-sitosterol (III), betulinic acid (IV), quercetin (V), quercetin-3-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranoside (VI), and D-glucose (VII). Compound I is identified as a new compound. Furthermore, compounds II, III, IV, V, VI, and VII were isolated from this plant for the first time.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe toxicity through the ingestion of grayanotoxins, leading to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dizziness, confusion, cardiac arrhythmia, seizures, and hypotension. These toxins interfere with the sodium channels in cell membranes, which disrupts the electrical signaling required for normal body functions. The resulting cardiovascular distress can manifest as a dangerous drop in blood pressure or irregular heartbeats. Neurological complications may also arise, potentially causing disorientation or uncontrollable physical tremors. Prompt medical intervention is necessary if someone consumes any part of this species.
