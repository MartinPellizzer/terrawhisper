Quisqualis caudata

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Quisqualis caudata has long, slender, creeping rhizomes that produce upright, unbranched stems reaching heights of up to 15 centimeters. The leaves are simple, alternate, and elliptical to lanceolate in shape, often featuring smooth margins. Each leaf is supported by a distinct petiole that connects the blade to the central stem. The reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These plants typically develop a dense, mat-like growth habit due to their spreading underground root systems.

By binding to these receptors, the plant's alkaloids trigger the release of neurotransmitters like dopamine and acetylcholine in the central nervous system. This interaction modulates cholinergic signaling pathways which are critical for cognitive function and motor control.     What are the herbal preparations of Quisqualis caudata?

    Quisqualis caudata is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture to extract its medicinal properties. The dried plant material is typically boiled in water to create a potent decoction for internal use. Alternatively, steeping the leaves in hot water results in a gentler infusion suitable for milder applications. For long-term storage and concentrated dosing, the plant is often macerated in alcohol to produce a tincture. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the strength and delivery of the herb's active compounds.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Quisqualis caudata can cause severe neurological symptoms such as muscle spasms, tremors, seizures, and loss of consciousness due to its high concentration of toxic alkaloids. These substances act as potent neurotoxins that disrupt the central nervous system upon ingestion. Symptoms often manifest rapidly after consumption and may include intense nausea or vomiting. In extreme cases, the physiological distress can lead to respiratory failure or death. Careful avoidance of this plant is necessary to prevent life-threatening toxic reactions.
