English Daisy (Bellis perennis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

English Daisy, or Bellis perennis, is a small, daisy-like flowering plant known for its medicinal properties and historical use in herbal medicine.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is English Daisy (Bellis Perennis)?

English Daisy, or Bellis perennis, is a low-growing perennial herb native to Europe, characterized by its daisy-like white flowers with a yellow center and a rosette of spoon-shaped leaves.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of English Daisy is Bellis perennis. This plant belongs to the family Asteraceae, order Asterales, class Magnoliopsida, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. It is commonly known as English Daisy in the United Kingdom and as Common Daisy in North America. Synonyms for this plant include Bellis dentata and Bellis annua, with regional names such as Mauve Daisy in France and Kleine Mauve in Germany.

Plant Description and Morphology

English Daisy is a low-growing perennial herb that typically reaches heights of 10 to 30 centimeters, with a rosette-like structure formed by basal leaves.

Its leaves are spoon-shaped, with a smooth or slightly toothed margin, and are arranged alternately along the stem. The flowers are composite, consisting of a central yellow disc surrounded by white to pale yellow ray florets, giving the plant its characteristic daisy appearance. The root system is a fibrous network that anchors the plant and facilitates nutrient absorption from the soil.

The plant has a mild, slightly sweet scent, with leaves that are soft and hairy to the touch, and flowers that are delicate and papery in texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

The English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, and is commonly found in meadows, grasslands, and along roadsides in temperate regions.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The entire plant, including the leaves, flowers, and roots, is used medicinally in traditional herbal practices.

Phytochemical Composition

The English Daisy (Bellis perennis) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) exhibits pharmacological properties that include anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and mild analgesic effects, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, and mucilage.

Mechanisms of Action

The English Daisy (Bellis perennis) exerts its effects through the presence of compounds such as sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, and phenolic acids, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and mild analgesic properties, supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to support digestive health, alleviate mild respiratory issues, and promote skin healing due to its anti-inflammatory and mild antiseptic properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

English Daisy is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or poultice, with the flowers harvested at full bloom and used fresh or dried for optimal potency.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of English Daisy (Bellis perennis) for herbal use typically ranges from 200 to 500 mg per day, either as a standardized extract or in a tincture form, with specific concentrations and preparation methods varying based on the intended therapeutic application.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is generally considered safe, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort or skin irritation in some individuals, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with known allergies to the Asteraceae family.

Drug Interactions

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) may interact with anticoagulant medications due to its potential mild anticoagulant properties, which could increase the risk of bleeding when combined with drugs like warfarin or aspirin.

Toxicity and Precautions

While English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy and lactation has not been fully established, so it should be used with caution and under professional guidance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is typically cultivated in temperate climates, preferring well-drained soil and partial to full sunlight, with harvesting of its leaves and flowers occurring in late spring to early summer for use in herbal preparations.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of English Daisy (Bellis perennis) typically involve harvesting the flowers and leaves during the early flowering stage, followed by drying to preserve active compounds, and then using methods such as maceration or distillation to isolate essential oils and phytochemicals for medicinal use.

Storage and Shelf Life

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) should be stored in a cool, dry, and dark place in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent moisture exposure, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Bellis perennis has primarily focused on its traditional uses in herbal medicine, with studies investigating its potential anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antimicrobial properties, though clinical evidence supporting its efficacy in human applications remains limited.

Traditional and Historical Uses

English Daisy has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, wound-healing, and mild sedative properties, often applied topically for skin irritations and internally for digestive support.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is not classified as a controlled substance and is generally permitted for use in herbal medicine, though specific regulations may vary depending on local pharmaceutical and botanical laws.
