English Daisy (Bellis perennis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is a common flowering plant native to Europe, known for its white petals and yellow center, and has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to treat various ailments, including skin conditions and inflammation.

What is English Daisy (Bellis Perennis)?

English Daisy, or Bellis perennis, is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, characterized by its daisy-like white petals and yellow center, commonly used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory and wound-healing properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

English Daisy, or Bellis perennis, is a low-growing perennial herbaceous plant native to Europe, belonging to the Asteraceae family and characterized by its daisy-like white flower with a yellow center.

Common Names and Synonyms

The English Daisy, scientifically known as Bellis perennis, is commonly referred to by several names including English Daisy, Lawn Daisy, and Common Daisy, and is also occasionally called the Mary's Mound or the Devil's Bit.

Plant Description and Morphology

The English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is a low-growing perennial herb with a rosette of basal, spoon-shaped leaves and daisy-like flowers consisting of a yellow central disk surrounded by white to pinkish ray florets.

Native Habitat and Distribution

The English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northwestern Africa, and it is commonly found in meadows, grasslands, and along roadsides in temperate regions.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and roots of English Daisy (Bellis perennis) are used medicinally, with the flowers being the most commonly utilized part for their anti-inflammatory, astringent, and mild analgesic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

The phytochemical composition of English Daisy (Bellis perennis) includes a variety of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, terpenoids, and mucilage, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine for digestive and inflammatory support.

Pharmacological Properties

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) exhibits various pharmacological properties, including anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antimicrobial, and antispasmodic effects, which are attributed to its bioactive compounds such as sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, and phenolic acids.

Mechanisms of Action

The English Daisy (Bellis perennis) exerts its effects primarily through the presence of compounds such as sesquiterpene lactones, flavonoids, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

English Daisy is used to treat digestive issues, skin irritations, respiratory infections, menstrual cramps, and as a diuretic to support kidney function.

Preparation Methods and Forms

English Daisy is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or poultice, with the flowers harvested at full bloom and dried for use in herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of English Daisy for herbal use is 2 to 4 grams of dried herb per day, divided into two or three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic application.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While English Daisy is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly those with known sensitivities to plants in the Asteraceae family.

Drug Interactions

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) may interact with certain medications due to its potential antiplatelet and anticoagulant properties, which could enhance the effects of blood-thinning drugs like warfarin or aspirin, increasing the risk of bleeding.

Toxicity and Precautions

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a herbal remedy, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and by those with known allergies to the Asteraceae family.

Cultivation and Harvesting

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is relatively easy to cultivate in well-drained soil and full to partial sunlight, requiring minimal care and thriving in temperate climates.

Processing and Extraction

Processing involves drying and grinding the dried flowers into a fine powder, which is then used in herbal preparations to harness its medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

English Daisy (Bellis perennis) should be stored in a cool, dry, and dark place in a sealed container to maintain its potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on English Daisy (Bellis perennis) has explored its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties, with some studies suggesting its use in traditional herbal medicine for treating skin conditions and respiratory ailments.

Traditional and Historical Uses

English Daisy has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, wound-healing, and digestive properties, often prepared as a tea or tincture to treat ailments such as respiratory infections, skin irritations, and digestive discomfort.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, English Daisy (Bellis perennis) is not classified as a controlled substance and is generally permitted for use in herbal medicine, though specific regulations may vary by region and depend on the form and intended use of the plant.
