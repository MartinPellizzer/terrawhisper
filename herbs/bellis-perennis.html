Scientific and Botanical Profile

English Daisy, with botanical name Bellis perennis, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

It is also referred to by several common names, including Common Daisy, Shinleaf, Pennywort, Bellis, Field Daisy, and Penny-Witts, reflecting its widespread presence and historical significance. Native to Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa, this perennial herb is well adapted to a variety of soil types and climates. Morphologically, it features a low-growing, spreading stem with basal leaves that are heart-shaped and hairy, and it produces daisy-like flowers with white petals surrounding a yellow central disk.

The plant typically grows to a height of 10-30 cm, making it a popular choice for gardens and wildflower meadows.

History and Cultural Relevance

English Daisy was used in ancient times by various cultures for its medicinal and symbolic properties, with evidence of its use dating back to medieval Europe and even earlier in herbal traditions.

In traditional medicine systems, it was valued for its ability to treat skin conditions, wounds, and digestive ailments, with its flowers and leaves often brewed into teas or salves. The plant held cultural significance in folklore, where it was associated with purity, innocence, and the Virgin Mary, leading to its inclusion in floral arrangements and religious ceremonies. In rural communities, daisies were also used in folk remedies, such as applying crushed petals to soothe insect bites or as a natural remedy for respiratory issues.

Today, English Daisy remains relevant in herbal medicine, with its anti-inflammatory and antiseptic properties still being utilized in modern natural health practices.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Bellis perennis has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, with various forms available including fresh plant, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the roots for a longer period. Topical applications include using the fresh leaves as a poultice or applying a diluted essential oil for skin conditions. For adults, a typical dosage is 1-2 cups of tea daily, while children may use a diluted form under medical supervision.

It is recommended to use bellis perennis sparingly, with short-term use to avoid potential toxicity, and to consult a healthcare professional before prolonged use.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Bellis perennis can be used with caution due to its potential toxicity, particularly when consumed in large amounts or over extended periods.

While it has been historically used in traditional medicine, it contains toxic compounds such as pyrrolizidine alkaloids, which can cause liver damage and are contraindicated in long-term use. Side effects may include gastrointestinal upset, nausea, and in severe cases, liver failure, especially with chronic exposure. It should not be combined with other hepatotoxic herbs or medications, as it may increase the risk of liver injury. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid bellis perennis due to potential harm to the fetus or infant, and individuals with chronic illnesses, particularly liver disease, should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, it is recommended to use bellis perennis only under medical supervision, in minimal amounts, and for short durations, while avoiding its use altogether if there are known contraindications.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Bellis perennis grows best in well-drained, fertile soil that is slightly acidic to neutral, in full sun to partial shade, requiring regular watering during dry periods but avoiding waterlogging.

It thrives in temperate climates and is relatively low maintenance, needing occasional weeding and pruning to encourage dense growth and prevent overcrowding. The best time to harvest bellis perennis is in late summer to early autumn when the flowers are fully open and the leaves are mature, using clean scissors to cut the entire plant at the base to ensure regrowth. After harvesting, the plant should be dried in a cool, dark, and well-ventilated area to preserve its medicinal potency, then stored in airtight containers away from light and moisture.

Proper storage helps maintain the plant's active compounds, ensuring its effectiveness for medicinal use over time.
