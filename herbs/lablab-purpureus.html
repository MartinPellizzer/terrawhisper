Lablab purpureus

What's the taxonomical classification of Lablab purpureus?

Lablab purpureus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Fabales. It is a member of the family Fabaceae, which places it among the legumes, and is specifically identified within the genus Lablab as the species purpureus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lablab purpureus has a climbing or trailing habit with trifoliate leaves consisting of three ovate to lanceolate leaflets and papilionaceous flowers that are typically purple or white.

The stems are herbaceous and can reach significant lengths through the use of twining mechanisms. Each leaf features a small stipule at the base of the petiole and a fine pubescence on the surface. The inflorescences are arranged in racemes that emerge from the leaf axils.

Its pods are elongated and contain several seeds characterized by a hard, smooth coat.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Lablab purpureus targets various enzymes and receptors including cyclooxygenase-2, alpha-glucosidase, and certain inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal properties. Its bioactive compounds, such as flavonoids and polyphenols, inhibit the cyclooxygenase-2 enzyme to reduce prostaglandin synthesis and alleviate pain. The plant also acts on alpha-glucosidase to slow down carbohydrate digestion, which helps in managing blood glucose levels. Additionally, specific phytochemicals interact with tumor necrosis factor-alpha and other pro-inflammatory signaling pathways to mitigate oxidative stress. These diverse molecular interactions provide the plant with its documented antioxidant, anti-diabetic, and anti-inflammatory activities.

    What are the herbal preparations of Lablab purpureus?

    Lablab purpureus is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, pastes, or juice extracted from its various parts including the seeds, leaves, and roots. The seeds are frequently boiled to create a medicinal decoction used to treat ailments such as dysentery or intestinal issues. Leaves are often crushed into a fresh paste or steeped in water to form an infusion for topical applications on skin inflammations. In some traditional practices, the dried roots are ground into a fine powder to be mixed with water or honey for oral consumption. These diverse preparations allow practitioners to utilize the plant's specific phytochemical properties for different therapeutic purposes.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Lablab purpureus can cause cyanogenic glycoside poisoning if the seeds or unripe pods are consumed without proper detoxification through soaking, boiling, or fermenting. These toxic compounds release hydrogen cyanide in the body, which can lead to symptoms such as dizziness, headache, and nausea. In cases of severe ingestion, the chemical reaction may result in more critical issues like vomiting, abdominal pain, or even respiratory failure. It is essential to ensure all parts of the plant are thoroughly cooked to neutralize these harmful substances before eating. Proper preparation is the only way to safely include this legume in a diet.
