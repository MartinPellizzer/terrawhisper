Scientific and Botanical Profile

Lablab, with botanical name Lablab purpureus, is an annual leguminous plant belonging to the Fabaceae family, commonly known as the legume or pea family.

Native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, southern Asia, and parts of the Middle East, it has been cultivated for centuries for its nutritional value and adaptability to diverse climates. Morphologically, Lablab is a climbing or bushy plant with trifoliate leaves, purple or pink flowers, and pods containing 5-10 angular, flat, and often hairy seeds.

It is also known by numerous common names such as Hyacinth Bean, Lablab Bean, Bean Of Heaven, Turk's Turban, French Bean, Cluster Bean, Pigeon Pea, Bengal Gram, Cultivated Hyacinth Bean, Cattle Bean, Climbing Hyacinth Bean, and Toothbrush Bean, reflecting its widespread use in traditional cuisines and agricultural systems across different cultures.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Lablab purpureus has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms such as fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare it, one can make a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or create a decoction by boiling the leaves for a longer period. It can also be used as an infusion or applied topically for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried leaves per day, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

It is advised to use lablab purpureus sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Lablab purpureus can be a beneficial medicinal plant with a variety of traditional uses, including as a source of protein and for its potential anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

However, it is important to be aware of its potential side effects, which may include gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, and in some cases, hypoglycemia, particularly when consumed in large quantities. Lablab purpureus may interact with certain medications, such as antidiabetic drugs or blood thinners, potentially enhancing their effects and increasing the risk of adverse outcomes. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid using lablab purpureus due to insufficient evidence on its safety during these periods, as well as individuals with chronic illnesses, who should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with small doses, monitor for adverse reactions, and consult with a qualified healthcare professional before incorporating lablab purpureus into a treatment regimen.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Lablab purpureus grows best in well-drained, loamy soil that is rich in organic matter, with a pH range of 6.

0 to 7. 5, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in warm climates with moderate rainfall, needing consistent moisture during the growing season but being drought-tolerant once established. Regular watering, weed control, and occasional fertilization with a balanced NPK fertilizer will ensure healthy plant development. Harvesting should occur when the pods are fully mature but still tender, typically 60 to 90 days after planting, using pruning shears to cut the pods without damaging the plant.

To preserve its medicinal potency, the harvested pods should be thoroughly dried in a cool, shaded area, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain their nutritional and therapeutic properties.
