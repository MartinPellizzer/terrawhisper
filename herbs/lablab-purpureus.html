What is Lablab (Lablab Purpureus)?

Lablab purpureus, also known as hyacinth bean or lablab, is a leguminous plant native to Africa and widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its nutritional and therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Lablab is Lablab purpureus. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Lablab, and species Lablab purpureus. It is commonly known as hyacinth bean in South Asia, and as lablab or Egyptian beans in Africa. The plant is also referred to as bird's wing in Australia and as pinto bean in parts of the Americas. Synonyms include Vigna purpurea (botanical) and various regional names such as kala chana in India and mogo in East Africa.

Plant Description and Morphology

Lablab is an erect, herbaceous annual legume that grows up to 2 meters tall, with a sprawling, branching structure.

Its leaves are trifoliate, consisting of three ovate to lanceolate leaflets with serrated margins. The flowers are large, showy, and typically purple or violet in color, with a distinctive spurred shape at the base of the petals. The root system is well-developed, consisting of a thick, fleshy taproot with numerous lateral roots.

The plant has a distinct, somewhat sweet and earthy smell, with a rough, hairy texture on the stems and leaves.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Lablab is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, particularly across West and Central Africa, where it grows wild in various habitats.

It is also found in parts of southern Asia, including India and Southeast Asia, and has been introduced to other warm climates around the world. The plant thrives in regions with warm temperatures, moderate rainfall, and well-drained soils. It prefers full sun exposure and is commonly found in grasslands, fallow fields, and along roadsides.

Lablab is adapted to a range of ecological conditions, from dry to moist environments, as long as the soil is not waterlogged.
