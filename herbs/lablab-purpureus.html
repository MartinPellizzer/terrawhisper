Lablab purpureus

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lablab purpureus has a climbing or trailing habit with trifoliate leaves consisting of three ovate to lanceolate leaflets and papilionaceous flowers that are typically purple or white. The stems are herbaceous and can reach significant lengths through the use of twining mechanisms. Each leaf features a small stipule at the base of the petiole and a fine pubescence on the surface. The inflorescences are arranged in racemes that emerge from the leaf axils. Its pods are elongated and contain several seeds characterized by a hard, smooth coat.

    What are the herbal preparations of Lablab purpureus?

    Lablab purpureus is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, pastes, or juice extracted from its various parts including the seeds, leaves, and roots. The seeds are frequently boiled to create a medicinal decoction used to treat ailments such as dysentery or intestinal issues. These diverse preparations allow practitioners to utilize the plant's specific phytochemical properties for different therapeutic purposes.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Lablab purpureus can cause cyanogenic glycoside poisoning if the seeds or unripe pods are consumed without proper detoxification through soaking, boiling, or fermenting. These toxic compounds release hydrogen cyanide in the body, which can lead to symptoms such as dizziness, headache, and nausea. In cases of severe ingestion, the chemical reaction may result in more critical issues like vomiting, abdominal pain, or even respiratory failure. It is essential to ensure all parts of the plant are thoroughly cooked to neutralize these harmful substances before eating. Proper preparation is the only way to safely include this legume in a diet.
