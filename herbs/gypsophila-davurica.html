Gypsophila davurica

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gypsophila davurica has a perennial rootstock with slender, branched stems that typically reach heights of 20 to 40 centimeters. The leaves are primarily basal and lanceolate, while the cauline leaves are smaller and more sparsely distributed along the stem. Small, opposite leaves emerge from the stem to support the flowering structure. The inflorescence consists of dense, many-flowered cymes featuring delicate, five-petaled flowers. These blossoms are usually white or pale pink and possess a characteristic bladder-like calyx.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and mountainous regions of Central Asia, specifically spanning across Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, and Uzbekistan. It primarily inhabits dry, rocky slopes and steppe environments within these specific territories. The species is often found at varying elevations where soil conditions are sparse and well-drained. Its distribution is closely tied to the continental climates of the inland Asian landmass.
