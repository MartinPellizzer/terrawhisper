Philadelphus delavayi

What's the taxonomical classification of Philadelphus delavayi?

Philadelphus delavayi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Cornales. As a member of the family Hydrageaceae, it falls under the genus Philadelphus, with the specific designation of the species delavayi.

What are the therapeutic actions of Philadelphus delavayi?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various gastrointestinal ailments such as diarrhea and dysentery. It contains bioactive compounds that help soothe the digestive tract and reduce inflammation. Traditional medicinal practices often involve using extracts from the plant to manage stomach upsets and intestinal discomfort. Some herbal preparations also utilize the plant to address minor digestive irregularities. Scientific studies continue to explore its potential effectiveness in regulating bowel functions.

What are the herbal preparations of Philadelphus delavayi?

Philadelphus delavayi is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or topical poultices to harness its various phytochemical properties. The woody stems and leaves are often simmered in water to create a concentrated liquid medicine used for internal consumption. For external ailments, the crushed plant material is applied directly to the skin in a poultice format. Some traditional practitioners also utilize alcohol-based tinctures to extract specific active compounds for long-term storage. These diverse methods allow for the targeted application of the plant's medicinal components depending on the specific health concern.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Philadelphus delavayi can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals upon direct physical contact with its foliage or stems. While it is not classified as highly toxic, some people may experience redness, itching, or a mild rash after touching the plant. There is limited clinical data regarding the ingestion of this specific species, but many members of the Philadelphus genus are considered mildly toxic if eaten. Accidental consumption could potentially lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or nausea. It is always best to handle the shrub with gloves to prevent any unexpected dermatological reactions.
