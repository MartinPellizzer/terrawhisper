Cyperus alternifolius

What's the taxonomical classification of Cyperus alternifolius?

Cyperus alternifolius belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Poales and is a member of the family Cyperaceae. Ultimately, this plant is defined by its genus, Cyperus, and its specific species designation, alternifolius.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cyperus alternifolius has a tufted perennial growth habit characterized by long, linear, grass-like leaves that emerge from a creeping rhizome system. These leaves are typically bright green, narrow, and arranged in a spreading manner to maximize light absorption. The plant produces an inflorescence consisting of several small, brownish spikelets clustered at the top of a leafless stalk called a scape. This scape is triangular in cross-section, a defining feature of the Cyperaceae family. The individual flowers are inconspicuous and lack large petals, relying on wind for pollination.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

These specific chemical constituents contribute significantly to the plant's overall therapeutic potential and biological activity. The presence of aldehydes often provides distinct aromatic properties and antimicrobial qualities. Meanwhile, the phenolic acids serve as potent antioxidants that help combat oxidative stress in biological systems. Together, these bioactive substances work in synergy to support the plant's medicinal efficacy.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extracts of Cypterus alternifolius tubers and aerial parts were found to be more enriched in phenolic acids. The ethyl acetate extract of the aerial part was more abundant in 2 aldehydes. These bioactive compounds provide a mechanism of action through α-carbonic anhydrase inhibition, anti-inflammatory and analgesic activity by antioxidant activity, and the downregulation of several inflammatory mediators. The study investigated the efficacy of methanol or ethyl acetate extracts of tubers and aerial parts at 2 dose levels of 50 or 100 mg/kg. These extracts significantly reduced the ulcer number, total ulcer score, and TNF-α content in the stomach compared to the administration of 30 mg/kg of indomethacin.
