Carpesium longifolium

What's the taxonomical classification of Carpesium longifolium?

Carpesium longifolium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Asterales. It is further categorized into the family Asteraceae, which is a large group of flowering plants, and falls under the genus Carpesium. Ultimately, its specific biological identity is defined by its species designation, longifoliuum.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains the medicinal compounds xanthanolides and germacranolides which contribute to its therapeutic properties. These specific sesquiterpene lactones are often studied for their potential anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activities. The presence of xanthanolides can influence various biological pathways within a cellular environment. Meanwhile, germacranolides are recognized for their unique chemical structures that aid in medicinal research. Together, these bioactive constituents define the chemical profile and pharmacological utility of the species.

    As discussed in a study, three new xanthanolides, 1-3, were isolated from the aerial parts of Carpesium longifolium along with nine known compounds. The three new structures were elucidated as 1beta,4beta-epoxy-5beta-hydroxy-10alphaH-xantha-11(13)-en-12,8beta-olide (1), 1beta,4beta,4alpha,5beta-diepoxy-10alphaH,11alphaH-xantha-12,8beta-olide (2), and 4-acetoxy-1beta,5beta-epoxy-10alphaH-xantha-11(13)-en-12,8beta-olide (3). These compounds were identified using spectroscopic methods such as IR, EIMS, HRESIMS, and 1D and 2D NMR experiments. Additionally, parthenolide and michelenolide from the plant exhibited significant cytotoxic activity against cultured SMMC-7721 human hepatoma and HO-8910 human ovarian carcinoma cells.

        What side effects this plant can have?

        This plant can cause severe allergic skin reactions, gastrointestinal distress, and potential neurological impairment if ingested. Contact with the leaves or stems often leads to contact dermatitis, characterized by redness, itching, and blistering. If swallowed, individuals may experience intense abdominal cramping, nausea, and vomiting. Some cases of toxicity have also been linked to dizziness and temporary disorientation. Careful avoidance is necessary to prevent these adverse physiological responses.
