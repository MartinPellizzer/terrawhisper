Melilotus indicus

What's the taxonomical classification of Melilotus indicus?

Melilotus indicus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Melilotus, and its specific species name, indicus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Melilotus indicus has an erect or ascending herbaceous annual habit characterized by slender, branched stems that typically reach heights of 20 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are trifoliate, consisting of three ovate to elliptical leaflets with serrated margins and long petioles. Small, fragrant, pale yellow flowers are densely arranged in elongated, terminal racemes that emerge from the upper leaf axils. The plant produces small, flat, oval-shaped pods that turn brown and contain several tiny seeds upon maturity. Its root system is composed of a slender taproot that allows it to colonize disturbed soils effectively.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to various regions across Asia, Africa, and Europe, but it has become widely naturalized in many other parts of the world. It is commonly found growing in disturbed soils and along roadsides throughout much of the Mediterranean basin. In North America, the species is frequently encountered in temperate climates where it thrives in sunny, open habitats. Its ability to spread across different continents is largely attributed to its resilience and tendency to colonize agricultural lands. Consequently, it is now considered a cosmopolitan weed in many subtropical and temperate environments globally.

What are the pharmacological activities of Melilotus indicus?

This plant has demonstrated diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antifungal, antioxidant, and cytotoxic properties. Its antioxidant capabilities help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also exhibits potent antibacterial and antifungal actions that can inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory effects assist in modulating immune responses to reduce swelling and pain. Research also suggests that its cytotoxic potential may play a role in inhibiting the proliferation of certain cancer cell lines.

According to a study published by "BMC complementary and alternative medicine", the methanolic extracts of Melilotus indicus were evaluated for antioxidant, antibacterial, antifungal, cytotoxic, and anti-inflammatory potential. In terms of cytotoxicity, the plant ranked fifth in the study following TTF, TTL, AI, and CP. Regarding in vitro anti-inflammatory potential, Melilotus indicus was ranked lowest among the tested extracts when evaluated at a concentration of 200 μg/mL. The study also investigated the plant's antimicrobial capabilities through various assays including the agar well diffusion method and the microdilution method.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating infected wounds, reducing inflammation, and managing digestive issues. Its medicinal properties help to cleanse skin lesions and prevent the spread of infection in damaged tissue. When applied topically, the plant acts as an anti-inflammatory agent to soothe swelling and redness. Furthermore, it is often utilized in traditional medicine to address internal ailments like stomach discomfort. These diverse applications make it a valuable resource in various herbal remedies.

According to a study published by "International journal of biological macromolecules", Melilotus indicus polysaccharide was successfully isolated and used for infected wound healing activity. The study evaluated the polysaccharide in films, such as P2, which demonstrated a swelling index of 92.52 ± 7.41 %, a bioadhesion force of 25.38 ± 1.23 mN, a tensile strength of 21.00 ± 4.24 kg/mm2, and an elongation of 2.50 ± 0.71 %. When incorporated into a composite laminated device, the Melilotus indicus polysaccharide showed the highest % wound contraction. This composite film also demonstrated the highest effective antibacterial activity compared to P2, F7, and Fucidin cream®. The treatment of infected wounds with this device resulted in a superior healing outcome.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion by steeping the dried flowering tops in hot water to extract its aromatic properties. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe health complications due to coumarin toxicity, which may lead to internal bleeding and organ damage. Consuming the species often results in gastrointestinal distress, specifically manifesting as nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. Some individuals may also experience neurological symptoms such as dizziness after ingestion. These adverse reactions highlight the significant risks associated with its chemical composition.
