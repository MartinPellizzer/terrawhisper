Melilotus indicus

What's the taxonomical classification of Melilotus indicus?

Melilotus indicus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Melilotus, and its specific species name, indicus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Melilotus indicus has an erect or ascending herbaceous annual habit characterized by slender, branched stems that typically reach heights of 20 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are trifoliate, consisting of three ovate to elliptical leaflets with serrated margins and long petioles. Small, fragrant, pale yellow flowers are densely arranged in elongated, terminal racemes that emerge from the upper leaf axils. The plant produces small, flat, oval-shaped pods that turn brown and contain several tiny seeds upon maturity. Its root system is composed of a slender taproot that allows it to colonize disturbed soils effectively.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to various regions across Asia, Africa, and Europe, but it has become widely naturalized in many other parts of the world. It is commonly found growing in disturbed soils and along roadsides throughout much of the Mediterranean basin. In North America, the species is frequently encountered in temperate climates where it thrives in sunny, open habitats. Its ability to spread across different continents is largely attributed to its resilience and tendency to colonize agricultural lands. Consequently, it is now considered a cosmopolitan weed in many subtropical and temperate environments globally.

What are the pharmacological activities of Melilotus indicus?

This plant has demonstrated diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antifungal, antioxidant, and cytotoxic properties. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize harmful free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative stress. Research indicates that its extracts possess potent antibacterial and antifungal capabilities against various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, the plant exhibits anti-inflammatory effects that can help reduce swelling and pain in biological tissues. Studies have also identified significant cytotoxic activities, suggesting potential applications in specialized medicinal therapies.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic extracts of Melilotus indicus were evaluated for their phytochemical composition, antioxidant activity, antibacterial potential, antifungal activities, cytotoxic potential, and in vitro anti-inflamatory potential. The plant was tested for antibacterial effectiveness using the agar well diffusion method against Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Klebsiella pneumonia, and Acinetobacter baumannii. Its antifungal activity was assessed against three species, specifically Aspergillus fumigatus, Aspergillus flavus, and Rhizopus oryzae. In terms of cytotoxicity, Melilotus indicus was ranked last in the study following the order of TTF > TTL > AI > CP > MI. Furthermore, when evaluating in vitro anti-inflamatory potential at a concentration of 200 μg/mL, Melilotus indicus was ranked lowest among the tested extracts.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

As discussed in a study, the Egyptian Melilotus indicus polysaccharide was successfully isolated to be incorporated into a bioadhesive film. From the formulated Melilotus indicus polysaccharide films, the P2 version showed a swelling index of 92.52 ± 7.41 %. This specific P2 film also demonstrated a bioadhesion force of 25.38 ± 1.23 mN, a tensile strength of 21.00 ± 4.24 kg/mm2, and an elongation of 2.50 ± 0.71 %. The study utilized these properties to develop a composite laminated film for efficient infected wound healing activity.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion by steeping the dried flowering tops in hot water to extract its aromatic properties. A decoction is made by boiling the harder parts of the plant, such as the roots, in water for an extended period to pull out tougher medicinal compounds. For a more concentrated dose, a tincture is created by soaking the plant material in alcohol to preserve its active constituents. The dried plant can also be ground into a fine powder to be mixed into foods or used in topical applications. These various methods allow for diverse ways to utilize the therapeutic potential of Melilotus indicus.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe health complications due to coumarin toxicity, which may lead to internal bleeding and organ damage. Consuming the species often results in gastrointestinal distress, specifically manifesting as nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. Some individuals may also experience neurological symptoms such as dizziness after ingestion. These adverse reactions highlight the significant risks associated with its chemical composition.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Coumarin toxicity
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Abdominal pain
  • Diarrhea
  • Dizziness
