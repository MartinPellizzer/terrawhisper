Cephalotaxus oliveri

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cephalotaxus olivei has small, leathery, lanceolate leaves that are arranged spirally along its woody, evergreen branches. These leaves typically measure between 1 and 3 centimeters in length and possess an acute apex with an entire margin. The foliage exhibits a deep green color on the upper surface and a slightly paler shade underneath. Its reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous cones rather than showy flowers. The plant develops fleshy, red-colored arils that surround the seeds once they reach maturity.

Within these habitats, the species typically inhabits hilly or mountainous terrain with well-drained soils. Its distribution is relatively localized to specific humid climatic zones in East Asia. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Researchers study these molecules because they can disrupt microtubule formation in rapidly multiplying cancer cells. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Cephalotaxus oliveri targets tubulin proteins to inhibit microtubule polymerization, a mechanism primarily driven by its high concentration of vinca alkaloids. These alkaloids bind specifically to tubulin subunits, preventing the assembly of the mitotic spindle necessary for cell division. By disrupting this structural process, the plant's compounds induce cell cycle arrest during the metaphase stage. This targeted interference leads to programmed cell death in rapidly proliferating cancer cells. Consequently, these molecular interactions provide the foundation for the plant's significant potential in developing chemotherapeutic agents.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Cephalotaxus oliveiri can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential organ toxicity if ingested due to its high concentration of potent alkaloids.
