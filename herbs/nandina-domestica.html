Nandina domestica

What's the taxonomical classification of Nandina domestica?

Nandina domestica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its evolutionary lineage continues through the order Ranunculales and the family Berberidaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by the genus Nandina and is specifically identified as the species domesticus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Nandina domestica has compound, pinnate leaves that consist of multiple small, lanceolate leaflets arranged symmetrically along a central stem. These leaves often exhibit striking color shifts from bright green to shades of red or bronze depending on the season and temperature. The plant produces upright, woody stems that can reach several feet in height. Small, clusters of white flowers emerge in the spring from the terminal ends of the branches. Following the bloom, the plant develops bright red berries that persist on the foliage throughout much of the winter.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the eastern regions of Asia, specifically spanning across China, Japan, and Korea. While its natural habitat is limited to these East Asian territories, it has been widely introduced to other parts of the world for ornamental use. In many temperate climates, such as those in North America and Europe, it grows easily in gardens and landscaping. Because it is hardy and adaptable, it has naturalized in several non-native locations outside of its original range. Its ability to thrive in diverse soil types has facilitated its global spread through the horticultural trade.

This plant has a diverse range of pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anticancer, anticonvulsant, antileishmanial, and cytotoxic properties. What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating adenoma, cancer, carcinoma, colorectal cancer, and various types of tumors. Research into its bioactive compounds suggests potential therapeutic applications in managing abnormal cell growths. Some studies investigate how its chemical properties might inhibit the progression of malignant tumors within the body. These medicinal qualities are often explored in the context of fighting specific forms of carcinoma. Scientists continue to examine its efficacy in addressing complex conditions like colorectal cancer and adenoma.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", the fruits of Nandina domestica Thunb. have served as folk medicines in Chinese and Japanese tradition for the treatment of several tumors, including pharynx tumor and tooth abscess. Through network pharmacology-based analysis, 22 compounds were identified, with 5 compounds and 4 molecular targets, specifically AKT1, CASP3, MAPK1, and TP53, being enriched in the network. Pathway analysis revealed that 15 cancer-related pathways were enriched, including colorectal cancer, endometrial cancer, and small-cell lung cancer. The study indicates that the constituents affect apoptosis and Akt-signaling pathways during early and intermediate adenoma through CASP3 and MAPK1 (ErC2) targets. During the stages of late adenoma and carcinoma, these compounds act through the p53 and ErbB signaling pathways.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, skin irritation, and dizziness if ingested or handled improperly. The berries and leaves contain toxic alkaloids that disrupt the digestive system upon consumption. Contact with the foliage may also lead to localized skin irritation for sensitive individuals. Some people may experience dizziness after accidental exposure to its chemical compounds. These adverse reactions highlight the necessity of exercising caution around all parts of the plant.
