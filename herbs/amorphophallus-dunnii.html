Amorphophallus dunnii

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Amorphophallus dunnii has a large underground corm that produces a single, massive unifoliate leaf consisting of a long petiole and a complexly divided leaf blade. The petiole is thick and fleshy, functioning as a primary storage organ for nutrients. The leaf blade features multiple leaflets that are deeply lobed or pinnately divided to increase surface area. During its flowering stage, the plant produces a large, fleshy spadix surrounded by a spathe that often emits a strong odor. This morphology is characteristic of the Araceae family and is adapted for efficient nutrient storage and specialized pollination strategies.

      Amorphophallus dunnii is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a crushed paste for medicinal use. The tubers are typically harvested and dried to facilitate the creation of fine powders.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Amorphophallus dunnii can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if the plant parts are ingested. Like many members of its genus, this plant contains calcium oxalate crystals that act as microscopic needles upon contact with sensitive tissues. These crystals can trigger intense burning sensations, swelling, and redness in the mouth or on the skin. If swallowed, the substance may lead to vomiting, abdominal pain, or inflammation of the throat. Careful handling is necessary to prevent accidental contact with the eyes or mucous membranes.
