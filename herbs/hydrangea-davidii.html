Hydrangea davidii

What's the taxonomical classification of Hydrangea davidii?

Hydrangea davidii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Cornales and is a member of the family Hydrangeaceae. At the most specific levels of classification, this plant is identified by the genus Hydrangea and is specifically designated as the species davidii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hydrangea davidii has woody, deciduous stems that support large, oppositely arranged elliptical leaves with serrated margins and prominent venation.

The foliage typically displays a deep green color and can reach significant sizes depending on the growing environment. Large, terminal corymb inflorescences emerge in late summer, consisting of numerous small, fertile flowers surrounded by showy, sterile sepals. These flower heads often transition through various shades of pink, white, or light blue.

The overall structure of the plant tends to be an upright, spreading shrub with a multi-stemmed growth habit.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Hydrangea davidii contains no documented medicinal parts for human consumption or therapeutic use in traditional or modern pharmacology. While various species within the Hydrangea genus are studied for specific compounds, this particular variety lacks established clinical applications. Most plants in this family are considered toxic to humans and animals if ingested due to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides. These chemical compounds can release cyanide into the body, making the leaves, stems, and flowers potentially dangerous. Consequently, medical professionals do not recognize any part of this plant as a safe or effective remedy.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Hydrangea davidii can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested by humans or animals. The plant contains cyanogenic glycosides which may release cyanide when the tissues are chewed or swallowed. Contact with the leaves or stems can lead to contact dermatitis, characterized by redness, itching, or a burning sensation on the skin. Small amounts of ingestion might cause nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain in sensitive individuals. It is important to keep this plant out of reach of pets and children to avoid accidental poisoning.
