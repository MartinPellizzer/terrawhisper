Thyme (Thymus vulgaris)

Thyme (Thymus vulgaris) is a member of the Lamiaceae family, native to Mediterranean Region, Southern Europe, and Middle East. Traditionally, its leaves, flowers, and stems have been used for infusions, decoctions, and oil infusions.

This herb is particularly valued for its anti-inflammatory, carminative, and antispasmodic actions, and has a long history of use in european herbal medicine, mediterranean herbal traditions, and ayurvedic medicine.

Botanical Description

Growth Habit
Perennial herbaceous plant.
Height
It typically reaches a height of 30 to 60 centimeters.
Leaves
Broad leaves with upper surface gray-green and lower surface whitish, bearing stomatal bands along the midrib.
Flowers
Bilaterally symmetric flowers with pink to purple petals, arranged in dense spikes, featuring four stamens and a two-lobed stigma, with glandular-hair covered calyces.
Stems
Erect growth habit with opposite branching, hairy surface, and square cross-section.

Medicinal Actions

Anti-inflammatory
Traditionally described as a warming anti-inflammatory, for irritation-related applications.
Carminative
In herbal literature, noted as a moderate carminative, in stomach-related herbal uses.
Antispasmodic
As described in traditional systems, a calming antispasmodic, in smooth muscle contexts.
Expectorant
In herbal texts, considered a gentle expectorant, in respiratory system contexts.
