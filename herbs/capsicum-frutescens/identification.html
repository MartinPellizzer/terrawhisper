Botanical Identification of Chili Pepper (Capsicum frutescens)

To identify Chili Pepper (Capsicum frutescens) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Chili pepper, or Capsicum frutescens, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Solanales, family Solanaceae, genus Capsicum, and species Capsicum frutescens. This classification places it within the nightshade family, known for its medicinal and culinary significance.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name for chili pepper is Capsicum frutescens, first described by Carl Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the nightshade family and has several synonyms, including Capsicum annuum var. glabrum. Common names vary widely, such as bird's eye, habanero, and chili, while regional and historical names reflect its cultural significance across different areas.

Morphological Characteristics

Chili pepper plants (Capsicum frutescens) are annual herbs with a bushy growth habit. They have a fibrous root system that anchors the plant and absorbs nutrients. The stem is erect, hairy, and branched, supporting the foliage and flowers. Leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate, with serrated edges and a slightly pubescent surface. The inflorescence consists of a terminal cluster of flowers, each with five white petals and yellow stamens. The fruit is a fleshy, elongated berry that matures from green to red, containing numerous small seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Capsicum frutescens can be distinguished by its upright, branched stems, ovate leaves with serrated edges, and bright red or yellow berries. It differs from similar species like Capsicum annuum by its smaller, more pointed fruits and thinner, more hairy stems. The plant also has a distinct spicy aroma when the leaves are crushed.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Capsicum frutescens is native to the Americas, particularly Central and South America. It thrives in warm, tropical to subtropical climates. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It typically grows at altitudes between 500 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Chili peppers (Capsicum frutescens) typically bloom 60-90 days after planting, producing yellow or white flowers. Seed formation occurs 4-6 weeks after flowering. Harvest timing varies, but fruits are usually picked when they reach full color and size, typically 70-100 days after planting.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Capsicum frutescens belongs to the genus Capsicum, which is part of the Solanaceae family. It is closely related to species like Capsicum annuum and Capsicum baccatum. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, including the presence of alkaloids and the ability to produce capsaicin.
