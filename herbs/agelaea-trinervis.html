Agelaea trinervis

What's the taxonomical classification of Agelaea trinervis?

Agelaea trinervis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Oxalidales. This specific plant is situated within the family Connaraceae and is categorized under the genus Agelaea, ultimately identifying as the species trinervis.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Agelaea trinervis contains bioactive compounds primarily located within its leaves, bark, and roots that are utilized for their therapeutic properties. Extracts from the bark are frequently applied topically or ingested to address inflammatory conditions. Additionally, the roots are sometimes processed to serve as traditional remedies for systemic infections. These different components work together to provide a diverse range of medicinal applications in local traditional medicine.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Agelaea trinervis targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), lipoxygenase (LOX), and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to mediate its medicinal properties. The plant contains bioactive phytochemicals like terpenoids and flavonoids that act as natural inhibitors of these protein pathways. By modulating the expression of nitric oxide synthase (NOS), these compounds help regulate oxidative stress within biological systems. These interactions effectively suppress the production of mediators that trigger pain and swelling in the body. Such molecular interference explains the plant's traditional use in treating inflammatory conditions and febrile illnesses.

    What are the herbal preparations of Agelaea trinervis?

    Agelaea trinervis is prepared as a decoction of the leaves or as a crushed topical paste for medicinal applications.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Agelaea trinervis can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested or handled improperly. The specific toxicological profile of this species is not extensively documented in clinical literature, necessitating extreme caution. Potential contact with the plant's sap or leaves may trigger localized dermatitis in sensitive individuals. If consumed, the chemical constituents could lead to nausea, vomiting, or more severe systemic disturbances. Always consult a medical professional or toxicologist if exposure is suspected.
