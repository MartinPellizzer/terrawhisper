Botanical Identification of Ceylon Cinnamon (Cinnamomum zeylanicum)

To identify Ceylon Cinnamon (Cinnamomum zeylanicum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Ceylon Cinnamon (Cinnamomum zeylanicum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Ceylon Cinnamon, or Cinnamomum zeylanicum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Laurales, family Lauraceae, genus Cinnamomum, and species Cinnamomum zeylanicum. These classifications place it within the broader group of flowering plants and the Lauraceae family, which includes other aromatic woods and spices.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Cinnamomum zeylanicum is the scientific name for Ceylon Cinnamon. It was first described by the botanist Robert James Burkill in 1854. Common synonyms include Cinnamomum tamala and Cinnamomum verum. It is also known by regional names such as "dal" in Sri Lanka and "kasi" in India.

Morphological Characteristics

Ceylon Cinnamon, or Cinnamomum zeylanicum, is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree. It has a shallow root system that spreads widely, supporting its growth in tropical climates. The stem is straight and cylindrical, often reaching heights of up to 10 meters. The leaves are lance-shaped, dark green on top, and lighter underneath, with a strong aromatic scent.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Ceylon Cinnamon can be distinguished by its thin, papery bark that peels in strips, its small, oval leaves with a slightly serrated edge, and its unique aromatic scent. It also has a distinct flavor compared to other cinnamon types, and its inner bark is lighter in color and less dense. These features help differentiate it from related species like Cassia or Chinese cinnamon.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Ceylon Cinnamon is native to the tropical regions of Sri Lanka. It thrives in warm, humid climates with high rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils. It is typically found at altitudes between 600 and 1,800 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Ceylon Cinnamon blooms during late spring to early summer, typically between May and July. Seed formation occurs in the following autumn, around September to November. Harvesting is done when the bark is fully mature, usually in late winter to early spring, which is crucial for accurate identification.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Ceylon Cinnamon (Cinnamomum zeylanicum) is closely related to other cinnamon species within the Cinnamomum genus. It shares genetic and morphological traits with species like Cinnamomum verum and Cinnamomum loureiroi. Its phylogenetic position reflects its close ties to the Lauraceae family, characterized by aromatic leaves and essential oils.
