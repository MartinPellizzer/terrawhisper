Microcos paniculata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Malvales and the family Malvaceae, eventually reaching its specific classification under the genus Microcos and the species paniculata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Microcos paniculata has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 10 to 40 centimeters and features small, opposite, lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is characterized by a dull green color and a relatively smooth texture along the leaf blades. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal panicles that bear numerous tiny, inconspicuous flowers. These flowers are primarily actinomorphic and possess a reduced structure typical of the family. The plant also develops small, dry fruits that facilitate its dispersal within its natural habitat.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds isorrhamnetin-3-O-beta-D-glucoside, isovitexin, liriodenine, microcosamine A, and vitexin. These bioactive molecules contribute to the complex chemical profile of the species. Each compound offers unique phytochemical properties that researchers study for potential therapeutic applications. The presence of these specific flavonoids and alkaloids highlights the plant's diverse biochemical makeup. Understanding these constituents helps clarify how the plant interacts with biological systems.

As discussed in a study, the leaves of Microcos paniculata L. contain a polyphenol-enriched fraction that exhibits various biological functions, including antioxidant activity and α-glucosidase inhibitory effect. HPLC analysis revealed that this fraction contains 4 predominantly phenolic compounds, which are narcissin, isorrhamnetin-3-O-β-D-glucoside, isovitexin, and vitexin. These phenolic components contribute to strong free radical scavenging activities and help prevent H2O2-induced ROS production and apoptosis in HepG2/Hepa1-6 cells. The medicinal potential of these compounds is further demonstrated through their ability to abrogate the activation of proapoptotic proteins caspase-3/9 and Bax while restoring Bcl2 expression. Additionally, these compounds increase the nuclear accumulation of Nrf2 and elevate the hepatic expression of target genes NQO1 and HO-1.
