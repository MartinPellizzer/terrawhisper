Virginia Thyme (Thymus virginicus)

Virginia Thyme (Thymus virginicus) is a member of the Lamiaceae family, native to Eastern North America, Southeastern United States, and Atlantic Coastal Plain. Traditionally, its leaves, flowers, and stems have been used for infusions, decoctions, and poultices.

This herb is particularly valued for its antispasmodic, carminative, and tonic actions, and has a long history of use in european herbal medicine, mediterranean herbal traditions, and native american herbal medicine.

Botanical Description

Growth Habit
Perennial herbaceous plant.
Height
It typically grows to a height of 20 to 60 centimeters.
Leaves
Smooth, dorsally gray-green, ventrally whitish, with two distinct stomatal bands on each surface.
Flowers
Inflorescence consists of dense clusters of small, pink to purple actinomorphic flowers with four petals and two stamens, arranged in compact spikes.
Stems
Stems are erect, branched, with opposite arrangement, covered in short, glandular hairs, and exhibit a square cross-section.

Antispasmodic

Carminative

Tonic

Expectorant
Historically regarded as a cooling expectorant, for airway-related applications.
