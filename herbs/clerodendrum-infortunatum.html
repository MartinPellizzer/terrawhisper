Clerodendrum infortunatum

What's the taxonomical classification of Clerodendrum infortunatum?

Clerodendrum infortunatum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Lamiales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Lamiaceae, falling within the genus Clerodendrum, and is specifically identified as the species Clerodendrum infortunatum.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Clerodendrum infortunatum contains bioactive phytochemicals including alkaloids, flavonoids, saponins, tannins, and terpenoids that provide its therapeutic properties. These organic compounds work together to exhibit significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities within biological systems. The presence of these secondary metabolites supports its traditional use in treating various ailments and infections. What are the therapeutic actions of Clerodendrum infortunatum?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, stomach aches, fever, and respiratory issues. Traditional medicinal practices utilize its leaves and roots to combat parasitic infections and soothe localized inflammation. It is frequently applied topically to address wounds or fungal growths on the skin. Some practitioners also use decoctions from the plant to alleviate digestive distress and intestinal discomfort. These applications highlight its diverse role in folk medicine for managing both internal and external health concerns.

What are the herbal preparations of Clerodendrum infortunatum?

Clerodendrum infortunatum is prepared as a decoction of its leaves, a poultice made from crushed plant parts, or an infusion of the flowers. These liquid preparations are frequently utilized to extract active compounds for therapeutic purposes. When applied topically, the mashed leaves serve as a direct medicinal paste for skin ailments. The decoction method is often preferred for internal use to ensure a concentrated strength of the herbal essence. Each form is chosen based on the specific ailment being targeted by the practitioner.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Clerodendrum infortunatum can cause skin irritation, contact dermatitis, and localized allergic reactions when the plant or its sap comes into direct contact with human skin. Individuals with sensitive skin may experience redness, itching, or swelling shortly after touching the foliage. Some people might also suffer from gastrointestinal distress if any part of the plant is ingested accidentally. While specific toxicological studies are limited, the presence of certain phytochemicals suggests that caution is necessary to avoid adverse physical reactions. It is generally advised to wear protective gloves when handling this species to prevent unexpected dermatological issues.
