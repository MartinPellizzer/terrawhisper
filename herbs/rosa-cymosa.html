Rosa cymosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Rosa cymosa?

Rosa cymosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Rosaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Rosa and is specifically designated as the species cymosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rosa cymosa has a deciduous shrub habit characterized by arching, slender stems that are typically armed with fine, spreading prickles. The leaves are pinnately compound, usually featuring five to seven elliptical leaflets with serrated margins and a slightly pubescent texture. Its inflorescence consists of clusters of fragrant, single or semi-double flowers that display a range of pale pink to white petals. The reproductive structures include a central cluster of numerous stamens and a globose hip that develops as the fruit matures. These hips often turn red or orange, serving as a distinctive feature of the plant's morphology during the fruiting season.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across countries such as Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, and Indonesia. It thrives in humid environments ranging from lowland forests to coastal shrublands. The species is frequently found in disturbed habitats and forest edges where sunlight is more abundant. Its distribution follows the moisture-rich corridors of the Indomalayan realm. Localized populations also occur within specific island archipelagos throughout the region.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rosa cymosa?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, immuno-modulatory, immunostimulatory, and α-glucosidase inhibitory pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage caused by oxidative stress. The plant also regulates the immune system through both immuno-modulatory and immunostimulatory mechanisms to maintain biological balance. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory effects assist in reducing swelling and pain within the body. Finally, the α-glucosidase inhibitory activity suggests a potential role in managing blood glucose levels by slowing carbohydrate digestion.

As discussed in a study, three new triterpenoids were isolated from the roots of Rosa cymosa, identified as 2α, 3α, 20β, 23-tetrahydroxyurs-13 (18)-en-28-oic-acid (1), 2β, 3α, 20β, 23-tetrahydroxyurs-13(18)-en-28-oic-acid (2), and 2α, 3α-dihydroxyurs-12(13), 18(19)-dien-28-oic-acid (3). These isolates were evaluated for their α-glucosidase inhibitory and anti-inflammatroy activities in vitro. The results demonstrated that compound 2 displayed moderate anti-inflammatroy activity. This specific activity was measured with an IC50 value of 9.4 μM.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds berberine, emodin, and limonene. Berberine is a potent alkaloid frequently studied for its ability to regulate blood sugar and improve lipid profiles. Emodin functions as an anthraquinone that provides significant anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Limonene serves as a terpene that contributes to the plant's aromatic profile and offers antioxidant benefits. Together, these specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse therapeutic potential of the species.

As discussed in a study, phytochemicals were detected in the flower petals of Rosa cymosa that had been extracted using methanol. The investigation of the species included assessing superoxide dismutase interactions with berbine, emodin, and limonene via molecular docking using PyRx software. Among the three species studied, Rosa cymosa demonstrated antioxidant activity that was lower than Rosa chinensis but higher than Rosa indica. Docking studies showed that berbine and emodin had substantial binding affinity with SOD. These extracts showed strong antioxidant properties suggesting they could be used as naturally occurring antioxidants for ailments related with oxidative stress.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, oxidative stress, wind-phlegm cough, contusions, and various physical injuries. Its bioactive compounds work to mitigate cellular damage caused by oxidative stress within the body. When applied topically, the plant helps reduce the swelling and pain associated with contusions and soft tissue injuries. It is also traditionally employed to soothe respiratory discomfort, specifically targeting a wind-phlegm cough. These diverse medicinal applications highlight its effectiveness in managing both internal and external health concerns.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested in large quantities. While many rose species are relatively benign, individual sensitivities to the oils or thorns can trigger redness and itching on contact. Some people may experience localized swelling or a rash if they touch the plant without protection. Internal consumption is generally discouraged due to a lack of comprehensive toxicological studies on this specific species. Always consult a medical professional if an adverse reaction occurs after contact.
