Scientific and Botanical Profile

Cleome Shrub, with botanical name Cleome gynandra, is a member of the Malvaceae family, commonly known as the mallow family.

Native to tropical Africa, this plant is also referred to as Common Spider Plant, Spiny Cleome, and African Spin spinach, among many other common names. It is a herbaceous shrub characterized by its erect, branching stems covered with spiny protrusions, and its alternate, palmately lobed leaves that are often hairy and have a slightly bitter taste. The flowers are small, yellow, and clustered in terminal inflorescences, while the fruit is a capsule containing numerous small seeds.

This versatile plant is also known by names such as Wild Cleome, Cow Cucumber, and Broom Cleome, and is sometimes called Common Mallow or Wild Spinach, reflecting its widespread use in traditional medicine and as a leafy vegetable in various regions.

History and Cultural Relevance

Cleome Shrub was used extensively in traditional African medicine for centuries, particularly among the Yoruba, Akan, and Igbo peoples of West Africa, where it was valued for its purported healing properties.

The plant has a long history of use in herbal remedies, with its leaves and seeds traditionally employed to treat ailments such as malaria, digestive disorders, and skin infections. In various cultural rituals, Cleome Shrub was also incorporated into ceremonies, symbolizing fertility and protection, and was sometimes used in rites of passage or to ward off negative energies. Today, its use persists in some traditional healing practices, with its leaves still being used to make teas for digestive relief and its seeds occasionally employed in herbal formulations for their purported anti-inflammatory effects.

Despite modern scientific skepticism, the plant remains culturally significant, reflecting the deep interconnection between nature and human health in many traditional societies.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Cleome gynandra has been traditionally used in various medicinal practices, and it is available in several forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, one can make a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the dried plant material for a longer period. It can also be used as an infusion or applied topically for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried leaves per cup of tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

It is advised to use cleome gynandra sparingly, with a frequency of 1-2 times daily and a duration not exceeding two weeks unless otherwise directed by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Cleome gynandra can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential therapeutic effects, but it must be approached with caution due to its potential safety concerns.

While some studies suggest it may have anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties, it is not without risks. Side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, and allergic reactions, particularly in individuals sensitive to plants in the Malvaceae family. It may interact with certain medications, such as anticoagulants or diabetes treatments, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of adverse effects. Due to limited research on its safety during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and in individuals with chronic illnesses, it is generally advised to avoid its use in these populations unless under the guidance of a healthcare professional.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a qualified practitioner before starting any regimen and to follow recommended dosages to minimize potential risks.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Cleome gynandra grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, thriving in full sun to partial shade and requiring regular watering to maintain consistent moisture without becoming waterlogged.

For optimal growth, it should be planted in spring after the last frost, with spacing of about 30-45 cm between plants to allow for adequate air circulation and reduce the risk of disease. Regular weeding and mulching around the base of the plant help control weeds and retain soil moisture, while periodic fertilization with a balanced organic fertilizer supports healthy foliage and seed development. Harvesting should occur when the leaves are young and tender, typically 4-6 weeks after planting, by cutting the stems just above a node to encourage new growth and maximize yield.

To preserve its potency, the harvested leaves should be thoroughly dried in a cool, shaded area or using a dehydrator, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain freshness and medicinal properties.
