Tilia (Tilia cordata): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Tilia (Tilia Cordata)?

Tilia (Tilia cordata), commonly known as small-leaved linden, is a deciduous tree native to Europe and parts of western Asia, valued in herbal medicine for its flowers, leaves, and bark, which are used to make teas and tinctures for their calming and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Tilia is Tilia cordata. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Equisetopsida, order Malvales, family Tiliaceae, genus Tilia, and species Tilia cordata. It is commonly known as small-leaved lime in Europe and American basswood in North America. The plant is also referred to by synonyms such as Tilia europaea and Tilia americana.

Plant Description and Morphology

Tilia is a small to medium-sized deciduous tree that typically grows to a height of 10-25 meters with a broad, rounded canopy.

Its leaves are heart-shaped with a serrated margin and a pointed tip, usually measuring 5-12 cm in length. The flowers are small, clustered in drooping catkins, and appear in early spring before the leaves emerge. The root system is shallow and fibrous, often forming a dense network near the soil surface.

The bark is smooth and grayish-brown, while the leaves are bright green on top and pale underneath, with a slightly rough texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Tilia is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, with Tilia cordata specifically originating from regions such as France, Italy, and the Balkans.

Its geographic distribution spans across temperate regions, including the UK, Germany, and parts of eastern Europe. The plant thrives in moderate climates with cool summers and cold winters, preferring areas with ample sunlight. It grows best in well-drained, fertile soils and is commonly found in woodland edges, hedgerows, and river valleys.

Ecological conditions favor moist but not waterlogged soil, and it often forms part of mixed deciduous forests.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and bark of Tilia cordata are used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly utilized part for their therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Tilia cordata contains a variety of phytochemicals including tannins, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and mucilage, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine for respiratory and digestive support.

Pharmacological Properties

Tilia cordata exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, sedative, and antispasmodic effects, primarily attributed to its bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and essential oils.

Mechanisms of Action

Tilia cordata exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and mild sedative properties, which are attributed to the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, tannins, and mucilage.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Tilia cordata has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to address respiratory conditions such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma due to its expectorant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Tilia (Tilia cordata) is typically prepared as a decoction, infusion, or tincture, with the dried flowers or leaves used to make teas, poultices, or topical applications, depending on the intended therapeutic use.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Tilia cordata for herbal use ranges from 200 to 400 mg of standardized extract, taken two to three times daily, though specific formulations may vary.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Tilia (Tilia cordata) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, and it should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and by individuals with known allergies to the Tiliaceae family.

Drug Interactions

Tilia cordata may interact with medications that affect the central nervous system, such as sedatives, antidepressants, or anticoagulants, due to its potential sedative and antispasmodic properties.

Toxicity and Precautions

Tilia (Tilia cordata) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and lactation due to limited safety data.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Tilia cordata is typically cultivated in temperate regions with well-drained soil and partial to full sunlight, and is harvested primarily from its flowers and leaves, with the flowers being collected in late spring to early summer and the leaves harvested in late summer.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Tilia cordata involve methods such as maceration, infusion, or distillation to obtain its active compounds, including tannins, flavonoids, and mucilage, which are then used in herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Tilia (Tilia cordata) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific research on Tilia cordata has shown its potential in traditional herbal medicine, with studies indicating its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and mild sedative properties, though more clinical trials are needed to confirm its efficacy and safety in modern medical applications.
