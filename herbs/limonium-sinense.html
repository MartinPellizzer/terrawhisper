Limonium sinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Limonium sinense?

Limonium sinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is categorized under the order Caryophyllales. It is a member of the family Plumbaginaceae and is further defined by its placement within the genus Limonium, ultimately reaching its specific identification as the species sinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Limonium sinense has perennial, herbaceous, or subshrubby growth habits characterized by erect, branching stems and narrow, linear, or lanceolate leaves that are typically arranged in basal rosettes. The foliage often exhibits a grayish-green hue due to a fine covering of hairs on the leaf surfaces. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal panicles composed of numerous small, delicate flowers that can vary in color from white to pale lavender. The individual flowers are structurally complex, featuring small petals and prominent stamens that contribute to the textured appearance of the cluster. The root system is generally sturdy and well-developed to support its upright structure in coastal or saline environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the coastal regions of southeastern China, specifically within provinces such as Zhejiang and Fujian. It primarily inhabits saline environments such as salt marshes and coastal wetlands where it can tolerate high salt concentrations. Its distribution is closely tied to tidal zones and sandy shorelines along the East China Sea. The species thrives in these specific maritime habitats due to its specialized physiological adaptations to brackish water. Consequently, its range remains restricted to these particular coastal ecosystems.

What are the pharmacological activities of Limonium sinense?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidative, anti-tumor, antiproliferative, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory effects by modulating various immune responses. Furthermore, its antiproliferative and anti-tumor actions suggest a potential role in inhibiting the growth and spread of malignant cells. Finally, the extract exhibits hepatoprotective qualities that assist in defending the liver against chemical or toxic damage.

As discussed in a study, UPLC-MS/MS method combined with database comparison identified 109 compounds in Limonium sinense, including flavonoids, alkaloids and polyphenols. The potential bioactivities of Limonium sinense were considered as anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidative, anti-tumor, hepatoprotective and hyperglycemic activities. Four derivatives of 12-oxo-phytoienoic acid and butenolide, including two new ones, were isolated from the whole plants. These isolates were evaluated for their anti-inflammatory activity. Compound 1 exhibited moderate anti-inflammatory activity with an IC50 value of 37.5±1.2 μM on NO production level.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including (-) -epi gallocatechin 3-O-gallate, flavonoids, flavanols, gallic acid, and myricetin. The presence of these polyphenolic substances contributes to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Specifically, the flavanols and flavonoids serve as key bioactive agents that help neutralize free radicals within biological systems. Gallic acid provides additional chemical stability and therapeutic potential through its unique molecular structure. Furthermore, the inclusion of myricetin enhances the overall medicinal profile by supporting cellular health and metabolic functions.

As discussed in a study, the root of Limonium sinense contains a new (2R,3S)-3,5,7,4'-tetrahydroxy-3',5'-dimethoxyflavanone named isodihydrosyringetin (3) along with nine other known compounds. These include (-) -epigallocatechin 3-O-gallate (1), samarangenin B (2), myricetin (4), myricetin 3-O-alpha-rhamnopyranoside (5), quercetin 3-O-alpha-rhamnopyranoside (6), (-) -epigallocatechin (7), gallic acid (8), N-trans-caffeoyltyramine (9), and N-trans-feruloyltyramine (10). Compounds 1 and 2 showed potent inhibitory activities against HSV-1 replication, with IC50 values of 38.6 +/- 2.6 microM and 11.4 +/- 0.9 microM respectively. These values were compared to the positive control acyclovir, which had an IC50 of 55.4 +/- 5.3 microM (P < 0.001 and P < 0.0005). No cell deaths were observed in Vero cells following 5 day treatments with compound 1 or 2.
