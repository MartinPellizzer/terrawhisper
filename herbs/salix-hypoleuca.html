What's the taxonomical classification of Salix hypoleuca?

Salix hypoleuca belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is part of the order Malpighiales and falls under the family Salicaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Salix and the species hypoleuca.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salix hypoleuca has lanceolate to narrowly elliptical leaves that are typically 3 to 10 centimeters long, featuring finely serrated margins and a distinctively pale, glaucous underside. The leaf texture is often somewhat leathery, while the upper surface remains a darker green. Its stems are characterized by a smooth texture in younger growth that eventually develops a lenticellate bark as the plant matures. The reproductive structures consist of catkins that appear in early spring, often before the leaves have fully expanded. These catkins are typically short and cylindrical, containing numerous small, inconspicuous flowers.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Salix hypoleuca contains salicin primarily within its bark and young twigs, which are the specific components utilized for medicinal purposes. These parts of the plant are harvested to extract glycosides that act as natural precursors to salicylic acid. When ingested or applied, the compounds from the bark function as analgesics to relieve pain and antipyretics to reduce fever. Historically, preparations made from these woody tissues have been used to treat inflammation and various aches. Modern pharmacology has utilized the chemical properties found in these specific plant structures to develop synthetic aspirin.
