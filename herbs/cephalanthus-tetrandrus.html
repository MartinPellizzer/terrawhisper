Cephalanthus tetrandrus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cephalanthus tetrandrus?

Cephalanthus tetrandrus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Gentianales. This plant is further categorized into the family Rubiaceae, which is a prominent group of flowering plants. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Cephalanthus, and its specific biological identity is defined by its species designation, tetrandrus.

Cephalanthus tetrandrus has medicinal properties that primarily include anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and analgesic actions used to treat various ailments. What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat malaria, intestinal worms, fever, and various types of skin infections. The bark and roots are frequently employed by traditional healers to combat parasitic infections in the digestive tract. Its medicinal properties are also applied topically to soothe inflammation and clear up dermatological ailments. By utilizing its bioactive compounds, practitioners aim to reduce high temperatures associated with systemic illnesses. This botanical specimen serves as a vital resource in many regional herbal medicine practices for managing these specific health conditions.

What are the herbal preparations of Cephalanthus tetrandrus?

Cephalanthus tetrandrus is prepared primarily as a decoction of the roots, a concentrated infusion of the bark, or a topical paste made from crushed leaves. Practitioners often boil the woody parts in water to extract bioactive alkaloids for internal consumption. In many traditional medicinal systems, the plant material is also processed into tinctures using alcohol as a solvent to increase potency. Additionally, the leaves can be mashing into a poultice to treat skin irritations or local inflammation. These varied methods allow for different absorption rates depending on the intended therapeutic use.

While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many plants in the Rubiaceae family contain alkaloids that may trigger sensitivities in certain individuals. Contact with the plant's sap or leaves might result in localized dermatitis or redness upon skin exposure. Ingestion of undocumented botanical species often carries a risk of nausea, vomiting, or abdominal discomfort.
