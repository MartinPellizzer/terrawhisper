Cyclosorus parasiticus

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cyclosorus parasiticus has a highly specialized morphology characterized by the complete absence of chlorophyll, true roots, and leaves, instead utilizing a fleshy, scale-like stem structure to facilitate its parasitic lifestyle. This plant lacks the typical photosynthetic organs found in autotrophic species, relying instead on specialized structures to penetrate the host tissue. Its body consists of thickened, brownish or yellowish scales that protect the internal vascular connection to the host plant. The reproductive organs are often the most prominent morphological feature, emerging from the stem to produce small, inconspicuous flowers. These morphological adaptations allow the organism to divert all its energy toward nutrient absorption and reproduction from its host.

This plant is native to tropical and subtropical regions of South America, specifically ranging from Brazil through parts of the Amazon basin. Because it is a parasitic species, its distribution is closely tied to the presence of specific host plants within these ecosystems. Cyclosorus parasiticus has astringent, hemostatic, and antidiarrehal properties that are utilized in traditional medicinal practices to treat various ailments.     What side-effects this plant can have?

    Cyclosorus parasiticus can cause severe skin irritation, localized swelling, and acute nausea if any part of the plant is ingested. Contact with the leaves often triggers an immediate inflammatory response characterized by red, itchy rashes. Some individuals may experience more serious systemic reactions such as dizziness or mild hallucinations following accidental exposure. Long-term contact with the sap is known to cause persistent dermatitis that requires medical intervention. These adverse effects are typically most intense in sensitive populations or during high concentrations of the plant.
