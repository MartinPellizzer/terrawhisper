What to know about Cornus officinalis (chinese dogwood) before using it medicinally

Cornus officinalis, commonly known as Chinese dogwood, is an herb that possesses a range of health-promoting properties, from anti-inflammatory to antioxidant, which can help improve cardiovascular and immune system health.

It is a versatile herb that requires a subtropical climate and well-draining soil to thrive, making it a challenging but rewarding plant to cultivate. Botanically speaking, Cornus officinalis is a deciduous shrub or small tree that belongs to the Cornaceae family, characterized by its showy white flowers and distinctive fruit.

Historically, this herb has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for centuries, with documented references in ancient texts such as the Shennong Bencao Jing dating back to the 16th century.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Cornus officinalis.

What are the medicinal properties of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Chinese dogwood, helps with various health issues, including fever, rheumatism, and digestive problems. It has anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, and diuretic properties, making it a popular herbal remedy in traditional Chinese medicine. Its medicinal properties have been utilized for centuries.

The active constituents of Cornus officinalis include flavonoids, iridoids, and triterpenoids, which contribute to its medicinal properties. Flavonoids, such as kaempferol and quercetin, exhibit anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. Iridoids, like cornuside, have been shown to possess antipyretic and anti-inflammatory activities. Triterpenoids, including ursolic acid, display anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

The parts of Cornus officinalis most commonly used for medicinal purposes are the berries, leaves, and bark. The berries are rich in flavonoids and iridoids, making them a popular ingredient in traditional Chinese medicine. The leaves and bark contain triterpenoids, which are used to treat fever, rheumatism, and digestive issues.

Improper use of Cornus officinalis can lead to side effects such as stomach upset, diarrhea, and allergic reactions. Its high content of flavonoids and iridoids can cause gastrointestinal disturbances in some individuals. In rare cases, excessive consumption may result in liver or kidney damage.

Precautions when using Cornus officinalis medicinally include avoiding its use in pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential toxicity to the fetus or infant. Individuals with kidney or liver disease should also consult a healthcare professional before using this herb. Additionally, patients taking medications for blood thinning or diabetes should be cautious when consuming Cornus officinalis.

What are the horticulural aspects of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Chinese dogwood, grow in zones 5-8, requiring full sun to partial shade and well-drained acidic soil with a pH of 5.5-6.5. They can tolerate a range of soil types, but prefer moist, fertile conditions.

For successful planting, choose a location with good air circulation to prevent disease. Plant in spring or fall, avoiding extreme temperatures. Space 3-6 feet apart, and water regularly during the first growing season. Mulch around the base to retain moisture and suppress weeds.

For harvesting, the berries of Cornus officinalis are typically ripe in late summer to early fall. Harvest in small quantities regularly to encourage repeat production. Wear gloves to protect yourself from the plant's thorns. Berries can be used fresh or frozen for later use in jams, jellies, and baked goods.

Common pests affecting Cornus officinalis include Japanese beetles, aphids, and spider mites. Diseases such as powdery mildew, root rot, and leaf spot can also occur. Regular inspection and maintenance, including pruning and fertilization, can help prevent or mitigate these issues.

What are the botanical characteristics of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese cornel, is a deciduous shrub or small tree that exhibits several distinct botanical characteristics.

The plant grows up to 6 meters tall and has an upright habit, with multiple stems originating from the base. Its leaves are oval-shaped, measuring 5-10 cm long and 3-5 cm wide, with a pointed tip and serrated margins. The leaf surface is dark green on top and pale green underneath, featuring a prominent midrib and lateral veins that run parallel to each other.

In autumn, the leaves turn yellow or reddish-purple before falling off. The plant produces small, white flowers in late spring, which are arranged in clusters and have four petals. Each flower measures approximately 1 cm in diameter and has numerous stamens with yellow anthers. Following pollination, red or purple berries form, each containing two seeds, and remain on the plant throughout winter.

Overall, Cornus officinalis displays a striking combination of leaf shape, color, and habit that sets it apart from other species within its genus.

What is the taxonomy of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae, class Magnoliopsida, order Cornales, family Cornaceae, genus Cornus, and species C. officinalis.

Specifically, it is classified as a deciduous shrub or small tree, native to East Asia, characterized by its yellowish-green leaves, white flowers, and red berries.

This classification reflects the plant's evolutionary relationships with other flowering plants and its unique characteristics that distinguish it from other members of the Cornaceae family.

chinensis, which is commonly used in traditional Chinese medicine, and C. officinalis var. angustifolia, which is characterized by narrower leaves and a more compact habit.

Another variant is C. officinalis var. stolonifera, which produces long stolons (above-ground stems) that root at the nodes to form new plants.

These variants exhibit varying degrees of adaptability, growth habits, and uses, reflecting the plant's ability to thrive in diverse environments and cultures.

What is the geographic distribution of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese Cornel or Chinese Dogwood, is native to East Asia.

Its geographic distribution spans across China, Japan, and Korea, where it can be found growing in mountainous regions and forests at elevations up to 2,000 meters above sea level.

In China, it is commonly distributed in the provinces of Shaanxi, Shanxi, Henan, and Gansu, while in Japan, it can be found on Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu islands.

What is the life cycle of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese Cornel or Dogwood, is a deciduous shrub native to East Asia.

Its life cycle typically begins in early spring when it produces small white flowers, followed by the formation of red berries that mature in late summer and early fall. The shrub grows rapidly, reaching maturity within 5-10 years, and can live for up to 20-30 years with proper care. In the fall, the leaves turn yellow or orange before dropping off, marking the end of the growing season.

During the dormant winter months, the shrub rests until new growth begins in the spring.

What are the historical uses of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese Cornel, has a rich history of traditional use in Asian medicine.

For centuries, its bark, fruit, and leaves were employed to treat various ailments, including fever, rheumatism, and digestive issues. In traditional Chinese medicine, it was used to harmonize the body's Qi and balance the Five Elements.

Its anti-inflammatory properties also made it a popular ingredient in herbal remedies for skin conditions and wounds.

What are the mythological referencec of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese Cornel or Chinese Dogwood, is steeped in mythological significance.

According to ancient Chinese folklore, the plant's fruit, called "cornelian cherry," was believed to have been created by the gods to bring good fortune and prosperity. In Korean mythology, the plant was associated with the goddess of mercy, who used its bark to heal the sick.

Its wood is also said to possess magical properties in Japanese folk tales.

What are the symbolic meanings of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese cornelian cherry, is a symbolically rich plant in various cultures.

In East Asian countries, it represents longevity, vitality, and good fortune, often used in traditional medicine and ceremonies. The fruit and flowers are also associated with abundance, prosperity, and purity.

In some Western traditions, the plant is linked to protection, spiritual growth, and wisdom, making it a popular choice for magical practices and rituals.

What are the historical texts of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese cornelian cherry, has a rich history in traditional Chinese medicine.

The earliest recorded mention of this plant dates back to the Shennong Ben Cao Jing (Divine Farmer's Herb-Root Classic), which was written around 2000 BC. The text describes its uses for treating fever and diarrhea.

Later, in the Ming dynasty's Ben Cao Gang Mu (Compendium of Materia Medica) written by Li Shizhen in 1578 AD, Cornus officinalis is listed as a medicinal herb used to treat various ailments, including respiratory issues and menstrual disorders.

What are the historical artifacts of Cornus officinalis?

Cornus officinalis, also known as Japanese Cornelian Cherry, has a rich history dating back thousands of years.

Archaeological findings have revealed ancient Chinese artifacts featuring carvings and paintings of its distinctive fruits and leaves. The tree's wood was prized for its durability and used to create intricate crafts and utensils.

Historical records also document the plant's use in traditional medicine, with written references dating back to the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE).
