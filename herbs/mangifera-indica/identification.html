Botanical Identification of Mango (Mangifera indica)

To identify Mango (Mangifera indica) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Mango (Mangifera indica) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Mango (Mangifera indica) belongs to the Kingdom Plantae, Division Magnoliophyta, Class Eudicots, Order Sapindales, Family Anacardiaceae, Genus Mangifera, and Species Mangifera indica. These classifications place it within the flowering plants and provide a structured way to understand its botanical relationships.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Mango is Mangifera indica, a classification established by the botanist Linnaeus in 1753. This name is widely accepted in botanical literature and serves as the standard identifier for the species. Synonyms and alternative names reflect its diverse regional and historical usage across cultures.

Morphological Characteristics

Mango trees are typically medium to large, with a spreading canopy and a straight trunk. Their root system is fibrous and deep, providing stability. The stem is sturdy and often has a rough, bark-like texture. Leaves are alternate, lanceolate, and have a smooth surface with a prominent midrib. Inflorescences are panicles, bearing small, clustered flowers that are usually green and inconspicuous. The fruit is a hairy, oblong drupe, containing a single seed surrounded by a fibrous pulp.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Mangifera indica can be distinguished by its large, elongated leaves with a serrated margin and a distinct midrib. The flowers are clustered in panicles, and the fruit is a hairy, yellow-orange drupe with a fibrous pulp. It differs from similar species through its leaf shape, floral structure, and unique fruit characteristics.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Mango (Mangifera indica) is native to South and Southeast Asia, particularly India, Bangladesh, and Myanmar. It thrives in tropical climates with high temperatures and distinct wet and dry seasons. The tree prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 0 and 800 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Mango trees typically bloom between late winter and early spring, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs after successful pollination, usually within a few weeks of flowering. Harvesting takes place when the fruit changes color and becomes firm, typically between late summer and early fall.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Mangifera indica is closely related to species like Mangifera oblonga and Mangifera sylvatica. It belongs to the genus Mangifera within the Anacardiaceae family. Shared traits include compound leaves, stipules, and drupes, which help in its classification and identification.
