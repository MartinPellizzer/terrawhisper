Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) Uses

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) is used to treat various conditions including psoriasis, skin infections, and inflammatory skin conditions. It supports wound healing and is effective for chronic wounds and fungal infections. Additionally, it has shown promise in managing inflammatory bowel disease and gastrointestinal inflammation, as well as certain inflammatory diseases and malaria.

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) Monograph

1. Psoriasis

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) has been used in traditional medicine to treat psoriasis due to its anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory properties. The alkaloids vincamine and vincaisoine help reduce skin inflammation and slow down excessive cell growth, making it a potential complementary treatment for psoriasis symptoms.

2. Skin infections

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) is used topically to treat skin infections due to its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. The plant contains alkaloids that inhibit bacterial and fungal growth, making it effective for conditions like eczema, fungal infections, and minor wounds. It is often applied as a poultice or infused oil to promote healing and reduce inflammation.

3. Inflammatory skin conditions

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) is used topically to treat inflammatory skin conditions due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. It helps reduce redness, swelling, and irritation, making it effective for eczema, psoriasis, and minor wounds. The plant's alkaloids contribute to its therapeutic effects on skin inflammation.

4. Wound healing

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) is used in traditional medicine to promote wound healing due to its rich content of alkaloids and antioxidants. These compounds help reduce inflammation, prevent infection, and accelerate tissue repair. It is often applied topically as a poultice or ointment to treat minor cuts, burns, and skin abrasions.

5. Chronic wounds

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) is used to treat chronic wounds due to its ability to stimulate tissue repair and reduce inflammation. The plant contains alkaloids that promote cell regeneration and enhance wound healing. It is often applied topically as a salve or ointment to accelerate recovery in non-healing ulcers and sores.

6. Fungal infections

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) contains alkaloids like vincamine and vincorine, which exhibit antifungal properties. These compounds inhibit fungal growth by disrupting cellular processes. It is used traditionally to treat skin infections and may support immune response against fungal pathogens. Its effectiveness is supported by historical use in herbal medicine.

7. Inflammatory bowel disease

Vinca rosea, or periwinkle, contains alkaloids that exhibit anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory properties. These compounds may help reduce intestinal inflammation in inflammatory bowel disease by inhibiting pro-inflammatory cytokines. Preliminary studies suggest potential therapeutic benefits, though clinical evidence remains limited.

8. Gastrointestinal inflammation

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) has been used traditionally to reduce inflammation in the gastrointestinal tract. Its alkaloids, such as vincamine, possess anti-inflammatory properties that help soothe conditions like gastritis and inflammatory bowel disease. It is often prepared as a tea or tincture for internal use. Clinical studies support its efficacy in alleviating digestive discomfort and promoting gut healing.

9. Inflammatory diseases

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) is used in herbal medicine to treat inflammatory diseases due to its alkaloid content, which exhibits anti-inflammatory properties. It helps reduce swelling and pain associated with conditions like arthritis and skin inflammation. The plant's compounds support immune regulation and tissue repair, making it a valued remedy in traditional and complementary medicine.

10. Malaria

Periwinkle (Vinca rosea) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat malaria due to its alkaloid content, particularly vincamine and vincamine. These compounds are believed to enhance cognitive function and may support the body's response to parasitic infections. However, its use for malaria is not widely supported by modern clinical studies.
