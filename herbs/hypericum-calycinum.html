Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Golden St. John's Wort, or Hypericum calycinum, is a flowering plant known for its traditional use in herbal medicine to support mood and nervous system health.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Golden St. John'S Wort (Hypericum Calycinum)?

Golden St. John's Wort, or Hypericum calycinum, is a low-growing perennial shrub native to Europe and parts of Asia, characterized by its bright yellow flowers and historical use in traditional medicine for its purported mood-enhancing and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Golden St. John's Wort is Hypericum calycinum. This plant belongs to the family Clusiaceae, genus Hypericum, and species Hypericum calycinum. It is commonly known as Golden St. John's Wort in Europe and as St. John's Wort in North America. The plant is also referred to as Wood St. John's Wort and Yellow St. John's Wort in various regional contexts. Synonyms include Hypericum hirsutum and Hypericum patulum.

Plant Description and Morphology

Golden St.

John's Wort is a low-growing perennial shrub that typically reaches heights of 10 to 30 centimeters, with a spreading, mat-like growth habit. Its leaves are oval to lance-shaped, with a slightly serrated edge, and are arranged alternately along the stems. The flowers are small, bright yellow, and have five petals, with prominent orange-red stamens that become visible as the flowers mature. The plant has a fibrous root system that spreads horizontally near the soil surface, allowing it to colonize disturbed soils effectively.

The stems, leaves, and flowers are covered in fine, glandular hairs, giving them a slightly sticky texture, while the leaves have a faintly aromatic, somewhat bitter scent.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Golden St.

John's Wort is native to the temperate regions of Europe, particularly the British Isles, and parts of western Asia. It has a geographic distribution that extends across northern Europe, including countries such as Germany, France, and Scandinavia. The plant thrives in mild, moist climates with cool summers and mild winters. It prefers well-drained soils and partial to full sunlight, often growing in woodlands, hedgerows, and along stream banks.

Ecological conditions favor its growth in areas with moderate humidity and low to moderate soil fertility.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in Golden St.

John's Wort are the flowers, leaves, and stems. These parts contain the active compounds responsible for the plant's therapeutic effects. The flowers are most commonly harvested during the summer months when the plant is in full bloom. Leaves and stems are often collected during the same period to ensure maximum potency.

Each part contributes to the overall composition and efficacy of the herbal preparation.

Phytochemical Composition

Golden St.

John's Wort contains a variety of active compounds that contribute to its therapeutic properties. These include hypericin, hyperforin, flavonoids, and phloroglucinol derivatives. Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids and phenolic acids play a significant role in its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. Chemical markers like hypericin and hyperforin are key components used in the identification and standardization of the herb.

These compounds are responsible for the plant's medicinal actions and are often targeted in quality control and research studies.

Pharmacological Properties

Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) exhibits pharmacological properties primarily attributed to its active compounds, including hypericin, hyperforin, and flavonoids, which contribute to its antidepressant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects.

Mechanisms of Action

Golden St. John's Wort exerts its effects primarily through the inhibition of neurotransmitter reuptake, particularly serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine, and also by modulating the activity of certain enzymes and receptors in the central nervous system.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support mood regulation, alleviate mild to moderate depression, and address symptoms of seasonal affective disorder due to its active compounds such as hyperforin and hypericin.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) is typically prepared as a dried herb for use in teas, tinctures, and capsules, with the most common forms being dried plant material, alcohol-based tinctures, and standardized extracts containing hypericin and hyperforin.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) typically ranges from 300 to 600 mg per day, divided into two or three doses, with standardized extracts containing at least 0.3% hypericin and 0.5% hyperforin being most commonly used for therapeutic effects.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Golden St. John's Wort is generally considered safe when used short-term and at recommended doses, it can cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, dry mouth, and skin sensitivity to sunlight, and it interacts with numerous medications, making it contraindicated for individuals on antidepressants, birth control, anticoagulants, or immunosuppressants.

Drug Interactions

Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) can interact with various medications, including antidepressants, birth control pills, blood thinners, and immunosuppressants, potentially reducing their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.

Toxicity and Precautions

Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts for short periods, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, dizziness, or photosensitivity, and should be avoided by individuals with certain medical conditions or those taking specific medications due to potential drug interactions.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) is typically cultivated in shaded to partially shaded environments with well-drained soil, and harvested during the summer months when its flowers are fully open, usually between June and August.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Golden St. John's Wort involve harvesting the flowering tops during the summer months, drying them to reduce moisture content, and then using methods such as solvent extraction or steam distillation to obtain the active compounds, primarily hypericin and hyperforin, which are responsible for its medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) has primarily focused on its potential antidepressant effects, with some studies suggesting it may be as effective as standard antidepressants for mild to moderate depression, though more research is needed to confirm its efficacy and safety in clinical settings.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Golden St. John's Wort has been traditionally used for its antidepressant and anti-inflammatory properties, with historical applications dating back to ancient times for treating wounds, skin conditions, and mood disorders.

Regulatory Status

In the United States, Golden St. John's Wort (Hypericum calycinum) is classified as a dietary supplement under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994, meaning it is not subject to the same pre-market approval process as drugs, and manufacturers are responsible for ensuring its safety and proper labeling.
