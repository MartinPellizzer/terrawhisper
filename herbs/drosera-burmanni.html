Drosera burmanni

What's the taxonomical classification of Drosera burmanni?

Drosera burmanni belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Caryophyllales and the family Droseraceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Drosera and the specific species epithet burmanni.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Drosera burmanni has a compact rosette of small, spatulate leaves that typically measure between 5 and 15 millimeters in length and are covered in glandular tentacles. These leaves are arranged in a tight circular pattern close to the soil surface to maximize light absorption. Each leaf is equipped with numerous red-tipped mucilaginous glands that serve to trap and digest small insects. The plant lacks a visible stem, instead growing from a short rhizome that keeps the foliage grounded. Small, pink or white flowers may occasionally emerge on thin, upright stalks rising above the foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad range of tropical regions throughout Southeast Asia, including parts of Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, and Vietnam. It thrives primarily in moist, sunny environments such as peat swamps and edges of marshes across these equatorial territories. Small populations can also be found spanning across certain islands in the Pacific region. The species prefers low-altitude habitats where humidity remains consistently high throughout the year. Its distribution is heavily influenced by the availability of acidic, nutrient-poor wetlands characteristic of these tropical landscapes.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires constant moisture through a tray of distilled or rainwater, high humidity levels, and bright indirect sunlight to thrive.

Because Drosera burmanni is a miniature sundew, it performs best when planted in a nutrient-poor substrate composed of peat moss and perlite. You should maintain warm temperatures year-round, as this species is native to tropical environments and does not require a dormancy period. Avoid using tap water or fertilizers, which will quickly damage the delicate root system.

Frequent misting can help sustain the necessary humidity for its carnivorous traps to remain effective.
