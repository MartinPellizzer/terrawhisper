Drosera burmanni

What's the taxonomical classification of Drosera burmanni?

Drosera burmanni belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Caryophyllales and the family Droseraceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Drosera and the specific species epithet burmanni.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Drosera burmanni has a compact rosette of small, spatulate leaves that typically measure between 5 and 15 millimeters in length and are covered in glandular tentacles. These leaves are arranged in a tight circular pattern close to the soil surface to maximize light absorption. Each leaf is equipped with numerous red-tipped mucilaginous glands that serve to trap and digest small insects. The plant lacks a visible stem, instead growing from a short rhizome that keeps the foliage grounded. Small, pink or white flowers may occasionally emerge on thin, upright stalks rising above the foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad range of tropical regions throughout Southeast Asia, including parts of Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, and Vietnam. Small populations can also be found spanning across certain islands in the Pacific region. The species prefers low-altitude habitats where humidity remains consistently high throughout the year. Its distribution is heavily influenced by the availability of acidic, nutrient-poor wetlands characteristic of these tropical landscapes.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Drosera burmanni contains bioactive naphthoquinones, most notably plumbagin, which exhibit significant antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties. These chemical compounds function by disrupting the cellular processes of various pathogenic microorganisms. Beyond plumbagin, the plant also yields various flavonoids and phenolic acids that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Researchers have explored these constituents for their possible roles in treating skin infections and oxidative stress.
