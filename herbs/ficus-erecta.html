Ficus erecta

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus erecta?

Ficus erecta belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Rosales. It further falls into the family Moraceae, belonging to the genus Ficus, and is specifically identified by the species epithet erecta.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus erecta has an erect growth habit characterized by woody stems, elliptical to lanceolate leaves with entire margins, and small, inconspicuous flowers produced within specialized structures called syconia. The foliage typically exhibits a glossy texture and a deep green coloration, often featuring prominent midribs. Its stems are relatively straight and upright, supporting a dense canopy of leaves. The reproductive syconia are small, fleshy fruits that house the tiny flowers inside a hollow receptacle. These morphological traits distinguish it within its genus through its specific upright stature and leaf configuration.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of West Africa, specifically spanning across countries such as Ghana, Nigeria, Cameroon, and Gabon. It thrives primarily within lowland rainforest ecosystems where humidity and rainfall are consistently high. These populations are often found in the understory of dense forests or along the margins of riverbanks. The species follows a distribution pattern dictated by the availability of moist, shaded habitats characteristic of the Guineo-Congolian forest zone. Consequently, its range is restricted to these specific equatorial belt territories.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ficus erecta?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its anti-inflammatory, anti-neuroinflammatory, antioxidant, antiproliferative, and neuroprotective activities. These biological properties suggest that extracts from Ficus erecta can effectively mitigate oxidative stress and reduce harmful inflammatory responses in various tissues. The plant's ability to combat neuroinflammation and provide neuroprotection makes it a candidate for addressing complex neurological conditions. Furthermore, its antiproliferative effects indicate a capacity to inhibit the rapid growth of certain abnormal cell lines. Such a diverse range of therapeutic actions highlights the medicinal importance of this species in pharmacological research.

As discussed in a study, ethanol extracts of Ficus erecta (EEFE) leaves demonstrate inhibitory effects on amyloid-β (Aβ) aggregation both in vivo and in vitro. The EEFE treatment markedly improved cognitive impairment in Aβ-injected mice during passive avoidance task and Morris water maze tests. This plant extract reduced neuronal loss and the expression of the neuronal marker NeuN in the brain tissues of Aβ-injected mice. Furthermore, EEFE significantly reversed the Aβ-induced suppression of cAMP response element-binding protein (CREB) phosphorylation and brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) expression. The study also showed that EEFE significantly suppressed the inflammatory cytokines interleukin 1beta (IL-1β) and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) alongside the expression of ionized calcium-binding adaptor molecule 1 (Iba-1).

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including neo-lignans, rutin, and specific chemical structures identified as 1a/b, 1a/b - 9a/b, and 1a/b-5a/b. These bioactive constituents contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile of the species. The neo-lignans are often studied for their antioxidant and neuroprotective properties. Rutin serves as a potent flavonoid known for strengthening capillaries and reducing inflammation. Furthermore, the complex series of 1a/b, 1a/b - 9a/b, and 1a/b-5a/b molecules provide unique structural variations essential for therapeutic research.

As discussed in a study, nine pairs of enantiomeric neolignans, identified as 1a/b - 9a/b, were isolated from the whole plant of Ficus erecta. This collection included seventeen previously undescribed compounds, specifically 1a/b-5a/b, 6a, 7a/b-9a/b. Compounds 1a/b and 5a/b-7a/b exhibited moderate antiproliferative effects against HepG2, A549, and HCT116 cancer cell lines. Furthermore, 4a/b, 8a/b, and 9a/b demonstrated neuroprotection in a 6-OHDA-induced oxidative stress model using PC12 cells. Specifically, 4a/b and 9a/b attenuated oxidative damage in a dose-dependent manner at concentrations of 6.25-25 μM.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive distress if its sap or parts are ingested. Contact with the milky latex typically leads to redness, itching, or blistering on sensitive skin surfaces. If the sap enters the eyes, it may cause significant inflammation and temporary vision discomfort. Ingesting any portion of the plant can result in nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramps due to its chemical composition. It is important to handle the specimen with gloves to prevent these adverse physical responses.
