Botanical Identification of Melilot (Melilotus officinalis)

To identify Melilot (Melilotus officinalis) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Melilotus officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Melilotus, and species Melilotus officinalis. This classification places it within the legume family, highlighting its botanical relationships and medicinal significance.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Melilot is Melilotus officinalis, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Melilotus suave and Melilotus albus. Common names include sweet clover, yellow sweetclover, and mellifera. Regional and historical names vary, reflecting its widespread use in traditional herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Melilotus officinalis is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has slender, branching stems that grow 30-100 cm tall. Its leaves are alternate, trifoliate, with oval leaflets. The inflorescence is a dense spike of small, yellow flowers. The fruit is a pod containing several elliptical, smooth seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Melilotus officinalis can be distinguished by its bright yellow flowers arranged in dense, cylindrical spikes, opposite leaf pairs with serrated edges, and a distinctive sweet, honey-like aroma. It is often confused with other Melilotus species, but its unique floral structure and fragrance help in accurate identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Melilotus officinalis is native to temperate regions of Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in climates with moderate temperatures and sufficient rainfall. It prefers well-drained soils, often found in grasslands and disturbed areas. It typically grows at altitudes between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Melilot blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn, around August to September. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the flowering period when the plant is most identifiable.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Melilotus officinalis is closely related to other members of the Fabaceae family, particularly species like Melilotus albus and Melilotus indicus. It shares key characteristics with its genus, such as trifoliate leaves and pod-like fruits. These relationships help in understanding its evolutionary history and distinguishing it from similar species.
