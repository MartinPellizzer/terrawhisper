Pericampylus glaucus

What's the taxonomical classification of Pericampylus glaucus?

Pericampylus glaucus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized as Equisetopsida, which further descends into the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Ranunculales, this plant is placed in the family Menispermaceae. Its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Pericampylus, with its distinct biological classification completed at the species level as glaucus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pericampylus glaucus has a woody, climbing shrub habit characterized by smooth, grayish-green stems and thick, leathery, lanceolate leaves that are arranged oppositely along the branches. The foliage is distinctly glaucous, meaning it possesses a waxy, blue-gray coating that provides a matte appearance. These leaves are typically evergreen and can reach several centimeters in length. During the flowering season, the plant produces small, tubular, yellowish-green flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. The overall structure is somewhat sprawling, often using other vegetation for support as it grows.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, specifically spanning across southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of western Asia. It typically thrives in rocky habitats and coastal areas where the climate is warm and relatively dry. Within these territories, populations are often found in scrublands known as maquis or garrigue. The species can be located in various microclimates ranging from the Atlantic coasts of the Iberian Peninsula to the shores of the Levant. Its distribution is closely linked to limestone-rich soils and Mediterranean-type vegetation zones.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal alkaloids (-)8-oxocanadine, (-)8-oxotetrahydropalmatine, norruffsciine, and the periglaucines A-D, along with the sterol daucosterol. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Pericampylus glaucus targets specific enzymes and receptors involved in inflammatory signaling pathways, most notably through the inhibition of cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX) enzymes. Such biochemical modulation contributes to its observed antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties in traditional medicinal applications. What health problems are treated using this plant?

This plant is used for treating Acanthamoeba infection, porcine epidemic diarrhea virus, hepatitis B virus, human immunoodeficiency virus, and tumours. Researchers have identified specific bioactive compounds within its tissues that exhibit potent antiviral properties against these various pathogens. In laboratory settings, extracts from the plant have shown the ability to inhibit the replication of both hepatitis B and HIV. Additionally, studies suggest its chemical constituents may play a role in combating certain types of tumours through targeted cellular interactions. The plant also demonstrates efficacy in neutralizing the porcine epidemic diarrhea virus and managing infections caused by Acanthamoeba.

As discussed in a study, Pericampylus glaucus (Lam.) Merr. is a medicinal plant used for the treatment of conjunctivitis in Malaysia. The ethanol fraction of the stem showed significant (p < 0.05) inhibition of trophozoites survival. At 100 μg/mL, betulinic acid and periglaucine A from this plant inhibited more than 70% survival of both cysts and trophozoites. The calculated therapeutic index for betulinic acid and periglaucine A was 170 and 1.5 for the trophozoites stage. For the cysts stage, the therapeutic indices were 3.75 and 8.5. These observed amoebiocidal efficacies indicate the beneficial aspects of this plant in the treatment of Acanthamoeba infection.
