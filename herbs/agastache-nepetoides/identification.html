Botanical Identification of Blue Hyssop (Agastache nepetoides)

To identify Blue Hyssop (Agastache nepetoides) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Blue Hyssop, Agastache nepetoides, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Agastache, and species nepetoides. It is a member of the mint family, known for its aromatic properties and traditional medicinal uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Blue Hyssop is Agastache nepetoides, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It is also known by several synonyms, including Hyssopus nepetoides and Agastache scrophularifolia. Common names such as Blue Hyssop and Wild Hyssop reflect its appearance and traditional use in herbal medicine. Regional and historical names vary but often relate to its aromatic properties and medicinal applications.

Morphological Characteristics

Blue Hyssop (Agastache nepetoides) is a perennial herb with a compact growth habit. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly in the soil. The stems are square and hairy, often branching near the base. Leaves are lance-shaped, opposite, and covered in fine hairs. The inflorescence is a dense spike of purple-blue flowers, arranged in whorls. Each flower has five petals and is tubular with a prominent throat. The fruit is a small, dry capsule containing numerous tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Blue Hyssop (Agastache nepetoides) can be distinguished by its narrow, gray-green leaves with serrated edges and spikes of pale blue to violet flowers. It has a square stem and a strong, mint-like aroma. It differs from similar species like Agastache rugosa by its smaller flowers and less hairy leaves.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Blue Hyssop (Agastache nepetoides) is native to the western United States and parts of Canada. It thrives in temperate climates with full sun to partial shade. It prefers well-drained soils, often found in rocky or sandy areas. It typically grows between 1,500 and 3,000 feet in elevation.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Blue Hyssop blooms from midsummer to early fall, typically between June and September, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn, around August to October. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering period, when its spikes of blue to purple flowers are fully developed.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Blue Hyssop (Agastache nepetoides) is closely related to other members of the genus Agastache within the Lamiaceae family. It shares key characteristics with related species such as Agastache foeniculum and Agastache rugosa, including square stems and aromatic leaves. These relationships help in understanding its ecological and medicinal significance within the plant family.
