Sapindus delavayi

What's the taxonomical classification of Sapindus delavayi?

Sapindus delaavay belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Sapindales and the family Sapindaceae. Ultimately, the plant is defined by its specific classification within the genus Sapindus as the species delaayi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sapinduus delavayi has pinnately compound leaves consisting of several pairs of closely spaced, elliptic to oblong leaflets that are glossy green on the upper surface and slightly paler beneath.

The leaflets feature entire margins and acute to acuminate apices, often appearing somewhat leathery in texture. The plant develops small, inconspicuous flowers that are typically arranged in panicles. Its fruit is a fleshy, globose drupe that transitions from green to a bright red or orange color when mature.

The woody stems and branches are characterized by a smooth to slightly fissured bark depending on the age of the tree.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Sapindus delavayi contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its seeds, bark, and leaves. These various components work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic benefits in traditional healing practices.

What are the herbal preparations of Sapindus delavayi?

Sapindus delavayi is prepared as a decoction, a paste, or an infusion depending on whether the seeds, bark, or leaves are being utilized for medicinal purposes. The seeds are frequently processed into a fine powder or an oil for topical application to address skin conditions. Practitioners often boil the bark in water to create a potent decoction used for internal cleansing or systemic ailments. Additionally, the leaves can be crushed into a fresh paste to serve as a poultice for localized inflammation. These diverse preparation methods allow for the targeted extraction of the plant's bioactive compounds.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Sapindus delavayi can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and potential toxicity if the unripe fruits or certain parts of the plant are consumed. The seeds contain saponins which may trigger nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in sensitive individuals. Contact with the plant's sap or crushed leaves can lead to contact dermatitis characterized by redness, itching, or swelling on the skin. Some users may also experience allergic reactions such as respiratory irritation if the pollen or dust from the plant is inhaled. It is essential to handle this species with caution to avoid these adverse physical reactions.
