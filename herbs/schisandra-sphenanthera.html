Schisandra sphenanthera

What's the taxonomical classification of Schisandra sphenanthera?

Schisandra sphenanthera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Austrobaileyales and the family Schisandraceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Schisandra, specifically identified as the species sphenanthera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Schisandra sphenanthera has woody climbing vines characterized by alternate, simple, elliptic to ovate leaves with entire margins and an obtuse apex. The foliage typically features a glossy upper surface and a slightly paler underside. Its inflorescences consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These flowers eventually develop into aggregate schizocarps, where each fruit is a small, fleshy drupelet. The overall structure allows the plant to scramble through forest undergrowth using its flexible stems.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several bioactive lignans including Schisandrin, Gomisin A, Schisantherin A, Schisantherin B, and Wuweizi chun A. These specific medicinal compounds are primarily responsible for the plant's pharmacological effects on the central nervous system and liver function. Researchers study these molecules to understand their potential roles in neuroprotection and antioxidant activity. The unique chemical structures of these compounds contribute to the traditional medicinal uses of the species. Such complex phytochemical profiles make the plant a subject of significant interest in modern drug discovery.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal compounds found in the fruits of Schisandra sphenanthera include schisantherin A, schizandrin, deoxyschizandrin, and gamma-schizandrin. The content of schisantherin A in Schisandra sphenanthera was measured according to the set standards of the Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2010 Edition. To determine these lignan contents, HPLC separation was performed using a Diamonsil C18 150 mm x 4.6 mm, 5 microm column. The process utilized a methanol-water 70 : 30 mobile phase at a flow rate of 0.8 mL/min. Detection for these compounds was conducted at a wavelength of 280 nm.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating APAP-induced liver injury, APAP-induced oxidative stress, chronic virus hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury, and hepatitis. Its bioactive compounds work to mitigate cellular damage caused by excessive acetaminophen consumption. Researchers have observed its potential to reduce oxidative stress levels that occur during toxic metabolic processes. Furthermore, the plant shows promise in managing various forms of hepatitis, including those caused by viral infections. These therapeutic properties make it a significant subject of study for protecting liver function against chemical and viral threats.

As discussed in a study, Schisandra sphenanthera fruit extract (SFE) can attenuate APAP-induced liver injury via key targets including MAPK3 and CASP3. SFE effectively alleviated APAP-induced oxidative stress involving MDA, SOD, and GSH, as well as inflammation involving IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-1β. Regarding the gut microbiota, SFE promoted the growth of Bacteroidales and Erysipelotrichales while decreasing the growth of Lactobacillales. This process led to an increased production of tryptophan metabolites. SFE pretreatment can alleviate APAP-induced liver injury by targeting the gut-liver axis.
