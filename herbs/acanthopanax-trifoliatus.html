Acanthopanax trifoliatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Acanthopanax trifoliatus?

Acanthopanax trifoliatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the hierarchy of biological classification, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Apiales, the plant is a member of the family Araliaceae. Its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Acanthopanax, with its unique identity established by the species name trifoliatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acanthopanax trifoliatus has a woody, deciduous shrub habit characterized by trifoliate leaves and stems covered in sharp, conical prickles. The leaves consist of three distinct leaflets that are typically ovate to elliptical in shape with serrated margins. These leaflets exhibit a dark green color on the upper surface and often possess a lighter, duller underside. The plant's stems are heavily armed with spines that serve as a defensive mechanism against herbivores. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in clusters, eventually developing into fleshy berries that facilitate seed dispersal.

As discussed in a study, Acanthopanax trifoliatus (AT) possesses antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and hemostatic activities. The petroleum ether layer (PEL) extract produced the strongest antibacterial effect. The ethyl acetate layer (EAL) extract contained the highest active substance content and demonstrated strong hemostatic and analgesic activities. Specifically, the EAL extract had the highest polyphenol and flavonoid levels, including rutin, chlorogenic acid, isochlorogenic acid A, and isochlorogenic acid C. Furthermore, the chloroform layer (CL) extract showed the strongest anti-inflammatory effect and significantly reduced IL-1β protein expression.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds chlorogenic acid, isochlorogenic acid A, isochlorogenic acid C, rutin, and saponin. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the diverse therapeutic potential of the species. Chlorogenic acid and its isomers, isochlorogenic acid A and isochlorogenic acid C, often provide significant antioxidant benefits. The presence of rutin further enhances the plant's ability to support vascular health through its flavonoid properties. Additionally, the saponin content can influence various biological functions within the body.

As discussed in a study, Acanthopanax trifoliatus (L.) Merr was ethanol-extracted and refined with petroleum ether, chloroform, ethyl acetate, and n-butanol. Active ingredients such as saponin, polyphenol, and flavonoid were analyzed using HPLC and UPLC-MS. The ethyl acetate layer (EAL) extract contained the highest levels of polyphenols and flavonoids, specifically including rutin, chlorogenic acid, isochlorogenic acid A, and isochlorogenic acid C. While the chloroform layer (CL) extract showed a significant reduction in IL-1β protein expression, the EAL extract demonstrated strong hemostatic and analgesic activities. These specific compounds and extraction results provide a basis for the medicinal and nutritional applications of the plant.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, managing pain, combating bacteria, and stopping bleeding. Its bioactive compounds act as natural agents to reduce swelling and soothe bodily discomfort. Researchers have noted its ability to fight various types of bacteria through antimicrobial properties. Additionally, the plant contains substances that help control bleeding in wounded tissues. These diverse medicinal functions make it a valuable resource in traditional healing practices.

As discussed in a study, Acanthopanax trifoliatus is an edible medicinal plant from Southeast Asia that exerts anti-inflammatory activity. The two triterpenoids from the plant, impressic acid (IA) and acankoreanogenin A (AA), were investigated for their effects in in vitro and in vivo chronic inflammation models. In RAW264.7 murine macrophage cells, AA and IA significantly reduced lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced production of nitric oxide. These constituents also down-regulated NF-κB activation and decreased the release of inflammatory mediators such as iNOS, COX-2, TNF-α, and IL-6, alongside factors MMP-9 and VEGF. In a tetradecanoylphorbolaacetate (TPA)-treated mouse model, AA and IA effectively attenuated ear edema and reduced levels of iNOS, COX-2, TNF-α, and IL-1β.
