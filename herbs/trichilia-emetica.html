Trichilia emetica

What's the taxonomical classification of Trichilia emetica?

Trichilia emetica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized into the order Sapindales. This species is a member of the family Meliaceae, specifically situated within the genus Trichilia, and is identified by the specific epithet emetica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Trichilia emetica has pinnate leaves consisting of several pairs of oblong to elliptic leaflets with entire margins and an acute apex.

The plant is a medium-sized tree characterized by a smooth, greyish bark and a spreading crown. Its small, fragrant flowers are typically yellowish-white and arranged in axillary panicles. The fruit is a fleshy, three-lobed capsule that turns reddish or orange when mature.

Inside these capsules, the seeds are embedded in a pulpy, sweet-tasting aril.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Trichilia emetica contains medicinal compounds found primarily in its bark, leaves, and seeds. The bark is frequently harvested to prepare decoctions used for treating digestive issues and skin infections. Leaves are often crushed or boiled to serve as topical applications for inflammation or systemic ailments. The seeds can also be utilized in traditional remedies to address various parasitic conditions. These diverse plant components provide a broad range of therapeutic properties used by local communities.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Trichilia emetica targets various enzymes and signaling proteins, most notably inhibiting acetylcholinesterase and cyclooxygenase enzymes to produce anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective effects. The bioactive limonoids and alkaloids within the plant act as ligands that modulate receptor activity to reduce systemic inflammation. These secondary metabolites also demonstrate the ability to interfere with the enzymatic pathways involved in pathogen replication and microbial growth. By binding to specific protein sites, these compounds disrupt cellular processes that contribute to oxidative stress and pain signaling. This multi-target approach allows the plant extracts to exert complex pharmacological actions across different biological systems.

What are the therapeutic actions of Trichilia emetica?

Trichilia emetica has therapeutic properties characterized by its bitter, astringent, anthelmintic, antidiarrheal, and emetic actions.

The plant contains bioactive compounds that trigger a bitter taste, which helps stimulate digestive secretions. Its astringent qualities make it effective as an antidiarrheal agent by tightening body tissues to reduce fluid loss. Furthermore, the plant functions as an anthelmintic to expel intestinal worms from the host.

In specific medicinal applications, its emetic properties can be utilized to induce vomiting for detoxification purposes.

  • Antidiarrheal
  • Emetic

Topical applications of the plant material are frequently employed to combat various skin infections.

What are the herbal preparations of Trichilia emetica?

Trichilia emetica is prepared as aqueous decoctions from its bark, infusions from its leaves, and various topical applications using its crushed seeds or bark. The bark is frequently boiled in water to create a bitter liquid used for treating digestive issues and skin infections. Leaf infusions are often consumed to manage fever or as a general tonic for systemic ailments. Additionally, the seeds can be processed into pastes or oils to serve as topical treatments for external wounds and parasitic skin conditions. These diverse preparation methods allow different parts of the plant to address both internal and external health concerns.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Trichilia emetica can cause potential gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation upon direct contact, and unknown systemic toxicities if consumed in improper doses. While many parts of the plant are used in traditional medicine, the lack of rigorous clinical trials means that precise toxicological profiles are not fully established. Some users may experience nausea or stomach cramping when ingesting extracts derived from the seeds or bark. Topical application of certain components might trigger allergic dermatitis or localized redness in sensitive individuals. It is essential to exercise caution because the concentration of bioactive alkaloids can vary significantly between different specimens.
