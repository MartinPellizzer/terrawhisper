Vicia amurensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Vicia amurensis?

Vicia amurensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Fabales. This plant is further categorized into the family Fabaceae, which is known for its diverse range of legumes. Within this family, it is assigned to the genus Vicia, and its specific identification is completed by its species name, amurensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Vicia amurensis has prostrate to ascending stems that reach lengths of up to 30 centimeters and feature trifoliate leaves with linear to lanceolate leaflets.

The leaflets are typically 1 to 3 centimeters long and possess smooth margins with distinct stipules at the base of the petioles. Small, papilionaceous flowers emerge in clusters and display a color palette ranging from pale violet to deep purple. Each leaf structure is completed by slender, curling tendrils that facilitate climbing and support the plant's growth habit.

The root system consists of a taproot that allows the species to establish itself firmly in various soil types.

        What are the herbal preparations of Vicia amurensis?

        Vicia amurensis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the specific medicinal requirement. When used for internal ailments, the dried plant material is boiled in water to extract its bioactive compounds into a concentrated liquid. For milder therapeutic effects, the leaves are steeped in hot water to create a gentle tea. The plant can also be crushed into a fresh paste to be applied directly to the skin for external treatment. These various methods allow practitioners to tailor the potency and delivery of the herb to the patient's needs.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Vicia amurensis can cause toxic reactions in livestock such as cattle, sheep, and horses if consumed in large quantities. The plant contains alkaloids that may lead to neurological impairment or digestive distress in grazing animals. Symptoms of ingestion often include muscle tremors, weakness, and a lack of coordination. In severe cases, high levels of consumption can result in death due to systemic poisoning. Monitoring grazing areas for this species is essential to prevent accidental poisoning of livestock.
