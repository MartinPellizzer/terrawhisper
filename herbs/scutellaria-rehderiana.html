Scutellaria rehderiana

What's the taxonomical classification of Scutellaria rehderiana?

Scutelariia rehderiana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Lamiales. Further taxonomic refinement places it in the family Lamiaceae, specifically within the genus Scutellaria, with its unique identity defined by the species name rehderiana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Scutelariia rehderiana has opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaves with entire or slightly serrated margins and a decussate arrangement on its woody stems. The foliage typically presents a bright green color and a smooth surface texture. Its inflorescence consists of small, tubular flowers that emerge from the leaf axils in a characteristic labiate structure. These blossoms are often colored in shades of blue, purple, or white depending on the specific variety. The overall growth habit is that of a perennial herb or small subshrub suited for temperate forest understories.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to specific mountainous regions within central and southern China. It is most frequently documented growing in the provinces of Hubei, Hunan, and Guizhou. These populations typically inhabit temperate forest environments located at moderate elevations. The species thrives in the moist, shaded conditions found along rocky slopes and near mountain streams. Such localized distribution patterns suggest that the plant is highly adapted to the unique microclimates of these subtropical highlands.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Scutellaria rehderiana contains medicinal bioactive compounds primarily concentrated in its dried aerial parts, specifically the leaves and stems. These harvested plant components are typically processed into a traditional herbal formulation known as Huang Qin. The effectiveness of these parts is attributed to a high concentration of flavones, such as baicalin and wogonin, which possess significant anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Practitioners often use the ground powder or decoctions of these dried stems and leaves to treat respiratory infections and gastrointestinal issues. Because the therapeutic potency resides in the foliage and stalks, the roots are generally not the focus of its medicinal application.

What are the pharmacological activities of Scutellaria rehderiana?

This plant has potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant pharmacological activities driven primarily by its rich concentration of bioactive flavonoids. These chemical compounds work together to neutralize harmful free radicals and prevent oxidative stress within biological systems. By inhibiting specific inflammatory mediators, the plant helps reduce swelling and pain in various physiological models. Research indicates that these properties contribute to its potential use in managing chronic inflammatory conditions. Such multifaceted biological actions make it a significant subject for future drug development.

As discussed in a study, Scutelariia rehderiana Diels (SD) is often used as a substitute for SG due to its abundant plant resources. A total of 113 compounds were tentatively identified in SD through an integrated strategy. Among these, the contents of baicalein, wogonin, chrysin, oroxylin A 7-O-β-D-glucuronoside, pinocembrin, and baicalin were evaluated, with the latter four being higher in SD. The plant exhibited prominent anti-inflammarotary activity by measuring the inhibitory effect on the release of NO from lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-stimulated RAW 264.7 cells. However, SD was found to be less effective in this activity compared to SG.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds baicalin, ganhuangemin, ganhuangenin, oroxylin A, and wogonin. These specific flavonoids are primary bioactive constituents found within its roots and stems. Researchers study these molecules extensively for their potential anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. The presence of these compounds contributes to the plant's traditional use in various therapeutic applications. Each chemical component plays a unique role in the overall pharmacological profile of the species.

As discussed in a study, five compounds were isolated from the acetone extract of Scutelariia rehderiana. These compounds include oroxylin A, wogonin, baicalein, ganhuangenin, and ganhuangemin. Ganhuangemin was isolated from the plant for the first time. Additionally, baicalin was obtained from the methanol extract of S. rehderiana. Both baicalein and ganhuangenin exhibit stronger antioxidant activity than that of BHT.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, fever, cough, and various respiratory ailments. Its bioactive compounds, particularly flavones, work to reduce swelling and soothe irritated tissues in the body. Practitioners often utilize it to manage symptoms associated with bronchitis and asthma through its calming effects on the lungs. Additionally, it is frequently applied to address skin infections and other inflammatory dermatological conditions. This medicinal herb remains a significant component in traditional practices for stabilizing internal heat and systemic discomfort.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be processed into various herbal preparations including decoction, powder, tincture, and granules for medicinal use. A decoction is typically prepared by boiling the dried aerial parts in water to extract its bioactive compounds. For more convenient administration, the dried material is often milled into a fine powder. Tinctures are created by soaking the plant in alcohol to produce a concentrated liquid extract. Granules offer another standardized form that allows for easy mixing into warm liquids.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause significant adverse reactions including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dizziness, and potentially severe hepatotoxicity. Individuals consuming this species may experience acute gastrointestinal distress such as stomach upset or bowel irregularities. Neurological symptoms like dizziness can also manifest during or after ingestion. Most critically, the chemical constituents within the plant may lead to hepatotoxicity, which implies serious damage to the liver. Monitoring for these specific symptoms is essential for anyone exposed to the plant.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Dizziness
  • Hepatotoxicity
