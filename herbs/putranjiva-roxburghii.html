Putranjiva roxburghii

What's the taxonomical classification of Putranjiva roxburghii?

Putranjiva roxburghii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent through the order Malpighiales, it is situated within the family Putranjivaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its specific classification under the genus Putranjiva and the species roxburghii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Putranjiva roxburghii has a small to medium-sized tree reaching heights of up to 5 meters, characterized by a slender trunk and spreading branches. The leaves are simple, alternate, and elliptic to oblong in shape with an entire margin and an acute apex. It produces small, fragrant, yellowish-white flowers that are arranged in axillary or terminal panicles. The fruit is a small, globose drupe that turns black or dark purple when fully mature. The bark is generally smooth and greyish, while the root system is robust to support its woody structure.

What are the pharmacological activities of Putranjiva roxburghii?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antileishmanial, antioxidant, antiparasitic, and sperm motility increasing pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce oxidative stress and combat harmful inflammation within the body. The plant also demonstrates significant effectiveness in treating parasitic infections, including those caused by Leishmania. Furthermore, research indicates that extracts from the plant can help improve reproductive health by increasing sperm motility. These diverse biological properties make it a subject of intense interest in medicinal studies.

As discussed in a study, Putranjiva roxburghii leaves serve as the major ingredient in the Yataprasen herbal remedy. The leaf extract exhibited the highest inhibitory effects on LPS-induced TNF-α and iNOS expression among plant ingredient extracts. Three biflavonoids, including putraflavone, podocarpusflavone A, and amentoflavone, were isolated from the P. roxburghii leaf extract. These isolated biflavonoids showed inhibitory effects on LPS-induced PGE2 secretion and NO synthesis in RAW264.7 cells. Among these, amentoflavone exhibited the strongest anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting the expression of TNF-α, COX-2, and iNOS.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a variety of medicinal compounds including flavonoids such as kaempferol, isorhamnetin, apipegitrin, and podocarpusflavone A. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the complex chemical profile that characterizes the species. Researchers often study these substances for their potential therapeutic properties and biological activities. The presence of these diverse molecules suggests that the plant may possess significant pharmacological potential. Understanding the concentration of these individual compounds is essential for evaluating its medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, GC-MS profiling revealed that the Putranjiva roxburghii extract was rich in myo-inositol-4-C-methyl, azulene and desulphosinigrin. Quantitative investigation confirmed that the plant was rich in phenols, flavonoids and terpenoids. The study found an IC50 of 25.61 ± 0.57 µg/mL against the sodium stibogluconate sensitive strain of L. donovani. Additionally, the IC50 was 29.02 ± 1.21 µg/mL against the resistant strain. These results support the importance of Putranjiva roxburghii as a medicinal plant with potential for exploration as an antileishmanial agent.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the roots in water, as a powder known as churna, as a fresh paste called kalka, as an infusion, or through maceration in liquid. The decoction is frequently used in traditional medicine to extract potent bioactive compounds through heat. Practitioners often utilize the fine churna for easier ingestion or to mix into other therapeutic substances. Applying the kalka directly to the skin serves as a topical remedy for various ailments. Maceration and infusion processes provide gentler methods for drawing out the plant's medicinal properties into a liquid medium.
