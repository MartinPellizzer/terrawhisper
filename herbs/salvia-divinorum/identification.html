Botanical Identification of Mexican Sage (Salvia divinorum)

To identify Mexican Sage (Salvia divinorum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Mexican Sage, or Salvia divinorum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Lamiales, family Lamiaceae, genus Salvia, and species Salvia divinorum. This classification places it within the mint family, known for its aromatic properties and traditional medicinal uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Salvia divinorum is the scientific name for Mexican Sage, first described by the botanist Edward Lee Greene in 1895. It belongs to the Lamiaceae family and is also known by various synonyms such as Salvia mexicana and Salvia divinorum var. divinorum. Common names include Mexican sage, diviner's sage, and sacred sage, reflecting its cultural and spiritual significance.

Morphological Characteristics

Mexican Sage (Salvia divinorum) is an herbaceous perennial with a taproot system. Its stems are upright and branching, often reaching up to 1 meter in height. Leaves are opposite, ovate to lanceolate, with serrated margins and a strong aromatic scent. The inflorescence is a dense spike of small, violet-blue flowers. The fruit is a dry capsule containing tiny seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Mexican Sage, or Salvia divinorum, can be distinguished by its broad, heart-shaped leaves with a velvety texture and its distinct blue or purple flowers. It differs from similar species like Salvia spathacea by its leaf shape and the absence of glandular hairs on the leaves. Its aromatic scent and unique leaf structure also help in its identification.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Salvia divinorum is native to the cloud forests of southern Mexico, particularly in the states of Oaxaca and Chiapas. It thrives in humid, subtropical climates with mild temperatures. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at altitudes between 600 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Mexican Sage (Salvia divinorum) typically blooms from late spring to early summer, with flowers appearing in clusters. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested during its flowering stage for accurate botanical recognition.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Salvia divinorum belongs to the Lamiaceae family, sharing traits with other sage species. It is closely related to Salvia mexicana and Salvia ambigua, which are also native to Mexico. These species share similar morphological and genetic characteristics, reflecting their close phylogenetic ties within the genus Salvia.
