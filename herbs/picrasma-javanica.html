Picrasma javanica

What's the taxonomical classification of Picrasma javanica?

Picrasma javanica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the taxonomic order Sapindales. This plant is further categorized into the family Simaroubaceae and is situated within the genus Picrasma, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species javanica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Picrasma javanica has pinnately compound leaves that typically consist of 7 to 15 leaflets arranged oppositely along a central rachis.

These leaflets are elliptical or lanceolate in shape and possess entire or slightly serrated margins. The plant is a deciduous tree that produces small, inconspicuous greenish-white flowers in terminal panicles. Its fruit is a fleshy, globose drupe that transitions to a yellowish or brownish color upon maturity.

The bark is generally greyish and smooth, providing a structural contrast to the dense foliage.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Picrasma javanica contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its bitter roots and bark. The root is the most frequently harvested part because it possesses the highest density of quassinoids. These chemical constituents are utilized for their intense bitter properties to stimulate digestion and treat intestinal parasites. Practitioners also employ the bark in traditional formulations to address various ailments related to liver function. Because the potency varies between different parts of the plant, careful preparation is necessary to manage its strong physiological effects.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a complex profile of bioactive chemical constituents including quassinoids, bitter principles, triterpenoids, and alkaloids.

These specialized metabolites are primarily concentrated within the root bark and serve as the basis for its traditional therapeutic uses. The presence of various quassinoids contributes significantly to the intense bitterness observed in the plant material. Additionally, the triterpenoids and alkaloids provide a diverse range of pharmacological activities that researchers continue to investigate.

These combined medicinal compounds work together to define the biological potency of the species.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Picrasma javanica targets various signaling pathways through its bitter cucurbitacins, specifically inhibiting protein kinase C and modulating inflammatory mediators like nitric oxide synthase. These bioactive compounds interact with cellular membranes to disrupt signaling cascades associated with tumor cell proliferation and migration. By interfering with these specific protein functions, the plant exhibits potent anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor properties in biological models. The interaction with various enzymes also contributes to its traditional use in treating digestive ailments and managing metabolic imbalances. This multi-target approach allows the plant to exert systemic physiological effects through diverse biochemical mechanisms.

What are the therapeutic actions of Picrasma javanica?

Picrasma javanica has therapeutic properties that include acting as a powerful bitter, alterative, aperient, emetic, cholagogue, and anthelmintic agent.

Its intense bitter components stimulate the appetite and improve digestive secretions through the stimulation of gastric juices. As a cholagogue, the plant promotes the discharge of bile from the gallbladder to aid in the digestion of fats. The substance functions as an emetic to induce vomiting and an aperient to facilitate bowel movements when necessary. Furthermore, its anthelmintic actions allow it to effectively expel parasitic worms from the intestinal tract.

Finally, its role as an alterative helps to gradually restore healthy metabolic functions within the body.

What are the herbal preparations of Picrasma javanica?

Picrasma javaica is prepared primarily as a decoction, a tincture, or a powdered extract derived from its dried roots and rhizomes. These preparations utilize the plant's intense bitterness to serve as a powerful stomachic and aperient. When made into a decoction, the bitter principles are extracted through boiling the root material in water. Tinctures are often preferred for long-term storage and precise dosing of the active alkaloids. The powdered form allows for direct ingestion or incorporation into other medicinal formulations.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Picrasma javanica can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and systemic toxicity if ingested or handled improperly. The plant contains potent bitter principles like picrasic acid that act as intense purgatives, leading to vomiting and diarrhea. Direct contact with the plant's sap may result in localized dermatitis or painful skin inflammation. Because of its strong emetic properties, even small doses can trigger violent physical reactions in sensitive individuals. Caution is necessary as the toxic compounds can disrupt internal biological processes if systemic absorption occurs.
