Picrasma javanica

What's the taxonomical classification of Picrasma javanica?

Picrasma javanica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the taxonomic order Sapindales. This plant is further categorized into the family Simaroubaceae and is situated within the genus Picrasma, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species javanica.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Picrasma javanica contains high concentrations of bitter quassinoids, specifically picrasin, picrason, and quassin, which serve as its primary medicinal agents. These complex triterpenoid compounds are responsible for the plant's intense bitterness and its potent biological activities. They act as effective antimalarial and anthelmintic agents by targeting specific cellular processes in parasites. Additionally, these substances possess significant antimicrobial properties that can inhibit the growth of various bacteria and fungi. The presence of these specialized metabolites makes the plant a valuable subject in pharmacological research for developing new treatments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Picrasma javanica?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various gastrointestinal disorders, including intestinal worms, indigestion, and loss of appetite. Its intense bitterness makes it an effective emetic and purgative for clearing the digestive tract. Historically, it has been utilized as a bitter tonic to stimulate digestive secretions and improve metabolic functions. The plant's active compounds are often employed in traditional medicine to combat parasitic infections within the gut. These medicinal properties stem from the high concentration of bitter principles found in its roots and bark.

What are the herbal preparations of Picrasma javanica?

Picrasma javaica is prepared primarily as a decoction, a tincture, or a powdered extract derived from its dried roots and rhizomes. These preparations utilize the plant's intense bitterness to serve as a powerful stomachic and aperient. When made into a decoction, the bitter principles are extracted through boiling the root material in water. Tinctures are often preferred for long-term storage and precise dosing of the active alkaloids. The powdered form allows for direct ingestion or incorporation into other medicinal formulations.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Picrasma javanica can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and systemic toxicity if ingested or handled improperly. The plant contains potent bitter principles like picrasic acid that act as intense purgatives, leading to vomiting and diarrhea. Direct contact with the plant's sap may result in localized dermatitis or painful skin inflammation. Because of its strong emetic properties, even small doses can trigger violent physical reactions in sensitive individuals. Caution is necessary as the toxic compounds can disrupt internal biological processes if systemic absorption occurs.
