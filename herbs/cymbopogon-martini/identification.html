Botanical Identification of Lemon Grass (Cymbopogon martini)

To identify Lemon Grass (Cymbopogon martini) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Lemon grass, or Cymbopogon martini, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Liliopsida, order Poales, family Poaceae, genus Cymbopogon, and species Cymbopogon martini. These classifications reflect its place within the plant kingdom and its close relation to other grasses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Lemon Grass is Cymbopogon martini, first described by the botanist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck. It is also known by several synonyms, including Cymbopogon flexuosus and Cymbopogon citratus, which reflect its historical classification and regional variations. Common names like "Lemon Grass" and "West Indian Lemongrass" highlight its aromatic properties and widespread use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Lemon grass is a perennial herb with a rhizomatous root system that spreads horizontally. Its stems are cylindrical and aromatic, often reaching up to 1 meter in height. Leaves are elongated, narrow, and have a strong lemony scent. The inflorescence is a dense cluster of small, spike-like flower heads. Flowers are pale green to brown, and the fruit is a small, dry seed enclosed in a leathery casing.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Lemon grass can be distinguished by its tall, grass-like appearance with narrow, pale green leaves. It has a strong lemon scent when crushed, and its flowering stems are usually shorter than the leaves. It is often confused with lemongrass (Cymbopogon citratus), but it has a more robust stem and a slightly different aroma.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Lemon grass (Cymbopogon martini) is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, particularly India, Sri Lanka, and Southeast Asia. It thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained, loamy soils. It is commonly found at low to moderate altitudes, typically between 0 and 1,500 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Lemon grass typically blooms from late spring to early summer. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is most identifiable for harvesting during the early to mid-summer when the leaves are mature and aromatic.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Lemon grass (Cymbopogon martini) is closely related to other species within the Cymbopogon genus. It shares characteristics with related grasses in the Poaceae family, such as narrow leaves and aromatic essential oils. Its phylogenetic position reflects its close ties to other lemongrass and related aromatic grasses.
