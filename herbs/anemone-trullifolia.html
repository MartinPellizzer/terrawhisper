Anemone trullifolia

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anemone trullifolia has trifoliate leaves characterized by three distinct, deeply lobed leaflets that are typically ovate or elliptical in shape. These leaves emerge from a basal rosette and feature serrated or dentate margins along their edges. The plant produces solitary flowers that are typically white or pale pink, supported by long, slender pedicels. The floral structure includes several delicate petals and a central cluster of numerous stamens. Its overall growth habit is low-lying, adapted to the specific soil conditions of its native habitat.

      What are the herbal preparations of Anemone trullifolia?

      Anemone trullifolia is prepared primarily as an aqueous tincture, a concentrated decoction, or a topical ointment for external application. Practitioners often extract the active constituents through alcohol-based tinctures to preserve the volatile compounds. Decoctions made from the roots are sometimes used in traditional liquid preparations, though caution is required due to potential toxicity.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Anemone trullifolia can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and localized swelling upon direct contact with its toxic sap. The plant contains protoanemonin, a chemical compound that reacts painfully when it touches sensitive tissues. If the sap enters the eyes, it may lead to intense inflammation or temporary vision impairment. Ingestion of any part of the plant can result in gastrointestinal distress, including burning sensations in the mouth and throat. Because of these potent irritants, caution is necessary when handling the specimen to avoid systemic or topical reactions.
