Maclura tricuspidata

What's the taxonomical classification of Maclura tricuspidata?

Maclurura tricuspidata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Moraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Maclurura, and its unique species designation, tricuspidata.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Maclurura tricuspidata contains bioactive alkaloids, saponins, and various flavonoids that serve as its primary medicinal constituents. These chemical compounds are often utilized in traditional medicine to address inflammatory conditions and skin ailments. The presence of saponins specifically contributes to the plant's antimicrobial and antifungal properties. Additionally, the concentrated flavonoids act as potent antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress. Researchers continue to study these specific phytochemicals to better understand their potential pharmacological applications in modern medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Maclura tricuspidata?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, intestinal worms, and various inflammatory conditions through its medicinal properties. The bark and leaves often contain bioactive compounds that help combat parasitic infections in the digestive tract. Local communities also apply decoctions from the plant to soothe skin ailments and reduce swelling. Some traditional practices involve using the extracts to manage stomach aches and general discomfort. These applications vary depending on the specific part of the plant being utilized for the remedy.

What are the herbal preparations of Maclura tricuspidata?

Maclura tricuspidata is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or topical pastes using the plant's leaves, bark, or fruit. The bark is often boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction for internal use. Leaves may be steeped in hot water to produce a mild infusion. Additionally, the plant materials can be crushed into a paste and applied directly to the skin to treat localized ailments. These various methods allow for the extraction of specific phytochemicals depending on the desired therapeutic effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Maclurura tricuspidata can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if any part of the plant is ingested. The milky white sap contains irritants that may lead to redness, swelling, or blistering upon contact with human skin. If these juices enter the eyes, they can cause significant inflammation or painful stinging sensations. Ingesting the fruit or other plant tissues may result in nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. Due to these risks, careful handling is necessary to avoid accidental exposure to the sap.
