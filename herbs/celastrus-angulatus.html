Celastrus angulatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Celastrus angulatus?

Celastrus angulatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized within the phylum Streptophyta. Its hierarchical classification continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Celastrales. Within this order, it is a member of the family Celastraceae, which contains the genus Celastrus. Ultimately, the specific identity of this plant is defined by its species designation, anguletus.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Celastrus angulatus targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. The plant contains bioactive compounds like triterpenoids and flavonoids that inhibit the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. By suppressing cyclooxygenase-2 activity, these metabolites effectively reduce the production of prostaglandins associated with pain and inflammation. Furthermore, its chemical constituents interact with tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6 to modulate the immune response. These multifaceted protein interactions contribute to the plant's documented anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.

What are the herbal preparations of Celastrus angulatus?

Celastrus angulatus is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical wash using the bark, leaves, or roots of the plant. Practitioners often simmer the woody parts in water to extract potent alkaloids and tannins for internal use. The resulting liquid can be consumed to address various traditional ailments or used as a rinse for skin conditions. Some preparations involve grinding the dried plant material into a fine powder to be mixed with oils or fats. This powder can then be applied directly to the skin as a paste for localized relief.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Celastrus angulatus can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, including vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain, if any part of the plant is ingested. The presence of toxic compounds within the plant makes it dangerous for humans and animals to consume. Contact with the plant's tissues may also lead to skin irritation or localized dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Because of these potential health risks, the plant should be handled with caution to avoid accidental poisoning or topical reactions. There is currently limited research regarding the long-term systemic effects of its toxins.
