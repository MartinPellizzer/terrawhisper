Trollius macropetalus

What's the taxonomical classification of Trollius macropetalus?

Trollius macropetalus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Ranunculales. It is a specific representative of the family Ranunculaceae, falling under the genus Trollius, and is ultimately identified by its unique species name, macropetalus.

What are the therapeutic actions of Trollius macropetalus?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat inflammatory conditions, skin irritations, and various gastrointestinal disturbances. Historically, its components have been applied to soothe external swellings and localized redness on the skin. In traditional medicinal practices, extracts might be utilized to address internal imbalances related to digestion. However, its chemical properties require careful handling due to the potential for toxicity if administered incorrectly. Modern scientific validation for specific clinical uses remains limited compared to more common medicinal herbs.

What are the herbal preparations of Trollius macropetalus?

Trollius macropetalus is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical tincture derived from its dried aerial parts. Practitioners often simmer the plant material in water to extract its active chemical constituents for internal consumption. These liquid preparations are sometimes combined with other botanical elements to balance their physiological effects. Additionally, the plant can be processed into oils or poultices for external application to the skin. This variety of forms allows the herb to be utilized for different therapeutic purposes depending on the desired delivery method.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Trollius macropetalus can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of ranunculin. This chemical compound converts into protoanemonin when the plant tissues are damaged or crushed. Contact with the sap can lead to blistering or redness on the skin through contact dermatitis. If eaten, individuals may experience nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. It is essential to handle this species with caution to avoid these adverse physiological reactions.
