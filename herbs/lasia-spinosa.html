Lasia spinosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Lasia spinosa?

Lasia spinosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic journey continues through the order Alismatales and the family Araceae, eventually leading to its specific placement within the genus Lasia. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its unique species name, spinosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lasia spinosa has a woody, scrambling shrub habit characterized by stems covered in sharp, recurved prickles and large, palmately lobed leaves that can reach up to 40 centimeters in diameter. The foliage is notably soft to the touch due to a dense covering of fine, stellate hairs on both the upper and lower surfaces. These leaves are typically divided into several distinct segments that radiate from a central point. The plant produces small, yellowish flowers that are organized into compact clusters. Following pollination, it develops distinctive, fleshy, reddish-pink fruits that are often protected by bristly structures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across India, Bangladesh, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of China. It thrives primarily in moist, deciduous forests and along the banks of rivers or streams throughout its range. The species is often found in secondary forests and disturbed habitats where humidity levels remain consistently high. Its distribution is closely tied to monsoonal climate patterns that provide necessary seasonal rainfall. These specific environmental conditions allow it to spread effectively across much of the Indo-Malayan botanical realm.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds betulinic acid, biochanin A, ursolic acid, flavonoids, and terpenoids. These specific chemical constituents contribute to a wide range of potential therapeutic activities within the organism. The presence of flavonoids and terpenoids often provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the user. Additionally, the triterpenoids like ursolic acid and betulinic acid are frequently studied for their ability to support cellular health. Biochanin A further enhances the medicinal profile by offering phytoestrogenic properties.

As discussed in a study, Lasia spinosa contains major phytochemicals including biochanin A, ursolic acid, betulinic acid, and beta-sitosterol. The compounds biochanin A, ursolic acid, and betulinic acid possess highly significant (p ≤ 0.001) and dose-dependent anthelmintiic effects. When exposed to concentrations of 0.25, 0.50, and 1 mg/ml, these compounds cause paralysis and mortality in the test parasite H. diminuta. Among the phytochemicals, betulinic acid at 1 mg/ml demonstrated the best anthelmintic effect. This specific concentration caused mortality of the test parasites at 3.4 ± 0.66 h. Conversely, beta-sitosterol showed a very weak anthelmintic effect.

Its various parts are applied in traditional medicine to soothe irritation within the gastrointestinal tract. Practitioners often use decoctions to alleviate acute stomach pain and chronic intestinal diseases. Additionally, the plant's properties are utilized to manage different respiratory issues and systemic inflammatory conditions. These diverse medicinal applications demonstrate the plant's broad utility in addressing multiple ailments.

As discussed in a study, Lasia spinosa is traditionally utilized in Ayurveda and the AYUSH system of medicine to treat digestive disorders, respiratory issues, and inflammatory conditions. GC-MS analysis of methanol extracts from the leaves and fruits identified 9 compounds. The methanol leaves extract exhibited superior antioxidant activity with a DPPH IC50 value of 111.81 ± 8.99 µg/ml compared to the fruit extract IC50 of 174.81 ± 4.86 µg/ml. In the nitric oxide scavenging assay, the leaves extract showed a higher potency with an IC50 of 138.59 ± 1.50 µg/ml against the fruit extract IC50 of 196.47 ± 1.72 µg/ml. These findings suggest that Lasia spinosa possesses potent antioxidant and antimicrobial activities.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, nausea, vomiting, gastric irritation, and dizziness if it comes into contact with the body or is ingested. The presence of irritating substances in its tissues can lead to localized redness or discomfort upon topical contact. If swallowed, the plant frequently triggers significant gastric irritation along with episodes of nausea and vomiting. Some individuals may also experience a sense of dizziness following exposure or consumption. These various side effects highlight the potential toxicity of the species.
