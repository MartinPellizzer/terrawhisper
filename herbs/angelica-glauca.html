Angelica glauca

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica glauca?

Angelica glauca belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is further categorized under the order Apiales. This plant is a member of the family Apiaceae and is specifically identified within the genus Angelica, with its unique designation being the species glauca.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica glauca has a perennial rootstock that produces upright, hollow stems reaching up to 100 centimeters in height with lanceolate, glaucous leaves arranged alternately along the stalk. The foliage consists of large, compound leaves that are deeply divided into narrow, linear segments with a distinct bluish-green hue. Small, white flowers are clustered in dense, compound umbels that sit atop the sturdy stems during the blooming season. These inflorescences are supported by peduncles that lack the heavy pubescence found in related species. The overall structure of the plant is characterized by its robust, erect growth habit and smooth, waxy leaf surfaces.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subarctic and arctic regions of North America, spanning from Alaska across much of Canada and into the northernmost parts of the United States. It thrives primarily in tundra ecosystems and alpine environments where rocky or gravelly soils are present. Its distribution is closely tied to cold, high-latitude climates and high-altitude mountain ranges. Many populations are found in moist meadows or near stream banks within these northern territories. The species occupies a niche specifically adapted to the harsh, sparsely vegetated landscapes of the far north.

What are the pharmacological activities of Angelica glauca?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, anticancer, antioxidant, and gastroprotective pharmacological activities. Its diverse chemical profile allows it to neutralize free radicals and mitigate oxidative stress within biological systems. These properties contribute to its ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogens and reduce swelling in inflamed tissues. Furthermore, the plant exhibits potential in suppressing tumor cell proliferation and protecting the gastric mucosa from acid-induced damage. These multifaceted therapeutic effects make it a significant subject for pharmacological research.

As discussed in a study, Angelica glauca root extract exhibits potent antioxidant activity at 68 μg/mL and was found to be non-toxic in acute oral toxicity tests. In ethanol-induced gastric ulcer models, a dose of 500 mg/kg significantly reduced the ulcer-index, enhanced gastric pH, and reduced pepsin levels. At the 500 mg/kg dose, SOD, CAT, GSH, and LPO levels were 173 U/g, 78 U/g, 195 μM GSH/g, and 194 nM MDA/g of tissue, respectively. The extract also demonstrates anti-inflammatory properties, as the IL-6 level was reduced by 31.2 % at 500 mg/kg and 57.1 % at 250 mg/kg. Additionally, the TNF-α concentration was reduced by 32 % and 60 % at these respective doses.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several potent medicinal compounds including ligustiide, butylidenephthalides, ferulic acid, p-hydroxyphenethyl trans-ferulate, and phthalides. These bioactive molecules are primarily responsible for the plant's diverse pharmacological properties. The presence of phthalides and butylidenephthalides often contributes to anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective effects. Additionally, ferulic acid and p-hydroxyphenethyl trans-ferulate provide significant antioxidant activity within the biological system. Together, these chemical constituents form a complex profile useful in various therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, the hydroalcoholic extract of Angelica glauca root contains significant levels of phenols, flavonoids, and tannins. Major bioactive compounds identified via LC-TOF-MS include ligustilide, butylidenephthalides, β-bisabolene, and p-hydroxyphenethyl trans-ferulate. The extract exhibited potent antioxidant activity at 68 μg/mL. At a 500 mg/kg dose, the treatment resulted in SOD levels of 173 U/g, CAT levels of 78 U/g, GSH levels of 195 μM GSH/g, and MDA levels of 194 nM MDA/g. Furthermore, the IL-6 level was reduced by 31.2 % at a 500 mg/kg dose and by 57.1 % at a 250 mg/kg dose. TNF-α concentration was also reduced by 32 % and 60 % at those respective doses.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating asthma, bronchitis, colds, coughs, and various respiratory infections. It functions as a natural remedy to soothe irritated airways and clear congestion from the lungs. Many traditional practitioners rely on its properties to alleviate the symptoms of heavy chest pressure and shortness of breath. The plant's medicinal components help manage the discomfort associated with persistent coughing fits. By addressing these specific ailments, it serves as a vital tool in managing seasonal and chronic lung conditions.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause nausea, dizziness, gastrointestinal upset, and photosensitivity in individuals who consume it or come into contact with it. These adverse reactions often manifest as digestive distress or stomach discomfort shortly after ingestion. Some people may also experience lightheadedness or a spinning sensation known as dizziness. Furthermore, the plant contains compounds that can lead to photosensitivity, making the skin more vulnerable to sunburn from UV exposure. It is important to handle this species with caution to avoid these various physical complications.
