Angelica glauca

What's the taxonomical classification of Angelica glauca?

Angelica glauca belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is further categorized under the order Apiales. This plant is a member of the family Apiaceae and is specifically identified within the genus Angelica, with its unique designation being the species glauca.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Angelica glauca has a perennial rootstock that produces upright, hollow stems reaching up to 100 centimeters in height with lanceolate, glaucous leaves arranged alternately along the stalk. The foliage consists of large, compound leaves that are deeply divided into narrow, linear segments with a distinct bluish-green hue. Small, white flowers are clustered in dense, compound umbels that sit atop the sturdy stems during the blooming season. These inflorescences are supported by peduncles that lack the heavy pubescence found in related species. The overall structure of the plant is characterized by its robust, erect growth habit and smooth, waxy leaf surfaces.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subarctic and arctic regions of North America, spanning from Alaska across much of Canada and into the northernmost parts of the United States. It thrives primarily in tundra ecosystems and alpine environments where rocky or gravelly soils are present. Its distribution is closely tied to cold, high-latitude climates and high-altitude mountain ranges. Many populations are found in moist meadows or near stream banks within these northern territories. The species occupies a niche specifically adapted to the harsh, sparsely vegetated landscapes of the far north.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, sandy or gravelly soil and a cool, temperate climate to thrive in rocky alpine environments.

It should be sown directly into its permanent location because the root system is highly sensitive to transplanting. Full sun to partial shade is ideal for promoting healthy foliage development throughout the growing season. Regular moisture is necessary, but the substrate must never remain waterlogged to prevent root rot.

Once established, this hardy perennial is relatively low-maintenance and can withstand significant temperature fluctuations.

What are the pharmacological activities of Angelica glauca?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its documented anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, anticancer, antioxidant, and gastroprotective activities. The antioxidant properties of Angelica glauca help neutralize harmful free radicals within biological systems. Its antibacterial effects allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms effectively. Additionally, the plant exhibits anticancer capabilities that may inhibit the growth of malignant cells. Finally, its gastroprotective mechanisms provide a defense against stomach lining damage and ulceration.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", the root extract of Angelica glauca exhibits potent antioxidant activity at 68 μg/mL and was found to be non-toxic in acute oral toxicity tests in rats. In ethanol-induced gastric ulcer models, a dose of 500 mg/kg significantly reduced the ulcer-index, enhanced gastric pH, and reduced pepsin levels. At a 500 mg/kg dose, SOD, CAT, GSH and LPO levels were 173 U/g, 78 U/g, 195 μM GSH/g and 194 nM MDA/g of tissue, respectively. The treatment reduced IL-6 levels by 31.2 % at 500 mg/Kg and 57.1 % at 250 mg/Kg, while TNF-α concentration was reduced by 32 % and 60 % at those same doses. These results highlight the gastroprotective efficacy of the plant due to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds ligustilide, (Z)-ligustilide, butylidenephthalides, phthalides, and p-hydroxyphenethyl trans-ferulate. These specific chemical constituents are primary components of the plant's bioactive profile. The various phthalides and butylidenephthalides serve as significant markers for its therapeutic potential. Furthermore, the presence of ligustilide and its (Z)-isomer contributes to the unique pharmacological properties observed in the species. The inclusion of p-hydroxyphenethyl trans-ferulate adds to the complex chemical diversity of its medicinal makeup.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", phytochemical quantification of the Angelica glauca hydroalcoholic extract revealed significant levels of phenols, flavonoids, and tannins. Major bioactive compounds identified via LC-TOF-MS include ligustilide, butylidenephthalides, β-bisabolene, and p-hydroxyphenethyl trans-ferulate. The extract exhibited potent antioxidant activity at 68 μg/mL. At a dose of 500 mg/kg, the treatment resulted in SOD, CAT, GSH, and LPO levels of 173 U/g, 78 U/g, 195 μM GSH/g, and 194 nM MDA/g of tissue, respectively. Furthermore, the level of IL-6 was reduced by 31.2 % at a 500 mg/kg dose and 57.1 % at a 250 mg/kg dose, while TNF-α concentration was reduced by 32 % and 60 % at those same doses.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating ague, asthma, bronchitis, colds, and various gastric ailments. Its medicinal properties are often applied to soothe respiratory distress and clear congestion during illnesses. It serves as a traditional remedy to alleviate the discomfort of seasonal colds and persistent bronchial inflammation. Additionally, the plant is utilized to manage digestive issues and settle various gastric ailments. These diverse applications highlight its importance in historical herbal medicine.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Angelica glauca is utilized for treating ague, cold, shivering, and gastric ailments. This specific plant species showed a high fidelity level of 97%. Within the study, the ague condition was associated with a high informant consensus factor of 0.98. The research involved interviewing 180 informants from 6 villages, with 30 individuals from each village. Among these participants, there were 20 male and 10 female informants per village.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause nausea, dizziness, gastrointestinal upset, and photosensitivity in individuals who consume it or come into contact with it. These adverse reactions often manifest as digestive distress or stomach discomfort shortly after ingestion. Some people may also experience lightheadedness or a spinning sensation known as dizziness. Furthermore, the plant contains compounds that can lead to photosensitivity, making the skin more vulnerable to sunburn from UV exposure. It is important to handle this species with caution to avoid these various physical complications.
