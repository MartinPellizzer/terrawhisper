Euphorbia pekinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia pekinensis?

Euphorbia pekinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae. At the most specific taxonomic levels, it is categorized under the genus Euphorbia, with its unique identity defined by the species name pekinenensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia pekinenensis has an erect, herbaceous growth habit characterized by smooth, cylindrical stems that reach heights of approximately 30 to 50 centimeters. The leaves are arranged in an alternate pattern along the stem and possess an elliptical to lanceolate shape with entire margins. Small, inconspicuous flowers are organized into specialized structures called cyathia, which are typical of the Euphorbia genus. These flowering units are often surrounded by colorful, petal-like bracts that attract pollinators. The plant also features a shallow, fibrous root system that helps anchor its delicate structure in moist soil.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia pekinenensis contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its whole aerial parts, including the stems, leaves, and the milky latex expressed when the plant is damaged. The latex is frequently harvested for its purgative and topical applications in traditional practices. These harvested components are often processed into decoctions or ointments to treat various skin ailments and inflammatory conditions. Some practitioners also utilize the dried herb to address specific digestive issues through controlled internal administration. Careful dosage is essential because the caustic nature of the plant's sap can cause significant irritation to the skin and mucous membranes.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, mucosal irritation, eye irritation, vomiting, nausea, abdominal pain, and diarrhea if handled or ingested. The milky sap contains diterpene esters that act as severe irritants upon contact with sensitive tissues. Direct exposure to the eyes can result in intense inflammation and temporary vision impairment. If the sap is swallowed, it frequently triggers gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Such ingestion often leads to persistent diarrhea as the body attempts to expel the toxic substances.
