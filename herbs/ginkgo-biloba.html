Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) is a ancient tree species known for its distinctive fan-shaped leaves and remarkable longevity, commonly used in traditional medicine for its cognitive and circulatory benefits.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Ginkgo (Ginkgo Biloba)?

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) is a species of tree known for its distinctive fan-shaped leaves and the medicinal use of its seeds and leaves in traditional and modern herbal medicine.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Ginkgo biloba is a deciduous tree belonging to the family Ginkgoaceae, characterized by its unique fan-shaped leaves and is the only living species in its genus and family.

Common Names and Synonyms

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) is commonly known by names such as Chinese male tree, sacred tree, and white fruit tree, and is also referred to as the maidenhair tree in some regions.

Plant Description and Morphology

Ginkgo biloba is a deciduous tree with fan-shaped leaves that turn yellow in autumn, a distinctive bipinnate structure, and a thick, rough bark that becomes fissured with age.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Ginkgo biloba is native to eastern Asia, particularly China, where it grows naturally in a variety of forested and mountainous regions.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, bark, and seeds of the Ginkgo biloba tree are the primary plant parts used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Ginkgo biloba contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, terpene lactones, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic effects.

Pharmacological Properties

Ginkgo biloba exhibits a range of pharmacological properties including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, and vasodilatory effects, primarily attributed to its flavonoids and terpene lactones which enhance cerebral blood flow and reduce oxidative stress.

Mechanisms of Action

Ginkgo biloba exerts its effects through a combination of antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective mechanisms, primarily by increasing cerebral blood flow, enhancing oxygen and nutrient delivery to brain cells, and reducing oxidative stress and free radical damage.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Ginkgo is used to treat conditions such as dementia, memory loss, peripheral artery disease, tinnitus, intermittent claudication, anxiety, depression, and macular degeneration.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) is commonly prepared as a dried leaf extract, tincture, capsule, or powder, with the leaves being the primary part used for its medicinal properties.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Ginkgo biloba for adults typically ranges from 120 to 240 milligrams per day, divided into two or three doses, with standardized extracts containing 24% to 26% flavoglycosides and 6% to 12% terpene lactones being most commonly used.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While generally considered safe when used in appropriate doses, Ginkgo biloba may cause side effects such as headaches, digestive upset, allergic reactions, and increased risk of bleeding, particularly in individuals with a history of bleeding disorders or those taking anticoagulant medications.

Drug Interactions

Ginkgo biloba may interact with medications such as blood thinners, anticonvulsants, and antidepressants, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or altering the effectiveness of these drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Ginkgo biloba is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as stomach upset, headaches, or allergic reactions, and it should be avoided by pregnant women, those with bleeding disorders, and individuals taking anticoagulant medications.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Ginkgo biloba is typically cultivated in temperate climates, requiring well-drained soil and full sun exposure, with proper pruning and care to ensure healthy growth and optimal leaf production for medicinal use.

Processing and Extraction

Processing ginkgo biloba involves drying the leaves, which are harvested in late summer or early autumn, and then grinding them into a fine powder or extracting them using ethanol to create concentrated supplements.

Storage and Shelf Life

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and extend its shelf life, which is typically up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Ginkgo biloba has shown potential benefits in improving cognitive function, enhancing circulation, and reducing oxidative stress, with some studies supporting its use in treating conditions such as dementia, ADHD, and peripheral vascular disease.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Ginkgo biloba has been used for thousands of years in traditional Chinese medicine to enhance cognitive function, improve memory, and treat circulatory disorders.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, ginkgo biloba is considered a dietary supplement and is regulated under the same framework as other herbal remedies, with varying degrees of oversight depending on the region.
