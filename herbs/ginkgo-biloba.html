Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Ginkgo biloba, also known as the maidenhair tree, is a ancient deciduous tree native to China and is widely used in herbal medicine for its purported cognitive-enhancing and circulatory benefits.

What is Ginkgo (Ginkgo Biloba)?

Ginkgo biloba, also known as the maidenhair tree, is a ancient, dioecious tree native to China and is widely used in herbal medicine for its leaves, which contain bioactive compounds such as flavonoids and terpene lactones.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Ginkgo is Ginkgo biloba. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Ginkgophyta, class Ginkgoopsida, order Ginkgoales, family Ginkgoaceae, genus Ginkgo, and species Ginkgo biloba. It is commonly known as the ginkgo tree in North America and the sacred tree in parts of Asia. The common names also include the maidenhair tree in Europe and the Chinese silver apricot in China. Synonyms for this plant include Ginkgo adiantifolia (botanical) and yang yuan shu, yang yuan tree, and bi gua shu (regional).

Plant Description and Morphology

Ginkgo is a deciduous tree that can grow up to 30 meters tall with a broad, spreading crown and a distinctive, symmetrical shape.

Its leaves are fan-shaped, with a bipinnate structure, and are arranged spirally along the branches. The tree produces small, inconspicuous flowers in early spring, with separate male and female trees, and the female trees produce orange-yellow, fleshy fruits. The root system is shallow and fibrous, spreading outwards to anchor the tree and absorb nutrients from the soil.

The bark is smooth and grayish-brown, with a rough, fissured texture, while the leaves are thick, leathery, and have a faint, spicy aroma.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Ginkgo biloba is native to China and is widely distributed across eastern Asia, including regions such as Japan, Korea, and parts of Southeast Asia, with historical cultivation extending to Europe and North America.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, bark, and seeds of the Ginkgo biloba tree have been used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly utilized part for their active compounds such as flavonoids and terpene lactones.

Phytochemical Composition

The phytochemical composition of Ginkgo biloba includes flavonoids, terpene lactones, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Ginkgo biloba exhibits pharmacological properties including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective effects, primarily due to its flavonoids and terpene lactones, which enhance cerebral blood flow and reduce oxidative stress.

Mechanisms of Action

Ginkgo biloba exerts its effects through multiple mechanisms, including enhancing cerebral blood flow, acting as an antioxidant by scavenging free radicals, and modulating neurotransmitter levels such as serotonin and dopamine.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Ginkgo biloba is traditionally used in herbal medicine to enhance cognitive function, improve circulation, and support memory, particularly in conditions such as dementia and age-related cognitive decline.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Ginkgo biloba is typically prepared as a standardized extract, often in the form of capsules, tablets, or liquid tinctures, with the most common dosage ranging from 120 to 240 mg per day, usually divided into two or three doses.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Ginkgo biloba for adults typically ranges from 120 to 240 mg per day, divided into two or three doses, with standardized extracts containing 24% to 26% flavoglycosides and terpene lactones being preferred for consistency and efficacy.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Ginkgo biloba is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses for short-term use, but it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, headaches, dizziness, and allergic reactions, and should be avoided by individuals with a known allergy to the plant or those taking certain medications like anticoagulants.

Drug Interactions

Ginkgo biloba may interact with medications such as anticoagulants, antiplatelet drugs, antidepressants, and anticonvulsants, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or altering the effectiveness of these medications.

Toxicity and Precautions

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, headaches, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and it should be avoided by those with a known allergy to the tree or its components.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Ginkgo biloba is typically cultivated in warm climates with well-drained soil, requiring full sun exposure and minimal pruning, with harvesting occurring in late summer to early autumn when the seeds are fully mature.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Ginkgo biloba involve harvesting the leaves, drying them, and then using methods such as solvent extraction or supercritical fluid extraction to obtain the active compounds like flavonoids and terpene lactones.

Storage and Shelf Life

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Ginkgo biloba has shown potential benefits in improving cognitive function, enhancing circulation, and reducing symptoms of conditions such as dementia and Alzheimer's disease, with some studies suggesting it may also have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Ginkgo biloba has been used for centuries in traditional Chinese medicine to improve cognitive function, enhance memory, and promote circulation, with historical records dating back over 2,000 years.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Ginkgo biloba is classified as a dietary supplement rather than a pharmaceutical drug, and its regulation varies by region, with some requiring labeling standards and others imposing restrictions on specific formulations.
