Ginkgo biloba

What's the taxonomical classification of Ginkgo biloba?

Ginkgo biloba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Ginkgoidae. The plant's taxonomic progression continues through the order Ginkgoales and the family Ginkgoaceae, which leads to its specific designation within the genus Ginkgo. Ultimately, its scientific identity is completed by its species name, biloba.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ginkgo biloba has unique, fan-shaped leaves with a central notch that can be deeply cleft or nearly entire, arranged alternately on woody branches. These leaves feature a distinctive reticulate venation pattern where veins radiate from the base without forming a network. The tree produces large, woody, cone-like structures on male trees and fleshy, ovule-containing structures on female trees. Its bark is thick, gray, and deeply fissured, providing protection for the long-lived trunk. The overall growth habit is typically pyramidal when young but becomes more spreading and irregular as the specimen matures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a very limited range centered primarily around the Zhejiang province in eastern China. While its natural occurrence is confined to these specific regions, it has been widely introduced to various temperate climates across the globe. Today, Ginkgo biloba is commonly cultivated in urban parks and botanical gardens throughout North America, Europe, and parts of Asia. It thrives in diverse environments where it is often used as a hardy street tree due to its resistance to pollution. This global distribution is entirely the result of human cultivation rather than natural expansion.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ginkgo biloba contains medicinal compounds primarily extracted from its fan-shaped leaves. While the seeds can be consumed in specific preparations, they are often toxic if not processed correctly and are rarely used in standard supplements. The leaves are the most significant part because they are rich in flavonoids and terpenoids. These specific bioactive substances are believed to enhance cognitive function and improve blood circulation. Most commercial extracts focus exclusively on the leaf to ensure a safe and standardized dose of active ingredients.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ginkgo biloba?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including cognitive enhancement, antioxidant activity, blood circulation-promoting effects, anti-platelet properties, and neuroprotection. The flavonoids and terpenoids within the leaves provide potent antioxidant activity that neutralizes free radicals to prevent cellular damage. Its ability to improve blood circulation-promoting functions helps increase oxygen delivery to vital organs. Furthermore, its anti-platelet action reduces blood viscosity and helps prevent excessive clotting. These combined mechanisms contribute to significant neuroprotection by shielding neurons from degeneration. Ultimately, these processes support cognitive enhancement by maintaining healthy brain function.

According to a study published by "Current opinion in clinical nutrition and metabolic care", the daily intake of ginkgo biloba extract can enhance cognitive performance. This plant extract has also been proved to delay cognitive decline in dementia. The effects of nutritional supplements are modest but do not seem to be very different from those of medicinal or investigational cognition-enhancing or anti-dementia drugs.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, terpene trilactones, bilobalide, ginkgolides, and ginkgolic acids as its primary medicinal compounds. The flavonoids serve as potent antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress. Within the terpene trilactone category, specific substances known as ginkgolides and bilobalide provide significant neuroprotective and circulatory benefits. While these compounds offer therapeutic potential, the plant also contains ginkgolic acids which can trigger allergic reactions in some individuals. Together, these diverse chemical constituents define the pharmacological profile of Ginkgo biloba.

According to a study published by "Naunyn-Schmiedeberg's archives of pharmacology", bilobalide is a constituent of Ginkgo biloba that inhibits NMDA-induced phospholipase A2 activation and phospholipid breakdown. In rat hippocampal slices, glutamate at 1 mM caused a large release of choline through phospholipid breakdown. Bilobalide inhibited this NMDA-induced efflux of choline with an IC50 value of 2.3 microM and prevented the formation of lyso-PC and GPCh. When administered systemically at 20 mg/kg i.p., bilobalide completely inhibited the choline release caused by NMDA in vivo. Furthermore, bilobalide almost totally suppressed the convulsions observed in NMDA-treated rats.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cognitive impairment, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, age-related macular degeneration, and improving cerebral blood flow. Many people utilize its extracts to enhance mental clarity and support memory retention in aging populations. It is believed to work by increasing circulation to the brain and protecting neurons from oxidative stress. Some studies suggest it may help mitigate the progressive symptoms of neurodegenerative disorders. These therapeutic properties make it a popular supplement for maintaining overall neurological health.

According to a study published by "CNS & neurological disorders drug targets", Ginkgo is known for its anti-oxidative and anti-inflammatoty responses. This plant has shown anti-Alzheimer's properties in various in vivo and in vitro studies. Its diverse phytochemicals play a protective role against amyloid-beta-induced neurotoxicity and improve cognitive and learning impairments. These chemicals scavenge free radicals, lower the amyloid burden, improve memory dysfunction, and inhibit acetylcholinesterase activity. Ginkgo and other medicinal plants can help reduce AD pathogenesis by controlling different pathways and could be used as a therapeutic agent against the symptoms.

The main health conditions this plant is used for are shown in the list below.

  • Alzheimer's disease
  • Parkinson's disease
  • Age-related macular degeneration
  • Cognitive impairment
  • Cerebral circulation disorders

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a standardized extract, a tincture, an infusion, a powder, or a decoction to utilize its medicinal properties. Most clinical research focuses on a standardized extract that contains specific concentrations of flavone glycosides and terpenoids. Practitioners often create a tincture by soaking the leaves in alcohol to extract active compounds for liquid dosing. For milder preparations, an infusion involves steeping the leaves in hot water, whereas a decoction uses boiling to extract substances from tougher plant materials. Additionally, the dried leaves can be processed into a fine powder for easier consumption in capsules or dietary supplements.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a variety of adverse reactions including headache, dizziness, increased bleeding risk, nausea, stomach upset, diarrhea, palpitations, anxiety, insomnia, and skin rash. Some users may experience digestive discomfort such as nausea, stomach upset, or diarrhea after consumption. Neurological or cardiovascular issues might manifest as headache, dizziness, palpitations, or feelings of anxiety. Sleep disturbances like insomnia can also occur as a side effect. Additionally, certain individuals might develop a skin rash or experience a heightened risk of bleeding.
