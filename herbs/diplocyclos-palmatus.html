What's the taxonomical classification of Diplocyclos palmatus?

Diplocyclos palmatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Cucurbitales and the family Cucurbitaceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Diplocyclos, with its specific identification belonging to the species palmatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Diplocyclos palmatus has palmately lobed leaves that typically feature five to seven distinct, finger-like leaflets radiating from a central point. These leaves are characterized by a deep, three-dimensional structure due to the prominent, winged petioles that extend along the leaf segments. The plant is a climbing herbaceous vine that uses tendrils to anchor itself to surrounding vegetation for support. Its small, yellow, actinomorphic flowers emerge from the leaf axils and eventually develop into specialized fruits. These fruits are distinctively shaped like small, winged wheels or stars, which facilitate seed dispersal through wind or water.

What are the pharmacological activities of Diplocyclos palmatus?

These diverse medicinal properties highlight the therapeutic importance of Diplocyclos palmatus in modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, Diplocyclos palmatus serves as a wide source of tetracyclic triterpens called cucurbitaicins and functions as a potent antioxidant, anti-diabetic, and anticancer bio-resource. The plant exhibits promising DPPH radical scavenging activity of 25.06 ± 0.1 µgAAE/g DW when using steam bath assisted extraction. It shows inhibition of the α-amylase enzyme at 68.68% and the α-glucosidase enzyme at 56.27% through MAE and UAE. Furthermore, fruit extracts demonstrate potent anticancer activity against the MCF-7 breast cancer cell line with an LC50 of 44.27 µg/mL. The extracts also target the HT-29 colon cancer cell line with an LC50 of 46.88 µg/mL. These bioactivities suggest the plant could be used to develop novel drugs for managing diabetes, cancer, and oxidative stress related disorders.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal triterpene compounds cucurbitacin B, cucurbitacin I, and various other cucurbitacins. These bioactive substances are primarily responsible for the characteristic bitter taste found within the plant tissues. Research indicates that these cucurbitacins possess significant anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor properties in pharmacological studies. Specifically, cucurbitacin B and cucurbitacin I are frequently studied for their ability to inhibit specific signaling pathways in cancer cells. The complex chemical structure of these molecules allows them to interact with various biological targets.

As discussed in a study, Diplocyclos palmatus (L.) C. Jeffrey serves as a wide source of tetracyclic triterpens called cucurbitaicns. Using continuous shaking extraction at a 1:30 SS ratio (g/mL), 80 rpm, 150 µm mean particle size, 30 min, and 50°C, the highest cucurbitacin I content was 2.345 ± 0.1686 mg/g DW. At a 1:40 g/mL SS ratio and 60 min time, the highest yield of cucurbitacin B was 1.584 ± 0.15 mg/g DW. Steam bath assisted extraction yielded 1.437 ± 0.03 mg/g DW of CU I, 0.782 ± 0.10 mg/g DW of CU B, and 2.17 ± 0.35 mg/g DW of CU I + B, alongside a DPPH radical scavenging activity of 25.06 ± 0.1 µgAAE/g DW. Furthermore, extracts showed inhibition of α-amylase at 68.68% and α-glucosidase at 56.27%, while anticancer activity against MCF-7 and HT-29 cell lines showed LC50 values of 44.27 and 46.88 µg/mL, respectively.
