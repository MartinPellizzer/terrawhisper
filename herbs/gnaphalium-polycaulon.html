Gnaphalium polycaulon

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gnaphaliuum polycaulon has a perennial growth habit characterized by multiple ascending to erect stems that are densely covered in white, woolly, tomentose hairs. These stems often branch extensively from the base to form a compact, shrubby structure. The leaves are typically small, lanceolate to linear, and possess a grayish-green appearance due to the thick coating of fine pubescence. Small, discoid flower heads are arranged in dense clusters or panicles at the ends of the branches. This dense hairiness serves as a key morphological adaptation for moisture retention in its native habitats.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and semi-arid regions of the southwestern United States, specifically spanning across California, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, and parts of New Mexico and Texas. It thrives primarily in desert scrublands, sagebrush steppes, and rocky slopes where drainage is rapid. The species is most frequently encountered in low-elevation habitats characterized by sandy or gravelly soils. Its range extends southward into the northern reaches of Mexico as well. These environmental preferences allow it to colonize vast stretches of the Mojave and Sonoran Deserts.
