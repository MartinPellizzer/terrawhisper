Lycium chinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Lycium chinense?

Lycium chinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Solanales. It is further categorized under the family Solanaceae, which places it among a diverse group of flowering plants. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Lycium and the species chinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lycium chinense has woody, perennial stems that are often spiny, with leaves that are simple, elliptical or lanceolate, and arranged alternately along the branches. The plant typically reaches a height of 0.5 to 2 meters and exhibits a sprawling or erect growth habit. Its small, bell-shaped flowers are usually purple or bluish and emerge from the leaf axils. These blossoms develop into bright red, oblong berries that are fleshy and contain numerous small seeds. The overall structure allows the shrub to adapt to various environments through its resilient woody framework.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to various regions across China, specifically spreading throughout central and southern provinces like Henan, Hebei, and Shandong. It thrives primarily in temperate and subtropical climates where it can find suitable soil conditions. While its primary range is concentrated in East Asia, some varieties have been introduced to other parts of the world for cultivation. The species is frequently found in disturbed areas, hillsides, and along riverbanks within its natural habitats. Its ability to adapt to different environments allows it to occupy diverse ecological niches across its native landscape.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Lycium chinense contains medicinal components primarily found in its fleshy dried fruits, which are commonly known as goji berries. The fruit is harvested and dried to preserve its high concentration of polysaccharides and antioxidants used in traditional therapies. While the berries are the most frequently utilized part, the roots and bark are sometimes employed in specific herbal formulations for their unique properties. These various plant parts are often prepared as decoctions, powders, or infused teas to support various health functions. What are the pharmacological activities of Lycium chinense?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including anti-aging, anti-inflammatory, anti-diabetic, antioxidant, and hepatoprotective properties. Its rich concentration of polysaccharides and vitamins helps neutralize free radicals to combat oxidative stress. These bioactive compounds also play a critical role in reducing systemic inflammation throughout the body. Furthermore, the plant supports metabolic health by assisting in blood sugar regulation and protecting liver cells from toxic damage. Such diverse therapeutic effects make it a valuable subject for modern medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, Lycium chinense has been advertised as a superfood with healthy properties. Its use in China can be traced back over the last 2000 years, with fruits documented for anti-aging, improving eyesight, and nourishment as early as 500C.E. The pharmacological foundations of these traditional uses include polysaccharides, zeaxanthin dipalmitate, vitamins, betaine, and mixed extracts. These specific components are reported to be responsible for anti-aging, improving eyesight, and anti-fatigue effects. Only L. chinense and L. barbarum have been transformed into globally traded commodities.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including flavonoid, anthocyanins, catechins, lyciumins A-D, and lyciumosides I-III. These bioactive molecules contribute to the plant's potent antioxidant and pharmacological properties. The presence of anthocyanins and flavonoids specifically helps in neutralizing free radicals within biological systems. Furthermore, the unique lyciumins A-D and lyciumosides I-III serve as specialized constituents that distinguish its chemical profile. Together, these substances work synergistically to provide various therapeutic benefits.

As discussed in a study, Lycium chinense leaf extract contains a profile of mineral and phenolic compounds that provide antioxidant and antimicrobial potential. The study identified several observed acids that show a strong association with the antioxidant potential of the plant. Regarding antimicrobial activity, the 80% methanol extract effectively inhibited the growth of Escherichia coli Castellani and Chalmers (ATCC35150). Additionally, the 625-ppm leaf extract completely suppressed the growth of Staphylococcus aureus Rosenbach (ATCC13150), Bacillus cereus (ATCC 14579), and Helicobacter pylori (ATCC43504). The presence of a high level of diverse minerals and phenolic compounds suggests medicinal value for nutraceutical and functional food applications.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a dried fruit known as goji berries, a decoction made by boiling the fruit in water, an extract, a tincture, or a medicinal wine. These dried berries are the most common form used in daily nutrition and traditional cooking. For therapeutic purposes, a decoction is often simmered to release its active compounds into a liquid base. Concentrated extracts and alcohol-based tinctures provide more potent doses of the plant's nutrients. Additionally, the fruit can be infused in alcohol to create a medicinal wine used in various traditional formulas.
