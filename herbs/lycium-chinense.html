What to know about Lycium chinense (goji berries) before using it medicinally

Lycium chinense, commonly known as goji berries, is a herb-rich in antioxidants that have been found to improve cardiovascular health, reduce inflammation, and boost immune function.

As a deciduous shrub, Lycium chinense is relatively low-maintenance and easy to cultivate in warm and dry climates, making it a popular choice for gardeners. Botanically, Lycium chinense is characterized by its small, oval-shaped berries and its ability to thrive in poor soil conditions.

With a history dating back over 2,000 years, Lycium chinense has been used in traditional Chinese medicine and is mentioned in the classic text "Bencao Gangmu" as a treatment for various ailments.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Lycium chinense.

What are the medicinal properties of Lycium chinense?

Lycium chinense helps with reducing oxidative stress, improving eye health, and supporting immune function. Its medicinal uses include treating age-related macular degeneration, cataracts, and other eye problems.

The active constituents of Lycium chinense, including flavonoids, carotenoids, and phenolic acids, give it its medicinal properties. These compounds have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects, contributing to the plant's therapeutic benefits.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are the berries, seeds, and leaves. The berries are rich in antioxidants and carotenoids, while the seeds contain flavonoids and other bioactive compounds. The leaves have been used to treat various health conditions.

Possible side effects of Lycium chinense include allergic reactions, digestive issues, and interactions with medications. Improper use or excessive consumption of the plant can lead to adverse effects, particularly in individuals with pre-existing health conditions.

Precautions when using Lycium chinense medicinally include consulting with a healthcare professional, following recommended dosages, and monitoring for potential interactions with medications. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should use the plant under medical supervision.

What are the horticulural aspects of Lycium chinense?

Lycium chinense grow best in full sun to partial shade and well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. They can tolerate temperatures from -20°C to 40°C (-4°F to 104°F) and require adequate moisture during the growing season.

To plant Lycium chinense, choose a location with good air circulation and space the plants 3-6 feet apart. Plant bare-root or seedlings in early spring or fall, and water thoroughly. Mulch around the base to retain moisture and suppress weeds.

Goji berries are typically ready for harvest 6-9 months after planting. Harvest the fruit when it is fully ripe, usually in late summer or early fall. Remove the entire cluster of berries, as cutting individual stems can damage the plant. Harvest at the right stage to ensure the best flavor and quality.

Common pests of Lycium chinense include the goji berry moth, which can cause damage to the fruit and leaves, and spider mites, which can cause yellowing of the leaves. Fungal diseases such as root rot and leaf spot can also occur if the plants are waterlogged or if the soil is too moist.

What are the botanical characteristics of Lycium chinense?

Lycium chinense, also known as Chinese wolfberry or goji berry, is a deciduous shrub that belongs to the Solanaceae family.

Botanically, it is characterized by its woody stems and branches, which are covered with fine hairs. The leaves of L. chinense are simple, alternate, and ovate-shaped, measuring approximately 2-5 cm in length and 1-3 cm in width, with a pointed tip and serrated margins.

The leaflets are dark green on the upper surface and lighter green on the lower surface, with prominent secondary veins that run parallel to the midrib. The flowers of L. chinense are small, yellowish-white, and bell-shaped, borne in clusters at the ends of branches.

Each flower has five petals and 20-30 stamens, which produce abundant nectar and attract pollinators such as bees and butterflies.

What is the taxonomy of Lycium chinense?

Lycium chinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae, class Magnoliopsida, order Solanales, family Solanaceae, and genus Lycium.

Specifically, it is classified as Lycium chinense Mill., a species of shrub native to China and East Asia.

This classification reflects its botanical relationships with other plants, including tomatoes, peppers, and potatoes, which also belong to the same family (Solanaceae).

What is the geographic distribution of Lycium chinense?

Lycium chinense, also known as Chinese wolfberry or goji berry, is native to northern China and southeastern Siberia.

Its geographic distribution includes the provinces of Gansu, Heilongjiang, Jilin, Liaoning, Nei Mongol, Ningxia, Qinghai, Shaanxi, Shanxi, and Xinjiang in China, as well as Primorsky Krai and Khabarovsk Krai in Russia.

It typically grows in dry mountainous regions with elevations ranging from 800 to 3,000 meters above sea level.
