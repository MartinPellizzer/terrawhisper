Triadica cochinchinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Triadica cochinchinensis?

Triadica cochinchinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malpighiales. The plant is further categorized within the family Euphorbiaceae, specifically placed in the genus Triadica, and is identified by its specific epithet, cochinchinenis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Triadica cochinchinensis has evergreen, woody branches bearing oppositely arranged, elliptical to oblong leaves that are typically coriaceous with entire margins. The foliage exhibits a glossy upper surface and often features a distinctively pale or glaucous underside. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary clusters, which eventually develop into fleshy, globose fruits. These fruits change color as they mature, often transitioning to a bright red or yellowish hue to attract dispersers. The overall architecture of the plant is that of a small to medium-sized tree with a dense, spreading canopy.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

According to a study published by "Foods (Basel, Switzerland)", the medicinal plant Triadica cochinchinensis produces nectar used to collect Triadica cochinchinensis honey. This honey variety is considered the most important in southern China and possesses significant potential medicinal properties. Researchers used liquid chromatography tandem-mass spectrometry to investigate the flavonoid profile of the nectar and the honey. The study identified (-) -Gallocatechin gallate (GCG) as a reliable flavonoid marker that is uniquely shared between the nectar and the honey. This specific marker was found to be absent in 11 other common varieties of Chinese commercial honey.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating alcohol-induced liver disease and alcohol-induced liver injury. Its bioactive compounds help to protect hepatic cells from oxidative stress caused by excessive ethanol consumption. Researchers have observed that extracts from the plant can reduce inflammation and minimize damage to liver tissues. These protective properties assist in maintaining liver function during periods of toxic exposure. Consequently, it serves as a significant subject in studies regarding hepatoprotective natural therapies.

According to a study published by "Frontiers in immunology", Triadica cochinchinenensis honey (TCH) shows potential therapeutic utility against alcohol-induced liver disease. Researchers identified a total of 190 compounds in TCH, with 27 of them being quantified using UPLC-Q/TOF-MS. In a mouse model of alcohol-induced liver injury, TCH polyphenols significantly restored the levels of ALT and AST. After 8 weeks of TCH intervention, a significant decrease was observed in the Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio. Furthermore, TCH intervention helped improve pathological changes in liver tissue and enriched specific KEGG pathways involving ABC transporters, two-component systems, and amino acid biosynthesis.

Some studies suggest that certain alkaloids within the species might interact with metabolic processes, though human clinical data remains limited. Therefore, cautious application and moderation are essential to avoid adverse physiological responses.
