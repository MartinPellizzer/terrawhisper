Podocarpus nagi

What's the taxonomical classification of Podocarpus nagi?

Podocarpus nagi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Pinidae, placing it within the order Pinales. Further taxonomic refinement identifies it as a member of the family Podocarpaceae and the genus Podocarpus, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species nagi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Podocarpus nagi has evergreen, lanceolate leaves that measure approximately 5 to 15 centimeters in length and exhibit a leathery texture with parallel venation. The tree typically grows into a medium-sized woody shrub or small tree characterized by a spreading crown and dark green foliage. Its reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous male cones and fleshy, berry-like female receptacles that turn reddish or purple when mature. The bark is generally smooth or slightly fissured depending on the age of the specimen. These morphological features allow the species to thrive in the shaded understories of subtropical forest environments.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains the medicinal compounds 19-hydroxytotarol, nagilactoside C, nagilactoside D, nagilactoside E, and totaradiol. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their unique biological activities and potential therapeutic applications. Research into these molecules helps scientists understand how the plant interacts with various cellular processes. The presence of these diverse compounds contributes to the overall pharmacological profile of the species. Each substance offers distinct chemical properties that may serve different medicinal functions.

    As discussed in a study, Podocarpus nagi contains diterpenoids including totarol (1), totaradiol (2), 19-hydroxytotarol (3), totaral (4), 4 beta-carboxy-19-nortotarol (5), and sugiol (6). These isolated compounds were evaluated as antioxidants for their ability to inhibit microsomal lipid peroxidation induced by Fe(III)-ADP/NADPH and mitochondrial lipid peroxidation induced by Fe(III)-ADP/ NADH. While these terpenoids inhibited linoleic acid autooxidation, they did not inhibit the generation of superoxide anion. Totarol (1) specifically protected mitochondrial respiratory enzyme activities against NADPH induced oxidative injury. Ultimately, these totarane diterpenes from P. nagi were shown to be effective in protecting biological systems and function against various oxidative stresses.

    What health problems is this plant used for?

    This plant is used for treating various conditions related to oxidative stresses, including cancer, by mitigating cellular damage. Its bioactive compounds work to inhibit linoleic acid autooxidation, which is a critical step in preventing harmful chemical reactions within the body. By suppressing lipid peroxidation, the plant helps protect cell membranes from degradation. These mechanisms effectively reduce oxidative injury caused by reactive oxygen species. Consequently, the plant serves as a potent agent in managing diseases driven by systemic metabolic imbalances.

    As discussed in a study, 3-deoxy-2β,16-dihydroxynagilactone E (B6) is a natural compound from Podocarpus nagi that acts as a potent inhibitor of STAT3 signaling. This compound B6 dose-dependently inhibited IL-6-induced STAT3 signaling with an IC50 of 0.2 μM. It works by interacting with the FERM-SH2 domain of JAK2 rather than the catalytic domain to inhibit the phosphorylation of STAT3. Furthermore, B6 potentily inhibited the growth and induced apoptosis of MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-468 breast cancer cells with overactivated STAT3. This discovery provides a novel mechanism for the regulation of JAK2 and a new therapeutic approach for treating cancers.
