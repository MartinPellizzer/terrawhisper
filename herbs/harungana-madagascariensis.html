Harungana madagascariensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Harungana madagascariensis?

Harungana madagascariensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malpighiales. This plant is further categorized into the family Hypericaceae, within which it is placed in the genus Harungana, ultimately identifying it as the specific species madagascariensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Harungana madagascariensis has a medium-sized tree habit characterized by an erect trunk, spreading branches, and glossy, elliptic leaves that are typically arranged in an alternate pattern. The foliage features thick, leathery textures with entire margins and prominent midribs. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the terminal or axillary clusters, often appearing in dense cymes. These blossoms are followed by the development of fleshy, globose fruits that contain several seeds. The bark is generally smooth or slightly fissured, providing a protective layer for the woody structure.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds betulinic acid, harunganin, harunganols C-F, kenganthranol A, and madagascin. These specific chemical constituents are primarily studied for their diverse pharmacological properties in various biological systems. Betulinic acid and harunganin are often noted for their potential anti-inflammatory and antitumor activities. The harunganols C-F group and kenganthranol A contribute to the complex chemical profile of the plant species. Additionally, madagascin serves as another significant bioactive molecule within its organic makeup.

As discussed in a study, Harungana madagascariensis contains medicinal compounds such as betulinic acid (1), madagascin (2), ferruginin A (3), and Kaempferol-3-O-β-d-glucopyranoside (4). The anthranol 3 is the main antibacterial constituent of the bark of the plant. When testing against a panel of 28 Gram-negative bacteria, MIC values below or equal to 1024µg/mL were recorded for HMl and HMBa 4 at 92.9%, for HMBb at 82.1%, for HMBa2 at 78.6%, for HMBa5 at 50.0%, and for HMBc at 42.9%. For crude material, the lowest MIC value below 8µg/mL was obtained with HMB against Escherichia coli ATC10536 and W3110 strains, and with sub-fraction HMBa3 against Klebsiella pneumoniae K2 strains. Furthermore, MIC values below 10µg/mL were recorded with compound 3 against E. coli ATC10536, Enterobacter aerogenes ATC13048 and EA294, Pseudomonas aeruginosa PA01, K. pneumoniae K2 and Kp55, and Enterobacter cloacae BM67.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating amoebiasis, malaria, gastrointestinal disorders, and various forms of antibacterial infection. Its medicinal properties are frequently employed to combat parasitic infections within the digestive tract. Practitioners also utilize the plant to address systemic issues caused by an antibacterial infection. The botanical extracts serve as a traditional remedy for managing malaria symptoms in local populations. Furthermore, its efficacy in soothing gastrointestinal disorders makes it a vital component of regional healthcare.

As discussed in a study, Harungana madagascariensis was among 45 plant extracts tested for in vitro antiamoebic activity. Out of these 45 extracts, 35 (77.78%) exhibited antiamoebiic activity while 10 (22.22%) were inactive. Harungana madagascariensis was identified as having the highest activity when using the stem bark. This level of activity was recorded as an MIC < 100 microg/ml. Metronidazole was used as a reference product and showed a more pronounced activity than all the plant extracts tested.
