Spermacoce hispida

What's the taxonomical classification of Spermacoce hispida?

Spermacoce hispida belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of its lineage, the plant is ordered within the Gentianaales and falls under the family Rubiaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement in the genus Spermacoce, with its unique designation being the species hispida.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Spermacoce hispida has a decumbent to ascending herbaceous habit characterized by stems and leaves covered in stiff, spreading, multicellular trichomes. These bristly hairs give the entire plant a distinctly hispid texture that serves as a primary diagnostic feature. The leaves are typically arranged in an opposite pattern and possess an ovate to lanceolate shape with entire or slightly serrated margins. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils, often presenting in compact clusters. The reproductive structures are subtle, integrated closely with the hairy foliage of the plant.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

These phytochemicals contribute to the diverse therapeutic profile found within the species. The presence of specific flavonoids like rutin and dalspinin derivatives suggests significant antioxidant potential. Additionally, the triterpenoids ursolic acid and β-sitosterol are known for their various biological activities in medicinal applications. Together, these substances form the complex chemical foundation of the plant's pharmacological properties.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Spepermacoce hispida is a potential medicinal plant traditionally used as an antibacterial, antieczemic, antihypertensive, antidiabetic and antihyperlipidemic agent. The methanolic extract of the plant was fractionated to yield 5 compounds through 18 fractions eluted from a silica gel column. These isolated compounds include the known phytochemicals β-sitosterol, ursolic acid, quercetin and rutin, alongside a new isoflavone identified as dalspinin-7-0-β-D-galactopyranoside (DBG). Among these, DBG demonstrated superior antioxidant potential in DPPH free radical scavenging and ORAC assays. DBG protects H9c2 cells from CoCl2-induced hypoxic damage by mitigating oxidative stress and preserving cell viability.
