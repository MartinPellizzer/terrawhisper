Scientific and Botanical Profile

Morphologically, Lycopodium clavatum features a compact, low-growing structure with a central stem that produces whorls of scale-like leaves, and its spore cones are typically clustered at the tips of the branches.

This versatile species is not only of botanical interest but also has traditional uses in medicine and horticulture.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Lycopodium clavatum grows in well-drained, loamy soil that is slightly acidic to neutral, with a pH range of 6.0 to 7.0, and thrives in full sun to partial shade.

It requires consistent moisture, especially during the growing season, but should not be overwatered to prevent root rot. Regular weeding and mulching help maintain soil moisture and reduce competition from weeds. The plant is typically harvested in the fall when the spores are mature and the fronds are fully developed, using a clean knife or shears to cut the stems just above the base.

For storage, the harvested plant material should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area to preserve its potency, then stored in airtight glass containers in a cool, dark place to maintain its medicinal properties.
