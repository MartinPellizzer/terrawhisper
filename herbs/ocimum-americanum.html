Ocimum americanum

What's the taxonomical classification of Ocimum americanum?

Ocimum americanum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Lamiales, it is placed in the family Lamiaceae, which is a group well-known for its aromatic properties. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Ocimum, and its specific species epithet, americanum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ocimum americanum has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically grows between 30 and 100 centimeters tall, characterized by a square cross-section and frequent branching. The leaves are opposite, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and possess serrated margins with a slightly pubescent texture. Small, tubular flowers emerge from verticillasters arranged in terminal racemes, displaying a color palette that ranges from white to pale lavender. The calyx is bilabiate and often features distinct longitudinal ribs. Finally, the plant produces small, dark nutlets that serve as its primary method of seed dispersal.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas, ranging from the southern United States through Central America and into South America. It is commonly found in various habitats including disturbed soils, roadsides, and forest edges throughout the Caribbean islands. Its distribution is heavily influenced by warm, moist climates that support its rapid growth. While primarily concentrated in the Western Hemisphere, some studies suggest its presence in other tropical zones due to human movement. This wide range allows it to thrive in diverse ecological niches across many different latitudes.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ocimum americanum?

Ocimum americanum has antimicrobial, antispasmodic, carminative, expectorant, diapheretic, and digestive stimulant therapeutic actions used to treat various ailments. Additionally, its diaphoretic action promotes sweating to assist in regulating body temperature during fevers.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse range of bioactive medicinal compounds including alkaloids, flavonoids, glycosides, saponosides, and tannins. These alkaloids often provide potent pharmacological effects by interacting with the central nervous system. The presence of flavonoids contributes significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the plant's profile. Additionally, glycosides and saponosides play critical roles in various metabolic and cellular signaling processes. Finally, the tannins act as astringents that can help protect tissues and reduce inflammation.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical analysis of Ocimum americanum revealed the presence of alkaloids, flavonoids, glycosides, saponosides, tannins, reducing compounds, and mucilage. The hydroethanolic extracts showed antibacterial activity against Staphylococci and uropathogenic Enterobacteria with inhibition zones ranging from 13.8-21.2 mm. These extracts also demonstrated minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) of 3.5-9 mg/mL. When administered at 200 mg/kg, the hydroethanolic extracts significantly reduced acetic acid-induced pain. Furthermore, no toxic effects were observed in the study even at 2000 mg/kg.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, dizziness, gastrointestinal upset, and allergic reactions in individuals who consume or interact with it. Some people may experience significant digestive distress or an upset stomach following exposure. Neurological symptoms like lightheadedness or dizziness might also occur during use. Furthermore, certain individuals may develop a localized or systemic allergic reaction to its components. Monitoring these specific physical responses is essential for anyone handling the species.
