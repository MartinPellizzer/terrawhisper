Daphne retusa

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Daphne retusa has an evergreen, shrubby growth habit characterized by woody stems and small, leathery, elliptical leaves that are arranged oppositely along the branches. The foliage typically exhibits a dark green color on the upper surface and can sometimes show a slightly paler underside. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters or singly during the blooming season. These blossoms are typically pink or reddish in hue and possess a fragrant quality that attracts pollinators. The plant's overall structure remains compact, making it well-suited for various garden environments.

It typically thrives in subalpine and alpine zones where rocky terrain and moist soils are prevalent. These botanical specimens are most frequently located at elevations ranging from 3,000 to 4,500 meters above sea level. The distribution is often limited to specific microclimates found along mountain slopes and forest edges. Its presence is closely tied to the cool, temperate conditions characteristic of these high-elevation ecosystems.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Daphne retusa can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and chemical burns upon direct contact with its tissues. The plant contains toxic compounds that may lead to intense redness and swelling if touched. If the sap or plant parts are accidentally ingested, it can result in serious gastrointestinal distress and systemic poisoning. Contact with the eyes can cause significant inflammation and potential vision damage. Because of these potent irritants, extreme caution is necessary when handling any part of the shrub.
