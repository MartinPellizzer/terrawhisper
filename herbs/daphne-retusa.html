Daphne retusa

What's the taxonomical classification of Daphne retusa?

Daphne retusa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Malvales and the family Thymelaeaceae. Ultimately, this plant is identified by its genus, Daphne, and its specific species designation, retusa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Daphne retusa has an evergreen, shrubby growth habit characterized by woody stems and small, leathery, elliptical leaves that are arranged oppositely along the branches. The foliage typically exhibits a dark green color on the upper surface and can sometimes show a slightly paler underside. Small, tubular flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters or singly during the blooming season. These blossoms are typically pink or reddish in hue and possess a fragrant quality that attracts pollinators. The plant's overall structure remains compact, making it well-suited for various garden environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Daphne retusa?

This plant has significant antibacterial, antifungal, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities that make it a valuable subject for medicinal research. The bioactive compounds found within its tissues demonstrate a strong ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria. Additionally, its chemical constituents exhibit potent antifungal properties capable of disrupting the cellular structures of specific fungi. These broad antimicrobial effects suggest that the plant can combat a wide range of microorganisms. Such diverse biological actions highlight its potential for developing new therapeutic agents against infectious diseases.

According to a study published by "Current topics in medicinal chemistry", Daphne retusa Hemsl. possesses medicinal values used in curing various human ailments. The study assessed the in-vitro antimicrobial activity of the whole plant crude extract (E1) and its derived fractions, including the chloroform fraction (E2), butanol fraction (E3), and ethyl acetate fraction (E4). Results showed that E2 achieved the maximum percent of inhibition against bacterial growth, while E1 showed the maximum percent of inhibition against fungal growth. Streptococcus pneumoniae was identified as the most susceptible bacteria, and among fungi, Gongronella butleri showed the highest susceptibility. These findings justify the use of D. retusa in the treatment of microbial infections.
