Cinchona pubescens

What's the taxonomical classification of Cinchona pubescens?

Cinchona pubeсens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its hierarchical classification, it falls under the order Gentianales and is a member of the family Rubiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Cinchona and is specifically designated as the species pubescens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cincona pubescens has an erect, woody stem that can reach heights of up to 10 meters and features leaves that are oppositely arranged, elliptical to oblong, and covered in fine, velvety hairs on both surfaces. The foliage is characterized by a dark green color and prominent venation that extends toward the margins. Its inflorescences consist of small, fragrant, white flowers that emerge in terminal clusters. These blossoms eventually produce small, fleshy berries that typically contain several dark seeds. The overall structure of the plant reflects its adaptation to the shaded understory of tropical rainforest environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the humid tropical montane forests located throughout the Amazon Basin in South America. Its range extends across several countries including Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Ecuador, and Bolivia. It typically thrives in high-altitude cloud forests where moisture levels remain consistently high. The species prefers shaded environments within the dense understory of the rainforest canopy. This specific distribution pattern is dictated by the need for consistent rainfall and stable, warm temperatures.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cinchona pubescens contains medicinal alkaloids primarily concentrated in the dried bark of its stem and roots. These alkaloids, most notably quinine, are extracted from the bark to treat malaria and other febrile illnesses. The bark is often harvested through controlled stripping to ensure the plant remains viable for future use. While the leaves and stems contain trace amounts of these compounds, the bark remains the most potent source for pharmaceutical applications. This plant has historically served as a critical foundation for the development of antimalarial medicine.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cinchona pubescens?

This plant has significant anti-malarial and cytotoxic activities derived primarily from its rich alkaloid content. The presence of quinine allows it to effectively inhibit the growth of Plasmodium parasites responsible for malaria. Beyond its traditional use against malaria, recent studies have identified its potential for cytotoxic activity against various cancer cell lines. These pharmacological properties make the species a vital source for developing new therapeutic agents. Researchers continue to investigate these bioactive compounds to improve drug efficacy and minimize side effects.

As discussed in a study, cinchonine is obtained in much lower quantities from the species Cinchona pube scens. This alkaloid has been used historically as an anti-malarial agent. It possesses a wide range of biological properties including anti-cancer, anti-obesity, anti-inflammatory, anti-parasitic, antimicrobial, anti-platelet aggregation, and anti-osteoclast differentiation. The anti-cancer activity is induced by activating caspase-3 and PARP-1 and triggering the endoplasmic reticulum stress response. It also up-regulates GRP78 and promotes the phosphorylation of PERK and ETIF-2α.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating malaria by providing the source for quinine, an alkaloid that effectively combats the Plasmodium parasite. Historically, the bark of this species served as the primary medicinal ingredient for managing periodic fevers. It also plays a role in addressing various febrile illnesses and certain types of heart rhythm disturbances. Beyond its antimalarial properties, the compounds derived from the plant have been studied for their ability to alleviate muscle cramps. This botanical source remains a cornerstone in the history of pharmaceutical development for tropical diseases.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause a wide range of serious side effects including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, dizziness, headache, visual disturbances, hypoglycemia, convulsions, cardiac arrhythmias, tinnitus, and hearing loss. The alkaloids found within the bark can disrupt various physiological systems, leading to significant gastrointestinal distress and neurological issues. Users may experience dangerous drops in blood sugar levels or irregular heart rhythms that require immediate medical attention. Auditory complications such as ringing in the ears or permanent damage to hearing can also occur. These multifaceted reactions demonstrate the high toxicity potential associated with the consumption of this species.
