What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cincona pubescens has an erect, woody stem that can reach heights of up to 10 meters and features leaves that are oppositely arranged, elliptical to oblong, and covered in fine, velvety hairs on both surfaces. The foliage is characterized by a dark green color and prominent venation that extends toward the margins. Its inflorescences consist of small, fragrant, white flowers that emerge in terminal clusters. These blossoms eventually produce small, fleshy berries that typically contain several dark seeds. The overall structure of the plant reflects its adaptation to the shaded understory of tropical rainforest environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cinchona pubescens contains medicinal alkaloids primarily concentrated in the dried bark of its stem and roots. These alkaloids, most notably quinine, are extracted from the bark to treat malaria and other febrile illnesses. The bark is often harvested through controlled stripping to ensure the plant remains viable for future use. While the leaves and stems contain trace amounts of these compounds, the bark remains the most potent source for pharmaceutical applications. This plant has historically served as a critical foundation for the development of antimalarial medicine.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cinchona pubescens?

This plant has potent anti-malarial, anti-inflammatory, and cytotoxic activities derived from its rich concentration of quinoline alkaloids. The presence of quinine within its bark makes it a foundational source for treating malaria by inhibiting the growth of Plasmodium parasites. Beyond its traditional uses, recent studies have highlighted its significant cytotoxic activity, which shows promise in evaluating its potential against various cancer cell lines. These pharmacological properties are primarily attributed to the complex chemical structure of its alkaloids. Consequently, Cinchona pubescens remains a vital subject in both ethnobotany and modern drug development.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment of malaria because its bark contains the potent alkaloid quinine. This specific compound works by interfering with the ability of the malaria parasite to digest hemoglobin within the human bloodstream. Historically, extracts from the bark provided one of the first effective defenses against the deadly fever caused by Plasmodium parasites. Beyond its primary role in fighting malaria, researchers have also studied its chemical properties for potential applications in managing various cardiac arrhythmias. The medicinal use of this species remains a fundamental cornerstone in the history of tropical medicine.

Biological assays were performed through antimalarial tests in vitro to evaluate the materials.
