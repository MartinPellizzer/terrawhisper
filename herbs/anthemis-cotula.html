Cotula (Anthemis cotula): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Cotula, also known as Anthemis cotula, is a small, flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported digestive and anti-inflammatory properties.

What is Cotula (Anthemis Cotula)?

Cotula, or Anthemis cotula, is a small flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its mild sedative and digestive properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cotula is Anthemis cotula. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division unknown, class unknown, order unknown, family Compositae, genus Anthemis, and species Anthemis cotula. It is commonly known as "mouse-ear" in Europe and "cotula" in parts of South America. The plant is also referred to by synonyms such as Anthemis cotula L. and Cotula tinctoria.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cotula is a small annual herb that typically grows to a height of 10-30 centimeters, with a branching stem that is hairy and slender.

Its leaves are alternate, linear to lanceolate in shape, with a serrated margin and a slightly hairy surface. The flowers are small, yellow, and daisy-like, arranged in clusters at the ends of the stems, with a central disc surrounded by ray florets. The root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing the plant to spread rapidly in disturbed soils.

The plant has a mild, slightly bitter scent, with a rough, hairy texture on the stems and leaves.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cotula is native to southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of the Middle East, with a historical presence in regions such as the Mediterranean Basin.

Its geographic distribution extends into temperate and subtropical areas, where it thrives in open fields, along roadsides, and in disturbed soils. The plant prefers mild to warm climates with moderate rainfall and well-drained soil, making it common in regions with Mediterranean or continental weather patterns. It grows best in full sun to partial shade, often found in areas with low to moderate humidity.

Ecological conditions favoring Cotula include nutrient-poor soils and environments with minimal competition from other plants.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The plant parts used medicinally in Cotula (Anthemis cotula) include the entire plant, with a particular focus on the leaves and flowers.

The leaves are commonly harvested for their medicinal properties, while the flowers are also utilized, often dried and used in formulations. Both parts are typically collected during the flowering season to maximize their active compounds. The roots may be used in some traditional preparations, though less frequently than the aerial parts.

The plant is often prepared as a decoction, infusion, or tincture for therapeutic use.

Secondary metabolites such as terpenoids and sesquiterpenes are also present and play a role in the plant's pharmacological activities. Pharmacological Properties

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) exhibits pharmacological properties that make it relevant in herbal medicine, primarily due to its therapeutic actions such as anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and sedative effects.

Its biological activity is attributed to the presence of compounds like flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and essential oils. Pharmacodynamically, Cotula interacts with the central nervous system and smooth muscles, contributing to its calming and muscle-relaxing effects. At the system level, it may influence the gastrointestinal tract by reducing spasms and promoting digestive comfort.

These properties collectively support its use in traditional remedies for stress, anxiety, and digestive disorders.

The mechanisms of action of Cotula (Anthemis cotula) involve interactions at the cellular, biochemical, and systemic levels.

At the cellular level, Cotula exhibits antioxidant properties by scavenging free radicals and modulating oxidative stress. Biochemically, it influences pathways related to inflammation and immune response through the modulation of cytokine production and enzyme activity. These effects are mediated by compounds such as sesquiterpene lactones and flavonoids.

The target systems include the nervous, respiratory, and immune systems, where Cotula exerts its therapeutic effects.

Therapeutic Uses

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is traditionally used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties, particularly in treating mild digestive issues and promoting relaxation.

It is commonly employed to alleviate symptoms of indigestion, bloating, and gastrointestinal discomfort. The plant is also used to support respiratory health, helping to ease conditions such as bronchitis and mild coughing. Cotula has been historically applied to reduce inflammation and soothe skin irritations.

Its calming effects make it a useful herb for managing stress and mild anxiety.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is typically prepared as a herbal tea by steeping dried leaves and flowers in hot water, or it may be used in tinctures, capsules, or topical applications depending on the intended use.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Cotula (Anthemis cotula) for herbal use is 2-4 grams of dried herb per day, usually prepared as a tea by steeping in hot water, though specific dosing may vary based on individual health conditions and guidance from a qualified herbalist or healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications, and it should be avoided during pregnancy and lactation.

Drug Interactions

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) may interact with medications that affect the central nervous system, such as sedatives, antidepressants, or anticoagulants, potentially increasing drowsiness, dizziness, or bleeding risk.

Toxicity and Precautions

While Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and there is limited research on its long-term safety, so it is advisable to consult a healthcare professional before use, especially during pregnancy or breastfeeding.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and full sun exposure, with harvesting occurring during the early flowering stage to ensure optimal essential oil content and medicinal potency.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Cotula (Anthemis cotula) typically involve harvesting the aerial parts of the plant, usually during its flowering stage, followed by drying to reduce moisture content and then employing methods such as maceration, distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain its essential oils and active compounds.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Cotula (Anthemis cotula) has primarily focused on its potential antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties, with limited studies exploring its efficacy in traditional herbal medicine applications.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to promote digestion, reduce inflammation, and support respiratory health, with historical references dating back to ancient Greek and Roman herbal practices.
