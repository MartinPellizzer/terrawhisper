Cotula (Anthemis cotula): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Cotula, also known as Anthemis cotula, is a small, flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported digestive and anti-inflammatory properties.

What is Cotula (Anthemis Cotula)?

Cotula, or Anthemis cotula, is a small flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its mild sedative and digestive properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cotula is Anthemis cotula. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division unknown, class unknown, order unknown, family Compositae, genus Anthemis, and species Anthemis cotula. It is commonly known as "mouse-ear" in Europe and "cotula" in parts of South America. The plant is also referred to by synonyms such as Anthemis cotula L. and Cotula tinctoria.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cotula is a small annual herb that typically grows to a height of 10-30 centimeters, with a branching stem that is hairy and slender.

Its leaves are alternate, linear to lanceolate in shape, with a serrated margin and a slightly hairy surface. The flowers are small, yellow, and daisy-like, arranged in clusters at the ends of the stems, with a central disc surrounded by ray florets. The root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing the plant to spread rapidly in disturbed soils.

The plant has a mild, slightly bitter scent, with a rough, hairy texture on the stems and leaves.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cotula is native to southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of the Middle East, with a historical presence in regions such as the Mediterranean Basin.

Its geographic distribution extends into temperate and subtropical areas, where it thrives in open fields, along roadsides, and in disturbed soils. The plant prefers mild to warm climates with moderate rainfall and well-drained soil, making it common in regions with Mediterranean or continental weather patterns. It grows best in full sun to partial shade, often found in areas with low to moderate humidity.

Ecological conditions favoring Cotula include nutrient-poor soils and environments with minimal competition from other plants.

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is traditionally used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties, particularly in treating mild digestive issues and promoting relaxation.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is generally considered safe for most adults, but it may cause mild side effects such as stomach upset, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Side effects are typically rare but can include skin irritation or respiratory symptoms if the plant is handled improperly or ingested in large amounts. Contraindications include individuals with known sensitivities to plants in the Asteraceae family, as Cotula may trigger similar allergic responses. Allergic reactions, though uncommon, can range from mild itching to more severe symptoms like swelling or difficulty breathing, and should be taken seriously.

Safety during pregnancy has not been well-established, and it is recommended to avoid use during pregnancy and breastfeeding unless advised by a healthcare professional.

Drug Interactions

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) may interact with certain pharmaceuticals due to its potential effects on the central nervous system and its anti-inflammatory properties.

It may enhance the sedative effects of central nervous system depressants such as benzodiazepines or opioids, increasing the risk of drowsiness and respiratory depression. Cotula may also interfere with the metabolism of some medications, particularly those processed by the liver's cytochrome P450 enzyme system. Interactions with supplements are less well-documented, but it is advisable to avoid combining Cotula with other herbs that have sedative or anticoagulant properties, as this could lead to additive effects.

Always consult a healthcare provider before using Cotula with any pharmaceuticals or supplements.

Toxicity and precautions are essential considerations when using Cotula (Anthemis cotula) in herbal medicine.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cotula (Anthemis cotula) is typically grown in well-drained, sandy or loamy soils with a slightly acidic to neutral pH.

It thrives in full sun to partial shade, though it performs best with at least 6 hours of direct sunlight daily. Regular watering is necessary during the growing season, but the soil should not remain waterlogged to prevent root rot. Propagation is usually done through seeds sown directly in the garden in early spring or by transplanting young seedlings. The plant reaches maturity in about 60 to 90 days, depending on climate and growing conditions.

Cotula is best harvested when the flowers are fully open, typically in late summer to early autumn, by cutting the stems just above the base to encourage regrowth.
