What to know about Anthemis cotula (mayweed) before using it medicinally

Anthemis cotula, commonly known as mayweed, is a herb renowned for its numerous health benefits, including its ability to soothe skin irritations, reduce inflammation, and alleviate stress.

As a hardy annual, it is easy to grow and can thrive in a variety of environments, making it a popular choice for gardeners. The botanical properties of Anthemis cotula are characterized by its small, white or yellow flowers and its pungent, bitter taste, which is due to the presence of sesquiterpene lactones.

With its long history of use dating back to ancient Greece, where it was employed for its medicinal and culinary properties, mayweed has been a staple in traditional herbalism for centuries.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Anthemis cotula.

What are the medicinal properties of Anthemis cotula?

Anthemis cotula helps with reducing inflammation, relieving headaches, and calming digestive issues. Its antispasmodic and analgesic properties make it useful for treating menstrual cramps, muscle spasms, and joint pain.

The medicinal properties of Anthemis cotula are attributed to its active constituents, including volatile oils, sesquiterpene lactones, and flavonoids, which have been shown to exhibit anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antispasmodic activities.

The leaves, flowers, and stems of the mayweed plant are used for medicinal purposes, with the leaves being the most commonly used for infusions, teas, and topical applications.

When used improperly, mayweed can cause gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and interactions with certain medications, such as antacids and diuretics. Its volatile oils may also irritate skin and mucous membranes.

Precautions when using mayweed medicinally include pregnant or breastfeeding women avoiding its use, as well as individuals with allergies or sensitivities to plants in the Asteraceae family. Additionally, its use should be discontinued before surgery or with certain medications.

What are the horticulural aspects of Anthemis cotula?

Anthemis cotula grow best in well-drained soil and full sun, tolerating a wide range of pH levels from slightly acidic to alkaline. It thrives in areas with moderate temperatures between 15°C to 25°C, and average annual rainfall of 600-800 mm.

Planting tips for Anthemis cotula include direct sowing in late spring or early summer, when the soil has warmed up, and spacing seeds 15-20 cm apart. Sowing too close can lead to overcrowding and reduced flowering. Seedlings can also be started indoors 4-6 weeks prior to the last frost.

Harvesting mayweed flowers for medicinal or decorative purposes involves cutting the stems when the buds are still tight, to encourage regrowth and prevent seed production. Flowers can be picked in the morning, when they are at their peak and have the best color and fragrance. Regular harvesting will promote a longer blooming period.

Common pests affecting mayweed include aphids, caterpillars, and thrips, while diseases such as powdery mildew, leaf spot, and root rot can occur. To minimize the risk of infestation or infection, growers can adopt integrated pest management techniques, using physical barriers, biological controls, and cultural practices to maintain a balanced and healthy ecosystem.
