Entada phaseoloides

What's the taxonomical classification of Entada phaseoloides?

Entada phaseoloides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic progression continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Fabales. As a member of the family Fabaceae, this plant is specifically categorized under the genus Entada, with its unique biological identity defined by its species name, phaseoloides.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Entada phaseoloides has woody climbing vines that develop large, pinnately compound leaves and thick, woody pods containing massive seeds.

The stems are robust lianas that can reach significant lengths as they scramble through forest canopies. Its leaves are composed of several pairs of leaflets that are typically elliptical or oblong in shape. The flowers are generally small and arranged in clusters, often appearing in shades of white or pale yellow. Most notably, the plant produces elongated, woody legume pods that can grow to impressive sizes.

These pods eventually split to reveal large, dark seeds that are characteristic of the genus.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Entada phaseoloides targets specific enzymes and signaling proteins including cyclooxygenase, lipoxygenase, and various inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. The plant contains bioactive compounds like alkaloids and saponins that interfere with the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. By inhibiting cyclooxygenase enzymes, these molecules effectively reduce the production of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins. Furthermore, the phytochemicals interact with cytokine-regulating proteins to dampen systemic inflammatory responses. These specific protein interactions underpin the plant's traditional use in treating swelling and pain.

What are the herbal preparations of Entada phaseoloides?

Entada phaseoloides is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a crushed paste from the roots, or a liquid infusion made from the leaves. These various preparations are used in traditional medicine to treat diverse ailments such as skin infections and digestive issues. What side-effects this plant can have?

Entada phaseoloides can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, including abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, if any part of the plant is ingested improperly. The presence of certain alkaloids and glycosides within the seeds and pods may lead to toxic reactions in sensitive individuals. While some traditional practices use parts of the plant medicinally, incorrect dosage or preparation can result in systemic toxicity. There is also limited documentation regarding potential skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis from handling the plant material. Caution is advised because the chemical composition varies significantly between different stages of plant maturity.
