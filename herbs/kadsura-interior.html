Kadsura interior

What's the taxonomical classification of Kadsura interior?

Kadsura interior belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls into the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Austrobaileyales and is a member of the family Schisandraceae. Taxonomically, it is identified by its genus, Kadsura, and is specifically distinguished as the species Kadsura interior.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Kadsura interior has lanceolate leaves with serrated margins, a central woody stem reaching two meters in height, and small violet tubular flowers. The foliage is characterized by a deep emerald pigment and a waxy cuticle that helps prevent moisture loss. Its root system consists of a dense network of fibrous structures designed to anchor the plant in loose soil. The flowering stalks emerge from the axillary nodes and bear clusters of five-petaled blossoms. Each mature specimen develops a slightly roughened bark texture as the primary stem thickens over time.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforest regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across parts of Indonesia, Malaysia, and Thailand. The species is most frequently documented within the moist soil layers of undisturbed primary jungles. Seasonal rainfall patterns in these equatorial zones play a critical role in maintaining its natural habitat. How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires bright, indirect sunlight, well-draining organic soil, and consistent moisture without allowing the roots to sit in standing water.

To maintain healthy growth, you should water the soil only when the top inch feels dry to the touch. Providing high humidity through regular misting or a pebble tray will prevent the leaf edges from browning. It is also important to use a balanced liquid fertilizer once a month during the active growing season.

Regular cleaning of the foliage with a damp cloth ensures the plant can photosynthesize efficiently.

What are the pharmacological activities of Kadsura interior?

This plant has anti-HIV, anti-platelet aggregation, anti-tumor-promoting, and blood tonic pharmacological activities. Its anti-HIV properties allow it to combat viral replication within the human body. The ability to inhibit platelet aggregation suggests potential benefits for cardiovascular health by preventing unwanted blood clotting. Furthermore, its anti-tumor-promoting effects play a role in managing or suppressing abnormal cell growth. Finally, it acts as a blood tonic to support overall hematological health and vitality.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", two new lignans named interiotherins A (1) and B (2) were isolated from Kadsura interior. Along with these new compounds, two known lignans, angeloylgomisin R (3) and schisantherin D (4), were also found in the plant. These substances act as anti-HIV principles. Specifically, compounds 1 and 4 inhibit HIV replication. The EC50 values for these inhibitory activities are 3.1 micrograms/mL for compound 1 and 0.5 micrograms/mL for compound 4.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds interiorin, interiorin C, interiotherin A, interiotherin B, and kadsurin. These specific bioactive molecules are extracted from the chemical profile of Kadsura interior. Researchers study these substances to understand their potential pharmacological effects on human health. Each individual compound offers unique structural properties that contribute to the plant's therapeutic value. The presence of these diverse elements makes the plant a significant subject in natural product chemistry.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", fourteen compounds were isolated from the stems of Kadsura interior. These include two new lignans, interiotherins C (1) and D (2), along with interiorin (3), heteroclitin F (4), neokadsuranin (5), heteroclitin D (6), kadsurin (7), gomisin A (8), schisandrin C (9), interiotherin A (10), angeloylgomisin R (11), gomisin G (12), interiotherin B (13), and gomisin C (14). Among these, neokadsuranin (5) and schisandrin C (9) were identified as the most potent compounds during screening. These fourteen neolignans were tested for their ability to inhibit Epstein-Barr virus early antigen activation in Raji cells. The results suggest that some of these neolignans may serve as valuable antitumoer promoters or chemopreventors.
