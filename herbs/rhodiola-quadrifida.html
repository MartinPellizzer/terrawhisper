Rhodiola quadrifida

What's the taxonomical classification of Rhodiola quadrifida?

Rhodiola quadrifidida belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is positioned in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Saxifrages (Saxifragales). This plant is a member of the family Crassulaceae and is further categorized into the genus Rhodiola, ultimately reaching its specific classification as the species quadrifida.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rhodiola quadrifida has a perennial, rhizomatous growth habit characterized by fleshy, creeping roots and small, ovate leaves that grow in dense, basal clusters. The leaves are typically succulent, displaying a smooth texture and a slightly fleshy consistency to aid in water storage. Flowering stems emerge from the base, bearing small, inconspicuous blossoms that are often arranged in compact cymes. The plant's reproductive structures include specialized carpels that can split into multiple segments, a feature reflected in its specific epithet. This morphology allows the species to thrive in the harsh, rocky environments of high-altitude alpine regions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Rhodiola quadrifida?

This plant has adaptogen, anti-inflammatory, antiallergic, antidepressant, and immunotropic pharmacological activities. Its classification as an adaptogen allows it to help the human body resist various physical and environmental stressors. The anti-inflammatory and antiallergic properties assist in reducing swelling and managing allergic responses within the system. Furthermore, its antidepressant effects contribute to the regulation of mood and mental well-being. Finally, its immunotropic nature helps modulate and strengthen the body's natural immune functions.

According to a study published by "Polish journal of veterinary sciences", Rhodiola quadrifida is a medicinal plant originating from Asia that has been used traditionally as adaptogens, antidepressants, and anti-inflammatory remedies. Extracts from the underground parts of Rhodiola quadrifida exhibit immuno-tropic activity. In studies involving pigs, in vitro supplementation of blood lymphocyte cultures with Rhodiola quadrifida stimulated T cell proliferative response to Con A in lower concentrations but inhibited it in higher concentrations. In mice, the in vivo effect of Rhodiola quadrifida on the in vitro proliferative response of splenic lymphocytes to Phaseolus vulgaris haemagglutinin showed significant stimulation in mice fed lower doses compared to controls. However, mice fed higher doses of the drug showed inhibition, no effect, or lower stimulation of the response.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds rhodiocyanoside A, rhodiocyanoside B, rhodioflavonoside [gossypetin 7-O-beta-D-glucopyranosyl(1-->3)-alpha-L-rhamnopyranoside], rhodioloside, and salidroside. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the unique bioactive profile of Rhodiola quadrifidida. The presence of these glycosides and flavonoids is often studied for their potential therapeutic properties. Researchers focus on these molecules to understand the plant's chemical complexity and pharmacological value. Identifying such diverse compounds helps categorize the species within its medicinal genus.

According to a study published by "Chemical & pharmaceucticat bulletin", the underground part of Rhodiola quadrifida contains two bioactive cyanoglycosides known as rhodiocyanoside A and rhodiocyanoside B. The plant also contains two oligoglycosides, specifically rhodioflavonoside [gossypetin 7-O-beta-D-glucopyranosyl(1-->3)-alpha-L-rhamnopyranoside] and rhodiooctanoside [octyl alpha-L-arabinopyranosyl(1-->6) beta-D-glucopyranoside]. Additionally, four known compounds were isolated from the plant, which are rhodioloside, n-hexyl beta-D-glucopyranoside, gossypetin 7-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranoside, and tricetin. Rhodiocyanoside A serves as the major constituent of this natural medicine. Both rhodiocyanoside A and B exhibited inhibitory activity on histamine release from rat peritoneal exudate cells sensitized with anti-2,4-dinitrophenyl IgE. Furthermore, rhodiocyanoside A showed antiallergic activity in a passive cutaneous anaphylaxis test in rat.
