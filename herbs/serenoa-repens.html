Scientific and Botanical Profile

Saw Palmetto, with botanical name Serenoa repens, is a flowering plant native to the United States, Mexico, Central America, the Caribbean, and parts of Tropical America, including Florida, the Gulf Coast, and the Southern United States.

It belongs to the family Arecaceae, commonly known as the palm family, and is also referred to by various common names such as Cabbage Palm, American Dwarf Palm, Serenoa, American Palmetto, Cabbage Palmetto, Small-Leaf Palmetto, Cabbage Tree, Smooth Palmetto, Bunchberry, Red-Barked Palm, Dwarf Palmetto, and Red Palmetto. Morphologically, it is a low-growing shrub with a rosette of stiff, needle-like leaves that are typically 30-60 cm long, and it produces small, yellowish flowers followed by red, fleshy berries that contain a single seed.

It thrives in sandy soils and is often found in coastal dunes and dry, open habitats across its native range.

History and Cultural Relevance

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Serenoa repens grows as a low-growing shrub in tropical and subtropical climates, thriving in well-drained, sandy or loamy soils with a slightly acidic to neutral pH.

It prefers full sun to partial shade and requires consistent moisture, though it is relatively drought-tolerant once established. Regular watering during the growing season and mulching to retain soil moisture are essential for healthy growth. For optimal yield, the fruiting stems should be harvested when the berries turn a dark red or black color, typically in the fall, using pruning shears to cut the stems just above the base.

After harvesting, the berries should be dried in a cool, dark place to preserve their potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry location or refrigerated to maintain their medicinal properties.
