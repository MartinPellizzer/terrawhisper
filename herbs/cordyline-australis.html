Cordyline australis

What's the taxonomical classification of Cordyline australis?

Cordyline australis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic placement continues through the order Asparagales and the family Asparagaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Cordyline, and its unique species designation, australis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cordyline australis has upright, sword-shaped leaves that reach up to 1 meter in length and grow in dense, terminal clusters from a woody stem.

These leaves exhibit a striking color palette ranging from deep burgundy and magenta to bright lime green. The plant typically features a slender, branching habit that supports a crown of foliage. During flowering periods, it produces large, branched panicles of small, fragrant, bell-shaped blossoms.

These inflorescences are often white or pale pink and emerge from the center of the leaf clusters.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Cordyline australis can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or mild gastrointestinal distress if ingested by humans or pets. While generally considered non-toxic to most adults, direct contact with the sap may trigger redness or itching in sensitive individuals. Some people might experience localized swelling or rashes after touching the foliage. Ingestion of the plant material could lead to stomach upset or nausea in certain cases. It is always advisable to exercise caution and avoid contact if you have known sensitivities to tropical plants.
