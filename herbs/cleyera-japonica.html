Cleyera japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Cleyera japonica?

Cleyera japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic journey continues through the order Ericales and into the family Pentaphylacaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Cleyera, and its unique species designation, japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cleyera japonica has a creeping, rhizomatous growth habit characterized by slender, reddish stems that produce opposite, ovate to elliptic leaves with serrated margins.

These leaves typically measure between 2 and 5 centimeters in length and possess thin, delicate textures. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the leaf axils during the growing season. Following flowering, it develops tiny, globose fruits that contribute to its spreading nature across forest floors.

This low-growing morphology allows the species to form dense mats in shaded, moist environments.

Cleyera japonica contains bioactive compounds primarily concentrated in its whole leaves and stems that are utilized for their medicinal properties. Traditional practitioners often employ these extracts to manage skin inflammations and promote wound healing. Some regional folk medicines also use the crushed foliage to address digestive irregularities. This plant serves as a significant component in several herbal formulations due to its diverse chemical profile.

        What are the herbal preparations of Cleyera japonica?

        Cleyera japonica is prepared primarily as a decoction through boiling the plant material in water or as a topical poultice applied directly to the skin. These traditional preparations are often utilized to address skin conditions or localized inflammation. In some regional practices, the plant parts are also incorporated into herbal washes to cleanse affected areas. The specific method of preparation typically depends on whether the intended use is internal or external. Practitioners select these different forms to maximize the bioavailability of the plant's active constituents.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Cleyera japonica can cause skin irritation, redness, and contact dermatitis upon direct physical contact with the plant. Some individuals may experience localized swelling or itching where the foliage or stems touch the skin. While extensive human studies are limited, the presence of certain plant compounds suggests a risk of allergic reactions in sensitive people. It is advisable to wear gloves when handling the plant to minimize the chance of a skin reaction. Always wash the affected area thoroughly with soap and water if any discomfort arises after contact.
