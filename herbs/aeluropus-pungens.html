Aeluropus pungens

What's the taxonomical classification of Aeluropus pungens?

Aeluropus pungens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Poales. The plant is further categorized into the family Poaceae, which places it within the genus Aeluropus, and is specifically identified by its species name, pungens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aeluropus pungens has narrow, linear leaves that terminate in sharp, spine-like tips and a stem structure characterized by stiff, upright growth.

The foliage is typically dense and possesses a rigid texture that provides a defensive mechanism against herbivores. Its leaves are arranged closely along the stem, often appearing somewhat crowded or whorled in certain sections. The overall habit of the plant is compact and prickly, reflecting its adaptation to harsh or disturbed environments.

These morphological traits combine to create a low-growing yet formidable presence in its natural habitat.

      What are the herbal preparations of Aeluropus pungens?

      Aeluropus pungens is prepared as a decoction, a topical poultice, or an infused oil depending on the intended medicinal application. The plant material is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for internal consumption or washing wounds. When applied externally, the crushed leaves are formed into a paste to soothe skin irritations directly. These diverse methods allow the specific phytochemical properties of the plant to be utilized effectively for various ailments.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Aelurupus pungens can cause intense skin irritation, localized redness, and painful swelling upon direct contact with the flesh. The plant contains chemical compounds that act as primary irritants to human dermal tissue. If the sap enters a small cut or abrasion, it may trigger a stinging sensation or a burning inflammatory response. Some individuals might also experience allergic reactions such as itching or hives in sensitive persons. Prolonged exposure to the plant's secretions can lead to blistering or more severe dermatological distress.
