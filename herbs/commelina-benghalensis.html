Commelina benghalensis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Commelina benghalensis has opposite, sessile, lanceolate to ovate leaves that are typically dark green with entire margins and prominent pinnate venation. The stems are woody and branched, supporting a dense arrangement of foliage. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary clusters, often featuring a tubular structure characteristic of the Rubiaceae family. The plant develops small, fleshy fruits that serve as a key identifying feature for the species. These morphological traits collectively distinguish it from closely related taxa within its genus.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the Bengalen hills in the northeastern region of India. It is primarily found inhabiting the tropical moist deciduous forests that characterize this specific hilly terrain. The species thrives within localized microclimates that offer the necessary humidity and shade required for its survival. Due to its highly restricted range, it is considered a rare component of the regional flora. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Commelina benghalensis targets specific enzymes and signaling proteins, including cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, lipoxygenase (LOX), and various proinflammatory cytokines. The presence of bioactive alkaloids and flavonoids within the plant allows it to modulate the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. By inhibiting COX-2, these compounds effectively reduce the production of prostaglandins, which are key mediators of inflammation and pain. Furthermore, the plant's phytochemicals can interact with nuclear factor kappa B (NF-kappaB) to suppress the expression of genes involved in the immune response. These molecular interactions collectively contribute to the plant's documented anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe allergic skin reactions, gastrointestinal distress, and potential neurotoxicity if ingested in large quantities. Contact with the sap may result in localized contact dermatitis, characterized by redness, itching, or blistering on the skin. If consumed, individuals might experience nausea, vomiting, and abdominal cramping. Some reports suggest that certain compounds within the plant could affect the central nervous system if systemic absorption occurs. Careful avoidance of direct contact and ingestion is necessary to prevent these adverse health outcomes.
