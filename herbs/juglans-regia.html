Juglans regia

What's the taxonomical classification of Juglans regia?

Juglans regia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its hierarchical descent, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Fagales and the family Juglandaceae. Ultimately, it is identified by the genus Juglans and the specific species regia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Juglans regia has pinnately compound leaves composed of 11 to 17 leaflets that are lanceolate in shape and possess serrated margins. These individual leaflets are arranged oppositely along a central rachis and exhibit a smooth or slightly pubescent texture. The tree typically develops a large, spreading crown with a thick, rugged bark that becomes deeply fissured as the specimen ages. Its reproductive structures consist of drooping male catkins and upright female flowers that eventually develop into large, green, fleshy drupes containing a hard woody shell. The overall growth habit produces a massive woody structure characterized by sturdy, spreading branches.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the region spanning from the Mediterranean Basin and the Balkans through to the Caucasus and Central Asia, including parts of China. It thrives primarily in temperate climates where it can find well-drained soils and sufficient water. Due to its commercial value for walnut production, it has been widely naturalized in many other parts of the world. In many temperate zones of the Northern Hemisphere, it is often cultivated in orchards and parks. Large populations can now be found in North America and parts of South America where environmental conditions mimic its original habitat.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apigenin, ellagic acid (EA), gallic acid, kaempferol, and quercetin. These bioactive substances belong to different classes of phytochemicals that provide various health benefits. For instance, the presence of quercetin and kaempferol contributes to the plant's antioxidant profile. Additionally, gallic acid and ellagic acid are known for their potential anti-inflammatory properties. The inclusion of apigenin further enhances the complex chemical makeup of the species.

As discussed in a study, the walnut septum membrane is a dry ligneous diaphragm tissue that exhibits medicinal properties due to its flavonoid content. A total of seven flavonoids were determined in the Greek walnut septa membranes of the Chandler, Vina, and Franquette varieties, which include catechin, rutin, myriceetin, luteolin, quercetin, apigenin, and kaempferol. The validated method used to explore these bioactive constituents showed high accuracy ranging from 90.8% for apiigenin to 97.5% for catechin in within-day assays. For between-day assays, the accuracy ranged from 88.5% for myriceetin to 96.2% for catechin. The precision of the study was confirmed by relative standard deviations of less than 6.2 and 8.5 for within-day and between-day assays, respectively.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe allergic reactions in sensitive individuals, ranging from mild discomfort to life-threatening anaphylaxis. Common symptoms of a reaction include skin irritation such as itching, hives, and swelling around the mouth or eyes. Some people may experience gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. In more critical cases, the reaction can escalate to cause shortness of breath and difficulty breathing. It is essential to monitor for these systemic responses immediately following any contact or ingestion.
