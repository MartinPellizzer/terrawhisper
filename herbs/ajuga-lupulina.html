Ajuga lupulina

What's the taxonomical classification of Ajuga lupulina?

Ajuga lupulina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Ajuga and is specifically designated as the species lupulina.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ajuga lupulina has a basal rosette of leaves that are typically oval or elliptical with serrated margins and a hairy texture. The plant produces upright flowering stems that can reach heights of up to thirty centimeters. Small, two-lipped flowers emerge in dense terminal whorls and are usually light blue, violet, or white in color. These blossoms are supported by square stems characteristic of the mint family. The fruit consists of four small nutlets that develop from the base of the calyx after pollination.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ajuga lupulina?

This plant has anticancer properties that include anti-angiogenic, anti-metastatic, anti-proliferative, and anti-tumor pharmacological activities. These diverse biological actions suggest that the chemical constituents of Ajuga lupulina can effectively inhibit the growth and spread of malignant cells. By disrupting the formation of new blood vessels, the plant demonstrates a significant anti-angiogenic effect crucial for starving tumors. Furthermore, its anti-proliferative and anti-tumor capabilities work to halt the rapid division of cancerous cells within the body. Ultimately, these combined mechanisms contribute to its potential as an anti-metastatic agent by preventing the migration of cancer to other organs.

According to a study published by "Biology", ajuforrestin A is a diterpenoid compound extracted from Ajuga lupulina Maxim that acts as a potent agent against lung cancer. In vitro, this compound markedly curtailed the proliferation of A549 cells by arresting them in the G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle. The compound provokes apoptosis in cancer cells and impedes their migration through the modulation of STAT3 and FAK signaling cascades. Additionally, ajuforrestin A obstructs tumor angiogenesis via selective engagement with VEGFR-2. Using a zebrafish xenograft tumor model, it was demonstrated that ajuforrestin A could effectively restrain tumor growth and metastasis in vivo.
