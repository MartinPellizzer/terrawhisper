Ajuga lupulina

What's the taxonomical classification of Ajuga lupulina?

Ajuga lupulina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is situated in the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Ajuga and is specifically designated as the species lupulina.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ajuga lupulina has a basal rosette of leaves that are typically oval or elliptical with serrated margins and a hairy texture. The plant produces upright flowering stems that can reach heights of up to thirty centimeters. Small, two-lipped flowers emerge in dense terminal whorls and are usually light blue, violet, or white in color. These blossoms are supported by square stems characteristic of the mint family. The fruit consists of four small nutlets that develop from the base of the calyx after pollination.

What are the pharmacological activities of Ajuga lupulina?

This plant has anti-angiogenic, anti-metastatic, anti-proliferative, anti-tumor, and anticancer pharmacological activities. These diverse biological properties suggest that extracts from Ajuga lupulina may play a significant role in inhibiting the development and spread of various malignancies. The anti-proliferative effects work to slow down the rapid division of malignant cells within a body. Furthermore, its anti-angiogenic capabilities help prevent the formation of new blood vessels that tumors require to grow. By exerting anti-metastatic actions, the plant may also hinder the ability of cancer cells to migrate to distant organs. Together, these combined mechanisms highlight its potential as a subject for further oncological research.

As discussed in a study, ajuforrestin A is a diterpenoid compound extracted from Ajuga lupulina Maxim that acts as a potent agent against lung cancer. In vitro, this compound markedly curtailed the proliferation of A549 cells. It functions by arresting A549 cells in the G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle and provoking apoptosis in cancer cells. The compound also impedes migration by modulating the STAT3 and FAK signaling cascades. Furthermore, ajuforrestin A can obstruct tumor angiogenesis via selective engagement with VEGFR-2. Finally, a zebrafish xenograft tumor model demonstrated that ajuforrestin A could effectively restrain tumor growth and metastasis in vivo.
