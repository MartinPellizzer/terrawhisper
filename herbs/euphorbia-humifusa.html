Euphorbia humifusa

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia humifusa?

Euphorbia humifusa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae, falling within the genus Euphorbia, and is specifically identified by the species name humifusa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia humifusa has a prostrate, creeping growth habit characterized by slender, branching stems that often spread along the ground. The leaves are small, linear to lanceolate in shape, and are arranged alternately along the stem. These plants typically feature tiny, inconspicuous flowers arranged in small umbels, which are characteristic of the Euphorbia genus. The stems often exhibit a reddish or brownish tint, especially in areas exposed to intense sunlight. Small droplets of milky latex are present within the plant tissues and are released when the stems are broken.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and semi-arid regions of Southern Africa, specifically spanning parts of South Africa, Namibia, and Botswana. It typically thrives in rocky outcrops and sandy soils where drainage is efficient. Its range is often tied to specific microclimates that provide seasonal moisture amidst otherwise dry landscapes. Populations are frequently found in succulent karoo habitats or along the margins of dry riverbeds. This localized distribution pattern makes the species highly adapted to its specific environmental niche.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia humifusa contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its entire aerial portion, including the stems, leaves, and the milky latex sap. The plant's latex is frequently harvested to treat various skin conditions and inflammatory ailments due to its bioactive properties. Additionally, the whole plant is often prepared as a decoction or infusion in traditional practices to address digestive issues. These extracts are utilized to manage symptoms such as stomach aches or to serve as a general tonic for the body. Careful dosage is essential because the sap can be highly irritating to the skin and mucous membranes.

What are the pharmacological activities of Euphorbia humifusa?

This plant has antibacterial, antifungal, detoxification, heat-clearing, and removing dampness pharmacological activities. The bioactive compounds within Euphorbia humifusa work to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. In traditional medicinal applications, it is frequently utilized for its ability to facilitate detoxification within the body. Its heat-clearing properties help to reduce inflammation and alleviate internal heat imbalances. Additionally, the plant serves to remove dampness, which is often associated with various metabolic and digestive ailments.

As discussed in a study, an exploration of the whole plant Euphorbia humifusa led to the isolation of 14 new triterpenoids, euphohumifusoids A-N (1-7 and 9-15), and 4 known analogues (8 and 16-18). Compound 1 displayed excellent antibacterial activities against Bacillus cereus, Staphylococcus aureus, and S. epidermidis with MIC values of 12.5, 25, and 25 μg/mL. Upon exposure to 1 and 2 MIC of 1, B. cereus underwent drastic morphological changes resulting in complete cell disruption. Compound 1 also exhibited remarkable antibiofilm activity against B. cereus at 1 MIC and 2 MIC.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse range of bioactive medicinal compounds including apigenin, coumarins, flavonoids, phenolic acids, and tannins. These specific phytochemicals work together to provide various therapeutic properties such as antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. The presence of flavonoids and apigenin contributes significantly to the plant's ability to combat oxidative stress in biological systems. Additionally, the coumarins and phenolic acids play vital roles in protecting cells from damage. Tannins are also found within the plant tissues and assist in providing astringent qualities. Together, these chemical constituents form a complex profile that supports the plant's overall medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, the investigation on the herbal of Euphorbia humifusa Wild was carried out to find its anti-HBV constituents. Through chromatography using Sephadex LH-20 and MCI GEL CHP 20P, five apigenin glycosides were identified. These constituents include apigenin-7-O-(6"-O-galloyl)-beta-D-glucopyranoside (1), apigenin-7-O-beta-D-apiofuranosyl (1-->2)-beta-D-glucopyranoside (2), apigenin-7-O-beta-D-lutinoside (3), apigenin-7-O-beta-D-glucopyranside (4), and apigenin (5). Within this group, compound 1 is a new compound found in the plant. Additionally, compounds 2 and 3 were isolated from Euphorbia humifusa for the first time.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating hepatitis B virus (HBV), Trichophyton rubrum fungal infections, diarrhea, various intestinal diseases, and jaundice. The medicinal properties of Euphorbia humifusa help alleviate digestive issues such as diarrhea and more complex intestinal diseases. It is also applied in traditional remedies to address symptoms of jaundice caused by liver dysfunction. Furthermore, its bioactive compounds are utilized to combat the Trichophyton rubrum fungus which causes skin infections. These diverse applications highlight the plant's role in addressing both systemic liver conditions and localized infections.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and temporary blindness if its milky sap comes into contact with the eyes or sensitive tissues. The caustic latex contains diterpene esters that act as potent irritants to the human body. Contact with the skin often results in redness, swelling, and painful blistering. If ingested, the sap may lead to intense gastrointestinal distress and internal inflammation. These adverse reactions are common whenever the plant's fluids touch mucous membranes.
