Cocculus orbiculatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cocculus orbiculatus?

Cocculus orbiculatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Menispermaceae. At the most specific levels of its classification, it is identified under the genus Cocculus, with the specific epithet orbiculatus.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Cocculus orbiculatus contains high concentrations of various isoquinoline alkaloids, most notably cocculine, orbiculatine, and various related derivatives. These bioactive compounds are primarily concentrated within the plant's roots and rhizomes. Historically, these specific chemical constituents have been utilized in traditional medicinal practices to treat conditions such as intestinal worms and certain neurological ailments. The alkaloid profile of the species is significant for its potential sedative and antispasmodic properties. Researchers continue to study these specific molecules to understand their complex pharmacological interactions with the human nervous system.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cocculus orbiculatus?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including parasitic infections, intestinal worms, and certain skin conditions. In traditional medicine, it has historically been applied to combat intestinal parasites such as threadworms and tapeworms. The berries contain alkaloids that act as a potent vermifuge to expel these organisms from the digestive tract. Some practitioners also utilize extracts of the plant to address inflammatory skin issues through topical applications. However, extreme caution is necessary because the chemical components are highly toxic to humans and animals if ingested in improper amounts.

What are the herbal preparations of Cocculus orbiculatus?

Cocculus orbiculatus is prepared primarily as a dried tincture or a concentrated liquid extract to harness its active alkaloids. These liquid preparations are often used in homeopathic dilutions because the raw plant contains potent neurotoxic compounds. Some traditional practitioners may also utilize the dried plant material in decoctions, although this requires extreme caution due to toxicity. Because the plant is highly poisonous, most modern applications focus on highly controlled, small-scale medicinal preparations. These forms allow for precise dosing to avoid the dangerous effects associated with the whole berry.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cocculus orbiculatus can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, neurological impairment, and potential death if ingested due to the presence of toxic alkaloids like cuculines. Consumption of the berries often leads to intense vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea as the body reacts to the poison. In more serious cases, individuals may experience dizziness, tremors, or convulsions that indicate central nervous system involvement. Prolonged or high-dose exposure can result in respiratory failure or a complete collapse of bodily functions. It is critical to avoid all parts of this plant to prevent these life-threatening complications.
