Saussurea amara

What's the taxonomical classification of Saussurea amara?

Saussurea amara belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae and is specifically identified within the genus Saussurea, with its unique biological designation being the species amaram.

During its flowering stage, the plant produces upright inflorescences consisting of small, densely packed heads. These flower heads are usually yellowish or greenish in color and emerge from a central stem. What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude alpine regions of central and western China, specifically within the provinces of Yunnan, Sichuan, and Tibet. It thrives in mountainous environments where rocky crevices and scree slopes provide its preferred habitat. These populations are typically found at elevations ranging from 3,000 to 4,500 meters above sea level. The species is restricted to these specific Himalayan and Hengduan mountain systems due to its requirement for cool, moist, and high-elevation microclimates. Such specialized ecological needs limit its natural range to these remote, rugged terrains.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating hepatobiliary disorders, digestive malfunctions, and various inflammatory ailments. Its medicinal properties are frequently harnessed to support liver function and resolve issues related to the gallbladder. Additionally, practitioners often utilize it to alleviate gastrointestinal distress and promote overall metabolic health. The bioactive compounds within the plant work to stimulate bile production and assist in detoxifying the digestive tract. Through these actions, it serves as a potent natural remedy for maintaining systemic wellness.

According to a study published by "Planta medica", Saussurea amaram is used in traditional Mongolian medicine for the treatment of hepato-biliary disorders. The methanolic extract of the plant exerted a dose-dependent increase in bile flow of 16%, 37%, 53%, and 61% at concentrations of 50 mg/L, 100 mg/L, 250 mg/L, and 500 mg/L. The aqueous crude extract and ethyl acetate extract also showed dose-dependent increases, although a continuous decrease in bile flow was observed at 1000 mg/L and 100 mg/L respectively. Additionally, the Apigenin 7-O-glucoside found in extracts 2 and 3 induced a dose-dependent increase of 20%, 30%, and 40% at doses of 5 mg/L, 10 mg/L, and 20 mg/L.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?
