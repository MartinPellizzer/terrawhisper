Gerbera delavayi

What's the taxonomical classification of Gerbera delavayi?

Gerbera delavayi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of botanical classification, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Gerbera, and its specific species designation, delavayi.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gerbera delavayi has a rosette-forming growth habit characterized by long, slender leaf petioles and large, deeply lobed, pinnatisect leaves that emerge from a central base. The foliage is typically dark green and possesses a slightly hairy or pubescent texture on both the upper and lower surfaces. From this basal rosette, tall, leafless flowering stalks called scapes rise to support the inflorescences. Each flower head is a composite structure consisting of numerous bright ray florets surrounding a central cluster of disc florets. These scape stems are often covered in fine hairs, providing structural support for the heavy terminal blooms.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of South Africa, specifically within the Drakensberg mountain range and parts of the Eastern Cape. It thrives in montane grasslands where the terrain is rugged and the climate is temperate. These populations are typically found at elevations ranging from rocky outcrops to open alpine slopes. The distribution is relatively localized to these specific southern African mountain systems. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds Gerbelavinside A, Gerbelavinside B, Gerbelavinside C, Gerbelavinside F, and gerbelavin G. These bioactive molecules belong to a unique class of iridoid glycosides found within the plant's tissues. Researchers study these particular constituents to understand their potential therapeutic properties in various biological systems. The presence of these diverse gerbelavin-type compounds contributes to the overall chemical profile of the species. Understanding the concentration of each compound is essential for evaluating the plant's medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical investigation of the whole plant of Gerbera delavayi afforded four new glycosides. These compounds include three coumarin glycosides, specifically Gerbelavinside A (1), Gerbelavinside B (2), and Gerbelavinside C (3). Additionally, the plant contains one acetophenone glycoside identified as Gerbelavinside F (4). These isolated compounds were examined for their effects on nitric oxide (NO) production inhibition in lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced RAW264.7 macrophage cells. Among these, Gerbelavinside C presented a certain inhibitory activity.
