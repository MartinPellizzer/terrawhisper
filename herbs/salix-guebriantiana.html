Salix guebriantiana

What's the taxonomical classification of Salix guebriantiana?

Salix guebriantiana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Malpighiales. The plant is a member of the family Salicaceae and is specifically categorized within the genus Salix, ultimately reaching its unique designation at the species level as guebriantiana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salix guebriantiana has lanceolate to narrowly elliptical leaves that are typically 5 to 12 centimeters long, featuring finely serrated margins and a smooth, glabrous texture. The plant exhibits a shrubby or small tree growth habit with slender, flexible branches that may appear somewhat pendulous. Its catkins are relatively short and emerge alongside the new foliage during the spring flowering season. The bark on mature stems is grayish and develops shallow longitudinal fissures over time. These morphological traits distinguish the species from other similar willow varieties found in its native montane habitats.

The species thrives in temperate climates where well-drained soils and sufficient moisture are available.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause adverse reactions such as gastrointestinal irritation, allergic dermatitis, and increased bleeding risks due to its high salicylate content. Because it contains compounds similar to aspirin, individuals with salicylate sensitivities may experience hives, swelling, or respiratory issues. The presence of salicin can also interfere with blood clotting, making it potentially dangerous for people taking anticoagulants. Children and teenagers should avoid consumption entirely to prevent the risk of Reye's syndrome during viral infections. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should also exercise extreme caution to avoid potential complications.
