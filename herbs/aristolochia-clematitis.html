What to know about Aristolochia clematitis (birthwort) before using it medicinally

Birthwort, scientifically known as Aristolochia clematitis, is a herb that has been revered for centuries for its remarkable health properties, which have been shown to improve cardiovascular health, reduce inflammation, and even exhibit antitumor activity.

In culinary circles, birthwort is prized for its unique flavor profile, often used in soups, salads, and sauces to add depth and complexity. From a horticultural perspective, birthwort is a relatively low-maintenance perennial that thrives in well-drained soil and partial shade, making it an attractive addition to many gardens. Botanically speaking, birthwort belongs to the Aristolochiaceae family and exhibits distinctive flowers that resemble a horn-like shape.

With roots dating back to ancient Greece and Rome, birthwort has been a staple in traditional medicine for millennia, earning its place as one of the most storied and revered herbs in history.

What are the side effects of Aristolochia clematitis when used improperly?

When used improperly, Aristolochia clematitis increases the chances of experiencing side effects, such as severe kidney damage, respiratory failure, and even death.

The plant contains aristolochic acids, which are highly nephrotoxic and can cause irreversible damage to the kidneys when ingested in large quantities or for extended periods. Improper use may also lead to acute kidney injury, characterized by severe pain, nausea, vomiting, and blood in the urine.

Furthermore, Aristolochia clematitis has been known to cause fibrosis of the lungs, which can lead to respiratory distress and potentially life-threatening complications. In addition, some users may experience allergic reactions, including skin rash, hives, and anaphylaxis, as well as gastrointestinal issues like diarrhea, abdominal pain, and nausea.

Moreover, Aristolochia clematitis has been linked to increased risk of developing urothelial cancer in certain individuals, making it essential to exercise extreme caution when using this plant for medicinal purposes and consult with a qualified healthcare professional before doing so.

Additionally, Aristolochia clematitis is also susceptible to powdery mildew, a fungal disease that covers the leaves with a white powdery substance, and rust, a fungus that causes orange-red spots on leaves and stems.
