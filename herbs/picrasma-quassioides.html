Picrasma quassioides

What's the taxonomical classification of Picrasma quassioides?

Picrasma quassioides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Sapindales and falls within the family Simaroubaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Picrasma and the species quassioidea.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Picrasma quassioides contains bitter principles primarily located in its dried root bark and rhizomes. These specific underground parts are harvested and processed to extract potent alkaloids used in traditional medicine. The bitter compounds found within the bark are frequently utilized to treat digestive issues, fever, and intestinal parasites. Practitioners often prepare decoctions or extracts from these woody structures to stimulate appetite or act as a purgative. Because the medicinal efficacy is concentrated in the roots, the foliage and stems are generally not used for therapeutic purposes.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse range of bioactive secondary metabolites including quassinoids, triterpenoids, flavonoids, alkaloids, and steroids that contribute to its pharmacological properties.

The presence of bitter quassinoids is a primary characteristic used in traditional medicine for their antiparasitic effects. Additionally, the triterpenoids and steroids found within the roots and bark provide significant anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activities. Flavonoids present in the plant tissue offer antioxidant protection by neutralizing free radicals in biological systems.

Finally, the alkaloids serve as key components that influence various physiological pathways during therapeutic use.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Picrasma quassioides targets various inflammatory mediators and metabolic enzymes, most notably inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and lipoxygenase (LOX) enzymes. These interactions effectively reduce the production of pro-inflammatory molecules like prostaglandin E2 and nitric oxide, which contributes to its potent anti-inflammatory properties. The plant's bitter quassinoids also influence glucose metabolism by modulating insulin signaling pathways and targeting enzymes involved in gluconeogenesis. Additionally, certain bioactive compounds interact with tumor-related proteins to induce apoptosis in malignant cells by disrupting cell cycle progression. These multi-target mechanisms allow the plant to exert complex biological effects across inflammatory, metabolic, and oncological pathways.

What are the therapeutic actions of Picrasma quassioides?

Picrasma quassioides has potent bitter principles like quassinoids that act as gastrointestinal stimulants, anthelmintics, and anti-inflammatory agents to treat digestive disorders and parasitic infections. These bioactive compounds stimulate the secretion of digestive juices, which helps to improve appetite and facilitate better nutrient absorption. The plant is also utilized in traditional medicine to reduce fever and address various skin conditions due to its antimicrobial properties. Furthermore, its ability to induce bile secretion supports liver function and helps manage systemic inflammation. These combined therapeutic actions make it a significant component in treating metabolic and parasitic ailments.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat digestive disorders such as constipation, stomach aches, loss of appetite, and intestinal worms.

Its intense bitterness makes it an effective stomachic that stimulates the secretion of digestive juices. Traditional practitioners often employ the root to combat malaria and various febrile illnesses. It also serves as a remedy for skin infections and inflammatory conditions.

The plant's chemical properties help regulate bowel movements and promote overall gastrointestinal health.

    What are the herbal preparations of Picrasma quassioides?

    Picrasma quassioides is prepared primarily as a decoction of the dried root or bark, which is boiled in water to extract its bitter principles. This liquid extract is frequently used in traditional medicine to treat various ailments such as fever, intestinal worms, and skin infections. In some regional practices, the plant material is also processed into a fine powder that can be mixed with other substances for topical application. Some preparations involve macerating the plant parts in alcohol to create a concentrated tincture for more potent medicinal use. These diverse forms allow the bitter compounds to be administered orally or applied externally depending on the specific therapeutic goal.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Picrasma quassioides can cause severe gastrointestinal distress including intense nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea due to its high concentration of bitter principles. These potent bitter compounds act as strong purgatives that may lead to dehydration if consumed in excessive amounts. Some individuals might also experience localized irritation or allergic skin reactions upon direct contact with the plant material. Because of its intense physiological impact, careful dosage is critical to avoid systemic toxicity. Prolonged or improper use could potentially disrupt the natural electrolyte balance in the body.
