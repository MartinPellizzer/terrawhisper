What's the taxonomical classification of Picrasma quassioides?

Picrasma quassioides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Sapindales and falls within the family Simaroubaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Picrasma and the species quassioidea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Picrasma quassioides has large, pinnately compound leaves that typically consist of seven to eleven leaflets arranged along a central rachis. The leaflets are generally elliptical or lanceolate in shape with entire margins and acute tips. This woody shrub or small tree exhibits a spreading growth habit with branches that can become quite sturdy. The flowers are small, yellowish-green, and arranged in dense terminal panicles. During the fruiting stage, the plant produces small, fleshy drupes that transition in color as they mature.

What are the pharmacological activities of Picrasma quassioides?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-cancer, anti-hepatoma, antitumor, cytotoxic, and inhibitory activity against cancer cells. The bioactive compounds within the plant act by inducing programmed cell death in various malignant cell lines. Specifically, its potent cytotoxic properties allow it to target and destroy rapidly dividing cells. Researchers have observed its significant inhibitory activity against cancer cells during several in vitro studies. These multifaceted effects make it a subject of great interest for developing new therapeutic strategies against hepatoma and other tumors.

As discussed in a study, Picrasma quassioides stems and leaves are recorded in the Chinese Pharmacopeia as Kumu with antimicrobial, anti-cancer, and anti-parasitic effects. Bioassay results demonstrated the efficacy of both leaves and stems against a tumor cell line (A549), 5 fungi, 6 Gram-positive bacteria, and 9 Gram-negative bacteria. While both parts were effective against these targets, only the leaves exhibited anthelmintiic activity against N2 wild-type Caenorhbditis elegans. A total of 181 compounds were identified from the plant, including 43 beta-carbolines, 20 bis beta-carbolines, 8 canthinone alkaloids, 56 quassinoids, 12 triterpenoids, 13 terpenoid derivatives, 11 flavonoids, 7 coumarins, and 11 phenolic derivatives. The antibacterial, antifungal, and anthelmintic activities of the PQ leaves were reported for the first time in this study.
