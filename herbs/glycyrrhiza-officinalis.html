Licorice

Glycyrrhiza officinalis

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) is a member of the Fabaceae family, traditionally used in various cultures for its anti-inflammatory properties, treatment of respiratory infections, and gastric ulcer management. The roots and rhizomes of the plant are most commonly utilized in herbal preparations.

Botanical Identification

Licorice, scientifically known as Glycyrrhiza officinalis, belongs to the Fabaceae family. It is also called sweet root or Chinese liquorice in various regions. A perennial herb native to southern Europe and parts of Asia, it has a thick, fleshy root, pinnate leaves, and purple flowers. Common synonyms include Glycyrrhiza glabra and Liquorice root in herbal texts.

Active Compounds

Licorice contains key active compounds such as glycosides, saponins, and flavonoids. These compounds work synergistically to support its therapeutic effects, influencing anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and immune-modulating activities. Their combined action enhances the herb's overall efficacy in herbal medicine.

Licorice Actions.

Preparation Methods

Decoction: Used for respiratory and digestive tract ailments due to its expectorant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Extract: Used for respiratory and cardiovascular conditions due to its glycyrrhizin content which has anti-inflammatory effects.

Tincture: Used for respiratory and gastrointestinal issues due to its ability to soothe inflammation and irritation.

Safety Profile

Licorice is contraindicated in individuals with hypertension due to its potential to elevate blood pressure. It should be used with caution in patients with cardiovascular diseases or those taking corticosteroids. Long-term use may lead to pseudoaldosteronism, characterized by sodium retention and potassium loss.
