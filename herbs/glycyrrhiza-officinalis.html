Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Licorice, or Glycyrrhiza officinalis, is a widely used herb in traditional medicine known for its potent anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and expectorant properties.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Licorice (Glycyrrhiza Officinalis)?

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) is a perennial herb native to Asia, widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its roots and stems, which contain compounds like glycyrrhizin and flavonoids that contribute to its therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Licorice is Glycyrrhiza officinalis. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Glycyrrhiza, and species Glycyrrhiza officinalis. Common names for Licorice include Chinese licorice in China, Indian licorice in India, and sweet root in North America. Synonyms for this plant include Glycyrrhiza glabra (botanical synonym) and various regional names such as sweetroot, licorice root, and wild licorice.

Plant Description and Morphology

Licorice is a perennial herb that grows up to 1 meter tall, with a straight, branching stem covered in fine, grayish hairs.

Its leaves are alternate, pinnately compound, consisting of 10 to 30 pairs of small, oval-shaped leaflets with serrated edges. The flowers are clustered in dense spikes, featuring small, pale yellow to greenish-white blossoms with a subtle, sweet scent. The root system consists of a thick, fleshy taproot that is often harvested for its medicinal properties.

The plant has a distinctive reddish-brown color, a strong licorice aroma, and a smooth, slightly sticky texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Licorice is native to regions of southern Europe, the Mediterranean, and parts of Asia, including the Middle East and South Asia.

Its geographic distribution includes countries such as Greece, Turkey, Iran, Afghanistan, and China, where it thrives in specific ecological zones. The plant prefers temperate climates with well-drained soils and moderate rainfall. It grows best in regions with mild winters and warm, dry summers.

Ecologically, licorice is often found in rocky or semi-arid areas, often along riverbanks or in open woodlands.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in licorice are the roots and rhizomes.

These parts contain the highest concentration of active compounds, including glycyrrhizin, flavonoids, and saponins. The root is typically harvested during the autumn or early winter when the concentration of medicinal compounds is at its peak. The rhizome, which is the underground stem, is also utilized for its therapeutic properties.

These plant parts are often dried and processed into powders, tinctures, or extracts for use in herbal preparations.

Phytochemical Composition

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) contains a variety of active compounds, including glycyrrhizin, which is responsible for its sweet taste and medicinal properties.

Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids, saponins, and polyphenols contribute to its pharmacological effects. Glycyrrhizin, a triterpene glycoside, is the primary chemical marker associated with licorice's anti-inflammatory and antiviral activities. Other key chemical markers include liquiritin and isoliquiritin, which are flavonoid glycosides with demonstrated therapeutic potential.

These compounds collectively define the phytochemical profile of licorice and underpin its use in traditional herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that contribute to its use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and immunomodulatory effects. The biological activity of licorice is largely attributed to compounds such as glycyrrhizin and flavonoids. Pharmacodynamically, it modulates the immune system and influences hormone levels through interactions with the adrenal cortex.

System-level effects include increased sodium retention and potassium excretion, which can affect fluid balance and blood pressure.

Mechanisms of Action

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its active compounds, such as glycyrrhizin and flavonoids, which interact with multiple biological systems.

At the cellular level, glycyrrhizin modulates cell membrane permeability and influences ion transport by interacting with sodium-potassium ATPase. Biochemical pathways involved include the inhibition of phospholipase A2, which reduces inflammation, and the activation of anti-inflammatory cytokines like IL-10. These mechanisms are mediated through interactions with key enzymes and receptors in the target systems, such as the adrenal cortex and gastrointestinal tract.

The cumulative effect of these actions contributes to licorice's anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and immunomodulatory properties.

Therapeutic Uses

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) has been widely used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties.

It is traditionally used to support respiratory health by soothing coughs and reducing inflammation in the throat. The plant is also known to aid in digestive health by calming stomach irritation and promoting the production of digestive enzymes. Licorice root is commonly employed to treat conditions such as bronchitis, asthma, and peptic ulcers.

Its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties make it effective for managing various inflammatory and infectious disorders.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Licorice is commonly prepared as a dried root, powdered root, or liquid extract, with traditional methods including decocting the root in water to create a tea or tincture, and modern formulations utilizing standardized extracts for consistent potency.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dosage of licorice root for herbal use typically ranges from 250 to 500 mg per day, taken in divided doses, with the exact amount depending on the form of the product and the individual's health condition.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While licorice root is generally safe when used in small amounts and for short periods, excessive or prolonged use can lead to side effects such as hypertension, fluid retention, and potassium loss, and it should be avoided by individuals with certain medical conditions.

Drug Interactions

Licorice can interact with certain medications, including corticosteroids, antihypertensive drugs, and diuretics, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.

Toxicity and Precautions

While licorice root is generally safe when used in moderate amounts, excessive consumption can lead to adverse effects such as hypertension, fluid retention, and electrolyte imbalances due to its glycyrrhizin content, which has mineralocorticoid-like activity.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) is primarily cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil, typically in regions such as China, Russia, and Eastern Europe, where it thrives in full sun and moderate rainfall conditions.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of licorice involve methods such as drying, cutting, and solvent-based extraction to isolate active compounds like glycyrrhizin and flavonoids, which are then used in herbal formulations and pharmaceutical applications.

Storage and Shelf Life

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 1 to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has shown that licorice contains compounds such as glycyrrhizin, which may have anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and immunomodulatory effects, though its use is often limited by potential side effects like hypertension and electrolyte imbalances.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) has been used for thousands of years in traditional Chinese medicine, Ayurvedic practices, and ancient Greek and Roman medicine to treat respiratory conditions, digestive issues, and as an anti-inflammatory and expectorant.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, licorice (Glycyrrhiza officinalis) is classified as a food supplement or herbal medicine, with varying regulatory classifications depending on the region, and its use is often subject to specific guidelines regarding safety, labeling, and permissible levels of active compounds.
