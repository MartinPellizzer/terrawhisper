Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Sanguisorba minor is the scientific name for the lesser sorrel. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. The genus Sanguisorba includes several species, with S. minor being one of the most commonly recognized. It is also known by various common names, including bloodroot and lesser sorrel, depending on the region.

Morphological Characteristics

Sanguisorba minor is a perennial herb with a rhizomatous root system that spreads horizontally. It has upright, branched stems that grow up to 60 cm tall. The leaves are lance-shaped, with a hairy texture and a reddish midrib. The inflorescence is a panicle of small, pinkish flowers that bloom in summer. The fruit is a small, three-lobed achene enclosed in a persistent calyx.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Sanguisorba minor can be distinguished by its basal rosette of lanceolate leaves with hairy margins, pinkish-red flowers in dense clusters, and a distinctive, hollow, branching stem. It lacks the reddish stems and clustered flowers of Sanguisorba officinalis, and its leaves are more sharply pointed and hairy compared to other similar species.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Sanguisorba minor is native to temperate regions of Europe and Asia. It prefers cool, moist climates and grows in well-drained soils. It is commonly found at low to moderate altitudes, typically in woodland edges and meadows.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Sanguisorba minor blooms from June to August, producing pink to red flowers. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested in early spring before flowering for fresh leaves and roots.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Sanguisorba minor is most closely related to species within the same genus, Sanguisorba. It shares key traits with other members of the Rosaceae family, such as stipulate leaves and aggregate fruits. Its phylogenetic placement highlights its evolutionary ties to other genera in the rose family.
