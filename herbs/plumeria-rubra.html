Plumeria rubra

What's the taxonomical classification of Plumeria rubra?

Plumeria rubra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Gentianales and the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Plumeria, and its unique species designation, rubra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Plumeria rubra has thick, fleshy, succulent stems that are gray-green in color and characterized by a milky, latex-filled interior. These branches are relatively blunt and grow in a spreading pattern to support large, leathery leaves. Each leaf is elliptical or oblong in shape, featuring prominent veins and a smooth margin. The flowers are funnel-shaped and grow in clusters, displaying vibrant petals that transition between shades of red, pink, and yellow. These floral structures are often highly fragrant and serve as a primary identifying feature of the species.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds plumericin, allamcin, kaempferol, rutin, and plumieride. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's diverse biological activities and therapeutic potential. The presence of flavonoids like kaempferol and rutin provides significant antioxidant properties to the organism. Additionally, unique compounds such as plumericin, allamcin, and plumieride may offer specialized pharmacological effects. Together, these substances form a complex profile used in various medicinal studies.

As discussed in a study, Plumeria rubra contains various phytochemical classes including alkaloids, tannins, flavonoids, glycosides, anthraquinones, and saponins. HPLC analysis of the plant confirmed the presence of specific phytoconstituents such as plumericiin, rutin, kaempferol, and isoquercetiin. The leaf extract used in the study was an aqueous-methanolic 30:70% extract. Testing involved doses of 200, 300, and 600 mg/mL for the extract, while thrombolytic activity was assessed at doses of 200, 300, and 600 µg/mL. Additionally, platelet adhesion was evaluated at 200, 300, and 600 µg/mL against adrenaline at 2 µM. These compounds demonstrated significant interactions with coagulation proteins and showed a p ≤ 0.05 significance level in various assays.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation upon contact with its milky sap and induce vomiting, nausea, abdominal pain, dizziness, and headache if ingested. The sticky latex released from the stems and leaves is a known irritant that may lead to redness or rashes on the skin. If any part of the plant is consumed, it can disrupt the digestive system and lead to significant gastrointestinal distress. Systemic reactions may also manifest as neurological symptoms like a pounding headache or a spinning sensation. These adverse reactions highlight the importance of handling the species with caution.
