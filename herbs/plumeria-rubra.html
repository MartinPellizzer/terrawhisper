Plumeria rubra

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Plumeria rubra has thick, fleshy, succulent stems that are gray-green in color and characterized by a milky, latex-filled interior. These branches are relatively blunt and grow in a spreading pattern to support large, leathery leaves. Each leaf is elliptical or oblong in shape, featuring prominent veins and a smooth margin. The flowers are funnel-shaped and grow in clusters, displaying vibrant petals that transition between shades of red, pink, and yellow. These floral structures are often highly fragrant and serve as a primary identifying feature of the species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Central America, the Caribbean, and parts of South America. It thrives in warm climates where humidity is high and frost is rare. Throughout history, travelers have introduced the species to various tropical locations across the Pacific Islands and Southeast Asia. It is now widely cultivated in many subtropical zones globally for its ornamental value. These widespread distributions are primarily facilitated by human interest in its vibrant floral displays.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Plumeria rubra targets cyclooxygenase enzymes, various inflammatory cytokines, and specific bacterial cell membrane proteins to exert its therapeutic effects. The plant contains bioactive iridoids and triterpenoids that inhibit the COX-2 enzyme, which effectively reduces prostaglandin synthesis and alleviates pain. These phytochemicals also interfere with the signaling pathways of pro-inflammatory mediators like tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6. Additionally, certain alkaloids within the species interact with microbial proteins to disrupt cellular processes, providing antimicrobial properties. These diverse molecular interactions allow the plant to function as an anti-inflammatory and antibacterial agent.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation upon contact with its milky sap and induce vomiting, nausea, abdominal pain, dizziness, and headache if ingested. The sticky latex released from the stems and leaves is a known irritant that may lead to redness or rashes on the skin. If any part of the plant is consumed, it can disrupt the digestive system and lead to significant gastrointestinal distress. Systemic reactions may also manifest as neurological symptoms like a pounding headache or a spinning sensation. These adverse reactions highlight the importance of handling the species with caution.

  • Nausea
