Sophora flavescens

What's the taxonomical classification of Sophora flavescens?

Sophora flavescens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Fabales and belongs to the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Sophora, and its specific species designation, flavescens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sophora flavescens has pinnately compound leaves consisting of multiple pairs of oblong to lanceolate leaflets that are arranged oppositely along a central rachis. The plant typically exhibits a shrubby or small tree growth habit with woody stems that possess a somewhat rough texture. Its flowers are characteristic of the Fabaceae family, featuring zygomorphic petals that are often pale yellow or cream in color. These blossoms are borne in clusters and eventually give rise to elongated, dehiscent legume pods. The overall morphology is defined by its fine-textured foliage and the structural complexity of its reproductive organs.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Sophora flavescens contains medicinal compounds primarily concentrated in its dried roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are harvested and processed to extract alkaloids such as matrine and oxymatrine. These specific chemical constituents are utilized in traditional medicine to treat various inflammatory conditions and skin diseases. Researchers also investigate the roots for their potential antibacterial and anti-tumor properties. The plant's efficacy in therapeutic applications relies heavily on the concentration of these bioactive substances found within the root system.

What are the pharmacological activities of Sophora flavescens?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, hepatoprotective, antibacterial, and antifungal pharmacological activities derived from its complex bioactive compounds. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage caused by oxidative stress. The plant also demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory effects by inhibiting various inflammatory mediators in the body. Furthermore, it provides hepatoprotective benefits that help defend the liver against toxic injuries and chemical insults. Finally, its antibacterial and antifungal capabilities allow it to combat a wide range of pathogenic microorganisms and fungal infections.

As discussed in a study, Sophora flavescens is a traditional Chinese medicine used for treating skin disorders, diarrhea, vaginal itching, and inflammatory diseases. Network analysis identified 35 alkaloids from Sophora flavescens, including 10 dominating alkaloids such as matrine, oxymatrine, sophoridine, sophocarpine, oxysophocarpine, allomatrine, sophoramine, anagyrine, cytisine, and N-methylcytisine. These alkaloids provide 71 related targets for the treatment of eczema. Using a 2,4-dinichlorobenzene-induced eczema Kunming mice model, matrine was administered at 50 mg/kg and 10 mg/kg for 22 days to decrease serum levels of TNF-alpha and IL-4. The effects of 50 mg/kg of matrine were almost identical to the 200 mg/kg dose of the positive drug dexamethasone. Additionally, matrine down-regulates TNF-alpha, STAT3, and TP53 at the transcriptional level in dorsal skin tissues.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal quinolizidine alkaloids matrine, oxymatrine, sophocarpine, and cytisine, along with the isoflavone formonoletin. These specific chemical compounds are studied for their diverse pharmacological activities, including antitussive and anti-inflammatory properties. Matrine and oxymatrine are particularly noted for their potential roles in regulating various biological pathways. Cytisine is frequently researched for its ability to act as a smoking cessation aid by interacting with nicotinic receptors. The presence of formonoletin adds further complexity to the plant's chemical profile, contributing to its overall therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, Sophora flavescens Aiton is a Chinese medicinal plant used in traditional Chinese medicine to treat cancer. Matrine (MAT) is an alkaloid extracted from Sophora flavescens. This compound can effectively inhibit the proliferation and invasion ability of TNBC cells such as MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-468. MAT works by inhibiting the expression of BCL-2 while up-regulating the expression of cleaved caspase-3. Furthermore, MAT inhibits the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway and downregulates the expressions of PI3K, AKT, p-AKT, and PGK1.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe gastrointestinal irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and liver toxicity. Ingesting its components may lead to intense discomfort throughout the digestive tract. Many individuals experiencing toxicity may suffer from persistent bouts of vomiting or diarrhea. Furthermore, the chemical compounds within the plant are known to trigger significant abdominal pain. Most critically, excessive consumption poses a serious risk of developing liver toxicity.
