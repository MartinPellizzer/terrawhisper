Commelina paludosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Commelina paludosa?

Commelina paludosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is positioned in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Commelinales and the family Commelinaceae, which uniquely characterizes its evolutionary placement. Ultimately, the plant is categorized under the genus Commelina, with its specific designation being the species paludosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Commelina paludosa has small, woody, perennial stems that branch extensively and produce narrow, linear to lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically dark green and possesses a somewhat leathery texture adapted to its wetland habitat. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in clusters or solitary arrangements. The reproductive structures are characterized by a distinct calyx that protects the developing seed capsule. These morphological traits collectively allow the plant to thrive in saturated, acidic soil conditions.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude wetlands and peatlands of the eastern Andes in South America, specifically spanning regions in Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Bolivia. It thrives in specialized alpine environments known as paramo and puna ecosystems. These habitats are characterized by high moisture levels and cold temperatures at elevations typically exceeding 3,000 meters. The species is often found in saturated soils or near the edges of mountain lakes and bogs. Its distribution is strictly limited to these specific montane niches across the tropical Andes.

What are the pharmacological activities of Commelina paludosa?

This plant has antibacterial, anti-diarrheal, antioxidant, and antipyretic pharmacological activities that demonstrate its medicinal potential. The antioxidant properties allow the plant to neutralize harmful free radicals within biological systems. Its antibacterial action helps inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, the plant exhibits anti-diarrheal effects that can assist in managing gastrointestinal distress. Finally, the antipyretic activity provides a mechanism for reducing elevated body temperatures during fever.

According to a study published by "Ayu", Commelina paludosa possesses antioxidant, antibacterial, antidiarrheal, and antipyretic activities. The antioxidant capacity was demonstrated as all fractions significantly scavenged DPPH radicals. In disc diffusion tests at 500 mg/disc, the zones of inhibition for the plant ranged from 7 mm to 30.67 mm. Minimum inhibitory concentrations for the antibacterial effect were detected within the range of 31.25 and 250 μg/ml. Furthermore, the crude fractions of the plant showed significant (P < 0.05) antidiarrheal and antipyretic activities in Swiss mice.
