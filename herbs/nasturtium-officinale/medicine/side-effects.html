10 Possible Side Effects Of Nasturtium Officinale

Nasturtium officinale has some side effects when used improperly, such as stomach upset and allergic reactions.

Consuming large quantities can cause digestive issues, while individual sensitivities may lead to skin irritation or respiratory problems. Severe cases of an allergic reaction can worsen life by causing anaphylaxis, a potentially life-threatening condition.

In addition, stomach upset can lead to fatigue and decreased appetite, affecting daily life and overall well-being.

1. Irritates skin occurs

Nasturtium officinale irritates skin occurs due to its high concentration of alkaloids and sulfur compounds.

These bioactive compounds can cause allergic reactions, contact dermatitis, and itching when coming into direct contact with the skin.

The skin irritation can range from mild redness and itching to more severe blistering and inflammation, depending on individual sensitivities and exposure levels.

2. Triggers allergic reactions happens

Nasturtium officinale triggers allergic reactions happens to some individuals.

This is due to its chemical composition, which includes compounds like allyl isothiocyanate and benzyl isothiocyanate.

These chemicals can stimulate the immune system, leading to an allergic response characterized by symptoms such as hives, itching, and difficulty breathing.

3. Causes digestive upset takes

Nasturtium officinale causes digestive upset taken by consuming excessive amounts of its volatile compounds, including isothiocyanates and glucosinolates.

These compounds can stimulate the stomach lining, leading to nausea, diarrhea, and stomach cramps in some individuals.

Additionally, the spicy and pungent flavor of nasturtium officinale may also irritate the digestive tract, exacerbating existing gastrointestinal issues or sensitivities.

Nasturtium officinale induces vomiting experiences when consumed in large quantities or used externally.

This is likely due to its high concentration of sulfur compounds, which can stimulate the digestive system and cause nausea and vomiting. Additionally, the plant's volatile oils may also contribute to these effects by irritating the stomach lining and triggering a reflexive response in the body.

As a result, individuals may experience an intense urge to vomit after using Nasturtium officinale, either internally or topically.

The compound 1-asparagusic acid in Nasturtium officinale has been shown to inhibit the action of thrombin, a protein involved in blood clotting, leading to an increased risk of bleeding when combined with anticoagulant medications or following trauma.

The plant's active compounds, such as isothiocyanates and alkaloids, help relax blood vessels, reducing peripheral resistance and decreasing blood pressure levels. As a result, individuals using Nasturtium officinale may experience decreased blood pressure, potentially leading to dizziness or lightheadedness in some cases.
