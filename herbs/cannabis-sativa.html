What to know about Cannabis sativa (marijuana) before using it medicinally

Cannabis sativa, commonly known as marijuana, is a herb that has been widely recognized for its medicinal properties, which include reducing pain, inflammation, and anxiety, while also improving sleep quality and appetite.

As a plant, it has specific horticultural requirements, such as a warm and dry climate, and is typically cultivated outdoors or in greenhouses. Botanically speaking, Cannabis sativa is a member of the Cannabaceae family, characterized by its tall, thin leaves with serrated edges and its ability to produce both male and female flowers.

Historically, the herb has been used for medicinal, recreational, and spiritual purposes, with ancient civilizations such as the Egyptians, Chinese, and Indians documenting its use in various forms, including tea, ointments, and rituals.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Cannabis sativa.

What are the medicinal properties of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa helps with relieving chronic pain, reducing nausea and vomiting, and improving sleep quality, particularly for patients undergoing chemotherapy or with multiple sclerosis. It may also help with anxiety and stress relief. Its anxiolytic properties are due to its ability to modulate neurotransmitters. Its anti-inflammatory properties reduce pain and inflammation.

Cannabis sativa contains over 100 active cannabinoids, including THC (tetrahydrocannabinol), CBD (cannabidiol), and CBG (cannabigerol), which give it its medicinal properties. THC binds to CB1 receptors, while CBD interacts with CB2 receptors, modulating pain, inflammation, and mood. CBG is an analgesic and anti-inflammatory compound.

The most used parts of Cannabis sativa for medicinal purposes are the flowers, leaves, and seeds. The flowers contain the highest concentration of THC and CBD, making them ideal for treating pain, inflammation, and anxiety. The leaves and seeds are used to make teas, oils, and tinctures. The resinous trichomes are extracted for topical use.

Improper use of Cannabis sativa can lead to side effects such as dizziness, disorientation, and short-term memory loss. It may also interact with other medications, including blood thinners, and increase the risk of seizures in people with epilepsy. High doses of THC can cause anxiety and paranoia.

To use Cannabis sativa medicinally, it is essential to consult a healthcare professional. Start with low doses and gradually increase as needed. Choose products with high CBD and low THC content to minimize the risk of psychoactive effects. Keep medications and cannabis products separate to avoid contamination. Monitor blood pressure, heart rate, and liver function regularly.

What are the horticulural aspects of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa grow in temperate climates with warm temperatures between 18-32°C (64-90°F). It requires a photoperiod of 12-14 hours of light and 10-12 hours of darkness. High humidity and adequate water supply are essential.

For optimal growth, Cannabis sativa should be planted in well-draining soil with a pH between 6.0-7.0. The seeds should be 1-2 cm deep and 2-3 cm apart. Direct sowing in late spring or early summer is recommended, when the soil has warmed up to at least 15°C (59°F).

When harvesting Cannabis sativa, it's essential to inspect the plant regularly for mature buds. The buds are ready when they turn golden brown and the leaves change color. Harvesting should be done in the morning to preserve the plant's potency. Cutting the plant close to the stem is recommended.

Cannabis sativa is susceptible to pests like spider mites, aphids, and whiteflies. Fungal diseases such as powdery mildew and leaf spot can also occur. Regular pruning and hygiene practices can help prevent infestations, while fungicides can be applied to control diseases.

What are the botanical characteristics of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa is an annual herbaceous plant that exhibits a range of botanical characteristics.

It has a taproot system, where a single main root grows downward into the soil and smaller roots branch out from it. The leaves of C. sativa are typically narrow and elongated, with a waxy texture and a prominent midvein. They are arranged in an opposite decussate pattern on the stem, meaning that pairs of leaves are positioned at right angles to each other.

The plant can grow quite tall, often reaching heights of 2-3 meters (6-10 feet), although some strains can exceed 5 meters (16 feet) under ideal conditions. Cannabis sativa produces small, inconspicuous flowers in clusters called inflorescences, which are typically white or greenish-white in color. The plant's stems and leaves are covered in tiny hairs that produce resinous secretions, which contain the active compounds responsible for its medicinal and recreational uses.

Overall, C. sativa is a versatile and adaptable species that has been cultivated for thousands of years for its unique properties and benefits.

What is the taxonomy of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Malpighiales, family Cannabaceae, genus Cannabis, and species C. sativa.

As a member of the Cannabaceae family, it is closely related to other flowering plants like hemp and hops.

What are the variants of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa has variants, such as Indica, Ruderalis, and Sativa.

The most common is Indica, which is known for its shorter stature, broader leaves, and potent THC content. It is often used for medicinal purposes due to its relaxing effects.

Ruderalis, also known as the "auto-flowering" variant, is a hardy and resilient plant that produces less THC but more CBD. Sativa, on the other hand, is known for its tall stature, narrow leaves, and lower THC content. It is often used for recreational purposes due to its uplifting effects.

These variants have led to numerous hybrids and strains, making cannabis a diverse and versatile crop.

What is the geographic distribution of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa is widely distributed across the globe, with its natural range extending from tropical and subtropical regions to temperate areas.

It is native to central Asia, specifically in modern-day China, India, and Pakistan. From there, it was introduced to the Mediterranean region by ancient trade routes and spread throughout Europe, Africa, and the Middle East.

Today, Cannabis sativa is found in many parts of the world, including North America, South America, Australia, and Southeast Asia, where it has been cultivated for centuries for its fiber, oil, and medicinal properties.

What is the life cycle of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa, also known as marijuana or hemp, has a relatively simple life cycle.

It begins as an annual plant, germinating from seeds that are planted in well-draining soil and receiving adequate sunlight and water. The seedlings emerge within 1-3 days and grow into mature plants over the next 60-90 days.

During this period, they develop leaves, stems, and roots, and produce small white flowers. As the plants reach maturity, they begin to produce larger, more noticeable buds and resinous trichomes. Once the buds are fully formed, the plant is ready for harvesting, which typically occurs between 60-120 days after germination.

After harvesting, the seeds can be saved for future planting or used for processing into various products such as oils, edibles, and textiles.

What are the historical uses of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa, a species of the cannabis plant, has been used for centuries in various ways across different cultures.

Historically, it was cultivated and consumed in ancient civilizations such as China, India, and Egypt for its medicinal, spiritual, and industrial purposes. The fibers of Cannabis sativa were used to create rope, fabric, and paper, while its seeds were eaten as a source of protein and oil.

Its psychoactive properties made it a popular drug for religious rituals and social gatherings.

What are the mythological referencec of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa, a plant revered for its psychoactive properties and versatility, has roots in ancient mythology.

In Hindu mythology, it is said to have been created by the gods Shiva and Vishnu to bring joy and bliss to humanity. Similarly, in Greek mythology, cannabis is associated with Dionysus, the god of wine, fertility, and ecstasy.

These mythological references not only highlight its revered status but also underscore the plant's symbolic connections to divinity, creativity, and transcendence.

What are the symbolic meanings of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa, also known as hemp or marijuana, has a rich history of symbolic meanings across various cultures.

In ancient times, it was often associated with the gods and goddesses of fertility, prosperity, and wisdom, signifying abundance and spiritual growth.

What are the historical texts of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa, a species of cannabis plant, has been documented in historical texts dating back thousands of years.

The oldest known written records of its use come from ancient China around 2700 BCE, where it was mentioned in the Shennong Ben Cao Jing, a herbal medicine book. Similarly, the Indian medical text Charaka Samhita (400-500 CE) and the Persian physician Avicenna's "The Canon of Medicine" (1025 CE) also described its medicinal and spiritual properties.

These historical texts highlight cannabis' significance in ancient cultures for both therapeutic and ritual purposes.

What are the historical artifacts of Cannabis sativa?

Cannabis sativa, one of humanity's most ancient and ubiquitous plants, has left behind a rich legacy of historical artifacts.

Excavations in ancient civilizations such as Egypt, Greece, and China have uncovered remnants of hemp ropes, clothing, and medicinal products dating back over 4,000 years.

These finds provide evidence of the plant's widespread use for industrial, cultural, and therapeutic purposes throughout history.
