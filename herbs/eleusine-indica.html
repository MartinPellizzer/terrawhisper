Eleusine indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Eleusine indica?

Eleusine indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down its taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Poales and is a member of the family Poaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Eleusine, and its unique species designation, indica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Eleusine indica has a fibrous root system, an erect or decumbent growth habit, and linear leaves that wrap around the stem at the base. The plant typically grows to a height of 10 to 40 centimeters and features smooth, narrow leaves that taper to a point. Its inflorescence consists of dense, spike-like clusters that emerge from the top of the stem. These flower spikes are composed of multiple branches that are tightly packed and often exhibit a dark green or purplish hue. The seeds are small, hard, and contained within the compact structure of the spike.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is distributed pantropically across much of the world, including widely established populations in Asia, Africa, Australia, and the Americas. It thrives in a variety of climates ranging from tropical to subtropical regions. Because it is highly adaptable, it is commonly found in disturbed habitats such as roadsides, agricultural fields, and compacted soils. Its ability to colonize diverse environments has allowed it to spread globally through human activity and seed dispersal. This widespread presence makes it a common weed in many different ecological zones.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds lutein, polyphenol, stigmasterol, β-sitosterol, and Se. These bioactive molecules work together to provide significant health benefits to the human body. The presence of lutein and polyphenol offers potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the phytosterols stigmasterol and β-sitosterol help regulate cholesterol levels and support hormonal balance. The inclusion of Se, or selenium, provides an essential trace mineral that aids in cellular protection and immune function.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating a wide range of medical conditions including diabetes, fever, digestive problems, worm infestation, and cough. Its medicinal properties are often utilized in traditional practices to manage elevated blood sugar levels in diabetic patients. The leaves and roots are frequently prepared as decoctions to help reduce body temperature during a fever. Additionally, the plant serves as a remedy for soothing digestive problems and clearing respiratory issues like a persistent cough. It also acts as an anthelmintic to help eliminate internal worm infestation in the body.

As discussed in a study, Eleusine indica was analyzed to determine the levels of macroelements and microelements in dry samples and teas. The results revealed the presence of K, Mg, Na, P, Al, Fe, Zn, Mn, Cu, Ni, and Se in the plant. The dry samples contained high concentrations of K and P, while Mg, Na, Al, Fe, Mn, Ni, Zn, and Cu were found above the limit permissible level set by the World Health Organization (WHO). Although all hazard index (HI) values in the plant teas were within safe limits for human consumption (HI < 1), ingestion through capsules or teas can be harmful to the health of diabetics. Therefore, the prescription of this plant for the treatment of diabetes should be treated with caution.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, redness, and itching if the leaves or stems brush against sensitive skin. While many people use it in traditional medicine, consuming improper amounts may lead to digestive upset or gastrointestinal distress. There is also a risk of toxicity if the plant has been treated with chemical pesticides or herbicides in agricultural settings. Because scientific research on its long-term human consumption is limited, potential interactions with pharmaceutical medications remain largely unknown. Users should approach its use with caution to avoid unintended adverse health outcomes.
