Manihot esculenta

What's the taxonomical classification of Manihot esculenta?

Manihot esculenta belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Malpighiales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae, specifically falling under the genus Manihot, with the specific epithet esculenta.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Manihot esculenta has a woody perennial structure featuring large, palmately lobed leaves, tuberous adventitious roots, and small, white or cream-colored flowers. The leaves typically consist of three to nine distinct lobes that radiate from a central point on the petiole. Below the surface, the plant develops thick, starchy storage roots that serve as the primary edible component. The stems are erect and can reach several meters in height, often displaying a greenish or brownish bark. Small, inconspicuous flowers are arranged in axillary clusters, leading to the production of capsule-like fruits.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of South America, specifically spanning from southern Mexico through the Amazon basin. While its origins are centered in these warm, humid climates, it has been widely introduced to other tropical and subtropical areas across the globe. Extensive cultivation is now found throughout much of Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Pacific Islands due to its ability to thrive in poor soils. In many of these new territories, it serves as a vital staple crop for local populations. The plant's distribution remains largely limited to regions that lack harsh frost.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Manihot esculenta contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its roots, leaves, and bark. The tuberous roots are frequently utilized in traditional medicine to treat digestive issues and inflammatory conditions. Extracts from the leaves are often applied topically to soothe skin irritations or ingested in controlled amounts to manage fever. The bark can also be processed to create decoctions intended to address various systemic ailments. Careful preparation is essential because many parts of the plant contain cyanogenic glycosides that can be toxic if not neutralized.

What are the pharmacological activities of Manihot esculenta?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including alpha-amylase inhibition, alpha-glucosidase inhibition, anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties. These biological effects contribute to its potential role in managing metabolic disorders through the regulation of blood glucose levels. The inhibition of digestive enzymes helps prevent rapid carbohydrate absorption, which supports its overall anti-diabetic classification. Additionally, the presence of bioactive compounds provides protective antioxidant and anti-inflammatory actions against oxidative stress and systemic inflammation. Together, these diverse activities highlight the therapeutic versatility of Manihot esculenta in medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, Manihot esculenta exhibits significant inhibitory potential against key enzymes involved in hyperglycemia and hypertension. In the alpha-amylase inhibition assay, the inhibitory potential was highest in Manihot esculenta for both the hexane extract at 59.22% and the dichloromethane extract at 54.15%. Furthermore, the hexane extract of Manihot esculenta demonstrated a 95.01% inhibitory potential against alpha-glucosidase. These findings suggest a nutraceutical potential for the plant in the prevention of hyperglycemia and hypertension associated with type 2 diabetes.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several potent medicinal compounds including rosmarinic acid, rutin, chlorogenic acid, kaempferol 3-O-rutinoside, and quercetin 3-O-rutinoside. These bioactive molecules function primarily as antioxidants to protect cells from oxidative stress. Rosmarinic acid and chlorogenic acid contribute to the plant's anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. The flavonoids rutin, kaempferol 3-O-rutinoside, and quercetin 3-O-rutinoside provide additional vascular support and immune modulation. Together, these specific phytochemicals form a complex chemical profile that supports various therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, Manihot esculenta is used in folk medicine to alleviate fever and pain, and in the treatment of conjunctivitis, rheumatism, hemorrhoid, and abscesses. The aqueous extracts of this plant contain certain polyphenols with well-known antioxidant activities, including caffeic acid, chlorogenic acid, hyperoside, rosmarinic acid, and rutin. The study utilized methods such as HPLC-UV/DAD analysis and the Foliin-Ciocalteu reagent to determine the total polyphenol and flavonoid contents. All aqueous extracts, including those from Manihot esculenta, developed concentration-dependent inhibitory effects.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating diarrhea, infectious diarrhea, gastrointestinal nematode parasite infections, and hyperglycemia. Its medicinal properties are often utilized to manage various digestive issues caused by pathogens or worms. Specifically, it helps alleviate the symptoms associated with severe diarrhea and infectious diarrhea. Furthermore, researchers have explored its potential to combat gastrointestinal nematode parasite infections within the gut. The plant also shows promise in addressing metabolic concerns such as hyperglycemia.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe cyanide poisoning if the roots are consumed without proper processing to remove toxic compounds. Initial symptoms of ingestion often include nausea, vomiting, headache, and dizziness. As the toxicity progresses, a person may experience ataxia, confusion, and violent convulsions. More advanced cases can lead to life-threatening respiratory distress. If medical intervention is not immediate, these physiological complications can result in death.
