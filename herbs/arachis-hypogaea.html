Arachis hypogaea

What's the taxonomical classification of Arachis hypogaea?

Arachis hypogaea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae, which places it within the genus Arachis. Finally, the plant is identified at the species level as hypogaea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Arachis hypogaeal has a taproot system, pinnately compound leaves with four leaflets, and trifoliate flowers that produce fruit via geocarpy.

The stems are typically herbaceous and can grow in an erect or trailing manner depending on the specific cultivar. Small yellow flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves before the plant undergoes a unique reproductive process. After pollination, the flower pedicel elongates and pushes the developing ovary into the soil to mature into a pod.

These subterranean pods contain two to four seeds, which are commonly known as peanuts.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Arachis hypogaea contains medicinal properties primarily found in its seeds, which are commonly known as peanuts. These seeds are rich in bioactive compounds such as resveratrol and various fatty acids that offer antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits. The peanut oil extracted from the seeds is frequently used in topical applications to soothe skin irritation and promote healing. Additionally, the plant's leaves and hulls have been studied for their potential roles in traditional remedies, though the seeds remain the most utilized part. Regular consumption of the seeds can also contribute to cardiovascular health due to their high nutrient density.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of bioactive substances including flavonoids, phenolic acids, resveratrol, sterols, triglycerides, tocopherols, carotenoids, and alkaloids.

Resveratrol and phenolic acids provide significant antioxidant protection against oxidative stress in the human body. The presence of various flavonoids and carotenoids further contributes to its medicinal potential through anti-inflammatory mechanisms. Additionally, the lipid composition consisting of triglycerides, sterols, and tocopherols supports cellular membrane integrity and nutrient absorption.

Small amounts of alkaloids are also present, adding to the complex chemical makeup of the seeds.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Arachis hypogea targets several key proteins including cyclooxygenase enzymes, various inflammatory cytokines, and oxidative stress-related enzymes like superoxide dismutase. The presence of resveratrol and other polyphenols in the plant allows it to inhibit the NF-kappaB signaling pathway, which reduces the production of pro-inflammatory mediators. Additionally, its bioactive compounds interact with peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors to regulate lipid metabolism and improve cardiovascular health. These molecular interactions also involve the modulation of antioxidant enzyme systems to mitigate cellular damage from reactive oxygen species. By engaging these diverse protein networks, the plant exerts its multifaceted pharmacological effects.

What are the herbal preparations of Arachis hypogaea?

Arachis hypogaea is prepared as oil, powder, paste, decoction, or infusion depending on the specific therapeutic application required. The most common form is peanut oil, which is extracted through pressing and used widely in culinary and topical preparations. For medicinal uses, the plant parts can be dried and ground into a fine powder to be incorporated into capsules or food. Aqueous extractions such as decoctions or infusions are sometimes utilized to draw out water-soluble nutrients from the plant matter. These various formats allow the plant to be integrated into diverse dietary and pharmacological regimens.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Arachis hypogaea can cause severe, potentially life-threatening anaphylactic reactions in individuals with a peanut allergy. These allergic responses often manifest as hives, swelling of the throat, and difficulty breathing. Some people may also experience gastrointestinal distress, including nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramps, after consumption. In rare cases, sensitivity to the proteins in the plant can lead to systemic inflammation or skin rashes. Managing these risks requires strict avoidance of the plant for those with known sensitivities.
