Clematis otophora

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis otophora?

Clematis otophora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its evolutionary lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Ranunculales. As a member of the family Ranunculaceae, this plant is further categorized into the genus Clematis, with the specific identification belonging to the species otophorus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis otophora has deciduous, opposite leaves composed of three to five leaflets that are ovate to lanceolate with entire or slightly wavy margins.

The plant typically exhibits a climbing growth habit supported by slender, twining petioles. Its flowers are characterized by small, pale to white perianth segments that lack true petals. The reproductive structures include numerous stamens that surround a central cluster of pistils.

Following flowering, the plant develops distinctive feathery achenes that facilitate wind dispersal.

        What are the herbal preparations of Clematis otophora?

        Clematis otophorus is prepared as a decoction by boiling the dried roots in water to extract its active medicinal components. This traditional method is used to create a potent liquid extract that can be consumed orally for various therapeutic purposes. In some regional practices, the plant material may also be processed into a powder for easier dosing or mixing with other substances. Some practitioners utilize the plant in topical applications, though internal decoctions remain the most documented form of administration. These various preparations allow for the controlled delivery of the plant's specific chemical properties.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Clematis otophora can cause skin irritation, digestive upset, and potential toxicity if ingested due to the presence of protoanemonin. This specific chemical compound is known to cause contact dermatitis or redness when the plant's tissues touch human skin. If a person consumes any part of the plant, they may experience symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. While many species in the Clematis genus share these properties, the concentration of irritants can vary between individual specimens. It is important to handle this plant with gloves to avoid any direct dermatological reactions.
