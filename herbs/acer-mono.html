Acer mono

What's the taxonomical classification of Acer mono?

Acer mono belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Sapindales and is a member of the family Sapindaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Acer and is specifically classified as the species mono.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acer mono has opposite, palmately lobed leaves that typically feature five to seven pointed lobes and a distinctively serrated margin. The leaf blades are often broad and can reach up to 15 centimeters in width, exhibiting a somewhat coarse texture. Its woody stem develops a grayish-brown bark that becomes more fissured as the tree matures. The plant produces small, winged samaras that grow in clusters and facilitate wind dispersal. During the spring, it displays inconspicuous yellowish-green flowers that emerge alongside the new foliage.

What are the pharmacological activities of Acer mono?

This plant has antiangiogenic, antioxidant, hepatoprotective, and α-glucosidase inhibitory pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals to prevent oxidative damage within cells. The plant also demonstrates hepatoprotective effects that assist in maintaining liver health and function. Furthermore, it exhibits antiangiogenic activity which can inhibit the formation of new blood vessels. Finally, its ability to act as an α-glucosidase inhibitor suggests a potential role in managing blood glucose levels.

As discussed in a study, extracts from Acer mono leaves inhibited angiogenesis by more than 50% in quail eggs. The research investigated the antiangiogenic properties of 59 plants used in traditional Korean medicine. Selected phytochemicals were further analyzed through molecular docking to the vascular endothelial growth factor receptors 1 and 2, known as VEGFR1 and VEGFR2. Additionally, the study employed microarray-based mRNA expression profiling to correlate the 50% inhibition concentrations (IC50) of a panel of 60 NCI cell lines to these phytochemicals. These findings suggest potential applications for diseases where inhibiting blood vessel formation is required.
