What to know about Cicer arietinum (chickpea) before using it medicinally

Cicer arietinum, commonly known as chickpea, is a herbaceous plant that possesses an array of health benefits, including lowering cholesterol levels and preventing heart disease due to its high content of soluble fiber and polyphenols.

From a horticultural perspective, the chickpea is a cool-season crop that requires a well-drained soil and a temperate climate to thrive, with optimal yields obtained when the temperature ranges from 20-30 degrees Celsius. Botanically, Cicer arietinum belongs to the family Fabaceae and is a member of the genus Cicer, which comprises several species of legumes.

Historically, chickpeas have been cultivated for over 7,000 years, with ancient civilizations such as the Egyptians, Greeks, and Romans relying heavily on them as a staple food.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Cicer arietinum.

What are the medicinal properties of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum helps with various health issues including diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and digestive disorders due to its high fiber and protein content, as well as its ability to regulate blood sugar levels.

The active constituents of Cicer arietinum that provide its medicinal properties include saponins, flavonoids, and phenolic acids. These compounds have been shown to possess antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anti-cancer activities.

The seeds and sprouts of the Cicer arietinum plant are the most commonly used for medicinal purposes, as they contain the highest concentration of active constituents. The seeds are also a rich source of nutrients, including protein, fiber, and various minerals.

Improper use of Cicer arietinum can cause gastrointestinal upset, such as nausea, diarrhea, and vomiting, especially in individuals with pre-existing digestive conditions. Additionally, excessive consumption of the plant may lead to allergic reactions or interact with certain medications.

To use Cicer arietinum medicinally safely, individuals should consult a healthcare professional and follow recommended dosages. It is also essential to be aware of any potential interactions with medications or underlying health conditions, and to monitor for any adverse effects.

What are the horticulural aspects of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum grow best in temperate climates with warm summers and cool winters. They require a well-draining soil with a pH range of 6.0-7.0 and adequate moisture levels. A temperature range of 10-25°C is ideal for growth.

Planting tips for Cicer arietinum involve sowing seeds 5-7 cm deep and 30-40 cm apart in rows spaced 60-90 cm apart. Sowing should be done in late spring or early summer, after the last frost. Seedlings emerge in 7-10 days.

Harvesting tips for Cicer arietinum involve checking for maturity by gently squeezing the pods; mature pods will have a slight crunch to them. Harvesting should be done in late summer or early fall, when the plants are dry and the pods are fully ripe. Cutting the plants near the base will make harvesting easier.

Common pests affecting Cicer arietinum include the aphid, whitefly, and pod borer. Common diseases include ascochyta blight, fusarium wilt, and powdery mildew. Regular monitoring and integrated pest management practices can help mitigate these issues.

What are the botanical characteristics of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum, also known as chickpea or garbanzo, is an annual legume species that belongs to the family Fabaceae.

Botanically, it is characterized by its distinctive morphology. The plant grows to a height of 30-60 cm, with compound leaves consisting of three leaflets that are ovate in shape and have serrated margins.

The flowers are arranged in terminal racemes, which are white or cream-colored and fragrant, with five petals and ten stamens. The fruit is a dehiscent legume pod, 10-15 cm long and 1-2 cm wide, containing one or two seeds that are kidney-shaped and light brown in color. Additionally, the plant has a distinctive trait called "pilosity", where the stems, leaves, and pods are covered with fine hairs, giving it a soft and velvety texture.

This unique combination of botanical characteristics enables Cicer arietinum to thrive in a wide range of environments and adapt to various soil types, making it one of the most widely cultivated and consumed legume species worldwide.

What is the taxonomy of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum belongs to the kingdom Plantae, phylum Anthophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, and genus Cicer.

Specifically, it is classified as Cicer arietinum L., where L. denotes the species was first described by Carl Linnaeus in 1753.

This classification reflects its relationship to other legume plants, including beans and peas, with which it shares many similarities in terms of morphology and physiology.

What are the variants of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum has variants, such as 'Desi', which is an older and smaller type of chickpea commonly grown in India and other parts of Asia.

Another variant is 'Kabuli', which is larger and cream-colored, widely cultivated in Mediterranean regions and the Middle East. There are also 'Garbanzo' and 'Gram' types, which are similar to Kabuli but with slightly different characteristics. What is the geographic distribution of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum, also known as chickpea or garbanzo bean, is widely cultivated and naturalized across various parts of the world.

Its geographic distribution spans from the Mediterranean region to Central Asia, India, and Africa. What is the life cycle of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum, also known as chickpea or garbanzo bean, has a relatively long life cycle that spans about 180 to 200 days.

The plant begins its growth cycle in the spring, when it is sown as a seedling in well-draining soil with adequate moisture. The seedling germinates and emerges into a rosette stage, where it grows leaves and stems for several weeks before producing a flower stem. The flower stem eventually bears small, white or purple flowers that are self-pollinating, followed by the formation of pods containing two to four seeds each.

After harvesting, the seeds can be stored for several months before being planted again in the next growing season.

What are the historical uses of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum, also known as chickpea, has been cultivated for over 10,000 years.

Historically, it was a staple crop in ancient civilizations such as Egypt, Greece, and Rome, where it was used as a primary source of protein. In Indian cuisine, chickpeas have been an integral part of curries, stews, and other dishes since Vedic times.

Its versatility and nutritional value made it a popular ingredient across various cultures and cuisines, earning it the nickname "poor man's meat".
