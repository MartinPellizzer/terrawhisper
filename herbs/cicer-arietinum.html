Cicer arietinum

What's the taxonomical classification of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Cicer, and its species designation, arietinum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cicer arietinum has a taproot system, pinnately compound leaves with numerous small leaflets, and trifoliate flowers that produce small, indehiscent pods containing seeds. The plant typically grows as an erect or semi-erect annual herb reaching heights of 20 to 50 centimeters. Its leaves are characterized by a glaucous, grayish-green color and are arranged alternately along the stem. The flowers are pale yellow and possess a distinct papilionaceous structure typical of the Fabaceae family. Once pollinated, the small, cylindrical pods develop and eventually contain the edible chickpeas.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region and the Middle East, particularly extending through parts of Central Asia and the Indian subcontinent. It has since been widely naturalized in diverse climatic zones across the globe due to its importance as a food crop. Major production centers are now located in India, which accounts for a vast majority of the global output. Other significant cultivation areas include Turkey, Ethiopia, and various nations within the Mediterranean basin. The species thrives in semi-arid environments where it can tolerate moderate drought conditions.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cicer arietinum contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its seeds, although the whole plant including the leaves and pods can also be utilized in traditional practices. These components are often processed into extracts or powders to help manage conditions such as diabetes and hypertension. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several potent isoflavones, which are a specific class of phytoestrogens known for their biological activity. Among these medicinal compounds, the plant provides significant amounts of genistein and daidzein. It also contains the glycoside genistin, which serves as a precursor to other active molecules. Additionally, the presence of biochanin A contributes to the complex chemical profile of the species. These various compounds are often studied for their potential roles in supporting hormonal balance and overall health.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal value of Cicer arietinum can be improved through specific environmental treatments. The research found that isoflavonoid concentrations, specifically genistein, genistin, daidzein, and daidzin, were significantly upregulated in the light environment compared to the dark environment. Additionally, soluble protein concentrations were also significantly upregulated for this species when grown in the light. While the study noted that Phytophthora sojae fungal elicitor caused less upregulation of isoflavonoid synthesis, the light-elicited environment enhanced these compounds. These findings indicate that growing seedlings in the light rather than the dark results in enhanced levels of isoflavonoids and soluble proteins.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause gastrointestinal issues such as flatulence, bloating, abdominal discomfort, constipation, and diarrhea. These digestive side effects are primarily due to the high concentration of complex sugars and fiber found in the seeds. When these components reach the large intestine, bacteria ferment them, which often leads to increased gas and swelling. Some individuals may also experience irregular bowel movements, ranging from difficulty passing stool to loose motions. Managing intake levels can help mitigate these common physical reactions.
