Cicer arietinum

What's the taxonomical classification of Cicer arietinum?

Cicer arietinum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Cicer, and its species designation, arietinum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cicer arietinum has a taproot system, pinnately compound leaves with numerous small leaflets, and trifoliate flowers that produce small, indehiscent pods containing seeds. The plant typically grows as an erect or semi-erect annual herb reaching heights of 20 to 50 centimeters. Its leaves are characterized by a glaucous, grayish-green color and are arranged alternately along the stem. The flowers are pale yellow and possess a distinct papilionaceous structure typical of the Fabaceae family. Once pollinated, the small, cylindrical pods develop and eventually contain the edible chickpeas.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region and the Middle East, particularly extending through parts of Central Asia and the Indian subcontinent. It has since been widely naturalized in diverse climatic zones across the globe due to its importance as a food crop. Major production centers are now located in India, which accounts for a vast majority of the global output. Other significant cultivation areas include Turkey, Ethiopia, and various nations within the Mediterranean basin. The species thrives in semi-arid environments where it can tolerate moderate drought conditions.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Cicer arietinum contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its seeds, although the whole plant including the leaves and pods can also be utilized in traditional practices. The seeds are most commonly used due to their high concentration of proteins, fiber, and bioactive phytochemicals like polyphenols and flavonoids. These components are often processed into extracts or powders to help manage conditions such as diabetes and hypertension. Additionally, some traditional medicine systems employ the leaves to treat various inflammatory issues or skin ailments. The various parts of the plant work together to provide a diverse range of nutritional and therapeutic benefits.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cicer arietinum?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including alpha amylase inhibitor activity, anti-cancer properties, anti-inflammatory effects, antioxidant capacity, and cholesterol lowering capabilities. Its ability to inhibit alpha amylase makes it a potential candidate for managing blood glucose levels in diabetic patients. The presence of bioactive compounds also provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits that protect cells from oxidative stress and systemic swelling. Furthermore, its anti-cancer potential and cholesterol lowering effects contribute to its role in preventing metabolic disorders and tumor growth. These multifaceted therapeutic properties highlight its medicinal importance in modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, twenty eight varieties of Cicer arietinum were analyzed for alpha amylase inhibitor activity using salivary amylase. Heat treatment and germination were shown to decrease the activity of this anti nutritional factor. The alpha amylase inhibitor activity of Cicer arietinum meal also decreased when subjected to UV irradiation and pressure cooking. Furthermore, the amylase inhibitor activity decreased as the days of germination increased. Negligible inhibitor activity was observed on the 6th day of germination.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal isoflavones genistein, daidzein, biochanin A, glycitein, and the glycoside genistin. These bioactive compounds are primarily utilized for their phytoestrogenic properties and their potential to influence hormonal balance. Genistein and daidzein are the most prominent constituents, often studied for their roles in bone health and cardiovascular protection. Biochanin A provides additional antioxidant benefits, while glycitein contributes to the complex profile of these plant-derived polyphenols. The presence of genistin serves as a precursor that can be metabolized into more active forms within the body.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal value of the garbanzo bean, also known as Cicer arietinum, is linked to its isoflavonoid and soluble protein concentrations. These isoflavonoids include genistein, genistin, daidzein, and daidziin. The study found that these concentrations were significantly upregulated in a light environment compared to a dark environment for this species. While the garbanzo bean showed upregulation, it showed less up-regulation of isoflavonoid synthesis when treated with the Phytophthora sojae fungal elicitor. In contrast, the Oligosaccharide fungal elicitor caused no such upregulation in these levels.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidemia, high cholesterol, inflammation, and diarrhea. Its high fiber content helps stabilize blood sugar levels in patients suffering from diabetes mellitus. The nutritional profile of the seeds also assists in reducing hyperlipidemia by lowering overall cholesterol levels in the bloodstream. Additionally, certain bioactive compounds within the plant possess properties that combat inflammation throughout the body. Finally, it can be utilized as a dietary intervention to help soothe and treat cases of diarrhea.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause gastrointestinal issues such as flatulence, bloating, abdominal discomfort, constipation, and diarrhea. These digestive side effects are primarily due to the high concentration of complex sugars and fiber found in the seeds. When these components reach the large intestine, bacteria ferment them, which often leads to increased gas and swelling. Some individuals may also experience irregular bowel movements, ranging from difficulty passing stool to loose motions. Managing intake levels can help mitigate these common physical reactions.
