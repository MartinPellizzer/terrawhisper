Scientific and Botanical Profile

Oxycedrus Juniper, with botanical name Juniperus oxycedrus, is a species belonging to the family Cupressaceae, commonly known as the cypress family.

It is also referred to by numerous common names including White Juniper, Common Juniper, Cypress Juniper, Cedarnut Juniper, Palea Juniper, Spanish Juniper, Cretan Juniper, Cedron, European Juniper, Greek Juniper, Caucasian Juniper, Meditteranean Juniper, and Oxycedrus Juniper. Native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East, this evergreen shrub or small tree is well adapted to arid and rocky environments. Morphologically, it features slender, often twisted branches, scale-like leaves that are typically pale green to whitish in color, and small, berry-like cones that are toxic to humans but serve as a food source for various birds and mammals.

Its ecological and medicinal significance has made it an important plant in both traditional and modern botanical studies.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Juniperus oxycedrus has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and antiseptic properties.

It is available in various forms, including fresh berries, dried berries, tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules, allowing for versatile application. To prepare, the dried berries can be used to make a tea by steeping in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the berries for a longer period. Topical applications include using the essential oil diluted in a carrier oil or applying a poultice made from fresh berries. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried berries per cup of tea, up to three times daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision.

Due to its potency, it is advisable to use juniperus oxycedrus cautiously, limiting duration to short-term use and consulting a healthcare provider for chronic conditions.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Juniperus oxycedrus can be used medicinally for its diuretic, antiseptic, and anti-inflammatory properties, but it must be approached with caution due to potential risks.

While generally safe in moderate amounts for short-term use, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, nausea, vomiting, and in some cases, allergic reactions. It may interact with certain medications, including diuretics, blood thinners, and diabetes treatments, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Special populations, such as pregnant or breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic conditions like kidney disease or diabetes should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is advisable to follow recommended dosages, consult with a qualified healthcare professional, and discontinue use if any adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Juniperus oxycedrus grows in well-drained, sandy or loamy soils in full sun to partial shade, preferring a dry to moderately moist environment with good air circulation.

It thrives in regions with cold winters and warm, dry summers, making it suitable for Mediterranean climates. Regular pruning is essential to maintain its shape and promote healthy growth, while mulching around the base helps retain moisture and suppress weeds. The best time to harvest its berries is in late summer to early autumn when they turn dark blue, using pruning shears to cut the entire cluster from the plant to avoid damaging the shrub.

After harvesting, the berries should be spread out in a single layer in a cool, dry place to dry completely, then stored in airtight containers away from light and moisture to preserve their potency and medicinal properties.
