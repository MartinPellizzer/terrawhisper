Salvia prionitis

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia prionitis?

Salvia prionitis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Lamiales and the family Lamiaceae. It is further categorized into the genus Salvia, with its specific biological identification being the species prionitis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia prionitis has an upright, bushy growth habit characterized by woody bases and slender, erect stems that typically reach heights of up to one meter. The leaves are primarily lanceolate or linear in shape with entire margins and a soft, pubescent texture. Its flowers are arranged in terminal spikes and exhibit a distinct pale yellow color with a prominent lower lip. The calyx is often hairy and serves to protect the developing floral structures. These morphological traits collectively define its appearance within its natural arid habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Salvia prionitis?

This plant has anti-angiogenic pharmacological activity. This specific biological action involves the inhibition of new blood vessel formation within tissues. By preventing the growth of these vessels, the plant may help limit the nutrient supply to abnormal cells. Such properties are often researched for their potential role in managing various pathological conditions. This mechanism highlights the plant's importance in studying complex physiological processes.

As discussed in a study, salvicine is a pharmacologically active derivative from the plant Salvia prionitis Hance. The cytotoxicity of salvicine was measured at doses between 0.625 and 200 µM, with an IC50 on A549 cells of 18.66 µM and an IC50 on HMECs of 7.91 µM. At doses of 1.25, 2.5, and 5.0 µM, salvicine significantly inhibited endothelial cell migration by up to 56, 73, and 82%, respectively. Changes in cell migration were detected with doses of 1.25-5 µM, while tube formation was assessed at concentrations ranging from 0.078 to 1.25 µM. Additionally, salvicine at 30 µM markedly reduced the mRNA expression of bFGF in A549 cells. These results suggest salvicine has potent anti-angiogenic activity by inhibiting proliferation, migration, and tube formation.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 3-keto-4-hydroxysaprorthoquinone, 4-hydroxysaprorthoquinone, hongencaotone, salvicine, and taxodione. These specific chemical constituents are responsible for many of the plant's observed biological activities. Researchers study these molecules to understand their potential therapeutic applications in pharmacology. Each compound contributes uniquely to the complex phytochemical profile of the species. Understanding the concentration of these substances helps in evaluating the plant's overall medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, the roots of Salvia prionitis contain several medicinal compounds including saprirearine (1), saprionide (2), 7,8-seco-para-ferruginoine (3), 4-hydroxysaprorthoquinone (4), and 3-keto-4-hydroxysaprorthoquinone (5). Compound 3 demonstrated antimicrobial activities against Staphylococcus aureus and Micrococcus luteus with MIC values of 20.0 and 15.0 microM. Compound 4 showed inhibition against topoisomerase Iota with an IC50 value of 0.8 microM. Additionally, compound 5 exhibited cytotoxic activities against HL-60 human leukemia, SGC-7901, and MKN-28 stomach cancer cell lines with IC50 values of 4.6, 0.2, and 0.3 microM.
