Cudrania tricuspidata

What's the taxonomical classification of Cudrania tricuspidata?

Cudrania tricuspidata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Rosales and the family Moraceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Cudrania, and its unique species designation, tricuspidata.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Cudrania tricuspidata contains a diverse profile of bioactive secondary metabolites including triterpenoids, flavonoids, alkaloids, and various phenolic acids. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's significant pharmacological activities, such as anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Research indicates that the presence of triterpenic acids may play a role in protecting cells against oxidative stress. Furthermore, the flavonoid content is often studied for its potential to inhibit inflammatory pathways in biological systems. These compounds collectively support the traditional medicinal uses of the plant in various regional practices.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cudrania tricuspidata?

Cudrania tricuspidata has pharmacological properties that include anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial activities used to treat skin infections and gastrointestinal issues. The plant contains various bioactive compounds like flavonoids and triterpenoids that help mitigate oxidative stress in cellular structures. Researchers have noted that its extracts can effectively inhibit the growth of certain pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Additionally, the medicinal use of its leaves and bark often focuses on reducing swelling and promoting wound healing. These diverse therapeutic actions make it a significant subject in traditional herbal medicine studies.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat digestive issues, skin inflammation, fever, cough, and certain types of tumors. The fruit and bark are often employed in traditional medicine to alleviate symptoms of indigestion and stomach discomfort. Its leaves and roots are frequently utilized to reduce swelling and treat various skin ailments through topical application. Additionally, the plant is believed to possess properties that help soothe respiratory distress and lower body temperature during febrile episodes. These applications are rooted in long-standing ethnobotanical practices within specific regional healing traditions.
