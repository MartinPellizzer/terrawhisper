Cudrania tricuspidata

What's the taxonomical classification of Cudrania tricuspidata?

Cudrania tricuspidata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Rosales and the family Moraceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Cudrania, and its unique species designation, tricuspidata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cudrania tricuspidata has woody climbing vines characterized by trifoliate leaves with three distinctively pointed leaflets and small, globose fruits. The foliage typically features serrated margins and a leathery texture that helps the plant thrive in forest edges. Its stems are often armed with small prickles that assist in its climbing habit through dense vegetation. The inflorescences consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into fleshy drupes. These fruits change color as they ripen, transitioning from green to a darker shade to attract seed dispersers.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cudrania tricuspidata?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-tumor, cytotoxic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant pharmacological activities. Its cytotoxic properties allow it to effectively inhibit the growth of various malignant cell lines. The presence of bioactive compounds contributes to its potent anti-tumor effects by preventing tumor progression. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates strong antioxidant capabilities that neutralize harmful free radicals in the body. These combined mechanisms also provide significant anti-inflammatory benefits by reducing systemic inflammation.

As discussed in a study, scandenolone extracted from Cudrania tricuspidata fruit demonstrates anti-cancer potential by suppressing the viability of MCF-7 breast cancer cells. In cell models, this isoflavone ceases the mitotic cell cycle, decreases mitochondrial membrane potential, up-regulates cleaved caspase-3, and promotes the phosphorylation of p53. The substance promotes cell apoptosis and induces sustained activation of p38 and ERK phosphorylation, though it does not affect JNK and Akt. In a human MCF-7 breast cancer xenograft model, scandenolone efficiently suppressed cancer growth and increased apoptotic cells in tumor tissue. Despite these effects, scandenolone showed certain toxicity to normal hepatocytes and breast epithelial cells.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds genistein, kaempferol, naringenin, quercetin, and rutin. These specific phytochemicals are categorized as flavonoids and isoflavones that offer various therapeutic benefits. For instance, quercetin and rutin are well-known for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the presence of kaempferol and naringenin can support cardiovascular health and cellular protection. Together, these bioactive molecules contribute to the complex medicinal profile of the species.

As discussed in a study, ten compounds were isolated and purified from cell suspension cultures of Cudrania tricuspidata using silica-gel column chromatography, semi-preparative HPLC and Sephadex LH-20. The identified structures include 1, 3, 5-trihydroxy4-(3, 3-dimethylallyl) xanthone (1), wighteone (2), 6-prenylapigenin (3), licoflavone C(4), cudraflavanone C(5), erythrivarone A (6), derrone (7), carthamidin (8), genistein (9) and aromadendrin (10). Among these, compounds 2-10 are classified as flavonoids. Additionally, compound 1 is a xanthone that was isolated from the plant for the first time.
