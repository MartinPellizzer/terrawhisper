Ehretia corylifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Ehretia corylifolia?

Ehretia corylifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Boraginales and the family Boraginaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Ehretia, and its unique species designation, corylifolia.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ehretia corylifolia contains bioactive compounds in its seeds, leaves, bark, and roots that are utilized for various therapeutic purposes. The seeds are most frequently employed in traditional medicine to treat intestinal worms and skin disorders due to their anthelmintic properties. Leaves are often prepared as a paste or decoction to alleviate inflammation and soothe dermatological conditions. The bark is sometimes used in formulations targeting digestive ailments and fever. Each part of the plant contributes specific phytochemicals that support its diverse medicinal applications.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Ehretia corylifolia targets several key enzymes and receptors including acetylcholinesterase, cyclooxygenase-2, and various inflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. Its bioactive compounds, such as coumarins and triterpenoids, act as potent inhibitors of acetylcholinesterase to enhance cholinergic neurotransmission in neurodegenerative models. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory activity by suppressing the expression of inducible nitric oxide synthase and tumor necrosis factor-alpha. These molecular interactions contribute to its documented pharmacological roles in treating cognitive impairment and inflammatory disorders. By modulating these specific protein pathways, the plant provides a multifaceted approach to managing complex physiological conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ehretia corylifolia?

Ehretia corylifolia has potent anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antimicrobial properties that are primarily utilized to treat skin infections, wounds, and muscular pain. The plant contains various bioactive phytochemicals, such as alkaloids and flavonoids, which contribute to its ability to reduce swelling and alleviate discomfort. In traditional medicine, its leaves and roots are often applied topically to promote faster healing of dermatological lesions. Additionally, the extracts are studied for their potential to combat bacterial pathogens through cellular membrane disruption. These diverse therapeutic actions make it a significant botanical resource for managing localized inflammation and infection.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, wounds, inflammation, digestive disorders, and fever.

Its various parts are frequently applied topically to promote the healing of open wounds and soothe irritated skin. Additionally, the plant is often utilized in folk medicine to help lower a high fever.

What are the herbal preparations of Ehretia corylifolia?

Ehretia corylifolia is prepared as decoctions, powders, infusions, pastes, and oils using its various plant parts including the seeds, leaves, bark, and roots. The seeds are frequently processed into a fine powder or oil to treat skin conditions and digestive issues. Leaves are often crushed into a fresh paste for topical application to manage inflammation or wounds. Bark and roots are commonly boiled in water to create medicinal decoctions used in traditional systemic treatments. These diverse preparations allow for both internal consumption and external medicinal use depending on the specific ailment being addressed.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Ehretia corylifolia can cause adverse reactions such as skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential allergic hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. While it is used in traditional medicine, the lack of extensive clinical trials means its long-term safety profile remains largely unestablished. Some users may experience nausea or stomach discomfort if the plant is ingested in improper doses. Topical application might lead to redness or localized itching for those with predisposed skin sensitivities. It is essential to consult a healthcare professional before using this plant to avoid unintended pharmacological complications.
