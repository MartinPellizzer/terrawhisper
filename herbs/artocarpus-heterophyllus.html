Artocarpus heterophyllus

What's the taxonomical classification of Artocarpus heterophyllus?

Artocarpus heterophyllus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Rosales and the family Moraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Artocarpus, and its specific species name, heterophyllus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artocarpus heterophyllus has large, alternate, leathery leaves that are elliptic to oblong in shape with entire margins and a glossy dark green upper surface.

The tree produces massive, singular fruits known as jackfruits that grow directly from the trunk or thick branches in a cauliflorous manner. These fruits possess a thick, bumpy green rind that protects a fleshy interior filled with numerous yellow bulbs. Each bulb is connected to the core by a fibrous structure and contains a single large, smooth seed.

The overall growth habit of the plant is characterized by a dense, spreading canopy with rugged, greyish-brown bark.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Artocarpus heterophyllus contains medicinal compounds found in its roots, bark, leaves, fruit, and seeds. The bark is frequently decocted to treat skin conditions, digestive ailments, and inflammatory issues. Extracts from the leaves are often used to manage hypertension and serve as an antimicrobial agent. The seeds can be processed to address intestinal parasites and various digestive disorders. Additionally, the fruit pulp provides essential nutrients that support overall immune function and metabolic health.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Artocarpus heterophyllus targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. Its bioactive compounds, particularly flavonoids and terpenoids, work by inhibiting the nuclear factor kappa B signaling pathway to reduce cellular stress. These phytochemicals also interact with antioxidant enzymes like superoxide dismutase and catalase to neutralize free radicals within the body. Additionally, certain extracts show affinity for modulating glucose metabolism by affecting enzymes involved in carbohydrate processing. This multi-target approach allows the plant to address diverse physiological conditions including inflammation, oxidative stress, and metabolic imbalance.

What are the herbal preparations of Artocarpus heterophyllus?

Artocarpus heterophyllus is prepared as decoctions from the bark, infusions from the leaves, and powders or pastes derived from the dried fruit or seeds. These various preparations are used in traditional medicine to address conditions such as digestive issues, skin ailments, and inflammation. For instance, a decoction made by boiling the bark is often consumed to treat various internal maladies. The leaves may be crushed into a paste to be applied topically to wounds or skin infections. Additionally, the seeds are frequently dried and ground into a fine powder for medicinal use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Artocarpus heterophyllus can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, itching, or swelling in individuals sensitive to the latex found within the plant. The milky sap contains various proteins that may trigger localized irritation upon contact with the skin or mucous membranes. Some people may experience gastrointestinal distress or minor digestive upset if they consume large quantities of the fruit unexpectedly. While generally considered safe for most, hypersensitivity to the tree's components can lead to more significant dermatological responses. Monitoring for any redness or rash is recommended when first introducing the fruit into a diet.
