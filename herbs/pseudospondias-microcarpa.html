Pseudospondias microcarpa

What's the taxonomical classification of Pseudospondias microcarpa?

Pseudospondias microcarpa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized under the order Sapindales. The plant is a constituent of the family Anacardiaceae, placing it within the genus Pseudospoondias, and is specifically identified by its species name, microcarppa.

What are the therapeutic actions of Pseudospondias microcarpa?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, stomach pains, diarrhea, and inflammatory conditions. The bark and leaves are often prepared as decoctions or poultices to address these specific issues. Local traditional medicine practitioners utilize its chemical properties to soothe digestive distress and promote wound healing. By applying these botanical extracts, patients aim to reduce localized swelling and alleviate systemic discomfort. The plant serves as a vital resource in regional ethnobotanical practices for managing common tropical maladies.

What are the herbal preparations of Pseudospondias microcarpa?

Pseudospondias microcarpa is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the specific ailment being treated. The bark and leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a potent medicinal tea used for internal consumption. Some traditional practitioners crush the fresh leaves into a paste to be applied directly to the skin for localized relief. Additionally, the dried plant material can be steeped in alcohol to produce a concentrated tincture. These various methods allow for the extraction of different active compounds suitable for diverse therapeutic needs.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Pseudospondias microcarpa can cause allergic reactions such as skin rashes, itching, or swelling if the plant or its sap comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. While specific clinical data on human toxicity is limited, many species in the Anacardiaceae family are known to contain urushiol-like compounds that trigger contact dermatitis. Ingestion of unknown plant parts might lead to gastrointestinal distress, including nausea or stomach cramping. It is important to avoid consuming any part of this plant without expert identification to prevent potential poisoning. Always exercise caution when handling tropical flora that may contain bioactive or irritating chemical compounds.
