Siraitia grosvenorii

What's the taxonomical classification of Siraitia grosvenorii?

Siraitia grosvenorii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic progression continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it firmly within the order Cucurbitales. As a member of the family Cucurbitaceae, it is grouped alongside other gourds and melons, specifically falling under the genus Siraitia. Finally, the plant is identified at the species level as grosvenorii.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Siraitiia grosvenorii targets adenosine receptors, specifically the A1 and A2A subtypes, through its primary active component mogroside V. These glycosylated steviol compounds act as allosteric modulators that influence neurotransmitter release and metabolic signaling pathways. By interacting with these receptors, the plant exhibits neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory properties within the central nervous system. Furthermore, its chemical constituents engage with glucose transporter proteins to assist in blood sugar regulation. This complex interaction with cellular signaling proteins accounts for its diverse pharmacological profile.

    What are the herbal preparations of Siraitia grosvenorii?

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Siraitia grosvenorii can cause adverse reactions such as nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea if the plant parts are consumed incorrectly. While the fruit is generally considered edible, improper preparation or excessive intake may lead to digestive distress. Some individuals might also experience allergic skin reactions or localized irritation when coming into direct contact with the plant's sap or foliage. Because specific toxicological profiles are not extensively documented in all clinical studies, caution is advised when handling or consuming any part of the species.
