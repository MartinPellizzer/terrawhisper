Atractylodes lancea

What's the taxonomical classification of Atractylodes lancea?

Atractylodes lancea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Asterales, it is a member of the family Asteraceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Atractylodes, specifically identified as the species lancea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Atractylodes lancea has lanceolate leaves that are typically 5 to 15 centimeters long, featuring serrated margins and a distinctively elongated shape. The leaf surfaces often exhibit a rough texture due to the presence of fine hairs or small scales. These leaves are arranged alternately along a sturdy, erect stem that can reach significant heights. The plant also produces small, composite flower heads that are clustered in terminal umbels. Below the surface, it develops a thick, fleshy rhizome which serves as a primary storage organ.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of southern and central China, specifically within provinces such as Yunnan, Guizhou, Sichuan, and Guangxi. It typically flourishes in high-altitude environments found within temperate or subtropical forest ecosystems. These populations are often located on shaded slopes or near rocky outcrops where the soil remains moist. The specific elevation requirements and terrain preferences limit its presence to these particular ecological niches. Because its range is localized to these specific Asian highlands, it is not found naturally in other parts of the world.

What are the pharmacological activities of Atractylodes lancea?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, anti-diarrheal, anti-oxidant, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds help reduce systemic inflammation and mitigate oxidative stress within cellular environments. The plant also demonstrates significant potential in inhibiting tumor growth and protecting liver cells from chemical damage. Additionally, its medicinal properties are effective in managing gastrointestinal issues by providing anti-diarrheal effects. These diverse therapeutic actions make it a subject of intense study in natural product pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract of Atractylodes lancea showed promising anti-cholangiocarcinoma activity in a nude mouse xenograft model at all oral dose levels of 1000, 3000, and 5000 mg/kg body weight. Acute and subacute toxicity tests in mice and rats indicated safety profiles for this material across a broad range of dose levels. No significant toxicity was observed except for stomach irritation and general CNS depressant signs. The investigation of pharmacological activities revealed that Atractylodes lancea possesses anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, antihypertensive, and anti-ulcer activities.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Atractylenolidides I, Atractylenolidides II, Atractylenolidides III, Atractylon, and Atractyloside-A. These specific sesquiterpene lactones and glycosides are responsible for many of the botanical's therapeutic properties. Researchers often study these chemical constituents to understand their effects on digestive health and inflammation. The presence of Atractyloside-A is particularly notable for its biological activity within cellular processes. Together, these compounds form a complex chemical profile used in various traditional and modern medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, lipophilic n-hexane extracts of Atractylodes lancea rhizomes show potent inhibitory activities with an IC50 for 5-LOX of 2.9 micrograms/mL and an IC50 for COX-1 of 30.5 micrograms/mL. The isolated compound atractylochromene (1) acts as a potent inhibitor with an IC50 of 0.6 microM for 5-LOX and 3.3 microM for COX-1. Another compound, 2-[(2E)-3,7-dimethyl-2,6-octadienyl]-6-methyl-2,5-cyclohexadiene-1,4-dione (2), displays selective 5-LOX inhibition with an IC50 of 0.2 microM and a COX-1 IC50 of 64.3 microM. The sesquiterpene atractylon (3) and the coumarin osthol (4) serve as moderate but selective 5-lipoxygenase inhibitors. Finally, atractylenolides I (5), II (6), and III (7) showed no significant inhibitory effects for either enzyme.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating various digestive disorders including gastric ulcer, functional dyspepsia, gastrointestinal syndromes, and general stomach disorders. Its medicinal properties are primarily utilized to regulate the movement of the digestive tract and alleviate discomfort in the abdominal region. By addressing these gastrointestinal syndromes, it helps manage symptoms like bloating, indigestion, and irregular digestion. Clinicians often look to its compounds to soothe the lining of the stomach and mitigate the pain associated with a gastric ulcer. This versatility makes it a significant component in managing a wide range of complex stomach disorders.

This plant can be prepared as a decoction by boiling the rhizomes in water, a tincture made with alcohol, a fine powder from dried roots, an essential oil extracted through distillation, or a mild infusion. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, digestive upset, dizziness, and various allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Additionally, skin irritation or systemic allergic reactions can occur if a person is hypersensitive to its components. It is essential to monitor how your body responds to this herb to avoid these adverse effects.
