Cordia myxa

What's the taxonomical classification of Cordia myxa?

Cordia myxa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Boraginales and the family Boraginaceae. Within this taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is identified by the genus Cordia and is specifically designated by the species name myxa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cordia myxa has an evergreen or deciduous shrub or small tree morphology characterized by opposite, simple, ovate leaves with scabrous surfaces and a woody, spreading canopy. The leaves typically measure between five and twelve centimeters in length and possess entire margins with prominent veins. Its inflorescences consist of dense, terminal cymes bearing small, white, tubular flowers that emit a subtle fragrance. Following flowering, the plant produces fleshy, drupaceous fruits that are globose and transition from green to a yellowish-brown color when mature. The bark is generally greyish and becomes somewhat fissured as the specimen reaches maturity.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cordia myxa?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties. The presence of diverse bioactive compounds allows it to effectively mitigate pain and reduce swelling in biological systems. Its antimicrobial and antibacterial qualities enable the plant to inhibit the growth of various harmful pathogens. Additionally, its potent antioxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals and protect cells from oxidative stress. These multifaceted therapeutic effects make Cordia myxa a valuable subject for medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, the hydro-alcoholic extract of Cordia myxa fruit was investigated for analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects in mice using six groups of 6 animals for the formalin test and another six groups of 6 mice for the acetic acid test. The study administered 100 mg kg(-1) oral extract, 200 mg kg(-1) oral extract, and 100 mg kg(-1) intraperitoneal extract to different groups. In the formalin test, the duration of foot lickings was calculated within the 0 to 5 min acute phase and the 15 to 25 min chronic phase. For the acetic acid test, writhes were counted within 10 min. The results showed that the hydro-alcoholic extract of Cordia myxa fruit was considerably effective in the formalin test.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including rosmarinic acid, chlorogenic acid, caffeic acid, various flavonoids, and quercetin. These phytochemicals are primarily recognized for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Specifically, the presence of chlorogenic and caffeic acids helps to combat oxidative stress within cellular structures. Furthermore, the flavonoids and quercetin contribute to cardiovascular health by supporting vascular function. Together, these bioactive substances work synergistically to provide significant therapeutic benefits to the human body.

As discussed in a study, the edible fruit of Cordia myxa contains thirty-two polyphenol compounds. Among the identified phenolics and flavonoids, rosmarinic acid was abundantly present in all extracts and fractions with concentrations ranging from 1.97-187.15 µg/10 mg. The butanol fraction of ripen fruit extract contains higher phenolics and flavonoids, followed by the chloroform fraction of unripe fruit extract. In contrast, the hexane fraction contains the least number of polyphenols. This research utilized a method with a standard range of 0.13-0.44 μg/mL for LOD and 0.53-1.67 μg/mL for LOQ. All regression equations for these compounds showed a favourable linear relationship with R2 values of 0.9970-0.9997.

As discussed in a study, leaf preparations of Cordia myxa are used in traditional medicine as remedies for osteoartolicular diseases. The petroleum ether and alcoholic extracts of Cordia myxa leaves showed a significant analgesic, anti-inflammatoroy and anti-arthritic activity in the rat. Investigations into the genus Cordia leaves used UV detection at 255 and 280 nm. The study evidenced four flavonoid glycosides, one flavonoid aglycone, and two phenolic derivatives.
