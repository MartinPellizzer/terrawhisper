Ophiopogon bodinieri

What's the taxonomical classification of Ophiopogon bodinieri?

Ophiopogon bodinieri belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Asparagales and the family Asparagaceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Ophiopogon, with its specific designation being the species bodinieri.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ophiopogon bodinieeri has slender, linear, dark green leaves that grow in dense tufts from a creeping rhizome.

These leaves typically reach a length of several centimeters and possess a slightly curved or arching habit. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that emerge from the foliage on thin stalks. Following the bloom, it develops small, fleshy berries that transition from green to a deep blue or black color.

The overall growth form is that of a low-growing, clumping perennial groundcover.

        What are the herbal preparations of Ophiopogon bodinieri?

        Ophiopogon bodinieri is prepared as a decoction, a concentrated liquid extract made by boiling the dried roots in water. This traditional method allows the active medicinal compounds to be thoroughly released into the liquid for consumption.         What side-effects this plant can have?

        Ophiopogon bodinieri can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, redness, or itching if handled without protection. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many plants in the Asparagaceae family may trigger sensitivity in predisposed individuals. Ingestion of the plant should be avoided because the precise chemical composition and potential for gastrointestinal distress are not thoroughly documented. Always consult a medical professional if you suspect an adverse reaction after exposure. Localized swelling or hives may occur upon direct contact with the foliage or soil surrounding the plant.
