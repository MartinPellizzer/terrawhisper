Botanical Identification of Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa)

To identify Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Malvales, family Malvaceae, genus Hibiscus, and species Hibiscus sabdariffa. These classifications place it within the flowering plants, specifically in the mallow family, which includes other economically important plants.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Hibiscus sabdariffa is the scientific name for Roselle. It was first described by Linnaeus in 1753. Common synonyms include Roselle, Jamaica flower, and red sorrel. It is also known by regional names such as karkade in Nigeria and bixa in Brazil. Historically, it has been referred to as the "Egyptian mallow."

Morphological Characteristics

Roselle is an annual herb with a robust root system that supports its rapid growth. Its stems are erect, hairy, and can reach up to 2 meters in height. Leaves are alternate, ovate, and have a hairy surface. The inflorescence is a terminal cluster of flowers, each with five pink to red petals and a prominent yellow stamen. Diagnostic Identification Features

Roselle can be distinguished by its large, hairy calyces that remain attached to the fruit, bright red or pink flowers, and distinctive purple-black seeds. It differs from similar species like Hibiscus cannabinus by having shorter, more rigid stems and smaller, more pointed leaves. Its calyces are also thicker and more pronounced compared to other Hibiscus varieties.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, southern Europe, and southern Asia. It thrives in warm climates with high humidity and well-drained, fertile soils. The plant typically grows at altitudes between 0 and 1,000 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Roselle typically blooms from late summer to early fall, producing vibrant red flowers. Seed formation occurs after the flowers wilt, usually in late summer to early autumn. Harvest timing for the calyces is critical, typically occurring when the calyces are fully expanded and the seeds are mature but not yet dry.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Roselle, or Hibiscus sabdariffa, is closely related to other species within the genus Hibiscus. It shares a common lineage with other Hibiscus species, which are part of the Malvaceae family. These relationships are evident in their floral structures and genetic similarities, aiding in botanical identification and understanding of their medicinal properties.
