Gnaphalium affine

What's the taxonomical classification of Gnaphalium affine?

Gnaphalium affine belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Specifically, this plant is identified by the genus Gnaphalium and is distinguished as the species affine.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gnaphaliuum affine has a perennial, woody-based herb characterized by silver-grey, woolly foliage and small, discoid flower heads arranged in terminal clusters. The leaves are typically linear to lanceolate in shape and are densely covered in fine, white tomentose hairs that provide a felt-like texture. These hairs serve to protect the plant from moisture loss and intense sunlight in its natural habitat. The stems are often branched and can become somewhat woody at the base as the plant matures. Small, inconspicuous flower heads lack ray florets and are composed entirely of tubular disc florets.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating collagen-induced arthritis, gout, inflammation, and rheumatoid arthritis. Researchers have studied its medicinal properties to evaluate how effectively it reduces various inflammatory symptoms. The plant contains bioactive compounds that may help manage the intense swelling associated with chronic joint conditions. By targeting the underlying causes of inflammation, it serves as a subject of interest for managing autoimmune-related ailments. These therapeutic effects make it a significant botanical candidate for addressing complex musculoskeletal health issues.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Gnaphaliuim affine D. Don has been traditionally used in China for the treatment of many ailments, including rheumatoid arthritis. The plant's anti-inflammatory potential was analyzed in vivo using carrageenan induced mice paw edema and in collagen-induced arthritis (CIA) rats. In these studies, the extract significantly reduced paw volume at doses of 300 and 600 mg/kg. Furthermore, in vitro experiments on the NR8383 rat alveolar macrophages cell line showed that the plant could reduce nitric oxide levels, TNF-α, IL-1β, and COX-2. Through HPLC-VWD, 15 compounds were identified within the plant. These results scientifically validate the use of the plant for various inflammatory conditions.
