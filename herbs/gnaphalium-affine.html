Gnaphalium affine

What's the taxonomical classification of Gnaphalium affine?

Gnaphalium affine belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Specifically, this plant is identified by the genus Gnaphalium and is distinguished as the species affine.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gnaphaliuum affine has a perennial, woody-based herb characterized by silver-grey, woolly foliage and small, discoid flower heads arranged in terminal clusters. The leaves are typically linear to lanceolate in shape and are densely covered in fine, white tomentose hairs that provide a felt-like texture. These hairs serve to protect the plant from moisture loss and intense sunlight in its natural habitat. The stems are often branched and can become somewhat woody at the base as the plant matures. Small, inconspicuous flower heads lack ray florets and are composed entirely of tubular disc florets.

    This plant is used for treating inflammation, collagen-induced arthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, carrageenan-induced mice paw edema, and gout. Researchers have demonstrated its ability to reduce swelling and pain associated with these specific inflammatory conditions. Studies involving carrageenan-induced mice paw edema show that the plant effectively mitigates acute swelling. Its medicinal properties are also being investigated for their potential to manage the chronic joint damage found in rheumatoid arthritis and collagen-induced arthritis. Additionally, the plant offers therapeutic benefits for individuals suffering from the painful buildup of uric acid seen in gout.

    As discussed in a study, Gnaphalium affine D. Don has been used traditionally to treat ailments such as rheumatoid arthritis. In an acute study involving carrageenan induced mice paw edema, the extract significantly reduced paw volume at doses of 300 and 600 mg/kg compared to indomethacin. In collagen-induced arthritis rats, it was shown to ameliorate inflammatory symptoms including cytokine, histological symptom, and paw swelling. Furthermore, GA treatment decreased the levels of phosphorylated p65 and IκBα in these cells. Chemical profiling via HPLC-VWD and LC-MS identified 15 compounds within the plant.
