Flemingia macrophylla

What's the taxonomical classification of Flemingia macrophylla?

Flemingia macrophylla belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further organized into the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae, which is characteristic of many legumes. Ultimately, this plant is identified by its specific genus, Flemingia, and its species designation, macrophyllia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Flemingia macrophylla has a perennial, woody-based shrub morphology characterized by large, pinnately compound leaves consisting of several pairs of broadly elliptic to ovate leaflets. The leaflets typically feature smooth margins and a distinctively textured surface that supports their large size. Its stems are sturdy and often develop a branched structure to accommodate the expansive foliage. The plant produces clusters of yellow, pea-like flowers that emerge from the leaf axils during its blooming period. Small, indehiscent pods serve as the primary fruit structure for seed dispersal.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including bound CT, condensed tannins, extractable CT, genistein, and genistin. These specific phytochemicals contribute to the diverse pharmacological profile observed in the species. The presence of condensed tannins and various condensed tannins fractions plays a significant role in its bioactive properties. Additionally, the isoflavones genistein and genistin provide essential therapeutic potential. Together, these diverse chemical constituents define the medicinal value of Flemingia macrophyllia.

According to a study published by "Journal of animal science", Flemingia macrophylla contains extractable condensed tannins at a concentration of 90 g/kg DM. When these extractable tannins were reduced from 90 to 50 g/kg of DM using PEG, forage intake increased by an average of 10% (P < .01). This plant showed greater (P < .01) N flow to the duodenum, N absorbed from the intestine, and fecal N compared to Desmodium ovalifoliuum. The addition of PEG resulted in less (P < .05) ruminal escape protein and less (P < .01) fecal N for this legume.
