Cilantro

Coriandrum sativum

Cilantro (Coriandrum sativum) is a flowering plant native to the Mediterranean region, widely cultivated for its aromatic leaves and seeds, and has been used for centuries in traditional medicine for its potential to support digestive health and alleviate symptoms of various ailments.

Botanical Identification

Cilantro, scientifically known as Coriandrum sativum, belongs to the Apiaceae family. Common names include coriander and Chinese parsley, though regional variations exist. It is an annual herb native to southern Europe and southwestern Asia. The plant has finely divided leaves and small white flowers, with a distinctive aromatic profile. Synonyms in herbal texts often reflect its dual use as both a herb and a spice.

The following list summarize the taxonomy of plant:

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Magnoliopsida
  • Order: Apiales
  • Family: Apiaceae
  • Genus: Coriandrum
  • Species: Coriandrum sativum

Cilantro Identification.

Active Compounds

Cilantro contains essential oils, flavonoids, and coumarin as its primary bioactive compounds. These compounds work synergistically to exert their medicinal effects, influencing various physiological processes through anti-inflammatory and antioxidant mechanisms.

  • Essential oil
  • Flavonoid
  • Coumarin

Cilantro Actions.

Preparation Methods

Powder: Used for digestive issues and as a general tonic.

Decoction: Used for respiratory conditions and to relieve nausea.

Infusion: Used for digestive issues and to promote relaxation.

Safety Profile

Cilantro is generally safe when used in culinary amounts. However, high doses may cause gastrointestinal upset. It should be avoided by individuals with kidney disease due to its potential to increase the risk of toxicity.
