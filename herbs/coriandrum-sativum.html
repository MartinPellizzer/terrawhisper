Coriandrum sativum

What's the taxonomical classification of Coriandrum sativum?

Coriandrrum sativum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Apiales and the family Apiaceae, which is characterized by its distinct floral structures. Finally, the plant is identified by the genus Coriandrum and its specific species name, sativum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Coriandrum sativum has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and features a branched structure with pinnately divided leaves. The plant exhibits heterophylly, meaning it produces two distinct leaf shapes: feathery, thread-like leaves on the lower part of the stem and broader, lobed leaves near the flowering top. Small, white or pale pink flowers grow in compound umbels, which are characteristic of the Apiaceae family. As the plant matures, it produces small, spherical, yellowish-brown schizocarps that contain the aromatic seeds. The entire morphology is designed to support rapid growth and efficient seed dispersal through its branching architecture.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region and parts of Western Asia, though it is now cultivated globally in diverse climates. Its primary centers of origin include the southern European coastlines and the Middle East. Due to its widespread use in culinary traditions, farmers grow it extensively in warm, temperate zones across India and North Africa. It also thrives in many parts of the Americas where irrigation and soil conditions support its growth. This global distribution is driven by the high demand for its seeds and leaves in international spice markets.

What are the pharmacological activities of Coriandrum sativum?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidant, anti-microbial, anti-diabetic, and digestive pharmacological activities that contribute to its diverse therapeutic potential. Its high concentration of bioactive compounds helps neutralize free radicals to provide significant antioxidant protection for cells. The presence of specific phytochemicals allows it to combat various pathogens through its potent antimicrobial properties. Finally, the plant promotes gastrointestinal health by stimulating enzyme production to assist with digestive processes.

According to a study published by "Environmental toxicology", Coriandrum sativuum is a notable medicinal plant known for its diverse properties, including anti-inflamatory, antioxidant, anticancer, analgesic, and anti-diabetic effects. Pretreatment with coriander extract (CE) can mitigate kidney malformations induced by aristolochic acid (AA). This extract also reduces the accumulation of red blood cells in the glomerular region and partially mitigates renal function impairment caused by AA exposure. Mechanistically, CE pretreatment reduces the expression of proinflamatory and proapoptotic genes induced by AA. This suggests that CE likely alleviates acute renal failure by reducing inflammation and apoptosis.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion of the leaves, a decoction of the seeds, an essential oil extracted from the fruit, a tincture made with alcohol, a powder from dried material, or a concentrated extract. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause an allergic reaction, hypoglycemia, nausea, dizziness, and gastric irritation in some individuals. Some people may experience skin rashes or respiratory issues if they are sensitive to the spice. Consuming large amounts might lower blood sugar levels significantly, leading to symptoms of hypoglycemia. Digestive discomfort such as nausea or gastric irritation can occur after ingestion. Additionally, some users report feeling a sense of dizziness following consumption.
