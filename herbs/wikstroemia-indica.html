Wikstroemia indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Wikstroemia indica?

Wikstroemia indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Malvales and the family Thymelaeaceae. Finally, the plant is placed within the genus Wikstroemia, identifying it specifically as the species indica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Wikstroemia indica has evergreen, woody shrubs or small trees characterized by opposite, simple leaves that are elliptical to lanceolate in shape with entire margins. The leaves typically possess a leathery texture and a glossy upper surface. Its stems are slender and can develop a brownish bark as the plant matures. The flowers are small, actinomorphic, and usually appear in clusters or solitary along the axils. These blossoms lead to the production of small, globose berries that transition in color during development.

What are the pharmacological activities of Wikstroemia indica?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its anti-inflammatory, anticancer, antibacterial, antifungal, and anti-HIV-1 activities. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Studies have also highlighted its capacity to suppress inflammatory responses and disrupt the replication cycle of the HIV-1 virus. Furthermore, the plant exhibits promising properties in targeting and killing malignant cancer cells. These diverse medicinal properties make Wikstroemia indica a subject of intense interest for drug development.

As discussed in a study, the ethyl acetate extract of the root of Wikstroemia indica was observed to inhibit nitric oxide production in a RAW 264.7 murine macrophage-like cell line activated by lipopolysaccharide and recombinant mouse interferon-gamma. Fractionation of this active extract led to the isolation of one new guaiane type sesquiterpene named indicanone (1) and two known biflavonoids, sikokianin B (2) and sikokianin C (3). The compound indicanone (1) inhibited nitric oxide production with IC50 values at 9.3 microM. Additionally, it was found to inhibit the inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) gene expression. These findings represent the first report of nitric oxide production inhibitory activity for Wikstroemia indica.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (+) -arctigenin, kaempferol, quercitin, tricin, and 7-hydroxycoumarin (umbelliferone). These specific bioactive molecules are responsible for many of the plant's observed therapeutic properties. For instance, the presence of flavonoids like kaempferol and quercitin contributes to significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Tricin further enhances the chemical complexity of the plant's profile through its unique structural contributions. Additionally, the presence of umbelliferone provides distinct biological functions often studied in pharmacological research. Together, these compounds form a potent chemical synergy within the plant's tissues.

As discussed in a study, a MeOH extract of powdered roots of Wikstroemia indica contains a new guaian-type sesquiterpene (1) identified as 4,10,11-guaiatrien-3-one-14-oic acid. The extract also yielded two known guaian-type sesquiterpene compounds, specifically oleodaphnal (2) and 1alpha,7alpha,10alphaH-guaia-4,11-dien-3-one (3). Furthermore, the plant contains 12 known compounds including (+)-arctigenin, (+)-matairesinol, (+)-trachelogenin, (+)-nortrachelogenin, (+)-hinokinin, (+)-kusunokinin, 7-methoxycoumarin, 7-hydroxycoumarin (umbelliferone), daphnogitin, daphnoretin, salicifoliol, and (-)-pinoresinol.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe poisoning due to the presence of toxic alkaloids and glycosides found within its tissues. Ingesting any part of the specimen may lead to significant gastrointestinal distress. Common symptoms of this toxicity include intense nausea and frequent vomiting. Individuals exposed to the plant often experience bouts of diarrhea as the body attempts to expel the harmful substances. These adverse reactions can escalate quickly if the plant is consumed in large quantities.
