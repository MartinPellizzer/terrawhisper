Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is a widely used herb in traditional medicine, valued for its sweet flavor and potent therapeutic properties.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Licorice (Glycyrrhiza Glabra)?

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is a perennial herb native to Europe, Western Asia, and northern Africa, known for its sweet, aromatic root that has been used for centuries in traditional herbal medicine.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Licorice is Glycyrrhiza glabra. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Glycyrrhiza, and species Glycyrrhiza glabra. It is commonly known as licorice in Europe and the Middle East, and as ganjiang in China. The synonyms for this plant include Glycyrrhiza uralensis and Glycyrrhiza inflata, which are botanically recognized and regionally used names.

Plant Description and Morphology

Licorice is a biennial or perennial herb that typically grows to a height of 1 to 2 meters, featuring a straight, cylindrical stem that is often branched at the top.

Its leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate in shape, with serrated margins and a hairy surface. The flowers are small, yellowish-green, and clustered in loose, cylindrical spikes, often appearing in late spring to early summer. The plant has a deep taproot system that is fibrous and can extend several meters underground.

The roots, leaves, and stems are generally pale yellow to brown, have a distinct sweet aroma, and possess a rough, fibrous texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is native to southern Europe, the Mediterranean region, and parts of western Asia, including regions such as Turkey, Iran, and the Caucasus.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots and rhizomes of Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) are primarily used medicinally due to their high concentration of active compounds such as glycyrrhizin, flavonoids, and saponins.

Phytochemical Composition

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including triterpenoid saponins such as glycyrrhizin, flavonoids like liquiritigenin and isoliquiritin, and alkaloids such as glycyrrhizin and β-amyrin.

Pharmacological Properties

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties, including anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and immunomodulatory effects, primarily due to its active compounds such as glycyrrhizin, flavonoids, and saponins.

Mechanisms of Action

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) exerts its effects through multiple mechanisms, including the modulation of inflammatory pathways via inhibition of cyclooxygenase and lipoxygenase enzymes, enhancement of adrenal cortex function through stimulation of cortisol release, and antiviral activity by interfering with viral replication processes.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support respiratory health, soothe digestive discomfort, and reduce inflammation due to its active compounds such as glycyrrhizin and flavonoids.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Licorice is commonly prepared as a root decoction, tincture, powder, or capsule, with the dried root being the primary source material used in traditional herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The standard recommended dosage for licorice root in herbal medicine typically ranges from 200 to 400 mg per day, often divided into two or three doses, with a maximum daily intake of 10 grams to avoid potential side effects such as hypertension.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While licorice root is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts and for short periods, prolonged use or high doses may lead to side effects such as hypertension, fluid retention, and electrolyte imbalances, and it should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and by individuals with hypertension or kidney disease.

Drug Interactions

Licorice can interact with various medications, including corticosteroids, diuretics, and antihypertensive drugs, potentially increasing the risk of side effects such as hypertension, electrolyte imbalances, and adrenal suppression.

Toxicity and Precautions

While licorice root is generally safe when used in moderate amounts and for short periods, excessive or prolonged use can lead to adverse effects such as hypertension, electrolyte imbalances, and potential toxicity due to its glycyrrhizin content.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil, typically in regions like China, Russia, and Europe, and is harvested primarily in the autumn when the roots are mature and contain the highest concentration of active compounds.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) typically involve harvesting the roots, drying them, and then using methods such as maceration, steam distillation, or solvent extraction to obtain the active compounds, including glycyrrhizin, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent spoilage, with a typical shelf life of 1 to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has demonstrated that licorice root contains bioactive compounds such as glycyrrhizin, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and immunomodulatory effects, supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for centuries, primarily for its expectorant, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties, with historical applications spanning ancient Chinese, Egyptian, and Greek medicinal practices.

Regulatory Status

In the United States, licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is classified as a dietary supplement and is regulated under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994, which means it is not subject to pre-market approval by the FDA but is monitored for safety and labeling compliance.
