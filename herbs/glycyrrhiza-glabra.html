Glycyrrhiza glabra

What's the taxonomical classification of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

Glycyrrhiza glabra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Fabales. This plant is a member of the family Fabaceae and falls under the genus Glycyrrhiza, with its specific designation being the species glabra.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Glycyrrhiza glabra contains glycyrrhizin, isoliquiritigenin, and several flavonoids that serve as its primary bioactive components. The triterpenoid saponin glycyrrhizin is responsible for the plant's characteristic sweet taste and its potent anti-inflammatory effects. Additionally, the presence of liquiritin and other polyphenols provides significant antioxidant and antimicrobial properties. These compounds work together to soothe gastrointestinal irritation and support liver function. Researchers continue to study these specific molecules for their potential applications in treating various systemic inflammatory conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat gastrointestinal disorders such as peptic ulcers, gastritis, and acid reflux, as well as respiratory conditions like bronchitis, asthma, and sore throats. Its active compound, glycyrrhizin, possesses significant anti-inflammatory and antiviral properties that help soothe irritated mucous membranes. In traditional medicine, it is frequently utilized to reduce inflammation in the digestive tract and ease coughing fits. Additionally, researchers study its potential to mitigate skin irritations and support liver function. These diverse therapeutic applications make it a staple in both herbal remedies and modern pharmaceutical research.

What are the herbal preparations of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

Glycyrrhiza glabra is prepared as decoctions, teas, tinctures, syrups, lozenges, extracts, and powders using its dried roots or rhizomes. The primary method involves boiling the tough, woody roots in water to create a sweet-tasting decoction rich in glycyrrhizin. Many manufacturers process the plant into standardized extracts to ensure a consistent concentration of active compounds for therapeutic use. In traditional applications, the root is often simmered into a thick syrup to soothe throat irritation and coughs. Additionally, the dried root can be ground into a fine powder for inclusion in capsules or mixed into topical salves for skin application.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Glycyrrhiza glabra can cause serious health issues such as high blood pressure, potassium depletion, edema, and congestive heart failure if consumed in large amounts over time. This occurs because the compound glycyrrhizin can trigger the body to retain sodium and water while losing essential electrolytes. Prolonged intake often leads to hypokalemia, which may result in muscle weakness or irregular heart rhythms. Some users might also experience bruising, swelling in the legs, or significant shifts in hormone levels. It is critical for individuals with kidney or heart conditions to avoid excessive doses of this plant.
