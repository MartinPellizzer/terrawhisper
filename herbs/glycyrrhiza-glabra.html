Glycyrrhiza glabra

What's the taxonomical classification of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

Glycyrrhiza glabra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Fabales. This plant is a member of the family Fabaceae and falls under the genus Glycyrrhiza, with its specific designation being the species glabra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Glycyrrhiza glabra has a perennial growth habit characterized by a thick, woody rhizome that spreads horizontally underground. This plant produces erect, branched stems that can reach heights of up to one meter and are covered in fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are pinnately compound, typically consisting of seven to seventeen small, oval-shaped leaflets arranged along a central axis. Small, papilionaceous flowers appear in clusters, displaying colors that range from pale violet to bluish-white. The resulting legume pods are elongated and contain numerous small, brownish seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, Central Asia, and parts of the Middle East, extending from Southern Europe through Iran and into Northern China. It thrives in diverse environments ranging from arid steppes to fertile river valleys where the soil is well-drained. Many species have been naturalized in other parts of the world, including North America and Australia, through intentional cultivation. The plant's ability to tolerate various climates has allowed it to spread beyond its original historical range. Its distribution is closely tied to specific soil compositions and sunlight requirements found in these semi-arid zones.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Glycyrrhiza glabra contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its dried roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are harvested and processed to extract glycyrrhizin, which provides a sweet flavor and significant therapeutic properties. The roots are frequently utilized in herbal formulations to soothe gastrointestinal inflammation and alleviate respiratory congestion. Beyond the roots, some traditional practices incorporate the plant's seeds or leaves, though the root remains the most potent medicinal component. This plant is widely valued in pharmacopeias for its ability to support immune function and reduce systemic inflammation.

What are the pharmacological activities of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidant, anti-ulcer, anti-viral, and hepatoprotective properties. Its primary bioactive constituent, glycyrrhizin, helps reduce swelling and systemic inflammation by modulating immune responses. The plant also acts as a potent scavenger of free radicals to prevent oxidative stress within cells. Additionally, it provides protective effects for the gastrointestinal lining against ulcers and supports liver health through its hepatoprotective capabilities. Finally, certain compounds within the species demonstrate the ability to inhibit the replication of various viral pathogens.

As discussed in a study, Glycyrrhiza glabra contains glycyrrhizic acid and glycyrrhetinic acid, which are known for their anti-inflammaforary, anti-oxidant, and hypoglycemic pharmacological activities. Derivatives of these compounds, such as chryseno[2,1-c]oxepin-12-carboxylic acid, demonstrate strong inhibition of NO release at 36.3% in low-dose MG compared to the model. These derivatives also cause transcriptional downregulation of tumor necrosis factor-α by 72.2%, interleukin-6 by 58.3%, and interleukin-1β by 76.4% in low-dose MG vs. model scenarios. Furthermore, these activities alleviate inflammatory responses through the NF-κB pathway by decreasing the abundance of P-IKK-α, P-IKB-α, and P-P65 proteins in LPS-induced RAW264.7 cells. The biosynthesis process using 5 g/L glycyrrhizic acid can achieve a final molar yield of 88.3%.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds glycyrrhizin, glabridin, liquiritigenin, isoliquiritigenin, and glycyrrhizinic acid. Glycyrrhizin and its derivative, glycyrrhizinic acid, are primarily responsible for the distinct sweet taste and various anti-inflammatory properties of the root. Glabridin serves as a potent antioxidant that offers significant skin-protective benefits. The flavonoids liquiritigenin and isoliquiritigenin contribute to the plant's overall bioactive profile by providing additional soothing effects. Together, these specific chemical constituents drive the traditional and pharmacological uses of the species.

As discussed in a study, HPLC profile analysis was performed to determine index compounds of MeOH extracts of aerial parts and EtOAc extracts of subterranean parts for Glycyrrhiza glabra. The research compared glycyrrhizin contents and growth rates of the underground parts of Glycyrrhiza glabra and Glycyrrhiza uralensis to select 4 excellent types as candidates for cultivation. Alkaloidal constituents of Glycyrrhiza glabra were also investigated during the study. From these investigations, anabasine and a new tricyclic alkaloid were obtained.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating gastric ulcer, asthma, inflammation, cough, and peptic ulcer through its diverse medicinal properties. The active compounds in the root help soothe the digestive lining to alleviate symptoms of a peptic ulcer. Its anti-inflammatory effects are also effective in reducing respiratory distress associated with asthma. Additionally, the plant acts as an expectorant to help manage a persistent cough. These various applications make it a versatile remedy in traditional herbal medicine.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a decoction, tincture, extract, powder, tea, or syrup to utilize its medicinal properties. A decoction involves boiling the tough roots in water to extract soluble compounds, while a tea provides a milder infusion of the plant material. For more concentrated doses, practitioners often use a tincture made with alcohol or a potent liquid extract. The dried roots can also be ground into a fine powder to be mixed into foods or capsules. Additionally, the sweet flavor of the root makes it an ideal base for a soothing syrup.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause serious physiological complications such as hypertension, hypokalemia, edema, muscle weakness, nausea, palpitations, diuresis, dizziness, confusion, and insomnia. Excessive consumption of its active compounds can trigger significant fluid retention and elevated blood pressure. These changes often lead to a dangerous drop in potassium levels, which may manifest as muscle weakness and irregular heart palpitations. Patients may also experience gastrointestinal distress like nausea or neurological symptoms including dizziness, confusion, and insomnia. Such systemic effects are frequently linked to the plant's influence on electrolyte balance and renal function.
