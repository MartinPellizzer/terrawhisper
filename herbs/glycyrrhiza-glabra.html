What to know about licorice before using it medicinally

Licorice, scientifically known as Glycyrrhiza glabra, is a herb that has been used for centuries for its numerous health benefits.

It contains compounds that can help reduce inflammation, soothe digestive issues, and even help with respiratory problems. In addition to its medicinal uses, licorice is also widely used in the culinary world. It is commonly used as a flavoring agent in candies, beverages, and even some savory dishes.

From a horticultural perspective, licorice is a perennial herb that thrives in well-drained soil and full sun. It has long, compound leaves and blue to purple pea-like flowers. Botanically speaking, licorice is a member of the legume family and its roots contain glycyrrhizin, the compound responsible for its sweet flavor.

Historically, licorice has been used for thousands of years, dating back to ancient Egypt, where it was used for its medicinal properties as well as a flavoring for food and drink.

What are the side effects of licorice when used improperly?

When used improperly, licorice increases the chances of experiencing side effects, such as high blood pressure, low potassium levels, and muscle weakness.

Prolonged use of licorice in large amounts can lead to a condition called hypokalemic myopathy, which is characterized by muscle weakness and decreased reflexes. Additionally, it can cause fluid retention, leading to swelling in various parts of the body. Moreover, licorice can interfere with the body's ability to regulate blood sugar levels, increasing the risk of developing diabetes.

In some cases, it can also cause hormonal imbalances, particularly in women, leading to menstrual irregularities and infertility. Furthermore, it can interact with certain medications, such as diuretics, corticosteroids, and blood pressure drugs, reducing their effectiveness or increasing their side effects. Pregnant women should avoid using licorice as it can cause preterm labor and low birth weight.

Therefore, it is crucial to consult a healthcare professional before using licorice for medicinal purposes.

One common pest of licorice is the licorice root aphid (Cavariella konoi), which feeds on the roots of the plant and can cause significant damage. Other pests that can affect licorice include mealybugs, spider mites, and scale insects.

These pests can cause leaves to yellow and curl, and can also stunt the growth of the plant. In addition to pests, licorice is also prone to several diseases, including root rot (caused by fungi such as Phytophthora and Fusarium), leaf spot (caused by fungi such as Septoria and Alternaria), and powdery mildew (caused by fungi such as Erysiphe and Sphaerotheca). These diseases can cause a variety of symptoms, including spots on leaves, wilting, and dieback.

Proper cultural practices, such as providing good drainage and avoiding overhead watering, can help to prevent many of these problems.
