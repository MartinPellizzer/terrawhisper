Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is a widely used herb in traditional medicine, valued for its sweet flavor and potent therapeutic properties.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Licorice (Glycyrrhiza Glabra)?

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is a perennial herb native to Europe, Western Asia, and northern Africa, known for its sweet, aromatic root that has been used for centuries in traditional herbal medicine.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Licorice is Glycyrrhiza glabra. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Fabales, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Glycyrrhiza, and species Glycyrrhiza glabra. It is commonly known as licorice in Europe and the Middle East, and as ganjiang in China. The synonyms for this plant include Glycyrrhiza uralensis and Glycyrrhiza inflata, which are botanically recognized and regionally used names.

Plant Description and Morphology

Licorice is a biennial or perennial herb that typically grows to a height of 1 to 2 meters, featuring a straight, cylindrical stem that is often branched at the top.

Its leaves are alternate, ovate to lanceolate in shape, with serrated margins and a hairy surface. The flowers are small, yellowish-green, and clustered in loose, cylindrical spikes, often appearing in late spring to early summer. The plant has a deep taproot system that is fibrous and can extend several meters underground.

The roots, leaves, and stems are generally pale yellow to brown, have a distinct sweet aroma, and possess a rough, fibrous texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Licorice is native to the temperate regions of southern Europe, the Mediterranean basin, and parts of western Asia, including areas of Turkey, the Middle East, and the Caucasus.

It thrives in dry, well-drained soils and is commonly found in grasslands, rocky slopes, and open woodlands. The plant prefers a temperate climate with moderate rainfall and full sun exposure. It is adapted to regions with mild winters and warm, dry summers.

Ecologically, licorice often grows in disturbed soils and can tolerate some degree of drought.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary medicinal parts of licorice are the roots and rhizomes, which contain the highest concentration of active compounds.

The root is typically harvested in the fall after the plant has matured for at least three years. The rhizome, which is the underground stem, is also used and often harvested alongside the root. In some cases, the leaves may be used, though they contain lower concentrations of active components.

These plant parts are dried and processed into various forms such as powders, tinctures, and teas for medicinal use.

Phytochemical Composition

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) contains a variety of active compounds, including triterpene saponins, flavonoids, and alkaloids.

The most notable triterpene saponin is glycyrrhizin, which is responsible for many of the plant's pharmacological effects. Secondary metabolites such as isoflavones and phenolic acids contribute to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Chemical markers like glycyrrhetinic acid and liquiritigenin are often used to authenticate the quality and purity of licorice products.

These compounds collectively define the therapeutic potential of licorice in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) possesses a range of pharmacological properties that underpin its use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and expectorant effects, making it valuable for respiratory and digestive conditions. Biologically, licorice contains glycyrrhizin, a triterpene glycoside, which modulates immune responses and exhibits antiviral activity. Pharmacodynamically, it interacts with corticosteroid receptors, enhancing their effects and influencing mineralocorticoid activity.

System-level effects include sodium retention and potassium excretion, which can impact blood pressure and electrolyte balance.

Mechanisms of Action

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) exerts its pharmacological effects primarily through its active compounds, such as glycyrrhizin and flavonoids, which interact with various cellular and biochemical processes.

At the cellular level, glycyrrhizin inhibits the enzyme 11β-hydroxylase, reducing cortisol synthesis and modulating the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. Biochemically, licorice compounds influence inflammatory pathways by suppressing pro-inflammatory cytokines and inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) activity. These effects are mediated through interactions with target systems such as the immune system, gastrointestinal tract, and adrenal glands.

The combined action of these mechanisms underlies licorice's therapeutic properties in herbal medicine.

Therapeutic Uses

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is widely used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties that support respiratory, digestive, and immune system health.

It is commonly employed to treat respiratory conditions such as coughs, bronchitis, and asthma due to its expectorant and anti-inflammatory effects. The plant is also used to alleviate digestive issues like indigestion, nausea, and inflammation of the stomach lining. Additionally, licorice has been traditionally used to support the immune system and reduce symptoms of viral infections.

Its ability to soothe mucous membranes makes it effective for conditions involving irritation or inflammation in the throat and lungs.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) vary depending on the desired form and application, with common forms including raw root, dried root, powdered root, and extracts.

The root is typically harvested, cleaned, and dried before being used in various medicinal preparations. It can be found as whole root pieces, sliced root, or ground into a fine powder. Licorice is also commonly used to make teas, tinctures, and syrups.

In traditional herbal medicine, it is often prepared as a decoction by simmering the root in water.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of licorice root for most therapeutic uses is typically 1 to 2 grams of dried root per day, either in powder or liquid form.

Extract concentrations should not exceed 5% glycyrrhizin, as higher concentrations may increase the risk of adverse effects. It is generally recommended to take licorice root 2 to 3 times daily, with intervals of several hours between doses. Treatment duration should not exceed 8 weeks without medical supervision to avoid potential side effects such as hypertension or electrolyte imbalance.

Due to its potent active compounds, licorice should be used cautiously and in moderation.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in recommended doses, licorice is generally considered safe for short-term use, but long-term or excessive consumption can lead to side effects.

Common side effects include high blood pressure, fluid retention, and electrolyte imbalances due to its glycyrrhizin content. People with hypertension, heart disease, or kidney disorders should avoid licorice or use it under medical supervision. Allergic reactions, though rare, may include skin rashes, itching, or respiratory symptoms in individuals sensitive to the plant.

Licorice is not recommended during pregnancy due to potential risks to fetal development and possible hormonal effects.

Drug Interactions

Licorice can interact with certain pharmaceuticals and supplements, potentially altering their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.

It may interfere with the metabolism of medications processed by the liver, such as corticosteroids, antihypertensives, and diuretics. These interactions can lead to increased drug levels in the body, raising the risk of toxicity. Licorice may also interact with supplements like potassium, magnesium, and certain herbal products, affecting their absorption or excretion.

These interactions are particularly important for individuals taking long-term medication or herbal regimens.

Toxicity and Precautions

The toxic dose of licorice can vary depending on the individual, but excessive consumption, typically more than 2 grams per day for several weeks, may lead to adverse effects.

Long-term use of licorice root can result in pseudoaldosteronism, characterized by high blood pressure, low potassium levels, and fluid retention. Chronic ingestion may also cause liver damage, especially in individuals with pre-existing liver conditions. Poisoning risks are primarily associated with the compound glycyrrhizin, which can trigger symptoms like headache, dizziness, and nausea in high doses.

It is crucial to consult a healthcare professional before using licorice, particularly for prolonged periods or in combination with other medications.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained, loamy soils that are rich in organic matter.

It thrives in full sunlight, requiring at least 6 to 8 hours of direct sun daily for optimal growth. The plant has moderate water needs, preferring consistent moisture without waterlogging, which can lead to root rot. Propagation is commonly done through seed sowing, though rhizome cuttings are also used for faster establishment. Licorice reaches maturity in 2 to 3 years, with the best harvest time occurring in the fall when the roots are fully developed.

Harvesting involves carefully digging up the entire plant, ensuring minimal damage to the roots, which are the primary medicinal part.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and Extraction involves several key steps to prepare licorice for medicinal use.

The first step is drying, where harvested roots are carefully spread out and allowed to dry in a controlled environment to reduce moisture content and prevent spoilage. Once dried, the roots are then processed through extraction methods, typically using water or alcohol to isolate active compounds like glycyrrhizin. Distillation may be employed to concentrate certain volatile components, although it is less common in licorice processing.

Finally, the dried and extracted material is ground into a fine powder to ensure uniformity and ease of use in formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Licorice root should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain its potency and prevent degradation.

The shelf life of licorice root is typically 2 to 3 years when stored properly. It is light sensitive and should be kept in an airtight container to avoid exposure to UV rays. Moisture control is essential to prevent mold growth and maintain quality; high humidity can shorten its shelf life.

Ideal storage conditions include temperatures below 25°C and low humidity levels.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has demonstrated that licorice root contains bioactive compounds such as glycyrrhizin, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and immunomodulatory effects, supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for centuries, primarily for its expectorant, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties, with historical applications spanning ancient Chinese, Egyptian, and Greek medicinal practices.

Regulatory Status

In the United States, licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is classified as a dietary supplement and is regulated under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994, which means it is not subject to pre-market approval by the FDA but is monitored for safety and labeling compliance.
