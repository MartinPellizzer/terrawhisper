Glycyrrhiza glabra

What's the taxonomical classification of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

Glycyrrhiza glabra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Fabales. This plant is a member of the family Fabaceae and falls under the genus Glycyrrhiza, with its specific designation being the species glabra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Glycyrrhiza glabra has a perennial growth habit characterized by a thick, woody rhizome that spreads horizontally underground.

This plant produces erect, branched stems that can reach heights of up to one meter and are covered in fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are pinnately compound, typically consisting of seven to seventeen small, oval-shaped leaflets arranged along a central axis. Small, papilionaceous flowers appear in clusters, displaying colors that range from pale violet to bluish-white.

The resulting legume pods are elongated and contain numerous small, brownish seeds.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Glycyrrhiza glabra contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its dried roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are harvested and processed to extract glycyrrhizin, which provides a sweet flavor and significant therapeutic properties. The roots are frequently utilized in herbal formulations to soothe gastrointestinal inflammation and alleviate respiratory congestion. Beyond the roots, some traditional practices incorporate the plant's seeds or leaves, though the root remains the most potent medicinal component. This plant is widely valued in pharmacopeias for its ability to support immune function and reduce systemic inflammation.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive constituents including saponins like glycyrrhizin and glycyrrhetinic acid, as well as various flavonoids such as liquiritin, liquiritigenin, and isoliquiritigenin.

These chemical components are categorized into broader classes including flavonoids, chalcones, and flavanols. Within the chalcone group, licochalcone A serves as a significant medicinal compound. The synergy between these saponins and polyphenolic structures provides the plant with its characteristic therapeutic properties.

Together, these complex molecules contribute to the extensive pharmacological profile associated with the species.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Glycyrrhiza glabra targets various proteins including 11 beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1, cyclooxygenase enzymes, and various inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. Its primary bioactive component, glycyrrhizin, inhibits the enzyme 11 beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1, which regulates the conversion of cortisone to active cortisol. Additionally, flavonoids like liquiritin interact with cyclooxygenase-2 and lipoxygenase to suppress the production of pro-inflammatory mediators. The plant also modulates tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6, contributing to its significant anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory properties. These multi-target interactions explain the wide-ranging therapeutic applications of the plant in treating skin conditions, digestive issues, and systemic inflammation.

What are the therapeutic actions of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

Glycyrrhiza glabra has diverse therapeutic actions including anti-inflammatory, demulcent, expectorant, anti-ulcer, cholagogue, diuretic, anti-viral, adaptogen, and adrenal tonic properties.

Its mucilaginous nature allows it to act as a soothing demulcent for irritated mucous membranes. As an expectorant, it helps clear respiratory pathways, while its anti-inflammatory and anti-ulcer effects support digestive and systemic health. The plant serves as a potent adaptogen and adrenal tonic to assist the body in managing stress.

Additionally, its cholagogue and diuretic functions support liver and kidney processes, complemented by its anti-viral capabilities.

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat gastrointestinal issues such as stomach ulcers and acid reflux, respiratory conditions like coughs and bronchitis, and inflammatory skin irritations.

Its primary active component, glycyrrhizin, possesses significant anti-inflammatory and antiviral properties that soothe mucous membranes. Practitioners often prescribe it to calm digestive tract spasms and reduce intestinal inflammation. In respiratory care, it acts as an expectorant to help loosen phlegm and ease throat irritation.

Additionally, topical applications can help manage eczema and other minor dermatological ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Glycyrrhiza glabra?

Glycyrrhiza glabra is prepared as decoctions, teas, tinctures, syrups, lozenges, extracts, and powders using its dried roots or rhizomes. The primary method involves boiling the tough, woody roots in water to create a sweet-tasting decoction rich in glycyrrhizin. Many manufacturers process the plant into standardized extracts to ensure a consistent concentration of active compounds for therapeutic use. In traditional applications, the root is often simmered into a thick syrup to soothe throat irritation and coughs. Additionally, the dried root can be ground into a fine powder for inclusion in capsules or mixed into topical salves for skin application.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Glycyrrhiza glabra can cause serious health issues such as high blood pressure, potassium depletion, edema, and congestive heart failure if consumed in large amounts over time. This occurs because the compound glycyrrhizin can trigger the body to retain sodium and water while losing essential electrolytes. Prolonged intake often leads to hypokalemia, which may result in muscle weakness or irregular heart rhythms. Some users might also experience bruising, swelling in the legs, or significant shifts in hormone levels. It is critical for individuals with kidney or heart conditions to avoid excessive doses of this plant.
