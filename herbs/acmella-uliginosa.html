Acmella uliginosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Acmella uliginosa?

Acmella uliginosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Acmella and the species uliginosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Acmella uliginosa has a perennial, herbaceous habit characterized by erect stems and pinnately compound leaves with distinctively emarginate or notched tips. The foliage is typically arranged oppositely along the stem and possesses a somewhat succulent texture. Small, yellowish flower heads are clustered in terminal corymbose inflorescences, each composed of numerous tiny florets. These heads are supported by specialized bracts that protect the developing reproductive structures. The plant's overall structure allows it to thrive in moist, tropical environments through its spreading growth pattern.

What are the pharmacological activities of Acmella uliginosa?

This plant has analgesic, antibacterial, antifungal, antioxidant, and antitumor activities that contribute to its significant therapeutic potential. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative stress and damage. The plant demonstrates strong antibacterial and antifungal capabilities by inhibiting the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, its analgesic effects provide relief from pain through natural bioactive compounds. Research also indicates that its antitumor activity may assist in suppressing the progression of certain cancer cells.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic extract of Acmella uliginosa flowers produced a significant antinociceptive response in chemical and thermal nociception models. The extract was administered orally at doses of 3, 10, 30, and 100 mg/kg. These results indicate that the plant has both centrally and peripherally mediated activities, with the central activity likely involving the opioid system as suggested by reversal via naloxone. No mortality or signs of toxicity were recorded following the treatment. Additionally, mice treated with the extract did not show any significant motor performance alterations.
