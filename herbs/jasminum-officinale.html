Jasminum officinale

What's the taxonomical classification of Jasminum officinale?

Jasminum officinale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It further falls under the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Oleaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Jasminum, with its specific designation being the species officinale.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Jasminum officinale has woody, climbing stems that produce opposite, pinnate leaves consisting of five to nine elliptical leaflets. The foliage is typically dark green and possesses a smooth, somewhat leathery texture. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge in clusters from the leaf axils during the flowering season. Each blossom features five to seven spreading lobes that create a star-like shape. These plants often develop sprawling vines that can reach significant lengths as they scramble over supporting structures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the temperate regions of southern Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in diverse climates ranging from Mediterranean environments to more continental zones. Due to its fragrant flowers, it has been widely introduced and naturalized in many other parts of the world, including parts of Asia and North America. It often colonizes disturbed areas and woodland edges where conditions are favorable. This extensive spread is largely attributed to human cultivation for its ornamental and medicinal properties.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, fertile soil and a location that receives full sun to partial shade to thrive.

It is a vigorous woody climber that needs a sturdy trellis or support structure to manage its spreading vines. Regular watering is essential to keep the soil consistently moist, though it should never sit in standing water. During the growing season, providing organic mulch helps retain moisture and suppress competing weeds.

Periodic pruning after the flowering period encourages new growth and maintains a manageable shape for the garden.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Jasminum officinale contains medicinal compounds primarily extracted from its fragrant white flowers and its green leaves. The flowers are frequently harvested to create essential oils and infusions used to soothe skin irritations and reduce anxiety. Leaves are also utilized in traditional practices to help alleviate digestive issues and reduce inflammation. These plant parts work together to provide various sedative and antiseptic properties in herbal preparations. This versatility makes multiple components of the plant valuable for both aromatherapy and topical treatments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Jasminum officinale?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and antiviral pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to alleviate pain and reduce swelling in various biological tissues. The presence of bioactive compounds also allows it to inhibit the growth of diverse pathogenic microorganisms. Additionally, it provides significant protection against oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals. These combined properties even extend to fighting viral infections, making it a versatile subject for medicinal research.

According to a study published by "Neurological sciences : official journal of the Italian Neurological Society and of the Italian Society of Clinical Neurophysiology", the medicinal herb jasmine has an analgesic effect. This effect has been proved by current medicine for the treatment of sexual headache. The efficacy of jasmine is discussed in the context of the "Canon of Medicine" authored by Avicenna during the Islamic Golden Age between the ninth and twelfth century AD. Such historical knowledge of medicinal herbs used by medieval physicians should be investigated further to extend new remedial options.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the monoterpene alcohol linalool along with several potent flavonoids such as kaempferol, quercetin, and rutin. These bioactive compounds contribute to the diverse therapeutic profile of Jasminum officinale, including its sedative and anti-inflammatory properties. Linalool is primarily responsible for the characteristic aroma and provides calming effects on the central nervous system. The presence of quercetin and kaempferol offers significant antioxidant protection by neutralizing harmful free radicals in the body. Additionally, the flavonoid rutin helps to strengthen capillary walls and improve overall vascular health.

According to a study published by "Endocrine, metabolic & immune disorders drug targets", Jasminum officinale contains the monoterpene alcohol linalool. This compound possesses antiviral, anti-inflammatory, and antibacterial properties. In A549 cell lines, linalool significantly reduces percent viability with IC30 values of 474.94 ± 43.12, 379.33 ± 49.5, and 183.77 ± 66.7 μM for 24, 48, and 72 h, respectively. The substance showed its highest interaction with BRAF with a binding energy of -5.6 kcal/mol. These results suggest that linalool possesses chemopreventive potential against lung cancer.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, reducing oxidative stress, fighting human infections, combating infections caused by antibiotic-resistant bacteria, and increasing alertness. Its chemical compounds act as potent antioxidants that protect cells from damage caused by free radicals. The plant also demonstrates significant antimicrobial properties that can target various pathogens, including those that have developed resistance to standard medications. Additionally, its bioactive constituents help modulate the body's inflammatory response to promote healing. Some studies also suggest that its aromatic properties can enhance cognitive function and mental alertness.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Jasminum officinale possesses an anti-inflammatory effect. This effect was observed against both acute inflammation, specifically xylene-induced ear oedema, and chronic inflammation, specifically the cotton pellet granuloma test. The anti-inflammatory effects were found to be dose dependent. These data affirm the traditional use of this plant for inflammatory conditions.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion, a tincture, an essential oil, an absolute, or a tea for various medicinal and aromatic purposes. Making a tea or a simple infusion involves steeping the dried flowers in hot water to extract soothing properties. For a more concentrated dose of its therapeutic compounds, herbalists often create a tincture using alcohol as a solvent. The fragrant essence of the blossoms is frequently captured through distillation to produce essential oil. Additionally, the flowers are processed to create an absolute, which provides a highly potent and concentrated aromatic extract.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause an allergic skin reaction, nausea, dizziness, digestive upset, vomiting, and headache in some individuals. Contact with the skin may trigger redness or irritation for those with sensitivities. Ingesting certain parts of the plant can lead to significant digestive upset or vomiting. Some people might also experience systemic symptoms like dizziness or a persistent headache. It is important to be cautious when handling or consuming any part of this species.
