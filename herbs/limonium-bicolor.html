Limonium bicolor

What's the taxonomical classification of Limonium bicolor?

Limonium bicolor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Caryophyllales and is a member of the family Plumbaginaceae. Ultimately, its specific classification is defined by the genus Limonium and the species bicolor.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Limonium bicolor has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, woody-based stems that produce terminal, branched inflorescences of small, two-toned flowers. The leaves are typically basal, forming a rosette of narrow, lanceolate, or linear shapes that are often grayish-green in color. These leaves possess a somewhat fleshy or leathery texture, which helps the plant retain moisture in its natural habitat. The flowering structures consist of many tiny, delicate blossoms clustered tightly atop thin, branching stalks. Each flower displays a distinct color contrast between its petals and calyx, giving the species its specific name. The overall architecture of the plant is airy and delicate, making it highly valued in floral arrangements.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the coastal regions of the Mediterranean Basin, specifically appearing along the shores of the Aegean and Adriatic Seas. It typically inhabits saline environments such as salt marshes and rocky seaside cliffs where it can tolerate high salt concentrations. Its distribution is largely restricted to southern European countries and parts of North Africa. The species thrives in these specific maritime climates due to its unique adaptation to coastal soil compositions. This localized range makes it a characteristic element of Mediterranean littoral ecosystems.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds alpha-amyrin, beta-amyrin, beta-sitosterol, gallic acid, and luteolin. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the plant's diverse biological activities and therapeutic potential. Triterpenoids like alpha-amyrin and beta-amyrin are known for their anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of beta-sitosterol further supports its role in regulating cholesterol and reducing swelling. Additionally, the antioxidant effects of gallic acid and the flavone luteolin provide protective benefits against oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the hypogeaal part of Limonium bicolor was analyzed to isolate and identify its chemical constituents. Four specific compounds were identified, which are beta-sitosterol (1), gallic acid (2), luteolin (3), and quercetin (4). Additionally, some white grease was obtained where nine constituents were identified by GC-MS. These nine constituents included 2,4-dimethyl-heptene (5), ethylbenzene (6), O-xylene (7), styrene (8), hexadecanoic acid etylester (9), 9,12-octodecadienoic acid etylester (10), diisooctyl phthalate (11), alpha-amyrin (12), and beta-amyrin (13). Compound 1 was specifically isolated from Limonium bicolor.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for mitigating Na+ damage caused by salinity stress in various agricultural crops. The physiological mechanisms of Limonium bicolor help plants maintain ionic balance when they encounter high salt concentrations in the soil. By managing these levels, the plant helps reduce the negative effects associated with salt damage during growth cycles. These properties make it a valuable subject in studies focused on increasing crop resilience in coastal or arid environments. Research continues to explore how its unique biological processes can combat the widespread issue of soil salinization.
