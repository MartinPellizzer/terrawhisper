Limonium bicolor

What's the taxonomical classification of Limonium bicolor?

Limonium bicolor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Caryophyllales and is a member of the family Plumbaginaceae. Ultimately, its specific classification is defined by the genus Limonium and the species bicolor.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Limonium bicolor has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, woody-based stems that produce terminal, branched inflorescences of small, two-toned flowers. The leaves are typically basal, forming a rosette of narrow, lanceolate, or linear shapes that are often grayish-green in color. These leaves possess a somewhat fleshy or leathery texture, which helps the plant retain moisture in its natural habitat. The flowering structures consist of many tiny, delicate blossoms clustered tightly atop thin, branching stalks. Each flower displays a distinct color contrast between its petals and calyx, giving the species its specific name. The overall architecture of the plant is airy and delicate, making it highly valued in floral arrangements.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the coastal regions of the Mediterranean Basin, specifically appearing along the shores of the Aegean and Adriatic Seas. It typically inhabits saline environments such as salt marshes and rocky seaside cliffs where it can tolerate high salt concentrations. Its distribution is largely restricted to southern European countries and parts of North Africa. The species thrives in these specific maritime climates due to its unique adaptation to coastal soil compositions. This localized range makes it a characteristic element of Mediterranean littoral ecosystems.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds alpha-amyrin, beta-amyrin, beta-sitosterol, gallic acid, and luteolin. These specific chemical constituents are known for providing various therapeutic benefits to human health. The triterpenoids alpha-amyrin and beta-amyrin are often associated with anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the presence of beta-sitosterol and luteolin contributes to the plant's antioxidant potential. Gallic acid further enhances the medicinal profile by offering significant bioactive activity.

According to a study published by "Zhong yao cai = Zhongyaocai = Journal of Chinese medicinal materials", four compounds were isolated and identified from the hypogeal part of Limonium bicolor as beta-sitosterol (1), gallic acid (2), luteolin (3), and quercetin (4). Additionally, some white grease was obtained from the plant. Nine constituents were identified by GC-MS elementarily from this grease, which include 2,4-dimethyl-heptene (5), ethylbenzene (6), O-xylene (7), styrene (8), hexadecanoic acid etylester (9), 9,12-octodecadienoic acid etylester (10), diisooctyl phthalate (11), alpha-amyrin (12), and beta-amyrin (13). Compound 1 is specifically isolated from Limonium bicolor.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for studying mechanisms of salinity tolerance to mitigate the negative effects of salinity stress on agricultural crops. Researchers examine how Limonium bicolor manages Na+ damage within its cellular structures to maintain metabolic functions. By investigating this species, scientists can better understand the biological pathways that allow certain organisms to survive high levels of salt damage. These studies aim to identify specific genes or traits that contribute to overall salinity tolerance in various environments. Such knowledge is essential for developing resilient plant varieties capable of growing in increasingly saline soils.

According to a study published by "Plant cell reports", Limonium bicolor is used to address salt damage and improve salt tolerance. The study investigated treating seedlings with 300 mM NaCl and different concentrations of 6-BA, specifically 0.5, 1.0, and 1.5 mg/L. Results showed that exogenous applications of 1.0 mg/L 6-BA significantly improved plant growth and photosynthesis. This treatment increased cytokinin and auxin contents, as well as K+ and organic soluble matter contents. Additionally, 1.0 mg/L 6-BA increased the activities of SOD, CAT, APX, and POD while decreasing Na+, H2O2, and O2- contents. These mechanisms help reduce the Na+ concentration in leaves to alleviate Na+ damage to the species.
