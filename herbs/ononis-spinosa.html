Scientific and Botanical Profile

Ononis Spinosa, with botanical name Ononis spinosa, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Fabaceae, commonly known as the legume or pea family.

It is also referred to by various common names such as Common Ononis, Spiny Restharrow, Spiny Honeysuckle, and Yellow Toadflax, among others, reflecting its widespread distribution and morphological characteristics. Native to regions across Europe, North Africa, and parts of western Asia, this species thrives in rocky, dry, and disturbed soils, often found in alpine and arid environments. Morphologically, Ononis Spinosa is a low-growing perennial herb with a creeping or ascending stem, covered in stiff, bristly hairs, and small, alternate, lanceolate leaves that are often glandular.

Its flowers are clustered in dense, spike-like inflorescences, typically yellow or greenish-yellow, and are followed by small, spurred pods that contain several seeds, contributing to its adaptability and ecological resilience.

Ononis spinosa has been traditionally used for its anti-inflammatory, astringent, and diuretic properties, making it beneficial for the urinary and respiratory systems.

Compared to similar herbs like Urtica dioica (nettle), Ononis spinosa offers a more targeted effect on the urinary tract, while being less likely to cause gastrointestinal irritation. Its potency is often noted as moderate compared to stronger herbs like Echinacea, but it provides a gentler, sustained action over time.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Ononis spinosa has a variety of forms available, including fresh plant, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule, each offering different methods of preparation and application.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping dried leaves or roots in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period to extract more active compounds. Topical applications include making an infusion for poultices or using the essential oil diluted in a carrier oil for massage or skin treatments. For adults, a recommended dosage of 1-2 grams of dried herb in tea form, taken up to three times daily, is often suggested, while children should only use it under medical supervision.

The duration of use should be conservative, typically not exceeding two weeks unless advised by a healthcare professional.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Ononis spinosa can be used with caution due to its potential safety concerns and side effects.

While it has been traditionally used for its purported health benefits, it may cause gastrointestinal upset, such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, in some individuals. There is limited scientific evidence on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with medications that affect the liver or kidneys. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid its use due to insufficient data on safety and potential risks to the fetus or infant. Individuals with chronic illnesses, particularly those affecting the digestive system or liver, should consult a healthcare provider before using Ononis spinosa.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to start with a low dose, monitor for adverse reactions, and seek professional guidance before incorporating it into a treatment regimen.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Ononis spinosa grows in well-drained, sandy or loamy soils with full sun to partial shade, preferring a slightly acidic to neutral pH.

It thrives in temperate climates and requires moderate watering, especially during its establishment phase, though it is somewhat drought-tolerant once established. Regular weeding and occasional mulching help control weeds and retain soil moisture, while pruning dead or diseased branches promotes healthy growth. The plant is typically harvested in late summer to early autumn when the leaves and roots are most potent, using a sharp spade or trowel to carefully dig up the entire root system without damaging the roots.

After harvesting, the roots should be washed, dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area, and stored in airtight glass jars or sealed containers in a cool, dark place to preserve their medicinal potency.
