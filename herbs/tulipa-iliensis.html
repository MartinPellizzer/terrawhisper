Tulipa iliensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Tulipa iliensis?

Tulipa iliensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Liliales, it is a member of the family Liliaceae, which further narrows down to its specific genus, Tulipa. Finally, the plant is identified at the species level as iliensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tulipa iliensis has a bulbous root system, lanceolate leaves that are typically glaucous in color, and upright stems that support solitary, cup-shaped flowers. The foliage is characterized by a distinctively elongated and pointed shape which helps distinguish it from other species in the genus. Each flower displays a range of hues, often featuring vibrant petals that taper to a gentle point at the apex. The tepals are arranged in a symmetrical pattern to protect the reproductive organs located at the center of the bloom. These morphological traits are essential adaptations for its specific growth cycle and environmental niche.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the Ili River valley region located within the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region of northwest China. It grows primarily in the mountainous areas surrounding the Ili Valley, specifically within the Tianshan mountain range. The species is typically found in alpine meadows and rocky slopes at high elevations. These specific habitats provide the unique environmental conditions required for its survival. How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-draining, sandy loam soil and a period of deep winter dormancy to trigger successful spring flowering.

Cultivators should plant the bulbs at a depth of approximately six inches to protect them from temperature fluctuations. It thrives best in full sun exposure where the soil remains moist but never waterlogged. Regular mulching helps maintain consistent moisture levels and regulates the root zone temperature.

Once the foliage yellows in late spring, the leaves should be removed to prevent fungal issues.
