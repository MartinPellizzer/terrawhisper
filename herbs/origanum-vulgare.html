Oregano

Origanum vulgare

Oregano (Origanum vulgare) is a member of the Lamiaceae family, commonly used in Mediterranean cuisine and traditional medicine. Its leaves and flowers have been traditionally employed for digestive aid, antimicrobial properties, and as a treatment for respiratory infections. The herb is widely cultivated in regions such as southern Europe and the Middle East.

Botanical Identification

Oregano, scientifically known as Origanum vulgare, belongs to the Lamiaceae family. It is also called wild marjoram in some regions. A perennial herb native to southern Europe, it has square stems, aromatic leaves, and pink or purple flowers. Common synonyms include oregano and origano.

Oregano Identification.

Active Compounds

Oregano contains essential oils, flavonoids, and phenolic acids as its primary bioactive compounds. These compounds work synergistically to provide its medicinal properties, with the essential oils contributing to its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects. Flavonoids and phenolic acids support antioxidant activity, enhancing overall therapeutic potential.

  • Essential oil
  • Flavonoid
  • Phenolic acid

Preparation Methods

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and respiratory infections.

Infusion: Used for colds, flu, and digestive issues.

Essential oil: Used for respiratory infections and muscle pain.

Safety Profile

Oregano is generally safe when used in typical culinary amounts. However, high doses may cause gastrointestinal upset. It should be avoided during pregnancy and by individuals with gallbladder issues due to its potential to stimulate bile production.
