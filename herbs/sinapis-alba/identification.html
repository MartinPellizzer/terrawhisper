Botanical Identification of White Mustard (Sinapis alba)

To identify White Mustard (Sinapis alba) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

White Mustard (Sinapis alba) belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of seed plants, class Caryophyllopsida, order Brassicales, family Brassicaceae, genus Sinapis, and species Sinapis alba. This classification places it within the mustard family, known for its economically important members.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of White Mustard is Sinapis alba, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It is also known by several synonyms, including Brassica alba and Sinapis arvensis. Common names include White Mustard, Yellow Mustard, and Field Mustard, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

White Mustard (Sinapis alba) is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has a branched, hairy stem that is erect and can grow up to 60 cm tall. The leaves are alternate, lobed, and have a distinctive mustard scent. The inflorescence is a raceme of yellow flowers, which bloom in early summer. The fruit is a cylindrical pod containing several small, oval seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

White Mustard (Sinapis alba) can be distinguished by its smooth, hairless stems and lobed leaves with a serrated edge. Its flowers are small, yellow, and arranged in clusters, while its pods are slender and cylindrical, containing several seeds. It is often confused with Brassica species but lacks the hairy texture and has a more delicate appearance.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

White Mustard (Sinapis alba) is native to Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained loamy soils and is commonly found at low to mid-altitude regions.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

White Mustard blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the plant is most easily recognized during its flowering stage, which is key for accurate botanical identification. Harvest timing for seeds is usually in late summer to early fall.

Phylogenetic Relationships

White Mustard (Sinapis alba) belongs to the Brassicaceae family, closely related to species like Sinapis arvensis and Brassica napus. It shares key genus traits such as cruciform flowers and siliquous fruits. Its phylogenetic position reflects its evolutionary ties to other mustard family plants, aiding in accurate identification and understanding of its botanical characteristics.
