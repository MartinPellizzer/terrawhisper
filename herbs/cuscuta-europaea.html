Dodder

Cuscuta europaea

Dodder (Cuscuta europaea) is a parasitic plant that has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its potential to improve conditions such as infertility and urinary tract infections.

Taxonomy

Dodder (Cuscuta europaea) is a parasitic flowering plant belonging to the family Convolvulaceae, within the order Solanales, class Caryophyllopsida, division Spermatophyta, and kingdom Plantae.

Morphology

Dodder (Cuscuta europaea) is a parasitic flowering plant characterized by its yellow-orange, thread-like stems that lack leaves, chlorophyll, and root systems, relying entirely on its host plant for nutrients and water.

Distribution

Dodder (Cuscuta europaea) is native to Europe, North Africa, and parts of western Asia, with a wide distribution across temperate regions.

Traditional Uses

Dodder has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat ailments such as coughs, asthma, and skin conditions due to its purported ability to reduce inflammation and support respiratory and skin health.

Medicinal Systems

In traditional medicinal systems such as Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, and Native American herbalism, Dodder (Cuscuta europaea) has been used for its purported ability to support vitality, promote longevity, and aid in the treatment of various ailments.

Phytochemistry

The phytochemistry of Dodder (Cuscuta europaea) is characterized by the presence of alkaloids, flavonoids, saponins, and various phenolic compounds, which contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine for their purported tonic, antiseptic, and anti-inflammatory properties.

Pharmacology

Dodder (Cuscuta europaea) is known for its traditional use in herbal medicine to support kidney function, enhance vitality, and promote reproductive health through its purported ability to tonify the body's essence and strengthen yang energy.

Botanical Identification

Dodder, scientifically known as Cuscuta europaea, is an annual parasitic plant belonging to the Convolvulaceae family. Commonly called yellow dodder in Europe, it lacks roots, leaves, and true stems, instead forming a yellow, thread-like vine that attaches to host plants. Its flowers are small, white or yellow, and it has been referenced under various names in historical herbal texts.
