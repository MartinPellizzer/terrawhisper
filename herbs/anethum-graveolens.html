Anethum graveolens

What's the taxonomical classification of Anethum graveolens?

Anethum graveolens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Apiales. It is situated within the family Apiaceae, which is characterized by its distinctive flowering structures. Specifically, the plant is categorized under the genus Anethum, with its unique identity defined by the species epithet graveolens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anethum graveolens has feathery, finely divided leaves that are bright green and bipinnate or tripinnate in structure. These slender, thread-like segments create a delicate, lace-like texture throughout the foliage. The plant produces small, yellowish-white flowers arranged in compound umbels atop long, hollow stems. The root system typically consists of a stout taproot that anchors the plant firmly in the soil. As the plant matures, it develops a bushy growth habit characterized by its airy and light appearance.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as chervil, thrives when sown in well-drained, fertile soil with a pH between 6 and 7 and requires consistent moisture throughout its growth cycle.

It prefers cool climates and should be planted in partial shade to prevent the delicate foliage from wilting under intense sunlight. Regular watering is essential to maintain even soil dampness, as drying out can cause the plant to go to seed prematurely. Once established, the herb can be harvested by clipping the tender stems for culinary use.

Providing organic mulch helps retain necessary moisture and keeps the root zone cool.

What are the pharmacological activities of Anethum graveolens?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties. These therapeutic effects are primarily attributed to the presence of bioactive secondary metabolites found within its essential oils and extracts. Research indicates that its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress in biological systems. Furthermore, the plant exerts potent antimicrobial and antibacterial actions against various pathogenic microorganisms. Its ability to inhibit inflammatory mediators and alleviate pain further supports its potential use in traditional and modern medicine.

According to a study published by "American journal of therapeuics", the aqueous extract of Anethum graveolenst L. from the Rissani-Errachiadia region of South Morocco demonstrates significant antidepressant and analgesic effects. When administered orally to Wistar rats, the extract shows these properties without any adverse effects compared to sertraline and tramadol. The dose of 250 mg/kg of body weight produces better antidepressant and analgesic results than a dose of 1 g/kg of body weight. Phytochemical screening of the aqueous extract highlights the presence of polyphenols, flavonoids, and tannins.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apiol, anethole, carvone, limonene, and myristicin. These volatile oils are responsible for the distinct aroma and therapeutic properties of the seeds. Apiol and anethole are frequently studied for their potential sedative and estrogenic effects. Carvone and limonene contribute to the plant's antimicrobial and digestive benefits. Additionally, myristicin is present in small amounts and plays a role in the plant's complex chemical profile.

According to a study published by "Scientific reports", the essential oil of Anethum graveolens contains major compounds such as alpha,2-dimethylstyrene, cuminyl alcohol, p-cymene, and linalool. Biochar treatments, specifically BNCs, increase the levels of monoterpenes including limonene, carvone, apiol, and dillapiol. The essential oil content in the seeds increases under salinity levels of 6 and 12 dS m-1. The research investigated the effects of biochar at 30 g kg-1 soil and BNCs of iron at 30 g BNC-FeO kg-1 soil and zinc at 30 g BNC-ZnO kg-1 soil. The combined form of 15 + 15 g of these nanocomposites was the best treatment to improve the content of basic commercial monoterpenes.
