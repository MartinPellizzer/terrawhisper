What to know about Asclepias syriaca (milkweed) before using it medicinally

Asclepias syriaca, commonly known as milkweed, is a herb that has been prized for its medicinal properties, which are believed to reduce inflammation and improve cardiovascular health by inhibiting platelet aggregation and lowering cholesterol levels.

Cultivated for its striking purple flowers and adaptable growth, this plant is often grown in gardens and used in floral arrangements. Botanically, Asclepias syriaca belongs to the Asclepiadaceae family and is characterized by its milky latex and distinctive follicles, which are used in traditional medicine to treat a range of ailments.

The herb has been referenced in traditional medicine since ancient times, with mentions in the writings of Greek physician Hippocrates and the Roman naturalist Pliny the Elder.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Asclepias syriaca.

What are the medicinal properties of Asclepias syriaca?

Asclepias syriaca helps with various health issues, including eczema, itching, and skin irritations. Topical applications have been used to soothe and calm irritated skin. It has also been used to treat minor wounds and burns.

The active constituents responsible for its medicinal properties include alkaloids, glycosides, and saponins. These compounds have been shown to possess anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and antiviral properties. The alkaloids, in particular, are thought to contribute to its wound-healing and skin-soothing effects.

The parts of the plant most commonly used for medicinal purposes are the leaves and roots. The leaves contain a higher concentration of glycosides and saponins, making them more effective for skin conditions and minor wounds. The roots, on the other hand, contain a higher concentration of alkaloids, which are useful for treating more serious conditions.

Improper use of Asclepias syriaca can lead to side effects such as skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive issues. Ingestion of large quantities can cause nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Skin contact with the sap or roots can cause skin irritation, redness, and itching.

When using Asclepias syriaca medicinally, it is essential to exercise caution. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid using the plant, as it may stimulate the uterus and cause contractions. Additionally, individuals with allergies or sensitivities should perform patch tests before using the plant topically.

What are the horticulural aspects of Asclepias syriaca?

Asclepias syriaca grow best in full sun to partial shade with a medium to dry soil moisture level. The optimal growth temperature is between 65°F to 95°F (18°C to 35°C). It thrives in well-draining soil with a pH between 6.0 to 7.0.

For optimal growth, plant Asclepias syriaca in a location with good air circulation, avoiding areas with standing water. Plant seedlings 18 to 24 inches apart in late spring or early summer. Water thoroughly after planting and maintain a 1-inch layer of mulch to retain moisture.

To harvest Asclepias syriaca, cut the flowering stalks when the seed pods are fully developed and dry. Harvest individual stems at 1- to 2-inch intervals to prevent the plant from becoming seedless. Leave some flowers intact to allow for self-seeding.

Common pests affecting Asclepias syriaca include aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites, which can be controlled with insecticidal soap or neem oil. Root rot and crown rot are common diseases, typically caused by overwatering, which can be prevented by proper drainage and water management.

What are the botanical characteristics of Asclepias syriaca?

Asclepias syriaca, also known as Common Milkweed, is a perennial plant species that exhibits several distinctive botanical characteristics.

It has a sprawling habit, often growing up to 3 feet tall and wide, with a taproot system that allows it to thrive in dry to moist soils. The leaves are ovate-shaped, ranging from 2-8 inches long, with wavy margins and a soft, fuzzy texture.

The plant's stems and leaves exude a milky sap when damaged, which is characteristic of the Asclepiadaceae family. The inflorescences consist of clusters of small, tubular flowers that are typically pinkish-purple in color, but can range from white to reddish-brown depending on the subspecies. Each flower is about 1-2 inches long and contains five fused petals, with a distinctive "hinge" or "folded" structure that allows for pollinator access.

The seeds of Asclepias syriaca are also notable, as they contain toxic compounds called cardenolides that make them unpalatable to many herbivores.
