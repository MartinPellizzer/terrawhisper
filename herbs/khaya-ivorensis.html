Khaya ivorensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Khaya ivorensis?

Khaya ivorensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Sapindales and falls within the family Meliaceae. Ultimately, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement in the genus Khaya, with its unique designation being the species ivorensis.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Khaya ivorensis contains medicinal compounds primarily found in its bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is frequently harvested to prepare decoctions used for treating various ailments such as stomach aches and digestive issues. Extracts from the leaves are often utilized in traditional medicine to address symptoms of malaria and skin infections. Some regional practices involve using the roots to create infusions aimed at reducing fever or inflammation. These different components provide a diverse range of therapeutic applications within local healing traditions.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

The bark and leaves are particularly valued for their potential antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Khaya ivorensis targets various enzymatic pathways and signaling proteins, most notably through the inhibition of cyclooxygenase enzymes and the modulation of inflammatory cytokine production. The bioactive limonoids present in the bark and leaves interact with the arachidonic acid cascade to reduce prostaglandin synthesis. These phytochemicals also exhibit potential activity against specific protein kinases involved in cellular proliferation and inflammatory signaling. Additionally, certain secondary metabolites may influence the activity of acetylcholinesterase, suggesting a neuroprotective mechanism. Through these diverse molecular interactions, the plant exerts its observed anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial therapeutic effects.

What are the therapeutic actions of Khaya ivorensis?

Khaya ivorensis has antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties derived primarily from its bark and leaf extracts. The plant contains bioactive alkaloids and terpenoids that help inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. These chemical compounds allow it to function as a natural remedy for treating skin infections and promoting wound healing. Additionally, the extracts are used in traditional medicine to alleviate pain and reduce systemic inflammation. Researchers continue to study these specific phytochemicals to better understand their potential in modern pharmacology.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, stomach pains, and respiratory issues. The bark and leaves contain bioactive compounds that exhibit antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Local communities often utilize extracts from the plant to manage symptoms of digestive distress and localized inflammation. What are the herbal preparations of Khaya ivorensis?

Khaya ivorensis is prepared as decoctions from the bark, infusions from the leaves, and poultices made from crushed roots or stem pieces. These various liquid extractions are frequently used to treat internal ailments such as fever and stomach issues. The solid plant parts are also applied topically to the skin to manage wounds and infections. Local healers often combine these different forms depending on the specific medicinal requirement of the patient. This versatility in preparation allows the tree to serve as a multi-purpose resource in traditional medicine.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Khaya ivorensis can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. The sap and bark contain bioactive compounds that may trigger redness or itching upon direct contact with the skin. If the plant parts are consumed, individuals might experience nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. While many studies focus on its medicinal properties, the potential for toxicity remains a concern for unregulated use. Careful handling is necessary to avoid unexpected physiological reactions.
