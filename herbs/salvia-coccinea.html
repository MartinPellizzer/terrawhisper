Salvia coccinea

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia coccinea?

Salvia coccinea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Lamiales and falls under the family Lamiaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Salvia, with the unique species designation coccinea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvias coccineas possess upright, square-shaped stems that typically reach heights of two to three feet and support densely packed, tubular scarlet flowers arranged in whorls. The foliage consists of lanceolate or ovate leaves that exhibit a rough, rugose texture and a grayish-green hue. These leaves are arranged oppositely along the stem and often feature serrated margins. The inflorescence is characterized by elongated racemes that bloom sequentially from the base to the tip. Small, inconspicuous bracts often surround the base of each individual flower within the spike.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southwestern United States and northwestern Mexico, primarily occupying the arid regions of Arizona, New Mexico, Texas, and Sonora. It thrives in rocky slopes, desert scrublands, and open woodlands where well-draining soil is present. Its distribution is often concentrated in elevations ranging from low deserts to mid-level montane environments. Because it is adapted to xeric conditions, it is frequently found in areas characterized by seasonal rainfall and intense sunlight. This specific range makes it a vital component of the local biodiversity in the Sonoran and Chihuahuan desert ecosystems.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires full sun and well-draining, sandy or loamy soil to thrive in USDA hardiness zones 7 through 10.

You should provide regular watering during the establishment phase, but allow the soil to dry out significantly between waterings once the plant is mature. It performs best when planted in locations with excellent air circulation to prevent fungal issues. Because it is a short-lived perennial, you can propagate it easily through stem cuttings taken in late spring.

Deadheading the scarlet flowers throughout the summer will encourage continuous blooms and attract pollinators like hummingbirds.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing diabetes mellitus by helping to regulate blood glucose levels and reducing high cholesterol levels. Its bioactive compounds are often studied for their potential to mitigate various cardiovascular disorders. Additionally, the plant possesses antimicrobial properties that can be applied to treat certain fungal infections. These diverse therapeutic applications make it a significant subject in ethnobotanical research.

According to a study published by "Acta histochemica", Salvia coccinea leaf aqueous extract (AESL) is used to treat inflammatory disorders like diabetes. In studies involving THP-1 cells and STZ-induced diabetic Wistar rats, AESL was administered orally for 14 days. The plant shows potential for controlling blood glucose and protecting against damage caused by hyperglycemia-induced inflammation. AESL treatment reduces ROS production and NF-κB dependent proinflammatory gene expression in HG treated THP-1 cells. Furthermore, oral administration of AESL inhibits increases in serum lipid peroxidation, aspartate transaminase, alanine transaminase, and lactate dehydrogenase in diabetic rats.
