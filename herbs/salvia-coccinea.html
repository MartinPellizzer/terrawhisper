Salvia coccinea

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia coccinea?

Salvia coccinea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Lamiales and falls under the family Lamiaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Salvia, with the unique species designation coccinea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvias coccineas possess upright, square-shaped stems that typically reach heights of two to three feet and support densely packed, tubular scarlet flowers arranged in whorls. The foliage consists of lanceolate or ovate leaves that exhibit a rough, rugose texture and a grayish-green hue. These leaves are arranged oppositely along the stem and often feature serrated margins. The inflorescence is characterized by elongated racemes that bloom sequentially from the base to the tip. Small, inconspicuous bracts often surround the base of each individual flower within the spike.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southwestern United States and northwestern Mexico, primarily occupying the arid regions of Arizona, New Mexico, Texas, and Sonora. It thrives in rocky slopes, desert scrublands, and open woodlands where well-draining soil is present. Its distribution is often concentrated in elevations ranging from low deserts to mid-level montane environments. Because it is adapted to xeric conditions, it is frequently found in areas characterized by seasonal rainfall and intense sunlight. This specific range makes it a vital component of the local biodiversity in the Sonoran and Chihuahuan desert ecosystems.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Salvias coccinea targets various enzymes and signaling proteins, most notably cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, lipoxygenase (LOX), and certain neurotransmitter receptors. The bioactive terpenoids and phenolic compounds found within the plant act as inhibitors of the COX-2 protein to reduce the synthesis of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins. These phytochemicals also interact with nitric oxide synthase to modulate inflammatory responses in cellular pathways. Additionally, certain constituents may influence GABAergic signaling by interacting with gamma-aminobutyric acid receptors in the central nervous system. Through these diverse protein interactions, the plant exhibits potential pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective effects.

What are the therapeutic actions of Salvia coccinea?

Salvias coccinea has medicinal properties that act as an astringent, an antispasmodic, and a nervine to treat various physical and emotional ailments. Its astringent qualities allow the plant to help tone tissues and reduce excessive secretions in the body. As an antispasmodic, it can effectively alleviate involuntary muscle contractions and cramping. The inclusion of nervine actions supports the nervous system by helping to calm anxiety and promote relaxation. These combined therapeutic actions make the plant a versatile tool in traditional herbal medicine.

What health problems are treated using this plant?

This plant is used for treating Candida spp., Mycobacterium tuberculosis, diabetes mellitus, hyperglycemia-induced inflammation, and various inflammatory disorders. The bioactive compounds within the plant exhibit potent antimicrobial properties that target fungal infections and bacterial pathogens. Researchers have observed its ability to help manage blood sugar levels, making it relevant for the treatment of diabetes mellitus. Furthermore, its medicinal properties assist in reducing the systemic damage caused by hyperglycemia-induced inflammation. By addressing these biochemical pathways, the plant serves as a potential agent for managing various chronic inflammatory disorders.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extracts of Salvia coccinea were evaluated for antifungal activity against clinical isolates of different Candida species, including C. albicans, C. parapsilosis, C. tropicalis, C. krusei, and C. glabrata. All extracts, which included Salvia coccinea, showed MIC values < 31.25μg/mL against at least one of the strains used. This finding is noted as very interesting because these were crude extracts. Additionally, the activity of Salvia coccinea against Mycobacterium tuberculosis strain H37rv was evaluated using the MABA method. Salvia coccinea showed MIC values of 125μg/mL in this evaluation.
