What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia peplus?

Euphorbia peplus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a representative of the family Euphorbiaceae, specifically situated within the genus Euphorbia, with its unique identity defined by its species designation, peplus.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia peplus contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its milky white latex, which is the sap found throughout the entire plant body. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds such as 20-deoxyingenol-3-angelate, ingenol-3-angelate (1a), jatrophane diterpenoids, jatrophane-type diterpenoid, and lathyrane-type diterpenoid. These chemical constituents belong to complex classes of secondary metabolites known for their biological activities. Specifically, the jatrophane diterpenoids and the lathyrane-type diterpenoid structures are significant in pharmacological research. The presence of ingenol-3-angelate (1a) and its deoxy derivative provides specific therapeutic potential. These unique diterpenoid structures are central to the plant's medicinal profile.

As discussed in a study, the latex of Euphorbia peplus contains the major component 20-deoxyingenol-3-angelate (DI3A). This DI3A component displays significant nematicidal activity against Caenorhabditis elegans and Panagrellus redivivus. The DI3A treatment inhibits the growth and development of these nematodes and negatively affects locomotion behavior, reproduction, and the accumulation of reactive oxygen species. Transcriptome analysis and RT-qPCR experiments confirmed that differential expression genes in DI3A-treated C. elegans were associated with metabolism, growth, and development processes. The expression level of the TPA-1 gene encoding a protein kinase C isotype was obviously upregulated by the DI3A treatment. Findings suggest that DI3A from Euphorbia peplus latex exerts a potent nematicidal effect through the TPA-1 gene.
