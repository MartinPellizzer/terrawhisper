Euphorbia peplus

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia peplus has a succulent, branched stem structure characterized by cylindrical, fleshy segments that can reach several centimeters in length. These stems often exhibit a glaucous or light green coloration and are capable of storing significant amounts of water. Small, scale-like leaves may appear temporarily on the stem segments before they drop off. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers arranged in specialized cyathia. This morphology allows the species to thrive in arid environments by minimizing surface area and maximizing moisture retention.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia peplus contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its milky white latex, which is the sap found throughout the entire plant body. This latex is harvested by breaking the stems or leaves to release the fluid for topical applications. Practitioners often use the sap to treat skin conditions such as warts, corns, and certain fungal infections. Some traditional uses also involve the plant's leaves or roots for specific internal remedies, though these must be handled with extreme caution. Because the sap is highly caustic, it can cause severe irritation or chemical burns if it touches the eyes or sensitive mucous membranes.

What are the therapeutic actions of Euphorbia peplus?

Euphorbia peplus has therapeutic actions that include being a potent purgative, a caustic agent, a counter-irritant, and an emetic. The milky latex derived from the plant serves as a powerful emetic to induce vomiting when specific doses are administered. The milky sap contains diterpene esters that trigger intense inflammation and painful blistering on the skin. If the substance touches the eyes, it can lead to significant eye irritation and potential vision damage. These adverse reactions occur because the chemical compounds in the sap act as potent irritants to mucous membranes and sensitive tissues.
