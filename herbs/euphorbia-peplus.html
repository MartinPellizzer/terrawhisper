Euphorbia peplus

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia peplus?

Euphorbia peplus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malpighiales. This plant is a representative of the family Euphorbiaceae, specifically situated within the genus Euphorbia, with its unique identity defined by its species designation, peplus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia peplus has a succulent, branched stem structure characterized by cylindrical, fleshy segments that can reach several centimeters in length. These stems often exhibit a glaucous or light green coloration and are capable of storing significant amounts of water. Small, scale-like leaves may appear temporarily on the stem segments before they drop off. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers arranged in specialized cyathia. This morphology allows the species to thrive in arid environments by minimizing surface area and maximizing moisture retention.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Euphorbia peplus contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its milky white latex, which is the sap found throughout the entire plant body. This latex is harvested by breaking the stems or leaves to release the fluid for topical applications. Practitioners often use the sap to treat skin conditions such as warts, corns, and certain fungal infections. Some traditional uses also involve the plant's leaves or roots for specific internal remedies, though these must be handled with extreme caution. Because the sap is highly caustic, it can cause severe irritation or chemical burns if it touches the eyes or sensitive mucous membranes.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including ingenol-3-angelate (1a), 20-deoxyingenol-3-angelate, jatrophane diterpenoids, jatrophane-type diterpenoid, and lathyrane-type diterpenoid. These specialized secondary metabolites are primarily categorized as complex diterpenes that exhibit significant biological activity. Specifically, the ingenane derivatives are known for their potent skin-irritant properties used in therapeutic dermatological applications. The diversity of the chemical profile is further expanded by the presence of both jatrophane-type and lathyrane-type diterpenoid structures. These various chemical constituents collectively contribute to the plant's unique pharmacological potential in medical research.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Euphorbia peplus targets cyclooxygenase-1 and cyclooxygenase-2 enzymes, as well as several signaling proteins involved in oncogenic pathways like PI3K and AKT. The plant contains diterpene esters that act as potent inhibitors of these specific protein targets to disrupt cancer cell proliferation. By suppressing cyclooxygenase activity, the plant's compounds effectively reduce inflammatory mediator production. Furthermore, the modulation of protein kinase pathways helps induce apoptosis in malignant cells. These diverse molecular interactions contribute to the plant's documented anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Euphorbia peplus?

Euphorbia peplus has therapeutic actions that include being a potent purgative, a caustic agent, a counter-irritant, and an emetic. The milky latex derived from the plant serves as a powerful emetic to induce vomiting when specific doses are administered. Because the sap is highly caustic, it can cause severe skin irritation or blistering upon contact. This property allows it to function as a counter-irritant by stimulating localized inflammation to relieve deeper pain. When ingested in controlled amounts, its intense purgative effect is utilized to clear the intestinal tract.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation, eye irritation, vomiting, nausea, abdominal pain, diarrhea, inflammation, and blistering upon contact or ingestion. The milky sap contains diterpene esters that trigger intense inflammation and painful blistering on the skin. If the substance touches the eyes, it can lead to significant eye irritation and potential vision damage. Ingesting any part of the plant often results in gastrointestinal distress including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. These adverse reactions occur because the chemical compounds in the sap act as potent irritants to mucous membranes and sensitive tissues.
