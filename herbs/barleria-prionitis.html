Barleria prionitis

What's the taxonomical classification of Barleria prionitis?

Barleria prionitis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Lamiales. This plant is a representative of the family Acanthaceae, specifically falling under the genus Barleria, with the specific epithet prionitis denoting its unique species identity.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Barleria prionitis has an erect, thorny shrub morphology characterized by stiff, woody stems that typically reach heights of up to two meters. The plant features long, straight, and sharp spines that emerge from the nodes along the branches. Its leaves are pinnately compound with several pairs of leaflets that possess an elliptical or oblong shape and entire margins. The flowers are bright yellow and grow in axillary clusters, often appearing in dense cymes. These botanical structures are complemented by small, globose fruits that develop after successful pollination.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including 6-O-trans-p-coumaroyl-8-O-acetylshanzhiside methyl ester, shanzhidside methyl ester, verbascoide, isoverbascoside, and iridoid glycosides. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their potential biological activities and therapeutic properties. The presence of these phenylethanoid glycosides contributes to the plant's complex phytochemical profile. Researchers frequently analyze these molecules to understand how they interact with various physiological systems. Such detailed chemical compositions are essential for identifying the plant's pharmacological potential.

As discussed in a study, Barleria prionitis contains two new iridoid glycosides identified as 6-O-trans-p-coumaroyl-8-O-acetylshanzhiside methyl ester (1) and its cis isomer (2). The plant also contains the known compounds barlerin (3) and verbascoside (4). A 3:1 mixture of compounds 1 and 2 demonstrated potent in vitro activity against respiratory syncytial virus. This mixture exhibited an EC50 of 2.46 microgram/mL and an IC50 of 42.2 microgram/mL.
