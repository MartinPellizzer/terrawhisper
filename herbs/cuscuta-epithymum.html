Scientific and Botanical Profile

Dodder, with botanical name Cuscuta epithymum, is a parasitic flowering plant belonging to the family Convolvulaceae, commonly known as the morning glory family.

It is also referred to by numerous common names including Love Vine, Epithymum Dodder, Dwarf Dodder, Dewy Cuscuta, Little Dodder, Dollar Weed, Twining Dodder, Common Dodder, Cuscuta Vine, Yellow Dodder, Strangle Vine, Sweet Dodder, and Love Vine. Native to temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, including parts of Europe, Asia, and North America, Cuscuta epithymum is a hemiparasitic plant that lacks chlorophyll and relies on host plants for nutrients. Morphologically, it is a yellowish, thread-like vine that lacks leaves, stems, and roots, with small, yellow flowers that bloom in clusters.

The plant is known for its ability to tightly wrap around its host, often causing damage to crops and ornamental plants.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Cuscuta epithymum can be used as a medicinal plant for its purported benefits in traditional medicine, but it must be approached with caution due to its potential safety concerns.

While some studies suggest it may have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, it is not without risks, including gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and in rare cases, more severe side effects such as liver toxicity. It may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and immunosuppressants, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to follow a healthcare professional's guidance, start with low doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Cuscuta epithymum grows as a parasitic vine that requires a host plant to thrive, typically in well-drained soil with full sun to partial shade.

It prefers nutrient-rich, loamy soil and moderate moisture, though it can tolerate dry conditions once established. Regular watering during the growing season is essential, but overwatering should be avoided to prevent root rot. To ensure optimal growth, it should be planted in the spring after the last frost, with the host plant already in place. Cuscuta epithymum should be monitored for pests and diseases, and its vines should be pruned to encourage healthy growth and prevent overcrowding. Harvesting is best done in late summer to early autumn when the seeds are mature and fully developed, using clean, sharp shears to cut the seed capsules carefully.

After harvesting, the seeds should be dried in a cool, dark place to preserve their potency, and stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry location or refrigerated for long-term preservation.
