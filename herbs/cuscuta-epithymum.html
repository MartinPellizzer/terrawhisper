What is Dodder (Cuscuta Epithymum)?

Dodder (Cuscuta epithymum) is a parasitic flowering plant that lacks chlorophyll and obtains nutrients by attaching itself to host plants, commonly found in temperate regions and used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported restorative and tonic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Dodder is Cuscuta epithymum. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Solanales, family Convolvulaceae, genus Cuscuta, and species Cuscuta epithymum. It is commonly known as Dodder in Europe and the United States, and as Love vine in parts of Australia and New Zealand. Synonyms for this plant include Cuscuta epithymum and Cuscuta epithyma, with regional names such as Devil's hair in some parts of Europe.

Plant Description and Morphology

Dodder is a parasitic flowering plant that lacks true roots, leaves, and stems, instead having a yellow-orange, serpentine stem that can grow up to 2 meters in length.

Its structure is composed of a thin, hollow, and flexible stem covered in tiny, hairless, and sticky pads that help it attach to host plants. The leaves are reduced to small, scale-like structures that are not visible, as the plant derives all its nutrients from its host. Flowers are small, unisexual, and clustered in spikes, with yellowish-white petals and a distinctive, tubular shape.

The plant has no odor, a smooth and leathery texture, and its color ranges from yellow to orange, depending on the host and environmental conditions.

Dodder is a parasitic plant native to temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, including Europe, Asia, and North America.
