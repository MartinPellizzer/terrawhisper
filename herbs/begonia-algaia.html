Begonia algaia

What's the taxonomical classification of Begonia algaia?

Begonia algaia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Cucurbitaleales, falling under the family Begoniaceae. Finally, it is categorized within the genus Begonia, specifically identified by the species name algia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Begonia algaia has succulent, asymmetrical leaves that are broadly ovate to cordate in shape, featuring a distinctively reddish-tinted underside and asymmetrical bases.

The leaf margins are often irregularly serrated or wavy, contributing to its complex silhouette. The plant displays a rhizomatous growth habit, with thick, fleshy stems that emerge from a creeping underground structure. Small, delicate flowers typically emerge from the leaf axils, presenting a contrast to the heavy foliage.

The overall morphology is defined by these fleshy, water-storing tissues and the characteristic uneven leaf geometry typical of the genus.

        What are the herbal preparations of Begonia algaia?

        Begonia algaia is prepared primarily as a topical paste, a concentrated decoction, or a diluted liquid infusion for medicinal use. The leaves are often crushed into a fresh poultice to be applied directly to skin irritations or wounds. For internal application, the plant parts are boiled in water to create a bitter tea used to address digestive issues. Some practitioners also extract the essential juices to create concentrated drops for more potent effects. These various preparations allow for different absorption rates depending on the specific ailment being treated.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Begonia algaia can cause skin irritation, mouth swelling, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested or touched. The plant contains calcium oxalate crystals which act as microscopic needles that penetrate soft tissues upon contact. When these crystals enter the mouth or throat, they trigger immediate inflammation and intense burning sensations. Skin exposure often results in redness, itching, or a localized rash. If a person accidentally eats a piece of the foliage, they may experience nausea, vomiting, or difficulty swallowing.
