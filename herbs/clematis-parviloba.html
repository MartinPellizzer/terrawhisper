Clematis parviloba

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis parviloba?

Clematis parviloba belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant further descends through the order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Ranunculaceae. Finally, it is defined by its specific taxonomic placement within the genus Clematis, specifically as the species parviloba.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis parviloba has opposite or whorled, ovate to elliptic leaves that measure approximately 3 to 7 centimeters in length and feature entire or slightly wavy margins.

These leaves are typically characterized by a dull green color and a relatively thin, membranous texture. The plant features small, inconspicuous flowers that lack showy petals, instead relying on numerous yellowish stamens for pollination. Its climbing habit is supported by delicate, twisting petioles that act as tendrils to grip surrounding vegetation.

The stems are slender and woody, allowing the plant to scramble through dense undergrowth.

        What are the herbal preparations of Clematis parviloba?

        Clematis parviloba is prepared primarily as a decoction of the dried roots, a concentrated infusion of the leaves, or a topical poultice made from crushed plant matter. These traditional methods allow the active botanical constituents to be extracted efficiently for medicinal use. When used as a tea or decoction, the liquid form is often ingested to address internal concerns. The poultice application involves placing the raw or macerated material directly onto the skin to treat external ailments. Such diverse preparations ensure that the plant's properties can be utilized for both systemic and localized purposes.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Clematis parviloba can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and respiratory issues if any part of the plant is ingested or touched. The plant contains protoanemonin, a toxin that triggers contact dermatitis and blistering upon contact with skin. If swallowed, the chemical components can lead to intense burning of the mouth, throat, and stomach. Some individuals may also experience nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea as the body reacts to the toxins. Inhalation of dust or particles from the plant can result in coughing or irritation of the airways.
