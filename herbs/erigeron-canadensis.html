Erigeron canadensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Erigeron canadensis?

Erigeron canadensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae, placing it among the composite flowers, and is specifically categorized under the genus Erigeron, with its unique identity defined by the species canadensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Erigeron canadensis has an erect, branching stem that typically reaches heights of 10 to 100 centimeters and is covered in fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem, transitioning from large, lanceolate basal leaves to smaller, sessile leaves toward the top. Small, white to lavender flower heads are clustered in terminal corymb-like cymes, each featuring numerous narrow ray florets surrounding a yellow disk. The phyllaries, or bracts located beneath the flower head, are distinctive because they are often tinged with purple and possess fine hairs. As the plant matures, it produces many small, wind-dispersed achenes equipped with a pappus of fine bristles.

What are the pharmacological activities of Erigeron canadensis?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-platelet, anticoagulant, antitussive, and bronchodilator pharmacological activities. The anti-inflammatory properties help reduce swelling and pain associated with various physiological conditions. Its anti-platelet and anticoagulant effects work together to manage blood clotting processes and improve circulation. Additionally, the plant functions as an antitussive to suppress coughing and acts as a bronchodilator to open the airways. These diverse medicinal qualities make it a subject of significant pharmacological interest.

As discussed in a study, Erigeron canadensis is recommended as a complementary supplementation for the treatment of diseases associated with immunological inflammation such as common cold, coughs, bronchitis, upper respiratory infections, and immunodeficiencies. The polyphenolic polysaccharide-proteins isolated from the flowers of Erigeron canadensis demonstrated significant immunostimulation of inflammatory TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1ß and IL-12 cytokines with p < 0.001. These proteins also triggered the cell release of anti-inflammatory interleukin IL-10 with p < 0.001. Additionally, the inductive cell release of growth factors M-CSF and GM-CSF was demonstrated with p < 0.001. The polyphenolic polysaccharide-proteins of Erigeron canadensis accelerated the efficacy of cellular phagocytosis and free radical release.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains 4-OMe-glucuronoxylan, arabinogalactan, various phenolic compounds, a polyphenolically polysaccharide-protein (PPP) complex, and polyphenolically polysaccharide-proteins. These specific medicinal compounds contribute to the plant's complex biochemical profile. The presence of arabinogalactan and 4-OMe-glucuronoxylan suggests significant structural and functional roles within its cellular makeup. Additionally, the phenolic constituents provide essential bioactive properties often sought in therapeutic applications. The intricate interactions within the polyphenolically polysaccharide-protein (PPP) complex and the various polyphenolically polysaccharide-proteins further enhance its medicinal potential.

As discussed in a study, the polyphenolic polysaccharide-protein complex isolated from the flowering parts of Erigeron canadensis has a molecular weight of 38,000 g/mol. This dark brown complex contains phenolics at 13.2 wt%, proteins at 16.3 wt%, and uronic acids at 6.3 wt%. The neutral carbohydrate portion consists mainly of galactose at 41.0 wt%, arabinose at 24.1 wt%, glucose at 13.3 wt%, and xylose at 12.1 wt%. Major polysaccharides identified within the Erigeron complex include arabinogalactan and 4-OMe-glucuronoxylan.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating acute lower respiratory tract infections, acute upper respiratory tract infections, bronchitis, the common cold, and various types of coughs. Its medicinal properties are often applied to soothe inflammation within the airways. People frequently utilize its components to manage the symptoms associated with the common cold. The plant also serves as a traditional remedy for suppressing persistent coughs. It remains a notable botanical option for addressing more severe issues like bronchitis and lower respiratory infections.

As discussed in a study, Erigeron canadensis has been used in traditional medicine to treat respiratory diseases, including acute lower respiratory tract infections. A dark brown Erigeron complex was isolated from flowering parts with a molecular weight of 38,000 g/mol. This complex contained phenolics at 13.2% wt%, proteins at 16.3% wt%, and uronic acids at 6.3% wt%. The neutral carbohydrate part consisted mainly of galactose at 41.0 wt%, arabinose at 24.1 wt%, glucose at 13.3 wt%, and xylose at 12.1 wt%. The Erigeron complex exhibited a dose-dependent antitussive activity that approached the effect of codeine.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, redness, itching, or swelling upon direct physical contact. Some individuals may experience localized irritation or small bumps where the plant touched their skin. If the pollen is inhaled, it may trigger respiratory symptoms like sneezing, coughing, or seasonal allergy flare-ups. In rare cases, people with hypersensitivity might encounter more systemic allergic responses. Always use caution when handling the foliage to avoid these potential physiological reactions.
