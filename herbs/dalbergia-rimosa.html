Dalbergia rimosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Dalbergia rimosa?

Dalbergia rimosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized under the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Within this family, it is identified by the genus Dalbergia, and it is specifically distinguished as the species rimosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dalbergia rimosa has a woody, deciduous tree habit characterized by fissured bark, pinnately compound leaves, and small, fragrant, pale yellow flowers.

The leaflets are typically elliptic to oblong in shape with entire margins and acute tips. Its canopy is often spreading, providing dense shade through its lush foliage. The small flowers are arranged in axillary or terminal racemes that emerge during the flowering season.

The fruit consists of small, indehiscent pods that facilitate seed dispersal.

What are the therapeutic actions of Dalbergia rimosa?

Dalbergia rimosa has therapeutic actions that include being an astringent, hemostatic, anti-inflammatory, and antiseptic agent.

The bark of this plant can be used to shrink tissues and control bleeding through its strong astringent and hemostatic properties. It also functions as an effective antiseptic to prevent infection in open wounds. Furthermore, the plant possesses anti-inflammatory qualities that help reduce swelling and irritation in affected areas.

These combined medicinal effects make it a valuable resource in traditional topical treatments.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Dalbergia rimosa can cause severe allergic contact dermatitis, skin irritation, and respiratory hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. The plant contains various bioactive compounds and resins that act as potent allergens when they come into contact with human tissue. People handling the wood or bark may experience redness, swelling, or itching on the skin. Inhalation of fine dust from the wood during processing can lead to asthma-like symptoms or inflammation of the airways. These adverse reactions are most common among workers in woodworking industries who are frequently exposed to the material.
