Datura metel

What's the taxonomical classification of Datura metel?

Datura metei belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant falls under the order Solanales and is a member of the family Solanaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Datura and the specific species metel.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Datura metel has an erect, herbaceous growth habit characterized by hollow, branched stems and large, ovate to lanceolate leaves with wavy or coarsely toothed margins.

The foliage is typically bright green and can grow quite large depending on the available nutrients. Its most striking feature is the large, trumpet-shaped flowers that are white or pale lavender and bloom primarily at night. These blossoms emerge from the leaf axils and possess a long, tubular corolla.

Following pollination, the plant produces distinctive, spiny, egg-shaped seed capsules that contain numerous small, dark seeds.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Datura metel contains medicinal alkaloids found primarily in its seeds, leaves, roots, and flowers. These components are utilized in traditional medicine to treat ailments such as asthma, coughs, and various skin conditions. The leaves are often applied topically to reduce inflammation or used in decoctions to alleviate respiratory distress. Because the plant is highly toxic, parts are used in extremely small, carefully controlled doses. Practitioners must exercise extreme caution due to the potent anticholinergic effects of its chemical constituents.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the tropane alkaloids tropine, scopoalamine, hyoscyamine, and atropine, which function as potent medicinal compounds.

These hyoscyamine alkaloids act as anticholinergic agents by blocking acetylcholine receptors in the nervous system. Scopolamine is frequently utilized in medical settings to treat motion sickness and various gastrointestinal spasms. Atropine serves as a critical pharmacological tool for increasing heart rate and dilating pupils during clinical procedures.

The precise concentration of these substances determines their therapeutic use or their potential toxicity.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Tropine
  • Scopolamine
  • Hyoscyamine
  • Atropine
  • Hyoscyamine alkaloids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Datura metel targets muscarinic acetylcholine receptors, specifically the M1, M2, and M3 subtypes, through its primary tropane alkaloids such as scopolamine and atropine. These alkaloids act as competitive antagonists that bind to the orthosteric binding sites of these G protein-coupled receptors. By blocking the action of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine, these compounds inhibit parasympathetic nervous system activity. This interaction leads to diverse physiological responses including pupil dilation, reduced secretions, and altered cognitive processing. The varying affinities of the specific alkaloids for different receptor subtypes determine the intensity and nature of the plant's medicinal and hallucinogenic effects.

What are the therapeutic actions of Datura metel?

Datura metel has anticholinergic properties that produce a potent deliriant effect through its high concentration of tropane alkaloids.

These chemical compounds act as a mydriatic agent, causing significant dilation of the pupils upon contact or ingestion. In traditional medicinal applications, the plant is utilized for its antispasmodic qualities to relieve smooth muscle contractions. While it can induce a sedative state, the physiological impact often transitions into profound confusion or hallucinations.

Careful dosing is essential because its therapeutic window is extremely narrow due to its toxicity.

The primary therapeutic actions of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Anticholinergic
  • Deliriant
  • Mydriatic
  • Antispasmodic
  • Sedative

What common ailments are treated using this plant?

This plant is used to treat asthma, dysentery, cough, spasms, and various forms of pain relief.

Its alkaloids act as potent anticholinergic agents that can help relax the bronchial passages in respiratory conditions. In traditional medicine, extracts are applied to alleviate intense physical discomfort due to their analgesic properties. The plant's ability to reduce intestinal contractions makes it a historical remedy for managing dysentery.

Furthermore, its sedative effects are utilized to calm muscle spasms throughout the body.

What are the herbal preparations of Datura metel?

Datura metel is prepared as infusions, decoctions, poultices, tinctures, or topical ointments depending on the intended medicinal application. Dried leaves are frequently steeped in hot water to create a liquid extract for internal or external use. The plant's roots and seeds are often processed into concentrated tinctures containing potent alkaloids. For localized skin issues, the crushed fresh leaves are applied directly as a moist poultice to the affected area. These various forms allow for different levels of potency and absorption rates in traditional herbal practices.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Datura metel can cause severe anticholinergic toxicity resulting in intense hallucinations, dilated pupils, rapid heart rate, urinary retention, and dangerous hyperthermia. These potent alkaloids trigger profound delirium and confusion that can last for several days. Physical symptoms often include extreme dry mouth, blurred vision, and skin flushing. In high doses, the plant may induce seizures, coma, or fatal respiratory failure. Due to the unpredictable concentration of toxins in each specimen, ingestion frequently leads to life-threatening medical emergencies.
