Thevetia peruviana

What's the taxonomical classification of Thevetia peruviana?

Thevetia peruviana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this classification, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its lineage further, it falls under the order Gentianales and is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Thevetia, and its specific species name, peruviana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Thevetia peruviana has an evergreen shrub or small tree habit characterized by narrow, lanceolate leaves and trumpet-shaped yellow flowers. The foliage consists of leathery, dark green leaves that are arranged in a spiral pattern along the woody stems. Its bright blossoms emerge from terminal clusters and feature a long, tubular corolla that flares at the tip. Small, fleshy drupes develop from the flowers, typically turning a dark color as they reach maturity. The plant often develops a dense, bushy structure as it grows in tropical environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Central and South America, specifically ranging from Mexico through much of the Caribbean and down to Brazil. It thrives in warm, humid climates where it can find ample sunlight and well-drained soil. Due to its ornamental value, it has been widely naturalized in many other tropical and subtropical areas around the world. Many gardeners plant it in regions like Florida, Southeast Asia, and parts of Africa to enjoy its bright yellow blooms. What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Thevetia peruvia contains toxic cardiac glycosides concentrated primarily in its seeds, leaves, and bark. These specific components are often utilized in traditional medicine to treat various ailments, though they require extreme caution due to their high potency. The seeds are sometimes processed for their potential to affect heart rate, while the bark is used in certain herbal preparations. Because the plant contains substances similar to digitalis, the parts are used with strict dosage control to manage cardiovascular conditions. Misuse of any plant part can lead to severe poisoning or fatal cardiac complications.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds cardenolide glycosides, cardiac glycosides, and thevefolin. These specific chemical constituents are known to exert potent physiological effects on the cardiovascular system. The presence of cardiac glycosides allows the plant to influence heart muscle contractions and rhythm. Within this category, cardenolide glycosides serve as a primary class of active steroids. Additionally, the compound thevefolin contributes to the unique medicinal profile of the species.

As discussed in a study, activity-guided fractionations of Thevetia peruviana led to the isolation of 4 cardenolide glycosides labeled 1-4. Specifically, cardenolide glycosides 1 and 2 from T. peruviava were shown to have a significant reversal effect on TRAIL resistance in human gastric adenocarcinoma cells. The compound thevefolin, identified as 2, enhanced mRNA expression of death receptor 4 (DR4) and DR5. Additionally, 1H and 13C NMR characterizations are shown for thevefolin 2 for the first time.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as a topical paste from crushed leaves to treat skin infections or as a concentrated decoction of the seeds and bark for traditional medicinal uses. However, because the entire plant contains lethal cardiac glycosides, these preparations must be handled with extreme caution. In some traditional practices, diluted extracts are used to manage heart-related ailments, though this carries a high risk of poisoning. Most modern herbalists advise against any internal consumption due to the high toxicity of the chemical compounds present. The primary safe use for these preparations is limited to external applications on the skin.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe toxicity resulting in nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, arrhythmia, bradycardia, dizziness, cardiac arrest, hypotension, confusion, and seizures. These dangerous symptoms occur because the plant contains cardiac glycosides that interfere with heart function. Ingestion often leads to significant cardiovascular instability such as hypotension and life-threatening heart rhythms. Neurological complications including confusion and seizures may also manifest during poisoning. Ultimately, the most critical risk is sudden cardiac arrest which can be fatal.
