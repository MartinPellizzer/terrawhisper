Thevetia peruviana

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Thevetia peruviana has an evergreen shrub or small tree habit characterized by narrow, lanceolate leaves and trumpet-shaped yellow flowers. The foliage consists of leathery, dark green leaves that are arranged in a spiral pattern along the woody stems. Its bright blossoms emerge from terminal clusters and feature a long, tubular corolla that flares at the tip. Small, fleshy drupes develop from the flowers, typically turning a dark color as they reach maturity. The plant often develops a dense, bushy structure as it grows in tropical environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Central and South America, specifically ranging from Mexico through much of the Caribbean and down to Brazil. It thrives in warm, humid climates where it can find ample sunlight and well-drained soil. Due to its ornamental value, it has been widely naturalized in many other tropical and subtropical areas around the world. Many gardeners plant it in regions like Florida, Southeast Asia, and parts of Africa to enjoy its bright yellow blooms. Consequently, its presence is now found far beyond its original evolutionary boundaries.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Thevetia peruvia contains toxic cardiac glycosides concentrated primarily in its seeds, leaves, and bark. These specific components are often utilized in traditional medicine to treat various ailments, though they require extreme caution due to their high potency. The seeds are sometimes processed for their potential to affect heart rate, while the bark is used in certain herbal preparations. Because the plant contains substances similar to digitalis, the parts are used with strict dosage control to manage cardiovascular conditions. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Thevetia peruvians contains high concentrations of cardiac glycosides, primarily resembling digitoxin and thevetin A and B, which act directly on the sodium-potassium pump of heart muscle cells. These potent organic compounds are responsible for the plant's ability to increase the force of myocardial contractions. Because of these chemical properties, extracts from the plant have been studied for their potential to treat specific heart conditions under strict medicinal supervision. However, the narrow therapeutic window of these glycosides means that even slight deviations in dosage can lead to severe toxicity. Careful extraction and processing are required to harness these molecules for pharmaceutical purposes.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Thevetia peruviana targets the Na+/K+-ATPase enzyme by utilizing cardiac glycosides like thevetin A and B to inhibit its activity. This inhibition leads to an increase in intracellular sodium concentrations, which subsequently affects the sodium-calcium exchanger. By modulating this exchanger, the plant induces an increase in cytosolic calcium levels within myocardial cells. These calcium fluctuations enhance the force of cardiac muscle contractions, explaining its potent cardiotonic effects. However, the high affinity for these specific protein pumps also accounts for the plant's significant potential toxicity in humans.

Thevetia peruviana has potent medicinal properties derived from its high concentration of cardiac glycosides which can influence heart muscle contraction. These chemical compounds are used in controlled pharmaceutical settings to manage specific heart conditions by increasing the force of the heartbeat. However, the plant is extremely toxic if ingested in its natural form. Consumption of any part of the plant can lead to severe poisoning, characterized by irregular heart rhythms and potential cardiac arrest. Because of this narrow therapeutic window, the plant must be handled with extreme caution to avoid accidental injury.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause severe toxicity resulting in nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, arrhythmia, bradycardia, dizziness, cardiac arrest, hypotension, confusion, and seizures. These dangerous symptoms occur because the plant contains cardiac glycosides that interfere with heart function. Ingestion often leads to significant cardiovascular instability such as hypotension and life-threatening heart rhythms. Neurological complications including confusion and seizures may also manifest during poisoning. Ultimately, the most critical risk is sudden cardiac arrest which can be fatal.
