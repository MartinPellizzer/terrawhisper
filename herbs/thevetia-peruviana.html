Thevetia peruviana

What's the taxonomical classification of Thevetia peruviana?

Thevetia peruviana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this classification, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its lineage further, it falls under the order Gentianales and is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Ultimately, the plant is identified by its genus, Thevetia, and its specific species name, peruviana.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Thevetia peruvia contains toxic cardiac glycosides concentrated primarily in its seeds, leaves, and bark. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains various medicinal compounds including cardenolide glycosides, cardiac glycosides, and thevefolin. These cardiac glycosides are potent substances that exert a direct effect on the heart muscle to alter contraction strength. The presence of cardenolide glycosides specifically contributes to the plant's significant pharmacological activity. Thevefolin serves as another important chemical component found within the species. Due to the high toxicity of these compounds, they must be handled with extreme caution in medical contexts.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Thevetia peruviana targets the Na+/K+-ATPase enzyme by utilizing cardiac glycosides like thevetin A and B to inhibit its activity. This inhibition leads to an increase in intracellular sodium concentrations, which subsequently affects the sodium-calcium exchanger. By modulating this exchanger, the plant induces an increase in cytosolic calcium levels within myocardial cells. These calcium fluctuations enhance the force of cardiac muscle contractions, explaining its potent cardiotonic effects. However, the high affinity for these specific protein pumps also accounts for the plant's significant potential toxicity in humans.

What are the therapeutic actions of Thevetia peruviana?

Thevetia peruviana has potent medicinal properties derived from its high concentration of cardiac glycosides which can influence heart muscle contraction. These chemical compounds are used in controlled pharmaceutical settings to manage specific heart conditions by increasing the force of the heartbeat. However, the plant is extremely toxic if ingested in its natural form. Consumption of any part of the plant can lead to severe poisoning, characterized by irregular heart rhythms and potential cardiac arrest. Because of this narrow therapeutic window, the plant must be handled with extreme caution to avoid accidental injury.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe toxicity resulting in nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, arrhythmia, bradycardia, dizziness, cardiac arrest, hypotension, confusion, and seizures. These dangerous symptoms occur because the plant contains cardiac glycosides that interfere with heart function. Ingestion often leads to significant cardiovascular instability such as hypotension and life-threatening heart rhythms.
