Salvia glutinosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia glutinosa?

Salvia glutinosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Lamiaceae, which places it within the genus Salvia, and it is specifically identified as the species glutinosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia glutinosa has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by erect, square-shaped stems that often reach heights of up to sixty centimeters. The leaves are opposite, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and possess a distinctively sticky or glandular texture on both surfaces. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal verticillasters that produce small, two-lipped flowers typically ranging in color from pale blue to violet. The calyx is notably enlarged and covered in a thick, viscid coating that provides a protective layer for the developing seeds. These morphological features, particularly the glandular hairs, distinguish it from many other members of the Lamiaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region, spanning across Southern Europe, North Africa, and parts of Western Asia. It is frequently found in rocky areas and scrublands throughout countries such as Italy, Greece, and Spain. In its natural habitat, it thrives in limestone-rich soils and dry, sunny environments. The species can also be discovered in the Levant and parts of the Middle East where similar climates exist. Its range is largely determined by the specific moisture and temperature requirements characteristic of the Mediterranean basin.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive substances including rosmarinic acid, total flavonoid, anthocyanin, phenolic acid, and tannin. These medicinal compounds contribute significantly to the plant's antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of phenolic acid and rosmarinic acid provides a strong chemical foundation for its therapeutic potential. Additionally, the concentration of total flavonoid and anthocyanin suggests high pigment-driven health benefits. Tannin also plays a crucial role in the plant's overall pharmacological profile.

According to a study published by "Plants (Basel, Switzerland)", the ethanolic leaf extracts of Salvia glutinosa exhibited significant DPPH and NO radical scavenging ability, lipid peroxidation inhibition, and reducing power, alongside moderate iron-chelating properties. This species was also found to possess the ability to inhibit α-glucosidase. Within the studied Salvia leaves, total flavonoid contents ranged from 0.37-0.90%, phenolic acids from 3.55-12.44%, tannins from 1.22-2.60%, and anthocyanins from 0.03-0.08%. Rosmarinic acid was the predominant hydroxycinnamic acid, with amounts ranging from 9400 to 38,800 μg/g. These compounds contribute to the plant's efficiency as a source of antioxidant, neuroprotective, and hypoglycemic agents.
