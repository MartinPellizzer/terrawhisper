Crateva trifoliata

What's the taxonomical classification of Crateva trifoliata?

Crateva trifoliata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it in the order Brassicales. Furthermore, this plant is a member of the family Capparaceae, falling under the genus Crateva, with the specific designation of the species trifoliata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crateva trifoliata has trifoliate leaves consisting of three distinct, elliptical leaflets with entire margins and acute apices.

The foliage is arranged alternately along woody stems that exhibit a slightly pubescent texture. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves, typically presenting a simple structure. The reproductive organs include a small fruit that develops following successful pollination.

These morphological characteristics are essential for identifying the species within its specific ecological niche.

        What are the herbal preparations of Crateva trifoliata?

        Crateva trifoliata is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the intended medicinal application. The leaves and stems are often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for internal consumption. For external ailments such as skin irritations or localized pain, the plant material can be crushed and applied directly to the body as a poultice. Some traditional practitioners also utilize alcohol-based tinctures to preserve the active compounds for long-term use. These various methods allow the medicinal properties of the plant to be delivered effectively to different parts of the body.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Crateva trifoliata can cause skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential neurological disturbances if ingested or applied topically. The chemical constituents within the plant may trigger localized contact dermatitis or allergic reactions upon skin exposure. When consumed, individuals might experience nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping as the body reacts to its bioactive compounds. There is also a risk of dizziness or more significant systemic toxicity depending on the dosage and concentration. Monitoring for these symptoms is essential for anyone handling or experimenting with the species.
