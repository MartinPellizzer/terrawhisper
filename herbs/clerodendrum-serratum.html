Clerodendrum serratum

What's the taxonomical classification of Clerodendrum serratum?

Clerodendrum serratum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized into the order Lamiales. It is a member of the family Lamiaceae, falling under the genus Clerodendrum, and is specifically identified by the species name serratum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clerodendrum serratum has opposite, simple leaves that are ovate to lanceolate in shape and characterized by prominent serrated margins and a dull green surface. The plant typically grows as a perennial shrub or small tree reaching heights of up to three meters. Its stems are woody at the base and often feature a slightly hairy texture. The inflorescences consist of terminal panicles bearing small, white, tubular flowers that emerge from the leaf axils. These flowers eventually give way to small, fleshy, black drupes that serve as the plant's fruit.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across India, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, and parts of Indonesia. It thrives predominantly in moist, low-altitude environments such as forest edges, riverbanks, and disturbed roadside areas. While its primary range is centered in the Indian subcontinent, certain populations have expanded into other tropical zones due to favorable climatic conditions. The species is frequently found in regions characterized by high humidity and seasonal rainfall patterns. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of warm, tropical climates that support its rapid growth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Clerodendrum serratum?

This plant has documented pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anti-arthritic, anti-edematous, antimicrobial, and anti-viral properties. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds work to reduce swelling and alleviate the painful symptoms associated with joint inflammation. The plant's ability to combat various pathogens makes it a significant subject for studies involving its antimicrobial and anti-viral potential. Furthermore, its capacity to manage edema suggests its utility in treating fluid retention and tissue swelling. These diverse medicinal qualities highlight its therapeutic importance in traditional and modern pharmacological applications.

As discussed in a study, Clerodendrum serratum (L.) Moon possesses unique anti-inflammatory and anti-arthritic properties. HPTLC methods revealed ursolic acid with an Rf value of 0.38 and a quantified amount of 0.03% w/w. The HPLC assay content for ursolic acid was found to be 0.059% with a relative standard deviation of less than 2.5% and spike recovery between 95-110%. The aqueous extract exhibited COX-2 and TNF-α inhibition with significant results for both (p < 0.0001). This plant extract also demonstrated in-vitro anti-inflammatory activity and showed reclamation of arthritis regarding body weight, arthritic score, paw edema, and joint diameter.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds hispidulin, luteolin, salvigenin, saponins consisting of both terpenoids and steroids, and ursolic acid. These specific bioactive molecules contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile. For instance, the flavonoids hispidulin and luteolin often provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of saponins, including both terpenoid and steroid varieties, can influence various physiological processes in the body. Furthermore, ursolic acid is recognized for its potential roles in promoting skin health and regulating cellular functions. Together, these chemical constituents define the therapeutic potential of the species.

As discussed in a study, Clerodendrum serratum leaf extract contains five major phytoconstituent compounds including Hispidulin, Luteolin, Salvigenin, Pectolinarigenin, and Uncinatone. These bioactive substances were investigated through in silico molecular docking against three critical biofilm-associated targets, which are FadD32, InhA, and MmpL3. The study demonstrated that these specific compounds exhibit strong binding affinities with a docking score of up to -11.0 kcal/mol. This data indicates the potential mechanisms of action for these medicinal compounds in disrupting biofilms.
