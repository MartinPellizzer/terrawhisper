Broussonetia papyrifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Broussonetia papyrifera?

Broussonetia papyrifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Rosales. This plant is situated within the family Moraceae and is specifically identified by the genus Broussonetia and the species papyrifera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Broussonetiia papyrifera has deciduous, heart-shaped leaves that range from 10 to 30 centimeters in length and exhibit highly irregular, deeply lobed margins.

The leaf texture is typically rough on the upper surface while the underside feels soft due to fine hairs. Its woody stems develop a pale, whitish bark that can be peeled away in thin strips. The plant produces small, greenish, globose flower heads that cluster at the ends of branches.

These inflorescences eventually develop into fleshy, aggregate fruits that turn dark red or purple when ripe.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Broussonetia papyrifera targets inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. Its bioactive compounds, including flavonoids and phenolic acids, inhibit the nuclear factor kappa B signaling pathway to reduce systemic inflammation. These phytochemicals also interact with matrix metalloproteinases to prevent tissue degradation during inflammatory processes. Furthermore, the plant's extracts modulate various protein kinases that regulate cellular growth and immune responses. This multi-target approach allows the plant to provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits across different biological systems.

What are the herbal preparations of Broussonetia papyrifera?

Broussonetiia papyrifera is prepared as decoctions, powders, poultices, and infusions using its various parts such as the leaves, roots, and stems. The dried leaves are often brewed into a tea to address respiratory issues or inflammatory conditions. For topical applications, the crushed leaves or stem bark can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat skin irritations. Additionally, the finely ground root can be administered in powder form to assist with internal ailments. These diverse preparation methods allow different therapeutic properties of the plant to be utilized effectively.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Broussonetiia papyrifera can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and localized swelling when the plant's sap or foliage comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals may experience redness or a stinging sensation after handling the leaves or stems. Ingestion of the plant is generally considered safe in traditional practices, but excessive consumption could potentially lead to gastrointestinal discomfort. Because the plant contains various bioactive compounds, people with known allergies to the Moraceae family should exercise caution. It is always advisable to perform a patch test before using any part of the plant for topical applications.
