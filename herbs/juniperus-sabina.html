Juniperus sabina

What's the taxonomical classification of Juniperus sabina?

Juniperus sabiina belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Pinidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Pinales and the family Cupressaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Juniperus, and its specific species, sabiina.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Juniperus sabiina has scale-like, overlapping leaves that are dark green and arranged in whorls of three, alongside occasional needle-like leaves in its juvenile stage. The plant typically grows as a spreading shrub or a small tree with a dense, irregular crown. Its bark is brownish and becomes shallowly fissured as the specimen matures. The fruit consists of small, fleshy, berry-like cones that turn blue-black when ripe. These cones are approximately six to nine millimeters in diameter and contain several small seeds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to much of central, southern, and eastern Europe as well as parts of western Asia including the Caucasus and the Middle East. It typically thrives in mountainous regions and rocky terrain across these territories. The species can be found ranging from the Mediterranean coast to the colder climates of the northern mountain ranges. Its distribution is often tied to specific altitudes where it can tolerate diverse soil types. Many populations are concentrated in temperate zones that experience seasonal temperature fluctuations.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Juniperus sabiina contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its aromatic needles, crushed berries, and the essential oils extracted from its woody branches. The berries are often harvested to create tinctures or infusions used for their purported diuretic and antiseptic properties. Additionally, the volatile oils derived from the foliage are applied topically to help soothe muscle aches and skin irritations. It is important to note that the plant also contains toxic components like sabinene, which requires careful dosing to avoid poisoning. These various parts are utilized in traditional folk medicine, though modern science cautions against unregulated consumption due to potential toxicity.

What are the pharmacological activities of Juniperus sabina?

This plant has anti-infection, anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, anticholinesterase, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively combat various pathogens and reduce systemic inflammation. Studies have also demonstrated its potential to inhibit tumor growth through potent cytotoxic mechanisms against specific cancer cell lines. Furthermore, the presence of certain metabolites suggests a capacity for anticholinesterase activity, which is relevant to neurological functions. These diverse therapeutic properties make Juniperus sabina a subject of significant pharmacological interest.

As discussed in a study, aqueous extracts of Juniperus sabiina L have inhibitory effects on HepG-2 and K562 tumor cells by decreasing cell viability and inducing apoptosis. This process occurs through the up-regulation of the expression of apoptosis-related genes FasL, caspase 3, and caspase 9. The extracts can block the tumor cell cycle at the G(0)/G(1) stage and significantly inhibit the adherence of HepG-2 cells. Furthermore, the extracts inhibit the migration of these cells by inhibiting the expression of ICAM-1, MMP-2, and MMP-9. The inhibition of pSTAT1 and 3 might be responsible for these inhibitory effects on tumor cells.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of medicinal compounds including essential oils and the specific chemical constituent myrtenyl acetate. These essential oils serve as the primary source of the plant's aromatic and therapeutic properties. Myrtenyl acetate is a key component within these oils that contributes to the plant's overall biological activity. Such compounds are often studied for their potential antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects in various applications. The synergy between these different chemical structures defines the plant's medicinal efficacy.

As discussed in a study, essential oils were isolated from eight juniper species including J. sabiina. The compositional analysis of 16 samples of Juniper essential oils revealed similarities and differences between the current analyses and those previously reported. While specific percentage data for J. sabiina was not provided, the study notes that high levels of cedrol were found in several essential oil samples. These essential oils or pure (+)-cedrol were found to induce intracellular Ca2+ mobilization in human neutrophils. Furthermore, the research suggests that cedrol acts as a novel neutrophil agonist that can desensitize cells to subsequent stimulation by N-formyl peptide.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating urinary tract infections, digestive issues, and various inflammatory diseases. Its essential oils possess antimicrobial properties that help combat bacterial infections within the body. Additionally, the plant is applied topically or ingested in controlled amounts to reduce swelling and pain associated with inflammatory diseases. Practitioners often utilize its diuretic effects to assist in flushing toxins from the kidneys. Careful dosage is necessary because the plant can be toxic if consumed excessively.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an essential oil, a tincture, an infusion, or a decoction for various medicinal and aromatic purposes. The essential oil is typically extracted through steam distillation of the foliage and berries to be used in aromatherapy or topical applications. For internal or topical use, a tincture can be made by soaking the plant parts in alcohol to concentrate its active compounds. An infusion involves steeping the softer green parts in hot water to extract delicate constituents. Conversely, a decoction requires boiling the harder woody material or berries in water to release more stubborn medicinal properties.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe physiological harm including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, dizziness, skin irritation, respiratory irritation, seizures, abortion or miscarriage, kidney irritation or damage, and hematuria. Consuming the toxic components of Juniperus sabiina often leads to intense gastrointestinal distress and neurological complications like seizures. The essential oils and compounds within the plant are particularly dangerous to the reproductive system and can trigger a miscarriage. Furthermore, the kidneys may suffer significant damage, which often manifests through hematuria or blood in the urine. Contact with the plant can also result in painful skin irritation and discomfort in the respiratory tract.

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Abdominal pain
  • Diarrhea
  • Abortion/miscarriage
  • Dizziness
  • Kidney irritation/damage
  • Hematuria (blood in urine)
  • Skin irritation
