Viburnum odoratissimum

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum odoratissimum?

Viburnum odoratissimum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is organized under the order Dipsacales. It is a member of the family Viburnaceae, which places it within the genus Viburnum, and it is specifically identified by its species name, odoratissimum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum odoratisimun has evergreen, opposite, ovate leaves that measure between five and twelve centimeters in length and feature serrated margins and a glossy, dark green upper surface. The leaf undersides often display a paler hue and a slightly pubescent texture. During the spring, the plant produces clusters of small, white, five-petaled flowers that emit a strong, sweet fragrance. These blossoms eventually transition into small, fleshy drupes that change from red to black as they mature. The stems are woody and can develop a somewhat rough bark as the shrub reaches maturity.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, moist, loamy soil and a location with full sun to partial shade to ensure vigorous growth and prolific flowering.

It thrives best when planted in a spot that provides consistent moisture without allowing the roots to become waterlogged. Regular mulching helps maintain soil humidity and suppresses competing weeds around the base of the shrub. Pruning should be performed after the flowering period to maintain its shape and encourage new wood development.

During periods of extreme heat, supplemental watering is necessary to prevent leaf scorch and maintain overall plant health.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viburnum odoratissimum?

This plant has antibacterial activity, cytotoxic activity, piscicidal activity, and the ability to inhibit the growth of plants. Researchers have identified that its chemical constituents can effectively combat various bacterial strains. The observed cytotoxicity demonstrates its potential for evaluating cell-killing properties in biological models. Furthermore, the extract exhibits piscicidal activity that can be used to target specific fish species. Finally, its ability to act as an inhibitor of plant growth suggests its potential application in weed control.

According to a study published by "Yao xue xue bao = Acta pharmaceutica Sinica", Viburnum odoratissimum is a folk medicinal plant that can dredge the meridian passage. The plant contains mainly diterpenes, triterpene, flavonoids, sesquiterpene, lignans, and coumarin glycosides. Vibsanin-type diterpenoids serve as characteristic compounds and are categorized into eleven-membered ring, seven-membered ring, and rearrangement-type subtypes. Representative compounds for these three subtypes include vibsanin B, vibsanin C, and neovibsanin A. Viburnum odoratissimum possesses antibacterial, fish piscicidal, and plant growth inhibiting activities, though cytotoxic activity is the main biological activity.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment and study of gastric cancer, human gastric (NUGC) tumor cells, and nasopharyngeal carcinoma. Researchers investigate the bioactive compounds within its leaves to determine their potential in inhibiting tumor growth. Studies have specifically demonstrated that extracts from the plant can induce apoptosis in various cancer cell lines. These medicinal properties are being closely evaluated to develop new therapeutic strategies against aggressive malignancies. This ongoing scientific exploration aims to harness the plant's natural chemical constituents for advanced oncological applications.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", Viburnum odoratissimum contains five known compounds including 5-epi-vibsanin H, vibsanins C, H, and G, and aldovibsanin B. Four new diterpenes, 5-epi-vibsanin G (1), 18-O-methylvibsanin G (2), vibsanin M (3), and aldovibsanin C (4), were isolated from an acetone extract of the leaves and flowers. Two acetyl derivatives 5 and 6 were also obtained from the naturally occurring diterpenes. These compounds were evaluated for cytotoxicity against 1 human nasopharyngeal carcinoma (HONE-1) tumor cells and 1 human gastric cancer (NUGC-3) cells.
