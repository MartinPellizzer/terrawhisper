Glycyrrhiza pallidiflora

What's the taxonomical classification of Glycyrrhiza pallidiflora?

Glycyrrhiza pallidiflora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Fabales. This plant is a member of the family Fabaceae and is specifically categorized within the genus Glycyrrhiza, with the distinct species designation of pallidiflora.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Glycyrrhiza pallidiflora has a perennial, woody-based herbaceous habit characterized by pinnately compound leaves, small pale flowers, and elongated, cylindrical legume pods. The foliage consists of several leaflets that are typically lanceolate or oblong in shape with smooth margins. Its inflorescence features delicate, pale-colored blossoms that emerge from the axils of the leaves. The fruit develops into slender pods that contain numerous small seeds essential for its reproduction. These structural features are characteristic of the legume family and help distinguish it from closely related species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the arid and semi-arid regions of the southwestern United States and northern Mexico. It primarily inhabits desert scrublands and dry alkaline basins throughout states like Arizona, New Mexico, and Texas. Its range extends southward into the Mexican states of Sonora and Chihuahua where it thrives in sandy or gravelly soils. These specific environments provide the low moisture and high sunlight levels required for its growth. The species remains localized to these specific subtropical and desert biomes.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Glycyrrhiza pallidiflora contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its underground rhizomes and roots. These subterranean structures are harvested to extract glycyrrhizin and other saponins used for their soothing properties. Practitioners often process the dried roots into decoctions or extracts to treat respiratory ailments and digestive inflammation. The chemical constituents found in these root tissues provide anti-inflammatory and expectorant effects.     What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains the medicinal compounds 2'-hydroxyformononetin 7-O-glucoside, calycosin 7-O-glucoside, formononetin 7-O-(6"-malonylglucoside), licoagroside D, and medicarpin 3-O-glucoside. These specific phytoalexins and isoflavonoids are known for their significant biological activities in various therapeutic studies. Each molecule plays a distinct role in the plant's chemical defense system against environmental stressors. Researchers study these compounds to understand their potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties in humans. The presence of these specialized glycosides highlights the complex chemical profile of the species.

    As discussed in a study, Glycyrrhiza pallidiflora hairy root cultures contain three flavonoids named licoaagroside D, E, and F. Four known flavonoids were also isolated from these cultures, specifically medicarpin 3-O-glucoside, calycosin 7-O-glucoside, formononetin 7-O-(6''-malonylglucoside), and 2'-hydroxyformononetin 7-O-glucoside. Licoaagroside E represents the first example of a 6a-hydroxypterocarpan glycoside. Additionally, licoaagroside F is the first example of an alpha-O-glycosidic alpha-hydroxydihydrochalcone.

    What health problems is this plant used for?

    This plant is used for treating cancer, human T-cell leukaeaemia, human monocyte cells, and lymphoblastoid leukaeaemia. Researchers have investigated its bioactive compounds for their potential to inhibit the growth of various malignant cells. Specific studies focus on how its extracts interact with human T-cell leukaeaemia to induce cell death. The plant also shows promise in affecting the behavior of human monocyte cells during immune responses. Additionally, its chemical properties are being studied for their effectiveness against lymphoblastoid leukaeaemia.

    As discussed in a study, twenty-one phenolic compounds including dihydrocinnamic acid, isoflavonoids, flavonoids, coumestans, pterocarpans, chalcones, isoflavan and isoflaven were isolated from the roots of Glycyrrhiza pallidiflora Maxim. Phloretinic acid (1), chrysin (6), 9-methoxycoumestan (8), isoglycyrol (9), 6″-O-acetylanonin (19) and 6″-O-acetylwistin (21) were isolated from the plant for the first time. Compounds 2-4, 10, 11, 19 and 21 were evaluated for cytotoxic activity against model cancer cell lines CEM-13, MT-4, and U-937 using MTT assays. The isoflavonoid calycosin (4) showed the best potency against human T-cell leukaeamia cells MT-4 with a CTD50 of 2.9 μM. Pterocarpans medicarpin (10) and homopterocarpin (11) exhibit anticancer activity in the micromolar range with selectivity on human monocyte cells U-937. The isoflavan (3R)-vestitol (16) was highly selective on lymphoblastoid leukaeamia cells CEM-13 and was more active than the drug doxorubicin.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause hypertension, edema, hypokalemia, and muscle weakness due to the presence of glycyrrhizin which mimics the effects of aldosterone. Excessive consumption may lead to significant imbalances in electrolytes, particularly a dangerous drop in potassium levels. Such physiological changes can trigger heart palpitations or irregular rhythms in sensitive individuals. Prolonged use is also associated with increased sodium retention and water weight gain. These symptoms necessitate caution for those with pre-existing kidney or cardiovascular conditions.
