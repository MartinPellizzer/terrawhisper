Tephroseris flammea

What's the taxonomical classification of Tephroseris flammea?

Tephroseris flammea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae and is specifically categorized under the genus Tephroseris, with its unique biological identity defined by the species name flammea.

What are the therapeutic actions of Tephroseris flammea?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including respiratory issues, digestive disturbances, and skin inflammations. It is often employed in traditional medicine to soothe coughs and manage bronchial congestion. Some practitioners utilize its extracts to alleviate stomach pains or gastrointestinal discomfort. Additionally, topical applications of the plant are sometimes used to reduce the swelling and irritation of minor skin wounds. These therapeutic properties are traditionally derived from the specific chemical compounds found within its leaves and stems.

What are the herbal preparations of Tephroseris flammea?

Tephroseris flammea is prepared as an infusion, a decoction, or a poultice depending on the specific medicinal application required. Practitioners often boil the plant parts in water to create a decoction for more robust internal remedies. For milder topical treatments, the leaves are steeped in hot water to produce a soothing infusion. The fresh material can also be crushed directly into a poultice to be applied to the skin for localized healing. These various methods allow for the extraction of different active components based on the desired therapeutic effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Tephroseris flammea can cause contact dermatitis, skin irritation, and allergic reactions when the plant comes into direct contact with human skin. The presence of specific chemical compounds or hairs on the plant may trigger redness, itching, or swelling in sensitive individuals. If the plant is ingested, it may lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or stomach pain. Some people might also experience respiratory irritation if they inhale dust or pollen particles from the foliage. Careful handling is recommended to avoid these localized or systemic adverse effects.
