What's the taxonomical classification of Kadsura coccinea?

Kadsura coccineas belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Austrobaileyales and is a member of the family Schisandraceae. Its taxonomic hierarchy is completed by its placement within the genus Kadsura, specifically identified as the species kadsura coccineas.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal plant Kadsura coccinea (Lem.) A.C. Smith provides the endophytic Streptomyces sp. PH9030 from which bioactive compounds are derived. Compounds 5, 6, 7, and 9 exhibit alpha-glucosidase inhibitory activities with IC50 values ranging from 66.4 ± 6.7 to 185.9 ± 0.2 μM. These values are compared against acarbose, which has an IC50 of 671.5 ± 0.2 μM. Additionally, compounds 9 and 12 demonstrated moderate antibacterial activity against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus. This antibacterial activity was measured with minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) values of 16 μg/mL.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds (+)-Anwulignan, Binankadsurin A, Kadcoccilactone A, Kadcoccinin A, and Kadsuric acid. These specific secondary metabolites are identified through advanced phytochemical screening and chemical analysis. Researchers study these unique molecules to determine their potential pharmacological activities and therapeutic benefits. Each compound contributes to the complex chemical profile that characterizes the plant's biological properties. Understanding these constituents is essential for developing future medicinal applications derived from this species.

As discussed in a study, Kadsura coccinea fruit is a traditional medicinal plant rich in dibenzocyclooctadiene lignans. One specific compound, Binankadsurin A (BKA), is a dibenzocyclooctadiene lignan isolated from the fruits. In research involving mice, BKA was tested at a low-dose of 50 mg/kg and a high-dose of 100 mg/kg to evaluate its efficacy against acetaminophen-induced injury. This compound functions by activating the Keap1/Nrf2/HO-1 signaling pathway to inhibit oxidative stress. BKA was shown to reduce serum levels of aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT) while improving CYP2E1 expression.
