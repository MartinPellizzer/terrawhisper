Dysoxylum hongkongense

What's the taxonomical classification of Dysoxylum hongkongense?

Dysoxylum hongkongense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further organized under the order Sapindales. It is a representative of the family Meliaceae, falling specifically under the genus Dysoxylum, with its unique biological identity defined by its species name, hongkongense.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Dysoxylum hongkongense targets various enzymes and signaling proteins, specifically focusing on inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), lipoxygenase (LOX), and specific pro-inflammatory cytokines to produce its medicinal effects. The bioactive compounds within the plant, such as various terpenoids and alkaloids, act as inhibitors of these key inflammatory mediators. By suppressing the COX-2 pathway, the plant effectively reduces the synthesis of prostaglandins, which helps alleviate pain and swelling. Additionally, its interaction with nitric oxide synthase (NOS) helps regulate oxidative stress within biological systems. These multi-target interactions contribute to its observed anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and potential antimicrobial activities.

    What are the herbal preparations of Dysoxylum hongkongense?

    Dysoxylum hongkongense is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a topical paste using the bark and wood of the plant. The bark is most frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea intended for internal consumption. Once dried, the plant material can be ground into a fine powder to be encapsulated or mixed into other substances. In some traditional applications, the crushed wood is processed into a paste for direct application to the skin. These various preparations allow the plant's active compounds to be utilized for different therapeutic purposes.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Dysoxylum hongkongense can cause skin irritation, localized inflammation, and potential allergic reactions upon direct contact with its sap or foliage. The chemical constituents within the plant may trigger dermatological distress such as redness or itching in sensitive individuals. If accidental ingestion occurs, it may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or more severe systemic toxicity depending on the dosage. There is limited clinical data regarding long-term exposure, so cautious handling is essential to avoid unpredictable adverse effects. Always use protective gear when working with this species to mitigate these biological risks.
