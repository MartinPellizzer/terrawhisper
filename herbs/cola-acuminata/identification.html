Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Kola Nut is Cola acuminata, first described by the botanist Robert Brown in 1810. It is also known by several synonyms, including Cola nitida and Cola acuminata var. nitida. Common names such as Kola Nut, Kola bean, and Coffee nut are widely used, while regional and historical names reflect its cultural and economic significance across Africa and beyond.

Morphological Characteristics

Kola Nut (Cola acuminata) is an evergreen shrub or small tree. It has a fibrous root system that anchors it firmly in the soil. The stem is typically short and woody, with a smooth, dark bark. Its leaves are alternate, elliptical, and have a glossy green surface. The plant produces panicles of white to yellowish flowers. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release two large, hard seeds surrounded by a sweet, white pulp.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Kola Nut (Cola acuminata) can be distinguished by its elongated, ridged pods containing smooth, oval seeds. It differs from similar species like Cola nitida through its more elongated pod shape and the presence of a distinct, narrow stipule at the base of the leaves. The seeds are also larger and have a more pronounced hilum compared to other Cola species.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Kola Nut (Cola acuminata) is native to western and central Africa. It thrives in tropical climates with high rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils. It is commonly found at altitudes between 200 and 1,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Kola Nut (Cola acuminata) blooms during the dry season, typically between December and April. Seed formation occurs from May to September. Harvesting is usually done when the fruit turns from green to yellowish, indicating maturity, which is key for accurate botanical identification.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Kola Nut (Cola acuminata) is closely related to other species within the Cola genus. It shares characteristics with related genera in the Malvaceae family, such as cotton and okra. These relationships are defined by shared morphological and genetic traits, aiding in its identification and classification in herbal medicine.
