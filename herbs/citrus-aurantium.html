Herbal Preparations

These teas are commonly used for their potential to support digestion, reduce stress, and promote cardiovascular health. Tinctures, made by soaking the herb in alcohol, offer a concentrated form that can be taken sublingually or diluted in water for quicker absorption of its bioactive components. Capsules provide a convenient and standardized method of consumption, making it easy to incorporate citrus aurantium into daily routines without the need for brewing.

In addition to teas and capsules, citrus aurantium is also used in essential oils, which are extracted through distillation and applied topically or inhaled for their aromatic and therapeutic properties, such as promoting relaxation and improving mood.

Side Effects of citrus aurantium

Citrus aurantium anxiety occurs frequently, as the herb contains compounds like synephrine and limonene that can stimulate the central nervous system, leading to heightened feelings of nervousness and restlessness.

Heart palpitations often accompany this anxiety, as the herb's stimulant properties can increase heart rate and cause irregular cardiac rhythms. Insomnia is a common side effect due to the herb's ability to interfere with sleep patterns and reduce the quality of rest.

Dizziness and nausea may occur as the body reacts to the herb's effects on blood pressure and gastrointestinal function. Additionally, tremors, sweating, fatigue, and stomach pain are frequently reported, indicating a range of systemic responses that can vary in severity among individuals.

These side effects highlight the importance of using citrus aurantium with caution and under professional guidance to minimize potential risks.
