Citron, scientifically known as Citrus aurantium, is a citrus fruit widely recognized for its medicinal properties and traditional use in herbal medicine.

It is often classified as a medicinal herb and adaptogen, valued for its ability to support emotional and physical well-being. The primary benefits of citron include its capacity to alleviate stress, enhance digestion, and promote cardiovascular health due to its high content of bioflavonoids and essential oils. Historically, citron has been used in traditional Chinese and Ayurvedic medicine to treat digestive issues, anxiety, and respiratory conditions, while in modern wellness practices, it is utilized in aromatherapy, supplements, and as a natural remedy for mood regulation.

One of its unique features is the presence of naringin, a rare flavonoid with potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, and its distinctive bitter-sweet flavor has made it a prized ingredient in both culinary and therapeutic applications throughout history.

Scientific and Botanical Profile

Citron, with botanical name Citrus aurantium, is a citrus fruit native to Southeast Asia, particularly the regions of India and China, though it has been widely cultivated in the Mediterranean and other warm climates.

It belongs to the family Rutaceae and is commonly known as Bitter Orange, Seville Orange, and Common Orange. Morphologically, it is a small, round or oval citrus fruit with a thick, yellowish-green rind that is segmented and covered in a rough, textured peel.

The fruit contains a high concentration of essential oils, acids, and flavonoids, which contribute to its characteristic bitter taste and medicinal properties.

History and Cultural Relevance

Citron was used in ancient civilizations such as Egypt, Greece, and Rome for both culinary and medicinal purposes, where it was valued for its aromatic properties and potential health benefits.

In traditional medicine systems like Ayurveda and Chinese medicine, citron has been utilized to treat ailments ranging from digestive issues to respiratory conditions, often in the form of essential oils or dried fruit. The plant also holds cultural significance in various rituals, including Jewish Passover ceremonies, where citron is used as part of the lulav bundle, symbolizing joy and spiritual renewal. Folk traditions in regions like Sicily and the Middle East have long incorporated citron into festive celebrations and healing practices, reflecting its deep-rooted presence in local heritage.

Today, citron-derived products such as essential oils and extracts continue to be used in aromatherapy and natural remedies, maintaining their relevance in modern holistic health practices.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Citron contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including alkaloids such as synephrine and limonine, along with flavonoids like hesperidin and naringin, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

It is also rich in essential oils, primarily composed of limonene and linalool, which are responsible for its distinctive aroma and potential therapeutic effects. Nutritionally, citron is a good source of vitamin C, dietary fiber, and various minerals such as potassium and magnesium, along with antioxidants that help combat oxidative stress. The active compounds in citron may influence neurotransmitter activity and metabolic processes, potentially supporting weight management and cardiovascular health.

Its mechanism of action involves interactions with the sympathetic nervous system and adipose tissue, promoting thermogenesis and fat oxidation.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Citrus aurantium has been widely recognized for its potent medicinal properties and numerous health benefits, primarily due to its high concentration of flavonoids and alkaloids such as synephrine and hesperidin.

It is known to support cardiovascular health by improving blood circulation and reducing cholesterol levels, while also aiding in weight management by stimulating metabolism and suppressing appetite. Additionally, citrus aurantium exhibits anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, which can benefit the respiratory and digestive systems by reducing inflammation and protecting cells from oxidative stress. Compared to similar herbs like bitter orange (Citrus aurantium var. amara), it offers more consistent potency due to its standardized extract forms, making it a preferred choice for therapeutic use.

However, it is important to note that citrus aurantium may have stronger effects and should be used with caution, especially in individuals with hypertension or heart conditions, unlike some milder alternatives such as grapefruit or lemon.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Citrus aurantium has been traditionally used for its therapeutic properties, available in various forms such as fresh fruit, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

It can be prepared as a tea by steeping the dried peel in hot water, or as a decoction by boiling the peel for a longer period, while an infusion involves using the dried flowers or zest. The essential oil is often diluted and applied topically for aromatherapy or skin conditions, and the powder or capsule forms are commonly used for internal consumption. For adults, a typical dosage is 200-400 mg of standardized extract daily, while children should only use it under medical supervision if safe.

It is recommended to use citrus aurantium sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Citrus aurantium can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential effects on mood and metabolism, but it must be approached with caution due to its potential safety risks.

It may cause side effects such as insomnia, anxiety, increased heart rate, and gastrointestinal discomfort, particularly at higher doses or with prolonged use. Citrus aurantium can interact with medications like antidepressants, anticoagulants, and stimulants, increasing the risk of adverse effects or reduced efficacy. It is contraindicated during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential risks to fetal or infant development, and it should be avoided by individuals with chronic illnesses like hypertension or heart conditions.

To use citrus aurantium safely, consult a healthcare professional before use, start with low doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Citrus aurantium grows best in well-drained, fertile soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, requiring full sun exposure and regular watering to maintain moist but not waterlogged conditions. It thrives in warm climates with temperatures above 15°C, and regular pruning helps promote air circulation and prevent disease. To ensure optimal growth, apply balanced fertilizers during the growing season and monitor for pests like aphids and scale insects. The fruit is typically harvested when it turns from green to a vibrant orange color, usually in late summer to early autumn, using hand-picking to avoid damaging the plant.

For preservation, the fruits should be dried in a cool, shaded area or refrigerated in airtight containers to maintain their potency and extend shelf life.
