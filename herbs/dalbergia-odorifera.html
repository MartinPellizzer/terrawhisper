Dalbergia odorifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Dalbergia odorifera?

Dalbergia odorifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent, it falls under the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Dalbergia, and its unique species designation, odorifera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dalbergia odorifera has pinnately compound leaves consisting of several pairs of oval or elliptical leaflets that are typically light green and glabrous. The tree produces small, fragrant, yellowish-white flowers that grow in dense axillary panicles. Its woody stems are characterized by a dark, dense heartwood that is highly valued for its aromatic properties. The fruit is a small, flat, indehiscent legume that contains one or two seeds. This species often develops a spreading canopy with a rugged bark texture as it matures.

Its specific range is heavily concentrated in areas that receive consistent seasonal rainfall to support its growth. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds genistein, formononetin, liquiritigenin, medicarpin, and daidzein. These specific isoflavones and phytoestrogens are known for their ability to interact with biological pathways related to hormone regulation. Researchers study these molecules for their potential antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anticancer properties in human health. Each compound contributes to the overall therapeutic profile of the species by modulating various cellular functions. Understanding the concentration of these bioactive substances is essential for developing standardized botanical extracts.

As discussed in a study, Dalbergia odorifera contains 17 flavonoids, of which 9 were detected in rat plasma. Among these, 3'-O-methylviolanone and sativanone exhibited peak concentration and area under the plasma concentration-time curve values significantly higher than other ingredients. The peak time for most of these flavonoids, with the exception of Genistein and Pruneion, was lower than 2 h. The elimination half-life for the 9 detected flavonoids ranged from 3.32 to 21.5 h. Furthermore, molecular docking indicated that daidzein, dalbergin, formononetin, and genistein possessed the potential to bind to target proteins.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause contact dermatitis, allergic skin reactions, and potential systemic toxicity if ingested in significant quantities. The wood and extracts often contain various chemical compounds that act as potent allergens when they touch sensitive skin. Individuals may experience redness, itching, or swelling upon direct contact with the plant material. Ingesting parts of the tree can lead to gastrointestinal distress or more severe internal complications. Due to these risks, caution should always be exercised when handling any part of the species.
