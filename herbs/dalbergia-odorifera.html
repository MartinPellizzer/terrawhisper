Dalbergia odorifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Dalbergia odorifera?

Dalbergia odorifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent, it falls under the order Fabales and the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Dalbergia, and its unique species designation, odorifera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dalbergia odorifera has pinnately compound leaves consisting of several pairs of oval or elliptical leaflets that are typically light green and glabrous. The tree produces small, fragrant, yellowish-white flowers that grow in dense axillary panicles. Its woody stems are characterized by a dark, dense heartwood that is highly valued for its aromatic properties. The fruit is a small, flat, indehiscent legume that contains one or two seeds. This species often develops a spreading canopy with a rugged bark texture as it matures.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds genistein, formononetin, liquiritigenin, medicarpin, and daidzein. These specific molecules belong to the isoflavonoid family and often exhibit significant biological activities in human cells. Genistein and daidzein are well-known phytoestrogens that can influence hormonal pathways. Formononetin and liquiritigenin contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile, including potential anti-inflammatory properties. Furthermore, the presence of medicarpin adds to the complex chemical defense and therapeutic potential of the species.

As discussed in a study, Dalbergia odorifera contains 17 flavonoids, of which 9 were detected in rat plasma. Among these, 3'-O-methylviolanone and sativanone exhibited peak concentration and area under the plasma concentration-time curve values significantly higher than other ingredients. Most flavonoids had a peak time lower than 2 h, with the exception of Genistein and Pruneion. The elimination half-life for the 9 detected flavonoids ranged from 3.32 to 21.5 h. Molecular docking indicated that daidzein, dalbergin, formononetin, and genistein had the potential to bind to target proteins.

Individuals may experience redness, itching, or swelling upon direct contact with the plant material. Ingesting parts of the tree can lead to gastrointestinal distress or more severe internal complications. Due to these risks, caution should always be exercised when handling any part of the species.
