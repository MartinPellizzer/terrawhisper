Celastrus paniculatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Celastrus paniculatus?

Celastrus paniculatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Celastrales and is a member of the family Celastraceae. Finally, it is placed within the genus Celastrus, specifically identified by its species name, paniculatus.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Celastrus paniculatus contains medicinal compounds found primarily in its bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is often harvested for its bitter principles which are used in traditional remedies to treat various ailments. Extracts from the leaves are sometimes utilized for their potential anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Additionally, the roots are known to hold potent bioactive constituents that have been studied for their therapeutic effects. These diverse plant parts collectively contribute to the plant's historical use in folk medicine.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Celastrus paniculatus targets various enzymes and signaling proteins, most notably cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, lipoxygenase (LOX), and specific inflammatory cytokines like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha). The plant contains bioactive secondary metabolites such as triterpenoids and flavonoids that act as inhibitors of these inflammatory mediators. By modulating the COX and LOX pathways, its constituents effectively reduce the production of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins and leukotrienes. These molecular interactions contribute to the plant's observed anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties in pharmacological studies. Additionally, certain extracts show an ability to interact with oxidative stress-related proteins, helping to mitigate cellular damage.

    What are the herbal preparations of Celastrus paniculatus?

    Celastrus paniculatus is prepared primarily as a decoction, infusion, or topical paste derived from its bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for medicinal purposes. Leaves can be steeped as a tea to extract soluble compounds for internal use. Additionally, the plant material is frequently crushed into a fine paste and applied directly to the skin to treat localized ailments. These various forms allow for both systemic absorption and direct external application of the plant's active constituents.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Celastrus paniculatus can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, and potential toxicity if any part of the plant is ingested. The berries and bark contain compounds that may lead to vomiting, diarrhea, or abdominal pain upon consumption. Contact with the plant's sap or foliage can trigger allergic reactions or dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Because the specific chemical makeup is not fully documented for human safety, ingestion poses a significant risk of poisoning. Caution should always be exercised when handling this species to avoid accidental exposure.
