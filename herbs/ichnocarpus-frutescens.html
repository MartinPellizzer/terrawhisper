Ichnocarpus frutescens

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ichnocarpus frutescens has a woody, shrubby growth habit characterized by erect, branched stems and small, elliptical to lanceolate leaves. The foliage is typically arranged in an opposite or sub-opposite pattern along the branches. Its flowers are relatively inconspicuous and emerge from the leaf axils during the blooming season. The plant produces small, fleshy fruits that serve as a primary reproductive structure. These morphological traits collectively define its structure as a perennial shrub within its specific habitat.

      What are the herbal preparations of Ichnocarpus frutescens?

      Ichnocarpus frutescens is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the intended therapeutic application. The woody stems and leaves are often simmered in water to create a concentrated decoction for internal use. For milder systemic effects, a hot water infusion allows for the extraction of delicate volatile compounds. When treating skin conditions, the crushed plant material is applied directly as a moist poultice to the affected area. Some traditional practices also involve using the plant in alcohol-based tinctures for long-term storage.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Ichnocarpus frutescens can cause severe skin irritation, localized dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap or foliage often results in redness, itching, and painful blistering on the affected area. If the plant is consumed accidentally, individuals may experience nausea, stomach cramping, or vomiting. These adverse reactions are primarily triggered by the chemical compounds present within the plant's tissues. It is essential to avoid direct skin contact and ensure the plant is never eaten to prevent these complications.
