Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Clove, or Eugenia caryophyllata, is a small evergreen tree native to the Maluku Islands of Indonesia, widely recognized for its aromatic buds used in both culinary and medicinal applications.

What is Clove (Eugenia Caryophyllata)?

Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) is a small, aromatic spice derived from the dried flower buds of the clove tree, native to the Maluku Islands in Indonesia.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Clove is Eugenia caryophyllata. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Myrtaceae, genus Eugenia, and species Eugenia caryophyllata. It is commonly known as clove in Southeast Asia, particularly in Indonesia and India, and as Syzygium aromaticum in parts of Australia and the Pacific Islands. The plant has several synonyms, including Syzygium aromaticum, which is the botanical synonym, and regional names such as kari in India and lal kari in Nepal.

Plant Description and Morphology

Clove is a small evergreen tree or shrub that typically grows to a height of 8-12 meters, with a dense, bushy crown and smooth, dark gray bark.

Its leaves are alternate, elliptic to ovate in shape, with a glossy dark green upper surface and a lighter green underside. The flowers are small, fragrant, and clustered in spikes, with white petals and a prominent yellow stamen that gives off a strong, spicy aroma. The root system is fibrous and shallow, adapting to the tropical climate and allowing the plant to absorb nutrients efficiently.

The buds, which are harvested before full bloom, are reddish-brown, have a rough texture, and emit a sharp, aromatic scent.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Clove is native to the Maluku Islands, also known as the Spice Islands, in Indonesia.

It thrives in tropical climates with high humidity and warm temperatures, typically between 20°C and 30°C. The plant prefers well-drained, fertile soil and full sunlight. It is commonly found in coastal areas and lowland forests where the climate remains consistently humid.

Clove trees are adapted to specific ecological conditions, including periodic dry seasons and seasonal rainfall patterns.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The primary plant parts used medicinally in clove are the dried flower buds, leaves, and essential oil derived from the buds.

The buds, which are the most commonly used part, are harvested when they are nearly mature and then dried for use. The leaves are also utilized, though less frequently, in traditional medicine for their aromatic and antiseptic properties. Essential oil extracted from the buds is a concentrated form of the plant's active compounds.

These parts are valued for their therapeutic properties, including antimicrobial, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory effects.

Phytochemical Composition

Clove contains a variety of active compounds, including eugenol, which is the primary aromatic component responsible for its medicinal properties.

Secondary metabolites such as flavonoids and terpenoids contribute to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Eugenol is also a key chemical marker used in the identification and quality control of clove products. Other chemical markers include cineole and camphor, which are commonly analyzed in phytochemical profiling.

These compounds collectively define the therapeutic potential of clove in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that underpin its use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include antimicrobial, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory effects, making it valuable for treating infections and pain. The biological activity of clove is primarily attributed to its essential oils, particularly eugenol, which interacts with various cellular targets. Pharmacodynamically, eugenol acts as a local anesthetic and antiseptic by inhibiting nerve conduction and reducing microbial growth.

System-level effects of clove include gastrointestinal protection and potential modulation of the immune system.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) involve multiple biological processes that contribute to its therapeutic effects.

At the cellular level, clove compounds such as eugenol and caryophyllin exhibit antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties by interacting with cell membranes and modulating intracellular signaling. Biochemically, these compounds inhibit key enzymes like cyclooxygenase and lipoxygenase, which are central to inflammatory responses. They also interfere with the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, reducing oxidative stress and tissue damage.

The target systems include the respiratory, digestive, and nervous systems, where clove exerts its antimicrobial, analgesic, and antispasmodic effects.

Therapeutic Uses

Therapeutic Uses Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) is widely used in herbal medicine for its antimicrobial, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory properties.

It is commonly employed to treat digestive issues such as indigestion, flatulence, and nausea. Clove oil is often used to alleviate toothache and gum inflammation due to its high eugenol content. It may also help in managing respiratory conditions like coughs and sore throats.

Additionally, clove is used to address skin infections and fungal disorders due to its antifungal and antibacterial effects.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Preparation methods for Clove involve various forms in which the plant is utilized.

The most common forms include whole cloves, ground cloves, essential oils, and clove oil. Whole cloves are often used whole in cooking or traditional remedies. Ground cloves are made by grinding dried flower buds into a fine powder.

Essential oils are extracted through distillation or cold pressing of the flower buds.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of clove for most herbal applications is typically 0.3 to 0.6 grams of whole dried flower buds per day.

For extracts, a concentration of 1 to 2% essential oil is commonly used, with a daily dose not exceeding 0.1 to 0.2 milliliters. Clove is usually taken 2 to 3 times per day, with each dose spaced evenly throughout the day. The recommended duration of use is generally up to 4 weeks, unless otherwise directed by a healthcare professional.

Prolonged or excessive use can lead to adverse effects, so it is important to follow these guidelines carefully.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in moderation, clove is generally considered safe for most people, but it can cause side effects such as oral irritation, gastrointestinal discomfort, and allergic reactions in some individuals.

High doses may lead to nausea, vomiting, or even toxicity due to its high eugenol content. Clove should be avoided by individuals with known allergies to related plants in the Myrtaceae family. It is not recommended for use during pregnancy due to potential risks to fetal development.

Additionally, clove may interact with certain medications, so it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use.

Drug Interactions

Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, and drugs metabolized by the liver, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or altering drug metabolism due to its high content of eugenol and other bioactive compounds.

Toxicity and Precautions

Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) is generally considered safe when used in typical culinary or medicinal amounts, but it may cause toxicity if consumed in large quantities or by individuals with specific health conditions such as gastrointestinal ulcers, diabetes, or those taking blood-thinning medications.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) is cultivated primarily in tropical regions such as Indonesia, Madagascar, and Zanzibar, where it thrives in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil, and is harvested by hand when the fruit is fully mature, typically between October and December, with the dried flower buds being the primary part used in herbal medicine.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) involve harvesting the dried flower buds, which are then ground or distilled to obtain essential oils and other active compounds used in herbal medicine.

Storage and Shelf Life

Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dry, and dark place to maintain its potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly stored.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) has demonstrated its potential pharmacological activities, including antimicrobial, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties, with studies supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine for digestive and respiratory ailments.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Clove has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its antiseptic, analgesic, and carminative properties, with historical applications ranging from dental care and digestive aid to respiratory and skin treatments.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata) is classified as a food spice and is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by regulatory agencies, though its use in medicinal contexts may require specific licensing or oversight depending on the region.
