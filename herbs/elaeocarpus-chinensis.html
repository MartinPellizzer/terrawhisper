Elaeocarpus chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeocarpus chinensis?

Elaeocarpus chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Oxalidales. This plant is a member of the family Elaeocarpaceae and is situated within the genus Elaeocarpus, ultimately being identified by its specific species name, chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Elaeocarpus chinensis has an evergreen habit characterized by spreading, elliptical leaves that measure up to 15 centimeters in length and possess a glossy, dark green upper surface. The branchlets are typically slender and can exhibit a slightly quadrangular shape in younger growth. Small, creamy white flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in small clusters. These flowers produce fleshy, globose drupes that transition from green to a deep reddish-brown color upon maturity. The woody stem structure supports a dense canopy, making the plant a prominent feature in subtropical landscapes.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across southern China, Taiwan, Vietnam, Laos, Thailand, and Malaysia. Its natural range extends from subtropical coastal regions to more inland tropical environments. It is frequently found growing in evergreen forests and along riverbanks where moisture is abundant. Many specimens have also been introduced to other tropical regions worldwide for ornamental purposes in urban landscaping. The species typically thrives in warm climates that do not experience harsh freezing temperatures.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds elaeocarpucin A, elaeocarpucin B, elaeocarpucin C, elaeocarpucin D, and elaeocarpucin E. These unique chemical constituents belong to a class of bioactive molecules often studied for their potential therapeutic properties. Researchers examine these specific derivatives to understand how they interact with various biological systems. The structural diversity among these five compounds allows for a wide range of pharmacological investigations. Each derivative may contribute differently to the overall medicinal profile of the plant species.

As discussed in a study, eight new 16,23-epoxycucurbitacin derivatives designated as elaeocarpucins A-H (1-8) and five known cucurbitacins (9-13) were isolated from the methanol extracts of the fruits and stem bark of Elaeocarpus chinensis. The isolation of these 13 compounds was facilitated using a LC/MS dereplication procedure and monitored using HT-29 human cancer cells. The structures of compounds 1-8 were determined using spectroscopic data, and the absolute configurations of isomers 1 and 2 were established by the Mosher ester method. Compounds 1-13 were evaluated in vitro against the HT-29 cell line and through a mitochondrial transmembrane potential assay. Elaeocarpucin C (3), produced by partial synthesis from 16α,23α-epoxy-3β,20β-dihydroxy-10αH,23βH-cucurbit-5,24-dien-11-one (13), was found to be inactive in an in vivo hollow fiber assay using three different cancer cell types at a dose range of 0.5-10 mg/kg/day.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and localized swelling if the sap comes into direct contact with human tissue. The chemical compounds present in the latex may trigger redness, itching, or painful rashes upon exposure. Some individuals might also experience more systemic reactions if the plant material is ingested accidentally. Protective clothing is highly recommended when handling any part of the tree to prevent accidental contact. Careful observation is necessary if someone develops a persistent or spreading rash after touching the foliage or bark.
