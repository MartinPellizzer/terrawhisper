Elaeocarpus chinensis

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Elaeocarpus chinensis has an evergreen habit characterized by spreading, elliptical leaves that measure up to 15 centimeters in length and possess a glossy, dark green upper surface. The branchlets are typically slender and can exhibit a slightly quadrangular shape in younger growth. Small, creamy white flowers emerge from the leaf axils, often appearing in small clusters. These flowers produce fleshy, globose drupes that transition from green to a deep reddish-brown color upon maturity. The woody stem structure supports a dense canopy, making the plant a prominent feature in subtropical landscapes.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across southern China, Taiwan, Vietnam, Laos, Thailand, and Malaysia. Its natural range extends from subtropical coastal regions to more inland tropical environments. It is frequently found growing in evergreen forests and along riverbanks where moisture is abundant. Many specimens have also been introduced to other tropical regions worldwide for ornamental purposes in urban landscaping. The species typically thrives in warm climates that do not experience harsh freezing temperatures.

    What are the herbal preparations of Elaeocarpus chinensis?

    Elaeocarpus chinensis is prepared as a decoction of the bark, a powder from the dried fruit, or a topical paste made from crushed leaves. The bark is most commonly boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for treating internal ailments. Practitioners often grind the dried seeds or fruits into a fine powder to be encapsulated or mixed with honey. For external applications, the fresh leaves are macerated to form a poultice for skin irritations. These various methods allow the plant's active compounds to be administered according to the specific therapeutic need.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Elaeocarpus chinensis can cause skin irritation, contact dermatitis, and potential allergic reactions upon direct contact with its sap or foliage. The milky latex found within the plant is known to be caustic to sensitive tissues. Individuals handling the plant without protection may experience redness, itching, or swelling at the site of contact. If the sap accidentally enters the eyes, it can lead to severe stinging and inflammation. Precautions should always be taken to avoid skin contact to prevent these adverse dermatological responses.
