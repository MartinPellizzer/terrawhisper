Viburnum cylindricum

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum cylindricum?

Viburnum cylindricum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is organized under the order Dipsacales and falls within the family Viburnaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Viburnum, specifically categorized as the species cylindricum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum cylindricum has an arching, shrubby growth habit characterized by opposite, ovate leaves with serrated margins and a woody, multi-stemmed structure. The foliage typically features prominent pinnate venation and a texture that can vary from smooth to slightly rugose. During the flowering season, the plant produces dense, cylindrical clusters of small, white, five-petaled flowers. These inflorescences transition into fleshy, drupaceous fruits that change color as they ripen. The bark on older stems becomes textured and provides a sturdy framework for the spreading canopy.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viburnum cylindricum?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its specific ureaase inhibitory activity and α-glucosidase inhibitory activity. These biochemical properties suggest that extracts from Viburnum cylindricum may play a role in managing metabolic disorders such as diabetes. By inhibiting α-glucosidase, the plant can help regulate blood glucose levels after meals. Furthermore, its ability to inhibit ureaase offers possibilities for treating conditions related to gastric ulcers caused by urea-splitting bacteria. These enzymatic inhibitory effects highlight the medicinal value of the species in modern pharmacology.

According to a study published by "Chemistry & biodiversity", Viburnum cylindricum leaves contain 14 aryltetralin lignan glycosides, which include three previously undescribed compounds called viburindrosides A-C (1-3) and two rare variable 9-O-2'-aryltetralin lignan glycosides (13 and 14). Regarding enzyme inhibitory activity, compounds 13 and 14 exhibited the strongest α-glucosidase inhibition with IC50 values of 7.69 and 7.25 µM, respectively. Additionally, compounds 5 and 7 demonstrated potent urease inhibitory activity with IC50 values of 16.01 and 15.23 µM, respectively. Molecular docking studies were further utilized to provide insights into the binding interactions of these 4 active compounds with α-glucosidase and urease.
