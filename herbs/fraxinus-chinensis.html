Fraxinus chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Fraxinus chinensis?

Fraxinus chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant falls under the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Oleaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic designation is defined by the genus Fraxinus and the species chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Fraxinus chinensis has pinnately compound leaves consisting of seven to thirteen lanceolate leaflets that are arranged oppositely along the rachis. The tree typically reaches a height of 10 to 20 meters with a spreading crown and grayish-brown bark that develops shallow longitudinal fissures with age. Its small, greenish-yellow flowers are borne in terminal panicles and are primarily wind-pollinated. During the fruiting season, the plant produces clusters of winged samaras that facilitate seed dispersal through the air. The woody twigs are slender and often feature small lenticels on their surface.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across central and southern China, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. It thrives primarily in temperate climates within deciduous forests and woodland habitats. The species is often found at varying elevations ranging from lowlands to mountainous regions. Its range covers diverse landscapes where moist, well-drained soils are present. Localized populations may also appear in specific ecological niches across its broad eastern distribution.

What are the pharmacological activities of Fraxinus chinensis?

This plant has pharmacological activities including estrogenic activity, antioxidant properties, anti-inflammatory effects, and antimicrobial actions. The presence of specific flavonoids and lignans within the bark and leaves contributes significantly to its ability to mimic or modulate estrogenic responses in biological systems. These bioactive compounds also help neutralize free radicals, which protects cells from oxidative damage. Furthermore, the plant extracts demonstrate a capacity to inhibit inflammatory mediators and suppress the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Such diverse properties make it a subject of interest for traditional medicine and modern pharmaceutical research.

The study utilized the recombinant yeast cell W303-1A/hER-ERE-Lac Z to determine these estrogenic compounds. While heat-clearing drugs reached a concentration of 6.35 x 10(-3) nmol x g(-1) E2 equivalent, the presence of phytoestrogens in ash bark was noted as being relatively high.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds aesculin, esculetiin, escuin, fraxin, and various coumarins. These specific chemical constituents are primarily found within the bark and seeds of the specimen. Aesculin and esculetiin are known for their distinct pharmacological properties in traditional medicine. The presence of escuin and fraxin further characterizes the complex biochemical profile of the plant's tissues. Additionally, the various coumarins contribute to the overall therapeutic potential of these organic substances.

As discussed in a study, Fraxinus chinensis contains four main coumarins that were selected as analytes for quality evaluation. The relative correlation factors at 340 nm for aesculin to asculetin, fraxin, and fraxeetin were determined to be 1.771, 0.799, and 1.409, respectively. The study utilized these values to calculate the contents of the three coumarins based on the determined aesculin levels. Within a certain range, these relative correlation factors demonstrated good reproducibility with an RSD of 2.5%-3.9%. This quantitative analysis method was validated to evaluate the quality of Fraxini Cortex and its Yinpian.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic skin reactions, gastrointestinal distress, and potential interference with certain medications when ingested. While specific clinical data on human toxicity is limited, contact with the bark or leaves may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals. Some users have reported nausea or stomach discomfort following internal consumption. There is also a theoretical risk of drug interactions due to the presence of bioactive compounds that might affect liver enzymes. It is essential to consult a medical professional before using any part of this species for medicinal purposes.
