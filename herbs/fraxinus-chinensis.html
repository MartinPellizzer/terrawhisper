Fraxinus chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Fraxinus chinensis?

Fraxinus chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant falls under the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Oleaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic designation is defined by the genus Fraxinus and the species chinensis.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Fraxinus chinensis contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is frequently harvested to create decoctions used in traditional practices to treat various inflammatory conditions. Extracts from the leaves are also utilized for their potential therapeutic effects on the body. Additionally, the roots serve as a significant source of bioactive constituents in several herbal formulations. These various components are often processed together to enhance their specific healing properties.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including flavonoids, terpenoids, phenolic acids, triterpenoids, and steroids.

These chemical constituents contribute to the various pharmacological activities attributed to the species. Phenolic acids and flavonoids are known for providing significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of terpenoids and triterpenoids often plays a role in the plant's antimicrobial and cytotoxic potential.

Additionally, the steroids found within the plant contribute to its complex chemical profile and therapeutic versatility.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Flavonoids
  • Terpenoids
  • Phenolic acids
  • Triterpenoids
  • Steroids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Fraxinus chinensis targets inflammatory mediators such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha, interleukin-6, and cyclooxygenase-2 to exert its medicinal effects. Its bioactive compounds, primarily secoiridoids and flavonoids, inhibit the activation of nuclear factor-kappa B to suppress various pro-inflammatory signaling pathways. These molecules also interact with nitric oxide synthase to regulate oxidative stress and vascular functions within the body. Additionally, specific polyphenols from the plant demonstrate an ability to modulate matrix metalloproteinases, which aids in tissue protection and anti-inflammatory responses. This multi-target approach allows the plant to address complex pathologies involving both immune dysregulation and oxidative damage.

What are the therapeutic actions of Fraxinus chinensis?

Fraxinus chinensis has therapeutic properties that primarily include anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic actions used to treat pain and fever. The plant's bark and leaves contain bioactive compounds that help reduce swelling and systemic inflammation in the body. These medicinal components are often utilized in traditional practices to alleviate symptoms of rheumatism and joint discomfort. By modulating immune responses, the extract can assist in managing various inflammatory conditions. Its ability to lower body temperature makes it a valuable resource for addressing febrile states.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions, skin ailments, and gastrointestinal disorders such as diarrhea and dysentery. It is frequently employed in traditional medicine to alleviate symptoms of rheumatism and joint pain. The bark and leaves are often prepared as decoctions to reduce swelling and treat skin infections or itching. Some practitioners also utilize the plant to help manage fever and digestive disturbances. Its medicinal properties are primarily attributed to its ability to reduce inflammation throughout the body.

What are the herbal preparations of Fraxinus chinensis?

Fraxinus chinensis is prepared primarily as a decoction using the dried bark, roots, or twigs to extract its medicinal compounds. Practitioners often boil these plant parts in water for an extended period to create a concentrated liquid tea. Some traditional methods also involve using the plant material in topical poultices or medicinal washes for external applications. In certain regional practices, the processed bark may be incorporated into alcoholic tinctures to enhance the solubility of its active constituents. These various methods ensure that the therapeutic properties of the species are effectively delivered to the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Fraxinus chinensis can cause allergic skin reactions, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if consumed in incorrect dosages or preparations. While often used in traditional practices, individuals may experience contact dermatitis or rashes upon direct skin exposure to the plant materials. Ingesting parts of the plant without proper processing may lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal discomfort. There is also a lack of extensive clinical data regarding long-term systemic effects in humans. Consequently, caution is necessary to avoid unpredictable adverse reactions.
