What's the taxonomical classification of Oplopanax elatus?

Oplopanax elatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Apiales and falls within the family Araliaceae. The specific classification of this plant is defined by the genus Oplopanax and the species elatus.

What are the pharmacological activities of Oplopanax elatus?

This plant has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, and anti-diabetic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. Research indicates that these properties contribute significantly to its traditional use in treating various ailments. Specific secondary metabolites within the species are primarily responsible for these diverse therapeutic effects. Studies continue to explore how these mechanisms can be applied to modern medical treatments.

As discussed in a study, Oplopanax elaatus adventitious roots demonstrate high antioxidant activity when treated with 100 μM salicylic acid for 4 days starting on day 35. Under these conditions, the plant reaches total flavonoid and phenolic contents of 387 rutin mg/g DW and 128 gallic acid mg/g DW, which are significantly higher (p < 0.05) than the control. The antioxidant capacity is further evidenced by increased DPPH scavenging, ABTS+ scavenging, and Fe2+ chelating rates. The EC50 values for these activities were recorded at 0.0117, 0.61, and 3.34 mg/L, respectively. These findings show that salicylic acid improves the production of flavonoids and phenolics in the culture.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds betulinc, cirensenoside Q, cirensenoside R, lupeol, and oleanolic acid. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the diverse biological activities often associated with the species. Betulin and lupeol are well-known triterpenoids that frequently demonstrate anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of cirensenoside Q and cirensenoside R provides unique glycoside profiles that may assist in various therapeutic applications. Furthermore, oleanolic acid is a significant component recognized for its potential antioxidant and protective effects on cellular health.

As discussed in a study, the endangered medicinal plant Oplopanax elatus contains pharmacologically important triterpenoids. Quantitative HPLC analysis showed that the contents of lupeol, oleanolic acid, and betulin were markedly higher in regenerated plantlets cultured for 8 W than in root tissues at 0 W. Among the oxidosqualene cyclases, the beta-amyrin synthase (Gene_22342T) showed threefold higher expression at 8 W compared to 0 W. Additionally, the lupeol synthase (Gene_05624T) exhibited 30-fold higher expression at the 8 W stage. These findings provide evidence for the accumulation of triterpenoids in regenerated O. elatus tissues.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating LPS-induced inflammation, acute lung injury, cardiovascular disease, neurasthenia, and schizophrenia. Researchers have investigated its potential to mitigate systemic inflammatory responses triggered by lipopolysaccharides. It also shows promise in reducing the severity of damage associated with acute lung injury. Furthermore, the plant's bioactive compounds may offer protective effects against various forms of cardiovascular disease. Clinical interest extends to its ability to manage neurological and psychological conditions such as neurasthenia and schizophrenia.

As discussed in a study, adventitious root crude extract of Oplopanax elatus at 200 mg/kg decreased cell infiltration in lungs and reduced the production of nitric oxide, TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β in LPS-stimulated mice. In vitro experiments using ethyl acetate fractions at 12.5, 25, and 50 μg/mL obviously inhibited the production of NO, prostaglandin E2, TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β in LPS-induced peritoneal macrophages (p < 0.05). These EtOAc fractions also suppressed the expression of inducible nitric oxide synthase and cyclooxygenase. The anti-inflammatory mechanism involved inhibiting the phosphorylation of extracellular regulated protein kinases, c-jun n-terminal kinase, and p38 MAPK. Furthermore, the EtOAc fractions from Oplopanax elatus ARs enhanced inhibitor of nuclear factor-kappa B-α expression and decreased nuclear translocation of p65 NF-κB.
