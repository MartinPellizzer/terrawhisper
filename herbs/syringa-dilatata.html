What's the taxonomical classification of Syringa dilatata?

Syringa dilatata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls into the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As a member of the order Lamiales, it is further nested within the family Oleaceae. The plant is identified by the genus Syringa and is specifically categorized under the species name dilatata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Its inflorescences consist of large, terminal panicles containing numerous small, tubular flowers that can range in color from white to pale lilac. The stems are woody and develop a greyish bark as the shrub matures into a larger spreading form. This plant typically exhibits a multi-stemmed growth habit that creates a dense, rounded shrubbery.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains oleoside-type secoiridois, secoiridois, and syringoleosides A-H as its primary medicinal compounds. These specific chemical constituents are known to contribute to the biological activities of the species. The presence of secoiridois often links the plant to various pharmacological properties in traditional medicine. Furthermore, the syringoleosides A-H group represents a unique set of glycosides found within its chemical profile. Together, these compounds form a complex phytochemical makeup that researchers study for potential therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, repeated column chromatography of Syringa dilatata flowers led to the isolation of eight new oleoside-type secoiridoiids, syringoleosides A-H (1-8), and five known secoiridoiids (9-13). The major secoiridoiids in the flowers include compounds 1, 4, 5, 8, 9, 12, and 13. Compounds 1, 2, 6, 7, 11, and 13 demonstrated suppression effects on NO production in LPS-induced RAW 264.7 cells. These specific compounds showed IC50 values ranging from 32.5 ± 9.8 to 65.7 ± 11.0 μM with no visible toxicity.
