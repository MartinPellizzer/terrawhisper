Botanical Identification of Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

To identify Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Yarrow, or Achillea millefolium, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically the division of flowering plants, class of eudicots, order of asterids, family of sunflowers, genus of Achillea, and species of millefolium. This classification places it within the broader group of flowering plants known for their composite flower heads.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Yarrow is Achillea millefolium, first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. It has several synonyms, including Achillea vulgaris and Achillea millefolium subsp. millefolium. Common names include yarrow, nosebleed plant, and thousand-leaf. Regional and historical names vary, with some cultures referring to it as "achillea" after the legendary warrior Achilles.

Morphological Characteristics

Yarrow is a perennial herb with a creeping rhizomatous root system. It has erect, branching stems that are usually hairless. Its leaves are pinnately divided into fine, feather-like segments. The plant bears flat-topped clusters of small, white or pale yellow flowers. The fruits are small, dry, and contain a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Yarrow can be distinguished by its finely divided, fern-like leaves and flat, clustered flower heads. It has a strong, aromatic odor and a rough, hairy stem. It is often confused with other members of the Asteraceae family, but its leaf structure and scent are key identifiers. The presence of white, rayless flowers also helps in differentiation.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Yarrow is native to temperate regions of Europe, western Asia, and North America. It thrives in sunny, well-drained soils and prefers moderate climates. Found in meadows, roadsides, and dry fields, it typically grows between 300 and 2,000 meters in elevation.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Yarrow blooms from late spring to early autumn, typically between June and September, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to October. For identification purposes, the most accurate harvest time is during the flowering period, when the plant is most identifiable.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Yarrow belongs to the Asteraceae family and is closely related to other genera like Arnica and Chamomilla. It shares key family traits such as composite flowers and alternate leaves. Its closest species include A. ptarmica and A. filipendula, which are similarly structured and used in herbal medicine.
