The scientific name of the plant is Balanites aegyptiaca, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. Morphological Characteristics

Balanites aegyptiaca is a small to medium shrub or tree with a deep taproot system that allows it to thrive in arid conditions. Its stems are typically cylindrical and branch sparsely. Leaves are alternate, simple, and elliptic, with a thick, waxy cuticle to reduce water loss. The plant produces small, yellowish-green flowers in axillary clusters, followed by hard, spiny fruits containing one or two seeds.

Balanites aegyptiaca can be distinguished by its palmate leaves with 5-7 lobes, rough bark with fissures, and yellowish-orange fruits with a thick, leathery skin. The leaves have a distinctive waxy coating, and the flowers are yellow with five petals. These features help differentiate it from other similar species in the Balanites genus.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Balanites aegyptiaca is native to North Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and parts of the Middle East. It thrives in arid and semi-arid climates, preferring well-drained sandy or loamy soils. This plant typically grows at elevations between 0 and 1,500 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Balanites aegyptiaca blooms from March to May, producing yellow flowers. Seed formation occurs between June and August. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the dry season, when the fruit is fully mature and the seeds are hard and dry.
