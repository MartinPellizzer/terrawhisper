Scientific and Botanical Profile

Water Pennywort, with botanical name Menyanthes trifoliata, is a small aquatic or semi-aquatic perennial herb belonging to the family Geraniaceae.

Native to the temperate regions of Europe, Asia, and North America, it thrives in wetlands, marshes, and along the edges of ponds and streams. Morphologically, it features a creeping rhizome with dense clusters of small, white, star-shaped flowers that bloom in early spring, followed by three-lobed, trifoliate leaves that are broad and floating or submerged, depending on the water level. Common names for this plant include Water Snowflake, Water Shield, and Trifoliate Water Mint, reflecting its distinctive appearance and habitat.

Additionally, it is known by names such as Lenten Rose, Meadow Snowflake, and Marsh Parsnip, highlighting its ecological and cultural significance across various regions.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Menyanthes trifoliata has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, with various forms available including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion for topical applications, such as poultices or salves, to address skin conditions. Due to its potency, it is advised to use menyanthes trifoliata cautiously, with short durations and limited frequency to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Menyanthes trifoliata can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties, but it must be approached with caution due to its potential toxicity.

While some studies suggest it may have therapeutic benefits, it is important to note that it can cause gastrointestinal upset, nausea, and vomiting in some individuals. There are limited clinical trials on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but caution is advised when combining it with medications that affect the liver or kidneys. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using Menyanthes trifoliata due to the risk of harm to the fetus or infant, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, it is recommended to use Menyanthes trifoliata only under the guidance of a qualified healthcare professional, start with small doses, and discontinue use if adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Menyanthes trifoliata grows in moist, acidic soils along the edges of ponds, marshes, and streams, preferring full sun to partial shade and consistent moisture.

It thrives in well-drained but consistently damp environments, making it suitable for wetlands or bog gardens. To ensure healthy growth, regular watering is necessary, especially during dry periods, and mulching can help retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Harvesting should occur in late summer to early autumn when the leaves and flowers are most potent, using sharp scissors to cut the stems just above the base to encourage regrowth.

After harvesting, the plant parts should be dried in a cool, dark place with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers to preserve their medicinal potency and extend shelf life.
