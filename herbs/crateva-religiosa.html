Crateva religiosa

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crateva religiosa has a woody shrub or small tree structure characterized by spreading branches, elliptic to lanceolate leaves with entire margins, and fragrant, white, funnel-shaped flowers. The foliage is typically evergreen, displaying a glossy texture and a deep green color that provides a dense canopy. Its inflorescences emerge from the leaf axils, featuring five petals that form a distinct tube. The flowers are often accompanied by nectar-producing structures that attract various pollinators. Once pollinated, the plant produces small, fleshy drupes that serve as reproductive units.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically spanning from India and Sri Lanka through Indochina to the Indonesian archipelago. It thrives predominantly in low-to-mid elevation areas characterized by warm, humid climates. Within these regions, it is frequently found in open forests, scrublands, and along the edges of cultivated fields. The species is also widely distributed across parts of the Pacific islands due to human introduction and cultivation. This broad range allows it to occupy various ecological niches across much of the Old World tropics.

Additionally, the phytochemicals interact with cytokine signaling pathways to suppress the production of pro-inflammatory mediators. Through these diverse protein interactions, the plant demonstrates potent antimicrobial, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory activities.

What are the therapeutic actions of Crateva religiosa?

Cratevva religiosa has potent pharmacological properties including hallucinogen, anticholinergic, anticonvulsant, anxiolytic, and sedative effects. Furthermore, the plant exhibits anxiolytic and sedative qualities that may help calm the central nervous system. Some research also explores its potential anticonvulsant activity in managing neurological excitability.

Cratevva religiosa is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, pastes, and juice extracted from its leaves, bark, and flowers. What side-effects this plant can have?

Cratevva religiosa can cause adverse reactions such as skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested or applied improperly. Some individuals may experience localized redness or itching upon contact with the plant's sap or foliage. Ingestion of certain parts of the plant could potentially lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping. While many people use it for traditional purposes, the lack of extensive clinical data means individual sensitivities can vary significantly.
