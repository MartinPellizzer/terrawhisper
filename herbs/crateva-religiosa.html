Crateva religiosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Crateva religiosa?

Crateva religiosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Brassicales and is a member of the family Capparaceae. Ultimately, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Crateva, and its species designation, religiosa.

What are the therapeutic actions of Crateva religiosa?

Cratevva religiosa has medicinal properties that include antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory activities used to treat various ailments. What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat respiratory ailments such as asthma, bronchitis, and coughs, as well as skin infections, inflammation, and digestive disorders. The bark and leaves often serve as a primary component in traditional remedies for managing fever and malaria symptoms. Some practitioners also utilize extracts from the plant to address wounds and various types of dermatological conditions. In certain cultures, it is applied topically to soothe skin irritation and promote healing. These medicinal properties are highly valued in various folk medicine practices across different regions.

What are the herbal preparations of Crateva religiosa?

Cratevva religiosa is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, pastes, and juice extracted from its leaves, bark, and flowers. The leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for treating various ailments. Bark extracts are often processed into decoctions to harness their astringent properties. Additionally, the plant material can be ground into a fine powder for topical applications or mixed with other substances to form a paste. These different forms allow for both internal consumption and external healing rituals.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Cratevva religiosa can cause adverse reactions such as skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested or applied improperly. Some individuals may experience localized redness or itching upon contact with the plant's sap or foliage. Ingestion of certain parts of the plant could potentially lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping. While many people use it for traditional purposes, the lack of extensive clinical data means individual sensitivities can vary significantly. Always exercise caution and consult a professional before using any botanical species for medicinal intent.
