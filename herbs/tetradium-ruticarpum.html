Tetradium ruticarpum

What's the taxonomical classification of Tetradium ruticarpum?

Tetradium ruticarpum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant further falls under the order Sapindales and belongs to the family Rutaceae. Finally, its specific biological identification is defined by the genus Tetradium and the species ruticarpum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tetradium ruticarpum has opposite, simple leaves that are ovate to elliptical in shape with serrated margins and a distinctively long, tapering tip. The foliage typically possesses a smooth texture and a vibrant green color that can transition during seasonal changes. Its woody stems are slender and branch frequently, forming a dense, shrubby growth habit. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in clusters, eventually developing into fleshy, berry-like drupes that serve as a key identifying feature. These fruits are often reddish or dark in color and are held on short pedicels along the branches.

What are the pharmacological activities of Tetradium ruticarpum?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antitumor, and hepatoprotective pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to reduce pain perception and mitigate inflammatory responses in biological systems. Research indicates that certain extracts also demonstrate significant potential in inhibiting the growth of malignant cells. Furthermore, the plant provides protective effects for liver functions against chemical-induced damage. These diverse biological properties suggest that the species holds considerable value for medicinal development.

As discussed in a study, the dried and nearly ripe fruits of Tetradium ruticarpum are used for treating headache and gastrointestinal disorders. The plant's volatile oil, aqueous extract, and ethanol extract were tested for analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects. While the aqueous extract showed higher toxicity in LD50 and LD5 tests, the use of Tetradium ruticarpum is safe under recommended doses. Processing significantly enhances the analgesic and anti-inflammatory efficacy of the volatile oil and ethanol extract. Furthermore, the processing procedure effectively decreases the acute toxicity in all 3 types of extracts. Potential efficacy is linked to indole and quinolone type alkaloids, limonoids, amides, and 18β-glycyrrhizic acid.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 18β-glycyrrhizic acid, berberine, evodiamine, paeoniflorin, and rutaecarpine. These bioactive molecules are responsible for various pharmacological effects within the human body. For instance, berberine and evodiamine are frequently studied for their potential antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, compounds like paeoniflorin and rutaecarpine contribute to the plant's complex chemical profile used in traditional medicine. The presence of 18β-glycyrrhizic acid further enhances its therapeutic utility in treating diverse ailments.

As discussed in a study, the dried and nearly ripe fruits of Tetradium ruticarpum contain several potential medicinal compounds. Indole and quinolone type alkaloids, limonoids, amides, and 18β-glycyrrhizetinic acid were identified as the potential main contributors of the plant's efficacy. In contrast, hydroxy or acetoxy limonoid derivatives and coumarins could be the major causes of toxicity. The study utilized LD50 and LD5 tests to evaluate the acute toxicity of the volatile oil, aqueous extract, and ethanol extract. Analysis of these extracts was performed using gas chromatography-mass spectrometry and ultra-performance liquid chromatography-quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating gastrointestinal disorders, pain, headache, menorrhagia, and aphtha. Its medicinal properties are often applied to soothe digestive inflammation and discomfort. It also serves as a remedy for alleviating various types of physical pain and persistent headaches. Additionally, the plant is utilized to manage heavy menstrual bleeding known as menorrhagia. Finally, it can be used to help heal mouth sores or aphtha.

As discussed in a study, the dried and nearly ripe fruits of Tetradium ruticarpum (TR) have long been used in treating headache and gastrointestinal disorders in oriental medicine. TR is usually processed by stir-frying with licorice extract before use to reduce toxicity and improve efficacy. Testing of the volatile oil, aqueous extract, and ethanol extract through LD50 and LD5 tests indicated that while the aqueous extract has higher toxicity, the use of TR is safe under the recommended doses. The processing procedure effectively decreases the toxicity of all 3 extracts, with the largest decrease occurring in the volatile oil. Integrated analysis identified indole and quinolone type alkaloids, limonoids, amides, and 18β-glycyrrhizic acid as potential main contributors of TR's efficacy.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and redness upon contact with the sap or foliage. The toxic compounds within the sap often lead to a painful condition known as contact dermatitis. If the plant is ingested, it may result in significant gastrointestinal distress, including vomiting and abdominal pain. Accidental exposure to the eyes can lead to intense inflammation and potential temporary vision impairment. Care should be taken to avoid all physical contact to prevent these adverse reactions.
