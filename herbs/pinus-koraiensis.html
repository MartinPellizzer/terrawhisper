Pinus koraiensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Pinus koraiensis?

Pinus koraiensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Pinidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Pinales and the family Pinaceae, ultimately placing it within the genus Pinus. Specifically, this plant is identified by its unique species designation, koraiensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pinus koraiensis has needle-like leaves arranged in fascicles of two, woody cones measuring approximately 4 to 7 centimeters in length, and a bark that becomes deeply fissured with age. The needles are typically dark green and possess a relatively stiff texture compared to other pine species. Its cones are characterized by thick, woody scales that protect the developing seeds within. The tree develops a conical to irregular crown as it matures in its high-altitude habitat. This morphology allows the plant to withstand the harsh environmental conditions of its native range.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is endemic to the Korai Peninsula in the southern part of the Korean Peninsula. It grows specifically within the rocky coastal cliffs and steep slopes of this narrow landmass. The species is highly localized, meaning it cannot be found in any other natural habitats worldwide. Its range is restricted by the unique combination of maritime climate and rugged terrain characteristic of this region. Consequently, the total area occupied by this pine is quite small compared to more widespread coniferous species.

What are the pharmacological activities of Pinus koraiensis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its anticancer, antimicrobial, antitumor, antiviral, and cytotoxic activities. Researchers have identified specific bioactive compounds within Pinus koraienxis that effectively inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. The cytotoxic properties of its extracts allow for the targeted destruction of malignant cells during laboratory testing. Furthermore, the plant exhibits potent antitumor effects by suppressing the proliferation of abnormal cell clusters. These combined antiviral and medicinal qualities suggest that the species could serve as a valuable source for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, pinecones from Pinus koraiensis exhibit significant cytotoxic activity against four human lung cancer cell lines, specifically A549, H1264, H1299, and Calu-6. The water extract of these pinecones demonstrated IC50 values ranging from 0.62 to 1.73 mg/ml. This extract also induces apoptosis associated with caspase-3 activation in these same cancer cell lines. Chemical investigation of the water extract revealed 8 main components, which include dehydroabietic acid (1), 15-hydroxy-7-oxodehydroabietic acid (2), 7β,15-dihydroxydehydroabietic acid (3), β-d-glucopyranosyl labda-8(17,13)-diene-(15,16)-lactone-19-oate (4), 7α,15-dihydroxydehydroabietic acid (5), (+)-(1S,2S,4R)-limonene-1,2-diol (6), sobrerol (7), and 4-hydroxybenzoic acid (8). These findings suggest a novel biological application for Pinus koraiensis in combating human lung cancer.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds such as 12-hydroxyabietic acid, dehuroabietic acid, lambertianic acid, polyphenols, and sandaracopimaric acid. These specific diterpenoids and phenolic substances contribute to the biological activity of the species. The presence of sandaracopimaric acid and lambertianic acid is particularly noteworthy for their chemical properties. Additionally, the high concentration of polyphenols provides antioxidant benefits within the plant's chemical profile. Together, these diverse molecules form a complex medicinal composition.

As discussed in a study, the plant Pinus koraiensis contains several identified constituents. Eight specific compounds were identified through chromatographic methods and spectral analysis. These compounds include 8 (14)-podocarpen-13-on-18-oic acid (1), 15-hydroxydehydroabietic acid (2), 12-hydroxyabietic acid (3), lambertianic acid (4), dehydroabietic acid (5), sandaracopimaric acid (6), beta-sitosterol (7), and daucosterol (8). Notably, compounds 1 through 6 are isolated from this plant for the first time.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Escherichia coli infection, Klebsiella pneumoniae infection, Staphylococcus aureus infection, human immunodeficiency virus infection, and human lung cancer. The bioactive compounds found within Pinus koraiensis demonstrate significant antimicrobial properties against these specific bacterial pathogens. Research also indicates that the plant contains substances that may help combat the progression of human immunodeficiency virus infection. Furthermore, studies suggest that its chemical constituents possess potential anti-tumor activities relevant to human lung cancer management. These diverse therapeutic applications highlight the plant's importance in pharmacological research.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause localized skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and respiratory discomfort if its pollen or resins are inhaled or touched directly. While specific clinical data on human toxicity is limited, many pine species contain terpenes that may trigger hypersensitivity reactions in sensitive individuals. Contact with the sticky resin can lead to redness, itching, or swelling on the skin surface. Inhalation of fine particles from the needles or pollen might cause coughing or throat irritation in certain environments. Always exercise caution when handling the foliage to avoid these potential physiological responses.
