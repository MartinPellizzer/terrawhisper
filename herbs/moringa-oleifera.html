Moringa oleifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Moringa oleifera?

Moringa oleifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its higher-level classification, the plant is placed in the order Brassicales and belongs to the family Moringaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Moringa and the species name oleifera.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Moringa oleifera has a rapid-growing, tree-like structure characterized by a slender, whitish-grey trunk and tripinnate compound leaves consisting of small, oval-shaped leaflets. The tree typically reaches heights of up to ten meters and features a somewhat sparse, irregular crown. Its leaves are arranged alternately along the branches, displaying a bright green color that provides a delicate texture to the foliage. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from axillary clusters, eventually developing into long, slender green seed pods known as drumsticks. These pods contain numerous winged seeds that are essential for the plant's reproductive cycle.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the sub-Himalayan tracts of India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan, but it has become naturalized across many tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. It thrives in various environments ranging from semi-arid zones to humid tropical climates where temperatures remain warm year-round. Due to its ability to withstand drought, it is widely cultivated in many African countries and throughout Southeast Asia. Many farmers in these diverse geographical areas grow it specifically for its nutritional and medicinal benefits. Its widespread distribution is largely driven by its high adaptability to different soil types and varying levels of rainfall.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds moringin, niaziminin, kaempferol, quercetin, and isothiocyanates. These specific phytochemicals are recognized for their diverse biological activities and potential therapeutic benefits. The flavonoids kaempferol and quercetin are well-studied for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Furthermore, the presence of moringin and niaziminin contributes to the plant's complex chemical profile. The isothiocyanates within the plant also play a significant role in supporting various cellular defense mechanisms.

As discussed in a study, the plant Moringa oleifera contains the edible isothiocyanate moringin, also known as GMG-ITC, which is recognized for its chemopreventive and medicinal properties. This compound suppresses IL-3-induced expression of STAT5 target genes in the nanomolar range. Additionally, moringin functions as a potent inhibitor of TNF-induced NF-κB activity when used in the micromolar range. The research identifies STAT5 and, to a lesser extent, STAT1/STAT2 as novel targets of this compound. These biological activities support a beneficial role in the prevention of chronic illnesses such as cancer, inflammatory diseases, and immune disorders.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions such as nausea, diarrhea, stomach cramps, low blood sugar, low blood pressure, dizziness, and uterine contractions if the root or bark is consumed. Some individuals may experience digestive distress including nausea, diarrhea, and painful stomach cramps after ingestion. Consuming moringa may lead to hypoglycemia or hypotension, which can result in feelings of dizziness. Furthermore, the roots and bark of the plant contain substances that can trigger uterine contractions, posing a risk during pregnancy.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Nausea
  • Diarrhea
  • Uterine contractions (from root/bark)
  • Stomach cramps
  • Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia)
  • Low blood pressure (hypotension)
  • Dizziness
