Drynaria roosii

What's the taxonomical classification of Drynaria roosii?

Drynaria roosii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, its lineage continues through the subclass Polypodiinae and the order Polypodiales. It is a member of the family Polypodiaceae, specifically falling under the genus Drynaria, and is identified by its unique specific epithet, roosii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Drynaria roosii has woody, climbing stems that feature specialized tendrils for support and pinnate leaves with multiple pairs of leaflets. The leaves are typically arranged alternately along the vine and possess an elliptical to oblong shape. Each leaflet exhibits a smooth or slightly serrated margin and a distinct midrib that extends toward the apex. The reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into characteristic legume-like pods. These pods are often curved or twisted, containing the seeds necessary for the plant's dispersal.

What are the pharmacological activities of Drynaria roosii?

This plant has demonstrated significant cytotoxic and osteoprotective pharmacological activities through its diverse bioactive secondary metabolites. Researchers have identified that certain extracts from Drynaria roosii can inhibit the proliferation of specific cancer cell lines by inducing apoptosis. Beyond its potential in oncology, the plant exhibits properties that help protect bone tissue from degradation. These osteoprotective effects suggest that its chemical constituents may play a role in managing bone density or preventing bone loss. Such multifaceted biological actions highlight the therapeutic potential of this species in modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, the rhizomes of Drynaria roosii Nakaike contain five undescribed bis(lauric acid-12-yl)lignanoates known as liglaurates A-E. These compounds, which include the known methyl and glyceryl 12-caffeoyloxylaurates, exhibit remarkable cytotoxic activities against the HeLa cell line. Specifically, the IC50 values for liglaurates (+) -A, (-) -A, (+) -B, (-) -B, (±) -C, and (±) -D were recorded as 0.11 ± 0.02, 0.24 ± 0.01, 0.02 ± 0.00, 0.13 ± 0.02, 0.34 ± 0.07, and 0.17 ± 0.01 μM, respectively. Among these, liglaurates A-D were isolated as racemates.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including alkaloids, flavonoids, naringin, neoeriocitrin, and terpenoids. The alkaloids and terpenoids contribute to its complex chemical profile and potential therapeutic properties. Within the flavonoid group, the specific glycosides naringin and neoeriocitrin provide distinct biological activities. These bioactive substances work together to offer various health benefits through their unique molecular structures. The presence of these varied constituents makes the plant a significant subject for pharmacological study.

As discussed in a study, a total of 1409 components were detected and authenticated in Drynaria roosii. The associated ORs were found to contain relatively high levels of flavonoids, quinones, tannins, alkaloids, terpenoids, and vitamins. Regarding gene expression, 7236 differentially expressed genes were identified between the SLs and NLs. Within these genes, 491 were involved in developmental processes, 420 were related to stress response, and 337 were linked to responses to abiotic stimuli. The spatial distribution analysis further indicated that flavonoid components were dispersively distributed in both NRs and ORs.

    What side effects this plant can have?
