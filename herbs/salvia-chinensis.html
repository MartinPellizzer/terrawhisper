Salvia chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia chinensis?

Salvia chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Lamiales and is a prominent representative of the family Lamiaceae. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Salvia, with its unique identity defined by the specific epithet chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia chinensis has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, square-shaped stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are oppositely arranged, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and feature serrated margins with a textured, rugose surface. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal verticillasters that produce small, two-lipped flowers ranging in color from pale blue to violet. These blossoms are supported by bracts that often exhibit a distinct purplish hue. The plant develops via a rhizomatous root system that allows it to spread through garden beds.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically spanning across China, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. Within these regions, the species is often localized in mountainous areas or along riverbanks where moisture is consistent. What are the pharmacological activities of Salvia chinensis?

This plant has anticancer, antiproliferative, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities that make it a significant subject in medicinal research. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate a strong ability to induce cell death in various malignant cell lines through direct cytotoxicity. These substances effectively inhibit the growth and division of tumor cells, which is the core mechanism of its antiproliferative action. By disrupting the normal cell cycle, the plant helps prevent the rapid spread of cancer within biological systems. Such multifaceted biological effects suggest that Salvia chinensis holds great potential for the development of new chemotherapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, a polyphenolic enriched extract obtained from Salvia chinensis exhibits anticancer activity across 5 different cancer cell lines, including MCF-7 human breast cancer, A549 human lung cancer, HCT-116 and COLO 205 human colon cancer, and MiapaCa-2 human pancreatic cancer cells. This extract induced potent cytotoxicity, with the COLO 205 and MCF-7 cell lines being the most susceptible to treatment. In MiapaCa-2 human pancreatic cancer cells, the extract induced G0/G1 cell cycle arrest in a dose-dependent manner. Furthermore, the treatment caused a significant and concentration-dependent reduction in the mitochondrial membrane potential of these cells. Morphological features of apoptosis and the appearance of apoptotic bodies were also detected following treatment with the extract.
