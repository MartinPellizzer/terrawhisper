Salvia chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Salvia chinensis?

Salvia chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Lamiales and is a prominent representative of the family Lamiaceae. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Salvia, with its unique identity defined by the specific epithet chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Salvia chinensis has a perennial growth habit characterized by erect, square-shaped stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are oppositely arranged, ovate to lanceolate in shape, and feature serrated margins with a textured, rugose surface. Its inflorescence consists of dense, terminal verticillasters that produce small, two-lipped flowers ranging in color from pale blue to violet. These blossoms are supported by bracts that often exhibit a distinct purplish hue. The plant develops via a rhizomatous root system that allows it to spread through garden beds.

Within these regions, the species is often localized in mountainous areas or along riverbanks where moisture is consistent. What are the pharmacological activities of Salvia chinensis?

This plant has anticancer, antiproliferative, and cytotoxic pharmacological activities that demonstrate its potential for therapeutic applications in oncology. Research indicates that specific bioactive compounds within the plant can inhibit the growth of various cancer cell lines. These antiproliferative effects work by disrupting the normal cell cycle and preventing rapid cellular division. Furthermore, the observed cytotoxicity suggests that the extracts can induce programmed cell death in malignant cells. Such properties make Salvia chinensis a significant subject of study for developing new medicinal treatments.

According to a study published by "Journal of natural products", six new phenolic acid derivatives named salviachinensinines A-F (1-6) were isolated from the Chinese medicinal plant Salvia chinensis along with 14 known compounds (7-20). The investigation explored the antiproliferative property of these substances. Specifically, compound 1 demonstrated the ability to induce apoptosis and arrest cell cycle progression in MOLM-13 cells. The research also analyzed the solvent-induced isomerization of compounds 5 and 6 and the hypothetical biogenetic pathway of compounds 1-6.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment and management of various forms of cancer, specifically breast cancer, colon cancer, lung cancer, and pancreatic cancer. Researchers investigate its bioactive compounds to determine how they might inhibit the growth of malignant cells. These studies focus on the plant's potential to disrupt tumor progression and improve survival outcomes in patients. By examining its phytochemical properties, scientists aim to understand its efficacy against these specific oncological conditions. This ongoing research explores how the plant may serve as a supportive therapeutic agent in cancer care.

According to a study published by "Molecular medicine reports", a polyphenol-rich extract of Salvia chinensis exhibits anticancer activity in different cancer cell lines. This extract induces cell cycle arrest at the G0/G1-phase, apoptosis, and loss of mitochondrial membrane potential in pancreatic cancer cells. The extract demonstrated potent cytotoxicity in five cancer cell lines including MCF-7 human breast cancer cells, A549 human lung cancer cells, HCT-116 and COLO 205 human colon cancer cells, and MiaPaCa-2 human pancreatic cancer cells. Among these, the COLO 205 and MCF-7 cancer cell lines were the most susceptible to treatment with the extract. In pancreatic cancer cells, the extract induced G0/G1 cell cycle arrest and a significant reduction in mitochondrial membrane potential in a dose-dependent manner.
