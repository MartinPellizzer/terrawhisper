Huperzia selago

What's the taxonomical classification of Huperzia selago?

Huperzia selago belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Lycopodiidae, falling under the order Lycopodiales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Lycopodiaceae, situated within the genus Huperzia, and is specifically identified by the species name selago.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Huperzia selago has a creeping, branched rhizome that produces upright, dichotomously branched stems bearing small, lanceolate, sessile leaves arranged spirally. These leaves are typically dark green, leathery, and possess entire margins with acute tips. The plant lacks true roots, instead utilizing rhizoids to anchor itself to the substrate. Its reproductive structures consist of small, inconspicuous strobili located at the tips of the branches. The overall growth habit is compact and characteristic of a clubmoss.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Huperzia selago contains the alkaloid huperzine A primarily within its entire aerial portion, including the stems and leaves. This specific compound is extracted from the plant's vegetative tissues to serve as a potent acetylcholinesterase inhibitor. While the whole plant is utilized, the concentration of active medicinal components is found most densely in the green, leafy structures. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds huperzine A, huperzine B, 16-hydroxyhuperzine B, lycoposerramine-G, and glycopecurine. These alkaloids are primarily studied for their potent effects on the cholinergic system within the human brain. Huperzine A and huperzine B serve as significant acetylcholinesterase inhibitors that may support cognitive function. The presence of 16-hydroxyhuperzine B and lycoposerramine-G adds to the complex chemical profile of the species. Additionally, glycopecurine contributes to the unique pharmacological properties found within its tissues.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe toxicity characterized by nausea, vomiting, dizziness, muscle twitching, excessive salivation, diarrhea, abdominal cramps, confusion, seizures, and respiratory depression. These adverse reactions stem from the plant's ability to inhibit acetylcholinesterase, leading to an accumulation of acetylcholine in the nervous system. Such an overdose can disrupt basic bodily functions and cause significant distress to the digestive and neurological systems. In extreme cases, the physiological strain may lead to life-threatening conditions like seizures or a dangerous slowing of breathing. Monitoring for these symptoms is essential if accidental ingestion is suspected.
