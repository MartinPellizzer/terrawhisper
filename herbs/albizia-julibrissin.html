Albizia julibrissin

What's the taxonomical classification of Albizia julibrissin?

Albizia juliibrissin belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Fabales and is a member of the family Fabaceae. Specifically, it is identified by the genus Albizia and is known by its species name juliibrissin.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Albizia juliibrissin contains medicinal compounds found primarily in its bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is frequently harvested to create decoctions used for treating inflammation and digestive issues. Its leaves are often utilized in traditional preparations to calm the nervous system or reduce fever. Additionally, the roots can be processed into extracts that serve as mild sedatives or skin treatments. These various plant parts work together to provide a broad range of therapeutic applications in herbal medicine.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Albizia julibrissin targets several key proteins including cyclooxygenase-2, tumor necrosis factor-alpha, and various interleukin signaling pathways to produce its anti-inflammatory effects. The phytochemical constituents of the plant, such as tannins and flavonoids, exert their influence by inhibiting the enzymatic activity of prostaglandin synthesis. These bioactive compounds also modulate the expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines through the regulation of nuclear factor-kappa B signaling. By interacting with these specific molecular mediators, the plant helps suppress systemic inflammation and oxidative stress. Such precise interactions with cellular proteins provide the biochemical basis for its traditional use in treating inflammatory conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Albizia julibrissin?

Albizia julibrissin has sedative, anxiolytic, and neuroprotective properties that primarily target the central nervous system to reduce anxiety and promote sleep. The plant contains various bioactive compounds, including flavonoids and alkaloids, which interact with neurotransmitter pathways to stabilize mood. These chemical constituents help lower cortisol levels and mitigate the physiological symptoms of chronic stress. Additionally, its antioxidant activity assists in protecting neurons from oxidative damage caused by inflammation. Regular application of its extracts in traditional practices supports emotional regulation and mental calm.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, respiratory infections, inflammation, and various types of pain. The bark and leaves contain bioactive compounds that help alleviate symptoms of chronic inflammation in the body. It is frequently employed in traditional medicine to soothe skin irritations and promote healing for external wounds. Additionally, its properties are utilized to calm coughs and manage discomfort associated with respiratory distress. These medicinal applications rely on the plant's natural ability to reduce swelling and fight microbial activity.

What are the herbal preparations of Albizia julibrissin?

Albizia juliibrissin is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a concentrated liquid extract derived from its bark, leaves, or flowers. Practitioners often boil the dried plant material in water to create a potent tea used for its sedative properties. The dried components can also be finely ground into a powder for easier ingestion or topical application. Some traditional methods involve macerating the plant parts in alcohol to create a tincture for long-term storage. These various forms allow for precise control over the dosage and the speed of absorption into the body.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Albizia juliibrissin can cause allergic reactions including skin rashes, itching, or swelling if the plant comes into contact with sensitive skin. Some individuals may experience digestive upset or nausea if any part of the tree is ingested accidentally. There is also a potential for respiratory irritation if the pollen or fine dust from the leaves is inhaled in large quantities. Because clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the long-term effects of exposure remain largely unknown. Users should exercise caution and avoid direct contact to prevent unforeseen physiological responses.
