Clematis brevicaudata

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis brevicaudata?

Clematis brevicaudata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic hierarchy continues through the order Ranunculales and the family Ranunculaceae, placing it within the genus Clematis. Ultimately, its specific scientific designation is defined by the species epithet brevicaudata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis brevicaudata has slender, woody stems that are often twining and produce opposite or whorled leaves composed of three to five ovate or lanceolate leaflets. The leaf margins are typically entire or slightly serrated, while the leaf surfaces may appear glabrous or possess fine pubescence. Its inflorescence consists of small, inconspicuous flowers that lack large, showy petals, instead featuring prominent sepals. The reproductive structures include numerous stamens that surround a central cluster of pistils. Following flowering, the plant develops characteristic feathery achenes that facilitate wind dispersal.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southeastern United States, ranging from North Carolina through Georgia and into Alabama. It is primarily found within the coastal plain regions where sandy soils are common. Populations often cluster in pine flatwoods and scrub habitats. Its distribution is closely tied to these specific subtropical ecosystems. Localized occurrences may also extend into parts of Florida.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, humus-rich soil and a location that provides full sun to partial shade with cool, moist roots and warm upper foliage.

You should plant it in a spot where it can climb using its twisting petioles, often near a trellis or sturdy shrub. Regular watering is essential to keep the substrate consistently damp, especially during the establishment phase. Mulching around the base helps retain moisture and keeps the root zone temperature stable.

Once established, this species is relatively hardy and will thrive if given enough vertical support to expand.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds beta-sitosterol, daucosterol, syringaresinol-O-D-glucopyranoside, trans-p-coumaraate of the n-alkanols, and docosanol. These specific chemical constituents are responsible for the various pharmacological activities associated with the species. The presence of sterols like beta-sitosterol and daucosterol often contributes to anti-inflammatory properties. Furthermore, the unique glycoside and fatty acid derivatives play significant roles in the plant's chemical profile. Researchers study these diverse molecules to understand the full therapeutic potential of Clematis brevicaudata.

According to a study published by "Zhong yao cai = Zhongyaocai = Journal of Chinese medicinal materials", eight compounds were isolated and identified from Clematis brevicaudata. These medicinal compounds include palmitic acid (1), 1-docosanol (2), pentacosanoic acid-2', 3'-dihydroxypropyl ester (3), beta-sitosterol (4), and daucosterol (5). The study also identified a mixture of the trans-p-coumarate of the n-alkanols (6), 3,4-dihydroxy-trans coumatate ethyl ester (7), and syringaresinol-O-D-glucopyranoside (8). All 8 of these compounds are obtained from Clematis brevicaudata for the first time.
