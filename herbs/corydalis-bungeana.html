Corydalis bungeana

What's the taxonomical classification of Corydalis bungeana?

Corydalis bungeanana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Ranunculales. This plant is a representative of the family Papaveraceae, specifically situated within the genus Corydalis, and is identified by its unique species name, bungiana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Corydalis bunghean has a herbaceous perennial structure characterized by deeply lobed, pinnately divided leaves and zygomorphic flowers that feature a prominent, elongated nectar spur. The foliage typically displays a bluish-green hue and arises from a tuberous rhizome located beneath the soil surface. Each flower consists of delicate petals where the upper petal extends into a long, curved spur used to store nectar. The reproductive organs are tucked within the floral structure, often displaying shades of pale blue, lavender, or white. What are the pharmacological activities of Corydalis bungeana?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and metabolic regulatory pharmacological activities. The bioactive alkaloids within Corydalis bungayana work to suppress inflammatory responses in various biological models. These compounds also demonstrate significant effectiveness in inhibiting the growth of certain bacterial strains. Furthermore, the plant plays a crucial role in metabolic regulatory processes by influencing glucose and lipid metabolism. Such diverse medicinal properties make it a significant subject for modern pharmacological research.

As discussed in a study, Corydalis bungeana Turcz. is an anti-inflammatoy medicinal herb used widely in traditional Chinese medicine for upper respiratory tract infections. Its active anti-inflammatoy component, corynoline, reduces production of nitric oxide (NO) and the protein and mRNA levels of inducible nitric oxide (iNOS) and cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2). Treatment with corynoline significantly increases the expression of Nrf2, quinone oxidoreductase 1 (NQO1) and hemeoxygenase-1 (HO-1) at the mRNA and protein levels. It also suppresses the expression of inflammatory cytokines, such as tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) and interleukin-1β (IL-1β), at the mRNA and protein levels. Furthermore, corynoline inhibits lipopolysaccharide-stimulated phosphorylation of c-jun NH2-terminal kinase (JNk) and p38. These results suggest that corynoline reduces levels of pro-inflammatoy mediators by suppressing extracellular signal-regulated kinase 1/2 (ERK) and p38 phosphorylation in RAW264.7 cells.

Together, these substances form the basis for investigating the plant's therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal compounds isolated from the herb of Corydalis bungeanana include corynoline and acetylcorynoline. These components were purified to levels of 99.5% for corynoline and 99.1% for acetylcorynoline. Quantitative analysis utilized calibration curves that were linear in the range of 6.9-110.4 mg.L-1 for corynoline and 8.7-139.5 mg.L-1 for acetylcorynoline. The determination process showed an RSD of 2.1% and 2.7% with an average recovery of 97.3% and 97.2% respectively. This HPLC method allows for the simultaneous determination of these two specific components within the plant.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause significant adverse reactions including nausea, vomiting, dizziness, sedation, confusion, and respiratory depression. These physiological effects often stem from the high concentration of isoquinoline alkaloids present in its tissues. Individuals consuming the plant may experience a rapid onset of sedation that can impair motor coordination. In more severe cases, the chemical compounds may lead to mental confusion or a dangerous slowing of breathing known as respiratory depression. Gastrointestinal distress such as nausea and vomiting is also a common response to its ingestion.
