Adhatoda vasica

What's the taxonomical classification of Adhatoda vasica?

Adhatoda vasica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls into the order Lamiales. The plant is a member of the family Acanthaceae, specifically situated within the genus Adhatoda, and is identified by the species name vasica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Adhatoda vasica has a woody shrub habit characterized by erect, quadrangular stems that can reach heights of up to 1.5 meters. The leaves are simple, opposite, and lanceolate in shape with entire margins and acute tips. These leaves exhibit a distinctively serrated edge when young and possess a prominent midrib. The inflorescence consists of small, white, tubular flowers that emerge from the leaf axils in a cyme arrangement. The plant's overall structure is defined by its sturdy, branched stems and dense foliage.

The species is most frequently encountered in tropical and subtropical climates where seasonal rainfall supports its growth. What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Adhatoda vasica contains therapeutic compounds primarily located in its leaves, stems, and roots. The leaves are most frequently harvested to extract vasicine, an alkaloid used to treat respiratory ailments like asthma and bronchitis. Stem extracts are also utilized in traditional medicine to help alleviate inflammation and fever. Additionally, the roots serve as a source for various medicinal decoctions meant to treat digestive issues. What are the pharmacological activities of Adhatoda vasica?

This plant has antiasthmatic, antiinflammatory, expectorant, and bronchodilatory properties that facilitate the treatment of cough and bronchial infections. Its primary mechanism involves reducing airway inflammation and relaxing the smooth muscles of the lungs to improve breathing. As a potent expectorant, it helps thin and loosen mucus, making it easier for patients to clear their airways during respiratory distress. These combined effects make it an essential herbal resource for managing chronic obstructive pulmonary diseases and various acute respiratory ailments. Consequently, the medicinal compounds within its leaves serve as a natural therapeutic approach for stabilizing lung function.

As discussed in a study, the herbal formulation Pentapala-04 is prepared from five medicinal plants including Adhatoda vasica Need. This formulation was tested on albino wistar rats which were divided into 3 groups of 4 animals each. The study investigated the effect of the formulation against ova albumin and aluminium hydroxide induced lung damage. Results showed that while the toxic group experienced increased lipid peroxidation and decreased antioxidants, the post-treated groups had restored levels of antioxidant enzymes. This restoration might be due to the ability of Pentapala-04 to scavenge reactive oxygen species.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a specific group of pyrroloquinazoline alkaloids including vasicine, vasicinone, deoxyvasicine, and vasicinol. These bioactive compounds are primarily recognized for their diverse pharmacological properties in medicinal studies. Vasicine itself is often studied for its potential bronchodilatory and expectorant effects. The presence of vasicinone and deoxyvasicine further enhances the complex chemical profile of the species. Together, these alkaloids represent the core chemical constituents that drive the plant's therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, Adhatoda vasica contains the medicinally important secondary metabolite vasicine. This specific compound was observed to increase under the various treatments tested. The research evaluated the individual and interactive effects of chromium and ultraviolet-B radiation on the plant. While other physiological aspects like chlorophyll and photosynthetic rates changed, the focus remained on the response of this metabolite. The study concluded that Adhatoda vasica shows variable responses towards the individual and interactive stress of chromium and ultraviolet-B radiation.
