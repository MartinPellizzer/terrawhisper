Coriaria intermedia

What's the taxonomical classification of Coriaria intermedia?

Coriaria intermedia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is situated under the order Cucurbitales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Coriariaceae and the genus Coriaria, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species intermedia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Coriaria intermedia has an erect or spreading shrubby habit characterized by small, leathery, oval-shaped leaves that are typically dark green and arranged alternately along the stems.

The foliage is often densely packed, creating a compact appearance common to many woody shrubs in this genus. Its flowers are small, inconspicuous, and borne in dense, greenish clusters that emerge from the leaf axils. The most distinctive morphological feature is the production of small, fleshy, dark purple to black drupes that serve as fruit.

These berries are often found in crowded clusters along the branches, providing a stark visual contrast to the dark foliage.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Coriaria intermedia contains medicinal compounds primarily located within its leaves and its small, dark berries. The leaves are often utilized in traditional herbal preparations to address various ailments due to their chemical properties. Some local medicinal practices involve using the berries, although they must be handled with extreme caution because they can be toxic if consumed in large quantities. Extracts from different parts of the plant have been studied for their potential bioactive effects. Careful preparation is essential when using any part of this shrub for therapeutic purposes.

Additionally, the tannins and terpenoids within the plant are known to exhibit antimicrobial and bioactive effects.

Together, these chemical constituents form the basis for the plant's medicinal potential.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Coriaria intermedia targets inflammatory enzymes such as cyclooxygenase and lipoxygenase, as well as various cytokine signaling pathways, to exert its medicinal effects. The plant contains bioactive compounds like tannins and phenolic derivatives that inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory mediators. These phytochemicals interact with specific protein kinases to modulate the cellular response to oxidative stress. By interfering with these molecular pathways, the extracts can reduce swelling and pain in biological systems. Such interactions suggest a multi-target mechanism that addresses both immune regulation and enzymatic activity.

What are the therapeutic actions of Coriaria intermedia?

Coriaria intermedia has therapeutic properties as an astringent and an emetic, though its use is strictly limited because the plant is highly toxic.

The tannins found within the species provide the astringent qualities used to treat skin irritations or mucosal inflammation. When ingested in specific controlled doses, the plant can act as an emetic to induce vomiting. However, the presence of coumarin derivatives makes the entire plant dangerous if consumed improperly.

Careful management is required to prevent accidental poisoning during any medicinal application.

    What are the herbal preparations of Coriaria intermedia?

    Coriaria intermedia is prepared as an infusion, a decoction, or a topical poultice depending on the intended medicinal application. The leaves and stems are often steeped in hot water to create a tea that is consumed orally for internal ailments. For skin irritations or localized pain, a decoction made by boiling the plant material is frequently used to create a concentrated liquid. This liquid can be applied directly to the skin or used as a wash to soothe inflammation. These various preparations allow practitioners to utilize the plant's bioactive compounds for both systemic and external treatments.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Coriaria intermedia can cause severe gastrointestinal distress, including intense vomiting and diarrhea, if any part of the plant is ingested. The berries contain toxic compounds known as coriamyrtin which act as potent emetics. These toxins can lead to significant dehydration and electrolyte imbalances due to the violent physical reaction they trigger in the body. Even small quantities may cause significant illness in humans and animals. Careful avoidance of the fruit is necessary to prevent accidental poisoning.
