What's the taxonomical classification of Coriaria intermedia?

Coriaria intermedia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is situated under the order Cucurbitales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Coriariaceae and the genus Coriaria, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species intermedia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Coriaria intermedia has an erect or spreading shrubby habit characterized by small, leathery, oval-shaped leaves that are typically dark green and arranged alternately along the stems. Its flowers are small, inconspicuous, and borne in dense, greenish clusters that emerge from the leaf axils. The most distinctive morphological feature is the production of small, fleshy, dark purple to black drupes that serve as fruit. These berries are often found in crowded clusters along the branches, providing a stark visual contrast to the dark foliage.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Coriaria intermedia contains high concentrations of toxic coriariin, a complex glycoside that acts as a potent neurotoxin and purgative. This specific compound is primarily found within the berries and leaves of the shrub. Beyond this primary toxin, the plant possesses various secondary metabolites that contribute to its chemical profile. Researchers study these substances to understand their physiological effects on the nervous system. The presence of these bioactive molecules makes the plant both a medicinal subject and a significant environmental hazard.

What are the therapeutic actions of Coriaria intermedia?

Coriaria intermedia has therapeutic properties as an astringent and an emetic, though its use is strictly limited because the plant is highly toxic. The tannins found within the species provide the astringent qualities used to treat skin irritations or mucosal inflammation. When ingested in specific controlled doses, the plant can act as an emetic to induce vomiting. However, the presence of coumarin derivatives makes the entire plant dangerous if consumed improperly. Careful management is required to prevent accidental poisoning during any medicinal application.
