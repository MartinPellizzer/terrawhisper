European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

The European Dogwood, scientifically known as Viburnum opulus, is a shrub native to Europe and parts of Asia, traditionally valued in herbal medicine for its potential therapeutic properties.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is European Dogwood (Viburnum Opulus)?

The European Dogwood, or Viburnum opulus, is a shrub native to Europe and parts of Asia, known for its distinctive white, star-shaped flowers and red berries, and used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of European Dogwood is Viburnum opulus. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Dipsacales, family Caprifoliaceae, genus Viburnum, and species Viburnum opulus. It is commonly known as European Dogwood in Europe and as American Dogwood in North America. The plant is also known by the synonyms Botanical: Cornus opulus and regional: White Dogwood, Elderwood, and Snowball Tree.

Plant Description and Morphology

European Dogwood is a deciduous shrub or small tree that typically grows between 2 to 6 meters tall, with a dense, rounded crown and multiple stems.

Its leaves are ovate to elliptical, with a pointed tip and toothed margins, and they turn yellow in autumn. The plant produces clusters of white, flat-topped flowers in early summer, which are small and clustered in umbels, giving it a distinctive appearance. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, allowing it to thrive in moist, well-drained soils.

The bark is smooth and grayish-brown, while the leaves are smooth on top and hairy underneath, with a mild, slightly sweet scent.

Native Habitat and Distribution

The European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) is native to temperate regions of Europe and western Asia, growing in moist woodlands, stream valleys, and mountainous areas, typically in the understory of forests with partial shade.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The bark, leaves, flowers, and berries of the European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) have been used medicinally in various traditional herbal preparations.

Phytochemical Composition

The European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including flavonoids such as quercetin and rutin, as well as triterpene acids, anthocyanins, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) contains compounds such as triterpene glycosides, flavonoids, and iridoids that exhibit anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, making it relevant in herbal medicine for treating conditions like inflammation and infections.

Mechanisms of Action

The European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) exerts its effects through a combination of anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as arbutin, gallic acid, and flavonoids, which interact with cellular pathways to modulate immune responses and reduce oxidative stress.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its potential to support digestive health, alleviate menstrual discomfort, and promote respiratory wellness through its anti-inflammatory and mild sedative properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) is typically prepared as a tincture, decoction, or powder, with the dried fruits and bark being the most commonly used parts, often harvested in late summer to early autumn.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) for herbal use is 2-4 grams of dried berries in a decoction, taken twice daily, though specific formulations may vary based on preparation method and intended therapeutic use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause gastrointestinal upset, allergic reactions, and interactions with certain medications, particularly anticoagulants, and should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

Drug Interactions

European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) may interact with medications that affect the liver or have anticoagulant properties, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or altering drug metabolism.

Toxicity and Precautions

European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) is toxic to humans and animals, particularly due to its high concentration of hypoglycin A and other cyanogenic glycosides, which can cause gastrointestinal distress, vomiting, diarrhea, and in severe cases, respiratory failure or death, especially in children and pets.

Cultivation and Harvesting

European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) is typically cultivated in temperate climates with well-drained soil and partial to full sun exposure, and its berries are harvested in late summer to early autumn when they turn from red to a darker, more intense hue.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) typically involve drying the berries and then using methods such as maceration or distillation to obtain the active compounds, which are then further refined to produce tinctures, essential oils, or dried extracts for medicinal use.

Storage and Shelf Life

European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its dried parts can maintain potency for up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) has shown potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, with some studies suggesting its use in traditional medicine for treating ailments such as fever, diarrhea, and skin conditions.

Traditional and Historical Uses

European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) has been historically used in European herbal medicine for its purported ability to support digestive health, relieve menstrual discomfort, and treat skin conditions, with its bark and berries being the primary parts utilized in traditional preparations.

Regulatory Status

The European Dogwood (Viburnum opulus) is not approved as a medicinal herb in the European Union or the United States, and its use in herbal medicine is not regulated under traditional herbal medicine legislation in most jurisdictions.
