Viburnum opulus

What's the taxonomical classification of Viburnum opulus?

Viburnum opulus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Dipsacales and falls under the family Viburnaceae. Ultimately, its taxonomic identity is defined by its placement in the genus Viburnum, specifically within the species opulus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viburnum opulus has opposite, simple leaves that are broadly ovate or kidney-shaped with three to five shallow lobes and coarsely serrated margins. These leaves typically measure between five and fifteen centimeters in length and possess a distinctively textured, veined surface. The plant produces flat-topped cymes of small, creamy-white flowers that are arranged in a ring around a center of sterile, larger florets. Its stems are woody and can grow into a multi-stemmed shrub reaching heights of up to four meters. In late summer, the plant develops bright red, fleshy drupes that provide a striking visual contrast to the foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to much of Europe and northern Asia, stretching from the British Isles through central Europe and into the mountainous regions of Siberia. It thrives primarily in temperate climates where moist, well-drained soils are readily available. In its natural habitat, the species is frequently found along riverbanks, in woodland edges, and within damp thickets. Due to its ornamental value, it has been widely introduced to North America and other temperate regions across the globe. This widespread cultivation has allowed it to establish itself in many non-native garden landscapes.

Viburnum opulus contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its berries and the bark of its stems. What traditional systems uses this plant?

This plant, commonly known as guelder-rose, has been utilized in traditional medicine across Europe and Asia to treat ailments involving the urinary tract, kidneys, and female reproductive system.

Historical practitioners often prepared decoctions from the bark or roots to address issues like inflammation and kidney stones. The red berries were frequently used in folk remedies to manage fevers and digestive discomfort. What are the pharmacological activities of Viburnum opulus?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, vasorelaxant, and cytoprotective activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within various biological systems. The plant also exhibits antimicrobial effects that can inhibit the growth of certain pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, its vasorelaxant activity contributes to improved blood flow by relaxing smooth muscle tissues. Finally, the cytoprotective mechanisms of Viburnum opulus serve to shield cells from damage caused by chemical or environmental stressors.

According to a study published by "Nutrients", Viburnum opulus is traditionally used to treat ailments such as cough, colds, tuberculosis, rheumatic aches, ulcers, stomach, and kidney problems. Many of its health-promoting properties are associated with antioxidant activity, which has been demonstrated in both in vitro and in vivo studies. In vitro studies show the antimicrobial potential of the plant, especially against Gram-positive bacteria. Cell-based studies have demonstrated anti-inflammatory, anti-obesity, anti-diabetic, osteogenic, cardio-protective, and cytoprotective properties. Furthermore, in vivo studies have confirmed the applicability of the plant in the treatment of urinary tract diseases, endometriosis, and some cancers.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, phenolic compounds, polyphenols, tannins, and anthocyanins. These bioactive substances contribute to the significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties of the species. The presence of anthocyanins is particularly notable for providing the characteristic pigmentation found in the berries. Additionally, the concentration of tannins and various polyphenols helps support various therapeutic functions within the human body. Together, these chemical constituents make the plant a subject of interest for pharmacological research.

According to a study published by "Protoplasma", Viburnum opulus is a highly valued medicinal plant that serves as a source of many bioactive compounds, including antioxidants. The fruits of Viburnum opulus contain lipids and lipid compounds such as carotenoids, essential oils, steroids, and saponins. Additionally, these fruits contain polyphenols including tannins, flavonoids, and anthocyanins, as well as pectins and proteins. The fruits of Viburnum opulus were found to contain greater quantities of carotenoids, polyphenols, flavonoids, steroids, and pectins compared to the V. lantana drupes. These metabolites are located in different pericarp layers, but the greatest amounts were identified in the drupe skin.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for various gynecological disorders including premenstrual syndrome, primary dysmenorrhea, secondary dysmenorrhea, and ovarian cysts. Its primary therapeutic application involves relieving painful menstrual cramps and regulating hormonal fluctuations. It acts as an antispasmodic to soothe uterine contractions that cause discomfort during the menstrual cycle. Additionally, it is often employed to manage the symptoms associated with persistent pelvic pain and cyst-related issues. This herbal remedy serves as a traditional approach to stabilizing female reproductive health.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", the fruits of Viburnum opulus L. have been used to treat gynecological disorders including primary and secondary dysmenorrhea and ovarian cysts. In a rat model, the plant was used as a remedy for the treatment of endometriosis. The EtOAc extract-administered group showed a reduction in endometriotic volumes to 30.1mm3, while the MeOH extract-administered group showed a reduction to 34.7mm3. HPLC analyses determined that chlorogenic acid was the major compound, with amounts calculated as 0.5112±0.0012mg and 1.7072±0.0277mg/100mg extract for the respective extracts. These extracts also led to significant remission in the levels of TNF-α, VEGF, and IL-6.
