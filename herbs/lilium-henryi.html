Lilium henryi

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lilium henryi has erect, slender stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bear whorls of lanceolate, dark green leaves. The foliage consists of narrow, pointed leaves that are arranged in distinct clusters along the stem. Its flowers are large, trumpet-shaped, and characterized by a deep reddish-orange or brownish-red coloration with prominent dark spots. The tepals curve outward, revealing long stamens with heavy, pollen-bearing anthers. This species often features a bulbous root system that provides the energy necessary for its seasonal blooming cycle.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of central and western China, specifically within the provinces of Sichuan, Yunnan, and Gansu. It thrives in mountainous areas where it can find suitable habitats among forest margins and grassy slopes. The species typically occupies elevations ranging from middle to high mountain zones. Its distribution is closely tied to specific temperate climates found in these inland mountainous corridors. Localized populations are often found in areas characterized by moist, well-drained soils.

      What are the herbal preparations of Lilium henryi?

      Lilium henryi is prepared as a dried decoction of its bulb, a concentrated liquid extract used in traditional medicinal applications. The dried bulb material is typically simmered in water to release its active compounds for oral consumption. Some practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of a powder derived from the desiccated bulb parts. This powder can be incorporated into warm liquids or mixed with honey to mask its natural taste. These various preparations allow for consistent dosing depending on the specific therapeutic goal.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Lilium henryi can cause severe kidney failure, vomiting, and lethargy if ingested by cats. While the specific toxic compounds are not fully documented, most members of the Lilium genus are known to be highly nephrotoxic to felines. Ingestion of even a small amount of the plant or its pollen can trigger acute renal failure. Symptoms often manifest within hours of consumption and require immediate veterinary intervention. Owners should keep this plant out of reach of pets to prevent life-threatening complications.
