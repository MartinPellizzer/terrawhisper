Sandalwood (Santalum album): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a sacred and highly valued plant in traditional herbal medicine, known for its aromatic wood and therapeutic properties used for centuries in Ayurvedic and Chinese healing practices.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Sandalwood (Santalum Album)?

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a small evergreen tree native to India, known for its aromatic wood and resin, which has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and calming properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Sandalwood is Santalum album. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Santalales, family Santalaceae, genus Santalum, and species Santalum album. It is commonly known as Sandalwood in India and Indonesia, and as Australian Sandalwood in Australia. The synonyms for this plant include botanical names such as Santalum album L. and regional names like Chandan in India and Kewra in parts of Southeast Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Sandalwood is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree that typically grows up to 10 meters tall with a straight, slender trunk and a narrow crown.

Its leaves are simple, alternate, and elliptic to ovate in shape, with a leathery texture and a dull green color on both sides. The flowers are small, fragrant, and white or pale yellow, arranged in clusters at the base of the leaves, blooming during the dry season. The root system is shallow and fibrous, often spreading widely near the surface to absorb moisture efficiently.

The wood is dense, pale yellow to reddish-brown in color, has a rich, woody aroma, and is smooth with a fine, even texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Sandalwood is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of India, particularly in the southern states of Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Kerala, as well as parts of Australia and Madagascar.

It thrives in regions with a warm, humid climate and well-drained soils, often found in dry deciduous forests or on rocky slopes. The tree prefers areas with moderate rainfall and high sunlight exposure, which supports its slow growth and essential oil production. Sandalwood requires a stable ecosystem with minimal disturbance, typically growing in association with other native flora in its natural habitat.

Its distribution is now limited due to overharvesting and habitat loss, making conservation efforts critical for its survival.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of sandalwood primarily involves the heartwood and the leaves of the Santalum album tree.

The heartwood is the main part utilized due to its high concentration of aromatic compounds and medicinal properties. Leaves are also used, though less commonly, for their therapeutic benefits. The heartwood is typically harvested when the tree is mature, ensuring maximum potency.

Both parts are processed into various forms for traditional and modern herbal applications.

Phytochemical Composition

Sandalwood contains a variety of active compounds, including sesquiterpenes, which are responsible for its distinctive aroma and therapeutic properties.

Among these, alpha-santalol and beta-santalol are the primary secondary metabolites that contribute to its medicinal value. These compounds are also classified as chemical markers, as they are unique to the species and used for quality assessment. The presence of these metabolites is indicative of the plant's potency and authenticity.

Secondary metabolites in sandalwood are also known to possess antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activities.

Pharmacological Properties

Sandalwood (Santalum album) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that underpin its use in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, which contribute to its traditional applications in treating skin disorders and respiratory conditions. The biological activity of sandalwood is attributed to its volatile oils, particularly santalol and sesquiterpenes, which interact with cellular receptors and modulate physiological responses. Pharmacodynamically, sandalwood influences the central nervous system by exerting sedative and anxiolytic effects, promoting relaxation and reducing stress.

System-level effects involve its ability to enhance immune function and support wound healing through its bioactive compounds.

Mechanisms of Action

Sandalwood (Santalum album) exerts its therapeutic effects through multiple biological mechanisms that influence cellular activity, biochemical pathways, and specific target systems in the body.

At the cellular level, its constituents interact with membrane receptors and modulate intracellular signaling cascades, leading to anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory responses. Biochemically, sandalwood compounds such as sesquiterpenes and oils inhibit pro-inflammatory cytokines and oxidative stress markers by interfering with key enzymatic pathways. These actions are mediated through the modulation of lipid metabolism and the suppression of reactive oxygen species.

The target systems include the central nervous system, where sandalwood exhibits sedative and anxiolytic properties, and the respiratory system, where it demonstrates bronchodilatory effects.

Therapeutic Uses

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is widely used in herbal medicine for its therapeutic properties that support skin health, respiratory function, and mental clarity.

It is traditionally applied to treat skin conditions such as acne, eczema, and fungal infections due to its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects. The plant is also used to alleviate symptoms of respiratory disorders like asthma and cough by promoting clear breathing and reducing inflammation in the airways. Additionally, sandalwood is valued for its calming influence on the mind, helping to reduce stress and anxiety.

Its essential oil is commonly used in aromatherapy to enhance mood and promote relaxation.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods of Sandalwood (Santalum album) in herbal medicine involve various forms based on its traditional use.

The plant is commonly used in powdered form, where the dried wood is ground into a fine powder for internal or external application. It is also prepared as an essential oil, extracted through steam distillation from the heartwood. Another common form is the resin, which is collected from the plant's surface and used in formulations.

Additionally, Sandalwood is sometimes incorporated into decoctions or tinctures for specific therapeutic purposes.

Dosage and Administration

The standard dose of sandalwood for herbal use is typically 250-500 mg per day, taken in divided doses.

Extracts should be prepared at a concentration of no more than 1:10, to ensure safety and minimize potential adverse effects. It is recommended to take sandalwood once or twice daily, depending on the individual's condition and response. The duration of treatment should not exceed 4 weeks unless under the guidance of a qualified healthcare practitioner.

Overuse or prolonged use may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or other side effects, so it is important to follow these guidelines carefully.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used appropriately, Sandalwood is generally considered safe for most individuals, though it may cause mild side effects in some cases.

Common side effects include skin irritation, digestive discomfort, and allergic reactions, particularly when applied topically or ingested in large amounts. Individuals with known allergies to plants in the Santalaceae family should avoid Sandalwood to prevent adverse reactions. It is contraindicated for people with hypersensitivity to its compounds, and caution is advised for those with a history of allergic responses to similar herbal remedies.

During pregnancy, Sandalwood should be used with caution and only under medical supervision due to limited research on its safety in expectant mothers.

Drug Interactions

Sandalwood (Santalum album) may interact with certain pharmaceuticals and supplements, potentially affecting their efficacy or increasing the risk of side effects.

When taken with medications such as anticoagulants or antiplatelet drugs, sandalwood may enhance their blood-thinning effects, leading to an increased risk of bleeding. It can also interfere with the metabolism of some drugs in the liver, altering their concentration in the bloodstream. Combining sandalwood with supplements like garlic, ginkgo biloba, or vitamin E may amplify its anticoagulant properties.

Therefore, it is important to consult a healthcare provider before using sandalwood alongside any pharmaceuticals or supplements.

Toxicity and Precautions

Toxicity and precautions are important considerations when using sandalwood in herbal medicine.

The toxic dose of sandalwood is not well established, but excessive consumption can lead to adverse effects. Long-term safety data is limited, and prolonged use may result in unknown health risks. Poisoning risks are primarily associated with high doses or improper preparation.

It is advisable to consult a healthcare professional before using sandalwood for extended periods.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Sandalwood trees require well-drained, loamy soils with a slightly acidic to neutral pH for optimal growth.

They thrive in full sunlight and need at least 6 hours of direct sun daily. Moderate watering is essential, with the soil allowed to dry slightly between waterings to prevent root rot. Propagation is typically done through seeds or cuttings, with seedlings taking up to five years to mature. The best harvest time occurs when the wood is fully mature, usually after 10 to 15 years, and the resin content is highest.

Harvesting involves carefully cutting the trunk and allowing the resin to harden before collection.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and Extraction of Sandalwood involves several key steps to obtain its valuable essence.

The wood is first dried to reduce moisture content and prevent spoilage, ensuring stability during subsequent processing. Once dried, the wood is ground into a fine powder to increase surface area for more efficient extraction. Extraction methods typically involve soaking the powder in solvents to dissolve the aromatic compounds.

Distillation is then used to separate the essential oils from the liquid mixture, yielding the highly sought-after sandalwood oil.

Storage and Shelf Life

Sandalwood, when properly stored, can maintain its potency and aroma for up to 5 years.

To ensure optimal shelf life, it should be kept in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. Exposure to light can degrade the essential oils and reduce its therapeutic properties. Moisture should be strictly controlled to prevent mold growth and maintain the integrity of the wood.

Ideal storage conditions include a temperature range of 15-25°C with low humidity levels.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has increasingly focused on Sandalwood (Santalum album) to evaluate its potential therapeutic benefits in herbal medicine.

Clinical trials have explored its use in managing skin conditions and inflammatory disorders, with some studies showing promising results. In vitro studies have identified bioactive compounds such as sesquiterpenes and oils that may contribute to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. Pharmacological research has further investigated its mechanisms of action, including its ability to modulate immune responses and reduce oxidative stress.

These findings support the traditional use of Sandalwood in herbal formulations, though more extensive research is needed for broader clinical applications.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Traditional and Historical Uses refers to the long-standing application of Sandalwood in various cultures for medicinal and ritualistic purposes.

It has been used for centuries in Ayurvedic medicine to treat ailments such as respiratory issues, skin conditions, and digestive disorders. The wood and its essential oil were highly valued for their aromatic properties and believed therapeutic effects. In ancient India, Sandalwood was also used in religious ceremonies and as a symbol of purity and spiritual awakening.

Its historical significance spans across Asia, where it was incorporated into traditional healing practices and cultural rituals.

Sustainability and Conservation

Sandalwood (Santalum album) faces significant sustainability challenges due to overharvesting and habitat loss, raising concerns about its long-term viability.

Wild harvesting has led to a dramatic decline in natural populations, particularly in India and Australia, where it is native. The species is now listed as vulnerable by the IUCN Red List, highlighting the urgent need for conservation efforts. Cultivation alternatives are being explored to reduce pressure on wild populations and ensure a sustainable supply.

These include controlled farming practices and seed propagation techniques aimed at restoring and maintaining sandalwood ecosystems.
