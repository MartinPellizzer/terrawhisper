Sandalwood (Santalum album): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a sacred and highly valued plant in traditional herbal medicine, known for its aromatic wood and therapeutic properties used for centuries in Ayurvedic and Chinese healing practices.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Sandalwood (Santalum Album)?

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a small evergreen tree native to India, known for its aromatic wood and resin, which has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and calming properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Sandalwood is Santalum album. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Santalales, family Santalaceae, genus Santalum, and species Santalum album. It is commonly known as Sandalwood in India and Indonesia, and as Australian Sandalwood in Australia. The synonyms for this plant include botanical names such as Santalum album L. and regional names like Chandan in India and Kewra in parts of Southeast Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Sandalwood is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree that typically grows up to 10 meters tall with a straight, slender trunk and a narrow crown.

Its leaves are simple, alternate, and elliptic to ovate in shape, with a leathery texture and a dull green color on both sides. The flowers are small, fragrant, and white or pale yellow, arranged in clusters at the base of the leaves, blooming during the dry season. The root system is shallow and fibrous, often spreading widely near the surface to absorb moisture efficiently.

The wood is dense, pale yellow to reddish-brown in color, has a rich, woody aroma, and is smooth with a fine, even texture.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Sandalwood is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of India, particularly in the southern states of Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Kerala, as well as parts of Australia and Madagascar.

It thrives in regions with a warm, humid climate and well-drained soils, often found in dry deciduous forests or on rocky slopes. The tree prefers areas with moderate rainfall and high sunlight exposure, which supports its slow growth and essential oil production. Sandalwood requires a stable ecosystem with minimal disturbance, typically growing in association with other native flora in its natural habitat.

Its distribution is now limited due to overharvesting and habitat loss, making conservation efforts critical for its survival.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of sandalwood primarily involves the heartwood and the leaves of the Santalum album tree.

The heartwood is the main part utilized due to its high concentration of aromatic compounds and medicinal properties. Leaves are also used, though less commonly, for their therapeutic benefits. The heartwood is typically harvested when the tree is mature, ensuring maximum potency.

Both parts are processed into various forms for traditional and modern herbal applications.

Phytochemical Composition

Sandalwood contains a variety of active compounds, including sesquiterpenes, which are responsible for its distinctive aroma and therapeutic properties.

Among these, alpha-santalol and beta-santalol are the primary secondary metabolites that contribute to its medicinal value. These compounds are also classified as chemical markers, as they are unique to the species and used for quality assessment. The presence of these metabolites is indicative of the plant's potency and authenticity.

Secondary metabolites in sandalwood are also known to possess antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activities.

Pharmacological Properties

Sandalwood (Santalum album) exhibits pharmacological properties including antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects, supported by both traditional use and modern scientific research.

Mechanisms of Action

Sandalwood (Santalum album) exerts its therapeutic effects through multiple mechanisms, including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and analgesic properties, primarily due to the presence of volatile oils, sesquiterpene lactones, and other bioactive compounds that interact with cellular pathways involved in immune response and pain modulation.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is traditionally used in herbal medicine for its calming effects on the nervous system, as a mild sedative, and for its ability to alleviate respiratory conditions such as asthma and bronchitis.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Sandalwood is typically prepared as a powder, tincture, or oil, with the most common forms being the powdered resin from the heartwood and the essential oil extracted through steam distillation.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Sandalwood (Santalum album) for medicinal use typically ranges from 300 to 600 mg per day, taken in divided doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the form of the herb and the intended therapeutic effect.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While sandalwood is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts for short periods, prolonged use or high doses may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, and potential interactions with certain medications, making it contraindicated for individuals with specific health conditions or those taking particular pharmaceuticals.

Drug Interactions

Sandalwood (Santalum album) may interact with medications that affect the central nervous system, such as sedatives, antihistamines, and antidepressants, potentially enhancing their effects and increasing the risk of drowsiness, dizziness, or impaired coordination.

Toxicity and Precautions

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts as a herbal remedy, but it may cause skin irritation or allergic reactions in some individuals, and long-term use should be avoided due to potential hepatotoxic effects.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions, primarily in India, Indonesia, and Australia, through a complex symbiotic relationship with specific mycorrhizal fungi, requiring careful soil preparation, shade management, and a lengthy growth period of up to 15 years before the wood is ready for harvesting.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Sandalwood (Santalum album) involve harvesting the heartwood, which is then dried, cut into small pieces, and subjected to steam distillation or solvent extraction to obtain the valuable aromatic oils and resinous compounds.

Storage and Shelf Life

Sandalwood (Santalum album) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to 2-3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has shown that Sandalwood contains compounds such as santalol and santalone, which may contribute to its traditional use in herbal medicine for respiratory and skin conditions.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Sandalwood (Santalum album) has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine for centuries to treat respiratory conditions, digestive disorders, and skin ailments, with its aromatic properties believed to promote mental clarity and spiritual purification.

Regulatory Status

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is listed as a Schedule 1 drug under the Indian Scheduled Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1955, due to its limited availability and high demand in traditional and herbal medicine.
