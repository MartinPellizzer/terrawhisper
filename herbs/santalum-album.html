Sandalwood (Santalum album): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a small evergreen tree native to India, known for its fragrant heartwood and various traditional medicinal applications, including the treatment of skin disorders and respiratory conditions.

What is Sandalwood (Santalum Album)?

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a small, evergreen tree native to the Indian subcontinent, known for its aromatic resinous wood and its use in traditional herbal medicine for its purported therapeutic properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a small evergreen tree belonging to the Santalaceae family, native to the tropical regions of India, Australia, and Madagascar, and is characterized by its aromatic wood and small, white, fragrant flowers.

Common Names and Synonyms

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is commonly known by various names such as Indian sandalwood, white sandalwood, and sacred wood, reflecting its cultural and medicinal significance across different regions.

Plant Description and Morphology

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree with a straight, slender trunk, dense, dark green foliage, and fragrant yellow flowers that bloom in clusters.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is native to the rainforests of southern India, particularly the western Ghats, and is also found in parts of Southeast Asia, including Indonesia and Malaysia.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The bark, leaves, and seeds of the Sandalwood tree are the primary plant parts used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Sandalwood (Santalum album) contains a complex array of phytochemical compounds, including sesquiterpenes, particularly alpha-santalol and beta-santalol, along with oils, resins, and aromatic compounds that contribute to its medicinal properties and distinctive fragrance.

Pharmacological Properties

Sandalwood (Santalum album) exhibits pharmacological properties including antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as santalols and sesquiterpenes.

Mechanisms of Action

Sandalwood (Santalum album) exerts its effects through a combination of phytochemical compounds, including sesquiterpenes, monoterpenes, and lignans, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and analgesic properties by modulating various cellular pathways and enzyme activities.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Sandalwood is used to treat respiratory conditions such as asthma, bronchitis, and cough, as well as digestive issues like indigestion and gastritis, and it is also applied externally for skin disorders, wounds, and as a calming agent for stress and anxiety.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is typically prepared as a powder, oil, or paste, with the most common forms being sandalwood powder, sandalwood oil, and sandalwood paste, each derived from the heartwood of the tree through processes such as drying, grinding, and distillation.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Sandalwood (Santalum album) for herbal use typically ranges from 300 to 500 mg per day, taken in divided doses, and should be prepared as a powder or decoction, with the exact form and duration depending on the specific condition being treated.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While sandalwood is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, excessive consumption may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

Drug Interactions

Sandalwood (Santalum album) may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the central nervous system, due to its potential sedative and antispasmodic properties, and it is important to consult a healthcare provider before use if taking other drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is generally considered safe when used in typical medicinal doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal upset, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, and long-term or excessive use should be avoided.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Sandalwood (Santalum album) is typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions with well-drained soil and high humidity, often in shaded environments to promote slow growth and enhance resin production.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of Sandalwood (Santalum album) involves drying the heartwood to remove moisture, followed by grinding or slicing it into fine powder or thin slices for use in herbal formulations.

Storage and Shelf Life

Sandalwood (Santalum album) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of up to 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence on Sandalwood (Santalum album) have explored its potential pharmacological properties, including antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, with some studies suggesting its traditional use in herbal medicine may have a scientific basis.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Sandalwood (Santalum album) has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine for centuries to treat ailments such as respiratory disorders, skin conditions, and digestive issues, with its essential oil and wood valued for their aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Regulatory Status
