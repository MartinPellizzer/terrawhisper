Canarium strictum

What's the taxonomical classification of Canarium strictum?

Canarium strictum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic descent, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Sapindales, it is placed in the family Burseraceae. The plant is specifically identified by its genus, Canarium, and its species, strictum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canarium strictum has an erect woody habit characterized by a straight trunk, smooth bark, and pinnate leaves composed of multiple oblong leaflets. The leaves are typically arranged alternately along the branches and possess a leathery texture with prominent midribs. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves to facilitate reproduction. These blossoms eventually develop into fleshy drupes that contain a single hard seed. The overall structure of the plant reflects its adaptation to tropical forest environments.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as the Kanukupi, requires well-draining, acidic soil and consistent humidity to thrive in tropical or subtropical environments.

It is best cultivated in partially shaded areas where it can avoid direct, scorching sunlight during the hottest parts of the day. Regular watering is essential to maintain moisture levels, although it is important to prevent water from pooling around the roots to avoid rot. Providing organic mulch around the base helps regulate soil temperature and retain necessary moisture.

As the plant matures, it benefits from occasional fertilization with a balanced liquid nutrient solution to support healthy growth.

What are the pharmacological activities of Canarium strictum?

This plant has demonstrated potent pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, 15-lipoxygenase inhibition, and insecticidal properties. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage within biological systems. The plant also functions as an anti-inflammatory agent by modulating various immune responses. Through the inhibition of 15-lipoxygenase, it plays a specific role in regulating lipid peroxidation and inflammatory pathways. Furthermore, its chemical constituents exhibit significant insecticidal effects useful for pest management.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", the resin of Canaarium strictum is traditionally used to treat colds, airway afflictions, rheumatoid arthritis, rheumatism, and asthma. The stem bark of the plant is used as a mosquito repellent. Research involving 10 folk healers investigated the resin and bark extracts for DPPH radical scavenging, 15-lipoxygenase inhibition, and effects on nitric oxide production in LPS-activated dendritic D2SC/I cells. Both resin and bark extracts demonstrated radical scavenging, 15-lipoxygenase inhibitory activity, and inhibition of NO production. Additionally, no toxicity was found towards Artemia salina nauplii during testing. The high content of triterpenoids in the resin may contribute to anti-inflammatory effects.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains gallic acid, methyl gallate, lupeol, taraxasterol, and various triterpenoids. These specific medicinal compounds are known for providing significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of gallic acid and methyl gallate contributes to the plant's ability to combat oxidative stress in biological systems. Additionally, the triterpenoids, which include lupeol and taraxasterol, play a crucial role in the plant's bioactive profile. These chemical constituents make the species a subject of interest for pharmaceutical research and natural medicine.

According to a study published by "Fitoterapia", the resin of Canarium strictum contains triterpenoids, with alpha-amyrin and beta-amyrin identified as the major constituents in the dichloromethane resin extract. Through GC-MS, alpha-amyrin, beta-amyrin and their acetates, lupeol, and taraxasterol were also identified. The stem bark contains procyaniidins as a major compound, along with gallic acid, methyl gallate, scopoletin, 3,3'-di-O-methylellagic acid 4-O-alpha-arabinofuranoside, and elephantorrhizol, which is also known as 3,3',4',5,6,7,8-heptahydroxyflavan. These resin and bark extracts demonstrated radical scavenging, 15-lipoxygenase inhibitory activity, and inhibition of NO production. Additionally, no toxicity was found towards Artemia salina nauplii during the investigation.
