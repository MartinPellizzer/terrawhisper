Canarium strictum

What's the taxonomical classification of Canarium strictum?

Canarium strictum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic descent, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Sapindales, it is placed in the family Burseraceae. The plant is specifically identified by its genus, Canarium, and its species, strictum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Canarium strictum has an erect woody habit characterized by a straight trunk, smooth bark, and pinnate leaves composed of multiple oblong leaflets. The leaves are typically arranged alternately along the branches and possess a leathery texture with prominent midribs. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves to facilitate reproduction. These blossoms eventually develop into fleshy drupes that contain a single hard seed. The overall structure of the plant reflects its adaptation to tropical forest environments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Canarium strictum?

Canarium strictum has potent therapeutic properties characterized by its ability to act as an astringent, hemostatic, antiseptic, and anti-inflammatory agent. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains gallic acid, lupeol, methyl gallate, procyanidins, and scoleporetin, which serve as its primary medicinal compounds. Gallic acid and methyl gallate function as potent antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative damage. The presence of procyanidins further enhances the plant's ability to combat inflammation throughout the body. Additionally, the triterpene lupeol provides significant anti-inflammatory and potential anti-tumor properties. Scopoletin contributes to these therapeutic effects by acting as a vasodilator and an anti-inflammatory agent.

As discussed in a study, the resin of Canarium strictum contains major compounds known as triterpenoids, specifically α-amyrin and β-amyrin and their acetates, lupeol, and taraxasterol. The stem bark contains procyaniidins, gallic acid, methyl gallate, scopoletin, 3,3'-di-O-methylellagic acid 4-O-α-arabinofuranoside, and elephantorrhizol (3,3',4',5,6,7,8-heptahydroxyflavan). Ten folk healers were interviewed regarding the medicinal uses of this plant. The study investigated the extracts for 15-lipoxygenase inhibition and effects on nitric oxide production in LPS-activated dendritic D2SC/I cells. While the resin is used for rheumatism and asthma, no toxicity towards Artemia salina nauplii was found.
