Senecio nemorensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Senecio nemorensis?

Seneciio nemorensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic lineage further, it falls under the order Asterales and is a member of the family Asteraceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Senecio and the species nemorensis.

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, stomach pains, and inflammatory conditions. Local practitioners often apply its processed leaves directly to wounds or skin irritations to promote healing. In some traditional practices, decoctions made from the plant are ingested to alleviate digestive discomfort. The phytochemical properties of the species are believed to provide relief from localized swelling and minor internal pains. These medicinal uses vary depending on the specific preparation method and the region where the plant is gathered.

What are the herbal preparations of Senecio nemorensis?

Senecio nemorensis is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion using the dried aerial parts of the plant to extract its active constituents. Practitioners often simmer the plant material in water to create a concentrated liquid medicine for internal consumption. Some traditional applications involve using the plant in topical poultices where the crushed leaves are applied directly to the skin. These preparations are typically administered in small, controlled doses due to the potent chemical profile of the species. The specific method of preparation depends heavily on the intended therapeutic goal and the part of the plant being utilized.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Senecio nemorensis can cause severe toxic reactions in humans and livestock due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids in its tissues. Ingesting this plant can lead to the development of hepatic veno-occlusive disease, which results in irreversible liver damage. Symptoms of poisoning often include jaundice, abdominal pain, and swelling of the extremities. If the toxins are consumed repeatedly, the damage may progress to liver failure or even death. Because these alkaloids are cumulative, even small doses over a long period can pose a significant health risk.
