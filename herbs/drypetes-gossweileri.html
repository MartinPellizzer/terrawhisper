What's the taxonomical classification of Drypetes gossweileri?

Drypetes gossweileri belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the order Malpighiales and belongs to the family Putranjivaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Drypetes, and its specific species designation, gossweileri.

What are the pharmacological activities of Drypetes gossweileri?

Its chemical constituents work to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. The bioactive compounds also demonstrate the ability to inhibit the growth of various bacteria and fungi. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant nematicidal properties that can target parasitic worms. These diverse therapeutic effects suggest that the species holds substantial potential for pharmaceutical development.

As discussed in a study, the essential oils of the stem bark of Drypetes gossweileri contain a benzylisothiocyanate content of 63.19 % and a benzylcyanide content of 35.72 %. These essential oils exhibited high antioxidant and DPPH radical scavenging effects, though they showed low ferric reducing power activity. The plant was found to be rich in phenolic compounds, ranking higher than Allium sativum and Allium cepa. Furthermore, the essential oil demonstrated anti-inflammarotory activities through the heat denaturation of Bovine Serum Albumin. However, the anti-inflammarotory activities of Drypetes gossweileri were lower than those of Pentadiplandra brazzeana and the reference non-steroidal anti-inflammarotory drug sodium diclofenac.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the medicinal treatment of Candida infections and various metabolic diseases. Its extracts are traditionally applied to combat fungal overgrowth caused by yeast. Researchers also study its potential to regulate physiological processes related to metabolic disorders. The phytochemical properties of the plant contribute to its diverse pharmacological applications. These therapeutic uses highlight its importance in traditional healing practices.

As discussed in a study, essential oils from Drypetes gossweileri stem bark showed activity against Candida albicans and Candida parapsilopsis with an MIC value of 62.5 µg/mL. These essential oils were fungicidal and demonstrated synergism on C. parapsilopsis and additivity on C. albicans, resulting in a 2 to 64-fold drop in MIC values. When combined with other essential oils, the MIC combination of 31.25/7.81 µg/mL and 1.95/31.25 µg/mL required 20 and 18 h of exposure to effectively kill 99.9% of the inoculum. This fungicidal effect was accompanied by the alteration of the cell walls and membranes of the yeasts.
