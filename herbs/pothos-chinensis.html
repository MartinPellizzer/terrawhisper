Pothos chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Pothos chinensis?

Pothos chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Alismatales into the family Araceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Pothos, and its unique species designation, chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Pothos chinensis has heart-shaped leaves that are typically bright green with varying degrees of yellowish variegation and a smooth, waxy texture. The foliage is arranged alternately along flexible, trailing stems that allow the plant to climb or hang. These stems possess aerial roots that emerge at intervals to provide support and moisture absorption when contacting a substrate. The leaf margins are entire, meaning they are smooth without any serration or lobes. Small, inconspicuous flowers may occasionally emerge from the leaf axils during specific growing conditions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Pothos chinensis?

This plant has antibacterial properties that allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. These bioactive compounds work by disrupting the cellular membranes or metabolic processes of harmful bacteria. Research suggests that the phytochemical constituents within the leaves contribute significantly to this defensive mechanism. By neutralizing certain microbes, the plant serves as a potential source for developing new antimicrobial agents. Such pharmacological potential makes it a subject of interest in traditional and modern medicinal studies.

As discussed in a study, two new unsaturated fatty acids, (Z)-octadec-13-en-11-ynoic acid (1) and (Z)-octadec-16-en-12,14-diynoic acid (2), were isolated from the whole plant of Pothos chinensis. These 2 new fatty acids were found alongside 6 known compounds. The structures of these compounds were determined through 1D and 2D NMR data. Specifically, compound 2 demonstrated moderate antibacterial activity against Staphylococcus aureus.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause oral irritation, swelling of the mouth or tongue, a burning sensation in the mouth or throat, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, difficulty swallowing, and skin irritation. Ingestion of its toxic compounds typically leads to immediate discomfort in the digestive tract and mucosal linings. If the plant touches the skin, it may result in redness or painful dermatological reactions. Contact with the mucous membranes can also lead to significant swelling that makes swallowing difficult. These symptoms are generally the body's response to the calcium oxalate crystals present in the foliage.
