Oxytropis melanocalyx

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Oxytropis melanocalyx has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by ascending or erect stems that feature pinnately compound leaves with several pairs of small, oval-shaped leaflets. The foliage is often covered in fine, grayish hairs that provide a soft texture to the plant surface. Its most distinguishing feature is the inflorescence, which consists of pea-like flowers held on peduncles. The calyx is notably dark or blackish in color, which provides a sharp contrast to the lighter hues of the petals. These floral structures are typically arranged in small clusters at the ends of the stems.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of western China, specifically within the mountainous provinces of Qinghai, Gansu, Sichuan, and Tibet. It typically flourishes in subalpine environments such as meadow slopes and rocky crevices. The distribution is largely concentrated within the eastern Tibetan Plateau and the surrounding Hengduan Mountains.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Oxytropis melanocalyx can cause severe poisoning if ingested due to the presence of toxic alkaloids such as swainsonine. These chemical compounds interfere with cellular processes and can lead to neurological impairment or digestive distress. Consumption often results in symptoms like vomiting, diarrhea, and muscle tremors in animals and humans. Long-term exposure to such toxins may cause permanent damage to the nervous system or organ failure. Because of these dangerous properties, the plant should be strictly avoided to prevent accidental toxicity.
