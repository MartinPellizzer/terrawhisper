What is Notopterygium Incisum (Notopterygium Incisum)?

Notopterygium incisum, also known as Chinese angelica or huajia, is a perennial herb native to China, widely used in traditional Chinese medicine for its therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Notopterygium incisum is Notopterygium incisum. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Rutaceae, genus Notopterygium, and species Notopterygium incisum. It is commonly known as qianghuo in China and qinghuo in Tibet. It is also known by the synonyms Notopterygium forbesii and Notopterygium sinense.

Plant Description and Morphology

Notopterygium incisum is a perennial herbaceous plant that typically grows to a height of 30-80 cm, with a slender, upright stem that is often branched towards the upper part.

Its leaves are pinnately compound, with elongated, lance-shaped leaflets that have serrated margins and a slightly hairy surface. The plant produces small, yellowish-green flowers arranged in dense, terminal clusters, which bloom in early summer and are borne on short pedicels. The root system consists of a taproot with several lateral branches, which are fibrous and white in color.

The plant has a distinctive aromatic odor, with a slightly spicy and woody scent, and its texture is fibrous and somewhat brittle when dried.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Notopterygium incisum is native to the mountainous regions of central and eastern China, particularly in provinces such as Sichuan, Hubei, and Hunan.

It thrives in temperate climates with cool summers and cold winters, typically at elevations between 1,500 and 3,000 meters. The plant prefers well-drained soils and is commonly found in forested areas or rocky slopes. It requires moderate humidity and is often associated with mixed deciduous or coniferous forests.

Its natural habitat is characterized by high altitude, low temperatures, and minimal human disturbance.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots and rhizomes of Notopterygium incisum are primarily used medicinally in traditional herbal medicine.

Phytochemical Composition

Notopterygium incisum contains a variety of bioactive compounds including essential oils, alkaloids, flavonoids, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its traditional use in treating inflammatory and respiratory conditions.

Pharmacological Properties

Notopterygium incisum exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic effects, primarily attributed to the presence of volatile oils, sesquiterpenes, and alkaloids such as notopterygin.

Mechanisms of Action

Notopterygium incisum exerts its pharmacological effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic properties, which are attributed to the presence of volatile oils, lignans, and other bioactive compounds that modulate cytokine production and inhibit inflammatory pathways such as COX-2 and NF-κB.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Notopterygium incisum is traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat conditions such as rheumatism, arthritis, and other inflammatory disorders due to its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Notopterygium incisum is commonly prepared as a decoction, tincture, or powder, with the root being the primary part used, typically harvested in late summer to early autumn and processed through drying and grinding to ensure potency and stability.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Notopterygium incisum for adults is 3-9 grams per day, divided into two or three doses, typically taken in the form of a decoction or tincture, and should be prepared under the guidance of a qualified herbalist or healthcare provider.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Notopterygium incisum is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interactions with certain medications, and should be avoided during pregnancy, breastfeeding, or in individuals with known allergies to plants in the Apiaceae family.

Drug Interactions

Notopterygium incisum may interact with medications that affect the central nervous system, such as sedatives, antihypertensive drugs, and immunosuppressants, potentially enhancing their effects or causing adverse reactions.

Toxicity and Precautions

Notopterygium incisum is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, and there is limited data on its safety during pregnancy, lactation, or in individuals with chronic health conditions.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Notopterygium incisum is typically cultivated in mountainous regions with cool climates and well-drained soil, with harvesting occurring in late summer to early autumn when the roots are most mature and contain the highest concentration of active compounds.

Storage and Shelf Life

Notopterygium incisum should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Traditional and Historical Uses

Notopterygium incisum has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for its ability to dispel wind-damp, relieve pain, and promote circulation, particularly in treating conditions such as rheumatism, arthritis, and musculoskeletal disorders.
