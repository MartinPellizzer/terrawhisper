Notopterygium Incisum (Notopterygium incisum): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Notopterygium Incisum (Notopterygium incisum) is a flowering plant native to the mountainous regions of China, known for its traditional use in treating respiratory and musculoskeletal conditions.

What is Notopterygium Incisum (Notopterygium Incisum)?

Notopterygium incisum, also known as Qiang Huo in traditional Chinese medicine, is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of China, Korea, and Japan, characterized by its aromatic rhizomes and yellow flowers.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Notopterygium incisum, also known as Notopterygium, is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of Asia, belonging to the family Apiaceae, and is characterized by its distinctive pinnate leaves and aromatic, rhizomatous root system.

Common Names and Synonyms

Common Names and Synonyms include Notopterygium Incisum, also known as Chinese Angelica, Fang Feng, and Hu Fang, with additional regional names such as Xian Feng and Xian Fang in traditional Chinese herbal medicine.

Plant Description and Morphology

Notopterygium incisum is a perennial herb with a straight, cylindrical rootstock, trifoliate leaves with serrated margins, and small, yellowish-green flowers arranged in loose panicles.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Notopterygium incisum is native to the mountainous regions of East Asia, particularly China, Korea, and Japan, where it thrives in cool, moist environments such as forests and rocky slopes.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots and rhizomes of Notopterygium incisum are primarily used medicinally in traditional Chinese medicine.

Phytochemical Composition

Notopterygium incisum contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including essential oils, flavonoids, lignans, and alkaloids, which contribute to its therapeutic properties in traditional herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Notopterygium incisum exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic, and immunomodulatory effects, primarily due to the presence of active compounds such as volatile oils, sesquiterpene lactones, and flavonoids.

Mechanisms of Action

Notopterygium incisum exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic properties, which are attributed to the presence of bioactive compounds such as ligustilide and notoginsenoside, which modulate inflammatory pathways and reduce pain and fever.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Notopterygium incisum is used to treat rheumatoid arthritis, rheumatic pain, colds with chills, headaches, toothaches, skin rashes, and as a general remedy for wind-cold invasion in traditional Chinese medicine.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Notopterygium incisum is typically prepared as a dried root decoction, tincture, or powder, with the root being harvested in late summer or early autumn and processed to remove impurities before use in traditional herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Notopterygium incisum for adults is 3-9 grams per day, divided into two or three doses, and it is usually taken in the form of a decoction or powdered root.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

Drug Interactions

Notopterygium incisum may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the nervous system or blood pressure, and it is important to consult a healthcare professional before combining it with other drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Notopterygium incisum is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause gastrointestinal upset, and its safety during pregnancy, lactation, and in children has not been fully established.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Notopterygium incisum is typically cultivated in mountainous regions with cool climates and well-drained soil, often in the wild or in controlled environments such as gardens or specialized herbal farms.

Processing and Extraction

Storage and Shelf Life

Notopterygium incisum should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence
