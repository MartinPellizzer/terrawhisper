Phyllanthus acidus

What's the taxonomical classification of Phyllanthus acidus?

Phyllanthus acidus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it falls into the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic lineage through the order Malpighiales, it is placed within the family Phyllanthaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Phyllanthus, and its unique species name, acidus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Phyllanthus acidus has a small to medium-sized tree structure characterized by thin, spreading branches and a dense, rounded canopy of pinnately compound leaves. These leaves consist of many small, oblong leaflets that grow in a single plane along the rachis, giving the branches a feathery appearance. The plant produces small, greenish-white flowers that emerge from the axils of the leaves in clusters. Following pollination, the tree develops small, ribbed, globose fruits that transition from green to a pale yellow color when ripe. The bark is typically thin and greyish, providing a smooth texture to the main trunk.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of Southeast Asia and the Indian subcontinent, specifically including countries like India, Malaysia, and Indonesia. Because it thrives in warm, humid climates, it has been widely introduced to other tropical zones across the globe. It is now commonly cultivated in parts of Africa, the Caribbean, and Central and South America. Many populations have become naturalized in these regions due to its ability to grow in diverse tropical soil types. Its successful spread is largely attributed to human cultivation for its edible fruit.

What are the pharmacological activities of Phyllanthus acidus?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antibacterial, and antimicrobial pharmacological activities that make it a significant subject for medicinal research. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize harmful free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also demonstrates potent antimicrobial and antibacterial effects by inhibiting the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory actions assist in managing swelling and pain by modulating immune responses. Finally, preliminary studies suggest that certain compounds within the plant may possess anti-cancer potential by interfering with tumor cell progression.

As discussed in a study, the refined nonpolar extracts of Phyllanthus acidus leaves showed significant cytotoxic effect against BEAAS-2B and L02 normal cell lines with IC50 values of 2.15 and 1.64 mg/mL, respectively. Four new rare dichapetalins, pacidusins A-D, were isolated and exhibited moderate cytotoxicity against BEAAS-2B and L02 normal cell lines with IC50 values ranging from 12.44 to 22.55 μM. These same compounds showed cytotoxicity against five human cancer cell lines with IC50 values ranging from 3.38 to 22.38 μM. Analytical HPLC showed that the leaves contained these four isolated dichapetalins with a total yield of 0.488 mg/g of dry plant material. These findings indicate that while the leaves may not be suitable for consumption in large quantities, they demonstrate potency as anti-cancer agents.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds phyllanthin, pacidusins A-D, phyllaciduloids A-D, hypophyllanthin, and kaempferol. These specific phytochemicals are responsible for the plant's diverse pharmacological activities within the body. The presence of phyllanthin and its derivatives like phyllaciduloids is often linked to hepatoprotective properties. Additionally, the flavonoid kaempferol contributes significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Together, these complex chemical constituents form the basis of the plant's therapeutic potential.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal properties of Phyllanthus acidus (L.) Skeels were assessed alongside 10 other species from the Euphorbiaceae family. Researchers examined the dried leaves and stems of the plant through methanol and water extractions. The study specifically screened for hepatoprotective activity at a concentration of 50 µg/mL against tert-butyl hydroperoxide induced toxicity in HepG2 cells. Additionally, the research included the determination of phyllanthin and hypophyllanthin contents within these species.
