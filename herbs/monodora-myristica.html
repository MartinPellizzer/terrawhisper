Monodora myristica

What's the taxonomical classification of Monodora myristica?

Monodora myristica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is classified under the subclass Magnoliidae and follows the order Magnoliales. The plant is a member of the family Annonaceae, specifically situated within the genus Monodora, and is identified by its specific epithet, myristica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Monodora myristica has woody climbing vines characterized by large, simple, alternate leaves that are broadly ovate with entire margins and distinct pinnate venation. The stems are robust and can climb through forest canopies using their twisting growth habit. Its inflorescences are typically solitary or in small clusters, bearing fleshy flowers that are often dull in color to attract specific pollinators. The fruit is a large, fleshy berry that turns a dark color when ripe and contains multiple seeds. This plant's overall structure is adapted for life in dense tropical environments where light competition is high.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical rainforests of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across regions such as Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand, and parts of the Philippines. It thrives primarily within the humid, low-altitude ecosystems of the Sundaland biodiversity hotspot. Within these dense jungles, the species is often found growing in shaded areas where soil moisture remains consistently high. Its distribution follows the patterns of equatorial climates that provide the necessary warmth and rainfall for its survival. Because it relies on specific tropical conditions, its presence is limited to these localized maritime and continental forest zones.

What are the pharmacological activities of Monodora myristica?

This plant has antioxidant, antibacterial, antifungal, antimicrobial, and acetylcholinesterase inhibitory activities. Its chemical constituents provide significant protection against oxidative stress by neutralizing free radicals in biological systems. The plant also demonstrates potent antimicrobial properties, effectively inhibiting the growth of various bacterial and fungal pathogens. Research indicates that its extracts can disrupt the cellular structures of microbes to prevent infection. Furthermore, the specific inhibition of acetylcholinesterase makes it a subject of interest for potential neuroprotective applications.

As discussed in a study, Monodora myristica exhibits antimicrobial, antibiofilm, and enzyme inhibitory activities including Acetylcholinesterase, Butyrylcholinesterase, urease, and tyrosinase inhibition. GC-MS analysis of Methanol-Dicloromethane (50:50) extracts identified 9 compounds in the plant. It demonstrated minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) values of 0.625 mg/mL on C. albicans and 2.5 mg/mL on E. coli. The plant showed a lowest MIC value of 0.1325 mg/mL on S. aureus. Regarding violacein inhibition in Chromobacterium violaceum CV12472, Monodora myristica reached 75.1% ± 2.5% at MIC and 15.5% ± 1.1% at MIC/8. Finally, the plant showed higher motility inhibition on P. aeruginosa PA01 than the other tested spice.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds apiagenin, caffeic acid, catechine, quercetin, and rutin. These specific bioactive molecules are recognized for their significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties within the human body. Apiagenin and quercetin serve as potent flavonoids that help stabilize cellular structures against oxidative stress. Caffeic acid and catechine provide additional protective benefits by neutralizing free radicals during metabolic processes. Finally, rutin contributes to vascular health and strengthens capillary walls through its unique chemical structure.

As discussed in a study, Monodora myristica contains several polyphenols identified through HPLC analysis, including 3,4-OH-benzoic, caffeic, gallic, O- and P- coumaric, syringic, and vanillic acids. The plant also contains alcohols such as tyrosol and OH-tyrosol, along with theobromine, quercetin, rutin, catechine, and apigenin. The aqueous ethanol extract of the bark showed a high content of polyphenolic compounds at 21.44 ± 0.24 mg caffeic acid/g of dried extract. This same bark extract contained 5.69 ± 0.07 quercetin equivalent mg/g of flavonoid of dried weight. These extracts demonstrate scavenging properties against DPPH(•), OH(•), NO(•), and ABTS(•) radicals while showing protective potential on superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase, and peroxidase activities.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cadmium-induced hepatotoxicity, hepatitis, hepatic damages, liver damage, and liver toxicity. Researchers have studied its medicinal properties to determine how it mitigates oxidative stress in the body. It shows significant potential in protecting hepatic cells from chemical insults caused by heavy metals. By reducing inflammation, the plant helps prevent the progression of various forms of liver damage. These protective effects suggest it could be a valuable resource in managing acute and chronic liver toxicity.
