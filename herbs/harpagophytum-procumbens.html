What to know about Harpagophytum procumbens (devil's claw) before using it medicinally

Harpagophytum procumbens, commonly known as devil's claw, is a herb that has been traditionally used for its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, which can help alleviate symptoms of arthritis, fibromyalgia, and other conditions.

As a hardy succulent, it thrives in arid environments and can be cultivated in a variety of conditions, making it a popular choice for gardeners and farmers. The plant's botanical name reflects its unique botanical characteristics, including its paired claw-like appendages, which are used to extract nutrients and water from the soil.

The use of Harpagophytum procumbens has a long history, dating back to the indigenous peoples of southern Africa, who have used the plant for centuries to treat a range of ailments and injuries.

What are the medicinal properties of Harpagophytum procumbens?

Harpagophytum procumbens helps with pain relief, particularly for arthritis, back pain, and headaches. It also aids in reducing inflammation and improving sleep quality. Clinical trials support its efficacy in treating chronic pain.

The active constituents of devil's claw include harpagoside, a sesquiterpene glycoside, and iridoid glycosides. Harpagoside is responsible for its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. Other constituents, such as iridoid glycosides, may contribute to its sedative effects.

The tubers of the plant are the primary source of medicinal compounds. They contain a higher concentration of harpagoside compared to other parts. The roots and stems are also used, although to a lesser extent.

Improper use of devil's claw may cause side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Interactions with certain medications, such as blood thinners and diabetes medications, are also possible.

Precautions when using devil's claw medicinally include pregnant or breastfeeding women, individuals with allergies, and those with underlying medical conditions. Consume in moderation, and consult with a healthcare professional before using as a supplement.

What are the horticulural aspects of Harpagophytum procumbens?

Harpagophytum procumbens grow in full sun to partial shade and require a well-draining soil with a pH range of 5.5 to 7.5. The plant thrives in temperatures between 15°C and 25°C, with high humidity and adequate air circulation.

When planting, dig a hole that is 30cm deep and 60cm wide, adding organic matter such as compost or manure to improve soil fertility. Plant the stem 10-15cm below soil level and water thoroughly, then reduce watering as the plant establishes. Space plants 1-2 meters apart.

For optimal harvesting, allow the plant to grow for at least 2 years, then cut back the stems to about 10cm from the ground to encourage new growth. Harvest the tubers in autumn, 3-4 months after flowering has finished. Harvest the tubers when the leaves have turned yellow and the plant is dormant.

Common pests include aphids, whiteflies, and mealybugs, while diseases such as root rot, leaf spot, and powdery mildew can affect the plant. Regular monitoring and treatment with insecticides and fungicides can help control infestations and prevent damage to the plant.
