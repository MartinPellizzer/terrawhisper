Feverfew

Harpagophytum procumbens

Ashwagandha is a prominent medicinal herb in Ayurvedic medicine, classified as a rasayana (rejuvenator). It is primarily utilized as an adaptogen to help the body manage physical and chemical stress.

Family
Solanaceae (Nightshade)

Native Region
India, Middle East, Africa

Part Used
Root, Leaf, Berry

Botanical Identification

Feverfew, scientifically known as Harpagophytum procumbens, belongs to the Asteraceae family. Common names include common feverfew and African snakeroot. It is a perennial herb native to southern Africa. The plant has dark green, serrated leaves and yellow flowers. It is also known by various regional names in herbal literature.

Active Compounds

Feverfew contains key active compounds such as terpenoids, flavonoids, and phenolic acids. These compounds work together to reduce inflammation and pain, with terpenoids playing a central role in its anti-inflammatory effects. Flavonoids and phenolic acids enhance these properties by providing antioxidant support.

  • Terpenoid
  • Flavonoid
  • Phenolic acid

Therapeutic Indications

System Condidtion Action
Musculoskeletal Osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, joint pain, gout, muscle pain Anti-inflammatory, analgesic
Digestive Inflammatory bowel disease, gastritis, gastrointestinal inflammation, digestive inflammation, colitis Anti-inflammatory
Integumentary Skin infections, skin inflammation, psoriasis, inflammatory skin conditions Antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory

Preparation Methods

Decoction: Used for joint pain and inflammation due to its anti-inflammatory properties.

Extract: Used for arthritis and muscle pain due to its analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects.

Tincture: Used for pain relief and inflammation due to its bioavailability and active compound content.

Safety Profile

Feverfew is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses for short periods. However, it may cause gastrointestinal upset, headaches, or allergic reactions in some individuals. Long-term use or high doses can lead to more severe side effects, including skin reactions and interactions with blood-thinning medications.
