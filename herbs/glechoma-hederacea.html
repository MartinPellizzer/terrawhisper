Scientific and Botanical Profile

Ground Ivy, with botanical name Glechoma hederacea, is a perennial herbaceous plant belonging to the family Lamiaceae, commonly known as the mint family.

It is also referred to by several common names, including Creeping Charlie, Field Ivy, False Pennywort, Alpine Ivy, and Alpine Violet, reflecting its widespread distribution and varied habitats. Native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, Ground Ivy has been introduced to many regions around the world, often becoming invasive in moist, shaded areas. Morphologically, it features a creeping rhizomatous stem that forms dense mats, with opposite, heart-shaped leaves that are hairy and have a distinctive violet or purple underside.

Its small, tubular flowers are typically blue or violet, arranged in whorls, and it produces small, oval berries that are dispersed by birds.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Glechoma hederacea has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its potential therapeutic properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare it, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant material for a longer period. Due to limited clinical studies, usage should be conservative, with short durations and careful monitoring to avoid adverse effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Glechoma hederacea can be used cautiously for its potential anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties, but it is important to be aware of its safety profile.

While generally considered safe in small amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, especially with prolonged or high-dose use. There is limited research on its interactions with medications, but it may interfere with certain drugs, particularly those affecting the central nervous system or blood clotting, so caution is advised. Special populations such as pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid using glechoma hederacea due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to consult a qualified healthcare professional, start with low doses, and monitor for adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Glechoma hederacea grows best in moist, well-drained soil in partial to full shade, making it ideal for woodland gardens or shaded borders.

It thrives in cool climates and requires consistent moisture, though it can tolerate short periods of drought once established. To ensure healthy growth, mulching around the base of the plant helps retain soil moisture and suppress weeds, while regular watering during dry spells is essential. Harvesting should occur in early spring or late summer when the leaves are lush and aromatic, using clean scissors to cut the top 10-15 cm of the plant to encourage regrowth.

For storage, the harvested leaves should be spread in a single layer in a shaded, well-ventilated area to dry completely, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to preserve their medicinal potency.
