Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea) is a creeping perennial herb commonly found in moist, shaded areas, known for its medicinal properties and traditional use in treating respiratory and skin conditions.

What is Ground Ivy (Glechoma Hederacea)?

Ground Ivy, or Glechoma hederacea, is a creeping perennial herb native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa, characterized by its heart-shaped leaves, white flowers, and ability to spread rapidly through runners, commonly found in moist, shaded environments.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Ground Ivy, scientifically known as Glechoma hederacea, is a low-growing perennial herbaceous plant belonging to the family Boraginaceae, characterized by its creeping growth habit, heart-shaped leaves, and small blue or purple flowers.

Common Names and Synonyms

Ground Ivy, also known as Aleurites, Creeping Charlie, and Lesser Periwinkle, is a common name for the plant Glechoma hederacea, which is widely used in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits.

Plant Description and Morphology

Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea) is a low-growing perennial herb with creeping stolons, heart-shaped leaves that are hairy on the underside, and small violet-blue flowers that bloom in early spring.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea) is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, and has been introduced to North America and other regions, often thriving in moist, shaded environments such as woodlands, stream banks, and rocky crevices.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves and aerial parts of Ground Ivy are primarily used medicinally, often harvested during the flowering period for maximum potency.

Phytochemical Composition

Ground Ivy contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, iridoids, phenolic acids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic effects.

Pharmacological Properties

Ground Ivy contains compounds such as rosmarinic acid, flavonoids, and essential oils that exhibit antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties, contributing to its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Mechanisms of Action

Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea) exerts its effects through the presence of bioactive compounds such as rosmarinic acid, flavonoids, and essential oils, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties, contributing to its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Ground ivy is used to treat respiratory tract infections, coughs, bronchitis, sore throats, skin infections, wounds, and digestive issues such as indigestion and nausea.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Ground Ivy is typically prepared as a topical poultice, tea, or tincture, with the leaves and flowers harvested during early spring to ensure potency and optimal medicinal properties.

Dosage and Administration

There is limited standardized dosage information for Ground Ivy in herbal medicine, but typical internal use ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried herb per day, often prepared as a tea, while topical applications typically involve 1 to 2 tablespoons of a fresh leaf poultice or a diluted tincture applied directly to the affected area.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Ground Ivy is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal upset, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals, particularly with prolonged or excessive use.

Drug Interactions

Ground Ivy may interact with medications that affect the liver or central nervous system, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects such as sedation, drowsiness, or impaired coordination when used concurrently with sedatives, antidepressants, or anticoagulants.

Toxicity and Precautions

Ground Ivy is generally considered safe when used in small amounts as a culinary herb, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal upset in some individuals and should be avoided during pregnancy and lactation due to limited safety data.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Ground Ivy is typically propagated through stem cuttings or by sowing seeds in well-drained soil, preferring partial shade and moist conditions, though it can thrive in a variety of soil types.

Processing and Extraction

Ground Ivy is typically processed by harvesting the fresh leaves and stems during the early spring or late summer when the plant is most potent, then drying them in a shaded, well-ventilated area to preserve their medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, in an airtight container to maintain potency and prevent moisture damage, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea) has primarily focused on its potential medicinal properties, including its antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory effects, with some studies suggesting possible applications in treating respiratory infections and skin conditions.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Ground Ivy has been historically used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to treat respiratory ailments, digestive issues, and skin conditions, with records dating back to ancient Greek and Roman herbalists.

Regulatory Status

Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea) is not currently regulated as a medicinal herb by the FDA, but its use in herbal medicine is subject to the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994, which governs the labeling, safety, and marketing of dietary supplements.
