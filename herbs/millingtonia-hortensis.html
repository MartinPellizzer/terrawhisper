Millingtonia hortensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Millingtonia hortensis?

Millingtonia hortensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant falls under the order Lamiales and is a member of the family Bignoniaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Millingtonia and the species hortensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Millingtonia hortensis has large, bipinnate leaves composed of numerous small, oblong leaflets that grow in an opposite arrangement along the stems. The tree typically features a smooth, greyish bark and produces striking, fragrant white flowers that emerge in large, terminal clusters. These blossoms consist of several spreading petals and prominent stamens that attract various pollinators. The plant also develops woody, elongated seed pods that eventually split to release small, winged seeds for wind dispersal. These structural characteristics reflect its adaptation to tropical forest environments.

What are the pharmacological activities of Millingtonia hortensis?

This plant has antimicrobial, antimutagenicity, antiproliferative, apoptosis induction, and antiaaging pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate the ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms effectively. Research also highlights its capacity to prevent genetic damage through antimutagenic properties and slow cellular aging processes. Furthermore, the plant exhibits potent antiproliferative effects by triggering apoptosis induction in specific cancer cell lines. These combined medicinal attributes suggest significant potential for developing new therapeutic agents.

As discussed in a study, the tree Millingtonia hortensis Linn. is inhabited by the endophytic Streptomyces cavourensis strain MH16. The antimicrobial metabolites produced by this strain found in M. hortensis show great potential for the biodiscovery of new anti-infective drugs. These metabolites effectively inhibited methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, and Candida albicans, along with other multi drug-resistant pathogens. The minimum inhibitory concentration of these antimicrobial metabolites was recorded at 25-100 μg mL-1.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the flavonoid hispidulin along with various other bioactive phytochemicals that contribute to its therapeutic potential. These medicinal compounds work through diverse mechanisms to provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the body. Research suggests that hispidulin specifically plays a role in protecting cells from oxidative stress. In addition to this, the plant may house other polyphenols that support overall metabolic health. The synergy of these different elements makes the plant a subject of interest in pharmacological studies.

As discussed in a study, the aqueous extract of Millingtonia hortensis L. flowers contains the active ingredient hispidulin. This extract significantly increased cell viability and decreased both apoptotic cells and ROS production in a dose-dependent manner compared to the aging group (P < 0.01). Treatment with Millingtonia hortensis reduced the number of SA-β-gal-positive cells (P < 0.01) while promoting the Sirt-1 protein. Additionally, Millingtonia hortensis promoted synaptic plasticity by decreasing acetylcholinesterase activity and increasing synaptophysin expression in aging neurons (P < 0.01).

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating Alzheimer's disease, colon cancer, diabetes mellitus, neurodegenerative disease, and asthma. Its medicinal properties are being studied for their potential to mitigate the progression of various neurodegenerative diseases. Researchers are also investigating its efficacy in managing metabolic issues related to diabetes mellitus. Furthermore, bioactive compounds within the plant show promise in addressing respiratory inflammation associated with asthma. Studies continue to explore how its chemical constituents might inhibit the growth of cells linked to colon cancer.

As discussed in a study, the aqueous extract of Millingtonia hortensis L. (MH) from flower was evaluated for its in vitro effects on hydrogen peroxide (H2O2)-induced senescence in SK-N-SH cells. MH significantly increased cell viability and decreased both apoptotic cells and ROS production in a dose-dependent manner compared to the aging group (P < 0.01). The treatment also reduced the number of SA-β-gal-positive cells (P < 0.01) while promoting the Sirt-1 protein. Furthermore, MH promoted synaptic plasticity by decreasing acetylcholinesterase activity and increasing synaptophysin expression in aging neurons compared to the aging group (P < 0.01). The active ingredient in MH, hispidulin, was revealed to have similarly effects.
