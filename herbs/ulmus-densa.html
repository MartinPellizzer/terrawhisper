Ulmus densa

What's the taxonomical classification of Ulmus densa?

Ulmus densa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant follows the biological lineage of the order Rosales. It is further categorized under the family Ulmaceae and is situated within the genus Ulmus, eventually reaching its specific identification as the species densa.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Ulmus densa contains bioactive compounds including polyphenols, flavonoids, and polysaccharides that contribute to its therapeutic properties. These chemical constituents are primarily concentrated in the bark and inner tissues of the plant. Researchers have identified specific tannins within the species that exhibit significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Such compounds work to neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress in biological systems. Furthermore, the presence of mucilage helps soothe irritated mucosal membranes when used in traditional preparations.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ulmus densa?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat inflammatory conditions, digestive disorders, skin ailments, and respiratory issues. Its bark contains mucilage which can help soothe irritated mucous membranes in the gut and throat. Practitioners often utilize it to alleviate symptoms of gastritis and various types of diarrhea. When applied topically, the plant serves as a remedy for minor wounds and skin irritations. It is also recognized for its potential to reduce swelling and discomfort in different parts of the body.

What are the herbal preparations of Ulmus densa?

Ulmus densa is prepared as decoctions, infusions, powders, or topical pastes using its bark and leaves to extract therapeutic compounds. Practitioners typically boil the woody bark in water to create a concentrated decoction for internal use. The softer leaves can be steeped in hot water as a mild infusion to soothe digestive or respiratory issues. In some traditional applications, the plant material is ground into a fine powder and mixed with liquid to form a medicinal paste for skin ailments. These various forms allow the plant's mucilaginous properties to be utilized effectively for both ingestion and external application.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Ulmus densa can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, respiratory irritation from airborne pollen, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Individuals with sensitivities to the Ulmaceae family may experience redness, itching, or swelling upon touching the leaves or bark. Inhalation of fine particles or pollen from the tree can trigger sneezing, coughing, or more severe asthma-like symptoms in sensitive people. While direct toxicity is not extensively documented, the presence of various plant compounds necessitates caution during handling. Always use protective gloves and avoid contact with mucous membranes to minimize these risks.
