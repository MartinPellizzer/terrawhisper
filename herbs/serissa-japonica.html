Serissa japonica

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Serissa japonica has small, elliptical to lanceolate leaves that measure approximately 1 to 3 centimeters in length and are arranged oppositely along its woody, branching stems. These leaves exhibit a glossy texture and a deep green color on the upper surface, while the undersides often appear slightly lighter. The plant produces clusters of tiny, white, five-petaled flowers that emerge from the axils of the foliage. Following the blooming period, the plant develops small, globose drupes that transition from green to a bright red color when mature. Its overall growth habit is typically shrubby, allowing it to form dense, rounded thickets.

      What are the herbal preparations of Serissa japonica?

      Serissa japonica is prepared primarily as a decoction, a tea made by boiling the plant material, or as a topical infusion used for external applications. Practitioners often simmer the leaves and stems in water to extract the active phytochemicals for medicinal consumption. These liquid extracts can also be concentrated into more potent tinctures using alcohol as a solvent to preserve the plant's properties. Additionally, some traditional methods involve creating poultices from crushed fresh leaves to treat skin irritations directly. These various forms allow the plant's chemical constituents to be utilized for both internal and external therapeutic purposes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Serissa japonica can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential digestive distress if ingested by humans or animals. Contact with the plant's leaves or stems may trigger contact dermatitis characterized by redness, itching, or a localized rash in sensitive individuals. While specific toxicity levels are not extensively documented in humans, the ingestion of any part of the plant can lead to nausea or stomach upset. Pet owners should be particularly cautious as many ornamental shrubs can cause similar gastrointestinal discomfort in cats and dogs. Monitoring for these symptoms is essential if accidental exposure occurs.
