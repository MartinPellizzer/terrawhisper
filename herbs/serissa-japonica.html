Serissa japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Serissa japonica?

Serissa japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Gentianaales. This plant is further categorized into the family Rubiaceae, which is the group to which its genus, Serissa, is assigned. Ultimately, the specific identity of this organism is defined by its species name, japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Serissa japonica has small, elliptical to lanceolate leaves that measure approximately 1 to 3 centimeters in length and are arranged oppositely along its woody, branching stems. These leaves exhibit a glossy texture and a deep green color on the upper surface, while the undersides often appear slightly lighter. The plant produces clusters of tiny, white, five-petaled flowers that emerge from the axils of the foliage. Following the blooming period, the plant develops small, globose drupes that transition from green to a bright red color when mature. Its overall growth habit is typically shrubby, allowing it to form dense, rounded thickets.

What are the pharmacological activities of Serissa japonica?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including antiviral, antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively inhibit the replication of certain viruses within host cells. The presence of specific polyphenols and flavonoids contributes to its ability to combat oxidative stress and reduce cellular inflammation. Additionally, its extracts demonstrate the capacity to suppress the growth of various pathogenic bacteria. These diverse biological effects make Serissa japonica a subject of interest for developing new therapeutic agents.

According to a study published by "Biological & pharmaceuctical bulletin", the hot water extract of Serissa japonica was evaluated for its in vitro anti-herpes simplex viruses activity, including HSV-1 and HSV-2, and its anti-adenoviruses activity, including ADV-3, ADV-8, and ADV-11. Among the twelve traditionally used medicinal plants tested, Serissa japonica possessed a broad spectrum of antiviral activity. The study utilized an XTT-based colorimetric assay to determine these effects. Additionally, a cell cytotoxic assay demonstrated that the CC50 values for the tested extracts were higher than their EC50 values.
