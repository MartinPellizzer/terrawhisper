Tylophora kerrii

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tylophora kerrii has climbing, herbaceous vines characterized by opposite, ovate to elliptic leaves with entire margins and an acute apex. The stems are slender and often twining, allowing the plant to ascend through surrounding vegetation. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in clusters, typically featuring a corolla that is pale or white in color. The reproductive structures include a specialized gynostegium, which is a hallmark of the Apocynaceae family. These morphological traits collectively define its identity as a climbing perennial within its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically found in countries such as Thailand, Vietnam, and Malaysia. The species is often distributed along the edges of dense vegetation and within secondary forest habitats. Its presence is typically tied to specific elevation ranges that support its climbing growth habit.     What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause significant toxicity due to the presence of cardiac glycosides which may lead to heart palpitations, nausea, vomiting, and dangerous changes in heart rhythm. Consuming parts of the plant can disrupt the electrical activity of the heart, potentially resulting in life-threatening arrhythmias. Other physiological reactions might include abdominal pain, diarrhea, and dizziness as the body reacts to the chemical compounds. Because these glycosides mimic the effects of digitalis, even small amounts can be hazardous to human health. It is essential to avoid ingestion to prevent severe cardiovascular distress or systemic poisoning.
