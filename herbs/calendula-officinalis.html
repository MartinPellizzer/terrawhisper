Calendula Officinalis Uses, Benefits, And Remedies

Calendula officinalis, commonly known as the pot marigold, is a flowering plant native to southern Europe and western Asia.

It is widely used in herbal medicine for its anti-inflammatory, antifungal, and wound-healing properties.

The bioactive constituents in calendula, such as flavonoids, triterpenes, and carotenoids, contribute to its therapeutic actions.

This herb can be prepared as an infusion, tincture, salve, or ointment to address skin irritations, digestive issues, and respiratory ailments.

Medicinal Parts

Calendula officinalis flower is widely recognized for its therapeutic properties and is commonly used in herbal medicine.

The bright orange or yellow petals of the flower contain bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, triterpenes, and carotenoids, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and wound-healing effects. These properties make calendula flower a popular ingredient in topical treatments for skin conditions like eczema, psoriasis, and minor burns.

In addition to its external applications, the flower is also used to make teas and tinctures that support digestive health and reduce inflammation in the gastrointestinal tract. The leaves of the calendula plant, while less commonly used than the flowers, also contain similar beneficial compounds and are sometimes utilized in herbal infusions to promote liver function and alleviate digestive discomfort.

Both the flower and leaf of Calendula officinalis offer a range of medicinal benefits, making them valuable components in traditional and modern herbal remedies.

Herbal Preparations

Calendula officinalis teas are a popular and simple way to harness the herb's anti-inflammatory and soothing properties, often used to support digestive health and alleviate mild gastrointestinal discomfort.

To prepare the tea, dried calendula flowers are steeped in hot water for several minutes, creating a mild, slightly floral beverage that can be consumed throughout the day. In addition to teas, calendula is commonly made into tinctures by soaking the flowers in alcohol, which concentrates its active compounds for topical or internal use. Calendula oils, often infused with carrier oils like olive or jojoba, are widely used in skincare products for their healing and protective benefits, particularly for eczema and minor burns.

The versatility of calendula extends to other preparations such as creams, ointments, and poultices, which are applied externally to treat skin irritations, wounds, and inflammation, making it a valuable herb in both traditional and modern herbal medicine.

Side Effects of calendula officinalis

Calendula officinalis eyes become red due to its potential irritant properties, which can cause inflammation and discomfort in the sensitive eye area.

While calendula is often used for its soothing effects, some individuals may experience skin irritation, including redness, rash, or even blistering upon topical application. The herb can also lead to a burning sensation on the skin, which may be mistaken for a positive reaction but is actually a sign of an adverse response. In more severe cases, skin may become dry or show swelling, indicating an allergic or hypersensitive reaction.

Additionally, oral consumption of calendula may result in stomach upset, cramps, or even numbness in the mouth, highlighting the importance of cautious use and consulting a healthcare professional before incorporating it into one's routine.
