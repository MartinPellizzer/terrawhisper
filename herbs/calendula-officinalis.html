Calendula officinalis

What's the taxonomical classification of Calendula officinalis?

Calendula officinalis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As a member of the order Asterales, it falls under the family Asteraceae, a group known for its composite flower heads. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Calendula and the species officinalis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Calendula officinalis has an annual or biennial growth habit characterized by branched, hairy stems that reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bear composite flower heads in shades of orange or yellow. The leaves are lanceolate to oblong in shape, featuring a somewhat succulent texture and a slightly sticky, pubescent surface. Each flower head consists of ray florets around the perimeter and disc florets at the center, which are surrounded by an involucral bract. The plant produces small, angular seeds that are typically dark in color and can be dispersed by wind or gravity. These morphological traits collectively enable the plant to thrive in diverse temperate environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean region but has naturalized in many temperate zones across Europe, North Africa, and parts of Asia. It thrives in various climates where it can access well-drained soil and ample sunlight. In many parts of North America, it is frequently found growing wild in gardens and disturbed sites due to its ability to adapt to local conditions. Its widespread distribution is further aided by its ability to grow in both cultivated landscapes and unmanaged natural habitats. This adaptability allows it to establish itself in diverse geographical locations across the globe.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant Calendula officinalis is cultivated by sowing seeds directly into well-drained soil in full sun or partial shade during the spring or autumn seasons.

It thrives in a variety of soil types provided they are rich in organic matter and maintain consistent moisture levels. Regular deadheading of the spent blooms encourages the plant to produce a continuous supply of vibrant flowers throughout the growing period. Gardeners often plant them as annuals or short-lived perennials to provide color and medicinal resources to the landscape.

Because they are relatively hardy, they can tolerate light frost and minimal pest interference.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Calendula officinalis contains medicinal compounds primarily located within the flower heads, which are harvested when they are fully bloomed. The petals and the receptacle of the flower are the most important parts used to create tinctures, salves, and oils. These floral components are rich in flavonoids, triterpenoids, and carotenoids that provide anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. While the entire plant can technically be used, the concentration of active therapeutic constituents is highest in the bright orange or yellow blossoms. This makes the flower heads the standard choice for treating skin irritations, wounds, and minor burns.

What are the pharmacological activities of Calendula officinalis?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, wound healing, and healing activities that make it a versatile therapeutic agent. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative stress and damage. The antimicrobial activity allows it to combat various bacterial and fungal pathogens effectively. Furthermore, its potent anti-inflammatory effects reduce swelling and redness in affected tissues. These combined properties significantly accelerate the wound healing process by promoting tissue regeneration and skin repair.

According to a study published by "Anti-cancer agents in medicinal chemistry", Calendula officinalis is addressed for its protective effects against the deleterious effects of ionizing radiation on the skin. The plant is utilized for its potential pharmacological activities including free radical scavenging, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, wound healing, and skin protective effects. These beneficial effects are investigated in the context of mitigating acute radiation dermatitis in humans undergoing curative radiotherapy. The review involved data mined from Google Scholar, PubMed, Embase, and the Cochrane Library for publications from 1901 up to July 2020.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including flavonoids, triterpenoids, rutin, kaempferol-3-O-rutinoside, and calendic acid. The presence of flavonoids provides the plant with significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Within this group, rutin and kaempferol-3-O-rutinoside serve as specific glycosides that support vascular health and cellular protection. The triterpenoids found in the tissues contribute to the plant's ability to soothe skin irritation and promote healing. Additionally, the unique fatty acid known as calendic acid plays a vital role in the overall chemical profile of the species.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion, tincture, oil, salve, essential oil, extract, or powder for various therapeutic applications. An infusion of the dried petals is commonly used to soothe skin irritations or create medicinal teas. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause allergic reactions, skin irritation, nausea, uterine contractions, and dizziness in some individuals. People with sensitivities to the Asteraceae family may experience contact dermatitis or localized rashes when applying it topically. If ingested in large quantities, it has been known to trigger gastrointestinal distress such as nausea. There are also concerns regarding its potential to stimulate uterine contractions, which makes it a risk during pregnancy. Some users may also report feeling dizziness following consumption.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Allergic reactions
  • Skin irritation
  • Nausea
  • Uterine contractions
