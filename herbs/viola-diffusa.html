Viola diffusa

What's the taxonomical classification of Viola diffusa?

Viola diffusa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Malpighiales, it is placed in the family Violaceae, which leads to its designation under the genus Viola. Ultimately, it is identified by its specific species name, diffusa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viola diffusa has prostrate, creeping stems that root at the nodes, small ovate to lanceolate leaves with crenate margins, and small violet flowers featuring a yellowish throat and delicate white streaks. The plant's growth habit is characterized by its spreading nature, allowing it to form dense mats across moist soil surfaces. Each leaf is typically petiolate and displays a soft, herbaceous texture. The flowers are zygomorphic, presenting a distinct structure with five petals and prominent reproductive organs centered in the corolla. These morphological traits assist the species in colonizing damp, shaded environments effectively.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viola diffusa?

This plant has anticancer pharmacological activity. Research into Viola diffusa suggests that certain phytochemicals within the species may inhibit the growth of malignant cells. These bioactive compounds work by interfering with the specific biological pathways required for tumor progression. Scientists continue to investigate the exact mechanisms by which these extracts exert their inhibitory effects. Further studies are necessary to fully understand the potential therapeutic applications of this plant in oncology.

According to a study published by "Phytomedicine : international journal of phytotherapy and phytopharmacology", thirty cyclotides were identified from Viola diffusa. The lead cycloide, Vdif A, exhibited the most potent cytotoxicity against A549 cells with an IC50 of 0.455 μM. This peptide dose-dependently suppressed proliferation at concentrations of 0.25-1 μM, induced S-phase arrest, triggered apoptosis, and caused DNA damage. In a Lewis lung carcinoma synegenic allograft model, Vdif A administered at 2-4 mg/kg inhibited tumor growth by 46.58%. These pharmacological activities occur through the regulation of CKS2 to inhibit non-small cell lung cancer growth.
