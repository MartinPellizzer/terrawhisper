Cinnamomum longepaniculatum

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cinnamomum longepaniculatum has lanceolate to elliptic leaves that are typically 5 to 15 centimeters long, possessing an entire margin and an acute or acuminate apex. The foliage is characterized by a smooth, coriaceous texture and an arrangement that is often opposite or sub-opposite along the woody stems. Its reproductive structures consist of elongated panicles, which give the species its specific epithet, bearing small, inconspicuous flowers. The fruit is a small, fleshy drupe that transitions in color as it matures. These morphological features collectively distinguish this species within the Lauraceae family.

      What are the herbal preparations of Cinnamomum longepaniculatum?

      Cinnamomum longepaniculatum is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical oil extracted from its bark and leaves. The woody bark is typically boiled in water for extended periods to concentrate its bioactive essential oils and aromatic compounds. These liquid extracts are often ingested to utilize their traditional medicinal properties. Additionally, the plant material can be processed into ointments or infused oils for application directly onto the skin. These various forms allow for both internal consumption and external therapeutic use.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Cinnamomum longepaniculatum can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, gastrointestinal upset, or dizziness if consumed in improper amounts. Individuals with sensitivities to the Lauraceae family should be particularly cautious when handling or ingesting this species. Potential skin irritation often manifests as redness, itching, or swelling at the site of contact. Some reports suggest that excessive ingestion might lead to nausea or abdominal discomfort. Monitoring for these physiological responses is essential when using this plant for medicinal or aromatic purposes.
