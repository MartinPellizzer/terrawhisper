Gentiana dahurica

What's the taxonomical classification of Gentiana dahurica?

Gentiana dahurica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is positioned in the subclass Magnoliidae and follows the order Gentianaales. It is a member of the family Gentianaceae, specifically falling under the genus Gentiana, and is identified by its unique species designation, dahurica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gentiana dahurica has lanceolate to ovate leaves that are oppositely arranged along a stem that typically reaches a height of 10 to 30 centimeters. The foliage is characterized by a smooth texture and a deep green color with entire margins. Its flowers are funnel-shaped or bell-shaped and display a vibrant blue or violet hue often marked by darker spots. These blooms emerge from the terminal end of the stem or from the upper leaf axils during the flowering season. The plant also features a woody taproot that provides structural stability within its natural habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the high-altitude regions of northeastern China, specifically within the provinces of Heilongjiang, Jilin, and Liaoning. It thrives primarily in alpine meadows and rocky slopes where the terrain is rugged and the climate is cool. Its distribution is closely tied to specific soil compositions and moisture levels found in these mountainous ecosystems. Because it prefers certain elevations, the species is often found in localized patches rather than continuous expanses. These environmental requirements limit its presence to specific ecological niches across its known range.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Gentiana dahurica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its dried roots and rhizomes. These underground structures are harvested and processed to be used in traditional formulations. The roots are valued for their bitter properties which help stimulate digestive functions and clear heat from the body. Practitioners often use the dried root material to treat various ailments related to inflammation and fever. This specific part of the plant is the most concentrated source of its therapeutic active ingredients.

What are the pharmacological activities of Gentiana dahurica?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, anti-rheumatic, antimicrobial, and antioxidant pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to alleviate pain and reduce swelling in various physiological systems. The plant also demonstrates effectiveness in combating microbial infections through its potent antimicrobial properties. Furthermore, its ability to neutralize free radicals provides significant antioxidant protection to cells. These combined therapeutic actions make it a valuable subject for studying natural treatments for inflammatory and infectious diseases.

As discussed in a study, Gentiana dahurica is used in traditional Chinese medicine as Qinjiao for the treatment of rheumatism, hepatitis, and pain. Chemical analysis of 56 Qinjiao samples included 3 from Gentiana dahurica. Bisphosphocholines were present in Gentiana dahurica, with irlbacholine as the major compound ranging from 2.0 to 6.2 mg per gram of dried material. This compound exhibited potent in vitro antifungal activity against Cryptococcus neoformans, Aspergillus fumigatus, Candida albicans, and Candida glabrata with minimum inhibitory concentration values of 0.63, 1.25, 10.0, and 5.0 μg/mL, respectively.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds gentiopicroside, loganic acid, sweroside, swertiamarin, and oleanolic acid. These specific iridoid glycosides and triterpenoids contribute to the biological activity of the species. Swertiamarin and gentiopicroside are particularly noted for their roles in stimulating digestive processes. The presence of loganic acid and sweroside further enhances the complex chemical profile of the plant. Additionally, the triterpene oleanolic acid provides significant therapeutic potential in various medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, Gentiana dahurica is a perennial herb used as a traditional Tibetan medicine due to its rich gentiopicroside constituents. The high-quality, chromosome-level genome of this species has a total length of 1,416.54 Mb. Research indicates that gentiopicrosides are mainly synthesized in the rhizomes, where they have the highest contents. This species underwent one whole-genome duplication event and had one additional species-specific whole-genome duplication after an ancient whole-genome triplication with other eudicots. These two whole-genome duplications contributed greatly to candidate genes involved in gentiopicroside biosynthesis.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating respiratory issues like cough and phlegm, inflammatory conditions such as sore throat and tonsillitis, and painful joint ailments like rheumatism. Its medicinal properties help clear excess mucus from the lungs to ease breathing. By reducing inflammation in the throat, it provides relief for swelling and discomfort caused by infections. Additionally, the plant is applied to alleviate the deep aches associated with rheumatic conditions. These diverse applications make it a significant component in traditional herbal remedies.

As discussed in a study, the flowers of Gentiana dahurica have been used as a Mongolian herb for curing cough sore throat and eliminating the phlegm due to its anti-inflammatory effect. The study identified 1 new lignan glycoside (1) along with 20 known compounds, which include 10 iridoid glycosides (2-11), three steroids (12-14), four lignans (15-18), one phenylpropanoid (19), and two triterpenes (20-21). Among the tested substances, triterpenoid 21 was the most active with an inhibitory value of 78% at a concentration of 30 μM. This same compound 21 displayed a potential inhibitory effect on NO production with an IC50 of 16.85 μM. Additionally, compound 21 displayed weak cytotoxicity on human myeloid leukemia HL-60 at an IC50 of 16.43 μM.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause gastrointestinal irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness in some individuals. These digestive symptoms often arise because the bitter compounds in the plant can stimulate the stomach lining too aggressively. Some users may experience significant abdominal pain or frequent bouts of diarrhea shortly after consumption. Beyond digestive issues, the plant is also known to trigger feelings of nausea and vomiting. Additionally, certain people may report experiencing dizziness as a systemic reaction to its chemical properties.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Gastrointestinal irritation
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Abdominal pain
  • Dizziness
