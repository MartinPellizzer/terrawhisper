Sedum sarmentosum

What's the taxonomical classification of Sedum sarmentosum?

Sedum sarmentosum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Saxifragales and the family Crassulaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Sedum, and its specific species name, sarmentosum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sedum sarmentosum has succulent, cylindrical, or slightly flattened leaves that are light green to yellowish-green and measure approximately 1 to 2 centimeters in length. These fleshy leaves are arranged in a spreading or creeping pattern along trailing stems. The plant produces small, star-shaped flowers that transition from pale yellow to a deeper golden hue during anthesis. These blossoms typically cluster in terminal cymes atop the stems. The overall growth habit is low-growing and mat-forming, allowing it to cover ground effectively.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad range across central and southern Europe, extending from the Atlantic coast of France through the Mediterranean basin to parts of Eastern Europe. It thrives in various microclimates ranging from temperate maritime regions to more arid, sun-drenched hillsides. While its primary natural habitat is found within these European territories, it has been widely introduced to other continents for use in rock gardens and succulent collections. In these non-native environments, it often colonizes rocky outcrops and well-drained slopes similar to its original range. Its ability to adapt to different soil types allows it to persist in diverse geographical settings across the globe.

What are the pharmacological activities of Sedum sarmentosum?

This plant has potent anticancer properties including anti-HCC activity, antioxidant capacity, antiproliferative effects, and cytotoxic activity. Researchers have identified that its bioactive compounds can effectively inhibit the growth of various malignant cell lines. The antioxidant mechanisms help protect cells from oxidative stress by neutralizing harmful free radicals. Furthermore, its cytotoxic activity allows it to induce programmed cell death in targeted tumor cells. These combined pharmacological actions highlight its significant therapeutic potential in oncology research.

As discussed in a study, an EtOAc extract of Sedum sarmentosum displayed a certain degree of cytotoxic activity. Bioassay-directed isolation of this extract yielded two new megastigmanes, compounds 1 and 2, along with 7 known flavonoids. When compounds 1-9 were tested against HepG2 and Hep3B hepatocellular carcinoma cell lines, they showed weak anti-HCC activity. In an ABTS radical cation-scavenging assay, compound 1 exhibited weak antioxidant activity at 0.039 μM Trolox equivalent, while compound 2 showed 0.042 μM Trolox equivalent. The flavonoid component quercetin, identified as compound 9, showed the highest antioxidant activities at 0.67 μM Trolox equivalent.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds isorhamnetin, kaempferol, quercetin, sedumosides A1, and triterpene delta-amyrone. These bioactive constituents are primarily classified as flavonoids and triterpenoids. The presence of quercetin and kaempferol often contributes to the antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties of the species. Additionally, the specific glycosides known as sedumosides A1 play a role in its unique chemical profile. The triterpene delta-amyrone further enhances the pharmacological potential of the plant's extracts.

As discussed in a study, Sedum sarmentosum contains medicinal compounds including total flavonoids, total phenolic, quercetin, kaempferol, and isorhamnetin. The accumulation of these bioactivity compounds was assessed under five light intensities of 100% full sunlight (G1), 77% full sunlight (G2), 60% full sunlight (G3), 38% full sunlight (G4), and 16% full sunlight (G5). The highest total flavonoids and phenolic contents were obtained in the G3 treatment. In contrast, the highest quercetin and isorhamnetin contents were obtained in the G1 treatment, while the difference in kaempferol content was not significant. Based on DPPH, FTC, and FRAP methods, the antioxidant activities of the aqueous extracts under G1 treatment were superior to the others. Results indicated that more than 60% full sunlight was the optimum condition to achieve high quality of Sedum sarmentosum.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating CCL4-induced liver injury, acute hepatitis, chronic hepatitis, hepatocellular carcinoma, and hepatoma. Research indicates that the medicinal properties of Sedum sarmentosum can help mitigate damage caused by chemical toxins in the liver. Its therapeutic potential extends to managing both sudden inflammatory conditions and long-term degenerative liver diseases. Furthermore, the plant shows promise in addressing serious malignancies such as hepatocellular carcinoma and hepatoma. These findings suggest that the plant may play a role in protecting hepatic cells from severe pathological changes.
