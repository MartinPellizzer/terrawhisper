Chloranthus fortunei

What's the taxonomical classification of Chloranthus fortunei?

Chloranthus fortunei belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows a specialized evolutionary lineage. The plant is positioned within the order Chloranthales and is a representative of the family Chloranthaceae. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Chloranthus, with its unique biological identity defined by its species name, fortunei.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Chloranthus fortunei has an upright, herbaceous growth habit characterized by opposite, ovate to elliptical leaves that measure up to 15 centimeters in length and possess serrated margins. The foliage typically features prominent venation and a bright green color that can transition to reddish hues in certain conditions. Small, inconspicuous white flowers emerge from the leaf axils in late spring, often appearing in small clusters. These flowers lack true petals, instead presenting prominent tepals that surround the reproductive structures. The plant develops a fleshy rhizomatous root system that allows it to spread effectively in shaded forest environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the central and southern regions of China, specifically spanning provinces such as Hubei, Hunan, Jiangxi, Zhejiang, and Fujian. It thrives primarily in moist, shaded environments within subtropical forest ecosystems. These habitats often include mountain slopes and valley floors where the soil remains consistently damp. Its natural range is characterized by temperate to subtropical climates that provide necessary humidity. Because of these specific environmental requirements, the species remains concentrated in these East Asian mountainous territories.

What are the pharmacological activities of Chloranthus fortunei?

This plant has anti-tumor, anti-inflammatory, antiproliferative, pro-apoptotic, and anti-HCC pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds function by suppressing the uncontrolled growth of malignant cells through various biochemical pathways. Specifically, the extract demonstrates significant antiproliferative effects that inhibit the cell cycle of cancerous tissues. It also induces pro-apoptotic signaling, which forces damaged cells to undergo programmed death. Furthermore, the plant shows targeted anti-HCC potential by fighting hepatocellular carcinoma development while simultaneously reducing systemic inflammation.

As discussed in a study, Chloranthus fortunei is used in folk decoctions to treat stagnant liver conditions like liver abscesses, cirrhosis, hepatitis, and liver cancer. The petroleum ether extracts of Chloranthus fortunei (PECFS) demonstrated the highest antiproliferative activity against MHCC97-H and HepG2 cells with IC50 values of 13.86 and 10.55 μg/mL. These extracts hinder the proliferation of HCC cells and promote apoptosis by upregulating the pro-apoptotic protein Bax and downregulating the anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2. The activity of PECFS is linked to the inhibition of cell cloning, invasion, and metastasis, as well as the arrest of the cell cycle at the G2/M phase. Furthermore, PECFS inhibits HCC cell viability by regulating the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway and suppresses tumor growth in BALB/c nude mice.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Henriol D, chlorafortulide, shizukaois E, shizukaois G, and shizukaois M. These specific bioactive molecules are responsible for much of the plant's unique pharmacological profile. Researchers study these substances to understand how they interact with various biological systems. The presence of these diverse chemical structures suggests a complex secondary metabolism within the plant tissues. Identifying these constituents is a critical step in developing new therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, the whole plant of Chloranthus fortunei contains a novel lindenane sesquiterpene with an unprecedented 18-membered triester ring named chlorafortulide (1). The plant also yields one known lindenane sesquiterpene (2) and nine known lindenane sesquiterpene dimers (3-11). Among these, Henriol D (4), shizukaols E (8), G (9), M (10), and O (11) demonstrated significant anti-inflammatory activities. Specifically, these compounds showed IC50 values of 1.90, 3.68, 1.95, 7.01, and 1.95 μM, respectively. These results were evaluated based on their inhibitory effects on lipopolysaccharide-induced nitric oxide production in RAW264.7 cells.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested by humans or animals. Contact with the foliage or stems may trigger localized redness, itching, or swelling on sensitive skin areas. Some individuals might experience more severe allergic reactions depending on their personal sensitivity to the plant's chemical compounds. If any part of the herb is consumed, it can lead to stomach aches, nausea, or vomiting. Careful handling and avoidance of ingestion are recommended to prevent these adverse physiological responses.
