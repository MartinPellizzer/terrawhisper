Landolphia owariensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Landolphia owariensis?

Landolphia owariensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Gentianaales and is a member of the family Apocynaceae. Its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Landolphia, with the unique identifier being the species owariensis.

What are the pharmacological activities of Landolphia owariensis?

This plant has antioxidant and neuroprotective pharmacological activities that contribute to its potential medicinal value. These antioxidant properties are crucial for preventing cellular damage caused by reactive oxygen species. Furthermore, its neuroprotective effects suggest a capacity to safeguard neuronal structures against degeneration and injury. Such multifaceted biological actions highlight the importance of studying this species for neurological health treatments.

As discussed in a study, Landolphia owariensis was assessed for its effect on alcohol-induced neurodegeneration of the cerebellar cortex in rats. The study utilized rats fed 5 g/kg ethanol (25% w/v) and a treatment group receiving 5 g/kg ethanol (25% w/v) plus Landolphia owariensis at 100 mg/kg body weight in a diluted nutritionally complete diet (50% v/v). After 4 days of binge alcohol treatment, Landolphia owariensis supplementation significantly lowered the levels of histologic and biochemical indices of neurodegeneration. The plant reduced the level of neurodegeneration and cytoa rchitecture distortion of the cerebellar cortex in rats exposed to ethanol. Furthermore, Landolphia owariensis attenuates alcohol-induced cerebellar degeneration by alleviating oxidative stress and alteration of NF protein expression in the Purkinje cells.

As discussed in a study, Landolphia owariensis leaf extract and its fractions show antiplasmodial activity that may be attributed to alkaloids, flavonoids, saponins and tannins. The extract and fractions elicited 29-86, 18-95 and 75-96% significant (p < 0.001) suppression of parasitemia in early, established and residual infections. The ED50 values for the suppressive activity of LOE, LOHF, LOEF and LOMF were 266.56, 514.93, 392.95 and 165.70 mg/kg, respectively. The post-day 30-survival index was 16.7-50, 16.7, 16.7-66.7 and 50-83.3% for LOE, LOHF, LOEF, and LOMF. Additionally, an oral LD50 value >5000 mg/kg in mice was established for LOE.
