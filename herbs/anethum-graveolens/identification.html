Botanical Identification of Dill (Anethum graveolens)

To identify Dill (Anethum graveolens) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Dill (Anethum graveolens) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Dill, or Anethum graveolens, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Anethum, and species Anethum graveolens. These classifications place it within the carrot family, reflecting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Dill is Anethum graveolens, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné in the 18th century. It belongs to the Apiaceae family and is also known by several synonyms, including Anethum donianum and Anethum sylvestre. Common names such as dill weed, dillseed, and leaf dill are widely recognized, while regional and historical names reflect its long use in herbal medicine and culinary traditions.

Morphological Characteristics

Dill is an annual herb with a taproot system. Its stem is erect, branching, and hairy. Leaves are pinnately compound, with finely divided leaflets. The inflorescence is a compound umbel, with small white to greenish flowers. The fruit is a dry, two-lobed seed called a "siliqua," containing aromatic seeds used in cooking and herbal medicine.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Dill can be distinguished by its finely divided, feathery leaves and small yellow flowers arranged in umbels. The stems are hollow and slightly hairy, while the seeds are small, oblong, and yellowish. It is often confused with caraway, but dill has a more delicate, herbaceous appearance and a distinct anise-like fragrance.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Dill is native to southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained loamy or sandy soils. It grows best at altitudes between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Dill typically blooms from late spring to early summer, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the plant is most identifiable for harvesting between mid-summer and early fall when the seeds are mature and the foliage is at its peak.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Dill belongs to the genus Anethum in the Apiaceae family. Its closest relatives include other members like cumin and parsley. These species share similar morphological and genetic traits, such as umbel structures and aromatic compounds. The genus relations highlight its position within the broader Apiaceae group.
