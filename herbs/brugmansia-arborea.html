Brugmansia arborea

What's the taxonomical classification of Brugmansia arborea?

Brugmansia arborea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Solanales. It is a member of the family Solanaceae, which is further divided into the genus Brugmansia, ultimately identifying the specific species as arborea.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Brugmansia arborea contains tropane alkaloids primarily concentrated in its leaves, seeds, and flowers that are used medicinally for their potent sedative and anticholinergic properties. Historically, these various plant parts have been prepared as topical poultices or diluted infusions to alleviate localized pain and inflammation. The alkaloids within the tissues can induce profound sleep and serve as powerful anesthetics when applied in strictly controlled amounts. However, the high toxicity of the entire plant means that medicinal application requires extreme caution to avoid dangerous systemic poisoning. Because the chemical potency varies between the flowers and the leaves, precise dosing is nearly impossible in traditional settings.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains tropane alkaloids including scopolamine, hyoscyamine, and atropine which act as potent anticholinergic agents.

These bioactive compounds are primarily concentrated in the flowers, leaves, and seeds of the specimen. Scopolamine and hyoscyamine function by blocking acetylcholine receptors in the central and peripheral nervous systems. Atropine is also present and can induce significant physiological changes such as pupil dilation and increased heart rate.

Because these substances are highly toxic, their medicinal application requires extreme precision to avoid fatal outcomes.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Tropane alkaloids
  • Scopolamine
  • Hyoscyamine
  • Atropine

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Brugmansia arborea targets muscarinic acetylcholine receptors, specifically the M1, M2, and M3 subtypes, through its primary bioactive alkaloids scopolamine and hyoscyamine. These tropane alkaloids act as competitive antagonists that bind to the orthosteric binding sites of these G protein-coupled receptors. By inhibiting the binding of acetylcholine, these compounds disrupt cholinergic neurotransmission within the central and peripheral nervous systems. This molecular interaction results in physiological effects such as decreased gastrointestinal motility, pupillary dilation, and significant alterations in cognitive processing. The specific affinity for these receptor subtypes governs the plant's potent hallucinogenic and anticholinergic properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Brugmansia arborea?

Brugmansia arborea has potent tropane alkaloids, specifically scopolamine, hyoscyamine, and atropine, which act as anticholinergic agents to induce sedation, hallucinations, and muscle relaxation. These chemical compounds function by blocking acetylcholine receptors in the central and peripheral nervous systems. Historically, these properties were utilized in extremely small, controlled doses for anesthetic purposes and to treat various neurological conditions. However, the therapeutic window is exceptionally narrow, meaning the difference between a medicinal dose and a lethal one is minimal. Due to the high risk of severe toxicity and delirium, modern medicine rarely uses this plant for direct clinical applications.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including asthma, respiratory issues, and certain types of pain through its alkaloid content. The chemical compounds found within its flowers can act as bronchodilators to help open airways during breathing difficulties. Some traditional medicinal practices also utilize its sedative properties to manage severe physical discomfort. However, the high toxicity of the plant means that any medicinal application must be handled with extreme caution. Improper dosage can lead to severe hallucinations, delirium, or even fatal poisoning.

What are the herbal preparations of Brugmansia arborea?

Brugmansia arborea is prepared as a topical ointment, an infusion, or a decoction using the leaves, flowers, or roots. These various preparations are historically utilized to deliver the plant's potent tropane alkaloids through different delivery methods. Infusions made from the flowers are often used for their sedative properties, while decoctions of the roots provide more concentrated doses. Topically applied ointments or poultices can be used to treat localized pain or skin conditions. Due to the extreme toxicity of the plant, all preparations must be handled with extreme caution to avoid accidental poisoning.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Brugmansia arborea can cause severe hallucinations, tachycardia, hyperthermia, and potentially fatal respiratory depression due to its high concentration of tropane alkaloids like scopolamine and atropine. Ingesting any part of this plant triggers intense anticholinergic toxicity that affects the central nervous system. Symptoms often include extreme confusion, dilated pupils, and a rapid, irregular heartbeat. Physical manifestations can also involve dry mouth, blurred vision, and a dangerous increase in body temperature. In severe cases, these effects lead to seizures, coma, or death.
