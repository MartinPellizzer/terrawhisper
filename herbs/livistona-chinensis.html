Livistona chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Livistona chinensis?

Livistona chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its evolutionary lineage continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent through the order Arecales, it is placed within the family Arecaceae. Ultimately, this specific plant is identified under the genus Livistona and is distinguished as the species chinensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Livistona chinensis has a solitary, stout trunk that can reach heights of up to 10 meters and is topped by a dense crown of large, arching, pinnate leaves measuring up to 3 meters in length. The individual leaflets are long, narrow, and lanceolate, characterized by a distinctively waxy, dark green surface. A defining feature of the foliage is the presence of fine, hair-like fibers that emerge from the margins of the leaflets, creating a fringed appearance. The plant produces large, pendulous inflorescences that bear clusters of small, yellowish-white flowers. These flowers eventually develop into fleshy, ovoid fruits that turn a deep blue or black when fully ripe.

What are the pharmacological activities of Livistona chinensis?

This plant has anti-tumor, anticancer, and anti-angiogenesis pharmacological activities that make it a subject of significant medical interest. These biological properties allow the plant to interfere with the development and spread of malignant cells within the body. By exhibiting anti-angiogenesis effects, it can potentially inhibit the formation of new blood vessels that tumors require to grow. Furthermore, its potent anticancer and antitumoer actions suggest it may play a role in suppressing tumor progression. Research continues into how these specific mechanisms can be harnessed for therapeutic applications.

As discussed in a study, the ethyl acetate part of the alcohol extract from seeds of Livistona chinensis R. Br. can selectively inhibit the proliferation of HT-29 and T24 cell lines with maximum inhibitory rates of 74.66% and 86.52% respectively. The petroleum ether part of the seeds also showed inhibitory effects, though its potency was inferior with maximum inhibitory rates of 43.80% and 38.67%. This ethyl acetate part significantly inhibits the secretion of VEGF protein across 4 tumor cell lines. Furthermore, the ethyl acetate part reduces the expression of Flk-1 protein and mRNA of HUVEC induced by VEGF. Consequently, the ethyl acetate part is considered the active part of the alcohol extract from seeds of Livistona chinensis R. Br. due to its anti-tumor activity.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and physical injury from its sharp, spiny leaf margins. Contact with the sap or the foliage may trigger redness, itching, or swelling in individuals with sensitive skin. The rigid, pointed edges of the leaves present a significant risk of puncture wounds or scratches if handled improperly. While systemic toxicity is not widely documented, accidental ingestion should be avoided to prevent potential gastrointestinal distress. Maintaining caution during pruning or repotting is essential to mitigate these physical and dermatological risks.
