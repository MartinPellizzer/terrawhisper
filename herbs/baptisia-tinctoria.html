What to know about wild indigo before using it medicinally

Wild indigo, scientifically known as Baptisia tinctoria, is a perennial herb with a range of health benefits.

It contains alkaloids that have been shown to have antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antiviral properties, thereby improving overall health. In addition to its medicinal uses, wild indigo is also used in culinary applications. Its roots and leaves can be used to make a tea or a tincture, and its flowers can be used as a garnish or in salads.

In horticulture, wild indigo is valued for its attractive blue-violet flowers and its ability to thrive in a variety of soils and conditions. It is a hardy plant that can grow up to 4 feet tall and is often used in landscaping and erosion control. Botanically, wild indigo is a member of the legume family and is native to North America. Historically, wild indigo has been used by Native Americans for a variety of purposes, including as a dye, a medicine, and a food.

It was also used by early European settlers as a substitute for true indigo, which was a valuable but expensive dye imported from India.

What are the side effects of wild indigo when used improperly?

When used improperly, wild indigo increases the chances of experiencing side effects, such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea.

In severe cases, it can lead to symptoms such as confusion, difficulty breathing, and rapid heartbeat. Additionally, prolonged use or high doses of wild indigo can result in a condition called baptisia poisoning, which can cause abdominal pain, decreased urination, and even seizures.

It is also important to note that wild indigo can interact with certain medications, such as immunosuppressants and blood thinners, and can increase the risk of bleeding and infection. Pregnant women and individuals with kidney or liver disease should avoid using wild indigo altogether.

It is crucial to consult with a healthcare professional before using wild indigo for medicinal purposes to ensure safe and appropriate use.
