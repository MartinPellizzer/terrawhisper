Tabernaemontana coronaria

What's the taxonomical classification of Tabernaemontana coronaria?

Tabernaemontana coronaria belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Gentianaales. This species is a member of the family Apocynaceae, falling under the genus Tabernaemontana, and is specifically identified by its species name, coronaaria.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Tabernaemontana coronaria has an erect, woody shrub habit characterized by opposite, elliptical, dark green leaves with entire margins and an acute apex. Its most striking morphological feature is the salverform white flowers that emerge from terminal clusters. These blossoms possess a long, slender corolla tube that expands into five spreading lobes. The plant also produces small, globose fruits that develop from the fertilized ovaries.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds dictamine, voacamine, tabernaemontanine, coronaridine, and arborinine. These specific alkaloids are extracted from the plant tissues to study their diverse pharmacological properties. Researchers analyze these substances to understand how they interact with human biological systems. Each compound possesses a unique chemical structure that contributes to the overall medicinal profile of the species. Scientific investigation into these molecules can lead to the development of new therapeutic treatments.

As discussed in a study, chemical investigation of the stem bark of Tabernaemontana coronaria led to the isolation of several medicinal compounds. The plant contains 3 carbazole alkaloids, 1 acridone alkaloid arborinine, 3 monomeric indole alkaloids including coronaridine, 10-methoxy coronaridine and tabernaemontanine, and 2 dimeric indole alkaloids voacamine and tabernaelegantine B. The methanol extract of the plant was evaluated against 3 known positive mutagens using 2 bacterial strains, Salmonella typhimurium TA 98 and TA 100. Both extracts exhibited significant antimutagenic activity against NPD and sodium azide for the TA98 and TA100 strains. These results indicated that the extracts could counteract mutagenicity induced by different genotoxic compounds.
