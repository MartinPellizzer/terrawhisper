Scientific and Botanical Profile

Beggarticks, with botanical name Bidens pilosa, is a flowering plant belonging to the Asteraceae family, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

Native to regions across South America, Central America, Mexico, Brazil, the Caribbean, the United States, Argentina, Peru, Colombia, Venezuela, and Ecuador, this species has spread to various parts of the world. Morphologically, Bidens pilosa is an erect, herbaceous annual or perennial plant with slender, branched stems and opposite, heart-shaped or ovate leaves that are covered in fine, soft hairs. Its flowers are small and yellow, typically clustered in groups of two, with five petals and a distinctive, spiny pappus that aids in wind dispersal.

Commonly referred to by names such as Pilose Tickseed, Blackroot, Tickseed, Pilose Beggar's Ticks, Beggar's Ticks, Briar Weed, Yellow Beggarticks, Common Beggar's-Tick, Hedge Beggar's Ticks, Beggars' Treasure, Bishop's Weed, Pilose Beggar's Tick, Goatweed, and Velvetleaf, this plant is both a weed and a plant with historical medicinal uses.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Bidens pilosa has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare Bidens pilosa, it can be made into a tea by steeping the dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction can be made by boiling the plant parts for a longer period. It is advised to use Bidens pilosa sparingly, with a maximum duration of one to two weeks, to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Bidens pilosa can be used as a medicinal plant for its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and analgesic properties, but it must be used with caution due to its potential toxicity.

Side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, vomiting, and in some cases, more severe reactions such as liver damage or allergic responses. It may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants and anti-inflammatory drugs, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding or reducing the effectiveness of these treatments. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using Bidens pilosa due to the risk of adverse effects on the fetus or infant, and individuals with chronic illnesses should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, it is recommended to use Bidens pilosa only under professional supervision, follow recommended dosages, and discontinue use if any adverse reactions occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Bidens pilosa grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain moist but not waterlogged conditions.

It thrives in warm climates and is often found in disturbed areas, making it a hardy and adaptable plant. For optimal growth, it should be planted in early spring, and regular weeding and pruning can help promote healthy development and prevent disease. Harvesting is most effective during the flowering stage, typically in late summer to early autumn, using clean shears to cut the stems just above the nodes to preserve the plant's structure and potency.

After harvesting, the plant material should be dried in a shaded, well-ventilated area or using a dehydrator to retain its medicinal properties, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to maintain potency and prevent degradation.
