Gentiana algida belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Gentianaales and the family Gentianaceae. Ultimately, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Gentiana, with the specific epithet algida denoting its species name.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gentiana algida has a perennial rootstock that produces upright, unbranched stems reaching up to 20 centimeters in height with lanceolate, opposite leaves that are sessile and entire. The foliage is typically dark green and lacks petioles, clinging closely to the stem structure. Its flowers are large, trumpet-shaped, and characterized by a deep blue or violet hue with prominent light-colored longitudinal stripes. The corolla consists of five fused petals that flare outward at the limb, often displaying a yellowish throat. Each blossom is supported by a single calyx with narrow, pointed lobes.

Its distribution is strictly limited to circumpolar environments where temperatures remain low throughout the growing season. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the bitter glycoside gentianose along with the triterpenoid oleanolic acid and the phytosterols sitosterol, stigmasterol, and daucosterol. These specific chemical constituents contribute to the various therapeutic properties observed in the species. The presence of gentianose is particularly significant for its ability to stimulate digestive functions. Furthermore, the sterol profile consisting of sitosterol, stigmasterol, and daucosterol supports the plant's complex biochemical makeup. Together, these medicinal compounds form the basis of its pharmacological potential.

According to a study published by "Phytochemistry", fractionation of an aqueous acetone extract of the whole herb of Gentiana algida yielded 1 new [2'-(o,m-dihydroxybenzyl)sweroside] and 5 known secoiridoiids. The extraction also identified anofinic acid, fomannoxin acid, sitosterol, daucosterol, stigmasterol, oleanolic acid, orientin, and gentianose. Both anofinic acid and fomannoxin acid showed activity against the plant pathogenic fungus Cladosporium cucumerinum. While the carboxylic moieties in these acids were linked to fungal activity, their methyl esters were found to be active against the human pathogenic yeast Candida albicans.
