Bupleurum chinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Bupleurum chinense?

Bupleurum chinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Apiales. This plant is a member of the family Apiaceae, which places it within the genus Bupleurum, and it is specifically identified by the species name chinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bupleurum chinense has an herbaceous perennial structure characterized by an erect stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 60 centimeters. The leaves are alternate, sessile, and often possess serrated margins with a distinctively lanceolate or ovate shape. Its inflorescence consists of small, yellowish-green flowers arranged in compound umbels. The plant also features a fleshy, tuberous taproot that serves as its primary storage organ. These morphological features are essential for identifying the species in various botanical environments.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Bupleurum chinense contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its dried roots, which are known as Chai Hu in traditional Chinese medicine. These roots are harvested and processed to extract active principles like saikosaponins that serve as the plant's therapeutic agents. While the roots are the principal part used for treating various ailments, other parts of the plant are rarely utilized in clinical practice. The chemical profile of the root allows it to act as an effective regulator of liver function and an immune modulator. Consequently, the root remains the sole part of the plant valued for its pharmacological properties in herbal formulations.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds saikosaponin A, saikosaponin B, saikosaponin C, saikosaponin D, and saikogelin A. These triterpenoid saponins serve as the primary bioactive constituents responsible for the plant's therapeutic properties. Researchers often study these specific molecules to understand their potential anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects. The varying chemical structures of these saponins allow them to interact with biological targets in unique ways. Understanding the concentrations of these compounds is essential for standardizing herbal extracts used in traditional medicine.

As discussed in a study, the active components identified from the extracts of Radix Bupleuri include saikosaponin A, saikosaponin B1, saikosaponin B2, saikosaponin C, and saikosaponin D. Through in vitro pharmacological MTT trials, saikosaponin B1, saikosaponin B2, and saikosaponin C were found to protect SH-SY5Y cells. The protective effects observed for saikosaponin B1 and saikosaponin C were concentration dependent. Conversely, saikosaponin A and saikosaponin D inhibited cell viability at concentrations >30 µg/mL with a p < 0.05. Consequently, saikosaponin B1, saikosaponin B2, and saikosaponin C may serve as anti-schizophrenic candidate drugs.
