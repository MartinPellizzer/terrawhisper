What to know about Bupleurum chinense (thorow-wax) before using it medicinally

Bupleurum chinense, commonly known as thorow-wax, is a plant herb that has been extensively used for its medicinal properties to improve cardiovascular health by reducing blood pressure and inflammation, while also acting as an antioxidant to protect against cell damage.

This perennial plant is native to East Asia and requires well-drained soil to thrive, making it a popular choice for gardens in dry climates. The botanical classification of Bupleurum chinense places it in the family Apiaceae, with its distinctive umbels of small white flowers and bitter flavor.

Historical references to the plant date back to the Traditional Chinese Medicine, where it has been used for centuries to treat various health conditions, including fever, rheumatism, and respiratory problems.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Bupleurum chinense.

What are the medicinal properties of Bupleurum chinense?

Bupleurum chinense helps with fever reduction, inflammation, and digestive issues. It has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for centuries to treat various health conditions. The plant has been studied for its potential to reduce oxidative stress and improve liver function.

The active constituents of Bupleurum chinense include sesquiterpenes, alkaloids, and flavonoids, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds have been shown to possess anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and immunomodulatory effects, making them effective against various diseases.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are the roots and rhizomes. These parts are rich in active constituents and have been traditionally used to treat various health conditions, including fever, cough, and respiratory issues. The roots and rhizomes can be used raw, dried, or processed into decoctions and extracts.

Improper use of Bupleurum chinense can lead to side effects, including allergic reactions, gastrointestinal upset, and liver toxicity. Prolonged use of the plant may also cause interactions with other medications, such as blood thinners and diabetes medications.

Precautions to take when using Bupleurum chinense medicinally include consulting with a healthcare professional before use, especially for pregnant or breastfeeding women. It is also essential to follow the recommended dosage and avoid using the plant in combination with other medications without medical supervision.

What are the horticulural aspects of Bupleurum chinense?

Bupleurum chinense grow in a wide range of light conditions, from full sun to partial shade, but optimal growth is achieved in well-drained soil and full sun. The plant requires a minimum temperature of 10°C to 15°C to germinate and grow. Soil pH range is between 6.1 and 7.8.

Planting tips for Bupleurum chinense include sowing seeds in the spring or fall, 1-2 inches deep and 12-18 inches apart. Thin the seedlings to 18-24 inches apart to allow for air circulation. Water the soil regularly, but avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot.

Harvesting tips for Bupleurum chinense involve collecting the flower buds and stems in the summer months, when the plant is in full bloom. Pinch off flower buds to encourage leaf growth. Harvest the entire plant, or cut back to the base to promote new growth.

Pests and diseases affecting Bupleurum chinense include aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites, which can be controlled with neem oil or insecticidal soap. Root rot, caused by overwatering, is also a concern, and can be treated with fungicides. Regular pruning and good air circulation can help prevent these issues.

What are the botanical aspects of Bupleurum chinense?

Bupleurum chinense is a perennial herb with erect stems, 30-100 cm tall, and simple, alternate leaves that are elliptic to ovate, 5-15 cm long, and 2-5 cm wide. Leaves are pubescent on the veins, with a distinct petiole.

Bupleurum chinense belongs to the family Apiaceae, tribe Bupleureae, and genus Bupleurum. The species was first described by the Chinese botanist, J. G. Baker, in 1906. It is a monotypic species, meaning it has only one known species. The plant has a unique characteristic, its stems and leaves having a milky latex.

The plant has several variants, including Bupleurum chinense var. chinense and Bupleurum chinense var. thunbergii, which are distinguished by differences in leaf shape and size. Some sources also recognize Bupleurum chinense as a synonym of Bupleurum thunbergii.

Bupleurum chinense is native to East Asia, specifically China, Japan, and Korea. It has been naturalized in other parts of the world, including North America, where it grows in coastal areas and meadows.

The life cycle of Bupleurum chinense typically takes about 3-6 months. The plant grows from seed in the spring, producing a rosette of leaves, and then sends up a flowering stem in mid-summer. The flowers are small, white, and arranged in umbels. After pollination, the seeds mature and disperse in late summer.

What are the historical aspects of Bupleurum chinense?

Bupleurum chinense is a plant with a long history of traditional medicine use in China and Korea. In traditional Chinese medicine, it has been used for over 2,000 years to treat fever, rheumatism, and liver disorders. It is still used today in various herbal remedies.

In ancient Chinese mythology, Bupleurum chinense was associated with the legend of the "Five Emperors". The plant was said to have been used by these emperors to treat various ailments. The mythological references to the plant's medicinal properties also appear in the classic Chinese medical text, the "Shennong Bencao Jing".

The symbolic meanings of Bupleurum chinense vary across cultures. In Chinese culture, the plant is associated with the element of metal, which symbolizes strength and clarity. In Korean culture, it is associated with the element of fire, which symbolizes energy and passion. In some cultures, the plant is seen as a symbol of longevity.

Historical texts that reference Bupleurum chinense include the "Shennong Bencao Jing" and the "Ben Cao Gang Mu", a 16th-century Chinese pharmacopeia. These texts provide detailed descriptions of the plant's medicinal properties and uses. The "Ben Cao Gang Mu" also includes illustrations of the plant's different parts and their uses.

Historical artifacts that feature Bupleurum chinense include ancient Chinese medical texts, such as the "Huangdi Neijing" and "Shang Han Lun", which contain illustrations and descriptions of the plant's use in traditional Chinese medicine. In addition, artifacts from ancient Chinese and Korean tombs have been discovered with carvings of the plant's image, highlighting its cultural and symbolic significance.
