Aconitum septentrionale

What's the taxonomical classification of Aconitum septentrionale?

Aconitum septentrionale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Ranunculales and is a member of the family Ranunculaceae. Specifically, it is categorized under the genus Aconitum, with the specific epithet septentrionale designating its unique species identity.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aconitum septentrionale has palmately lobed leaves that are deeply divided into several narrow, finger-like segments and produces zygomorphic flowers characterized by a prominent, helmet-shaped upper sepal. The foliage typically features a dull green color and a slightly hairy texture on the undersides of the leaflets. Its flowering structures consist of a cluster of blue to violet blossoms that emerge from a sturdy, upright stem. The root system is composed of thick, tuberous rhizomes that allow the plant to persist in its subarctic habitats.     What side effects this plant can have?

Once ingested, the toxic alkaloids rapidly affect the gastrointestinal and neurological systems. Patients may first experience gastrointestinal distress followed by neurological sensations like numbness and tingling in the extremities. As the toxins impact the heart, dangerous rhythm changes such as arrhythmia and bradycardia can occur. Severe cases often progress to life-threatening convulsions, total paralysis, and complete respiratory failure. Ultimately, the systemic toxicity can lead to fatal cardiac arrest.
