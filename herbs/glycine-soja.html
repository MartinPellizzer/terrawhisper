Glycine soja

What's the taxonomical classification of Glycine soja?

Glycine soja belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Fabales, it is a member of the family Fabaceae. Finally, it is categorized under the genus Glycine, with its specific designation being the species soja.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Glycine soja has an annual or perennial life cycle characterized by an erect or climbing herbaceous habit with trifoliate leaves, stipules, and small, papilionaceous flowers. The compound leaves consist of three leaflets that are typically ovate to elliptical with pubescent surfaces. Its reproductive structures include small, white to light purple flowers that develop into hairy, dehiscent pods. These pods contain several small, globose seeds that are often darker in color than those of cultivated soybean. The entire plant surface is frequently covered in fine, spreading hairs that provide a distinct texture.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to East Asia, specifically encompassing regions across China, Japan, Korea, and parts of eastern Russia. It primarily inhabits moist environments such as riverbanks, wetlands, and coastal areas where it can thrive in temperate climates. While its natural range is concentrated in the northern hemisphere, it has also been introduced to various other parts of the world through human activity. In many non-native territories, it can spread easily along waterways due to its ability to produce buoyant seeds. This widespread distribution makes it a significant wild relative of the cultivated soybean.

What are the pharmacological activities of Glycine soja?

This plant has hepatoprotective properties and provides an effective treatment for acute menopausal syndrome. The bioactive compounds found in Glycine soja help stabilize liver functions and protect hepatic cells from oxidative damage. Additionally, its high concentration of isoflavones allows it to mimic estrogen, which mitigates the severity of menopausal symptoms. These phytochemicals work synergistically to regulate hormonal imbalances and support metabolic health. Consequently, the plant serves as a significant natural resource for managing both liver health and endocrine transitions.

According to a study published by "Planta medica", the aerial parts of Glycine soja contain four known saponins identified as soyasaponin I, II, III, and IV. These saponins all share the same aglycone, which is soyasapogenol B. In tests regarding hepatoprotective actions on primary cultured rat hepatocytes, the efficacy of soyasaponin II was almost comparable to soyasaponin I. The study found that soyasaponin III and IV were more effective than soyasaponin I and II. Results indicate that a disaccharide group shows greater action than a trisaccharide group. Additionally, a saponin with a hexosyl unit shows a slightly greater action than one with a pentosyl unit within each disaccharide or trisaccharide group.

The primary pharmacological activities of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • hepatoprotective
  • treatment of acute menopausal syndrome

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains specific medicinal compounds such as eucomic acid and piscidic acid which contribute to its therapeutic potential. These organic acids are part of a complex chemical profile that offers various biological activities. Research into these constituents suggests they may play roles in supporting metabolic health and providing antioxidant effects. Beyond these specific acids, the plant also hosts a variety of other bioactive molecules like isoflavones and peptides. Studying these unique substances helps scientists understand how the plant can be utilized in pharmaceutical applications.

According to a study published by "Molecular plant", Glycine soja possesses black pods due to the presence of the L1 gene. This gene encodes a hydroxymethylglutaryl-coenzyme A (CoA) lyase-like (HMGL-like) domain protein that functions as a eucomic acid synthase. This enzyme facilitates the synthesis of two specific compounds, eucomic acid and piscidic acid. Both of these compounds contribute to the coloration of the pods and seed coats. The dark pigmentation resulting from these compounds increases photothermal efficiency.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating liver injury and managing various menopausal symptoms. Its pharmacological effects include the inhibition in the rate of in vivo intestinal absorption of D-galactosae and L-leucine. Research also indicates that it can cause inhibition of the in vitro intestinal absorption of D-galactosae. Furthermore, the plant is associated with a reduction in the intestinal disaccharidase activity. These mechanisms contribute to its diverse medicinal applications.

According to a study published by "Planta medica", the constituents of the aerial parts of Glycine soja include 4 known saponins identified as soyasaponin I, II, III, and IV. Within these groups, the disaccharide group demonstrated greater action than the trisaccharide group. Additionally, the saponin containing a hexosyl unit showed a slightly greater action than the one with a pentosyl unit in each of the 2 groups.
