Scientific and Botanical Profile

Leuzea, with botanical name Leuzea carthamoides, is a flowering plant belonging to the family Asteraceae, commonly known as the sunflower or daisy family.

It is also referred to by various common names such as Lupine, Russian Coneflower, Common Leuzea, Goldenweed, Russian Tea, Cape Marigold, Bishop's Weed, Cape Golden, Lewisia, Golden Weed, Bromegrass, Rhubarb Dropseed, Common Lupin, Love-Lace, Golden Dock, Bromarius, Purpletop, Carthamus-Like Plant, Common Leuzea, and Carthamus-Like. Native to regions across Europe, Asia, and North Africa, this plant thrives in temperate climates and is often found in grasslands and disturbed soils. Morphologically, Leuzea carthamoides is an annual or biennial herb with erect, branched stems, alternate leaves that are lanceolate to ovate, and yellow or orange composite flowers arranged in terminal or lateral heads.

Its medicinal and ornamental uses have contributed to its widespread recognition and cultivation in various parts of the world.

History and Cultural Relevance

Leuzea was used in traditional medicine systems across various cultures, particularly in Europe and Asia, for its purported health benefits and medicinal properties.

Known as "cowplant" or "leuzea carthamoides," it has a long history of being employed in herbal remedies for digestive issues, respiratory ailments, and as a general tonic. In ancient Greece and Rome, it was believed to have purifying properties and was used in rituals to cleanse the body and spirit. Traditional practices in Eastern Europe and Russia often incorporated Leuzea into folk remedies, such as treating headaches, reducing inflammation, and supporting liver function.

Today, its extracts are still used in some herbal formulations for digestive health, highlighting its enduring relevance in both traditional and modern wellness practices.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Leuzea carthamoides has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, and it is available in various forms including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare it, one can make a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the plant material for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion or applied topically for skin conditions. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1 to 2 grams of dried leaves per cup of tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

Due to limited research, it is advisable to use leuzea carthamoides sparingly, with a short duration of use and not exceeding recommended frequency.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Leuzea carthamoides can be considered relatively safe when used in recommended doses for short periods, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, or allergic reactions in some individuals.

While there are no well-documented major adverse effects, long-term use or high doses may lead to more severe reactions, including skin irritation or systemic effects. It may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the nervous system or blood pressure, so it is important to consult a healthcare provider before combining it with other drugs or herbs. Special caution is advised for pregnant or breastfeeding women, as its safety during these periods has not been fully established, and individuals with chronic illnesses should monitor their condition closely.

To use leuzea carthamoides safely, always follow recommended dosages, start with a low dose, and discontinue use if any adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Leuzea carthamoides grows best in well-drained, loamy soil that is rich in organic matter, with a pH ranging from 6.

0 to 7. 5, and requires full sun to partial shade for optimal growth. It thrives in moderate climates with consistent moisture, requiring regular watering during the growing season but avoiding waterlogged conditions. To ensure healthy growth, regular weeding, mulching, and occasional fertilization with a balanced fertilizer are recommended, along with monitoring for pests and diseases. The plant is typically harvested in late summer to early autumn when the leaves and flowers are fully developed, using clean shears to cut the stems just above the base to encourage regrowth.

After harvesting, the plant material should be dried in a cool, shaded area with good air circulation, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place to preserve its medicinal potency.
