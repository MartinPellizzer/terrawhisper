Annona glabra

What's the taxonomical classification of Annona glabra?

Annona glabra belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Magnoliales and the family Annonaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Annona, with its specific designation being the species glabra.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Annona glabra has a woody, evergreen shrub or small tree habit characterized by elliptical, coriaceous leaves that are glabrous on both the upper and lower surfaces. The foliage typically measures between 5 and 12 centimeters in length and possesses entire margins with distinct pinnate venation. Small, yellowish-green flowers emerge from the axils of the branches, often appearing solitary or in small clusters. The fruit develops into a large, fleshy, somewhat globose or oblong syncarp with a smooth, greenish skin. These botanical features distinguish the species from other members of the Annonaceae family that may possess pubescence.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of South America, specifically spanning across Brazil, Paraguay, and northern Argentina. It thrives primarily in seasonally dry forests and savannas where it can find suitable drainage. Its range is closely tied to specific climatic conditions found within the Cerrado and Chaco biomes. Within these habitats, the species often populates riparian zones or edges of woodlands. This distribution allows it to occupy distinct ecological niches across the central and eastern parts of the continent.

What are the pharmacological activities of Annona glabra?

This plant has anticancer, cytotoxic, antibacterial, antifungal, and antiviral pharmacological activities. Its cytotoxic properties allow it to inhibit the growth of various malignant cell lines through targeted cellular mechanisms. The plant also exhibits potent antibacterial and antifungal actions that can combat diverse pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, its antiviral capabilities provide a defense against several viral infections by interfering with replication processes. These diverse biological activities highlight the therapeutic potential of Annona glabra in medicinal applications.

As discussed in a study, Annona glabra hexane extract contains major compounds such as tritriacontane (30.23%), 13, 17-dimethyl-tritriacontane (22.44%), and limonene (18.97%). The extract demonstrated significant cytotoxic activity against CACO-2 and A-549 cancer cell lines with IC50 values of 47 ± 0.74 μg/mL and 56.82 ± 0.92 μg/mL, along with SI values of 3.8 and 3.1. Moderate-to-weak activities were also observed against PC-3, PANC-1, MCF-7, and HepG-2 with IC50 values of 81.86 ± 3.26, 57.34 ± 0.77, 80.31 ± 4.13, and 57.01 ± 0.85 μg/mL, respectively. Regarding antiviral properties, the extract showed promising protective and virucidal activity against HSV1 with results of 79.55 ± 1.67 vs. 68.44 ± 7.62 and 70.91 ± 7.02 vs. 83.76 ± 5.67. Additionally, the plant showed mild protective antiviral activity against HAV with a value of 48.08 ± 3.46.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds 16 alpha-17-dihydroxy-ent-kauran-19-oiic acid, annoglabasin A, annoglabasin B, glabracins A, and glabracins B. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their potential biological activities and therapeutic properties. The presence of these various diterpenoids and prenylated flavonoids contributes to the plant's unique chemical profile. Researchers analyze these molecules to understand how they interact with human biological systems. Such detailed phytochemical characterization is essential for developing new pharmaceutical treatments.

As discussed in a study, phytochemical analysis of the fruits of Annona glabra yielded 2 new kaurane diterpenoids, specifically annoglabasin A (methyl-16 beta-acetoxy-19-al-ent-kauran-17-oate)(1) and annoglabasin B (16 alpha-hydro-19-acetoxy-ent-kauran-17-oic acid)(2). The analysis also identified 11 known kaurane derivatives categorized as 3-13. Among these compounds, methyl-16 alpha-hydro-19-al-ent-kauran-17-oate (11) exhibited mild activity against HIV replication in H9 lymphocyte cells. Additionally, 16 alpha-17-dihydroxy-ent-kauran-19-oic acid (4) showed significant inhibition of HIV-revearsse transcriptase.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause neurotoxicity and potential paralysis due to the presence of toxic alkaloids known as annonacin. These neurotoxins are capable of crossing the blood-brain barrier and disrupting cellular energy production within the nervous system. Long-term exposure to such compounds has been linked to the development of atypical Parkinsonism and other degenerative neurological disorders. Ingesting parts of the plant may also lead to gastrointestinal distress or systemic toxicity depending on the dosage. Consequently, caution is necessary when handling or consuming any part of the species to avoid severe health complications.
