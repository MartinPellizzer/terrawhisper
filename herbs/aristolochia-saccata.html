Aristolochia saccata

What's the taxonomical classification of Aristolochia saccata?

Aristolochia saccata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows a lineage that places it under the order Piperales. The plant is a specific representative of the family Aristolochiaceae, falling under the genus Aristolochia, and is identified at the species level as saccata.

Its reproductive structures consist of unique, tube-shaped flowers that possess a distinct sac-like swelling at the base. What are the pharmacological activities of Aristolochia saccata?

This plant has wound healing, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory pharmacological activities. The phytochemical constituents within its leaves and roots promote tissue regeneration and accelerate the closure of skin lesions. These bioactive compounds also demonstrate the ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant properties that help reduce swelling and pain in affected areas. Through these diverse mechanisms, Aristolochia saccata serves as a potent natural agent for managing physical injuries and infections.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic leaf extract of Aristolochia saccata exhibits in vitro wound healing potency possibly mediated by its stimulatory effect on collagen-1 expression. An MTT assay on L929 cells revealed that the extract had no cytotoxic effect, with higher concentrations showing mild toxicity resulting in the death of just 2.88% cells. In a scratch assay, Aristolochia saccata showed 34.05%, 70.00%, and 93.52% wound closure at 12hrs, 24hrs, and 48hrs of incubation respectively. These results were compared to a positive control that showed 37.60, 56.41, and 99.05% wound closure. Flow cytometry-based studies further revealed that the extract induced the expression of the extracellular matrix remodelling factor collagen-1.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause severe nephrotoxicity, carcinogenicities, and uropathy, as well as gastrointestinal issues like nausea and vomiting. The presence of aristolochic acids in the plant leads to permanent kidney damage through direct cellular injury. These compounds are also known for their potent carcinogenicity, significantly increasing the risk of developing urinary tract cancers. Prolonged exposure often results in uropathy, which is characterized by structural changes and dysfunction in the urinary system. Patients consuming this species may also experience acute symptoms such as nausea and vomiting shortly after ingestion.
