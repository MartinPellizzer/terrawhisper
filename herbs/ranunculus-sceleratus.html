Ranunculus sceleratus

What's the taxonomical classification of Ranunculus sceleratus?

Ranunculus sceleratus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. This plant is a member of the order Ranunculales and falls under the family Ranunculaceae. Within this family, it is categorized under the genus Ranunculus, and its specific designation is the species sceleratus.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Ranunculus sceleratus contains high concentrations of protoanemonin, a highly irritating lactone that can cause severe blistering and inflammation upon contact with skin or mucous membranes. This unstable compound is formed through the dehydration of ranunculin, a glycoside found throughout the plant's tissues. When the plant material is crushed, the chemical conversion releases these potent irritants into the surrounding environment. While these compounds are primarily defensive mechanisms to deter herbivores, they have been historically studied for their intense biological activity. However, the toxicity of these substances makes the plant dangerous for any medicinal application without extreme caution.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ranunculus sceleratus?

Ranunculus sceleratus has potent vesicant and rubefacient properties that are utilized to induce localized skin irritation and increase blood circulation to specific areas. The plant contains high concentrations of protoanemonin, a toxic compound that causes blistering upon contact with the skin. In traditional topical applications, this irritation is leveraged to stimulate healing processes through the redirection of blood flow. However, its therapeutic window is extremely narrow due to the high risk of severe chemical burns and systemic toxicity. Because of these volatile effects, its medicinal use is strictly limited to controlled external applications.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin conditions such as ringworm, scabies, and various types of warts. Its chemical properties, particularly the presence of protoanemonin, allow it to act as a caustic agent on the epidermis. Historically, practitioners applied the crushed plant material directly to lesions to induce blistering and eventual shedding of the infected skin. However, its high toxicity means it must be used with extreme caution to avoid severe chemical burns or systemic poisoning. Modern medical practices generally avoid its use due to the risk of irritation and unpredictable reactions.
