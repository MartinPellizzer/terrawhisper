Geranium carolinianum

What's the taxonomical classification of Geranium carolinianum?

Geranium carolinianum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Geraniales and the family Geraniaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Geraniuim, and its specific species designation, carolinianum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Geranium carolinianum has deeply lobed, palmately shaped leaves that are typically three to five centimeters wide and feature serrated margins. The plant exhibits a low-growing, spreading habit with slender stems that can sometimes root at the nodes. Its flowers are small and actinomorphic, characterized by five pink to lavender petals and a central cluster of stamens. The reproductive structures include a long, beak-like fruit known as a schizocarp that assists in seed dispersal. Small, fine hairs often cover the foliage and stems, providing a slightly textured surface.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the southeastern United States, ranging from North Carolina and South Carolina through Georgia and Alabama into Mississippi and Louisiana. It thrives primarily in the coastal plain regions where sandy soils and open woodlands are prevalent. Within these areas, it is frequently found in pine flatwoods, savannas, and along the edges of deciduous forests. Its distribution is closely tied to the presence of well-drained, acidic environments common to the Atlantic and Gulf Coastal Plains. While it remains concentrated in these southern states, its presence is most consistent in the humid subtropical climates of the southeast.

What are the pharmacological activities of Geranium carolinianum?

This plant has anti-hepatitis B virus activity, anti-inflammatory properties, and antioxidant capabilities. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can effectively inhibit the replication of the hepatitis B virus in specific cellular models. Additionally, the extracts demonstrate a significant ability to reduce oxidative stress and mitigate inflammatory responses within biological systems. These diverse pharmacological actions suggest that the plant may hold therapeutic potential for treating liver-related diseases and other inflammatory conditions. Further clinical investigation is necessary to fully characterize its medicinal applications in humans.

As discussed in a study, the polyphenolic extract from Geraniumum carolinianum L. (PPGC) suppresses the secretion of HBV antigens in the HepG(2) 2.2.15 cell line with IC50 values of 46.85 microg/ml for HBsAg and 65.60 microg/ml for HBeAg at day 9. When using 100 microg/ml, PPGC reduces the HBV DNA level by 35.9%. In ducks infected with duck hepatitis B virus (DHBV), PPGC was dosed intragastrically once a day for 10 days and achieved an ED50 value of 47.54 mg/kg. This extract also reduces plasma and liver DHBV DNA levels in a dose-dependent manner. Finally, histopathological analysis showed a significant improvement of the liver after PPGC treatment.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds ellagic acid and gallic acid which provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits. These polyphenolic compounds work together to neutralize free radicals and protect cells from oxidative stress. Gallic acid is particularly recognized for its ability to inhibit certain enzymes associated with inflammation. Ellagic acid contributes to the plant's potential therapeutic properties through its role in supporting cellular health. Together, these bioactive molecules make the plant a valuable subject for pharmacological research.

As discussed in a study, the medicinal compounds gallic acid and ellagic acid are present in Geraniumia carolinianum. The calibration curves for these components were linear in the range of 0.075-5.00 microg (r = 0.9995) for gallic acid and 0.05-2.00 microg (r = 0.9995) for ellagic acid. The average recovery for gallic acid was 99.88% with an RSD of 1.19%, while the average recovery for ellagic acid was 99.08% with an RSD of 2.81%. Both gallic acid and ellagic acid content in Geraniumia carolinianum began to increase during the flowering stage. These levels subsequently increased to a maximum value during the immature-fruit stage.
