Botanical Identification of Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana)

To identify Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Mangosteen, or Garcinia mangostana, belongs to the kingdom Plantae. Its classification includes the family Guttiferae, genus Garcinia, and species Garcinia mangostana. The other taxonomic levels—division, class, and order—are not specified in the provided data.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name of Mangosteen is Garcinia mangostana, a species within the genus Garcinia in the family Clusiaceae. It was first described by the French botanist Pierre Poiret in 1794. The plant has several synonyms, including Garcinia calycina and Garcinia mangostana L., reflecting historical taxonomic revisions. Common names vary widely, including "mangosteen" in English and "rambutan" in some regions, though rambutan refers to a different fruit.

Morphological Characteristics

Mangosteen is a small evergreen tree with a compact growth habit. Its root system is fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in moist, well-drained soils. The stem is smooth, woody, and branched, growing up to 10 meters tall. Leaves are simple, alternate, and elliptical, with a waxy surface and a pointed tip. Inflorescences are axillary, bearing small white flowers with five petals. The fruit is a bright red, segmented berry with a thick, white rind and pinkish pulp, containing numerous seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Mangosteen can be distinguished by its distinctive red, spiky fruit covering a white, segmented interior. The leaves are oval with prominent veins, and the stem has a rough, ridged texture. It is often confused with other Garcinia species, but its unique fruit structure and leaf shape are key identifiers.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Mangosteen is native to Southeast Asia, particularly Malaysia, Indonesia, and the Philippines. It thrives in tropical climates with high humidity and rainfall. It prefers well-drained, fertile soils and is commonly found at altitudes between 0 and 600 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Mangosteen flowers typically bloom between April and June. Seed formation occurs from July to November. Harvesting is done when the fruit turns bright red, usually between November and January, depending on the region and climate.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) is closely related to species within the genus Garcinia. It shares key traits with other members of the Clusiaceae family, including its structure and chemical composition. Its phylogenetic position highlights its evolutionary ties to other tropical fruit plants.
