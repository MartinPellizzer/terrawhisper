Cynanchum wilfordii

What's the taxonomical classification of Cynanchum wilfordii?

Cynanchum wilfordii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Gentianales and the family Apocynaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Cynanchum, and its specific epithet, wilfordii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cynanchum wilfordii has perennial climbing stems that reach lengths of up to several meters and possess ovate to lanceolate leaves with entire margins. The foliage is typically arranged in an opposite pattern along the twining vines. Small, tubular flowers emerge in umbel-like clusters, characterized by a pale yellowish or greenish color. These blossoms feature a complex structure with a distinct corona surrounding the central reproductive organs. The plant eventually produces small, elongated follicles that contain numerous tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cynanchum wilfordii?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, hepatoprotective, neuroprotective, and anti-apoptotic properties. Its bioactive compounds effectively neutralize free radicals to mitigate oxidative stress within various biological systems. The extract demonstrates significant capacity to protect liver cells from chemical toxicity and reduce systemic inflammation. Furthermore, it exerts neuroprotective effects by shielding neurons from damage and regulating pathways related to programmed cell death. These combined mechanisms suggest that the plant holds substantial potential for treating metabolic and degenerative diseases.

As discussed in a study, Cynanchum wilfordii is a widely used medicinal plant in Oriental medicine for treating various conditions. An extract of Cynanchum wilfordii roots, known as CWE, and its isolated component cynandione A (CA) significantly decrease LPS-induced nitric oxide production and iNOS expression in a concentration-dependent manner. CWE does not exhibit cytotoxic activity at concentrations up to 500 µg/mL, while CA remains non-cytotoxic up to 80 µM. CA significantly attenuates the expression of tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and IL-1β in LPS-stimulated BV-2 cells. These findings indicate that CWE and CA may possess effective anti-inflammatory activities via NF-κB inactivation in stimulated microglial cells.
