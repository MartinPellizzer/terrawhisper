What's the taxonomical classification of Crataegus scabrifolia?

Craeataegus scabrifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is categorized under Equisetopsida, falling into the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Ultimately, its taxonomic identity is defined by its membership in the genus Crataegus, specifically identified as the species scabrifolia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Crataegus scabrifolia has small, ovate to elliptical leaves with serrated margins and a scabrous, sandpaper-like texture on both surfaces. The foliage is characterized by a rough, scabrid pubescence that distinguishes it from other hawthorn species. Its woody stems are typically armed with sharp spines and may exhibit a grayish bark texture. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers that eventually develop into fleshy, globose pomes. These morphological traits collectively reflect its adaptation to specific environmental niches.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause adverse reactions such as gastrointestinal distress, dizziness, palpitations, and potential interactions with heart medications like digoxin or beta-blockers. Individuals consuming the plant may experience nausea, stomach cramping, or diarrhea as the body processes its bioactive compounds. Because it can affect cardiac rhythm, people with pre-existing heart conditions should exercise extreme caution to avoid dangerous irregularities. Furthermore, the plant may lower blood pressure, which could lead to lightheadedness or fainting in sensitive individuals. Always consult a medical professional before using any botanical species that impacts cardiovascular function.
