Itea omeiensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Itea omeiensis?

Itea omeiensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Saxifrages, it is assigned to the family Iteaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Itea, with its specific designation being the species omeiensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Itea omeiensis has small, evergreen leaves that are elliptic to lanceolate in shape, measuring approximately 1 to 3 centimeters in length with serrated margins and a leathery texture.

The plant typically grows as a compact shrub with woody stems that exhibit a spreading growth habit. Its inflorescences consist of small, fragrant white flowers that emerge from the leaf axils during the blooming season. Following pollination, the plant produces tiny, dry capsules that contain small seeds for dispersal.

The overall structure is characterized by dense foliage and a fine-textured appearance.

      What are the herbal preparations of Itea omeiensis?

      Itea omeiensis is prepared primarily as a decoction through boiling the dried leaves or branches in water to extract its medicinal components. This liquid infusion is often consumed as a tea to utilize its traditional properties in folk medicine. Some practitioners may also process the plant material into a concentrated tincture by soaking it in alcohol for an extended period. Additionally, the dried plant parts can be ground into a fine powder for topical applications or mixed with other ingredients. These various forms allow for different methods of administration depending on the intended therapeutic use.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Itea omeiensis can cause skin irritation, digestive distress, and potential allergic reactions if ingested or touched directly. While specific clinical data on human toxicity is limited, many plants in the Itea genus contain compounds that may trigger dermatitis upon contact with the skin. Ingesting parts of the plant could lead to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramps. Individuals with known sensitivities to woody shrubs should approach the plant with caution to avoid unexpected hypersensitivity. Monitoring for any localized redness or swelling is advised after handling the foliage.
