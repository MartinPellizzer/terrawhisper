Dendrobium terminale

What's the taxonomical classification of Dendrobium terminale?

Dendrobium terminale belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is ordered within Asparagales and is a member of the diverse family Orchidaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Dendrobium, with the unique species designation terminale.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dendrobium terminale has an epiphytic growth habit characterized by elongated, slender pseudobulbs that are typically thin and somewhat compressed. These pseudobulbs serve as water storage organs and can reach varying lengths depending on the age of the plant. From the apex of these stems, several narrow, lanceolate leaves emerge in a distichous arrangement. The plant produces showy, fragrant flowers that are predominantly white with distinctive purple or pink markings on the labellum. These inflorescences often appear at the terminal ends of the stems, giving the species its specific name.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of northern and eastern Australia, including parts of Queensland and the Northern Territory. It is also found across various islands in the Indo-Pacific region, extending through parts of Southeast Asia. Within its natural range, the species typically inhabits coastal areas and rainforest margins where humidity is high. What are the pharmacological activities of Dendrobium terminale?

This plant has significant pharmacological potential characterized primarily by its potent antitumor activities and marked cytotoxicity against various cancer cell lines. Research indicates that specific bioactive compounds within the plant can effectively inhibit the proliferation of malignant cells. These cytotoxic effects are often linked to the induction of apoptosis, which leads to programmed cell death in targeted tumor tissues. Furthermore, the chemical constituents of Dendrobium terminale contribute to its ability to disrupt the metabolic processes of rapidly dividing cells. Such properties make it a subject of intense interest for developing new therapeutic agents in oncology.

As discussed in a study, two previously undescribed dihydrophenanthrene derivatives, identified as 1 and 2, were isolated from the whole plant of Dendrobium terminale along with 12 known analogues. The isolated compounds were evaluated for cytotoxicity against 3 kinds of tumor cell lines, specifically sw1990, HCT-116, and HepG2. Among the isolated substances, compounds 11 and 14 showed stronger antitumor effects. The study also involved the revision of NMR data for known phenanthrene derivatives 7 and 9 using 2D NMR.
