Dendrobium terminale

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dendrobium terminale has an epiphytic growth habit characterized by elongated, slender pseudobulbs that are typically thin and somewhat compressed. These pseudobulbs serve as water storage organs and can reach varying lengths depending on the age of the plant. From the apex of these stems, several narrow, lanceolate leaves emerge in a distichous arrangement. The plant produces showy, fragrant flowers that are predominantly white with distinctive purple or pink markings on the labellum. These inflorescences often appear at the terminal ends of the stems, giving the species its specific name.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Dendrobium terminale can cause localized skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or a burning sensation upon direct contact with the plant's sap or tissues. Ingestion of various Dendrobium species can lead to nausea or stomach discomfort in sensitive users. There is also a possibility of systemic allergic reactions in people with specific botanical sensitivities. Scientific documentation regarding long-term toxicity remains limited, necessitating caution when handling the plant.
