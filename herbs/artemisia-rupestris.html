Artemisia rupestris

What's the taxonomical classification of Artemisia rupestris?

Artemisia rupestris belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae, which is further categorized into the genus Artemisia, ultimately identifying it as the specific species Artemisia rupestris.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Artemisia rupestris has a perennial, woody-based growth habit characterized by low-growing, spreading stems and deeply lobed, silvery-green leaves. The foliage is typically divided into narrow, linear segments that provide a fine texture to the plant. These leaves often possess a dense covering of fine hairs, which contributes to their distinct grayish appearance. Small, inconspicuous flower heads are produced on short stalks, appearing in clusters during the flowering season. The overall structure is compact and adapted to rocky or alpine environments where it can withstand harsh conditions.

What are the pharmacological activities of Artemisia rupestris?

This plant has demonstrated diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anti-tumor, antiviral, immunomodulatory, and liver protection properties. Its anti-inflammatory effects help reduce systemic swelling and pain by inhibiting specific biochemical pathways. Research indicates that its anti-tumor capabilities may suppress the growth and spread of malignant cells. Furthermore, the plant exhibits antiviral potential by interfering with the replication cycles of certain pathogens. Its immunomodulatory action helps regulate the body's natural defenses, while its liver protection properties assist in shielding hepatic tissues from toxic damage.

As discussed in a study, Artemisia rupestris L. demonstrates therapeutic potential against hepatocellular carcinoma by targeting inflammatory pathways and macrophage polarization. When treated with concentrations of 25, 50, and 100 μg/mL, the plant showed significant effects on cell behavior. Specifically, at 100 μg/mL, it significantly inhibited HCC cell proliferation, reduced migration by 31.9-49.3%, and decreased cell invasion by up to 3.2 folds. The plant caused dose dependent suppressions of IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α while reducing levels of TLR4, MyD88, NF-κB, and phosphorylated IκB. Furthermore, ARL treatment downregulated CD86 expression to impair M1 macrophage activation. These dual mechanisms allow the plant to interfere in the TLR4/MyD88/NF-κB cascade and reprogram macrophage polarization.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential gastrointestinal distress if ingested. Contact with the foliage may lead to contact dermatitis characterized by redness or itching in sensitive individuals. When consumed, the compounds within the plant may trigger nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain. There is also a risk of systemic toxicity if large quantities are taken due to its chemical composition. Careful avoidance is recommended for those with known sensitivities to the Asteraceae family.
