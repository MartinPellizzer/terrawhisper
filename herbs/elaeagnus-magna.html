Elaeagnus magna

What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeagnus magna?

Elaeagnus magna belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant falls under the order Rosales and is a member of the family Elaeagnaceae. Finally, it is defined by its genus, Elaeagnus, and its specific species designation, magna.

What are the therapeutic actions of Elaeagnus magna?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin diseases, digestive disorders, and inflammatory conditions. The bark and leaves contain bioactive compounds that exhibit antimicrobial and antioxidant properties. These chemical constituents help reduce swelling and soothe irritations on the skin surface. When consumed in traditional preparations, the plant helps regulate stomach functions and alleviate internal discomfort. Research continues to explore its potential efficacy in managing various systemic ailments.

What are the herbal preparations of Elaeagnus magna?

Elaeagnus magna is prepared as decoctions, infusions, or tinctures utilizing the bark, leaves, or fruit of the plant. The bark is frequently simmered in water for extended periods to extract its dense tannins and active compounds. Leaves are often steeped in hot water to create a milder tea suitable for topical or internal use. Tinctures are made by soaking dried plant parts in high-proof alcohol to ensure a concentrated extract. These diverse methods allow for different concentrations of the plant's chemical constituents depending on the intended application.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Elaeagnus magna can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, and digestive upset if the plant parts are handled or ingested. The fine, scale-like leaves and bark may trigger contact dermatitis in individuals with sensitive skin through direct physical contact. Ingestion of the berries or foliage can lead to nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain due to unknown chemical compounds. Some people may also experience respiratory discomfort if they inhale pollen or dust from the plant during blooming periods. Monitoring for these reactions is essential when working around or consuming parts of this species.
