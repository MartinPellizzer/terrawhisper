White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) is a common flowering plant found in woodland and grassy areas, known for its medicinal properties and historical use in treating various ailments, including digestive issues and skin conditions.

What is White Dead-Nettle (Lamium Album)?

White Dead-Nettle, or Lamium album, is a perennial herb native to Europe and parts of Asia, characterized by its heart-shaped leaves and clusters of pale pink to white flowers, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported health benefits.

Botanical Identity and Classification

White Dead-Nettle, or Lamium album, is a perennial herbaceous plant belonging to the family Lamiaceae, commonly known as the mint family, and is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa.

Common Names and Synonyms

White Dead-Nettle, scientifically known as Lamium album, is also referred to by several common names including Dead-Nettle, White Nettle, and Spring Beauty, reflecting its widespread distribution and historical use in herbal medicine.

Plant Description and Morphology

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) is a perennial herb characterized by its heart-shaped, hairy leaves with a distinctive white or pale purple underside, and its tubular, two-lipped flowers that range in color from white to purple, typically blooming in clusters at the base of the plant.

Native Habitat and Distribution

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, typically growing in moist, shaded environments such as woodlands, hedgerows, and along stream banks.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, flowers, and roots of White Dead-Nettle are used medicinally, with the leaves being the most commonly utilized part for their mild diuretic and anti-inflammatory properties.

Phytochemical Composition

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) contains a variety of bioactive compounds including flavonoids, iridoids, tannins, mucilage, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties and therapeutic uses in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) exhibits pharmacological properties including anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and mild diuretic effects, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as rosmarinic acid and iridoids.

Mechanisms of Action

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) exerts its effects through the presence of compounds such as iridoids, flavonoids, and mucilage, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and mild sedative properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

White dead-nettle is used to treat urinary tract infections, inflammation of the bladder, kidney stones, skin irritations, wounds, and as a general tonic for digestive and circulatory health.

Preparation Methods and Forms

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) is commonly prepared as a decoction, tincture, or topical poultice, with the leaves and stems harvested during early spring and dried for use in herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) for herbal use typically ranges from 2 to 4 grams of dried herb per day, divided into two or three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the preparation method and intended therapeutic purpose.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While White Dead-Nettle is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal upset, such as nausea or diarrhea, and in some cases, skin irritation if applied topically.

Drug Interactions

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) may interact with medications that affect the cardiovascular system, such as anticoagulants and antiplatelet agents, due to its potential to influence blood clotting and vascular tone.

Toxicity and Precautions

While White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal upset in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy, breastfeeding, and for children has not been fully established, so it should be used with caution and under professional guidance.

Cultivation and Harvesting

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) is typically cultivated in shaded, moist environments with well-draining soil, often in woodland areas or along the edges of gardens, and it thrives in temperate climates.

Processing and Extraction

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) is typically processed by harvesting the leaves and flowers during the early flowering stage, then drying them in a cool, dark place to preserve their medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, in an airtight container to preserve its potency and prevent moisture damage, with a shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) has primarily focused on its potential anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and wound-healing properties, with some studies suggesting its use in traditional herbal medicine for treating conditions such as hemorrhoids, skin irritations, and minor wounds.

Traditional and Historical Uses

White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to support urinary health, alleviate symptoms of urinary tract infections, and promote kidney function through its diuretic properties.

Regulatory Status

The regulatory status of White Dead-Nettle (Lamium album) as an herbal medicine varies by country, with some regions recognizing it as a traditional remedy under specific herbal regulations, while others require additional scientific validation and licensing for commercial use.
