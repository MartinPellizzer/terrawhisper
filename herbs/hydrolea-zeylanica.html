Hydrolea zeylanica

What's the taxonomical classification of Hydrolea zeylanica?

Hydrolea zeylanica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is a member of the order Solanales and the family Hydroleaceae. Ultimately, it is identified by the genus Hydrolea and is specifically designated as the species zeylanica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hydrolea zeylanica has opposite, simple leaves that are typically ovate or elliptical in shape with entire or slightly serrated margins and acute tips. The stems are woody and often exhibit a sprawling or climbing growth habit depending on the specific environment. Small, inconspicuous flowers are arranged in terminal cymes and possess a characteristic structure typical of the Hydroleaceae family. The fruit develops into a small, fleshy drupe that facilitates seed dispersal. These morphological features collectively allow the plant to thrive in its native wetland or riparian habitats.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, specifically occurring in countries such as India, Sri Lanka, and parts of the Malay Archipelago. Its distribution is closely tied to humid, lowland environments where seasonal rainfall is abundant. Many populations are found near freshwater sources like riverbanks, marshes, and coastal wetlands. The species thrives in these warm, moist habitats that characterize the Indo-Malayan realm. Consequently, its presence is most concentrated in areas with high humidity and consistent tropical temperatures.

What are the pharmacological activities of Hydrolea zeylanica?

This plant has potent anti-inflammatory, antidiabetic, antioxidant, and free radical scavenging pharmacological activities. Its phytochemical constituents work together to suppress inflammatory responses in biological systems. The plant also demonstrates a significant capacity to regulate blood glucose levels, making it a subject of interest for antidiabetic research. Furthermore, its high antioxidant properties allow it to neutralize harmful molecules within the body. By providing strong free radical scavenging abilities, it helps protect cells from oxidative stress and damage.

As discussed in a study, network pharmacology analysis was conducted on 32 bioactive components of Hydrolea zeylanica (HZH). The analysis identified a core relationship between 13 selected compounds and 194 key target genes related to insulin resistance, type II diabetes mellitus, and insulin signaling pathways. When HZH was administered at doses of 300 and 400 mg/kg, it was evaluated for its effect on GLUTs expression and inflammatory cytokines in T2DM rats. Specifically, HZH-400 significantly restored glucose homeostasis and inflammatory markers in these rats. This dosage altered GLUT2 and GLUT4 expression in the liver and skeletal muscle to normal levels. Through the modulation of insulin signaling pathways, the bioactive compounds of HZH improved glucose uptake and insulin production.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains caffeic acid, ferulic acid, flavonoids, gallic acid, and quercetin, which serve as its primary medicinal compounds. These bioactive constituents contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Specifically, the presence of gallic acid and caffeic acid helps neutralize harmful free radicals within biological systems. Flavonoids and quercetin provide additional cellular protection by modulating various enzymatic pathways. Together, these phytochemicals work synergistically to support overall health and mitigate oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the green and leafy vegetables of Hydrolea zeylanica serve as a rich source of nutrition, dietary fibers, vitamins, and flavonoids including quercetin, caffeic acid, rutin, gallic acid, and ferulic acid. The hydroalcohol extracted fraction of leaves, known as HHZ, demonstrated potential cellular antioxidant protection in ORAC and CAP-e tests. When administered orally at doses of 300 and 400 mg/kg b.w. for 4 weeks, the HHZ-400 dose significantly (p < 0.001) improved learning and memory impairment in rats. This treatment also significantly (p < 0.001) improved oxidative markers like MDA, SOD, CAT, and GSH, as well as proinflammatory markers including TNF-α, IL-6, and hs-CRP. Additionally, the plant showed mixed competitive inhibition towards acetylcholinesterase (AChE) and butyrylcholineseraase (BChE) activities.
