What's the taxonomical classification of Zanthoxylum acanthopodium?

Zanthoxylum acanthopodium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant falls under the order Sapindales and the family Rutaecae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Zanthoxylum, and its unique species designation, acanthopodium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Zanthoxylum acanthopodium has a woody structure characterized by spreading branches, small elliptical leaves, and prominent spines located along the stems. The foliage consists of pinnately compound leaves with several pairs of leaflets that possess smooth or slightly serrated margins. Small, inconspicuous flowers typically emerge in clusters from the axillary regions of the branches. These blossoms eventually develop into small, fleshy drupes that serve as the plant's fruit. The bark of the plant is generally rough and provides a protective layer for the underlying vascular system.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including terpenoids, alkaloids, flavonoids, limonene, and terpine-4-ol. The presence of terpenoids and terpine-4-ol provides significant antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties to the plant's chemical profile. Alkaloids within the structure contribute to complex pharmacological activities that can affect the central nervous system. Additionally, flavonoids act as potent antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress. Finally, the inclusion of limonene adds a distinct aromatic quality while offering potential digestive benefits.

As discussed in a study, the essential oil from China contains 29.78% limonene and 26.65% β-myrcene, while the sample from Myanmar is dominated by Terpinen-4-ol at 43.35%. Both essential oils exhibited antifungal activity with 90% minimum inhibitory concentration values between 26.3 and 499 μg/mL. The China sample showed anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting nitric oxide in RAW 264.7 cells with an IC50 of 16 μg/mL. The Myanmar sample demonstrated antiviral activity against the dengue virus with an IC50 value of 13 μg/mL. Furthermore, both samples showed dose-dependent larvicidal activity against Aedes albopictus with LC50 and LC90 values ranging from 45.8 to 144.0 μg/mL.
