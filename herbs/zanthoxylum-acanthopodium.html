Zanthoxylum acanthopodium

What's the taxonomical classification of Zanthoxylum acanthopodium?

Zanthoxylum acanthopodium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, the plant falls under the order Sapindales and the family Rutaecae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Zanthoxylum, and its unique species designation, acanthopodium.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Zanthoxylum acanthopodium has a woody structure characterized by spreading branches, small elliptical leaves, and prominent spines located along the stems. The foliage consists of pinnately compound leaves with several pairs of leaflets that possess smooth or slightly serrated margins. Small, inconspicuous flowers typically emerge in clusters from the axillary regions of the branches. These blossoms eventually develop into small, fleshy drupes that serve as the plant's fruit. The bark of the plant is generally rough and provides a protective layer for the underlying vascular system.

What are the pharmacological activities of Zanthoxylum acanthopodium?

This plant has anti-diabetic, anti-hyperglycemic, anti-inflammatory, antifungal, and antioxidant pharmacological activities. Its ability to manage blood glucose levels makes it a significant subject for studying anti-diabetic and anti-hyperglycemic treatments. The presence of bioactive compounds also provides potent anti-inflammatory effects to reduce swelling and pain. Additionally, its antifungal properties offer a natural way to combat various fungal infections. These diverse therapeutic benefits are further supported by its strong antioxidant capacity which protects cells from oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extract of Zanthoxylum acanthopodium fruit exerts an attenuative effect on hyperglycemia, sensory and motoric function disorders, and tissue oxidative stress in mice induced by 200 mg/kg body weight of alloxan monohydrate. When mice were treated daily with doses of 0, 150, 300, and 450 mg/kg BW per oral for 28 days, higher doses significantly attenuated elevations in random and fasting blood glucose (p < 0.05). These doses also improved paw sensitivity responses (p < 0.05) and motoric balances (p < 0.05) while attenuating the degeneration of cerebellar Purkinje cells (p < 0.05). Furthermore, the extract suppressed MDA levels in the blood (p < 0.05) and blunted MDA in brain tissue (p < 0.05). Phytochemical screening of the extract revealed 39 compounds, consisting of 26 alkaloids, 12 flavonoids, and 1 terpenoid.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a complex profile of medicinal compounds including terpenoids, flavonoids, alkaloids, limonene, and terpinen-4-ol. The presence of various alkaloids provides the plant with significant biological activity and potential therapeutic properties. Within the terpenoid class, terpinen-4-ol serves as a primary constituent responsible for many of its antimicrobial effects. Additionally, the limonene content contributes to its distinct aromatic qualities and antioxidant capabilities. Flavonoids further enhance the medicinal value by offering anti-inflammatory and protective benefits to the body.

As discussed in a study, the essential oil from China contains limonene at 29.78% and β-myrcene at 26.65%. The essential oil sample from Myanmar is dominated by Terpinen-4-ol at 43.35%. Both essential oils possess antifungal activity with 90% minimum inhibitory concentration values ranging from 26.3 to 499 μg/mL. The Chinese oil sample showed anti-inflammatory activity with an IC50 of 16 μg/mL in RAW 264.7 cells. Furthermore, the Myanmar sample exhibited antiviral activity against the dengue virus with an IC50 value of 13 μg/mL. Both oils demonstrated dose-dependent larvicidal activity against Aedes albopictus with LC50 and LC90 values between 45.8 and 144.0 μg/mL.
