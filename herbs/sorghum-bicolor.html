Sorghum bicolor

What's the taxonomical classification of Sorghum bicolor?

Sorghum bicolor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Poales and the family Poaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Sorghum, and its species name, bicolor.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sorghum bicolor has a highly branched, erect stem that can reach heights of up to four meters and is characterized by numerous nodes and internodes. The leaves are linear and elongated, featuring a prominent midrib and parallel venation that extends to a tapered tip. At the top of the stem, the plant produces a dense, terminal panicle that holds numerous small, single-seeded spikelets. The root system is fibrous and extensive, providing both stability and efficient nutrient uptake from the soil. This morphological structure allows the plant to thrive in various environmental conditions and withstand significant drought stress.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to sub-Saharan Africa but is now cultivated extensively across the tropical, subtropical, and warm temperate regions of the entire world. It is a major crop in many developing nations across Africa and Asia where it provides essential food and fodder. In the United States, production is concentrated in the southern states due to the requirement for warm growing seasons. Its ability to thrive in semi-arid environments allows it to spread into regions with limited rainfall where other cereals might fail. Large-scale cultivation also occurs in parts of South America and Australia, making it a globally distributed staple.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds such as apiogenin, flavonoids, phenolic acids, polyphenol, and sorgoleone. These diverse phytochemicals contribute to a wide range of biological activities within the organism. For instance, the presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids often provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The polyphenol content further supports cellular protection against oxidative stress. Additionally, the specific compound sorgoleone plays a unique role in the plant's chemical defense mechanisms.

As discussed in a study, Sorghum bicolor leaf contains apigenin and apigeninidin isolates that target inflammation via cyclo-oxygenase-2 and prostaglandin-E2 blockade. The study examined various fractions including crude extract (J), ethyl-acetate (JE), and aqueous (JA) fractions. The JA fraction of Sorghum bicolor leaf had the highest COX-2IC50:COX-1IC50 ratio of 41.214, whereas the JE fraction had the lowest ratio of 1.161. Additionally, the JE5 fraction showed a ratio of 0.495, while the P8 compound derived from JE5 demonstrated a dose-dependent reduction in both the COX-2IC50:COX-1IC50 ratio and prostaglandin-E2 production. These medicinal properties were tested using purified (P9) and semi-purified (P8) compounds from JE5 at concentrations ranging from 5-200 μg/mL for 1 hour.

      What side effects this plant can have?

      This plant can cause nausea, digestive upset, abdominal discomfort, and bloating in some individuals due to its high fiber content or specific anti-nutrients like tannins. These gastrointestinal reactions often occur when a person consumes large amounts of the grain without proper preparation or soaking. Monitoring individual tolerance is recommended to avoid these unpleasant side effects.
