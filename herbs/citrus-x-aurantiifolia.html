Citrus × aurantiifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrus × aurantiifolia?

Citrus × aurantiifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant follows the biological order Sapindales. It is further categorized under the family Rutaceae, which is characteristic of many citrus species. Within this family, it is placed in the genus Citrus, with its specific designation being the species aurantifolia.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Citrus × aurantiifolia contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its fruit juice, the rind, and the leaves. The juice is frequently utilized for its high vitamin C content to support immune function and treat respiratory ailments. Essential oils extracted from the peel are applied topically or inhaled to reduce anxiety and alleviate digestive discomfort. Additionally, the leaves are often brewed into teas to help lower fever and reduce inflammation. These diverse parts provide a versatile range of therapeutic applications in traditional medicine.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive chemical constituents including flavonoids, terpenoids, alkaloids, phenolic acids, coumarins, and limonoids that contribute to its therapeutic potential.

The presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the species. Terpenoids and limonoids are also abundant, often playing critical roles in the plant's antimicrobial and anticancer activities. Additionally, the alkaloid content contributes to the complex pharmacological profile utilized in various traditional medicines.

These synergistic combinations of secondary metabolites make the plant a subject of interest for pharmaceutical research.

The primary medicinal compounds of this plant are shown in the list below.

  • Flavonoids
  • Terpenoids
  • Alkaloids
  • Phenolic acids
  • Coumarins
  • Limonoids

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Citrus × aurantiifolia targets various enzymes and receptors including cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and acetylcholinesterase to exert its therapeutic effects. Its bioactive compounds, such as flavonoids and limonoids, act as potent inhibitors of inflammatory mediators by modulating the arachidonic acid metabolism pathway. These phytochemicals also interact with neurotransmitter systems to influence cognitive function and neuroprotection through the modulation of cholinergic signaling. Furthermore, certain constituents interfere with the activity of specific cytokines and nuclear factor kappa B pathways to reduce systemic inflammation. Such diverse molecular interactions allow the plant to exhibit antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Citrus × aurantiifolia?

Citrus × aurantiifolia has antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties that allow it to combat various bacterial, fungal, and viral pathogens. The essential oils extracted from its fruit and leaves contain bioactive compounds like limonene and citral which disrupt microbial cell membranes. These phytochemicals also neutralize free radicals, helping to reduce oxidative stress within the body. Additionally, the plant's extracts can inhibit pro-inflammatory enzymes to alleviate swelling and pain. Such diverse therapeutic actions make it a valuable component in both traditional medicine and modern pharmacological research.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including digestive issues, respiratory infections, skin infections, and minor wounds. The essential oils found in the fruit and leaves possess antimicrobial properties that help combat bacteria and fungi. In traditional medicine, it is often applied topically to soothe skin irritations or used in teas to alleviate symptoms of the common cold. Additionally, its acidic components can aid in managing nausea and promoting better digestion after meals. Some practitioners also utilize its extracts to assist with the healing of minor cuts and abrasions.

What are the herbal preparations of Citrus × aurantiifolia?

Citrus × aurantiifolia is prepared as essential oils, fruit juices, dried leaf infusions, and alcoholic tinctures derived from its peel, pulp, and foliage. The essential oil is typically extracted through cold pressing the rind to capture its potent aromatic compounds. For internal use, the fresh juice provides a direct source of vitamin C and citric acid. Herbalists often create decoctions by simmering the dried peels to extract bitter flavonoids and tannins. Tinctures made from the fruit or leaves serve as concentrated extracts for long-term storage and standardized dosing.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Citrus × aurantiifolia can cause phototoxicity, skin irritation, and gastrointestinal distress if ingested in large quantities. The essential oils found in the fruit contain furanocoumarins, which react with sunlight to cause painful blisters or redness on the skin. Applying the juice to sensitive areas may also lead to localized allergic reactions or contact dermatitis. If swallowed excessively, the high acidity can irritate the lining of the stomach or esophagus. These reactions vary depending on individual sensitivity and the concentration of the plant's compounds.
