Selaginella vaginata

What's the taxonomical classification of Selaginella vaginata?

Selaginella vaginata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Lycopodiidae. Following its taxonomic hierarchy further, it falls under the order Selaginellales and the family Selaginellaceae. Within this lineage, it is placed in the genus Selaginella, ultimately identifying the specific species as vaginaata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Selaginella vaginaata has a creeping rhizome that produces small, dichotomously branched stems with microphylls arranged in four rows along the axis.

The plant typically grows in dense, low-lying mats where the scale-like leaves are densely overlapping. These small leaves are characterized by their narrow, lanceolate shape and often feature a distinct central vein. The overall structure allows the plant to spread efficiently across moist substrates in its natural habitat.

Such morphological adaptations are essential for its survival in shaded, humid environments.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Selaginella vaginata can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive upset if ingested or handled without protection. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many members of the Selaginella genus contain secondary metabolites that may act as irritants to sensitive tissues. Some individuals might experience localized redness or itching upon direct contact with the plant's surface. If consumed accidentally, it could potentially lead to nausea or abdominal discomfort depending on the concentration of bioactive compounds present. It is essential to exercise caution and avoid skin contact until more comprehensive toxicological profiles are established.
