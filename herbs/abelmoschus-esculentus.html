Abelmoschus esculentus

What's the taxonomical classification of Abelmoschus esculentus?

Abelmoschus esculentuus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malvales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Malvaceae, which places it in the genus Abelmoschus. Finally, the specific identification of this plant is completed by its species name, esculentus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Abelmoschus esculentus has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 0.2 to 2 meters and features a slightly hairy or pubescent texture. The leaves are palmately lobed with three to seven distinct segments and possess serrated margins. Its flowers are large, funnel-shaped, and pale yellow with a dark red or purple center known as the hibiscus-like corolla. The fruit develops as an elongated, five-angled capsule that contains numerous small, round, dark seeds. These botanical characteristics are essential for identifying the plant within the Malvaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of West and East Africa, though it has been widely naturalized throughout the global tropics. It thrives in warm climates characterized by high humidity and significant rainfall, particularly across much of South and Southeast Asia. Cultivation is extensive in countries like India, Nigeria, and China, where it serves as a staple crop. Due to its adaptability, it is also frequently grown in parts of the Americas and the Caribbean. The species continues to expand its footprint in any region that provides frost-free growing seasons.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Abelmoschus esculentus contains medicinal compounds located primarily in its seeds, leaves, flowers, and mucilaginous fruit pods. What are the pharmacological activities of Abelmoschus esculentus?

This plant has antioxidant, anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and hypoglycemic pharmacological activities that contribute to its medicinal value. The antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within the body. Its anti-diabetic and hypoglycemic effects work together to regulate blood glucose levels and manage metabolic dysfunction. Additionally, the plant exhibits anti-inflammatory actions that help mitigate swelling and pain. Finally, its antimicrobial capabilities allow it to combat various pathogenic microorganisms.

As discussed in a study, Abelmoschus esculentus pods possess anti-fatigue activity, specifically within the okra seeds (OSD) which contain 29.5% total polyphenols, 14.8% total polysaccharides, 5.35% total flavonoids, 2.067% isoquercitrin, and 2.741% quercetin-3-O-gentiobiose. In contrast, the okra skins (OSK) contained 1.25% total polyphenols and 43.1% total polysaccharides. The OSD demonstrated significant antioxidant and anti-fatigue effects through reducing blood lactic acid (BLA) and urea nitrogen (BUN) levels. Furthermore, the OSD enhanced hepatic glycogen storage and promoted antioxidant ability by lowering malondialdehyde (MDA) levels while increasing superoxide dismutase (SOD) and glutathione peroxidase (GSH-PX) levels.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, phenolic compounds, rosmarinic acid, quercetin, and rutin. These bioactive substances contribute to the plant's significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Among the specific flavonoids present, quercetin and rutin serve as potent agents that help protect cells from oxidative damage. The inclusion of rosmarinic acid further enhances its therapeutic potential by supporting various biological functions. Together, these phenolic compounds form a complex chemical profile that offers numerous health benefits.

As discussed in a study, Abelmoschus esculentus contains flavonoids that are responsible for antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anti-diabetic effects. In a model using streptozotocin-induced diabetic male Wistar rats, a 75% ethanolic extract of okra was administered at oral dosages of 200-400 mg/kg for eight weeks. This okra extract may lower blood glucose (BS), insulin, Triglyceride (TG), Cholesterol (Cho), and glucose transporter protein type-4 (GLUT4) levels. The extract also inhibits PTP1B and Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor alpha (PPAR-α), which are important regulators of glucose and lipid homeostasis. These findings support the anti-hyperglycemic and hypolipidemic properties of Abelmoschus esculentus in controlling diabetes.
