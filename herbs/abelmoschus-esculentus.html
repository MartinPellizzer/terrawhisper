Abelmoschus esculentus

What's the taxonomical classification of Abelmoschus esculentus?

Abelmoschus esculentuus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Malvales. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the family Malvaceae, which places it in the genus Abelmoschus. Finally, the specific identification of this plant is completed by its species name, esculentus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Abelmoschus esculentus has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches heights of 0.2 to 2 meters and features a slightly hairy or pubescent texture. The leaves are palmately lobed with three to seven distinct segments and possess serrated margins. Its flowers are large, funnel-shaped, and pale yellow with a dark red or purple center known as the hibiscus-like corolla. The fruit develops as an elongated, five-angled capsule that contains numerous small, round, dark seeds. These botanical characteristics are essential for identifying the plant within the Malvaceae family.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of West and East Africa, though it has been widely naturalized throughout the global tropics. It thrives in warm climates characterized by high humidity and significant rainfall, particularly across much of South and Southeast Asia. Cultivation is extensive in countries like India, Nigeria, and China, where it serves as a staple crop. Due to its adaptability, it is also frequently grown in parts of the Americas and the Caribbean. The species continues to expand its footprint in any region that provides frost-free growing seasons.

What are the pharmacological activities of Abelmoschus esculentus?

This plant has anti-diabetic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and analgesic pharmacological activities that contribute to its significant therapeutic potential. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within the body. The plant also demonstrates anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects, which assist in managing pain and swelling. Additionally, its anti-diabetic capabilities support blood glucose regulation, while its antimicrobial actions provide defense against various pathogens. These diverse biological functions make Abelmoschus esculentus a valuable subject for medicinal research.

According to a study published by "Journal of food biochemistry", Abelmoschus esculentus improves blood glucose, lipid, and down-regulates PPAR-α and PTP1B gene expression in diabetic rats. Using a 75% ethanolic extract, oral dosages of 200-400 mg/kg were administered for eight weeks. The findings indicate that okra extract may lower blood glucose, insulin, Triglyceride, Cholesterol, and glucose transporter protein type-4 levels. This extract also has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anti-diabetic effects due to its flavonoids. Furthermore, the okra extract inhibits PTP1B and PPAR-α, which are important regulators of glucose and lipid homeostasis, supporting its anti-hyperglycemic and hypolipidemic properties.

What health conditions is this plant used for?

This plant is used for managing diabetes mellitus, constipation, gastric ulcer, hyperglycemia, and dyslipidemia. The high mucilage content in its tissues helps soothe the digestive tract to alleviate symptoms of a gastric ulcer. Its dietary fiber is particularly effective at promoting regular bowel movements to combat constipation. Furthermore, the plant plays a significant role in regulating blood glucose levels to treat hyperglycemia and diabetes mellitus. It also assists in improving lipid profiles to help manage dyslipidemia.

According to a study published by "Evidence-based complementary and alternative medicine : eCAM", Abelmoschus esculentuus, referred to as okra pods, is among the most commonly used traditional medicines. The study involved 140 patients attending diabetic clinics and 20 additional diabetic patients for focus group discussions. Within the 140 participants who answered the questionnaire, 67.2% reported using traditional medicines to manage their diabetes. Additionally, 58.6% of those participants reported using both conventional medicines and traditional medicines. These traditional medicines, including okra pods, are used by patients and 8 local herbalists to treat diabetes.
