What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis delavayi?

Clematis delavayi belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical structure, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Ranunculales and the family Ranunculaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic placement is defined by the genus Clematis and the unique species epithet delavayi.

    Clematis delavayi has traditional medicinal applications primarily involving its use as a diuretic, emetic, and purgative to treat various internal ailments. However, the plant contains toxic elements that require extreme caution and precise dosage to prevent accidental poisoning.     What are the herbal preparations of Clematis delavayi?

    Clematis delavayi is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion made from its dried leaves and stems to extract its bioactive compounds. This botanical material is typically steeped in hot water to create a medicinal tea used in traditional herbal practices. Some practitioners may also incorporate the plant into concentrated tinctures by using high-proof alcohol as a solvent. These liquid preparations allow for more precise dosing of the plant's constituent alkaloids. Additionally, external applications such as poultices can be made from crushed fresh plant matter for topical use.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Clematis delavayi can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and respiratory issues due to the presence of protoanemonin in its tissues. If the plant comes into contact with skin, it may trigger contact dermatitis characterized by redness, itching, or blistering. Ingesting any part of the plant can lead to intense burning of the mouth and throat, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Even the sap can be harmful if it touches the eyes, potentially causing significant inflammation or irritation. It is essential to wear protective gear when handling this species to avoid these unpleasant reactions.
