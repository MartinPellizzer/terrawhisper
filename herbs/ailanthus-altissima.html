Ailanthus altissima

What's the taxonomical classification of Ailanthus altissima?

Ailanthus altissima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Sapindales. This plant is specifically categorized under the family Simaroubaceae, which places it within the genus Ailanthus. Ultimately, its unique biological identity is defined by its specific classification as the species altissima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ailanthus altiissima has large, pinnately compound leaves that typically reach 1 to 3 feet in length and consist of 10 to 40 pairs of lanceolate leaflets arranged oppositely along a central rachis.

The leaflets are characterized by an entire margin and a smooth, somewhat shiny surface texture. The tree exhibits a fast-growing, upright growth habit with bark that becomes deeply furrowed and grayish as the specimen matures. Small, yellowish-green flowers appear in dense panicles during the summer months.

These flowers are eventually replaced by numerous small, winged samaras that facilitate wind dispersal.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Ailantus altissima contains medicinal compounds found in its bark, leaves, and roots that are utilized to treat various ailments. The bark is frequently harvested to create decoctions used for managing fever and inflammatory conditions. Leaves from the tree are often prepared as poultices or infusions to address skin irritations and digestive issues. Additionally, the roots are sometimes processed for their potential antimicrobial and antipyretic properties. These diverse parts provide a wide range of phytochemicals used in traditional herbal medicine practices.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Ailantus altirossima targets inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its medicinal effects. The plant contains bioactive compounds like quassinoids that interfere with the signaling pathways of these specific proteins. By inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2, the extracts can effectively reduce the production of prostaglandins responsible for pain and swelling. Furthermore, the modulation of nitric oxide synthase levels helps in controlling oxidative stress and cellular inflammation. These interactions with enzymatic and cytokine targets provide the biochemical basis for the plant's traditional use in treating skin diseases and inflammatory conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Ailanthus altissima?

Ailantus altissima has therapeutic properties characterized by its use as a bitter, purgative, and vermicide agent in traditional medicinal practices.

The intense bitterness of the plant bark can stimulate digestive secretions and improve appetite. When administered in specific doses, it acts as a purgative to help clear the intestinal tract. Additionally, its chemical constituents allow it to function as a vermicide to expel intestinal parasites.

These biological actions make it a significant subject in ethnobotanical studies.

  • Bitter
  • Purgative
  • Vermicide

    What are the herbal preparations of Ailanthus altissima?

    Ailantus altissima is prepared as decoctions, infusions, tinctures, or topical poultices derived from the bark, leaves, or roots of the tree. The bark is most commonly boiled to create a bitter decoction used for its traditional medicinal properties. Leaves can be steeped in hot water to produce a mild infusion for various applications. Tinctures made from ethanol extractions allow for a more concentrated dose of the plant's alkaloids. Additionally, crushed plant material can be applied directly to the skin as a poultice for localized treatments.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Ailanthus altissima can cause severe skin irritation, allergic reactions, and respiratory distress if the plant's sap or dust is handled or inhaled. The milky sap contains quassinoids that act as potent irritants, often leading to redness, itching, or blistering upon contact with human skin. Individuals with sensitive respiratory systems may experience coughing or throat irritation from inhaling airborne particles during pruning or removal. Ingestion of the plant is also toxic and can result in significant gastrointestinal upset. Because of these chemical properties, protective gear is essential when working near this invasive species.
