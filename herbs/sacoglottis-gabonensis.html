Sacoglottis gabonensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Sacoglottis gabonensis?

Sacoglottis gabonenis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant follows the lineage of the order Malpighiales. It is further categorized under the family Humiriaceae, which contains the genus Sacoglottis, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species Sacoglottis gabonenis.

What are the pharmacological activities of Sacoglottis gabonensis?

This plant has significant antioxidant properties that allow it to neutralize harmful free radicals within biological systems. Research into its chemical constituents reveals a diverse range of secondary metabolites that contribute to these protective effects. Beyond its antioxidant capacity, studies suggest the plant may exhibit various other bioactive potential in medicinal applications. These pharmacological activities are primarily attributed to the specific phenolic compounds found in its extracts. Such findings highlight the importance of investigating this species for its potential roles in therapeutic development.

According to a study published by "Vascular pharmacology", the aqueous ethanol extract of Sacoglottis gabonenesis stem bark exhibits biological antioxidant properties against tissue damage induced by 2,4-dinitrophenyl hydrazine (2,4-DNPH) and ethanol. This bark extract demonstrated a protective action on brain glucose by significantly inhibiting the glucose-depleting effects of both 2,4-DNPH and ethanol. Additionally, the extract inhibited the lowering action of 2,4-DNPH and ethanol on the packed cell volume (PCV), red blood cell (RBC) count, and haemoglobin (Hb) concentration, although it inhibited the proliferation of total and differential white blood cells (WBC). The isolate bergermin showed an essentially similar trend to the bark extract regarding its effect on brain glucose and exerted a protective action on brain tissue against the oxidants, though to a lesser extent. These activities occur after 3 days of exposure to the extract or bergermin in drinking water prior to a single intraperitoneal 2,4-DNPH administration.
