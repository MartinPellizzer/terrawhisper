Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Denseflower Lousewort is Pedicularis densiflora, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It belongs to the genus Pedicularis in the family Orobanchaceae. Synonyms include Pedicularis densiflora subsp. densiflora and Pedicularis densiflora var. densiflora. It is also known by regional names such as "dense-flower lousewort" and "lousewort."

Morphological Characteristics

Denseflower Lousewort is a perennial herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branched stems that are hairy. Its alternate, ovate leaves are hairy and have toothed margins. The inflorescence is a dense spike of showy red flowers. Each flower has five petals and is pollinated by insects. The fruit is a capsule containing numerous small seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Denseflower Lousewort can be distinguished by its dense, clustered flowers and hairy stems. It has heart-shaped leaves with serrated edges and a distinctive reddish-brown stem. It differs from similar species like Pedicularis falcata by its more compact flower arrangement and shorter, more hairy inflorescence.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Pedicularis densiflora is native to the mountainous regions of western North America, including parts of British Columbia and the Pacific Northwest. It thrives in cool, moist climates and prefers well-drained, loamy soils. This species typically grows at elevations between 1,500 and 3,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Denseflower Lousewort blooms from June to August. Seed formation occurs from late summer into early fall. For identification purposes, the plant is best harvested in late summer when flowers are fully open and seeds are beginning to form.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Denseflower Lousewort belongs to the genus Pedicularis, closely related to species like Pedicularis lutea and Pedicularis falcata. It shares key traits with other members of the Orobanchaceae family, such as parasitic growth habits and specialized floral structures. Its phylogenetic position reflects evolutionary adaptations to its ecological niche.
