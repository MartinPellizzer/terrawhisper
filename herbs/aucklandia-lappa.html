Aucklandia lappa

What's the taxonomical classification of Aucklandia lappa?

Aucklandia lappa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further organized into the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Asterales. As a member of the family Asteraceae, it is specifically categorized within the genus Aucklandia, with its distinct biological identity defined by the species name lappa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Aucklandia lappa has large, leathery, elliptical leaves that measure up to 30 centimeters in length and feature prominent, parallel venation with slightly serrated margins. The tree typically develops a dense, spreading canopy supported by a sturdy, dark-colored trunk. Its small, yellowish-white flowers are clustered in axillary panicles and produce fleshy, globose fruits that transition from green to a deep purple or black when ripe. The bark is often smooth or slightly fissured depending on the age of the specimen. These morphological traits collectively define its appearance as a robust evergreen species.

What are the pharmacological activities of Aucklandia lappa?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antiulcer, and epigastric pain treatment pharmacological activities. These medicinal properties make it a valuable resource for managing gastrointestinal distress and systemic swelling. The plant works by mitigating the biological markers associated with inflammation throughout the body. Its ability to protect the gastric mucosa helps prevent the formation of ulcers in the stomach lining. Consequently, it serves as an effective natural remedy for alleviating intense epigastric pain.

As discussed in a study, alantolactone is a sesquiterpene lactone isolated from the root of Aucklandia lappa that demonstrates anti-inflammatory effects in LPS-stimulated RAW 264.7 cells and peritoneal macrophages. This component inhibits inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) and cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) protein and mRNA transcription, along with downstream products including nitric oxide (NO), prostaglandin E(2) (PGE(2)), and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α). The substance inhibits the phosphorylation of inhibitory κB (IκB)-α and IκB kinase (IKK), which prevents the translocation of p65 and p50 NF-κB subunits to the nucleus. It also inhibits mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs), specifically c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK), and p38 MAPK, as well as activator protein-1 (AP-1). Furthermore, alantolactone attenuates the phosphorylation of Akt and inhibits the expression of MyD88 and Toll-interleukin 1 receptor domain-containing adaptor protein (TIRAP).

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds beta-caryophyllene, costunoide, dehydrocostuslactone, alpha-caryophyllene, and elemene. These specific sesquiterpenes and lactones contribute to the complex chemical profile that defines the plant's biological activities. The presence of beta-caryophyllene and alpha-caryophyllene is often associated with significant anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, costunoide and dehydrocostuslactone are key constituents studied for their potential therapeutic roles in various pharmacological applications. The inclusion of elemene further enhances the diverse chemical makeup found within its tissues.

As discussed in a study, the essential oil from raw Radix Aucklandiae contains 29 identified compounds. Processing the plant using 4 different methods results in the identification of 33, 32, 34, and 33 compounds in the resulting essential oils. During these processes, certain components like alpha-phellandrene disappear, while others such as alpha-ionone, alpha-caryophyllene, beta-sesquiphellandrene, and alpha-longifolene come into being. Additionally, the contents of costunolide and dehydrocostuslactone in Radix Aucklandiae decrease when using methods 2, 3, and 4. The study notes that components such as elemene, dihydro-a-ionone, and beta-caryophyllene increase in content during processing.
