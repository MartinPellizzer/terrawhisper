Scientific and Botanical Profile

Turpethum, with botanical name Operculina turpethum, is a flowering plant native to tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Americas.

It belongs to the family Cucurbitaceae, commonly known as the gourd family, and is also referred to as the Indian Turpethum, Wild Cucumber, and Golden Apple. Morphologically, it is a climbing vine with heart-shaped leaves and small, yellow or greenish flowers that give way to orange to red, fleshy, berry-like fruits, often called Cape Gooseberry or Small-Flowered Turpethum.

The plant is also known by various common names such as Operculina Turpethum Plant, Operculina Plant, and Indian Cucumber, and it is valued for its ornamental and medicinal properties.

History and Cultural Relevance

Turpethum was used by ancient civilizations in India, China, and the Middle East for its medicinal properties, particularly for treating digestive ailments and skin conditions.

It also holds cultural significance in traditional rituals, where it was used in purification ceremonies and as an offering to deities during religious festivals. Today, Turpethum remains a key ingredient in herbal remedies, particularly in Ayurvedic medicine, where it is still used to treat digestive issues and support overall wellness.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Operculina turpethum has been traditionally used for its medicinal properties, with various forms available including fresh leaves, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsule.

To prepare the plant, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried leaves in hot water, or a decoction by boiling the dried material for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion for topical applications, such as applying the liquid to the skin for specific ailments. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of the dried powder per day, while for children, it should be used only under medical supervision and at a lower dose.

It is advised to use operculina turpethum sparingly, with a short duration of treatment to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Operculina turpethum can be used as a medicinal plant for its purported therapeutic properties, but it should be approached with caution due to its potential toxicity.

Side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort, nausea, vomiting, and in severe cases, liver damage or renal failure, particularly with prolonged or high-dose use. It may interact with medications such as anticoagulants, diuretics, and antihypertensives, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid its use due to insufficient safety data, and individuals with chronic illnesses like liver or kidney disease should consult a healthcare provider before use.

To ensure safety, it is advisable to use operculina turpethum only under medical supervision, start with low doses, and monitor for any adverse reactions.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Operculina turpethum grows in well-drained, sandy or loamy soil with a pH between 6.

0 and 7. 5, thriving in full sun to partial shade. It requires regular watering during the growing season, but the soil should not be waterlogged to prevent root rot. To ensure optimal growth, it benefits from occasional fertilization with a balanced organic fertilizer during the spring and summer months. Harvesting is best done in the late summer to early autumn when the leaves are fully developed and the roots are mature, using a spade or shovel to carefully extract the entire plant.

After harvesting, the roots should be thoroughly washed, sliced, and dried in a cool, shaded area or in a dehydrator at a low temperature to preserve potency, then stored in airtight containers in a cool, dry place or refrigerated to maintain its medicinal properties.
