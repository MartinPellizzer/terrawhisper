Cibotium barometz

What's the taxonomical classification of Cibotium barometz?

Cibotium barometz belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Polypodiinae, it falls under the order Cyatheales. Further taxonomic refinement places this plant in the family Cyatheaceae, within the genus Cibotium, and it is specifically identified by the species name barometz.

Its unique reproductive structures emerge from the rhizome and are often characterized by a distinctive, somewhat fleshy texture. The root system is well-developed to support the substantial weight and spread of its foliage in moist habitats. What are the pharmacological activities of Cibotium barometz?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, anti-osteoporosis, antioxidant, antiulcer, and gastroprotective properties. Its antioxidant capabilities help neutralize free radicals to reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. The plant also demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory effects by modulating immune responses to decrease swelling and pain. As discussed in a study, Cibotium barometz (L.) J. Sm. (Dicksoniaceae) has been traditionally used as anti-inflammatoroy and anodyne. Three unusual sesquiterpenes having 1-indanone nucleus (1, 3 and 4) and an unusual orthoester spiropyranosyl derivative of protocatechuic acid (2) were isolated from the rhizomes of Cibotium barometz. The permeation rates of 1, 3 and 4 increased linearly as a function of time up to 180 min and with the concentration within the test range of 25-200 microM. The apparent permeability coefficients (P(app)) of 1 was identical with those of propranolol, whereas the P(app) values of 3 and 4 were in agreement with those of atenolol. Compound 1 is assigned for a well-absorbed, and 2 and 3 are assigned for the poorly absorbed compounds in human intestine.

As discussed in a study, Cibotium barometz contains a total of 761 secondary metabolites. Phenolic acids and flavonoids account for over 50% of these identified compounds. The medicinal rhizome shows stage-specific shifts where phenolic acids enrich in juvenile stages, alkaloids in intermediate stages, and flavonoids in mature stages. Quantitative analysis confirmed higher flavonoid content in the mature stage compared to the juvenile stage. What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating gastric hemorrhagic abrasions, gastric ulcers, inflammation, inflammatory diseases, and oxidative stress. It acts as a potent medicinal agent specifically targeted toward repairing damage within the digestive tract. The substance helps mitigate the painful symptoms associated with various inflammatory diseases. Furthermore, its chemical properties are utilized to combat oxidative stress at a cellular level. This application assists in the healing process for patients suffering from severe gastric conditions.

As discussed in a study, Cibotium barometz is a pharmaceutical plant customarily used in traditional medicine in Malaysia for the treatment of diseases like gastric ulcer. The gastroprotective effect of Cibotium barometz leaves against ethanol-induced gastric hemorrhagic abrasions was evaluated in seven groups of Sprague Dawley rats. These groups included a normal control, an ulcerated control, omeprazole at 20 mg/kg, and Cibotium barometz at 62.5, 125, 250, and 500 mg/kg. Data demonstrated a significant decrease in the ulcer area of rats pretreated with the plant extract in a dose-dependent manner. Histology of the Cibotium barometz extract groups showed moderate-to-mild disruption of the surface epithelium with reduction in submucosal edema and leucocyte infiltration. Additionally, the extract showed heat shock protein 70 protein up-expression and BCL2-associated X protein down-expression.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be processed into a rare medicinal substance known as musk deer secretion, which is harvested from the scent gland of the male musk deer rather than being prepared from botanical parts. This preparation is highly valued in traditional Chinese medicine for its ability to promote blood circulation and resolve stagnation. Practitioners often use it in decoctions or powders to treat conditions such as abdominal pain, menstrual irregularities, and various circulatory disorders. Because the substance is animal-derived, its use is strictly regulated under international conservation laws. It is frequently combined with other potent herbs to enhance its warming and moving properties in complex formulas.
