Millettia versicolor

What's the taxonomical classification of Millettia versicolor?

Millettia versicolor belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Fabales. The plant is a member of the family Fabaceae, and its specific taxonomic hierarchy is completed by its placement in the genus Millettia and its designation as the species versicolor.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Millettia versicolor has pinnately compound leaves consisting of multiple pairs of ovate to elliptic leaflets with entire margins and an acute apex. The woody stems are characterized by a smooth bark texture that often transitions to a lighter greyish tone as the tree matures. Its inflorescences are small and axillary, producing fragrant, cream-colored flowers that emerge in clusters. The fruit is a woody, dehiscent capsule that typically contains several small, rounded seeds. This species often exhibits a spreading canopy with a dense arrangement of foliage.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, sandy or loamy soil and a location that receives full sun to thrive in tropical or subtropical climates.

To ensure healthy growth, it should be watered regularly during its establishment phase but allowed to dry slightly between applications to prevent root rot. Mulching around the base helps maintain consistent soil moisture and regulates temperature during warmer months. Pruning is also necessary to maintain its shape and encourage the development of its fragrant, pinkish-white blossoms.

Providing adequate airflow in its planting site will help minimize the risk of fungal diseases on its foliage.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Millettia versicolor leaves are used in traditional medicine for the treatment of malaria. The ethanolic and dichloromethane extracts of these leaves were investigated for in vitro antiplasmodial activity. The study found that these extracts were moderately active. Specifically, the activity for Millettia versicolor was measured at 10 microg/ml < IC(50) < 50 microg/ml. These results support the traditional use of the plant as an antimalarial agent.
