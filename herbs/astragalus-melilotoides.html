Astragalus melilotoides

What's the taxonomical classification of Astragalus melilotoides?

Astragalus melilotoides belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is positioned in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Fabales, the plant is a member of the family Fabaceae. It is further categorized into the genus Astragalus, and its specific designation is the species melilotoides.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Astragalus melilotoides contains medicinal properties primarily associated with its roots and seeds. The roots are often sought for their potential to support immune function and act as an adaptogen in traditional herbal practices. Some historical uses suggest that the plant's chemical constituents may provide various therapeutic benefits when processed correctly. However, caution is necessary because certain parts of the plant can become toxic if they develop certain fungal molds. Most modern research focuses on the specific alkaloids found within these organic structures.

    What are the herbal preparations of Astragalus melilotoides?

    Astragalus melilotoides is prepared as decoctions, infusions, tinctures, or dried herbal powders depending on whether the roots or aerial parts are being utilized. The woody roots are typically simmered in water for extended periods to extract dense polysaccharides and saponins. Leaves and stems can be steeped as a lighter tea to preserve more delicate volatile compounds. Tinctures made with alcohol serve as a concentrated method for long-term storage and standardized dosing. Many practitioners also incorporate the finely ground dried plant material directly into capsules for easier consumption.

    What side-effects this plant can have?

    Astragalus melilotoides can cause severe damage to the liver and central nervous system due to the presence of the toxic alkaloid coumarin which converts into dicoumarol. This chemical process occurs when the plant begins to decay or becomes moldy. Ingesting the plant can lead to significant blood thinning and uncontrolled internal bleeding. Symptoms of poisoning often include bruising, hemorrhage, and potential organ failure. Animals that graze on this plant are at the highest risk for these life-threatening complications.
