Zanthoxylum austrosinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Zanthoxylum austrosinense?

Zanthoxylum austrosinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Sapindales and the family Rutaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Zanthoxylum, and its unique species designation, austrosinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Zanthoxylum austrosinense has a woody structure characterized by pinnately compound leaves, small yellowish-green flowers, and small, globose drupes that turn red when ripe. The leaves typically consist of several pairs of leaflets with serrated margins and an acute apex. Its branches often exhibit fine, light-colored lenticels on the bark. The plant features a spreading growth habit with a relatively small stature compared to other species in its genus. Small, inconspicuous flowers are arranged in axillary panicles.

What are the pharmacological activities of Zanthoxylum austrosinense?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory and antiproliferative properties. Researchers have identified specific bioactive compounds within the plant that help reduce swelling and inhibit the production of inflammatory mediators. Furthermore, its antiproliferative effects suggest a potential role in suppressing the uncontrolled growth of certain abnormal cells. These medicinal qualities are primarily attributed to the presence of unique alkaloids and terpenoids found in its tissues. Continued studies aim to further isolate these constituents to develop targeted therapeutic applications.

According to a study published by "Bioorganic chemistry", twelve alkaloids, including three new carbazole alkaloids zanthoaustrones A-C (1-3) and nine known compounds 4-12, were isolated from the roots of Zanthoxylum austrosinense. All isolated alkaloids 1-12 displayed notable antiproliferative activities against diverse human cancer cell lines with IC50 values in the range of 0.85 ± 0.06 to 29.56 ± 0.17 µM. These alkaloids also exhibited pronounced inhibitory activities on nitric oxide (NO) production with IC50 values ranging from 0.89 ± 0.05 to 9.62 ± 0.15 µM. These findings suggest that the alkaloids from the roots of Z. austrosinense could be meaningful for the research and development of new anti-cancer drugs and anti-inflammatory agents.
