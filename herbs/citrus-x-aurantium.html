Citrus × aurantium

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurauntium belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Sapindales. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues into the family Rutaceae, where it is placed within the genus Citrus, eventually reaching its specific designation as the species aurauntium.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Citrus × aurauntium contains a complex array of bioactive compounds including hesperidin, naringin, polymethoxyflavones, and various essential oils like limonene. These flavonoids are primarily concentrated in the peel and pulp, providing significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of alkaloids and citric acid further contributes to the plant's diverse chemical profile. Many of these phytochemicals are studied for their ability to improve cardiovascular health and regulate metabolic processes. Additionally, the volatile oils found in the fruit serve as antimicrobial agents in traditional medicinal applications.

What are the therapeutic actions of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium has therapeutic actions primarily driven by its concentration of bioactive compounds like hesperidin, naringin, and synephrine that influence metabolic and cardiovascular functions. The presence of synephrine acts as a sympathomimetic agent that can increase metabolic rate and assist in weight management by stimulating thermogenesis. Its flavonoid content provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits by neutralizing free radicals in the body. Additionally, certain extracts are used to improve digestive health and support blood vessel integrity through improved circulation. These combined chemical properties allow the plant to serve various roles in nutritional supplementation and traditional medicine.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat digestive issues such as indigestion, constipation, bloating, and abdominal pain. The essential oils and flavonoids found in the fruit and peel are often employed to stimulate bile production and improve gastrointestinal motility. Some traditional practices also utilize its properties to assist with respiratory ailments like coughs and congestion. Additionally, the plant is sometimes used in topical applications to address minor skin irritations or fungal issues. These medicinal uses stem from the high concentration of bioactive compounds present in the citrus species.
