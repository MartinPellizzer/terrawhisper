What to know about Citrus × aurantium (bitter orange) before using it medicinally

Citrus × aurantium, commonly known as bitter orange, is a herb that boasts an array of health benefits, including antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties that can improve cardiovascular health, boost energy levels, and enhance digestion.

Cultivated primarily for its fragrant flowers and peel, which are used in perfumery and as a flavoring agent, Citrus × aurantium is a semi-tropical evergreen tree that thrives in warm and humid climates. The herb's botanical profile is characterized by its glossy green leaves, small white flowers, and edible peel, which contain high concentrations of limonene and other essential oils.

Historically, Citrus × aurantium has been a prized herb in traditional medicine, with references dating back to ancient China, where it was used to treat a range of ailments, including fever and indigestion.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Citrus × aurantium.

What are the medicinal properties of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium helps with weight loss, digestion, and as a diuretic. Its essential oil has been used to treat anxiety, insomnia, and nausea. It has also been used to reduce inflammation and pain.

The active constituents of Citrus × aurantium include synephrine, naringenin, and hesperidin. These compounds are responsible for its medicinal properties, including stimulating the nervous system and improving circulation. They also have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are the peel, seeds, and essential oil. The peel is often used to make teas, while the seeds are used to make tinctures. The essential oil is used topically for pain relief and as a flavoring agent.

Improper use of Citrus × aurantium can lead to side effects such as increased heart rate, high blood pressure, and insomnia. It can also interact with certain medications, including blood thinners and high blood pressure medications.

Precautions when using Citrus × aurantium medicinally include avoiding its use in pregnant or breastfeeding women, and individuals with heart conditions or high blood pressure. It is also recommended to consult a healthcare professional before using it as a diuretic or for weight loss.

What are the horticulural aspects of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium grow best in full sun to partial shade and well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. They require a warm and humid climate with average temperatures between 64°F and 90°F (18°C and 32°C). Watering is essential, with regular watering during the growing season.

Proper planting of Citrus × aurantium involves selecting a location with good air circulation and sufficient space for the plant to grow. Dig a hole 2-3 times the size of the root ball, and mix in a balanced fertilizer. Plant the tree at the same depth as it was previously, and water thoroughly. Mulching around the base helps retain moisture and suppress weeds.

Harvesting Citrus × aurantium fruit typically occurs between November and May, with peak production in January and February. Fruit is ready to pick when it is fully colored and has a slight softening to the touch. Avoid picking fruit too early, as it may not develop its full flavor. Regular harvesting encourages further production.

Common pests affecting Citrus × aurantium include aphids, whiteflies, and scales. Diseases such as citrus canker, root rot, and greasy spot can also occur. Regular inspection and treatment can prevent infestation and disease, with systemic pesticides and fungicides providing effective control. Pruning and hygiene practices can also help prevent pest and disease issues.

What are the botanical characteristics of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium, also known as bitter orange or Seville orange, is a hybrid citrus species that exhibits a combination of characteristics from its parents, sweet orange (Citrus sinensis) and pomelo (Citrus maxima).

Botanically, it is classified as a medium-sized evergreen tree that can grow up to 10-15 meters tall, with a broad, spreading canopy and dark green, elliptical leaves that are typically 6-12 centimeters long. The flowers of Citrus × aurantium are small, white, and highly fragrant, with five petals and numerous stamens, produced in clusters at the ends of branches. The fruit is a juicy, globular hesperidium, typically yellow-green when ripe, with a rough, pebbled surface and several segments or "wedges" that contain numerous oil glands.

The peel is thick and easy to remove, releasing a strong citrus fragrance, while the pulp is bitter and not suitable for human consumption, hence its name "bitter orange".

What is the taxonomy of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium belongs to the kingdom Plantae, class Magnoliopsida, order Sapindales, family Rutaceae, genus Citrus, and species Citrus × aurantium.

Specifically, it is a hybrid citrus species that is a cross between the pomelo (C. maxima) and the sour orange (C. reticulata).

This classification reflects its botanical relationship to other citrus fruits, including oranges, lemons, limes, and grapefruits.

What is the geographic distribution of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium, also known as sour orange, has a widespread geographic distribution across the tropical and subtropical regions of the world.

It is native to southern China and Southeast Asia, but it has been widely cultivated and naturalized in many parts of Africa, the Indian subcontinent, the Mediterranean region, Central America, the Caribbean, and South America.

The species thrives in warm and humid climates with average temperatures ranging from 15°C to 30°C (59°F to 86°F) and annual rainfall exceeding 600 mm (24 inches).

What is the life cycle of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium, also known as sour orange, is a hybrid citrus plant that can live for many years.

The life cycle of Citrus × aurantium begins with germination from seed, which typically occurs within 1-3 weeks after planting. Young trees require about 2-5 years to mature and produce flowers and fruit.

In optimal conditions, they can grow up to 10 meters (33 feet) tall and live for over 50 years. As the tree ages, its productivity may decline, but it can still thrive as a rootstock for other citrus varieties.

Citrus × aurantium is also often used as an ornamental plant or for landscaping purposes due to its attractive evergreen foliage and fragrant flowers.

What are the historical uses of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium, also known as bitter orange or Seville orange, has a rich history of use dating back to ancient China.

In traditional Chinese medicine, its peel was used to treat a variety of ailments, including fever and digestive issues. In the Mediterranean, it was used in cooking and as a flavoring agent for liqueurs and spirits.

What are the historical artifacts of Citrus × aurantium?

Citrus × aurantium, also known as bitter orange, has a rich history dating back thousands of years.

Archaeological discoveries have revealed ceramic vessels and coins from ancient civilizations in China, Egypt, and Greece, adorned with depictions of the fruit. The oldest recorded mention of citrus × aurantium dates back to 2200 BCE in Chinese texts.
