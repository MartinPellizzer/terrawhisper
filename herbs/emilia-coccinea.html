Emilia coccinea

What's the taxonomical classification of Emilia coccinea?

Emilia coccinea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and follows the order Asterales. This plant is a member of the family Asteraceae, which is one of the most prominent plant families, and is specifically categorized under the genus Emilia. Ultimately, its precise scientific identification is completed at the species level as Emilia coccinea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Emilia coccinea has erect, branched stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and are covered in fine, spreading hairs. The leaves are primarily basal and lanceolate in shape, while the upper stem leaves are smaller, narrower, and arranged oppositely. Its inflorescence consists of small, tubular flower heads that feature vibrant crimson or bright red corollas. These flower heads are clustered in terminal cymes, often appearing in dense groups at the tips of the branches. Each flower head is supported by a green involucre composed of several layers of bracts.

What are the pharmacological activities of Emilia coccinea?

This plant has antibacterial, antimicrobial, antidiarrhoeal, and hypoglycemic pharmacological activities that make it a significant subject in ethnopharmacology. Its antimicrobial properties allow it to effectively combat various pathogenic microorganisms. The plant also demonstrates strong antibacterial capabilities by inhibiting the growth of specific harmful bacteria. Research indicates that its antidiarrhoeal effects can help manage gastrointestinal distress. Furthermore, the hypoglycemic activity of Emilia coccinea suggests a potential role in regulating blood glucose levels.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extract of the Emilia coccinea whole plant was tested for antimicrobial activity against Salmonella typhi, S. paratyphi and S. typhimurium. The study utilized minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) and minimum bactericidal concentration (MBC) assays to evaluate these effects. While specific numerical ranges for this individual plant were not listed, the study concluded that extracts with MIC and MBC values of 1 mg/ml and lower may contain compounds with therapeutic activity. Salmonella paratyphi was found to be the most sensitive to formulations with a concentration range of 0.02 to 1 mg/ml in both MIC and MBC assays. In contrast, S. typhimurium was the least sensitive and required concentrations of up to 4 mg/ml to be bactericidal.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal alkaloids ligularidine, neoligularidine, neosenkirkine, platyphylline, and senkirkine. These specific chemical compounds are extracted from the plant's tissues for pharmacological study. Researchers analyze these alkaloids to understand their potential therapeutic applications in medicine. The presence of such a diverse array of nitrogenous substances makes the species a subject of biochemical interest. Each compound contributes to the unique chemical profile that defines the plant's biological activity.

As discussed in a study, the plant Emilia coccinea contains various types of pyrrolizidine alkaloids. The medicinal compounds identified in this species include platyphylline-N-oxide, ligularidine, neoligularidine, and neosenkirkine. Additionally, the study found platyphylline, which exists as three stereoisomers with the same MS(n) spectrum, along with the previously reported senkirkine. These findings were achieved using a newly elaborated RP-HPLC ion trap MS method with an atmospheric pressure chemical ionization interface.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause skin irritation, contact dermatitis, and localized swelling if the sap comes into direct contact with human tissue. Individuals with sensitive skin may experience redness or an itchy rash following accidental exposure. While there is limited research on internal toxicity, ingesting parts of the plant could potentially lead to gastrointestinal distress. It is always advisable to use gloves when handling this species to avoid these adverse physical reactions. Protective measures should be taken to prevent accidental contact with the eyes or mucous membranes.
