What to know about Rubus chamaemorus (cloudberry) before using it medicinally

Rubus chamaemorus, commonly known as cloudberry, is a rich source of antioxidants and anthocyanins that have been shown to have anti-inflammatory and anti-cancer properties, improving overall health and well-being.

This herb is relatively low-maintenance and can thrive in poor soil and cold climates, making it a popular choice for gardeners in northern latitudes. Botanically, Rubus chamaemorus is a member of the rose family (Rosaceae) and is characterized by its small white flowers and edible red berries.

In traditional medicine, cloudberry has been used for centuries in Scandinavia and Russia to treat a range of ailments, including digestive issues and respiratory problems.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Rubus chamaemorus.

What are the medicinal properties of Rubus chamaemorus?

Rubus chamaemorus helps with digestive issues, particularly constipation and diarrhea. It has been traditionally used to treat these ailments due to its high content of dietary fiber and tannins. Additionally, it has been used to treat inflammatory conditions such as arthritis.

The active constituents of Rubus chamaemorus include anthocyanins, ellagic acid, and gallic acid, which give the plant its medicinal properties. These compounds have been shown to have anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects. Ellagic acid, in particular, has been found to inhibit cancer cell growth and proliferation.

The parts of the Rubus chamaemorus plant most used for medicinal purposes are the fruit, leaves, and roots. The fruit, also known as the cloudberry, is a rich source of dietary fiber, vitamins, and minerals. The leaves and roots contain higher concentrations of tannins and anthocyanins, which give the plant its medicinal properties.

Improper use of Rubus chamaemorus can cause gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. The plant's high content of tannins can also cause allergic reactions in some individuals. Excessive consumption of the plant's fruit or leaves can lead to an overload of anthocyanins, which can cause kidney and liver damage.

Precautions when using Rubus chamaemorus medicinally include consulting with a healthcare professional before use, especially for pregnant or breastfeeding women. Additionally, individuals with kidney or liver disease should avoid consuming the plant's leaves or roots, as they can exacerbate existing conditions. Doses above 1 gram per day can cause adverse effects, so it is essential to follow recommended dosages.

What are the horticulural aspects of Rubus chamaemorus?

Rubus chamaemorus grow in wet acidic soils, typically in bogs and marshy areas. It thrives in environments with full sun to partial shade, but is adaptable to some shade. The ideal temperature range is between 2-10°C.

For successful planting, seeds or suckers are best planted in spring or fall, 1-2 cm deep, and 30-60 cm apart. Roots should be 10-15 cm deep to encourage strong growth. Water thoroughly and maintain moist soil until established. Mulch around plants to retain moisture.

Harvesting typically occurs in mid to late summer when fruits are ripe and red. Select stems with bright red or purple berries and gently twist them to avoid damaging the plant. Harvest about one-third of the available fruit to maintain plant health and encourage continued production.

Common pests of Rubus chamaemorus include the aphid and spider mite. Diseases affecting the plant include leaf spot (Mycosphaerella ruborum) and root rot (Phytophthora cactorum). Regularly inspect plants for signs of infestation or disease and take appropriate action to prevent their spread.

What are the botanical characteristics of Rubus chamaemorus?

Rubus chamaemorus, also known as cloudberry, is a perennial shrub belonging to the Rosaceae family.

Its botanical characteristics include a sprawling habit, forming dense mats or cushions up to 60 cm in diameter, with stems that are hairy and glandular. The leaves are trifoliate, meaning they have three leaflets, which are dark green on top and pale green on the bottom, measuring approximately 4-12 cm long and 3-10 cm wide.

Each leaflet has a pointed tip and a serrated margin. The flowers are produced in late spring to early summer, occurring in clusters of 2-5, with each flower consisting of five petals that are white or pale yellow, measuring around 1-2 cm in diameter. The fruits, also known as "cloudberries", are aggregate berries, typically yellow-orange in color, ellipsoidal in shape, and measuring approximately 10-20 mm long.

They contain many seeds and are edible, but often have a slightly tart taste due to their low sugar content.

What is the life cycle of Rubus chamaemorus?

Rubus chamaemorus, also known as cloudberry, is a perennial species that exhibits a unique life cycle.

The plant begins its life cycle by producing runners, which are above-ground stems that produce new shoots and roots at their nodes. These shoots grow into new plants, while the original parent plant continues to produce fruit and flowers.

As the plants mature, they produce stolons, which are underground stems that produce new roots and shoots, allowing the cloudberry to spread and form large colonies. Cloudberry flowers typically bloom in June or July, followed by the production of bright orange-red berries in August and September. The plant dies back during the winter months, only to regrow from its underground stolons in the spring.

This life cycle allows Rubus chamaemorus to thrive in its alpine and arctic habitats.
