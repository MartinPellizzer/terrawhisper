Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Cloudberry (Rubus Chamaemorus)?

Cloudberry, or Rubus chamaemorus, is a low-growing perennial shrub native to Arctic and subarctic regions, known for its small, tart berries used in traditional herbal medicine for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Cloudberry, scientifically known as Rubus chamaemorus, is a low-growing evergreen shrub belonging to the Rosaceae family, characterized by its edible, red, berry-like fruits that ripen in late summer.

Common Names and Synonyms

Cloudberry, also known as red raspberry, is a low-growing shrub native to Arctic and alpine regions, and is commonly referred to by various names such as crowberry, false strawberry, and red whortleberry in different regions and contexts.

Plant Description and Morphology

Cloudberry, or Rubus chamaemorus, is a low-growing perennial shrub characterized by its prostrate or ascending stems, trifoliate leaves with serrated margins, and small, white to pinkish flowers that bloom in late spring to early summer.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is native to the circumpolar regions of the Northern Hemisphere, including Arctic and subarctic areas of Europe, Asia, and North America, where it thrives in acidic, nutrient-poor soils and cold climates.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, roots, and berries of Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) are used medicinally, with the berries being the most commonly utilized part for their high content of antioxidants, vitamins, and anti-inflammatory compounds.

Phytochemical Composition

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids such as quercetin and kaempferol, proanthocyanidins, carotenoids like beta-carotene and lutein, and organic acids such as ellagic acid and gallic acid, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) contains bioactive compounds such as proanthocyanidins, flavonoids, and anthocyanins, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties, supporting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Mechanisms of Action

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) exerts its effects primarily through the presence of bioactive compounds such as anthocyanins, ellagic acid, and flavonoids, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and immune-modulating properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Cloudberry is used to treat urinary tract infections, digestive issues, respiratory infections, skin conditions, and as a general tonic to support overall health.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is typically prepared as a dried herb, used in teas, tinctures, and topical applications, with the berries often harvested and processed to retain their active compounds.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) for herbal use typically ranges from 200 to 500 milligrams per day, taken in divided doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic effect and product concentration.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is generally considered safe when consumed in moderate amounts, excessive use may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, due to its high tannin content.

Drug Interactions

Cloudberry may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting blood clotting, blood sugar levels, or the liver, and it is important to consult a healthcare professional before using it concurrently with other drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is generally considered safe when consumed in typical food amounts, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals, and its safety during pregnancy, lactation, and for children has not been fully established.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is typically cultivated in cool, moist environments with well-drained, acidic soil, often in regions with long summers and short, cold winters.

Processing and Extraction

Cloudberry is typically processed by drying the berries or using them fresh in various herbal preparations, often in tinctures, decoctions, or as a dietary supplement to harness its medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, and when dried, it can maintain its potency for up to two years if kept in an airtight container.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) has primarily focused on its potential antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties, with studies suggesting its bioactive compounds may support immune function and skin health.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Cloudberry, or Rubus chamaemorus, has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to support kidney function, reduce inflammation, and aid in the treatment of urinary tract infections.

Regulatory Status

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is not approved as a medicinal herb by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and is not listed in the United States Pharmacopeia (USP) or the National Formulary (NF), meaning it is not officially recognized as a standardized medicinal substance in the United States.
