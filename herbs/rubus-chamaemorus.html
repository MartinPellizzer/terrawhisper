Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Cloudberry (Rubus Chamaemorus)?

Cloudberry, or Rubus chamaemorus, is a low-growing evergreen shrub native to the Arctic and subarctic regions, known for its small, red, edible berries that are rich in antioxidants and used in traditional herbal medicine for their anti-inflammatory and immune-boosting properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Cloudberry is Rubus chamaemorus. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Rosales, family Rosaceae, genus Rubus, and species Rubus chamaemorus. It is commonly known as cloudberry in North America and as sheep's sorrel in Europe. It is also known as cranberry in parts of Scandinavia. Synonyms for this plant include Rubus tormentillifolius (botanical) and names like "sámi" in Sámi languages (regional).

Plant Description and Morphology

Cloudberry is a low-growing perennial plant that typically reaches a height of 10-30 cm, with a sprawling or upright growth habit depending on environmental conditions.

Its leaves are oval to elliptical, with a smooth margin and a pointed tip, arranged alternately along the stems. The flowers are small, five-petaled, and white to pale pink, appearing in clusters of 3-7 during late spring to early summer. The root system consists of a network of fibrous roots and rhizomes, which help anchor the plant and store nutrients.

The fruit is a red, juicy berry with a distinctive sweet-tart flavor, covered in a thin, leathery skin that becomes softer as it ripens.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is native to the northern hemisphere, primarily found in Arctic and subarctic regions such as Scandinavia, Russia, northern Canada, and Alaska, growing in wet, acidic, and often peat-rich environments.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, and berries of Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) are used medicinally, with the berries being the most commonly utilized part for their nutritional and therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) contains a variety of bioactive compounds, including anthocyanins, flavonoids, proanthocyanidins, and phenolic acids, which contribute to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) contains bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, anthocyanins, and phenolic acids, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and potential antimicrobial properties that contribute to its use in traditional herbal medicine.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Cloudberry, or Rubus chamaemorus, has been traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat urinary tract infections, kidney stones, and digestive issues due to its diuretic, anti-inflammatory, and antiseptic properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or powder, with the fruit being dried and then used in various formulations to maximize its medicinal properties.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) for herbal use ranges from 1 to 3 grams of dried root or leaf per day, though specific preparations may vary depending on the form and intended use.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is generally considered safe when consumed in typical food amounts, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interact with certain medications when used in higher doses or as a medicinal supplement.

Drug Interactions

Cloudberry may interact with medications that affect the liver or blood clotting, and it is important to consult a healthcare provider before using it if you are on any prescription drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is generally considered safe when consumed in typical food amounts, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort in some individuals, and there is limited research on its safety during pregnancy, breastfeeding, or in long-term therapeutic use.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) is typically cultivated in cool, moist environments with acidic soil, often in boreal or subarctic regions, and is harvested by hand during its short blooming season, usually between June and August, when the berries are fully ripe and most flavorful.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) typically involve harvesting the ripe berries, followed by mechanical pressing or cold maceration to release the bioactive compounds, with further purification through filtration and solvent-based extraction methods to concentrate the beneficial phytochemicals such as anthocyanins and flavonoids.

Storage and Shelf Life

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight, ideally in an airtight container to maintain its potency and prevent moisture damage, with a shelf life of up to 12 months when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus) has primarily focused on its antioxidant properties, anti-inflammatory effects, and potential role in supporting cardiovascular and metabolic health, with some studies suggesting its compounds may contribute to skin protection and immune modulation.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Cloudberry, or Rubus chamaemorus, has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its purported ability to support urinary tract health and as a diuretic, with historical records indicating its use by indigenous Arctic peoples and later by European herbalists for similar purposes.

Regulatory Status
