What's the taxonomical classification of Anoectochilus formosanus?

Anoectochilus formosanus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving through its hierarchical lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, falling under the order Asparagales. As a member of the family Orchidaceae, it is specifically categorized under the genus Anoectochilus, with its unique biological identity defined by its species name, formosanus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Anoectochilus formosanus has small, fleshy, elliptical leaves that are arranged in a distichous pattern along a creeping rhizome. The inflorescence consists of a raceme bearing several small, fragrant flowers characterized by a prominent, trilobed lip. This lip features a distinctively thickened, fleshy callus that extends toward the column. The petals and sepals are typically greenish or yellowish with subtle reddish markings. These morphological traits distinguish this miniature epiphytic orchid from similar species in its genus.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment of various types of cancer, specifically demonstrating inhibitory effects against CT-26 murine colon cancer cells. Research into its bioactive compounds suggests potential efficacy in combating various forms of human cancers. Scientists study its chemical constituents to understand how they might suppress tumor growth and cell proliferation. These investigations aim to develop new therapeutic strategies for managing complex oncological conditions. Ongoing studies continue to evaluate the specific mechanisms by which its extracts target malignant cells.

As discussed in a study, the water extract of Anoectochilus formosanus Hayata showed potent tumor inhibitory activity in BALB/c mice with CT-26 murine colon cancer cells. Mice pre-administered with the extract for 2 days before transplantation and treated for 12 consecutive days showed tumor-inhibition ratios of 55.4% at an oral dose of 50 mg/mouse per day and 58.9% at 10 mg/mouse per day. For tumor-bearing mice treated for 12 consecutive days, the tumor inhibition ratios were 23.8% at 50 mg/mouse and 40.5% at 10 mg/mouse. Since the low-concentration water extract of Anoectochilus formosanus does not show direct cytotoxicity in CT-26 tumor cells, its activity may be associated with stimulating lymphoid tissue proliferation and activating phagocytosis of peritoneal macrophages.
