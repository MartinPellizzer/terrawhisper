Botanical Identification of Cumin (Cuminum cyminum)

To identify Cumin (Cuminum cyminum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Cumin (Cuminum cyminum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Cumin, or Cuminum cyminum, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically within the division of Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Cuminum, and species Cuminum cyminum. This classification places it within the carrot family, known for its aromatic herbs and spices.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Cuminum cyminum is the scientific name for cumin, a member of the Apiaceae family. It was first described by the botanist Carl von Linné in 1753. The plant is also known by various synonyms, including Cuminum sativum and Cuminum arabicum, reflecting its historical use and regional variations.

Morphological Characteristics

Cumin is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, slender stems that grow up to 50 cm tall. The leaves are pinnately compound, with narrow, lance-shaped leaflets. The inflorescence is a branched umbel, bearing small white to pale green flowers. The fruit is a globular, three-lobed seed enclosed in a thin husk.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cuminum cyminum can be distinguished by its slender, ridged stems, narrow lanceolate leaves, and distinctive yellow flowers in umbels. The fruit is a slender, bumpy achene with a longitudinal groove. It differs from similar species like Carum carvi by its smaller, more pointed fruits and lack of hairy leaves.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cumin is native to the Mediterranean region, including parts of southern Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. It thrives in dry, warm climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It is typically found at altitudes between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cumin plants typically bloom in late summer to early autumn, around 60-90 days after germination. Seed formation occurs shortly after flowering, usually in late summer. Harvest timing for seeds is critical, as they are ready when the stems turn brown and the seeds are fully developed, usually 3-4 weeks after flowering.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cumin belongs to the genus Cuminum in the family Apiaceae. Its closest relative is the black cumin, Carum carvi. Within the genus, Cuminum shares morphological and genetic traits with other members like Foeniculum vulgare. These relationships help in understanding its ecological and medicinal significance.
