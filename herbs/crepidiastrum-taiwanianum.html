Crepidiastrum taiwanianum

What's the taxonomical classification of Crepidiastrum taiwanianum?

Crepidiastrum taiwanianum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Asterales. This plant is a representative of the family Asteraceae, specifically situated within the genus Crepidiastrum, with its unique designation being the species taiwananium.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions, skin infections, and gastrointestinal ailments through its traditional medicinal properties. Local healers often utilize its leaves to address skin irritations and topical wounds by applying crushed plant matter directly to the affected area. It is also frequently employed in herbal formulations to mitigate symptoms of stomach discomfort and internal heat. Some practitioners believe the plant possesses antimicrobial qualities that assist in fighting minor bacterial imbalances. These applications reflect the plant's long-standing role in regional folk medicine for managing diverse physical discomforts.

What are the herbal preparations of Crepidiastrum taiwanianum?

Crepidiastrum taiwanianum is prepared primarily as a decoction through boiling the dried plant material in water to extract its medicinal compounds. This liquid extract is then consumed orally to harness the plant's traditional therapeutic properties. In some applications, the plant parts may also be processed into a fine powder for easier administration. This powdered form can be mixed with warm water or other liquids to facilitate ingestion. Depending on the specific traditional use, the plant may occasionally be used in topical applications such as poultices.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Crepidiastrum taiwanianum can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis or localized rashes upon direct physical contact with its foliage. While extensive clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, individuals with sensitive skin may experience itching or redness after handling the plant. There is also a potential risk of gastrointestinal distress if any part of the specimen is accidentally ingested. It is advisable to use gloves when pruning or managing this species to prevent unexpected irritation. Always consult a medical professional if symptoms persist after exposure.
