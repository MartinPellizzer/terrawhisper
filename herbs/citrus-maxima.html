Citrus maxima

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrus maxima?

Citrus maxima belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is further categorized under the order Sapindales. The plant is a member of the family Rutaecae, which places it within the genus Citrus, and its specific identity is defined by the species name maxima.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Citrus maxima has large, evergreen leaves that are elliptical to oblong in shape, possess leathery textures, and feature winged petioles. The tree produces white, fragrant flowers that grow in small clusters from the axils of the branches. Its fruit is characterized by a thick, pithy rind and large segments filled with juicy vesicles. The woody stems are often equipped with sharp spines to protect the developing foliage and fruit. Mature trees can reach significant heights, developing a broad and dense canopy over time.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to Southeast Asia, specifically within the tropical and subtropical regions of countries like China, Malaysia, and Indonesia. While it originated in these humid environments, it has been widely introduced to other warm climates around the world. Cultivation is now common in many tropical regions including parts of India, Thailand, and even parts of the Americas. The species thrives in areas characterized by high rainfall and consistent warmth. Today, its distribution is largely driven by human agricultural practices rather than natural dispersal alone.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Citrus maxima contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its peel, juice, and leaves. The thick rind is often used in traditional remedies to treat skin inflammation and digestive issues due to its high concentration of essential oils. Its juice provides a significant source of vitamin C, which supports immune function and helps prevent scurvy. Additionally, the leaves are sometimes utilized in herbal teas to help reduce fever or soothe stomach discomfort. These various components work together to provide a wide range of therapeutic benefits in different medicinal applications.

What are the pharmacological activities of Citrus maxima?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidative, anticancer, antimicrobial, and cytotoxic activities derived from its rich concentration of bioactive compounds. The presence of flavonoids and polyphenols allows it to neutralize free radicals and reduce systemic inflammation. Its antimicrobial properties make it effective against various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant cytotoxic activity that can inhibit the growth of malignant cells. These combined pharmacological effects highlight its immense therapeutic potential in modern medicine.

As discussed in a study, the honey made from the floral source Citrus maxima was evaluated for its antioxidant, antibacterial, and anti-inflamatory activities. This specific honey was compared against other sources such as B. pilosa, Dimocarpus longan, and Litchi chinensis. While the study noted that B. pilosa honey had significantly higher total phenolic and flavonoid contents, the Citrus maxima honey was included in the assessment of scavenging activities for 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH·) and hydroxyl radical. Furthermore, the research examined whether the honey from Citrus maxima showed inhibitory activity against IL-8 secretion. The findings suggest that the antioxidant and antibacterial activities of the honey are attributable to phenolic and flavonoid contents, whereas IL-8 inhibition stems from other components.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds naringin, bergamottin, bergaptol, naringenin, and kaempferol. These specific flavonoids and furanocoumarins provide the plant with various therapeutic properties, including antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Narigin and naringenin serve as important bioactive molecules that contribute to the plant's overall chemical profile. The presence of bergamottin and bergaptol can influence metabolic processes and skin sensitivity. Additionally, the flavonoid kaempferol offers significant cellular protection through its ability to combat oxidative stress.

As discussed in a study, the shadock (Citrus maxima) peel contains phenolic compounds such as quercetin, caffeic acid, kaempferol, catechin and naringin. These extracts were evaluated for their effect on matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) and proteasome activities in primary human colonic tumor (Caco-2) and metastatic cell lines (LoVo and LoVo/ADR). The shaddock peel extracts showed the least MMP inhibition in Caco-2 and LoVo cells compared to other citrus peels. Furthermore, shaddock had the least MMP inhibition in LoVo/ADR lysates. There was no significant (p>0.05) difference in the proteasome inhibition of the peel extracts in LoVo/ADR lysates.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an essential oil extracted from the rind, a fresh juice from the pulp, an infusion made from the leaves, a tincture derived from the peel, or a concentrated extract. The essential oil provides a potent aromatic profile often used in aromatherapy and topical applications. Fresh juice serves as a nutritious liquid source rich in vitamin C. Herbalists often create a soothing infusion by steeping the leaves in hot water. A tincture uses alcohol to preserve the bioactive compounds found in the citrus peel. Finally, a concentrated extract offers a potent method for delivering the plant's medicinal properties.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, diarrhea, stomach upset, and allergic reactions, while also triggering significant drug interactions with various medications. Consuming large amounts of the fruit may lead to digestive distress such as nausea or an upset stomach. Some individuals might experience diarrhea following consumption or develop an allergic reaction ranging from mild skin irritation to more severe symptoms. Furthermore, the compounds in the fruit can interfere with how the body processes certain drugs, potentially leading to dangerous side effects. Users should remain cautious when combining this citrus with prescribed medical treatments.
