Calanthe graciliflora

What's the taxonomical classification of Calanthe graciliflora?

Calanthe graciliflora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the taxonomic hierarchy further, it is placed in the order Asparagales and the family Orchidaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Calanthe, and its unique species designation, graciliflora.

What are the therapeutic actions of Calanthe graciliflora?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, inflammation, and various digestive ailments. Its chemical properties allow it to combat bacterial pathogens that cause topical irritation. Local communities often apply crushed leaves to wounds to promote faster healing and reduce swelling. Additionally, decoctions made from the plant are consumed to alleviate stomach cramps and intestinal distress. These medicinal applications highlight the plant's role in traditional herbal remedies.

What are the herbal preparations of Calanthe graciliflora?

Calanthe graciliflora is prepared primarily as a concentrated aqueous decoction or a distilled tincture extracted from its rhizomes and aerial parts. These liquid preparations are often administered in small, measured doses to harness the plant's specific bioactive compounds. Some practitioners also utilize the dried, powdered form of the plant to create topical poultices for external applications. Additionally, the plant material can be infused into botanical oils to serve as a base for medicinal salves. These various methods allow for the different delivery of its chemical constituents depending on the intended therapeutic use.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Calanthe graciliflora can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or mild gastrointestinal distress if ingested. While primarily an ornamental orchid, contact with its sap or foliage may trigger redness and itching in sensitive individuals. Some people might experience swelling or a localized rash upon touching the plant surfaces. There is also a risk of nausea or stomach discomfort if any part of the specimen is consumed accidentally. It is advisable to handle the plant with care to avoid these potential reactions.
