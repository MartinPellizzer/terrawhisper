Sapindus rarak

What's the taxonomical classification of Sapindus rarak?

Sapindus rarak belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Further taxonomic refinement places the plant in the order Sapindales and the family Sapindaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Sapindus, and its unique species name, rarak.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Sapindus rarak has pinnately compound leaves that are typically composed of 5 to 15 leaflets, small fleshy fruits, and a woody trunk. The leaflets are arranged oppositely along the rachis and possess an elliptical to oblong shape with entire margins. Its small, globose fruits transition from green to a bright red or orange color when they reach maturity. These fruits contain a single seed enclosed within a thin, pulpy layer. The tree exhibits a spreading crown with bark that becomes textured and fissured as the plant ages.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically occurring in countries such as Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, and parts of the Philippines. It typically thrives in lowland rainforest environments where high humidity and consistent warmth are present. Within these ecosystems, the species is often found growing in well-drained soils along riverbanks or within secondary forest growth. Its distribution is closely tied to the seasonal monsoon patterns that characterize the maritime climate of the Indo-Malayan archipelago. Consequently, the plant is restricted to specific latitudes that support its requirement for a frost-free habitat.

What are the pharmacological activities of Sapindus rarak?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antiprotozoal, and antimalarial properties. Research indicates that the bioactive compounds within Sapindus rarak effectively neutralize free radicals to reduce oxidative stress in biological systems. Its chemical constituents also demonstrate the ability to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Furthermore, the plant shows significant potential in combating parasitic infections through its specific antiprotozoal and antimalarial actions. These combined therapeutic effects make it a subject of great interest for developing new medicinal treatments.

As discussed in a study, the ethanolic extracts of Sapinduus rarak DC. exhibited potent antimalarial activity against Plasmodium falciparum K1. This specific activity was measured using the lactate dehydrogenase method and resulted in a half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) value of 2.46 μg/ml. The study also involved analyzing aqueous and ethanolic extracts of the plant for their phytochemical constituents. These investigations were part of an evaluation that included cytotoxicity testing in Vero cells using the MTT assay.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including oleanane-type triterpene oligoglycosides, rarasaponin I, rarasaponin II, rarasaponins I-III, and theasaponin E. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their unique biological properties and therapeutic potential. The presence of various rarasaponin isomers highlights the complex phytochemical profile of the species. Furthermore, the oleanane-type triterpene oligoglycosides contribute to the overall pharmacological significance of the plant. Research into these individual molecules helps scientists understand how the plant interacts with biological systems.

As discussed in a study, the methanolic extract from the pericarps of Sapindus rarak DC. showed pancreatic lipase inhibitory activity with an IC50 of ca. 614 microg/mL. From this extract, oleanane-type triterpene oligoglycosides, including rarasaponins I-III (1-3) and raraoside A (4), were isolated alongside 13 known saponins and four known sesquiterpene glycosides. Several saponin constituents inhibited pancreatic lipase activity, specifically rarasaponin I (1, IC50=131microM), rarasaponin II (2, 172microM), and raraoside A (4, 151microM). These compounds demonstrated stronger inhibition than theasaponin E(1), which had an IC50 of 270microM.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and gastric irritation due to the presence of toxic saponins. These chemical compounds act as irritants when ingested, leading to significant gastrointestinal distress. The toxicity from saponins often results in severe discomfort throughout the digestive tract. Many individuals who consume the plant experience acute symptoms like stomach cramping and frequent bowel movements. Such reactions highlight the dangerous nature of the plant if it is consumed improperly.
