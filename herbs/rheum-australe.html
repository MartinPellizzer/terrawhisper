Rheum australe

What's the taxonomical classification of Rheum australe?

Rheum australe belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage leading to the order Caryophyllales. This plant is a representative of the family Polygonaceae, situated within the genus Rheum, and is specifically identified by its species name, australe.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Rheum australe contains high concentrations of anthraquinone glycosides, such as emodin and rhein, which function as potent stimulant laxatives. These specific chemical compounds work by increasing intestinal motility and stimulating the secretion of water into the colon. In addition to these glycosides, the plant contains various polyphenols and tannins that provide antioxidant and antimicrobial properties. These secondary metabolites contribute to the plant's overall pharmacological profile in traditional medicine. The synergy between these diverse compounds allows the plant to be used for treating digestive ailments and inflammatory conditions.

What are the therapeutic actions of Rheum australe?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat digestive ailments such as constipation, stomach aches, and general dysentery. The rhizomes contain anthraquinones which act as potent laxatives to stimulate bowel movements. It is also applied topically to manage skin irritations and minor wounds due to its astringent properties. Traditional practitioners often utilize the plant to reduce inflammation within the gastrointestinal tract. Careful dosing is required because excessive consumption can lead to severe abdominal cramping.

What are the herbal preparations of Rheum australe?

Rheum australe is prepared primarily as a decoction of the dried rhizomes and roots to extract its medicinal anthraquinone compounds. The plant material is typically boiled in water for an extended period to ensure the active constituents are fully released into the liquid. Once the decoction is strained, it is often administered in small, controlled doses to manage digestive issues. Some practitioners also utilize the dried, powdered root to create tinctures using alcohol as a solvent for greater potency. These various preparations allow for the specific targeting of gastrointestinal functions through different methods of extraction.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Rheum australe can cause gastrointestinal irritation, abdominal cramping, diarrhea, and potential electrolyte imbalances due to its anthraquinone content. These compounds act as stimulant laxatives, which may lead to dehydration if consumed in large quantities. Prolonged or excessive use can result in dependency or damage to the intestinal lining. Some individuals might also experience localized skin irritation if the plant comes into direct contact with sensitive areas. It is essential to monitor dosage to avoid these adverse physiological reactions.
