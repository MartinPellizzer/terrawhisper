Macleaya microcarpa

What's the taxonomical classification of Macleaya microcarpa?

Macleaya microcarpa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy places it under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, following the order Ranunculales. As a member of the family Papaveraceae, it is specifically identified by the genus Macleaya, with its unique biological classification completed by the species epithet microcarppa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Macleaya microcarpa has a rosette-forming habit characterized by large, pinnately lobed leaves and small, globose fruits. The leaves are deeply divided into several segments and possess a somewhat coarse, textured surface. Its inflorescences consist of small clusters that eventually develop into tiny, berry-like fruits. These fruits are notably smaller than those of related species in the genus. The overall structure of the plant is robust and adapted to its specific environmental niche.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse group of isoquinoline alkaloids, specifically chelidonine, sanguinarine, berberine, and chelerythrine. These potent medicinal compounds are often found in high concentrations within the plant's tissues. Sanguinarine and chelerythrine are particularly noted for their significant biological activities. Berberine also contributes to the plant's complex chemical profile and therapeutic potential. Together, these alkaloids form the basis of the plant's traditional medicinal uses.

As discussed in a study, Macleaya microcarpa contains major alkaloids such as berbine, sanguinarine, and chelidonine. The plant also provides the components for the drug Sanguirythrine, which is a mixture of the alkaloids sanguinarine and chelerythrine in a weight-to-weight ratio of 3:7. These substances act as irreversible inhibitors of the oxidative deamination reaction of serotonin and tyramine catalyzed by rat liver mitochondrial monoamine oxidase. At a concentration of 1 mM or less, these substances do not influence the oxidative deamination reaction of benzylamine as a substrate. Sanguinarine and Sanguirythrine exhibit similar values of bimolecular rate constants during their interaction with mitochondrial MAO.
