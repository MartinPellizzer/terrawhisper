Corydalis turtschaninovii

What's the taxonomical classification of Corydalis turtschaninovii?

Corydalis turtschaninovii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Ranunculales. This plant is a member of the Papaveraceae family, specifically falling within the genus Corydalis, and is identified at the species level as turtschaninovii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Corydalis turtschaninovii has deeply lobed, pinnatisect leaves that are typically bright green and arranged in a basal rosette or along a slender stem. The plant produces nodding, zygomorphic flowers that exhibit a range of colors from pale yellow to soft violet. Each blossom features a long, nectar-bearing spur extending from the rear of the corolla. The stem is relatively delicate and supports a few small, spreading leaves alongside the inflorescence. Small, elongated seed capsules develop following pollination to facilitate the dispersal of its progeny.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compound Canadine, which is a sesquiterpene found within its chemical profile. This specific substance is often studied for its potential biological activities in various therapeutic applications. Beyond Canadine, the plant hosts a diverse array of secondary metabolites that contribute to its overall pharmacological properties. These various compounds work together to produce the unique medicinal effects observed in the species. Researchers continue to investigate how these individual elements interact to support human health.

As discussed in a study, researchers isolated 6 alkaloids from Corydalis turtschaninovii to evaluate their myogenic potential. Among these tested compounds, canadine demonstrated the strongest transactivation of MyoD and increased MHC expression during myogenesis. The activation of p38 MAP kinase and Akt serve as major mechanisms by which canadine contributes to myogenesis. This compound increased the number of multinucleated and cylinder-shaped myotubes during the myogenesis of C2C12 myoblasts. Additionally, canadine ameliorated muscle protein degradation caused by CT26-CM by down-regulating the muscle specific-E3 ligases, MAFbx/atrogin-1 and MuRF1.
