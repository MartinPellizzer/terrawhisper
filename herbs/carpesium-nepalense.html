Carpesium nepalense

What's the taxonomical classification of Carpesium nepalense?

Carpesium nepalense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the taxonomic lineage of the order Asterales. This plant is further categorized under the family Asteraceae, a large group of flowering plants, and is specifically identified by the genus Carpesium and the species nepalense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

What are the pharmacological activities of Carpesium nepalense?

This plant has antibacterial, anticancer, and antioxidant pharmacological activities that stem from its diverse bioactive compounds. The antioxidant properties allow it to neutralize harmful free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within biological systems. Its antibacterial efficacy demonstrates a significant capacity to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, the plant exhibits anticancer potential by interfering with the proliferation of malignant cell lines. These multifaceted medicinal properties highlight its importance in pharmacological research and drug development.

According to a study published by "Pakistan journal of pharmaceuticaal sciences", the n-hexane fraction of Carpesium nepalense seeds essential oil showed highly significant (P<0.001) antioxidant activity at a concentration of 500μg/mL. This antioxidant effect resulted in percentage inhibitions of 86.60±1.6%, 82.55±1.0%, and 80.50±1.0% in the 2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl, 2,2'-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid, and superoxide assays. The extract also produced highly significant anticancerous activity against different cell lines at 500μg/mL. Furthermore, significant antibacterial activity was observed with zones of inhibition measuring 24.3±0.8, 28.20±0.10, 22.33±0.11, and 33.22±0.10 mm. These findings suggest that Carpesium nepalence is a potential candidate as an alternative medicinal agent.
