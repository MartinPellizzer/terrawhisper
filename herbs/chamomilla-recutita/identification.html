Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Chamomilla recutita is the scientific name for German Chamomile. It was first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. The species has several synonyms, including Anthemis cuneifolia and Matricaria recutita. It is also known by common names such as German Chamomile and Wild Chamomile. Regional and historical names vary but often reflect its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

German Chamomile is an annual herb with a branched root system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and usually grow up to 30 cm tall. Its leaves are alternate, pinnately divided, and covered in fine hairs. The inflorescence consists of a single flower head, with white petals and yellow central disc. The flowers produce small, dry fruits called achenes, each containing a single seed.

Diagnostic Identification Features

German Chamomile can be distinguished by its daisy-like flower with white petals and a yellow center, narrow linear leaves, and a strong, sweet aroma. It is often confused with other chamomile species, but its specific leaf shape and floral structure are key identifiers. The plant also has a distinctive, slightly bitter taste when the leaves are chewed.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

German Chamomile (Chamomilla recutita) is native to southern Europe and western Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with mild summers and cool winters. It prefers well-drained, sandy or loamy soils. It is commonly found at altitudes between 200 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

German Chamomile blooms from June to September, with flowers opening in the morning and closing by midday. Seed formation occurs from late summer to early autumn. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested during the flowering period, when the flower heads are fully open and the essential oils are most concentrated.

Phylogenetic Relationships

German Chamomile (Chamomilla recutita) belongs to the Asteraceae family, sharing close phylogenetic relationships with other genera like Anthemis and Matricaria. It is closely related to species such as Matricaria chamomilla, which is a closely related species often confused with it. The genus Chamomilla is part of the tribe Anthemideae, characterized by specific morphological and chemical traits. These relationships are important for accurate identification and understanding its medicinal properties.
