Bruguiera gymnorhiza

What's the taxonomical classification of Bruguiera gymnorhiza?

Bruguiera gymnorhiza belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Malpighiales. This plant is situated within the family Rhizophoraceae, which falls under the genus Bruguiera, and is specifically identified by its species name, gymnorhiza.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bruguiera gymnorhiza has woody stems with small, opposite, elliptic leaves that feature an entire margin and a prominent midrib. The plant is characterized by its distinctive pneumatophores, which are specialized aerial roots that grow vertically from the mud to facilitate gas exchange. These breathing roots emerge from the substrate around the base of the main trunk to support survival in anaerobic wetland environments. The leaves are typically coriaceous in texture and possess a somewhat leathery feel. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves during the blooming season.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical coastal regions of West and Central Africa, spanning from Senegal to Angola. It typically inhabits lowland mangrove forests and swampy estuaries where saltwater influences the ecosystem. These distributions are closely tied to the intertidal zones of major river deltas and estuarine environments. Within these habitats, the species thrives in muddy substrates characterized by high moisture and periodic tidal flooding. Its presence is often concentrated along the Atlantic coastline where specific salinity levels and tropical climates are maintained.

What are the pharmacological activities of Bruguiera gymnorhiza?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its ability to act as an AChE inhibitor, a BChE inhibitor, an amylase inhibitor, an antimicrobial agent, and an antioxidant. The inhibition of acetylcholinesterase and butyrylcholinesterase suggests its possible utility in managing neurodegenerative conditions like Alzheimer's disease. Additionally, its amylase inhibitory properties indicate a capacity to assist in the regulation of glucose levels in the blood. The plant also exhibits strong antioxidant capabilities, which help protect cells from oxidative stress and damage. Furthermore, its antimicrobial activities provide a natural defense against various pathogenic microorganisms.

As discussed in a study, maceated fruits of Bruguieira gymnorhiza exhibited high antioxidant capacities for radical scavenging with DPPH at 547.75 ± 10.99 and ABTS at 439.59 ± 19.13 mg TE/g, and reducing potential with CUPRAC at 956.04 ± 11.90 and FRAP at 577.26 ± 4.55 mg TE/g. This same extract significantly depressed enzymatic activities for AChE at 3.75 ± 0.03 and BChE at 2.19 ± 0.13 mg GALAE/g, tyrosinase at 147.01 ± 0.78 mg KAE/g, elastase at 3.14 ± 0.08 mg OE/g, and amylase at 1.22 ± 0.01 mmol ACAE/g. Phytochemical results confirmed the presence of 119 compounds in all maceration samples and 163 compounds in all decoction samples. While the decocted root and leaf showed low to moderate antidiabetic activity, the fruit was identified as the most active organ.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating diabetes, microbial infections, and the Zika virus. Traditional medicinal practices utilize its various parts to manage blood sugar levels in diabetic patients. The plant also contains properties that help combat various microbial infections throughout the body. Researchers have explored its potential effectiveness in addressing the symptoms and complications associated with the Zika virus. These therapeutic applications highlight the significant medicinal importance of the species in local healthcare.

As discussed in a study, Bruguiera gymnorhiza collected in Mauritius was investigated for its anti-ZIKV activity and antimicrobial properties. The twig ethyl acetate extract was the most potent, inhibiting all 9 microbes tested with minimum inhibitory concentrations ranging from 0.19-0.39 mg/mL. Both root aqueous and fruit aqueous extracts were identified as potent inhibitors of ZIKV infection in human epithelial A549 cells. In silico docking suggested that cryptochlorogenic acid, a chemical compound identified in B. gymnorhiza, could be a target for the ZIKV E protein. ADME results predicted that this cryptochlorogenic acid is not orally bioavailable because it is too polar.
