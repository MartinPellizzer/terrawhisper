What to know about Coffea arabica (arabica) before using it medicinally

Coffea arabica, commonly known as arabica, is a herb that has been prized for its numerous health properties, including its ability to lower blood pressure and cholesterol levels, improve circulation, and provide anti-inflammatory benefits that can ease symptoms of anxiety and insomnia.

As a popular and highly sought-after crop, Coffea arabica is grown in tropical regions around the world, requiring careful cultivation and a consistent supply of heat and moisture to thrive. From a botanical standpoint, Coffea arabica is a member of the Rubiaceae family and is characterized by its bright green, glossy leaves and small, white flowers that bloom in clusters at the branch tips.

With a rich history dating back to 15th century Ethiopia, arabica has been revered for centuries as a sacred plant, not only for its medicinal and culinary uses but also for its spiritual significance in various cultures.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Coffea arabica.

What are the medicinal properties of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica helps with insomnia, fatigue, and depression due to its stimulatory and analgesic properties. It has been used in traditional medicine for its ability to improve mental alertness and reduce anxiety. The plant has been shown to increase the production of certain neurotransmitters, such as dopamine and serotonin.

The active constituents of Coffea arabica responsible for its medicinal properties are caffeine, theobromine, and theophylline. Caffeine is the primary stimulant, accounting for approximately 70% of the plant's dry weight. Theobromine and theophylline also contribute to its stimulatory and analgesic effects.

The parts of the plant used for medicinal purposes are the seeds, leaves, and flowers. The seeds, or beans, are the most commonly used for their caffeine content. The leaves and flowers are also used in traditional medicine, particularly in the treatment of fever, rheumatism, and skin conditions.

Improper use of Coffea arabica can lead to side effects such as insomnia, jitteriness, and anxiety. Caffeine overdose can cause symptoms including palpitations, tremors, and vomiting. In severe cases, caffeine toxicity can result in seizures, coma, and even death.

Precautions when using Coffea arabica medicinally include pregnant and breastfeeding women limiting or avoiding its use. Children and individuals with caffeine sensitivity or anxiety disorders should also use the plant with caution. Furthermore, excessive consumption can interact with certain medications, such as antidepressants and blood thinners.

What are the horticulural aspects of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica grow best in high altitudes (1,000-2,500 meters) and require a mild climate with temperatures between 15-25°C (59-77°F). It thrives in well-drained acidic soils with a pH between 5.5 and 6.5. High humidity and adequate water supply are also essential.

Proper planting tips for Coffea arabica include using disease-free seedlings or grafted plants. Plant them 1-2 meters apart, depending on the pruning method, and ensure the soil is rich in organic matter. Mulching around the plants can help retain moisture and suppress weeds. Adequate sunlight is also required for healthy growth.

Harvesting tips for Coffea arabica include observing the color change of the coffee cherries, which turn from green to yellow, red, or purple depending on the variety. Harvesting is usually done by hand to avoid damaging the delicate fruit. Coffee cherries should be picked when fully ripe to ensure high-quality coffee beans.

Common pests and diseases affecting Coffea arabica include coffee berry borer, coffee leaf miner, and coffee powdery mildew. Other issues include coffee leaf spot and root rot. Regular pruning, crop rotation, and integrated pest management strategies can help control these pests and diseases.

What are the botanical characteristics of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica, also known as Arabica coffee, is a species of coffee bean that exhibits several distinctive botanical characteristics.

It is an evergreen shrub or small tree that typically grows to a height of 5-10 meters (16-33 feet), with elliptical-shaped leaves that are dark green on top and lighter green on the underside. The leaves are approximately 6-10 cm (2.4-3.9 inches) in length, with a pointed tip and serrated edges. Arabica coffee plants produce small, white, fragrant flowers that are arranged in clusters of 1-5 flowers per inflorescence, which bloom for a short period of time before producing small, red or yellow fruit called coffee cherries.

The cherries contain two seeds, which are the coffee beans, and have a fleshy outer layer called the exocarp, a thin inner layer called the mesocarp, and a hard inner shell called the endocarp.

What is the taxonomy of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica belongs to the kingdom Plantae, class Magnoliopsida, order Gentianales, family Rubiaceae, genus Coffea, and species C. arabica.

What are the variants of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica has variants, such as Gesha, Yirgacheffe, Sidama, and Bourbon, among others.

These variations can be distinguished by their flavor profiles, growing conditions, and processing methods. For example, Gesha is known for its distinctive floral and citrus notes, while Yirgacheffe is characterized by its delicate fruit and honey flavors. Bourbon, on the other hand, is often described as having a balanced sweetness and acidity.

These variants can result from natural mutations or intentional breeding programs to develop new flavor profiles.

What is the geographic distribution of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica, the species most widely cultivated and consumed as coffee, has a geographic distribution primarily centered around the equatorial region between latitudes 25° north and south.

It is native to Ethiopia and southern Arabia, but is now grown in many countries with suitable climates, particularly in Central and South America, the Caribbean, Africa, Asia, and Pacific Islands.

What is the life cycle of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica, a perennial plant, typically completes its life cycle within 5-7 years.

The process begins when a coffee cherry is pollinated and develops into a fruit, containing two seeds (coffee beans). After harvesting, the cherries are processed to remove the outer skin and pulp, leaving just the inner seeds.

The seeds are then planted in nurseries or directly in fields, where they germinate and grow into seedlings. It takes about 3-4 years for the seedlings to mature and produce flowers, followed by coffee cherries. As the plant grows, it requires regular pruning, fertilization, and pest management to maintain its health and productivity.

Eventually, after 5-7 years, Coffea arabica plants reach their maximum production capacity, at which point they can be replanted or replaced with new seedlings.

What are the historical uses of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica has a rich history dating back over 1,000 years.

Originating in Ethiopia, it was first cultivated by local farmers and later spread to Arabia and other parts of the Middle East. The coffee cherries were consumed as a stimulant, and its leaves and seeds were used in traditional medicine and religious rituals.

The plant's popularity grew throughout the 17th century, and it became a major export commodity in many countries.

What are the mythological referencec of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica, also known as the Arabian coffee, has its roots in ancient mythological tales.

In Ethiopian and Yemeni cultures, it is believed to have been discovered by the goatherd Kaldi, who noticed his goats became more energetic after eating the red cherries of a certain plant. This led him to try the fruit himself, and soon he was energized and alert, just like his goats.

This mythological origin story has been passed down through generations and is still celebrated today as the discovery of the world's most popular coffee species.

What are the symbolic meanings of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica, also known as Arabian coffee, is steeped in symbolism and cultural significance.

In many cultures, it represents hospitality, warmth, and generosity. Its rich aroma and flavor are said to evoke feelings of joy, comfort, and relaxation, making it a popular symbol of friendship and social bonding.

Additionally, the plant's delicate petals and berries are often associated with feminine qualities such as nurturing and protection.

What are the historical texts of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica, one of the most widely consumed coffee species, has a rich history reflected in its ancient texts.

Historical texts related to Arabica date back to the 17th century, with accounts from European explorers and traders describing its cultivation and trade routes in Ethiopia and Yemen.

These primary sources, such as those by Giovanni Battista Ramazzini and Carl Linnaeus, provide valuable insights into the plant's origins, growth habits, and cultural significance.

What are the historical artifacts of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica, considered one of the oldest cultivated coffee species, has a rich history that dates back over 1,000 years.

Historical artifacts related to Arabica coffee include ancient Ethiopian and Arabian manuscripts, 17th-century European trade records, and antique coffee grinders from the Ottoman Empire.

These relics provide valuable insights into the evolution of coffee culture, its spread across the globe, and the development of trade routes that shaped the industry.
