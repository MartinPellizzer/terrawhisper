Coffea arabica

What's the taxonomical classification of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it follows the biological lineage of the order Gentianaales. This plant is a prominent representative of the family Rubiaceae, falling under the genus Coffea, and is specifically identified by the species name arabica.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Coffea arabica contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its seeds, leaves, and fruit pulp. The seeds, commonly known as coffee beans, are the most frequently utilized part due to their high concentration of caffeine and chlorogenic acids. These chemical constituents are often studied for their ability to improve cognitive function and metabolic rates. The leaves are also harvested to create herbal teas that may offer antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits. Additionally, the mucilage found in the fruit pulp contains various bioactive compounds that contribute to the plant's overall medicinal profile.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains caffeine, chlorogenic acid, trigonelline, quinic acid, caffeic acid, cafestol, kahweol, lutein, and rutins, which serve as its primary bioactive medicinal compounds.

Caffeine acts as a central nervous system stimulant, while chlorogenic acid and caffeic acid provide potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits. Trigonelline contributes to metabolic regulation, and quinic acid helps mitigate oxidative stress within the body. Diterpenes such as cafestol and kahweol are notable for their complex effects on lipid metabolism.

Finally, the presence of lutein and rutins offers additional neuroprotective and vascular support through their antioxidant properties.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Coffea arabica targets adenosine receptors, specifically the A1 and A2A subtypes, to produce its primary physiological effects. By acting as a competitive antagonist to adenosine, the caffeine contained within the plant prevents the inhibitory signaling that typically promotes sleep and relaxation. This interaction also modulates neurotransmitter release, influencing dopamine and glutamate pathways in the central nervous system. Beyond adenosine, various polyphenolic compounds in the plant interact with enzymes involved in oxidative stress pathways, such as glutathione peroxidase. These biochemical interactions contribute to the plant's potential neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory properties.

What are the therapeutic actions of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica has stimulant properties primarily driven by its high concentration of caffeine, which acts as an adenosine receptor antagonist to increase alertness and reduce fatigue. This chemical interaction enhances cognitive functions such as memory, attention, and reaction time in the central nervous system. Beyond mental stimulation, the plant contains chlorogenic acids that provide antioxidant benefits by neutralizing free radicals in the body. Some studies also suggest that regular consumption may influence metabolic rates and improve insulin sensitivity. These diverse phytochemicals contribute to both the psychoactive and physiological effects observed in consumers.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat symptoms of migraine headaches, certain types of depression, and fatigue-related lethargy. The caffeine contained within the beans acts as a central nervous system stimulant that can alleviate chronic pain. It is frequently utilized in combination with analgesics to enhance the effectiveness of pain relief medications. Additionally, its stimulant properties help improve alertness and cognitive function in individuals experiencing drowsiness. While it is not a primary cure for many ailments, its chemical compounds play a significant role in managing neurological and metabolic discomfort.

What are the herbal preparations of Coffea arabica?

Coffea arabica is prepared primarily as a concentrated aqueous infusion through roasting, grinding, and brewing the dried seeds into various liquid beverages. Once the beans undergo thermal processing, they can be extracted using methods such as espresso, drip filtration, or pressure infusion to create a potent stimulant drink. Beyond standard liquid brewing, the plant can be processed into fine powders for instant dissolution or used in concentrated extracts for flavoring applications. Some specialized preparations involve the use of the dried fruit husks, known as cascara, which are steeped to produce a tea-like infusion. These diverse methods allow for varying levels of caffeine concentration and flavor profiles depending on the extraction technique employed.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Coffea arabica can cause jitteriness, insomnia, rapid heart rate, anxiety, and gastrointestinal distress due to its high caffeine content. Excessive consumption often leads to restlessness and difficulty falling asleep at night. Some individuals may experience increased blood pressure or heart palpitations after drinking it. It can also trigger acid reflux or stomach upset in sensitive people. Regular heavy intake might result in dependency or withdrawal symptoms like headaches.
