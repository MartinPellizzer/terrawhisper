Inula japonica

What's the taxonomical classification of Inula japonica?

Inula japonica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic progression, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. As a member of the order Asterales, it falls under the family Asteraceae, a group known for its diverse floral structures. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Inula, with its specific designation being the species japonica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Inula japonica has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by erect stems that typically reach heights of 30 to 100 centimeters and feature large, lanceolate, serrated leaves. The foliage is arranged alternately along the stem and possesses a distinctively rugose texture with prominent veining. Its inflorescences consist of solitary, bright yellow flower heads that belong to the Asteraceae family, each containing numerous disc florets and a ring of ray florets. These flower heads are supported by sturdy involucral bracts that protect the developing seeds. The plant eventually produces small, wind-dispersed achenes which facilitate its spread through various habitats.

What are the pharmacological activities of Inula japonica?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-neuroinflammatory, antioxidant, anti-cancer, and anti-tumor pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize harmful free radicals, thereby providing significant antioxidant protection to cellular structures. These properties extend to the central nervous system by suppressing neuroinflammation and reducing inflammatory responses throughout the body. Furthermore, the plant demonstrates potent inhibitory effects against malignant cell growth, contributing to its anti-cancer and anti-tumor potential. Such diverse therapeutic actions make Inuia japonica a subject of interest for medicinal research.

As discussed in a study, Inula japonica contains the main bioactive compounds alantolactone and isoalantolactone. These compounds have been found to possess various pharmacological actions including anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and anticancer properties. The study notes that these actions occur with no significant toxicity. Furthermore, alantolactone and isoalantolactone induce apoptosis by targeting multiple cellular signaling pathways that are frequently deregulated in cancers. This suggests that the simultaneous targeting by these compounds could result in efficacious and selective killing of cancer cells.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds Faberidilactone A, alantolactone, isoalantolactone, sesquiterpenes, and terpenes. These specific bioactive substances are responsible for much of the plant's biological activity and therapeutic potential. The sesquiterpenes and terpenes serve as fundamental structural building blocks within its chemical profile. Furthermore, the presence of alantolactone and its isomer isoalantolactone provides distinct pharmacological properties. Faberidilactone A represents another significant specialized component found within the plant's complex organic makeup.

As discussed in a study, Inula japonica contains the sesquitepenoid dimer faberidilactone A. This compound exhibits potent cytotoxicity against various human tumor cell lines and demonstrates remarkable antitumor potential. In HepG2 cells, faberidilactone A induces apoptosis and ferroptosis while modulating the STAT3 and FAK pathways. When the concentration of faberidilactone A reaches 10 µM, it achieves specific inhibition rates in a zebrafish model. These rates include 76.9% for tumor proliferation, 72.6% for migration, and 46.2% for intersegmental vessels length.
