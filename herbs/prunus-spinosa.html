Prunus spinosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Prunus spinosa?

Prunus spinosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the hierarchy of biological classification, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its order, Rosalales, the plant is a member of the family Rosaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by its membership in the genus Prunus and its designation as the species spinosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Prunus spinosa has woody, deciduous stems characterized by sharp, terminal spines and a dense, spreading shrubby growth habit. The leaves are simple, elliptical to oblong in shape, and possess finely serrated margins with alternating arrangements along the branches. Small, five-petaled white flowers emerge in early spring from clusters located at the ends of the previous year's wood. During the summer, these blossoms develop into small, spherical drupes that transition from green to a deep blue-black color when fully ripe. The bark is typically greyish and becomes increasingly fissured as the plant matures into a larger thicket.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a wide range of Europe and western Asia, extending from the British Isles and the Iberian Peninsula across to the Caucasus Mountains. It thrives primarily in temperate climates where it can occupy diverse habitats such as hedgerows, scrublands, and woodland edges. While its core range is centered in Europe, certain varieties and cultivated forms have been introduced to North America and other temperate regions globally. The species typically prefers well-drained soils and can tolerate a variety of environmental conditions across its vast longitudinal spread. This broad distribution allows the plant to exist in many different ecological niches throughout the northern hemisphere.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Prunus spinosa contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its dark blue fruits, though the bark and roots are also utilized in traditional herbal practices. The berries are frequently processed into jams, syrups, or wines to treat symptoms of the common cold and influenza. These fruits are rich in anthocyanins and antioxidants, which help support the immune system and reduce inflammation. Additionally, decoctions made from the bark have been used historically to address digestive issues and skin ailments. The roots are sometimes prepared as a tincture to assist with various systemic ailments in folk medicine.

What are the pharmacological activities of Prunus spinosa?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antiproliferative, and antitumor pharmacological activities due to its rich concentration of polyphenols and flavonoids. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, thereby reducing oxidative stress within biological systems. The antimicrobial effects allow the plant to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic bacteria and fungi. Furthermore, its antiproliferative and antitumor capabilities suggest a significant potential for controlling abnormal cell growth and treating certain types of cancer. These diverse therapeutic actions make Prunus spinosa a valuable subject for pharmaceutical research.

As discussed in a study, the fresh fruits of Prunus spinosa L. are used for medicinal applications in disorders related to inflammation and oxidative stress. Fifty-seven phenolic components were identified in the extracts, including twenty-eight that were new for the analysed fruits. Fractionation enriched polyphenols up to 126.5 mg gallic acid equivalents/g dw total contents, including 91.3 mg/g phenolic acids, 41.1 mg/g flavonoids, 44.5 mg/g condensed proanthocyanidins, and 9.2 mg/g anthocyanins. These extracts revealed a significant ability to modulate the pro-oxidant, pro-inflammatory, and anti-inflammatory functions of human neutrophils and peripheral blood mononuclear cells. The extracts strongly downregulated the release of reactive oxygen species, TNF-alpha, and neutrophils elastase while upregulating the secretion of IL-10. Additionally, the extracts slightly inhibited the production of IL-8 and IL-6 in cells stimulated by fMLP, fMLP+cytochalasin B, and LPS.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal phenolic acids, most notably 3-O-caffeoylquinic acid and various other derivatives. These bioactive components include specific caffeoylquinic acids that contribute to its therapeutic potential. Additionally, the presence of coumaroylquinic acids and feruloylquinic acids enhances its complex chemical profile. These diverse phenolic acids work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Such a concentration of phytochemicals makes the plant a valuable subject for pharmacological study.

As discussed in a study, Prunus spinosa fruits presented the highest concentration in phenolic acids at 29.78 mg/100 g dry weight, with 3-O-caffeoylquinic acid being the most abundant. The fruits also contained 57.48 mg/100 g of flavone/ols, among which quercetin3-O-rutinoside was the majority compound at 15.63 mg/100 g. While cyanidin 3-O-glucoside was found in all studied fruits, cyanidin 3-O-rutinoside and peonidin 3-O-rutinoside were the predominant anthocyanins in Prunus spinosa. This plant exhibited the most complex anthocyanin profile and the highest anthocyanin concentrations among those analysed. Overall, Prunus spinosa contained the highest levels of phenolic acids and flavonoids, including anthocyanins, flavonols, and flavones, although no flavan-3-ols were identified.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause adverse reactions such as nausea, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and dizziness in some individuals. While many people use its fruit for medicinal purposes, improper consumption or high dosages may lead to significant gastrointestinal distress. The occurrence of abdominal pain and diarrhea often stems from the plant's high fiber content or specific chemical compounds. Some users have also reported feeling dizziness after ingesting extracts derived from its berries or bark. It is important to monitor how your body responds to any part of this species to avoid these uncomfortable side effects.
