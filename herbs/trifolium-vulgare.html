White Clover

Trifolium vulgare

White Clover (Trifolium vulgare) is a common flowering plant known for its trifoliate leaves and small white blossoms, traditionally used in herbal medicine to support digestive health and alleviate symptoms of conditions such as irritable bowel syndrome.

Botanical Identification

White Clover (Trifolium vulgare) is a flowering plant in the legume family Fabaceae. Common names include shamrock and cow's parsley, though these are often used for other species. It is a perennial herb native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa, with trifoliate leaves and small white or pink flowers. Synonyms in herbal texts include Trifolium pratense and Trifolium alpestre.

The following list summarize the taxonomy of plant:

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Fabales
  • Order: Fabales
  • Family: Fabaceae
  • Genus: Trifolium
  • Species: Trifolium vulgare

Active Compounds

White Clover contains key active compounds such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, and tannins, which contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds work together to provide antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects. They also synergize with other plant compounds to enhance overall therapeutic benefits.

  • Flavonoid
  • Phenolic acid
  • Tannin

White Clover Actions.

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for digestive issues and inflammation due to its mild antispasmodic and anti-inflammatory properties.

Decoction: Used for respiratory issues and as a general tonic due to its warming and stimulating effects.

Poultice: Used for skin irritations and wounds due to its soothing and healing properties.

Safety Profile

White Clover is generally considered safe when used in moderate amounts. However, it may cause allergic reactions in individuals sensitive to plants in the legume family. It should be avoided by those with known allergies to related plants.
