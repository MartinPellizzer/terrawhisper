Momordica charantia

What's the taxonomical classification of Momordica charantia?

Momoirdica charantia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Cucurbitales and is a member of the family Cucurbitaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Momordica, and its specific species, charantia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Momordica charantia has an herbaceous, climbing vine morphology characterized by slender, angular stems and five-lobed, palmately shaped leaves with serrated margins. The plant utilizes thin, coiled tendrils that emerge from the leaf axils to facilitate upward climbing. Its reproductive structures consist of small, yellow, unisexual flowers that are typically solitary and emerge from the leaf axils. Following pollination, the plant produces distinctive oblong, warty fruits that transition from green to a bright orange-red color when mature. These fruits eventually split open to reveal bright red, fleshy arils surrounding the seeds.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several key medicinal compounds including charantin, Momordica charantia polysaccharides, momordicoside, momordicine, and cucurbitane-type triterpenoids. These bioactive constituents are primarily recognized for their significant roles in regulating blood glucose levels and managing metabolic health. The polysaccharides and charantin work together to enhance insulin sensitivity and glucose uptake in the body. Additionally, the specific triterpenoids and alkaloids like momordicine contribute to the plant's diverse pharmacological profile. Together, these diverse chemical structures provide the foundation for the plant's therapeutic applications in traditional medicine.

As discussed in a study, Momordica charantia is rich in bioactive chemical constituents including cucurbitane type triterpenoids, triterpene glycosides, phenolic acids, flavonoids, essential oils, saponins, fatty acids, and proteins. Several isolated compounds such as Kuguacin J, Karaviloside XI, Kuguaglycoside C, Momordicoside Q-U, Charantin, and α-eleostearic acid possess potent biological activity. Additionally, the plant contains specific proteins like α-Momorcharin, RNase MC2, and MAP30. These components contribute to the plant's reported anti-oxidant, anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer, anti-diabetic, anti-bacterial, anti-obesity, and immuno-modulatory activities. The plant extract works by inducing apoptosis, cell cycle arrest, and autophagy while inhibiting cancer stem cells.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, nausea, diarrhea, abdominal pain, stomach cramps, vomiting, and dizziness in some individuals. The potential for low blood sugar is a significant concern because the plant contains compounds that mimic insulin. Digestive issues such as stomach cramps, abdominal pain, and diarrhea may also occur after consumption. Some people might experience nausea, vomiting, or dizziness as their bodies react to the plant's bioactive constituents. Monitoring these symptoms is essential for anyone using the plant for medicinal purposes.
