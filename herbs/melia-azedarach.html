Melia azedarach

What's the taxonomical classification of Melia azedarach?

Melia azedarach belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is organized under the order Sapindales. This plant is a member of the family Meliaceae, specifically placed within the genus Melia, and is identified as the species azedarach.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Melia azedarach has pinnately compound leaves typically measuring 15 to 40 centimeters in length, arranged oppositely along woody stems that reach heights of up to 15 meters. Each leaf consists of 7 to 15 leaflets that are ovate or lanceolate in shape with serrated margins. The tree produces large, drooping panicles of fragrant, five-petaled lilac or pale yellow flowers during the spring season. Following the flowering stage, the plant develops small, fleshy, globose drupes that turn from green to a bright yellow when mature. These fruits are often borne in dense clusters and can persist on the branches long after the leaves have fallen.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to subtropical and tropical regions of Asia, specifically spanning from China and India to parts of Southeast Asia and Australia. It has since been widely naturalized throughout much of the world due to its ability to thrive in various climates. In North America, it is frequently found in the southern United States and has become an invasive species in many coastal and riparian habitats. It also establishes itself successfully in many Mediterranean-style environments across Africa and South America. Because it can tolerate a range of soil types and moisture levels, its presence is increasing in many non-native territories.

What are the pharmacological activities of Melia azedarach?

This plant has diverse pharmacological activities including acaricidal, analgesic, anthelmintic, anti-cancer, and antioxidant properties. Its acaricidal potential makes it an effective natural agent for controlling various species of mites and ticks. The plant also demonstrates significant anthelmintic efficacy in eliminating intestinal parasites. Furthermore, its bioactive compounds contribute to analgesic relief and serve as potent antioxidants to combat oxidative stress. Research also indicates that certain extracts from the species possess promising anti-cancer capabilities against specific cell lines.

As discussed in a study, Melia azedarach extracts showed significant toxic effects on embryoanated eggs and engorged nymphs of Hyalomma dromedarii. In the egg immersion test, the LC50 of petroleum ether extracts of Melia azedarach was 3.14% and the LC50 of the respective ethyl alcohol extracts was 1.77%. During the nymphal immersion test, the LC50 of petroleum ether extracts of Melia azedarach was 0.26% while the LC50 of the ethyl alcohol extracts was 4.17%. Observed abnormalities in larvae hatched from treated eggs included incomplete development of legs and mouth parts. Adults emerged from treated nymphs also exhibited shrinkage mainly in the legs and mouthparts.

As discussed in a study, Melia azedarach is known as a medicinal plant that is used to treat various skin diseases. This research utilized response surface methodology to optimize the extraction of two secondary metabolites, lupeol and stigmasterol, from the young roots of Melia azedarach. The highest amount of lupeol obtained was 7.82 mg/g DW using 50% methanol at 45 °C and ultrasonication for 30 min, while the highest stigmasterol was 6.76 mg/g DW using 50% methanol at 35 °C and ultrasonication for 30 min. Predicted conditions for lupeol were 100% methanol, 45 °C, and 40 min of ultrasonication to achieve 14.540 mg/g DW. For stigmasterol, the predicted conditions were 43.75% methanol, 34.4 °C, and 25.3 min of ultrasonication to yield 5.832 mg/g DW. The HPLC-DAD system used to detect these amounts required 8 min for the analysis of each sample.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause severe toxicity characterized by nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and intense abdominal pain. Ingestion may lead to neurological symptoms such as dizziness, confusion, lethargy, and even frightening hallucinations. More critical complications include life-threatening seizures, respiratory distress, and death. These dangerous reactions occur because the plant contains toxic compounds that affect multiple bodily systems.
