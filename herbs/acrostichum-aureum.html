Acrostichum aureum

What's the taxonomical classification of Acrostichum aureum?

Acrostichum aureum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Polypodiinae and the order Polypodiales. This specific fern is a member of the family Pteridaceae, falling under the genus Acrostichum, and is identified by its specific species name, aureum.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, and various inflammatory conditions through its traditional medicinal applications. In certain cultures, the crushed leaves are applied topically to promote the healing of open sores and minor abrasions. Some practitioners also utilize extracts from the fern to alleviate symptoms of localized swelling and irritation. The chemical properties of the plant are believed to provide antimicrobial effects that assist in managing cutaneous ailments. These traditional uses rely on the plant's natural ability to soothe damaged tissues.

What are the herbal preparations of Acrostichum aureum?

Acrostichum aureum is prepared as a decoction, a topical poultice, or a juice extracted from the fresh fronds. Traditional practitioners often boil the fern parts in water to create a concentrated liquid for internal or external use. The plant can also be crushed into a moist paste to be applied directly to the skin for localized treatment. Some methods involve macerating the foliage in oil or alcohol to create infused tinctures. These various preparations allow the medicinal properties of the golden ferns to be delivered in different ways depending on the intended application.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Acrostichum aureum can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis when a person comes into direct contact with its fronds. The plant contains various secondary metabolites that may trigger redness, itching, or a rash upon touching the foliage. While specific systemic toxicity is not extensively documented in humans, handling the plant without protection is generally discouraged to avoid these dermatological reactions. Some individuals might also experience respiratory sensitivity if they inhale spores or dust from the fern. Careful management is necessary to prevent accidental skin exposure during cultivation or maintenance.
