Elaeis guineensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeis guineensis?

Elaeis guineensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Arecales and belongs to the family Arecaceae. Ultimately, it is identified by the genus Elaeis and the specific species name guineensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Elaeis guineensis has a single, unbranched trunk that can reach heights of up to 20 meters and is crowned by a dense rosette of pinnate leaves measuring 4 to 5 meters in length. Each leaf consists of numerous narrow, linear leaflets arranged along a central rachis. The plant produces large, woody inflorescences that emerge from the leaf axils, giving rise to heavy fruit bunches. These bunches contain numerous oval-shaped drupes, which feature a thin exocarp, a fleshy mesocarp, and a hard endocarp protecting the seed. The overall structure is robust and adapted for efficient light interception and fruit production.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of West and Central Africa, stretching from Senegal in the west to the Congo Basin in the east. It thrives primarily in humid, lowland rainforest environments where rainfall is consistent throughout the year. While its center of origin is the African continent, human cultivation has led to its widespread establishment across other tropical zones. Significant populations are now found in Southeast Asia, Latin America, and the Caribbean due to global agricultural trade. The species continues to expand its footprint in any equatorial region that provides sufficient heat and moisture.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Elaeis guineensis contains medicinal properties found in its fruit, palm kernel, leaves, roots, and stem bark. Additionally, decoctions derived from the roots and bark are employed to address ailments such as malaria or gastrointestinal distress. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, catechins, beta-carotene, tocotrienols, and triterpene as its primary medicinal compounds. These bioactive constituents work together to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. The presence of flavonoids and catechins helps to neutralize free radicals and protect cellular structures from oxidative stress. Additionally, beta-carotene supports vision and immune health, while tocotrienols contribute to cardiovascular wellness. The inclusion of triterpene further enhances the plant's medicinal profile by offering potential antimicrobial and anti-tumor properties.

As discussed in a study, the hydromethanolic extract of Elaeis guineensis leaves exhibited a parasite growth inhibition of 94% ± 0.07 at a dose of 25 μg/mL without being cytotoxic. The dereplication and purification processes highlighted the presence of various medicinal components, including saccharides 1-2, aliphatic acids 3 and 8-11, cardanols 12-13, a triterpene 14, a sterol 15, and a nor-sesquiterpene 16. Other identified substances included megastigman derivatives 7 and 17-19, glycosylated benzyl compounds 20-21, flavonoids 4-6 and 22, a pseudoalkaloid 23, and an allyl glycoside 24. During the research, 9 compounds 1-9 were identified through dereplication, while 15 compounds 10-24 were identified after purification. Among these, 4 specific compounds 16-18 and 20 were previously undescribed.
