Elaeis guineensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeis guineensis?

Elaeis guineensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further organized into the order Arecales and belongs to the family Arecaceae. Ultimately, it is identified by the genus Elaeis and the specific species name guineensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Elaeis guineensis has a single, unbranched trunk that can reach heights of up to 20 meters and is crowned by a dense rosette of pinnate leaves measuring 4 to 5 meters in length. Each leaf consists of numerous narrow, linear leaflets arranged along a central rachis. The plant produces large, woody inflorescences that emerge from the leaf axils, giving rise to heavy fruit bunches. These bunches contain numerous oval-shaped drupes, which feature a thin exocarp, a fleshy mesocarp, and a hard endocarp protecting the seed. The overall structure is robust and adapted for efficient light interception and fruit production.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical regions of West and Central Africa, stretching from Senegal in the west to the Congo Basin in the east. It thrives primarily in humid, lowland rainforest environments where rainfall is consistent throughout the year. While its center of origin is the African continent, human cultivation has led to its widespread establishment across other tropical zones. Significant populations are now found in Southeast Asia, Latin America, and the Caribbean due to global agricultural trade. The species continues to expand its footprint in any equatorial region that provides sufficient heat and moisture.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Elaeis guineensis contains medicinal properties found in its fruit, palm kernel, leaves, roots, and stem bark. The oil extracted from the fruit pulp and kernels is frequently used to treat skin inflammations and various dermatological conditions. Infusions made from the leaves are often consumed to manage fever or digestive issues in traditional medicine. Additionally, decoctions derived from the roots and bark are employed to address ailments such as malaria or gastrointestinal distress. These diverse parts provide a wide array of phytochemicals that contribute to the plant's therapeutic applications.

What are the pharmacological activities of Elaeis guineensis?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological potential through its documented anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antiparasitic, and wound healing activities. Various extracts from the oil palm possess bioactive compounds that effectively combat harmful pathogens and oxidative stress within biological systems. These phytochemical properties allow the plant to reduce swelling and accelerate the repair of damaged skin tissues. Additionally, its ability to neutralize parasites and inhibit microbial growth makes it a versatile subject for medicinal research. Such diverse therapeutic actions highlight the plant's importance in traditional and modern pharmacology.

As discussed in a study, palm frond hemicellulose recorded the highest antimicrobial activity against Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, and Candida albicans with zones of 22 mm, 22 mm, and 26 mm, respectively. Phosphorylated palm frond hemicellulose exhibited the highest potential antioxidant activity of approximately 60%. Most of the extracted celluloses and their derivatives from the palm frond showed promising probiotic activities to reduce gastric mucosa side effects and possibly cure gastric ulcers. Methylcellulose, sulfated and phosphorylated hemicelluloses showed anti-inflammatory and gastroprotective activities. All tested compounds demonstrated the capability to ameliorate the ethanol-induced gastric ulcer in rats' stomach.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, catechins, beta-carotene, tocotrienols, and triterpene. These bioactive constituents work synergistically to provide significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits to the human body. The presence of catechins and flavonoids helps to neutralize free radicals and protect cellular structures from oxidative stress. Additionally, beta-carotene serves as a vital precursor for vitamin A, supporting immune function and vision. Tocotrienols and triterpene further enhance the plant's therapeutic profile by promoting skin health and regulating lipid metabolism.

As discussed in a study, the hydromethanolic extract of Elaeis guineensis leaves exhibited a parasite growth inhibition of 94% ± 0.07 at a dose of 25 μg/mL. The dereplication and purification of the extract highlighted the presence of saccharides (1-2), aliphatic acids (3 and 8-11), cardanols (12-13), a triterpene (14), a sterol (15), and a nor-sesquiterpene (16). Other identified compounds included megastigman derivatives (7 and 17-19), glycosylated benzyl (20-21), flavonoids (4-6 and 22), a pseudoalkaloid (23), and an allyl glycoside (24). During the process, 9 compounds (1-9) were identified through dereplication, while 15 compounds (10-24) were identified after purification. Among these, 4 compounds (16-18 and 20) were previously undescribed.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be used to create various medicinal preparations including oil from the seeds, an infusion from the leaves, a decoction from the roots, and an extract from the bark. The oil extracted from the fruit kernels is frequently utilized for its nourishing and topical healing properties. What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause various adverse physiological reactions including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, allergic reactions, and dizziness. Individuals consuming parts of the plant may experience significant gastrointestinal distress characterized by stomach cramping and irregular bowel movements. Some people might also suffer from acute nausea or sudden vomiting after ingestion. Allergic reactions such as skin irritation or respiratory issues can occur in sensitive individuals. Furthermore, some users have reported experiencing bouts of dizziness following exposure.
