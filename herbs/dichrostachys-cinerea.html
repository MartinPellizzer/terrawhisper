Dichrostachys cinerea

What's the taxonomical classification of Dichrostachys cinerea?

Dichrostachys cinerea belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. It falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, following the taxonomic order Fabales. As a member of the family Fabaceae, this plant is categorized under the genus Dichrostachys, with its specific designation being the species cinerea.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dichrostachys cinerea has pinnate leaves consisting of numerous small, oblong leaflets arranged along a central axis and small, woody, bipinnate flowers that produce long, silky, yellowish-brown stamens. The plant typically grows as a spreading shrub or small tree with dark, fissured bark. Its leaves are arranged alternately on the stems and provide a dense, feathery texture to the canopy. The characteristic flowers are arranged in globular heads that appear at the ends of branches. After flowering, the plant develops distinctive, flattened, woody seed pods that are often curved or twisted in shape.

What are the pharmacological activities of Dichrostachys cinerea?

These diverse medicinal properties make it a significant subject for drug development and traditional healing practices.

As discussed in a study, the leaves of Dichrostachys cinerea Linn (LDC) were used to produce crude saponin extracts with a percentage yield of 1.62%. The acute toxicity study in mice determined an intraperitoneal LD50 for these LDC extracts of 1264.9 mg/kg. When tested for anti-inflammaotory activity using a carrageenan-induced rat paw oedema test, the extracts showed statistically significant (P<0.05) decreases in oedema. The LDC extracts achieved a percentage inhibition of 72% after four hours. This value of 72% was found to be comparable to the 63% inhibition produced by ketoprofen.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the specific medicinal compounds dichrostachine A, dichrostachine B, dichrostachine C, dichrostachine D, and dichrostachine E. These specialized molecules are secondary metabolites that contribute to the biological activity of the species. Researchers study these chemical constituents to understand their potential therapeutic applications in modern medicine. Each individual compound may interact with different biological pathways within a living organism. Detailed chemical analysis is required to isolate these substances for further pharmaceutical development.

As discussed in a study, eighteen new meroterpene derivatives known as dichrostachines A-R (1-18) have been isolated from the root and stem barks of Dichrostachys cinerea. These compounds arise from a Diels-Alder reaction between a labdane diene of the raimonol type and a flavonoid B-ring-derived quinone. Most of these compounds were assayed for their ability to inhibit the enzyme protein farnesyl transferase. The most active compounds exhibited IC50 and cytotoxicity values in the 1 microM range.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating microbial infections, malaria, diarrhoea, dermatophilosis, and HIV/AIDS-associated opportunistic infections. Its medicinal properties are leveraged to combat various bacterial and fungal ailments affecting the skin and digestive tract. Practitioners often employ its extracts to manage the symptoms of malaria and reduce intestinal distress caused by diarrhoea. Furthermore, the plant plays a significant role in managing the secondary illnesses that frequently arise in patients living with HIV/AIDS. This wide range of applications highlights its importance in traditional medicine for addressing diverse pathological conditions.

As discussed in a study, Dichrostachys cinerea is one of twelve plant species used for the treatment of perceived HIV and AIDS-associated opportunistic infections by traditional healers in Ngamiland District in Northern Botswana. The determination of four potentially toxic heavy metals, arsenic, chromium, lead and nickel, was carried out using atomic absorption spectrometry. These plants contained arsenic 0.19-0.54 μg g(-1), chromium 0.15-1.27 μg g(-1), lead 0.12-0.23 μg g(-1) and nickel 0.09-0.21 μg g(-1) of dry weight. All metals determined were below the WHO permissive maximum levels. The possible maximum weekly intakes following treatment regimes were insignificant compared to the provisional tolerable weekly intake levels recommended by WHO and the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives. This suggests that heavy metal exposure from consumption of these traditional medicinal plant preparations is within non health-compromising limits.
