Ficus rumphii

What's the taxonomical classification of Ficus rumphii?

Ficus rumphii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further organized into the order Rosales. This plant is a member of the family Moraea (often referred to as Moraeceae in specific taxonomic contexts) and is situated within the genus Ficus, eventually reaching its specific designation as the species rumphii.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ficus rumphii has large, leathery, elliptic to oblong leaves that measure up to 40 centimeters in length and feature prominent pinnate venation with a distinctively glossy upper surface. The leaf margins are typically entire or slightly wavy, while the base may be asymmetrical or slightly cordate. These leaves are arranged alternately along woody, often gnarled stems that support a spreading canopy. The plant produces small, inconspicuous greenish figs that develop within specialized structures known as syconia. These fruits eventually mature into fleshy drupes that serve as a vital food source for local fauna.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across India, Myanmar, Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia. It thrives primarily in moist, lowland areas and is frequently found in coastal forests or near riverbanks. Within these habitats, the species often grows as an epiphyte or a hemi-epiphyte, clinging to larger host trees. Its presence is most concentrated in the humid environments of the Indo-Malayan realm. This widespread distribution allows it to occupy various ecological niches throughout the maritime Southeast Asian archipelago.
