Altingia chinensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Altingia chinensis?

Altingia chinensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Following its taxonomic progression, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Saxifragales, this plant is uniquely situated in the family Altingiaceae. Finally, its specific biological identity is defined by the genus Altingia and the species chinensis.

What are the pharmacological activities of Altingia chinensis?

This plant has potent anti-cancer and anti-tumor pharmacological activities that allow it to inhibit the growth and spread of various malignant cells. Research indicates that its bioactive compounds can induce apoptosis in cancer cell lines, effectively triggering programmed cell death. These substances also interfere with the cell cycle, preventing the rapid proliferation characteristic of tumor development. Furthermore, the plant exhibits significant cytotoxic properties that target specific metabolic pathways within cancerous tissues. These diverse mechanisms underscore its potential as a source for developing new chemotherapeutic agents.

According to a study published by "Phytomedicine : international journal of phytotherapy and phytopharmacology", the petroleum ether extract (APE) of Altingiia chinensis suppresses non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC) by inducing apoptosis, attenuating epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), and downregulating the PI3K/Akt pathway. In vitro testing using LLC and H1299 cells showed that APE dose-dependently decreased cell viability, specifically inducing apoptosis at concentrations of 31.25, 62.5, and 125 μg/ml. This induction of apoptosis was demonstrated by increased Annexin V-FITC/PI-stained cells, cleaved-caspase 3 levels, and the Bax/Bcl-2 ratio. Furthermore, APE inhibited cell metastasis by increasing E-cadherin expression and downregulating vimentin. In vivo, the intragastric administration of 100 mg/kg APE significantly inhibited tumor growth in LLC tumor-bearing C57BL/6J mice models without apparent side effects. Finally, APE was found to directly bind to PI3Kα to inhibit its activity and the subsequent PI3K-Akt pathway.
