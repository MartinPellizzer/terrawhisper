Lannea zenkeri

What's the taxonomical classification of Lannea zenkeri?

Lannea zenkeri belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant falls under the order Sapindales and is a member of the family Anacardiaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Lannea and the specific epithet zenkeri.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Lannea zenkeri contains bioactive secondary metabolites including tannins, saponins, flavonoids, and alkaloids that contribute to its therapeutic properties. These chemical constituents provide the plant with significant antioxidant and antimicrobial activities. Research indicates that extracts from the bark and leaves can be used to treat various ailments such as skin infections and digestive issues. The presence of specific phenolic compounds also helps in neutralizing free radicals within biological systems. Consequently, these medicinal compounds make the plant a subject of interest for pharmacological studies in traditional medicine.

What are the therapeutic actions of Lannea zenkeri?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat stomach aches, diarrhea, skin infections, and various types of inflammation. The leaves and roots are frequently prepared as decoctions to soothe digestive distress and gastrointestinal discomfort. When applied topically, the plant extracts help manage wounds and dermatological issues. It is also utilized in traditional medicine to reduce swelling and general bodily aches. These applications highlight the diverse medicinal properties attributed to the species in local practices.

What are the herbal preparations of Lannea zenkeri?

Lannea zenkeri is prepared as a decoction by boiling the leaves, bark, or roots in water to create a medicinal liquid. This aqueous extract is frequently consumed orally to treat various ailments such as stomach issues or skin infections. In some traditional practices, the plant material is also crushed or macerated to produce a topical paste for external application. This paste is applied directly to wounds or inflamed areas to utilize its healing properties. These different preparation methods allow practitioners to tailor the treatment to the specific needs of the patient.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Lannea zenkeri can cause adverse reactions such as gastrointestinal distress, skin irritation, or allergic responses if ingested or applied incorrectly. While many parts of the plant are used in traditional medicine, concentrated extracts may trigger nausea or abdominal pain. Some individuals might experience localized redness or itching upon topical contact with the plant's sap or leaves. Because specific toxicological studies on humans are limited, the full extent of its potential toxicity remains partially unknown. Caution is necessary to avoid unexpected systemic reactions during medicinal use.
