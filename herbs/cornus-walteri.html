Cornus walteri

What's the taxonomical classification of Cornus walteri?

Cornus walteri belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it within the order Cornales. As a member of the family Cornaceae, it is further categorized under the genus Cornus, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species walteri.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cornus walteri has opposite, simple leaves that are ovate to elliptic in shape, featuring prominent pinnate venation and entire to slightly serrated margins. The plant typically exhibits a multi-stemmed shrubby growth habit with smooth, grayish bark. Its inflorescences consist of small, white, four-petaled flowers clustered in dense cymes. Following flowering, the plant produces small, fleshy drupes that transition from green to a dark color upon maturity. These botanical features distinguish it from other species within the dogwood family.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cornus walteri?

This plant has pharmacological activities including adipogenesis suppression, anti-diarrheal, anti-inflammatory, and anti-photoaging properties. Its ability to suppress adipogenesis suggests a potential role in managing obesity and metabolic disorders. The anti-diarrheal effects indicate that extracts from the plant can help regulate intestinal function during gastrointestinal distress. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory actions work to reduce swelling and systemic irritation in biological tissues. Finally, the anti-photoaging properties make it a candidate for protecting skin cells against ultraviolet radiation damage.

As discussed in a study, Cornus walteri has been broadly used in traditional East Asian medicine for the treatment of various disorders, including skin inflammation and diarrhea. Seven triterpenoids, identified as compounds 1-7, were isolated from the stems and stem bark of Cornus walteri. These seven triterpenoids were examined for their regulatory effects on mesenchymal stem cell differentiation to adipocytes and osteoblasts in the C3H10T1/2 cell line. While the compounds had little effect on differentiation to osteoblasts, lipid droplet formation in adipocyte-differentiated MSCs decreased in the presence of the 7 triterpenoids. Specifically, compounds 1 and 4 showed adipogenesis suppression at higher concentrations. Findings demonstrate that the active compounds 1 and 4 can exert beneficial effects in the regulation of adipocyte differentiation.

Additionally, the tirucallane and triterpenoids contribute to the overall complex structure of its bioactive makeup. Together, these compounds form the foundation of the plant's pharmacological potential.
