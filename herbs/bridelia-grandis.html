Bridelia grandis

What's the taxonomical classification of Bridelia grandis?

Bridelia grandis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized into the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Phyllanthaceae. Ultimately, its taxonomic position is defined by its placement within the genus Bridelia, specifically identified as the species grandis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bridelia grandis has woody stems with rough bark and leaves that are oppositely arranged, elliptical in shape, and possess finely serrated margins. The foliage is typically dark green and can reach significant sizes depending on the maturity of the tree. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in clusters from the axils of the leaves or the branches. These flowers eventually develop into fleshy, globose drupes that change color as they ripen. The overall structure of the plant is characterized by its sturdy, upright growth habit and dense canopy.

What are the pharmacological activities of Bridelia grandis?

This plant has potent antibacterial and antimicrobial activities that allow it to inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Research indicates that the bioactive compounds found in its leaves and bark can effectively combat diverse bacterial strains. These medicinal properties make it a significant subject of study for developing new therapeutic agents against infections. Beyond its antimicrobial capabilities, the plant also exhibits other biological effects that contribute to its traditional use in medicine. Its diverse chemical profile provides a strong foundation for exploring its potential in treating infectious diseases.

According to a study published by "Journal of ethnopharmacology", Bridelia grandis is a medicinal plant traditionally used in Cameroon by pygmies Baka for oral cavity affection. Bioassay-guided stem bark extracts were investigated for antimicrobial properties using solvents such as methanol, ethyl acetate, and mixtures of methanol-water. Water, methanol, and methanol-water extracts exhibited antibacterial activity against oral Streptococci with a minimum inhibitory concentration between 0.5 and 2mg/ml. The most active extract was the methanol extract, which underwent phytochemical analysis using methods like TLC and RP-HPLC/DAD. Data obtained from this analysis indicate that tannins constitute the chemical family responsible for the biological activity.
