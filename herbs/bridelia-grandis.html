Bridelia grandis

What's the taxonomical classification of Bridelia grandis?

Bridelia grandis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it falls under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant is further categorized into the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Phyllanthaceae. Ultimately, its taxonomic position is defined by its placement within the genus Bridelia, specifically identified as the species grandis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Bridelia grandis has woody stems with rough bark and leaves that are oppositely arranged, elliptical in shape, and possess finely serrated margins. The foliage is typically dark green and can reach significant sizes depending on the maturity of the tree. Small, inconspicuous flowers emerge in clusters from the axils of the leaves or the branches. These flowers eventually develop into fleshy, globose drupes that change color as they ripen. The overall structure of the plant is characterized by its sturdy, upright growth habit and dense canopy.

What are the pharmacological activities of Bridelia grandis?

This plant has significant antibacterial and antimicrobial activities that make it a valuable subject for pharmacological research. Various studies have demonstrated that its extracts can effectively inhibit the growth of diverse pathogenic bacteria and fungi. These bioactive properties are often attributed to the presence of specific phytochemicals like tannins, flavonoids, and alkaloids. By disrupting cellular processes in microbes, the plant offers potential for developing new therapeutic agents against infectious diseases. Continued investigation into its chemical composition may further unlock its medicinal utility in clinical settings.

As discussed in a study, Bridelia grandis stem bark is a medicinal plant traditionally used by pygmies Baka in Cameroon for oral cavity affection. Water, methanol, and methanol-water extracts of the stem bark exhibited antibacterial activity against oral Streptococci. These extracts demonstrated a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) between 0.5 and 2mg/ml. Preliminary phytochemical analysis using TLC and RP-HPLC/DAD on the methanol extract indicates that tannins are the chemical family responsible for this biological activity.
