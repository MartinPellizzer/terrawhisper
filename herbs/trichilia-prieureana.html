Trichilia prieureana

What's the taxonomical classification of Trichilia prieureana?

Trichilia prieureana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized into the subclass Magnoliidae. This species is a member of the order Sapindales and falls under the family Meliaceae. Finally, its taxonomic placement is completed within the genus Trichilia, specifically identified as the species prieureana.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Trichilia prieureana contains various bioactive secondary metabolites including alkaloids, terpenoids, saponins, and phenolic compounds that contribute to its therapeutic potential. These chemical constituents are often studied for their antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties in traditional medicine. Specific limonoids found within the plant species are particularly noted for their ability to inhibit certain biological processes. The presence of these complex organic molecules suggests significant pharmacological value for treating infectious diseases. Researchers continue to investigate these specific compounds to isolate more potent medicinal agents.

What are the therapeutic actions of Trichilia prieureana?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, gastrointestinal issues, and various inflammatory conditions. The bark and leaves contain bioactive compounds that act as antimicrobial agents against specific pathogens. Local traditional healers often prepare decoctions to alleviate symptoms of stomach aches and diarrhea. Additionally, topical applications are applied to wounds or skin irritations to promote healing. These medicinal properties make the species a significant resource in regional ethnobotanical practices.

What are the herbal preparations of Trichilia prieureana?

Trichilia prieureana is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste using the plant's bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is often boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal ailments. Leaves may be crushed into a fine paste to be applied directly to the skin for treating sores or infections. Additionally, some preparations involve steeping the plant material in oils to facilitate external applications. These diverse methods allow the various bioactive components of the plant to be utilized for different therapeutic purposes.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Trichilia prieureana can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested improperly. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the plant contains bioactive compounds that may trigger hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. Topical application of its extracts might lead to redness, itching, or localized swelling upon contact with the dermis. If consumed, the chemical constituents could potentially disrupt digestive processes or cause nausea. Users should exercise caution and avoid direct contact with raw plant material to prevent these adverse reactions.
