Celosia trigyna

What's the taxonomical classification of Celosia trigyna?

Celosia trigyna belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this botanical framework, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, the plant falls under the order Caryophyllales and is a member of the family Amaranthaceae. Finally, it is identified by the genus Celosia and is specifically distinguished as the species trigyna.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Celosia trigyna has an erect, herbaceous stem that typically reaches a height of 30 to 60 centimeters and supports terminal, densely packed flower spikes.

The leaves are arranged oppositely along the stem, featuring an oblong to lanceolate shape with entire margins and acute tips. Small, inconspicuous flowers are clustered tightly within the colorful, plume-like inflorescences that characterize the genus. These flowers possess three tepals and three stamens, which is a key morphological trait reflected in the specific epithet.

The plant also develops a branched root system that anchors it firmly into the soil.

        What are the herbal preparations of Celosia trigyna?

        Celosia trigyna is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice depending on the intended therapeutic application. For internal use, the dried aerial parts are typically boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid extract. This decoction is often consumed to address various ailments related to the digestive or urinary systems. When treating skin conditions or wounds, the plant material can be crushed into a paste to form a soothing poultice. Some practitioners also utilize the plant in the form of a medicinal tea for milder systemic support.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Celosia trigyna can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested improperly. While many species in the Celosia genus are used in traditional medicine, consuming unknown parts may lead to nausea or stomach cramps. Some individuals might experience contact dermatitis if the plant's sap touches sensitive areas of the skin. There is also a potential for systemic toxicity if high doses are consumed without professional guidance. It is essential to exercise caution and consult a medical expert before any use.
