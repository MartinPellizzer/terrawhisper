Cissus repens

What's the taxonomical classification of Cissus repens?

Cissus repens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Vitales, the plant is placed in the family Vitaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Cissus and the species repens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cissus repens has woody, climbing stems characterized by opposite, trifoliate leaves that feature three distinct leaflets with serrated margins. The plant utilizes tendrils that emerge from the leaf axils to facilitate its climbing growth habit. Each leaflet is typically ovate or elliptical in shape and possesses a textured surface with prominent venation. The leaves are arranged in an opposite pattern along the flexible, twining vines. Small, inconspicuous flowers often emerge from the foliage to support its reproductive cycle.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Cissus repens targets cyclooxygenase-2, inducible nitric oxide synthase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukin-6 to exert its medicinal effects. By inhibiting the cyclooxygenase-2 enzyme, the plant reduces the production of prostaglandins that drive pain and inflammation. It also modulates the nitric oxide synthase pathway, which helps regulate vascular tone and inflammatory responses in the body. Furthermore, the suppression of specific cytokines prevents the overactivation of the immune system during chronic inflammatory states. These diverse protein interactions collectively contribute to its potential as an anti-inflammatory and analgesic agent.

What are the therapeutic actions of Cissus repens?

Cissus repens has potent therapeutic properties including hemostatic, astringent, venotonic, vasoprotective, and anti-inflammatory actions that primarily target the vascular system. Its astringent qualities help to constrict tissues, which assists in the hemostatic process to control bleeding. The plant acts as a powerful venotonic agent by improving the tone and contraction of vein walls. Furthermore, its vasoprotective effects help protect the integrity of blood vessels against damage. These combined mechanisms also provide an anti-inflammatory response that reduces swelling and irritation in the circulatory pathways.
