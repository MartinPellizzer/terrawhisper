Physalis peruviana

What's the taxonomical classification of Physalis peruviana?

Physalis peruviana belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is a member of the order Solanales and falls within the family Solanaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Physalis, and its unique species designation, peruvianana.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Physalis peruviana has perennial herbaceous stems that branch extensively to support clusters of bell-shaped, pale yellow flowers and fleshy, orange-to-red berries enclosed within a persistent, papery calyx. The leaves are typically ovate or elliptical with slightly serrated margins and a soft, pubescent texture. Each plant produces distinctive inflated husks that protect the developing fruit from environmental stressors and predators. These protective structures eventually dry into a lightweight shell as the fruit reaches full maturity. The root system consists of a robust rhizomatous structure that allows the plant to spread through its local habitat.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Andean regions of South America, specifically occurring within the territories of Peru, Ecuador, and parts of Colombia. It thrives primarily in high-altitude tropical environments where the climate remains cool and moist. These botanical specimens are frequently found growing along mountain slopes and in temperate valleys throughout the central cordillera. Because it is adapted to such specific elevations, its natural range is restricted to these unique montane ecosystems. The plant's distribution is closely tied to the presence of volcanic soil and consistent seasonal rainfall patterns.

What are the pharmacological activities of Physalis peruviana?

This plant has anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, hepatoprotective, and immunomodulatory pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize free radicals, which effectively reduces oxidative stress within cellular structures. The anti-inflammatory effects allow the plant to mitigate swelling and pain by regulating various chemical mediators. Additionally, its hepatoprotective capabilities provide essential support for liver function and protection against chemical damage. Finally, its immunomodulatory action assists in balancing the immune system to enhance natural defense mechanisms.

As discussed in a study, Physalis peruviana is used for medicinal purposes and contains withanolides that inhibit the growth of colon cancer monolayer and spheroid cultures. The isolated compound 4β-hydroxywithanolide E (4HWE) inhibits growth in HT-29 cells at low concentrations by enhancing p21(Waf1/Cip1) levels and inducing apoptosis at higher concentrations. Treatment with 4HWE causes G0/G1 cell cycle arrest and downregulates HsP90 client proteins. This process involves the expression of 21 altered genes, including the downregulation of PTGS2, which correlates with reduced COX-2 protein levels. Additionally, 4HWE increases nuclear SIRT1 and decreases histone H3 acetylated at lysine 9.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including withanolides, flavonoids, physapruin A, withaperuviin, and alkaloids. These bioactive constituents are primarily responsible for the plant's various pharmacological activities and therapeutic potential. The withanolides and withaperuviin serve as significant steroidal lactones that influence cellular processes. Additionally, the presence of flavonoids provides antioxidant properties, while the alkaloids contribute to its complex chemical profile. Physapruin A further distinguishes the plant's unique chemical makeup within its genus.

As discussed in a study, Physalis peruviana contains withanolides, specifically 4β-hydroxywithanolide E (4HWE), which inhibits the growth of colon cancer monolayer and spheroid cultures. Treatment of HT-29 cells with low concentrations of 4HWE inhibits growth by enhancing levels of p21(Waf1/Cip1) and inducing G0/G1 cell cycle arrest. At higher concentrations, 4HWE induces apoptosis and downregulates levels of Hsp90 client proteins. The compound increases nuclear sirtuin 1 (SIRT1) and decreases histone H3 acetylated at lysine 9. Furthermore, the expression of 21 genes is altered, including the downregulation of PTGS2 which correlates with reduced protein levels of cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2).

As discussed in a study, Physalis peruviana calyces have been used in folk medicine for medicinal properties including antiinflammatory effects. To evaluate this effect, thirty-eight secondary fractions were obtained by column chromatography of the primary hydroalcoholic fraction. Out of these, six fractions showed significant antiinflammatory activity (p<0.05) in a 12-O-tetradecanoylphorbol-13-acetate-induced inflammation assay. The major fraction, Pp-D28-LF, demonstrated a significant dose-dependent response at doses over 250 microg/ear. These results confirmed the antiinflammatory activity attributed to Physalis peruviana calyces.
