Streblus indicus

What's the taxonomical classification of Streblus indicus?

Streblus indicus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of its taxonomic classification, it falls under the order Rosales and the family Moraceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Streblus, and its unique species name, indicus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Streblus indicus has simple, alternate, elliptical to oblong leaves with an entire margin, a woody stem, and small, greenish-white, dioecious flowers. The leaves are typically thick and leathery with a shiny upper surface and prominent venation. Its bark is generally greyish and becomes rough with the age of the tree. The plant produces small, fleshy drupes that transition from green to a bright red or orange color when mature. These fruits are often borne in dense clusters directly from the branches or axils.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of South and Southeast Asia, spanning from India and Sri Lanka through Myanmar, Thailand, and Malaysia to Indonesia and southern China. It thrives primarily in low-elevation areas where warm, humid climates provide consistent moisture. Because it is well-adapted to these tropical environments, it is frequently cultivated in botanical gardens and home landscapes throughout the Pacific Islands. The species is often found growing in coastal forests and along riverbanks where soil remains damp. Its broad range allows it to inhabit diverse ecosystems ranging from dry deciduous forests to moist evergreen jungles.

What are the pharmacological activities of Streblus indicus?

This plant has inhibitory activity against A549 and MCF-7 tumor cells. These specific cancer cell lines represent lung and breast malignancies, respectively. By suppressing the growth of these cells, the plant demonstrates potential as a source for anti-cancer drug development. The biological mechanisms involved in this inhibition provide a basis for further pharmacological investigation into its medicinal properties. Research into these specific interactions helps clarify how the plant's compounds target tumor progression.

As discussed in a study, the bark of Streblus indicus (Bur.) Corner yielded a pair of enantiomers and a pair of 2,3-dihydro-1H-indene epimers known as rac-indidene A (rac-1) and indidenes B and C (2, 3). These derivatives, along with four new coumarin glucosides (4-7) and four known coumarin glucosides (8-11), were isolated and structurally defined. The 2,3-dihydro-1H-indene derivatives rac-indidene A and indidenes B and C demonstrated inhibitory activity against A549 and MCF-7 tumor cells. This cytotoxic activity was measured with IC50 values ranging from 2.2 ± 0.1 to 7.2 ± 0.9 μM.
