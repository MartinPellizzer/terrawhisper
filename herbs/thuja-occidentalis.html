What to know about Thuja occidentalis (arborvitae) before using it medicinally

Thuja occidentalis, commonly known as arborvitae, is a herb rich in health-promoting properties, including antimicrobial and antifungal compounds that can boost the immune system and improve overall health.

This versatile herb is also prized for its ornamental and landscaping value, with conical shape and fragrant foliage making it a popular choice for gardens and parks. From a botanical standpoint, Thuja occidentalis is a member of the cypress family (Cupressaceae), with scale-like leaves and small, spherical cones that are often used in floral arrangements.

Historically, Thuja occidentalis has been used in traditional medicine for centuries, with references to its healing properties dating back to the early American colonists and indigenous populations.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Thuja occidentalis.

What are the medicinal properties of Thuja occidentalis?

Thuja occidentalis helps with various health issues, including fever reduction, respiratory problems, and skin conditions, such as eczema and dermatitis, due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties.

The active constituents responsible for the medicinal properties of Thuja occidentalis include terpenes, particularly thujone and bornyl acetate, as well as flavonoids and lignans, which contribute to its antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant activities.

The parts of the plant most used for medicinal purposes are the leaves, bark, and twigs, which are rich in essential oils and contain the active constituents responsible for its medicinal properties, making them effective in treating various health conditions.

Improper use of Thuja occidentalis may lead to side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, dizziness, and allergic reactions, particularly when taken in large quantities or for extended periods, highlighting the need for careful dosage and monitoring.

Precautions to take when using Thuja occidentalis medicinally include consulting a healthcare professional, especially for pregnant or breastfeeding women, as well as individuals with liver or kidney disease, and using it in conjunction with other medications only under medical supervision.

What are the horticulural aspects of Thuja occidentalis?

Thuja occidentalis grow best in full sun to partial shade with well-drained soils and a slightly acidic to neutral pH. They prefer temperatures between 65°F and 75°F (18°C and 24°C). This evergreen can tolerate drought but performs best with regular watering.

When planting Thuja occidentalis, choose a location with good air circulation to prevent disease. Plant the tree at the same depth as it was in the nursery, with the root flare visible above the soil. Mulch around the base to retain moisture and suppress weeds. Water thoroughly after planting.

Harvesting Thuja occidentalis can be done for foliage, cones, or as a specimen plant. Prune in late winter or early spring to maintain shape and size. Cones can be harvested in fall after they turn brown. Foliage can be harvested as needed for craft or decorative purposes.

Common pests and diseases affecting Thuja occidentalis include bagworms, spider mites, and root rot. Bagworms are the most significant threat, feeding on the foliage and causing damage. Regularly inspect the tree for signs of these pests and diseases, and take action promptly if you notice any issues.

What are the botanical characteristics of Thuja occidentalis?

Thuja occidentalis, also known as Eastern White Cedar, is a coniferous evergreen tree characterized by its distinctive botanical features.

Overall, the botanical characteristics of Thuja occidentalis set it apart as a unique and fragrant addition to its native eastern North American forests.
