Cotoneaster bullatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cotoneaster bullatus?

Cotoneaster bullatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down its taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, it is situated within the genus Cotoneaster, specifically identified by its species name, bullatus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cotoneaster bullatus has small, densely packed, oval-shaped leaves that are characterized by a distinctly convex or bullate surface texture. These leaves are typically dark green on the upper side and often possess a slightly fuzzy or pubescent underside. The plant exhibits a low-growing, spreading growth habit with woody stems that form a compact mound. Small, white, five-petaled flowers emerge in clusters during the spring season. Following the flowering period, the plant produces small, fleshy red berries that persist into the colder months.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

These specific habitats provide the well-drained soil and temperate climate necessary for its survival. What are the pharmacological activities of Cotoneaster bullatus?

This plant has significant pharmacological activities including antioxidant properties and the ability to achieve inhibition of pro-inflammatory enzymes. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals, which helps reduce oxidative stress within cellular environments. Furthermore, specific phytochemicals found in the species interfere with the biochemical pathways responsible for inflammation. By suppressing these enzymatic processes, the plant may help mitigate various inflammatory conditions. These combined mechanisms suggest that the species holds potential for therapeutic development in managing oxidative and inflammatory disorders.

As discussed in a study, dry extracts from the leaves of Cotoneaster bullatus are rich in structurally diverse polyphenols containing 47 analytes with content up to 650.8 mg GAE/g dw. These extracts are dominated by proanthocyanidins with levels between 83.3 and 358.2 mg CYE/g. In chemical in vitro tests, the extracts demonstrated dose-, phenolic-, and extraction solvent-dependent effects regarding the inhibition of pro-inflammatory enzymes like lipoxygenase and hyaluronidase, as well as antioxidant activity via DPPH and FRAP. At in vivo-relevant levels of 1-5 µg/mL, the extracts normalized or enhanced the non-enzymatic antioxidant capacity of human blood plasma and prevented peroxynitrite-induced oxidative and nitrative damage of plasma proteins and lipids. Furthermore, cytotoxicity tests showed the extracts were safe and did not affect the viability of human peripheral blood mononuclear cells.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause gastrointestinal distress, including vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea, if any part of it is ingested by humans or animals. The toxicity is primarily attributed to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides found within its berries and tissues. When these compounds are consumed, they can release hydrogen cyanide into the body, which interferes with cellular respiration. While accidental ingestion by humans is uncommon, pets are at a higher risk of suffering from poisoning after eating the fruit. Symptoms of poisoning may appear quickly and require immediate veterinary or medical attention.
