Cotoneaster bullatus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cotoneaster bullatus?

Cotoneaster bullatus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down its taxonomic hierarchy, the plant is part of the order Rosales and the family Rosaceae. Finally, it is situated within the genus Cotoneaster, specifically identified by its species name, bullatus.

What are the pharmacological activities of Cotoneaster bullatus?

This plant has antioxidant properties and the ability to facilitate the inhibition of pro-inflammatory enzymes. These pharmacological activities are largely attributed to the high concentration of bioactive polyphenolic compounds found within its tissues. By neutralizing free radicals, the plant helps mitigate oxidative stress in biological systems. Furthermore, its capacity to suppress inflammatory enzymes suggests potential utility in managing chronic inflammatory conditions. Such mechanisms provide a scientific basis for investigating its medicinal applications in various therapeutic contexts.

According to a study published by "Molecules (Basel, Switzerland)", extracts from the leaves of Cotoneaster bullatus are rich in structurally diverse polyphenols, containing 47 analytes with a content up to 650.8 mg GAE/g dw. Proanthocyanidins dominate these extracts with levels ranging from 83.3 to 358.2 mg CYE/g. The extracts demonstrate antioxidant activity through DPPH and FRAP tests, showing effects that are dose-, phenolic-, and extraction solvent-dependent. At in vivo-relevant levels of 1-5 µg/mL, these extracts normalize or enhance the non-enzymatic antioxidant capacity of human blood plasma and prevent peroxynitrite-induced oxidative/nitrative damage to plasma proteins and lipids. Furthermore, cytotoxicity tests confirmed the extracts are safe as they did not affect the viability of human peripheral blood mononuclear cells.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause gastrointestinal distress, including vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea, if any part of it is ingested by humans or animals. The toxicity is primarily attributed to the presence of cyanogenic glycosides found within its berries and tissues. When these compounds are consumed, they can release hydrogen cyanide into the body, which interferes with cellular respiration. While accidental ingestion by humans is uncommon, pets are at a higher risk of suffering from poisoning after eating the fruit. Symptoms of poisoning may appear quickly and require immediate veterinary or medical attention.
