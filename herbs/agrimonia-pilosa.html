Agrimonia pilosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Agrimonia pilosa?

Agrimonia pilosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Rosales. Furthermore, it is a member of the family Rosaceae, falling into the genus Agrimonia, and is specifically identified by its species name, pilosa.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Agrimonia pilosa has a perennial herbaceous growth habit characterized by stems and leaves densely covered in fine, soft hairs. The foliage consists of deeply lobed, pinnatisect leaves that exhibit a soft, velvety texture due to this extensive pubescence. Its flowers are typically small and yellow, arranged in dense, terminal umbel-like clusters. The reproductive structures include small capsules that facilitate seed dispersal. These morphological traits collectively help the plant retain moisture and protect its tissues.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive medicinal compounds including agrimoniin, apigenin, quercetin, rutin, and tannins. The presence of the flavonoid apiigenin and quercetin provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the plant's chemical profile. Additionally, the glycoside rutin works alongside agrimoniin to help support vascular health and cellular protection. Tannins are also found within the tissues, contributing to the plant's astringent qualities and ability to bind with proteins. These combined phytochemicals work synergistically to offer various therapeutic benefits.

As discussed in a study, agrimoniin is a polyphenol from the group of tannins isolated from Agrimonia pilosa Ledeb by multistep purification. The content of agrimoniin in the resulting substance reached 80% as determined by NMR spectroscopy. When tested on isolated rat liver mitochondria, agrimoniin at a low concentration of 10 µM induced low-amplitude swelling. This compound activated the opening of MPTP by calcium ions and stimulated the respiration supported by succinate oxidation. Additionally, agrimoniin reduced the electron acceptor DCPIP in a concentration-dependent manner and chelated iron ions.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating inflammation, microbial infections, viral infections, parasites, and dysentery. Its medicinal properties are often applied to reduce swelling and soothe bodily irritation. It serves as a natural defense against various harmful microorganisms and viruses that cause illness. Additionally, the plant helps eliminate internal parasites from the digestive system. It is also frequently utilized as a remedy to manage the symptoms of dysentery.

As discussed in a study, Agrimonia pilosa Ledeb root extracts (APL-ME) were examined for their anti-inflamatory effects in P. gingivalis LPS-induced RAW 264.7 cells. The APL-ME suppressed nitrite concentrations and pro-inflamatory cytokines, specifically IL-1, IL-6, and TNF-alpha. Additionally, the expression of the anti-inflamatory cytokine IL-10 was increased by APL-ME. The protein expressions of COX-2 and iNOS were inhibited dose-dependently by the APL-ME extracts. These results indicate that APL-ME has anti-inflamatory effects in these specific cells.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause intense skin irritation, localized swelling, and painful blistering upon direct contact with the dermis. The fine hairs covering the leaves contain irritating compounds that trigger immediate inflammatory responses in sensitive individuals. Once the plant touches the skin, the chemical reaction may lead to prolonged redness and itching that lasts for several days. If the sap enters an open wound or the eyes, it can result in severe chemical burns or temporary vision impairment. Individuals with known allergies to similar botanical species should exercise extreme caution when handling this specimen.
