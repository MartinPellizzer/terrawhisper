Scrophularia nodosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Lamiales, order Scrophulariales, family Scrophulariaceae, genus Scrophularia, and species Scrophularia nodosa. Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

Scrophularia nodosa is the scientific name for the plant, first described by the botanist Carl von Linné. It belongs to the family Scrophulariaceae. Common synonyms include Antirrhinum nodosum and Veronica nodosa. It is also known by various regional and historical names, reflecting its traditional use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Scrophularia nodosa is a perennial herb with a stout, fleshy rhizome. It has erect, branched stems that are hairy and often reddish. The leaves are opposite, lanceolate, and slightly hairy. The inflorescence is a loose raceme with showy, tubular flowers that are pink to purple. The fruit is a capsule containing small, black seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Scrophularia nodosa can be distinguished by its distinctive purple to reddish stems, opposite leaves with rounded tips, and tubular flowers in clusters. It lacks the hairy stems and larger leaves of S. umbrosa, and its flowers have a more prominent lower lip. These features help in accurate identification during field studies or herbal practice.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Scrophularia nodosa is native to Europe and parts of Asia. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers moist, well-drained soils. It is typically found at low to mid-altitude regions, often in woodland or meadow environments.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Scrophularia nodosa blooms from May to June, producing pink to purple tubular flowers. Seed formation occurs from July to August. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested in late summer when the leaves are mature and the stems are woody.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Scrophularia nodosa belongs to the family Scrophulariaceae and is closely related to species like Scrophularia scorpioides and Scrophularia myrtifolia. It shares key morphological and genetic traits with other members of the genus, including leaf structure and flower morphology. Its phylogenetic placement reflects its evolutionary ties within the family, highlighting its role in the broader context of herbal medicine.
