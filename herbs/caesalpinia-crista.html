Caesalpinia crista

What's the taxonomical classification of Caesalpinia crista?

Caesalpinia crista belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized within the phylum Streptophyta. It is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Within the order Fabales, this plant is a member of the family Fabaceae. Its specific taxonomic hierarchy concludes with the genus Caesalpinia and the species crista.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Caesalpinia crista has bipinnate leaves characterized by numerous small, oblong leaflets and bright yellow flowers that feature distinctively fringed or crested petals. The plant exhibits a woody, shrubby growth habit with branches that often possess a slightly textured bark. Its inflorescences are typically arranged in racemes, emerging from the axils of the foliage to create vibrant displays. The reproductive structures include prominent stamens that contribute to the ornamental appearance of the blooms. Small, flattened legumes serve as the fruit, eventually drying to release seeds.

What are the pharmacological activities of Caesalpinia crista?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, anti-oxidant, neuroprotective, and anthelmintic pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds work to reduce swelling and inhibit the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. The presence of specific phytochemicals also helps neutralize free radicals to prevent cellular damage through antioxidant mechanisms. Research suggests these constituents can protect neural cells from degeneration, offering potential neuroprotective benefits. Additionally, the plant exhibits anthelmintic properties that allow it to combat parasitic worm infections.

As discussed in a study, Caesalpinia crista is a medicinal plant known for its anti-microbial, anti-inflammatory and anti-oxidant properties. The methanolic extracts of C. crista (MECC) significantly and dose dependently ameliorated aluminium-dependent cognitive impairment, AChE hyperactivity and oxidative stress in the hippocampus and in the frontal cortex of the rat brain. MECC also reduced neuronal injury induced by aluminium through the diminution of neuron loss and pyknosis in the CA1 and CA3 regions of the hippocampus. These results suggest that MECC protects against aluminium-induced behavioral alterations, cognitive function, oxidative stress and neuroinflammation in vivo. This plant may serve as a source of natural products having multiple functions and could be utilized as an anti-AD preparation.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains several medicinal compounds including caesaldekarin e, caesaalmins B, C, E, G, caesaalpins F, caesaalpins C-G, and caesaalpins MA-ME. These specific chemical constituents are categorized as bioactive phytochemicals found within the plant's organic structure. The presence of various caesaalmins and caesaalpins suggests a complex profile of secondary metabolites. Researchers study these diverse compounds to understand their potential therapeutic properties and biological activities. Such detailed chemical compositions are essential for determining the plant's overall medicinal value.

As discussed in a study, the CH2Cl2 extract of the seed kernels of Caesalpinia crista resulted in the isolation of seven new furanocassane-type diterpenes known as caesalpinins C-G (1-5) and norcaesalpinins D and E (6, 7). This extraction also yielded norcaesalpinins A-C (8-10) along with 11 known compounds such as caesalmin B, caesalmin G, and caesalpin F. These isolated diterpenes demonstrated significant dose-dependent inhibitory effects on the growth of Plasmodium falciparum FCR-3/A2 in vitro. The IC50 values for these compounds ranged from 90 nM to 6.5 microM. Among them, norcaesalpinin E (7) exhibited the most potent inhibitory activity with an IC50 of 90 nM.
