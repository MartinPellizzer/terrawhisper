Euphorbia prolifera

What's the taxonomical classification of Euphorbia prolifera?

Euphorbia prolifera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is classified under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic progression, it falls under the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Euphorbiaceae. Finally, the plant is identified within the genus Euphorbia, with its specific designation being the species proliferas.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euphorbia prolifera has a succulent, creeping growth habit characterized by cylindrical, fleshy stems that are segmented and often reddish or pale green in color.

These stems are typically leafless or possess vestigial, scale-like leaves that quickly drop off as the plant matures. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in axillary cyathia, which is the characteristic inflorescence structure of the Euphorbia genus. The plant lacks thorns or spines, relying instead on its thick, water-storing tissues to survive in arid environments.

Its branching pattern allows it to spread horizontally across the substrate, forming dense mats or mats of vegetation.

      What are the herbal preparations of Euphorbia prolifera?

      Euphorbia prolifera is prepared as a topical decoction, a topical paste, or an oral infusion depending on the intended therapeutic use. The plant material is often boiled in water to create a concentrated liquid used for cleansing skin ailments or washing wounds. For direct application to specific lesions, the succulent leaves can be crushed into a fresh, moist paste. Some traditional practices involve steeping the dried stems in hot water to produce a tea for internal consumption. Careful dosage is required because the milky latex released during preparation can be highly irritating to the skin and mucous membranes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Euphorbia prolifera can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, and temporary blindness if its milky white sap comes into contact with the eyes or sensitive tissues. This toxic latex contains diterpene esters that act as potent irritants upon contact with human skin. If the sap is accidentally ingested, it may lead to intense gastrointestinal distress including vomiting and abdominal pain. Prolonged exposure to the plant's fluids can result in redness, swelling, and blistering of the affected area. Protective gear should always be worn when handling this species to prevent accidental injury.
