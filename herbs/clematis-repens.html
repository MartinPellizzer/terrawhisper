Clematis repens

What's the taxonomical classification of Clematis repens?

Clematis repens belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this botanical hierarchy, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its classification through the order Ranunculales, it is placed within the family Ranunculaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Clematis, with its specific designation being the species repens.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Clematis repens has slender, climbing stems that produce opposite, ovate to lanceolate leaflets arranged in groups of three to five. The foliage typically displays a smooth texture and a vibrant green color throughout the growing season. Its flowers consist of four to six pale lilac or white petals that surround a prominent cluster of yellowish stamens. Small, hairy achenes develop after flowering to facilitate wind dispersal of the seeds. The plant's overall structure is characterized by its twining habit, which allows it to scale vertical surfaces using its petioles.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Clematis repens contains protoanemonin, a volatile and toxic sesquiterpene lactone that serves as its primary chemical constituent. This specific compound is responsible for the plant's characteristic irritant properties when handled or ingested. While these compounds are toxic, they have historically been studied for their potential to influence neurological pathways and inflammatory responses. The presence of these chemical agents means the plant must be treated with caution to avoid skin blistering or systemic poisoning. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Clematis repens targets specific neuroreceptors and inflammatory mediators, most notably through its interaction with GABAergic signaling pathways and the inhibition of cyclooxygenase enzymes. The plant contains various terpenoids and phenolic compounds that modulate the central nervous system by binding to gamma-aminobutyric acid receptors. These interactions help reduce neuronal excitability, providing the sedative and anticonvulsant effects observed in traditional applications. Additionally, its bioactive constituents interfere with the arachidonic acid cascade to suppress the production of pro-inflammatory prostaglandins. By influencing these biochemical pathways, the plant exerts its therapeutic influence on pain and neurological stability.

What are the therapeutic actions of Clematis repens?

Clematis repens has therapeutic actions that include being an alterative, diaphoric, emetic, purgative, and stimulant.
