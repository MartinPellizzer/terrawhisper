What to know about Vaccinium vitis-idaea (cranberry) before using it medicinally

Cranberry, scientifically known as Vaccinium vitis-idaea, is a small, round fruit that has been prized for its impressive health properties, which have been shown to improve urinary tract health and reduce the risk of certain infections.

While it's perhaps most familiar in its sweetened sauce form, cranberry also has a variety of culinary uses, from adding a tart flavor to salads and sauces to pairing with cheese and crackers as a snack. Horticulturally, cranberry is a low-growing shrub that thrives in acidic soils and is often cultivated for its fruit. From a botanical perspective, cranberry belongs to the heath family (Ericaceae) and is characterized by its small, evergreen leaves and distinctive blue-black berries.

With roots dating back to Native American tribes who revered it as a sacred food, cranberry has also played an important role in historical cuisine and culture, with records of its use dating back to the 17th century.
