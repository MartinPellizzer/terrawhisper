Cratoxylum cochinchinense

What's the taxonomical classification of Cratoxylum cochinchinense?

Cratoxylum cochinchinense belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is situated under the order Malpighiales. This specific plant is a representative of the family Hypericaceae, falling under the genus Cratoxylum, and is identified by the species name cochinchinense.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cratoxylum cochinchinense has a woody structure characterized by an erect trunk, lanceolate to elliptic leaves with entire margins, and small, actinomorphic flowers arranged in terminal panicles. The leaves are typically alternate and possess a coriaceous texture with prominent pinnate venation. Its bark is often reddish or brownish, containing a distinctive sap that serves as a source for traditional dyes. The fruit is a capsule that eventually dehisces to release small, winged seeds. These morphological features collectively define its classification within the Celastraceae family.

Within these habitats, the species thrives in areas with high humidity and consistent rainfall typical of equatorial climates. Its distribution is closely tied to specific ecological niches where the soil remains nutrient-rich and well-drained. What are the pharmacological activities of Cratoxylum cochinchinense?

This plant has demonstrated significant pharmacological activities including anti-inflammatory, cytotoxic, NF-κB inhibitory, anti-HIV, and anti-HIV-1 properties. Its ability to inhibit NF-κB signaling pathways contributes directly to its potent anti-inflammatory effects by reducing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Researchers have also identified specific cytotoxic capabilities that suggest potential applications in cancer research. Furthermore, the plant exhibits specialized anti-HIV and anti-HIV-1 activities which target viral replication processes. These diverse biological actions highlight the therapeutic potential of its various chemical constituents.

As discussed in a study, the stems of Cratoxylum cochinchinense from Vietnam yielded a new caged xanthone (1), a new prenylxanthone (2), seven known xanthones, and a known sterol glucoside. Certain substances derived from these components showed cytotoxicity toward HT-29 human colon cancer cells, with 3,6-di-O-acetyl-α-mangostin (8) reaching an ED50 of 1.0 μM, though it was inactive at a dose of 20 mg/kg in an in vivo hollow fiber assay. In a NF-κB p65 inhibition assay, 1,3,7-trihydroxy-2,4-diisoprenylxanthone (5) was the most potent with an IC50 of 2.9 μM. Furthermore, activity in a mitochondrial transmembrane potential assay was observed for compound 1 (IC50, 3.3 μM), compound 10 (IC50, 1.4 μM), α-mangostin (3, IC50, 0.2 μM), and 3,6-di-O-methyl-α-mangostin (11, IC50, 0.9 μM).

As discussed in a study, the root of Cratoxylum cochinchinense contains various bioactive metabolites such as xanthones, triterpene, and flavonoids. A series of xanthones (1-6) bearing geranyl and prenyl groups were isolated, including three new xanthones identified as compounds 1-3. For example, xanthone 1 demonstrated an IC50 of 0.38 μM and a KA of 2.4434 x 10^5 L·mol^-1. In comparison, xanthone 3 showed a 100-fold difference with an IC50 of 35.8 μM and a KA of 0.0002 x 10^5 L·mol^-1. The most potent compound, xanthone 1, acted as a competitive inhibitor with a Ki app of 0.1440 μM, a k3 of 0.1410 μM^-1s^-1, and a k4 of 0.0203 min^-1.
