Gentianella acuta

What's the taxonomical classification of Gentianella acuta?

Gentianeella acuta belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it falls into the subclass Magnoliidae and is further categorized under the order Gentianales. This plant is a member of the family Gentianaceae, which places it within the genus Gentianeella, and its specific designation is identified by the species name acuta.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Gentianeella acuta has upright, slender stems that typically reach heights of 5 to 20 centimeters and feature narrow, lanceolate leaves arranged in opposite pairs along the stem. The foliage is characterized by its acute, pointed tips and smooth margins. Small, bell-shaped flowers emerge from the leaf axils, displaying a pale blue to violet coloration. These blossoms are supported by short pedicels and possess a tubular corolla that flares slightly at the opening. The plant often develops in dense, low-growing clusters suitable for alpine or subalpine environments.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the alpine and subalpine regions of the European Alps, primarily found in countries such as Switzerland, Austria, Italy, and France. It typically occupies high-altitude habitats where rocky terrain and calcareous soils are prevalent. These populations are often scattered across mountain meadows and scree slopes above the treeline. The specific distribution is closely tied to the presence of certain soil types and the cool, moist microclimates of high elevations. Because of its niche environment, the species remains localized to specific mountain ranges rather than spreading across vast lowland areas.

What are the pharmacological activities of Gentianella acuta?

This plant has anti-diarrheal, anti-fibrotic, anti-myocardial fibrosis, antioxidant, and protective effects on cardiovascular health. Its bioactive compounds work to neutralize free radicals, providing a robust antioxidant defense for various body systems. These chemical properties also assist in reducing excessive tissue scarring through its anti-fibrotic mechanisms. Specifically, the plant demonstrates significant anti-myocardial fibrosis activity which helps maintain heart muscle integrity. Ultimately, these combined actions offer substantial protective effects on cardiovascular health by preventing structural damage to the heart.

As discussed in a study, Gentianella acuata is used for the treatment of diarrhea, hepatitis, arrhythmia, and coronary heart disease. A 70% EtOH extract of the whole plants demonstrated an inhibitory effect on intestine contraction tension in a mouse isolated intestine tissue model. Researchers obtained nineteen constituents from the plant, including five new compounds named gentiiridosides A (1), B (2), gentilignanoside A (3), (1R)-2,2,3-trimethyl-4-hydroxymethylcyclopent-3-ene-1-methyl-O-β-d-glucopyranoside (4), and (3Z)-3-hexene-1,5-diol 1-O-α-l-arabinopyranosyl(1→6)-β-d-glucopyranoside (5). Analysis of these compounds and thirteen other iridoid- and secoiridoid-type monoterpene constituents (7-10, 13-16, 18, 19, 21, 22, and 25) showed specific inhibitory effects on motility. Specifically, compound 5, secoiridoid-type monoterpenes 7, 10, 12-14, 16, and 17, and 7-O-9'-type lignans 31 and 32 displayed a significant inhibitory effect on contraction tension at 40 μM.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating cardiovascular disease, coronary heart disease, ischemic heart disease, myocardial ischemia/reperfusion injury, and cardiovascular disease induced by oxidative stress damage. Its bioactive compounds work to mitigate the cellular degradation caused by free radicals during heart-related ailments. By reducing oxidative stress, it helps protect the heart tissue from the inflammatory processes associated with ischemic heart disease. This medicinal property is particularly effective in managing the complex damage occurring during myocardial ischemia/reperfusion injury. Consequently, it serves as a potential natural intervention for various forms of coronary heart disease.

As discussed in a study, Gentianella acuta is an important Mongolian medicine in China that elicits protective effects on cardiovascular health. The main component bellidifolin was identified through liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry and network pharmacology as a treatment for ischemic heart disease. In H9c2 cell injury models induced by H2O2, bellidifolin pretreatment decreased the rate of apoptosis and the levels of LDH, CK, and ALT. It also increased the contents of SOD and GSH-Px in a dose-dependent manner to protect against cardiomyocyte injury. This component minimized H2O2-induced injury by activating the PI3K-Akt signal pathway and downregulating GSK-3β and p-Akt1/Akt1.

    What side effects this plant can have?
