Citrus medica

What's the taxonomical classification of Citrus medica?

Citrus medica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving further down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Sapindales and the family Rutaceae. Finally, its specific identity is defined by the genus Citrus and the species medica.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Citrus medica has a large, evergreen shrub or small tree morphology characterized by spreading branches, prominent spines, and large, leathery, oblong leaves with serrated margins. The foliage is often fragrant and possesses a distinctively coarse texture compared to other citrus species. Its flowers are white, solitary, and possess five petals accompanied by numerous stamens. The resulting fruit is typically large, globose or oval, and features a very thick, pitted rind containing numerous oil glands. This thick rind is a primary distinguishing morphological feature of the species.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant, commonly known as the citron, requires a subtropical climate with well-drained, slightly acidic soil and consistent warmth to thrive.

It is typically propagated through grafting or seed sowing in controlled environments before being transplanted into sunny locations. Because the species is sensitive to frost, growers often provide protection or use greenhouses in cooler regions. Regular irrigation is necessary to prevent soil moisture fluctuations, though overwatering must be avoided to prevent root rot.

Adequate sunlight and nutrient-rich soil ensure the development of its thick, fragrant rind.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains limonene, limonin, limonol, nomilinic acid, and a high vitamin C content. The terpene limonene provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the fruit. Limonin and its derivative limonol are bitter limonoids that contribute to the plant's potential medicinal effects. Nomilinic acid is another important bioactive compound found within the citrus structure. Additionally, the high vitamin C content supports immune function and overall cellular health.

According to a study published by "Dental research journal", the essential oil of Citrus medica contains limonene as its major chemical component at 92.31%. The research utilized the peels of Citrus medica for the analysis of its chemical composition. What side effects this plant can have?

Excessive intake may lead to a burning sensation in the digestive tract or significant abdominal cramping. Such symptoms are typically linked to the plant's potency when used in medicinal or concentrated forms. Monitoring dosage is essential to minimize the risk of these unpleasant digestive reactions.
