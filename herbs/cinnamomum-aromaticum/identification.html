Botanical Identification of Cinnamon (Cinnamomum aromaticum)

To identify Cinnamon (Cinnamomum aromaticum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Cinnamon (Cinnamomum aromaticum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Cinnamon, or Cinnamomum aromaticum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Laurales, family Lauraceae, genus Cinnamomum, and species Cinnamomum aromaticum. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the laurel family, which includes other aromatic trees and shrubs.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Cinnamon is Cinnamomum aromaticum, a species within the Lauraceae family. This name was first described by the botanist Carl Peter Thunberg in 1794. It is also known by several synonyms, including Cinnamomum zeylanicum and Cinnamomum verum, which reflect its historical use and regional variations.

Morphological Characteristics

Cinnamomum aromaticum is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree with a straight trunk and a dense, rounded crown. It has a shallow, fibrous root system that spreads widely near the surface. The stem is smooth and greyish-brown, often flaking off in strips. Leaves are alternate, elliptical, and dark green with a slightly serrated margin. The inflorescence is a terminal panicle, bearing small, greenish-white flowers. The fruit is a dark blue, fleshy drupe containing one or two elliptical seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cinnamon (Cinnamomum aromaticum) can be distinguished by its dark brown, fragrant bark that peels into narrow, rolled strips. It has opposite, lance-shaped leaves with a slightly hairy underside. The small, yellowish flowers are clustered, and the fruit is a capsule containing numerous tiny seeds. It is often confused with other cinnamon species but has a distinct aromatic profile and bark structure.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Cinnamon (Cinnamomum aromaticum) is native to Southeast Asia, particularly Sri Lanka and India. It thrives in tropical climates with high humidity and moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils and is typically found at low to mid-altitude regions, usually between 500 and 1,500 meters above sea level.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cinnamon trees bloom during late spring to early summer, producing small green flowers. Seed formation occurs in the following autumn, with the fruit maturing by winter. For identification purposes, the bark is typically harvested during late winter to early spring when the outer layer is dry and easily peels.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Cinnamomum aromaticum belongs to the Lauraceae family, closely related to species like Cinnamomum zeylanicum and Cinnamomum cassia. It shares morphological and genetic traits with other Cinnamomum species, differing primarily in leaf shape and bark texture. These relationships help in accurate identification and understanding its ecological and medicinal significance.
