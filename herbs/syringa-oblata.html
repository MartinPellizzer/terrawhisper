Syringa oblata

What's the taxonomical classification of Syringa oblata?

Syringa oblata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, this plant is further categorized under the order Lamiales. It is a prominent representative of the family Oleaceae, falling under the genus Syringa, with the specific designation of the species oblata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Syringa oblata has opposite, elliptic to ovate leaves that measure approximately 3 to 7 centimeters in length and possess entire margins with a slightly revolute edge. The foliage typically exhibits a dark green upper surface and a paler underside, often showing a smooth or slightly pubescent texture. Its inflorescences consist of dense, terminal panicles that bear small, tubular flowers which can range in color from white to pale lilac. The stems are woody and can develop a grayish bark as the shrub matures into its perennial form. These botanical features collectively define the compact, shrubby habit of this specific lilac species.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the mountainous regions of central and western China, specifically within the provinces of Sichuan, Gansu, and Shaanxi. It thrives in high-altitude environments where it typically inhabits rocky slopes and forest margins. The species is often found at elevations ranging from several hundred to over two thousand meters above sea level. Its distribution is largely influenced by the temperate, montane climates characteristic of these specific Asian highlands. These localized habitats provide the necessary moisture and soil conditions required for its survival.

What are the pharmacological activities of Syringa oblata?

This plant has antibacterial, anti-biofilm, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and angina-treating pharmacological activities. Its chemical constituents work to inhibit the growth of harmful bacteria and prevent the formation of protective biofilms. The plant also demonstrates significant antioxidant properties that help neutralize harmful free radicals in the body. Additionally, its anti-inflammatory effects assist in reducing swelling and pain associated with various conditions. These diverse medicinal qualities make it a valuable subject for treating cardiovascular issues such as angina.

As discussed in a study, Syringa oblata Lindl. serves as an effective anti-biofilm medicinal plant. The main bioactive ingredient in S. oblata is the flavonoid rutin, which inhibits Streptococcus suis biofilm formation. Research shows that the rutin content of S. oblata increases significantly under 50% ~ 60% (Z1) light intensity compared to natural (Z0) and 25% ~ 35% (Z2) light intensities. Integrated analysis revealed that 13 putative structural genes and a transcription factor bHLH were significantly up-regulated in Z1. Within this group, 3 putative structural genes, including 4CL1, CYP73A, and CYP75B1, significantly up-regulated rutin biosynthesis.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds including flavonoids such as rutin, as well as sesquiterpene derivatives like elemane-type sesquiterpene and cadinane. These diverse chemical constituents contribute significantly to the biological activity and therapeutic potential of the species. The presence of rutin, a specific flavonoid, provides antioxidant and vascular-protective properties. Furthermore, the sesquiterpene profile is characterized by specialized structures such as the elemane-type sesquiterpene and cadinane skeletons. Together, these bioactive molecules form a complex chemical fingerprint essential for the plant's medicinal functions.

As discussed in a study, Syringa oblata Lindl. is a medicinal plant that possesses effective broad-spectrum antibacterial activity. This plant can also inhibit Streptococcus suis biofilm formation. Researchers examined the content of rutin and flavonoids within the plant using four processing methods. One optimized method involved stir-frying Syringa oblata with vinegar to achieve the best rutin content.
