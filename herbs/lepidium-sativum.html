Lepidium sativum

What's the taxonomical classification of Lepidium sativum?

Lepidium sativuum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its higher taxonomic ranks, it falls under the order Brassicales and the family Brassicaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Lepidium, and its specific species epithet, sativum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lepidium sativuum has an erect, branched annual stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bears small, white, four-petaled flowers arranged in dense racemes. The leaves are highly variable, ranging from simple, linear shapes to deeply pinnately lobed forms with serrated margins. These leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and often become more complex as the plant matures. Small, circular, flat silicles serve as the fruit, containing tiny seeds that are characteristic of the Brassicaceae family. The entire plant exhibits a delicate, herbaceous structure that distinguishes it from many other garden annuals.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad region encompassing parts of Central and Western Asia, stretching from the Mediterranean basin through the Middle East into the Indian subcontinent. In North America, it is frequently grown in home gardens and small-scale agricultural plots. Its ability to thrive in diverse soil types allows it to spread across many different continents. What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, lignans, kaempferol, quercetin, and phenolic acids. The presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the organism. Specifically, the flavonols kaempferol and quercetin contribute to cellular protection and metabolic health. Additionally, the lignans within the plant offer unique phytoestrogenic activities that support hormonal balance. Together, these bioactive molecules form a complex chemical synergy that promotes various therapeutic benefits.

As discussed in a study, Lepidium sativum seeds contain a total phenol content in the order of 10 mg GAE/g. The seeds registered a flavonoid content of 3.09 ± 0.04 mg QE/g and a condensed tannins amount of 1.4 ± 0.22 mg/g. Regarding total sugar content, Lepidium sativum shows 67.86 ± 0.87 mg/g and is richer in mucilage with a content of 240 mg. Antioxidant activity using a DPPH test revealed IC50 values of 15.7 and 52.64 μg/mL for the EButOH and EAE extracts of Lepidium sativum, respectively. The extract showed significant hemagglutination at a value of 1/50 and a hemolysis rate of 34% for the crude extract.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, blood thinning effects, nausea, dizziness, and gastrointestinal upset in some individuals. The reduction in blood sugar levels may pose a risk to those already managing diabetes with medication. Due to its ability to slow blood clotting, it may increase the risk of bruising or bleeding for people on anticoagulants. Some users may also experience digestive issues such as nausea and general gastrointestinal upset after consumption. Additionally, certain people might report feeling lightheaded or experiencing dizziness as a reaction to the herb.

  • Hypoglycemia
