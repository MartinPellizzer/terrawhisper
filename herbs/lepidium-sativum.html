Lepidium sativum

What's the taxonomical classification of Lepidium sativum?

Lepidium sativuum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving through its higher taxonomic ranks, it falls under the order Brassicales and the family Brassicaceae. Finally, the plant is defined by its genus, Lepidium, and its specific species epithet, sativum.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Lepidium sativuum has an erect, branched annual stem that typically reaches heights of 30 to 60 centimeters and bears small, white, four-petaled flowers arranged in dense racemes. The leaves are highly variable, ranging from simple, linear shapes to deeply pinnately lobed forms with serrated margins. These leaves are arranged alternately along the stem and often become more complex as the plant matures. Small, circular, flat silicles serve as the fruit, containing tiny seeds that are characteristic of the Brassicaceae family. The entire plant exhibits a delicate, herbaceous structure that distinguishes it from many other garden annuals.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to a broad region encompassing parts of Central and Western Asia, stretching from the Mediterranean basin through the Middle East into the Indian subcontinent. It has since been widely naturalized in various temperate and subtropical climates across the globe due to human cultivation. In North America, it is frequently grown in home gardens and small-scale agricultural plots. Its ability to thrive in diverse soil types allows it to spread across many different continents. This wide distribution is primarily driven by its historical use as a culinary and medicinal herb.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Lepidium sativuum contains medicinal compounds distributed throughout its entire plant, including the seeds, leaves, and stems. What are the pharmacological activities of Lepidium sativum?

This plant has analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antibacterial, and hypoglycemic pharmacological activities that contribute to its diverse medicinal properties. The presence of specific phytochemicals allows it to reduce pain sensations and suppress systemic inflammation effectively. Its antioxidant capacity helps neutralize free radicals, thereby protecting cells from oxidative stress and damage. Additionally, the plant exhibits potent antibacterial effects by inhibiting the growth of various pathogenic microorganisms. Finally, its hypoglycemic properties assist in regulating blood glucose levels, making it relevant for metabolic health management.

As discussed in a study, Lepidium sativum seeds possess extraordinary antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, hypothermic, and analgesic properties. The n-hexane extract showed potent in vitro cytotoxic efficiency against 2 hepatocellular carcinoma cell lines, specifically HuH-7 and HEPG-2. This cytotoxicity was supported by the significant downregulation of EGFR and BCL2 gene expression levels and the upregulation of SMAD3, BAX, and P53 expression levels in both HuH-7 and HEPG2 cell lines. GC-MS analysis of the n-hexane extract identified 30 compounds, which mainly included hydrocarbons and terpenoids. These findings regarding the binding affinities against EGFR and BCL2 molecular targets reveal the potential use of Lepidium sativuum in the management of HCC.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse profile of medicinal compounds including flavonoids, lignans, kaempferol, quercetin, and phenolic acids. The presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the organism. Specifically, the flavonols kaempferol and quercetin contribute to cellular protection and metabolic health. Additionally, the lignans within the plant offer unique phytoestrogenic activities that support hormonal balance. Together, these bioactive molecules form a complex chemical synergy that promotes various therapeutic benefits.

As discussed in a study, Lepidium sativum seeds contain a total phenol content in the order of 10 mg GAE/g. The seeds registered a flavonoid content of 3.09 ± 0.04 mg QE/g and a condensed tannins amount of 1.4 ± 0.22 mg/g. Regarding total sugar content, Lepidium sativum shows 67.86 ± 0.87 mg/g and is richer in mucilage with a content of 240 mg. Antioxidant activity using a DPPH test revealed IC50 values of 15.7 and 52.64 μg/mL for the EButOH and EAE extracts of Lepidium sativum, respectively. The extract showed significant hemagglutination at a value of 1/50 and a hemolysis rate of 34% for the crude extract.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating diabetes mellitus, anemia, diarrhea, asthma, and abdominal cramps. Its nutritional profile helps regulate blood sugar levels in patients managing diabetes mellitus. The high iron content found in the seeds serves as an effective remedy for combating anemia. Additionally, the plant's medicinal properties can soothe the digestive tract to alleviate diarrhea and abdominal cramps. Some traditional practices also incorporate it to help manage respiratory issues like asthma.

What are the herbal preparations of this plant?

This plant can be prepared as an infusion, a tincture, a powder, a fresh herb for salads, or an oil. Making a warm infusion involves steeping the seeds or leaves in hot water to extract soluble compounds. For a more concentrated dose, a tincture can be crafted by soaking the plant parts in alcohol. The seeds are often ground into a fine powder to be added to foods or used in capsules. Eating the fresh herb directly in salads provides a peppery flavor and preserves its raw nutritional profile. Additionally, the seeds can be pressed to produce a nutritious oil used in culinary or topical applications.

What side effects this plant can have?

This plant can cause hypoglycemia, blood thinning effects, nausea, dizziness, and gastrointestinal upset in some individuals. The reduction in blood sugar levels may pose a risk to those already managing diabetes with medication. Due to its ability to slow blood clotting, it may increase the risk of bruising or bleeding for people on anticoagulants. Some users may also experience digestive issues such as nausea and general gastrointestinal upset after consumption. Additionally, certain people might report feeling lightheaded or experiencing dizziness as a reaction to the herb.

The main side effects this plant can have are shown in the list below.

  • Hypoglycemia
  • Blood thinning effects
  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Gastrointestinal upset
