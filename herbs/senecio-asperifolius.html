Senecio asperifolius

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Seneciio asperifolius has deeply lobed, lanceolate leaves characterized by a distinctly rough, scabrous texture caused by numerous small, stiff hairs across the surface. These leaves are typically arranged alternately along a sturdy, upright stem that can reach varying heights depending on the environment. The leaf margins are often irregular or coarsely toothed, contributing to their rugged appearance. Small, yellowish flower heads emerge in clusters from the terminal ends of the stems during the blooming season. The overall structure of the plant is robust and adapted to withstand physical contact or wind.

These populations are often found at elevations ranging from 3,500 to over 4,500 meters above sea level.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Senecio asperifolius can cause severe liver damage, kidney failure, and death in animals or humans if the plant is ingested due to the presence of toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids. These toxic compounds are metabolized by the body into reactive substances that destroy hepatic cells. Once the liver is compromised, the damage can become irreversible and lead to systemic organ failure. Symptoms of toxicity often include vomiting, loss of appetite, and jaundice as the liver ceases to function properly. Prolonged exposure to even small amounts of these alkaloids significantly increases the risk of developing fatal complications.
