What's the taxonomical classification of Euonymus kengmaensis?

Euonymus kengmaensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Its taxonomic hierarchy continues through the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, placing it under the order Celastrales. Within the family Celastraceae, it is situated in the genus Euonymus, ultimately reaching its specific designation as the species kengmaensis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Euonymus kengmaensis has opposite, elliptic to obovate leaves that measure roughly 2 to 5 centimeters in length and possess entire margins with acute apices.

The stems are typically woody and can exhibit a slightly quadrangular shape during early growth stages. Small, inconspicuous flowers are produced in clusters, often appearing in the axils of the foliage. These flowers eventually give way to distinctive capsule-like fruits that split open to reveal brightly colored arils.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Euonymus kengmaensis can cause severe skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, and potential toxicity if ingested. Contact with the plant's sap or foliage often results in contact dermatitis, characterized by redness, itching, and blistering on the skin. If any part of the plant is swallowed, it may trigger nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. While specific clinical studies on this exact species are limited, most members of the Euonymus genus contain alkaloids and glycosides that are toxic to humans and animals. Therefore, extreme caution should be exercised when handling or being near this plant to avoid accidental exposure.
