Hoya pandurata

What's the taxonomical classification of Hoya pandurata?

Hoya panduratata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Gentianales. This plant is a member of the family Apocynaceae, falling under the genus Hoya, and is specifically identified by the species name pandurata.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hoya pandurata has succulent, ovate to elliptical leaves that measure up to 10 centimeters in length and are characterized by a thick, waxy texture and a deep green color.

These leaves are arranged oppositely along trailing or climbing woody stems that allow the plant to function as an epiphyte. The inflorescence consists of umbels containing multiple star-shaped, fragrant flowers with a distinct corona. Each individual blossom features creamy white or pale green petals paired with a central, often colorful, fleshy corona.

The overall structure of the plant supports a robust, climbing habit suited for tropical environments.

        What are the herbal preparations of Hoya pandurata?

        Hoya pandurata is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion made from its leaves and stems to treat various ailments. These liquid extracts are often administered orally to address issues such as fever or digestive discomfort. Some traditional practitioners may also utilize the crushed plant material as a topical poultice applied directly to the skin.         What side-effects this plant can have?

        Hoya pandurata can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals due to the presence of unidentified secondary metabolites in its sap. When the plant's milky latex comes into contact with the skin, it may trigger redness, itching, or a burning sensation. If the sap is accidentally ingested, it could potentially lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or stomach upset. While there is limited clinical data regarding its toxicity in humans, it is best to avoid direct contact with the fluids. Always wash your hands thoroughly after handling the stems or leaves to prevent accidental exposure to the eyes or mouth.
