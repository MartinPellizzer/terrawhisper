Hoya pandurata

What's the taxonomical classification of Hoya pandurata?

Hoya panduratata belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is placed in the order Gentianales. This plant is a member of the family Apocynaceae, falling under the genus Hoya, and is specifically identified by the species name pandurata.

What are the therapeutic actions of Hoya pandurata?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, respiratory issues, and certain types of internal inflammation. Its extracts are often applied topically to address wounds or fungal conditions on the skin. In some traditional practices, the plant is prepared as a decoction to help alleviate symptoms of coughs and bronchitis. Researchers study its chemical properties to understand how it might combat bacterial growth. These medicinal applications rely on the unique bioactive compounds found within its leaves and stems.

What are the herbal preparations of Hoya pandurata?

Hoya pandurata is prepared primarily as a decoction or an infusion made from its leaves and stems to treat various ailments. These liquid extracts are often administered orally to address issues such as fever or digestive discomfort. Some traditional practitioners may also utilize the crushed plant material as a topical poultice applied directly to the skin. The specific method of preparation depends heavily on the intended therapeutic effect and the part of the plant being used. These different forms allow for versatile applications in folk medicine.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Hoya pandurata can cause skin irritation or allergic contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals due to the presence of unidentified secondary metabolites in its sap. When the plant's milky latex comes into contact with the skin, it may trigger redness, itching, or a burning sensation. If the sap is accidentally ingested, it could potentially lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or stomach upset. While there is limited clinical data regarding its toxicity in humans, it is best to avoid direct contact with the fluids. Always wash your hands thoroughly after handling the stems or leaves to prevent accidental exposure to the eyes or mouth.
