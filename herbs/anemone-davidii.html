What's the taxonomical classification of Anemone davidii?

Anemone davidii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the hierarchical structure of botanical classification, it falls under the order Ranunculales and the family Ranunculaceae. Finally, its specific taxonomic identity is defined by the genus Anemone and the species davidii.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Anemone davidii contains protoanemonin, which is a toxic lactone produced when the plant tissue is damaged or crushed. This specific compound is responsible for the plant's irritant properties and can cause skin inflammation upon contact. While it possesses certain bioactive qualities, these chemical components are generally considered harmful rather than therapeutic to humans. Most studies focus on the chemical defense mechanisms of the plant rather than its potential as a source of medicine. Consequently, the plant is primarily viewed as a source of chemical irritants instead of a medicinal resource.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Anemone davidii can cause skin irritation, redness, and blistering upon contact due to the presence of protoanemonin in its tissues. This toxic chemical is released when the plant parts are crushed or damaged. If the sap touches the skin, it may lead to painful dermatitis or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Ingestion of any part of the plant can result in severe gastrointestinal distress, including vomiting and abdominal pain. It is important to wear gloves when handling this species to prevent these adverse effects.
