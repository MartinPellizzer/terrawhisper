Psoralea corylifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Psoralea corylifolia?

Psoralea corylifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. At the class level, it is situated under Equisetopsida, falling into the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic lineage continues through the order Fabales and into the family Fabaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its genus, Psoralea, and its specific species epithet, corylifolia.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Psoralea corylifolia contains furanocoumarins such as psoralen, xanthotoxin, and bergapten, which serve as its primary bioactive constituents. These compounds are known for their photoactive properties and are frequently utilized in the treatment of skin disorders like vitiligo and psoriasis. In addition to these furanocoumarins, the plant seeds possess various alkaloids, flavonoids, and essential oils that contribute to its therapeutic profile. These secondary metabolites work together to exhibit anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant activities. The medicinal efficacy of the plant is largely dependent on the specific concentration and synergy of these chemical components.

What are the therapeutic actions of Psoralea corylifolia?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat vitiligo, psoriasis, eczema, and various other skin conditions characterized by pigmentation issues or inflammation. It contains psoralens, which are bioactive compounds that increase skin sensitivity to ultraviolet light. This process, known as PUVA therapy, helps stimulate melanocytes to produce pigment in depigmented areas. Beyond dermatological applications, it is also utilized in traditional medicine to manage digestive disorders and certain inflammatory conditions. Researchers continue to study its potential for managing autoimmune-related skin ailments through its immunomodulatory effects.

What are the herbal preparations of Psoralea corylifolia?

Psoralea corylifolia is prepared as a powder, decoction, oil, or topical paste derived from its dried seeds to utilize its medicinal properties. The seeds are frequently ground into a fine powder called Babchi, which can be encapsulated or mixed into various formulations. In traditional practices, the seeds are often boiled in water or milk to create a decoction for internal consumption. Additionally, the seeds can be processed into a concentrated oil used for direct application to the skin. These various forms are primarily employed to treat dermatological conditions such as vitiligo and psoriasis.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Psoralea corylifolia can cause severe skin irritation, blistering, and increased photosensitivity when the plant's essential oils come into contact with sunlight. These compounds, known as furanocoumarins, react with ultraviolet radiation to trigger a condition called phytophotodermatitis. Users may experience redness, swelling, or even painful chemical burns on the skin surface. If ingested in incorrect doses, the plant might also lead to gastrointestinal discomfort or toxicity. It is critical to avoid direct sun exposure after topical application to prevent these adverse reactions.
