What is Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea Corylifolia)?

Corylifolia Psoralea, also known as Psoralea corylifolia, is a flowering plant native to tropical regions of Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, including enhancing bone density and treating skin conditions.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Corylifolia Psoralea is Psoralea corylifolia. This plant belongs to the family Fabaceae, order Fabales, class Fabales, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. It is commonly known as "Brahmi" in India and "Kudzu" in parts of East Asia. The plant is also referred to as "Psoralea corylifolia" in botanical contexts and has regional names such as "Mogra" in some parts of South Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Corylifolia Psoralea is a shrub that typically grows to a height of 1 to 3 meters with a compact, bushy structure.

Its leaves are compound, consisting of 5 to 11 pairs of elliptic to ovate leaflets arranged alternately along the stem. The flowers are small, yellowish-green, and clustered in dense spikes at the leaf axils, emitting a faint, sweet aroma. The root system is fibrous and extensively branched, often extending deep into the soil.

The plant has a woody stem with a rough, greyish-brown bark, and its leaves are smooth on the upper surface with a slightly hairy underside.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Corylifolia Psoralea is native to Southeast Asia, specifically countries such as China, India, and parts of Indonesia.

It thrives in tropical and subtropical regions, often found in moist, well-drained soils. The plant prefers a warm climate with high humidity and adequate rainfall throughout the growing season. It is commonly found in forested areas, along riverbanks, and in cultivated fields.

Ecological conditions favoring its growth include partial shade to full sun exposure and moderate to high soil fertility.
