Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea corylifolia): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea Corylifolia)?

Corylifolia Psoralea, also known as Psoralea corylifolia, is a traditional Chinese medicinal plant belonging to the legume family, used for its therapeutic properties in treating various ailments.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Psoralea corylifolia, commonly known as Indian breadnut or Corylifolia Psoralea, is a leguminous plant belonging to the family Leguminosae (Fabaceae), characterized by its compound leaves, stipules, and pod-like fruits containing flat, oval seeds.

Common Names and Synonyms

Psoralea corylifolia, also known as Bajra, Babool, or Indian beech, is a leguminous plant native to the Indian subcontinent and widely used in traditional herbal medicine.

Plant Description and Morphology

Psoralea corylifolia is a shrub characterized by its compound leaves with 3-5 pairs of elliptical, dark green leaflets, pale yellow flowers arranged in dense clusters, and small, dark brown elliptical pods containing flat, kidney-shaped seeds.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Psoralea corylifolia is native to the tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, including countries such as India, China, Indonesia, and Malaysia, where it thrives in moist, fertile soils and humid climates.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The plant parts used medicinally in Corylifolia Psoralea include the seeds, which are the primary source of active compounds, and occasionally the leaves, though the seeds are most commonly utilized in traditional and modern herbal formulations.

Phytochemical Composition

Psoralea corylifolia contains a variety of bioactive compounds including psoralens, flavonoids, lignans, fatty acids, and essential oils, which contribute to its medicinal properties.

Pharmacological Properties

Psoralea corylifolia exhibits various pharmacological properties including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, and hepatoprotective effects, primarily attributed to its active constituents such as psoralens, flavonoids, and other phytochemicals.

Mechanisms of Action

Psoralea corylifolia exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through the modulation of oxidative stress, inflammatory pathways, and hormonal balance, with its active compounds such as psoralen and scopoletin interacting with cellular receptors and enzymes to promote tissue repair and immune regulation.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Corylifolia Psoralea is used to treat conditions such as arthritis, rheumatism, bone fractures, osteoporosis, diabetes, male infertility, leucorrhoea, skin diseases, and digestive disorders.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea corylifolia) typically involve drying the fruit pods, grinding them into a powder, or using them in decoctions, tinctures, or capsules as standardized herbal formulations.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Psoralea corylifolia for herbal use ranges from 1 to 3 grams per day, usually prepared as a decoction or powder, and should be taken with food to minimize gastrointestinal irritation.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in excess or over a prolonged period, Corylifolia Psoralea may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, and may also lead to increased body heat, irritability, and in some cases, liver toxicity.

Drug Interactions

Psoralea corylifolia may interact with medications that affect the immune system, such as immunosuppressants, and those that influence hormone levels, including corticosteroids and thyroid hormones, due to its potential phytoestrogenic and immunomodulatory effects.

Toxicity and Precautions

Psoralea corylifolia, when used in recommended doses and under proper guidance, is generally considered safe, but excessive intake or improper use may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, or interactions with certain medications, necessitating consultation with a qualified healthcare provider before use.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Corylifolia Psoralea is typically cultivated in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil, often in regions of South and Southeast Asia, requiring careful attention to soil pH and adequate sunlight for optimal growth.

Processing and Extraction

The processing of Corylifolia Psoralea involves drying the seeds under the sun or using low-temperature drying methods to preserve their active compounds and enhance their medicinal properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Psoralea corylifolia should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and effectiveness, with a typical shelf life of up to two years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research has shown that Psoralea corylifolia contains compounds such as psoralen and isoflavones, which may contribute to its traditional uses in enhancing bone health, reducing inflammation, and supporting immune function.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Psoralea corylifolia has been traditionally used in Chinese medicine for centuries to treat conditions such as impotence, infertility, and bone fractures, with its roots tracing back to the Tang Dynasty (618-907 AD).

Regulatory Status

In many countries, Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea corylifolia) is considered an herbal medicine and may be regulated under traditional herbal medicine frameworks, though specific regulations vary by region and jurisdiction.
