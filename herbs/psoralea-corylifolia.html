What is Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea Corylifolia)?

Corylifolia Psoralea, also known as Psoralea corylifolia, is a flowering plant native to tropical regions of Asia, commonly used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported health benefits, including enhancing bone density and treating skin conditions.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Corylifolia Psoralea is Psoralea corylifolia. This plant belongs to the family Fabaceae, order Fabales, class Fabales, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae. It is commonly known as "Brahmi" in India and "Kudzu" in parts of East Asia. The plant is also referred to as "Psoralea corylifolia" in botanical contexts and has regional names such as "Mogra" in some parts of South Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Psoralea corylifolia is a small shrub characterized by its compound leaves with 10-20 pairs of elliptic to ovate leaflets, pale yellow to yellowish-brown flowers arranged in dense clusters, and small, woody, lens-shaped fruits that turn brown when mature.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Psoralea corylifolia is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly India, Nepal, and Bhutan, and is commonly found in dry, rocky, and open habitats such as scrublands, grasslands, and forest margins.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, and seeds of Psoralea corylifolia are primarily used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Psoralea corylifolia contains a variety of bioactive compounds including flavonoids, alkaloids, saponins, and lignans, which contribute to its pharmacological properties and therapeutic potential in traditional herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Psoralea corylifolia exhibits pharmacological properties including immunomodulatory, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and hypoglycemic effects, supported by both traditional use and modern scientific research.

Mechanisms of Action

Psoralea corylifolia exerts its therapeutic effects primarily through the modulation of steroidogenesis via the activation of the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR), the inhibition of 17α-hydroxylase, and the upregulation of 17β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase, thereby influencing hormone synthesis and metabolism.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Psoralea corylifolia is traditionally used in herbal medicine to support bone health, enhance immunity, and treat conditions such as osteoporosis, rheumatism, and skin disorders due to its rich content of bioactive compounds like psoralen and flavonoids.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea corylifolia) typically involve drying the fruit or seeds, which are then ground into a powder, decocted in water, or used in formulations such as pills, capsules, or topical ointments.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Psoralea corylifolia is 3 to 6 grams per day, usually prepared as a decoction or powder, with specific formulations and dosages varying based on the intended therapeutic use and individual patient conditions.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Psoralea corylifolia is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, and interactions with certain medications, and should be avoided during pregnancy and lactation.

Drug Interactions

Psoralea corylifolia may interact with medications that affect the immune system, such as immunosuppressants, due to its potential immunomodulatory effects, and may also interact with anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents because of its possible influence on blood clotting mechanisms.

Toxicity and Precautions

Psoralea corylifolia is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as gastrointestinal discomfort, and excessive use can lead to toxicity, particularly in individuals with pre-existing health conditions or those taking other medications.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Psoralea corylifolia is typically cultivated in warm, tropical climates with well-drained soil, requiring minimal water and thriving in full sunlight, with harvesting occurring primarily during the dry season when the seeds are fully mature.

Processing and Extraction

Processing and extraction of Corylifolia Psoralea involve methods such as soaking, drying, and grinding the seeds to obtain a powder, which is then used in traditional formulations or further processed through solvent extraction to isolate active compounds like psoralen and bergapten.

Storage and Shelf Life

Corylifolia Psoralea should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 2 to 3 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence on Corylifolia Psoralea (Psoralea corylifolia) have primarily focused on its pharmacological properties, including its effects on bone density, immune modulation, and anti-inflammatory activities, with studies suggesting potential therapeutic applications in conditions such as osteoporosis and autoimmune disorders.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Psoralea corylifolia, also known as Corylifolia Psoralea, has been traditionally used in Chinese and Ayurvedic medicine for centuries to support bone health, enhance vitality, and treat conditions such as osteoporosis and rheumatism.
