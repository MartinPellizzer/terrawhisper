Photinia crassifolia

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Photinia crassifolia has an evergreen shrub architecture characterized by dense, spreading branches and thick, leathery leaves that measure up to 10 centimeters in length. The leaf margins are typically serrated, while the upper surfaces exhibit a deep green color and the undersides often appear paler. Small, white flowers emerge in terminal clusters called corymbs during the blooming season to attract pollinators. These floral structures eventually give way to small, fleshy drupes that turn dark red or black when fully ripe. The woody stems develop a textured bark as the plant matures, providing structural support for its heavy foliage.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical and temperate rainforests located along the eastern coast of Australia, stretching from northern New South Wales up through southeastern Queensland. It frequently inhabits various ecosystems including coastal heaths, littoral rainforests, and moist sclerophyll forests. Within these regions, it thrives in areas characterized by high humidity and consistent rainfall. The species is commonly found at various elevations, ranging from sea level to higher montane environments. Localized populations are often concentrated where soil moisture remains relatively high throughout the year.
