Valerian (Valeriana officinalis): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

What is Valerian (Valeriana Officinalis)?

Botanical Identity and Classification

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is a flowering plant in the family Caprifoliaceae, native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa, and is characterized by its aromatic roots and distinctive violet-like flowers.

Common Names and Synonyms

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is known by numerous common names and synonyms across different regions and cultures, including valerian root, all-heal, valerian herb, and officinal valerian.

Plant Description and Morphology

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is a flowering plant with a perennial rhizomatous root system, opposite basal leaves that are ovate to lanceolate in shape, and small, fragrant flowers arranged in dense spike-like clusters at the top of the stem.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is native to Europe, western Asia, and parts of North Africa, with its natural habitat primarily consisting of moist soils in woodlands, meadows, and along stream banks.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The roots, leaves, and flowers of Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) are the primary plant parts used medicinally.

Phytochemical Composition

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) contains a variety of phytochemical compounds, including essential oils, iridoids, flavonoids, and alkaloids, with the primary active constituents being valerenic acid, valerenol, and bornyl acetate.

Pharmacological Properties

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) exhibits pharmacological properties primarily attributed to its active compounds, including valepotriates, valerenic acid, and essential oils, which contribute to its sedative, anxiolytic, and sleep-inducing effects.

Mechanisms of Action

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) exerts its effects primarily through the modulation of GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) activity in the central nervous system, enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission, which contributes to its anxiolytic, sedative, and sleep-promoting properties.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Valerian is used to treat anxiety, insomnia, restlessness, nervous tension, and digestive issues such as colic and spasms.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Valerian is typically prepared as a tincture, infusion, or capsule, with the most common method involving steeping the dried root in alcohol or water to extract its active compounds.

Dosage and Administration

Valerian root is typically standardized to contain 0.2-0.5% essential oil, with a common dosage of 400-600 mg per day, divided into two or three doses, and may be taken with or without food depending on individual tolerance and formulation.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While Valerian is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, it may cause side effects such as dizziness, headaches, gastrointestinal discomfort, and in some cases, fatigue or vivid dreams, particularly when taken in high doses or over prolonged periods.

Drug Interactions

Valerian may interact with central nervous system depressants, anticoagulants, and other sedatives, potentially increasing drowsiness, dizziness, or impairing coordination.

Toxicity and Precautions

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild side effects such as drowsiness, headache, or gastrointestinal discomfort, and should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential risks.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Valerian is typically cultivated in well-drained soil with partial to full shade, requiring consistent moisture and a temperate climate for optimal growth.

Processing and Extraction

Valerian is typically processed by drying the roots of the plant after harvesting, which are then ground into a fine powder or used to make tinctures, teas, or capsules for medicinal use.

Storage and Shelf Life

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture, and its shelf life is typically 1 to 2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) has shown promising evidence for its efficacy in treating insomnia and anxiety, with studies indicating its active compounds, such as valerenic acid and bisabolol, may interact with the central nervous system to produce calming effects.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Valerian has been traditionally used for its calming effects, particularly to treat insomnia, anxiety, and nervous disorders, with historical records dating back to ancient Greek and Roman times.

Regulatory Status

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is classified as a dietary supplement in the United States and is regulated by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994.
