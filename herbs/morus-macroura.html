Morus macroura

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Morus macroura has deciduous leaves that are typically ovate to elliptical with serrated margins and a variable base, growing on woody stems that support small, fleshy aggregate fruits. The leaf texture can range from smooth to slightly rugose, often exhibiting a distinct venation pattern on the underside. Its flowering structures consist of small, inconspicuous inflorescences that develop into dark, berry-like syncarps. The bark of the mature plant is generally grayish and develops shallow fissures as it ages. These morphological characteristics distinguish it within the Moraceae family through its specific leaf shape and fruit structure.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the subtropical and tropical regions of Southeast Asia, specifically spanning across parts of China, Vietnam, and Thailand. Within these regions, it is often found growing in forest edges or near riverbanks where soil moisture is consistent. The distribution is somewhat fragmented, appearing in specific ecological niches rather than across a continuous landmass. What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Morus macroura targets specific proteins including alpha-glucosidase, alpha-amylase, and various inflammatory mediators such as cyclooxygenase-2 and nitric oxide synthase. The bioactive flavonoids and polyphenols found within the plant act as competitive inhibitors to these enzymes to regulate glucose metabolism. By suppressing alpha-glucosidase activity, the plant extracts effectively slow down the breakdown of carbohydrates into glucose. Additionally, the inhibition of cyclooxygenase-2 pathways helps reduce the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines in the body. These diverse molecular interactions contribute to the plant's documented antidiabetic and anti-inflammatory medicinal properties.

      What are the herbal preparations of Morus macroura?

      Morus macroura is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a tincture to utilize its medicinal properties. The bark and leaves are most commonly simmered in water to extract bioactive compounds for internal consumption.       What side-effects this plant can have?

      Morus macroura can cause allergic skin reactions, digestive upset, and potential toxicity if consumed in excessive quantities. Contact with the plant's sap or leaves may trigger contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals, resulting in redness or itching. Ingesting parts of the plant can lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramping. There is limited clinical data regarding long-term systemic effects, so caution is advised when handling or consuming it.
