Botanical Identification of Alfalfa (Medicago sativa)

To identify Alfalfa (Medicago sativa) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Alfalfa (Medicago sativa) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Alfalfa, or Medicago sativa, belongs to the plant kingdom, specifically within the flowering plants. It is classified under the order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Medicago, and species Medicago sativa. This classification reflects its role as a legume with a wide range of medicinal and agricultural uses.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Alfalfa is Medicago sativa, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It belongs to the legume family and is widely recognized as a key herb in traditional medicine. Synonyms include Hedysarum sativum and Medicago sativa var. trifida. It is also known by common names such as lucerne, alfalfa, and medick.

Morphological Characteristics

Alfalfa is a perennial legume with a taproot system that can extend deep into the soil. Its stems are erect and branching, often hairy. Leaves are trifoliate, with each leaflet being ovate and slightly hairy. The inflorescence is a dense cluster of small, yellow flowers. Each flower is surrounded by two pinnae, and the fruit is a pod containing several round seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Alfalfa can be distinguished by its trifoliate leaves with three oval leaflets, often with a white midrib. It has yellow flowers in dense clusters and a taproot with numerous lateral roots. These features help differentiate it from similar plants like clover or vetch. Its distinctive leaf shape and flowering pattern are key identification markers.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Alfalfa is native to Southwestern Asia and the Mediterranean region. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It is commonly found at altitudes between 200 and 2,000 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Alfalfa blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July, depending on the climate. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall, around August to September. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the flowering stage, when the plant is most recognizable and the leaves are at their most characteristic shape.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Alfalfa (Medicago sativa) is closely related to species within the genus Medicago, such as Medicago truncatula. It belongs to the family Fabaceae, sharing traits like compound leaves and legume pods. These relationships help in understanding its ecological and medicinal significance.
