Rinorea subintegrifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Rinorea subintegrifolia?

Rinorea subintegrifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is positioned in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Violaceae. Finally, its specific classification is defined by the genus Rinorea and its unique species designation, subintegrifolia.

What are the therapeutic actions of Rinorea subintegrifolia?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including skin infections, stomach aches, and inflammatory conditions. Local communities often apply its extracts to heal wounds or manage dermatological issues. The leaves or roots may also be prepared to alleviate digestive discomfort and abdominal pain. Some traditional practices involve using the plant to mitigate symptoms of fever or general bodily inflammation. These medicinal applications rely on the specific chemical properties found within its biological structure.

What are the herbal preparations of Rinorea subintegrifolia?

Rinorea subintegrifolia is prepared primarily as aqueous decoctions or infusions derived from its leaves and stems for medicinal use. These liquid extracts are often administered orally to harness the plant's bioactive compounds. In some traditional practices, the crushed plant material is also utilized in topical applications such as poultices. Some preparations involve the use of ethanol-based tinctures to increase the solubility of specific phytochemicals. These diverse methods allow for the extraction of different chemical profiles depending on the intended therapeutic outcome.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Rinorea subintegrifolia can cause adverse physiological reactions such as skin irritation, gastrointestinal distress, or allergic dermatitis if ingested or applied topically. There is currently a lack of extensive clinical data to definitively categorize the specific toxicity levels for human consumption. Some researchers suggest that alkaloids present in the plant family may interfere with metabolic processes if not properly processed. Contact with the leaves or sap might trigger localized redness or swelling in sensitive individuals. Because its chemical profile is not fully mapped, any contact should be approached with caution to prevent unknown systemic reactions.
