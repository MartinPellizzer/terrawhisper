Ceriops tagal

What's the taxonomical classification of Ceriops tagal?

Ceriops tagal belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The taxonomic progression continues through the order Malpighiales and the family Rhizophoraceae, ultimately placing the plant within the genus Ceriops. At the most specific level of classification, it is identified as the species tagal.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Ceriops tagal has a globular to cylindrical stem that typically reaches heights of up to 30 centimeters and features a surface covered in prominent, longitudinal ribs. These ribs are often somewhat irregular and are punctuated by small, dark areoles that contain short, inconspicuous spines. The plant possesses a woody central core that provides structural support to its fleshy, water-storing body. Small, colorful flowers emerge from the upper portions of the stem during its blooming period. The overall growth habit remains compact, often forming small clusters as it matures.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the dry, rocky regions of southern Madagascar. It is primarily found in the spiny thickets that characterize the island's southwestern arid zones. Within these habitats, the species colonizes limestone outcrops and sandy soils where drainage is rapid. Localized populations are often restricted to specific microclimates that provide necessary seasonal moisture. What are the pharmacological activities of Ceriops tagal?

This plant has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antitumor, cytotoxic, and nematicidal pharmacological activities. Its antioxidant properties help neutralize harmful free radicals within biological systems. The plant also demonstrates significant cytotoxicity and antitumor activity, which suggests a potential role in inhibiting the growth of cancer cells. Furthermore, its anti-inflammatory effects can assist in reducing swelling and pain caused by various physiological triggers. Finally, the presence of nematicidal properties indicates that it can be used to combat parasitic worms.

As discussed in a study, the endophytic fungus Penicillium herquei JX4 hosted in Ceriops tagal was analyzed for anti-inflammatory activities using an OSMAC strategy. Two new pinophol derivatives, pinophol H(1) and pinophol I(2), were isolated from the fungus. These compounds were evaluated for their inhibitory activities against nitric oxide (NO) production induced by lipopolysaccharide (LPS) in mouse macrophage RAW264.7 cells. The results showed that compound 1 had significant inhibitory activity on NO production. This activity was measured with an IC50 value of 8.12 μmol·L-1.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including betulonic acid, lupeol, betulin, pentacyclic triterpenoids, and triterpenes. These specific chemical constituents are categorized as triterpenes, which are a large class of naturally occurring compounds. Among these, the pentacyclic triterpenoids provide significant biological activity that is often studied for therapeutic potential. Betulin and its derivative, betulonic acid, are particularly notable for their presence within the plant's chemical profile. Lupeol further contributes to the complex medicinal properties found in the plant's structure.

As discussed in a study, Ceriops tagal contains a variety of medicinal compounds including pentacyclic triterpenoids such as lupeol (1), betunal (2), betulin (3), lupenone (4), betulonaldehyde (5), betulonic acid (7), methyl 3-acetoxy-27-O-(3,4-dihydroxy-E-cinnamoyl)-20(29)-lupen-28-oaate (8), and beta-amyrin. The plant also contains phenylpropanoid esters, fatty acids and their derivatives, benzamide, indole derivatives, and palmitic acid. Among the twenty-four compounds identified or isolated, only five constituents, which are 1, 3, 6, 7, and palmitic acid, were previously isolated from Ceriops tagal. One abundant column fraction, CC-9-18, contained 145 mg of material. The lupeol (1) found in the plant showed an LC50 value of 0.061 after 72 h exposure.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for the treatment of human liver cancer, human cervical carcinoma, hyperglycemia, and specific cancer cell lines such as HL-60 and MCF-7. Scientific investigations have demonstrated that its bioactive compounds exhibit significant cytotoxic properties against these various malignant cells. The extracts show potential in inhibiting the growth of leukemia cells represented by the HL-60 line and breast cancer cells found in the MCF-7 line. Furthermore, research indicates its efficacy in targeting human cervical carcinoma and human liver cancer cells. Beyond its anti-cancer potential, the plant is also studied for its ability to manage hyperglycemia.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, redness, and allergic contact dermatitis if the sap or spines come into direct contact with human skin. The presence of sharp spines poses a significant risk of physical puncture wounds and localized swelling upon contact. Some individuals may experience an inflammatory reaction that leads to itching or a burning sensation in the affected area. If the sap accidentally enters the eyes, it can lead to intense irritation or temporary corneal discomfort. Proper protective gear should be worn to prevent these adverse physical and dermatological reactions.
