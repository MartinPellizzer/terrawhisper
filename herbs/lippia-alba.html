What to know about Lippia alba (lemon verbena) before using it medicinally

Lippia alba, commonly known as lemon verbena, is a versatile herb that boasts a multitude of health benefits, including its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, which have been shown to reduce stress, improve digestion, and boost the immune system.

Cultivated for its fragrant leaves and flowers, Lippia alba is a popular ornamental plant in warm climates, valued for its compact growth and tolerance of dry conditions. Botanically, Lippia alba belongs to the Verbenaceae family, with a distinctive, lemon-scented foliage and small white or yellow flowers.

The herb has a long history of use in traditional medicine, dating back to the ancient Mayans and Aztecs, who revered it for its culinary and medicinal properties.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Lippia alba.

What are the medicinal properties of Lippia alba?

Lippia alba helps with anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues due to its calming and carminative properties. Its essential oil is used to treat nausea, headaches, and fever. Traditional medicine uses it to treat colds, coughs, and bronchitis.

The active constituents of Lippia alba, including limonene, beta-pinene, and beta-caryophyllene, contribute to its medicinal properties. These compounds have been shown to have anxiolytic, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects. They also possess antioxidant and antispasmodic properties.

The parts of Lippia alba used for medicinal purposes are the leaves, stems, and essential oil. The leaves are used to make teas and infusions, while the stems are used to prepare tinctures. The essential oil is obtained through steam distillation and used topically or internally.

Improper use of Lippia alba can cause side effects such as allergic reactions, photosensitivity, and digestive issues. High doses of the plant's essential oil can cause respiratory problems and interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners and diabetes medications.

When using Lippia alba medicinally, precautions should be taken to avoid interactions with other medications and health conditions. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult a healthcare professional before using the plant, and individuals with allergies or sensitive skin should perform a patch test before using the essential oil.

What are the horticulural aspects of Lippia alba?

Lippia alba grow best in full sun to partial shade with temperatures between 64°F to 75°F (18°C to 24°C). Well-draining soil with a pH between 6.1 to 7.8 is ideal for this plant. It requires moderate watering, avoiding waterlogged soil.

Planting tips for Lippia alba include choosing a location with good air circulation, which can prevent fungal diseases. Plant the shrub 2-3 feet away from other plants to prevent overcrowding. Water thoroughly after planting and fertilize with a balanced fertilizer once a month during the growing season.

To harvest Lippia alba, pick the leaves and flowers in the morning, after the dew has evaporated. Pinch or cut off individual stems to encourage new growth and prevent the plant from flowering. Harvesting regularly also helps control the plant's height and encourages bushy growth. Harvesting can be done throughout the year.

Lippia alba is susceptible to pests such as aphids, whiteflies, and mealybugs, which can be controlled with insecticidal soap or neem oil. Fungal diseases like root rot and leaf spot can be managed with fungicides. Regular pruning and good air circulation can help prevent pest and disease issues.

What are the botanical characteristics of Lippia alba?

Lippia alba, commonly known as Mexican Oregano or Lemon Verbena, is a perennial herb that exhibits several distinct botanical characteristics.

It belongs to the Verbenaceae family and is characterized by its slender stems, which can grow up to 30 inches in height, and are adorned with narrow, linear leaves that are typically 2-4 cm in length and have a distinctive citrusy aroma. The leaves are often arranged oppositely on the stem and have a pointed tip, giving them a lance-shaped appearance.

The plant produces small, white or yellowish flowers that are clustered in axillary spikes, each containing four petals with a distinct notch at the apex. These flowers are highly attractive to pollinators and produce tiny seeds that are dispersed by wind and animals.

Lippia alba is also notable for its ability to spread quickly through underground stolons, forming dense clumps of stems and leaves that can choke out other vegetation in ideal conditions.
