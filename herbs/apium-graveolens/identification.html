Botanical Identification of Celery (Apium graveolens)

To identify Celery (Apium graveolens) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Celery belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Apium, and species Apium graveolens. These classifications place it within the carrot family, highlighting its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of celery is Apium graveolens, a member of the Apiaceae family. It was first described by Carl von Linné in 1753. Common synonyms include Apium dulce and Apium petroselinum. It is known by various regional and historical names, reflecting its widespread use in traditional herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

Celery is an annual or biennial herb with a taproot system. Its stem is cylindrical and hollow, often ribbed. Leaves are alternate, deeply lobed, and have a pungent odor. The inflorescence is a compound umbel, with small white flowers. The fruit is a dry, two-lobed schizocarp, and seeds are small, brown, and aromatic.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Celery can be distinguished by its hollow, ridged stems and alternate, smooth, light green leaves with serrated edges. Its umbels of small white flowers and aromatic, juicy stalks set it apart from similar species like parsley and celeriac. The strong, distinctive odor when crushed is another key identifier.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Celery is native to the Mediterranean region and parts of southern Europe. It thrives in temperate climates with moderate rainfall. It prefers well-drained, loamy soils. It typically grows at elevations between 0 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Celery blooms from June to August, producing small, green, umbrella-like flower clusters. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, the plant is typically harvested in early summer before flowering, when the stalks and leaves are most developed.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Celery belongs to the Apiaceae family, sharing close relations with other aromatic herbs like parsley and dill. Its closest species include Apium nodiflorum and Apium bermejoi, which are also found in similar habitats. These species exhibit similar morphological and genetic traits, reflecting their evolutionary proximity within the genus Apium.
