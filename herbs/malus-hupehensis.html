Malus hupehensis

What's the taxonomical classification of Malus hupehensis?

Malus hupehensis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is classified under the subclass Magnoliidae and falls within the order Rosales. This plant is a member of the family Rosaceae, which is further narrowed down to the genus Malus, and is specifically identified by the species name hupehensis.

What are the pharmacological activities of Malus hupehensis?

This plant has antihyperglycemic and hypoglycemic properties that function by preventing diabetes and stabilizing blood glucose levels. These pharmacological activities are largely driven by its ability to act as an α-glucosidase inhibitory agent. By inhibiting this specific enzyme, the plant helps slow down the digestion of carbohydrates in the digestive tract. This mechanism effectively limits the postprandial rise of sugar in the bloodstream. Such multifaceted actions make it a significant subject for natural diabetes management research.

As discussed in a study, Malus hupehensis is an edible and medicinal plant that possesses significant antioxidant and hypoglycemic activity. Its polysaccharides, referred to as MHP, can be obtained with a yield of 1.68% and a purity of 89.6% using an ultrasonic-assisted aqueous two-phase system. In vitro activity analysis demonstrated that MHP shows a significant antioxidant capacity. Furthermore, MHP exhibits an inhibition activity of α-glucosidase and the advanced glycation end products (AGEs) formation in the BSA/Glc reaction model. These findings regarding the antihyperglycemic mechanism suggest that Malus hupehensis has broad application prospects in functional foods, healthcare products, and pharmaceuticals.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds phlorizin, phloretin, huperoide B, and huperoide C. These specific chemical constituents are often studied for their unique biological activities within various therapeutic contexts. Phlorizin and phloretin are known dihydrochalcones that may influence glucose metabolism. The compounds huperoide B and huperoide C represent distinct elements of the plant's chemical profile. Researchers investigate these substances to understand their potential roles in pharmacology and medicine.

As discussed in a study, eight undescribed phenylpropanoid-dihydrochalcone hybrids including (+)- and (-)-malahupin A, (+)- and (-)-malahupin B, (±)-malahupin C, malahupinosides A and B, and 7‴-epi-malahupinoside B were isolated from the leaves of Malus hupehensis. Two known compounds, phloretin and phlorizin, were also identified from the plant. Compounds (+)-malahupin B and malahupioside B showed weak inhibition activities against nitric oxide production in murine RAW264.7 macrophages with IC50 values of 36.7 and 27.0 μM, respectively. Additionally, (+)- and (-)-malahupin A, and (+)- and (-)-malahupin B exhibited significant α-glucosidase inhibitory activity with IC50 values of 22.5, 19.1, 19.2, and 17.4 μM, respectively.

What health problems is this plant used for?

This plant is used for treating diabetes, inflammation, and various digestive disorders. Researchers have investigated its potential to help manage blood sugar levels in diabetic patients. The plant also contains bioactive compounds that may reduce swelling and systemic inflammation in the body. Furthermore, it is often employed in traditional practices to soothe stomach discomfort and improve gastrointestinal function. These therapeutic properties make it a subject of interest for studying natural metabolic regulators.

As discussed in a study, three dihydrochalcone-derived polyphenols known as huperolides A-C (1-3) were isolated from the leaves of Malus hupehensis. Huperolide A features a new type of carbon skeleton, while huperolides B and C are atropisomer pairs isolated from natural sources for the first time. To evaluate antihyperglycemic effects, the inhibitory activities of these compounds against α-glucosidase were tested. Among the compounds, phlorizin (4), 3-hydroxyphloridzin (5), 3-O-coumaroylquinic acid (12), and β-hydroxypropiovanillone (15) showed significant concentration-dependent inhibitory effects. These specific substances might be responsible for the antihyperglycemic effect of Malus hupehensis and could lead to the discovery of drugs against diabetes.
