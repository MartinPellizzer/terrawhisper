Chrysophyllum subnudum

What's the taxonomical classification of Chrysophyllum subnudum?

Chrysophyllum subnudum belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is further categorized under the subclass Magnoliidae and the order Ericales. This plant is a member of the family Sapotaceae, falling under the genus Chrysophyllum, and is specifically identified by its species name, subnudum.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Chrysophyllum subnudum contains bioactive secondary metabolites including flavonoids, tannins, saponins, and terpenoids that exhibit therapeutic potential. These chemical constituents are primarily concentrated in the bark and leaves of the species. Research suggests that the presence of these phenolic compounds may provide antioxidant and antimicrobial properties. Such medicinal qualities are often utilized in traditional ethnobotanical practices to treat various ailments. Further pharmacological investigation is required to fully isolate and quantify these specific bioactive molecules.

What are the therapeutic actions of Chrysophyllum subnudum?

Chrysophyllum subnudum has medicinal properties used primarily to treat skin ailments, digestive issues, and inflammatory conditions through its bioactive phytochemical constituents. The plant contains various secondary metabolites such as tannins and flavonoids that contribute to its antimicrobial and antioxidant activities. These compounds help in reducing oxidative stress and preventing cellular damage within the body. Local traditional practices often involve using decoctions or infusions from the leaves and bark to manage localized infections. Furthermore, its astringent qualities can assist in healing wounds and soothing irritated tissues.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat diarrhea, stomach aches, skin infections, and inflammatory conditions. The medicinal properties of its various parts are often applied to soothe gastrointestinal distress through traditional preparations. Extracts from the leaves or bark may also be used topically to address wounds or skin irritations. These therapeutic applications are rooted in the ethnobotanical practices of regions where the species grows naturally. Local healers rely on these specific components to manage a variety of common ailments.
