10 Precautions To Take When Using Gaultheria Procumbens (Wintergreen)

Gaultheria procumbens has some precautions to consider before using it medicinally, such as excessive consumption leading to kidney damage due to its methyl salicylate content, similar to aspirin.

High doses can also cause bleeding disorders, interact with blood thinners, and exacerbate stomach ulcers.

Taking wintergreen without these considerations may result in headaches, nausea, and gastrointestinal upset, similar to the effects of overusing aspirin.

This article explains in details the 10 most important precautions to take when using Gaultheria procumbens medicinally.

1. Dispose of properly afterwards

When using Gaultheria procumbens medicinally, it's important to take under professional supervision due to its potential for interactions with certain medications and blood thinners.

The methyl salicylate content in wintergreen can cause adverse effects when combined with anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, or NSAIDs, increasing the risk of bleeding complications.

Additionally, wintergreen's stimulant properties may exacerbate conditions such as hypertension, asthma, or stomach ulcers if not monitored by a healthcare professional.

9. Dispose of properly afterwards

When using Gaultheria procumbens medicinally, it's important to store in cool conditions.

This precaution is crucial because wintergreen contains methyl salicylate, a compound similar to aspirin that can degrade or oxidize when exposed to heat, light, or humidity. Prolonged exposure can lead to a reduction in potency and effectiveness, as well as the formation of harmful compounds that may cause adverse reactions or interactions with other medications.

By storing wintergreen in cool conditions, users can ensure its medicinal properties remain intact and effective for optimal use.

10. Dispose of properly afterwards

When using Gaultheria procumbens medicinally, it's important to keep away from children.

This precaution is crucial because the plant contains methyl salicylate, a compound similar to aspirin that can be toxic in large amounts. If ingested by a child, even a small amount of wintergreen oil or extract can cause nausea, vomiting, and stomach pain.

Moreover, children's developing bodies may not be able to metabolize the substance effectively, increasing the risk of serious harm if not treated promptly.
