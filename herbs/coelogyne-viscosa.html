Coelogyne viscosa

What's the taxonomical classification of Coelogyne viscosa?

Coelogyne viscosa belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it falls under the order Asparagales and is a member of the family Orchidaceae. Finally, it is identified by its specific genus, Coelogyne, and its unique species designation, viscosa.

What are the therapeutic actions of Coelogyne viscosa?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various inflammatory conditions, skin infections, and digestive disorders in traditional medicinal practices. Its viscous properties are often utilized to soothe localized irritations and promote healing in wounded tissue. Some practitioners apply the plant to manage symptoms of topical fungal infections or minor dermatological ailments. Additionally, certain preparations of the plant are ingested to address gastrointestinal distress and internal swelling. These applications rely on the unique chemical composition of its sticky secretions to provide relief.

What are the herbal preparations of Coelogyne viscosa?

Coelogyne viscosa is prepared as a decoction, a powder, or a topical paste for various medicinal applications. Practitioners often boil the plant parts in water to extract active compounds for internal consumption. The dried material can also be finely ground into a powder to be mixed with honey or other substances. For external ailments, the plant is crushed into a thick paste to be applied directly to the skin. These diverse forms allow for flexible administration depending on the specific ailment being treated.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Coelogyne viscosa can cause localized skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or respiratory distress if its pollen or sap is inhaled or touched. While specific clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, many epiphytic orchids contain alkaloids or resins that may trigger hypersensitivity in sensitive individuals. Contact with the plant's sticky exudate can lead to redness, itching, or a burning sensation on the skin. If ingested, the plant may cause gastrointestinal upset, though its systemic toxicity levels are not fully documented in medical literature. It is advisable to handle this species with care to avoid unexpected dermatological reactions.
