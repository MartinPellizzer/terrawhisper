Mango (Mangifera indica): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Mango (Mangifera indica) is a tropical fruit-bearing tree native to South Asia, known for its sweet, juicy fruit and a variety of traditional medicinal uses. It has been used for centuries to improve digestion, reduce inflammation, and support overall health.

What is Mango (Mangifera Indica)?

Mango (Mangifera indica) is a tropical fruit-bearing tree native to South and Southeast Asia, widely cultivated for its edible fruit and used in traditional herbal medicine for its purported health benefits.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Mango (Mangifera indica) is a tropical fruit-bearing tree belonging to the Anacardiaceae family, characterized by its large, edible, yellow-orange fruits with a fibrous pulp and a single large seed.

Common Names and Synonyms

Common Names and Synonyms include Mangifera indica, mango, mangoe, mango tree, and in various regional languages such as "aloo" in Hindi, "amrood" in Punjabi, "mangosteen" in some Southeast Asian contexts, and "mangga" in Indonesian and Filipino.

Plant Description and Morphology

The mango tree is a tropical evergreen tree that typically grows 10-40 meters tall, with a broad, spreading canopy, smooth bark that is gray to brown in color, and compound leaves that are alternate, ovate to elliptic in shape, and have a glossy green upper surface.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Mango (Mangifera indica) is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly India, and has been cultivated for thousands of years, with its natural range extending through Southeast Asia, including countries like Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The leaves, bark, roots, and fruit of the mango tree are commonly used medicinally in traditional herbal practices.

Phytochemical Composition

The phytochemical composition of Mango (Mangifera indica) includes a variety of bioactive compounds such as polyphenols, carotenoids, flavonoids, and enzymes like mangiferin and amylase, which contribute to its medicinal properties and health benefits.

Pharmacological Properties

Mango (Mangifera indica) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and hypoglycemic activities, which are attributed to its rich content of bioactive compounds such as polyphenols, flavonoids, and enzymes like mangiferin.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of mango (Mangifera indica) in herbal medicine involve its bioactive compounds such as polyphenols, carotenoids, and enzymes, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and immunomodulatory effects through various cellular pathways.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Mango is used to alleviate symptoms of digestive disorders, reduce inflammation, support skin health, manage diabetes, enhance immunity, relieve respiratory conditions, and promote overall vitality.

Preparation Methods and Forms

The preparation methods and forms of Mango (Mangifera indica) in herbal medicine include fresh fruit consumption, drying for powder or tea, making syrups, and using extracts or tinctures, each method influencing the potency and bioavailability of its active compounds.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of mango (Mangifera indica) for herbal use typically ranges from 100 to 500 mg of dried fruit powder per day, divided into two to three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic effect.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While mango is generally safe when consumed in moderation, excessive intake may lead to digestive issues such as diarrhea, nausea, or abdominal discomfort due to its high fiber and natural sugar content.

Drug Interactions

Mango (Mangifera indica) may interact with certain medications, including anticoagulants, diabetes medications, and drugs metabolized by the liver, potentially increasing the risk of side effects or reducing the effectiveness of the medication.

Toxicity and Precautions

While mango is generally safe when consumed in moderate amounts, excessive intake may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, and the fruit's peel and seeds contain compounds that can be toxic in large quantities.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Mango trees are typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical climates, requiring well-drained soil, ample sunlight, and a consistent supply of water, with optimal temperatures ranging between 20°C and 30°C for flowering and fruiting.

Processing and Extraction

Processing mango involves removing the outer skin, slicing the fruit, and separating the pulp from the fibrous core, which can then be used in various forms such as fresh fruit, dried fruit, juice, or processed into products like mango puree or jam.

Storage and Shelf Life

Proper storage of Mango (Mangifera indica) in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight is essential to maintain its potency and extend its shelf life, which typically lasts up to 6 months when stored in an airtight container.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research and evidence supporting the use of Mango (Mangifera indica) in herbal medicine have shown its potential in various therapeutic applications, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, with studies indicating its role in managing conditions such as diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, and skin disorders.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Mango has been traditionally used in various cultures for its medicinal properties, including treating respiratory conditions, digestive issues, and skin ailments, with historical records tracing its use back to ancient Ayurvedic and Chinese medicinal practices.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, mango (Mangifera indica) is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by regulatory authorities for use in food and traditional herbal medicine, though specific regulations may vary depending on the country and the intended application.
