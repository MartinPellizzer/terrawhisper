Mangifera indica

What's the taxonomical classification of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. Moving down the taxonomic hierarchy, it is placed in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. It falls under the order Sapindales and is a member of the family Anacardiaceae. Finally, the plant is categorized under the genus Mangifera, with its specific designation being the species indica.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Mangifera indica contains medicinal compounds located in its leaves, bark, roots, seeds, and fruit pulp. The leaves are frequently prepared as decoctions to manage diabetes and reduce inflammation through their high antioxidant content. Bark extracts are often utilized in traditional medicine to treat gastrointestinal issues and skin infections. The seeds contain significant amounts of lipids and proteins that can support digestive health when processed correctly. Additionally, the fruit pulp provides essential vitamins that bolster the immune system and promote overall wellness.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains a diverse array of bioactive secondary metabolites including mangiferin, flavonoids, phenolic acids, terpenoids, tannins, alkaloids, steroids, and coumarins.

Mangiferin serves as a primary xanthone derivative known for its potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. The presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids contributes significantly to the plant's ability to scavenge free radicals. Additionally, the complex mixture of terpenoids, tannins, and alkaloids provides antimicrobial and cytotoxic effects.

Steroids and coumarins further enhance the pharmacological profile of Mangifera indica within traditional and modern medicine.

  • Flavonoids
  • Phenolic acids
  • Terpenoids
  • Tannins
  • Alkaloids
  • Steroids
  • Coumarins

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Mangifera indica targets cyclooxygenase-2, lipoxygenase, and various pro-inflammatory cytokines to exert its potent anti-inflammatory effects. The bioactive polyphenols and mangiferin found in the plant act by inhibiting the expression of nuclear factor-kappa B, which regulates the inflammatory response. Additionally, these compounds interact with various kinases and enzymes involved in signal transduction pathways to modulate cellular signaling. They also demonstrate antioxidant activity by neutralizing reactive oxygen species and interacting with superoxide dismutase. Through these specific protein interactions, the plant exhibits diverse pharmacological properties including anticancer and antimicrobial activities.

What are the therapeutic actions of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica has potent antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties driven by high concentrations of mangiferin, polyphenols, and flavonoids. These bioactive compounds help neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress within human cells. The plant's extracts also demonstrate significant antibacterial activity against various pathogens, making it useful in treating skin infections and gastrointestinal issues. Additionally, its therapeutic potential extends to managing metabolic conditions such as diabetes and hyperlipidemia through improved glucose regulation. These diverse medicinal actions make the mango plant a valuable subject in pharmacological research.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, diarrhea, inflammation, diabetes, and various respiratory ailments. The leaves and bark contain bioactive compounds like polyphenols and flavonoids that possess strong antimicrobial properties. These medicinal components help reduce intestinal inflammation and manage blood glucose levels in diabetic patients. Additionally, topical applications of the plant extracts can soothe skin irritations and combat bacterial growth. Traditional medicine also utilizes its extracts to alleviate coughs and asthma symptoms.

What are the herbal preparations of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica is prepared as decoctions of the bark, infusions of the leaves, powders from the dried fruit pulp, and various extracts derived from the seed kernel. The leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a bitter tea used for managing inflammatory conditions. In many traditional practices, the dried fruit pulp is processed into a fine powder that can be mixed with honey or water. Seed extracts are often concentrated into standardized capsules or tinctures to utilize their antioxidant properties. Additionally, the resin from the tree trunk can be processed into topical ointments for skin ailments.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Mangifera indica can cause allergic skin reactions, respiratory distress, or gastrointestinal upset in sensitive individuals. The sap from the plant contains urushiol, a compound similar to that found in poison ivy, which may trigger contact dermatitis upon touching the skin. Some people experience swelling of the lips or throat if they consume the fruit due to oral allergy syndrome. High consumption of the fruit may also lead to increased blood sugar levels because of its natural fructose content. In rare cases, the skin of the fruit can cause irritation if handled without protection.
