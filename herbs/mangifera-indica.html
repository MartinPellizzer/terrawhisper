Mango (Mangifera indica): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Mango (Mangifera indica) is a tropical fruit widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its diverse therapeutic properties, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and digestive benefits.

Here you can find a more about medicinal plants.

What is Mango (Mangifera Indica)?

Mango (Mangifera indica) is a tropical fruit-bearing tree native to South Asia, widely used in traditional herbal medicine for its rich nutritional content and various therapeutic properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Mango is Mangifera indica. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Sapindales, family Anacardiaceae, genus Mangifera, and species Mangifera indica. It is commonly known as mango in India, Africa, and Southeast Asia. In Latin America, it is referred to as mangó or manguera. The synonyms for this plant include Mangifera indica L., Mangifera indica var. indica, and regional names such as amboina in Indonesia and hong kong in China.

Plant Description and Morphology

Mango is a tropical evergreen tree that can grow up to 30 meters tall with a broad, spreading canopy.

Its leaves are alternate, simple, and elliptic to ovate in shape, typically measuring 10-40 cm in length. The flowers are small, fragrant, and clustered in panicles, with white or pale yellow petals and prominent yellow stamens. The root system is shallow and fibrous, often spreading widely near the surface.

The fruit is typically yellow to orange when ripe, has a sweet aroma, and features a smooth, leathery skin with a fibrous, juicy pulp underneath.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Mango is native to South and Southeast Asia, with its origin traced to the Indian subcontinent, particularly the regions of India, Nepal, and Bangladesh.

It has since been widely cultivated across tropical and subtropical regions, including parts of Africa, the Middle East, Central and South America, and the Caribbean. The tree thrives in warm climates with well-defined wet and dry seasons, preferring temperatures between 25°C and 35°C. It requires abundant sunlight, moderate rainfall, and well-drained, fertile soils to grow optimally.

Ecological conditions such as humidity, soil pH, and pest management play a crucial role in its successful cultivation and natural spread.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The medicinal use of mango primarily involves the fruit, leaves, bark, and seeds.

The fruit is commonly used for its nourishing properties and is often consumed to treat digestive issues and boost energy. Leaves are utilized in traditional remedies for their antiseptic and anti-inflammatory effects. The bark is prepared into decoctions to manage diabetes and skin conditions.

Seeds, or kernels, are ground into pastes or oils and applied topically for their healing properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Mango (Mangifera indica) contains a variety of active compounds that contribute to its medicinal properties, including polyphenols, flavonoids, and terpenoids.

Secondary metabolites such as carotenoids, alkaloids, and tannins are present in significant amounts and play a key role in the plant's defense mechanisms and therapeutic effects. Chemical markers like mangiferin, quercetin, and β-carotene are particularly notable for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. These compounds work synergistically to provide the plant's broad spectrum of health benefits.

The phytochemical profile of mango is also influenced by factors such as ripening stage, variety, and environmental conditions.

Pharmacological Properties

Mango (Mangifera indica) exhibits a range of pharmacological properties that make it valuable in herbal medicine.

Its therapeutic actions include anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects. The biological activity of mango is attributed to its high concentration of bioactive compounds such as polyphenols, carotenoids, and vitamins. Pharmacodynamically, these compounds interact with cellular pathways to modulate immune responses and reduce oxidative stress.

System-level effects of mango consumption may include improved cardiovascular health and enhanced gastrointestinal function.

Mechanisms of Action

The mechanisms of action of Mango (Mangifera indica) involve multiple biological processes that contribute to its therapeutic effects.

At the cellular level, Mango compounds exhibit antioxidant activity by neutralizing free radicals and reducing oxidative stress. These bioactive components also modulate inflammatory responses through the inhibition of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Biochemical pathways implicated include the activation of Nrf2, a key regulator of antioxidant defense, and the suppression of NF-κB, which is central to inflammatory signaling.

Target systems affected include the immune system, the gastrointestinal tract, and the central nervous system, where Mango exerts its diverse health benefits.

Therapeutic Uses

The therapeutic uses of Mango (Mangifera indica) are well-documented in traditional herbal medicine, particularly for its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

It is commonly used to treat digestive issues such as indigestion and gastritis due to its high enzyme content. Mango leaves are also employed in herbal remedies for their ability to alleviate symptoms of respiratory conditions like coughs and bronchitis. The fruit's high vitamin C content supports immune function, making it useful in preventing and managing infections.

Additionally, mango is used in topical applications to treat skin conditions such as eczema and wounds due to its soothing and healing properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Mango (Mangifera indica) is typically prepared in various forms such as fresh fruit, dried fruit, juice, powder, or as part of herbal formulations, often used in traditional medicine to address digestive issues, inflammation, and skin conditions.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of mango (Mangifera indica) for herbal use ranges from 100 to 500 mg of dried fruit powder per day, divided into two or three doses, though specific formulations may vary depending on the intended therapeutic purpose.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

While mango (Mangifera indica) is generally considered safe when consumed in typical food amounts, it may cause allergic reactions in some individuals, and excessive consumption can lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, hyperglycemia, or interactions with certain medications.

Drug Interactions

Mango (Mangifera indica) may interact with certain medications due to its potential effects on liver enzymes and its impact on blood sugar levels, requiring careful monitoring when used concurrently with drugs such as anticoagulants, anti-diabetic agents, or those metabolized by the liver.

Toxicity and Precautions

While mango (Mangifera indica) is generally safe when consumed in normal quantities, excessive intake of its fruit or seeds may lead to gastrointestinal discomfort, and the seeds contain trace amounts of toxic compounds that can be harmful if ingested in large quantities.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Mango trees are typically cultivated in tropical and subtropical climates, requiring well-drained soil, ample sunlight, and a warm climate with consistent temperatures, with the fruit usually harvested between 4 to 6 months after flowering.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of mango (Mangifera indica) involve methods such as cold pressing, solvent extraction, and enzymatic hydrolysis to obtain bioactive compounds like mangiferin, phenolic acids, and vitamin C, which are utilized in herbal medicine for their antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and therapeutic properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Proper storage of mango (Mangifera indica) in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight is essential to maintain its potency and efficacy as an herbal medicine, with dried mango typically retaining its medicinal properties for up to 6 months when stored in airtight containers.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Mango (Mangifera indica) has demonstrated its potential health benefits, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, with studies supporting its use in traditional herbal medicine for conditions such as digestive disorders, skin health, and immune support.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Mango (Mangifera indica) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine across various cultures for its antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and digestive properties, with historical records dating back to ancient Ayurvedic and Chinese medicinal practices.

Regulatory Status

In many countries, mango (Mangifera indica) is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by regulatory authorities for use in food and traditional herbal medicine, but specific regulations regarding its use in medicinal formulations may vary by region and depend on local pharmacopoeias and herbal licensing standards.
