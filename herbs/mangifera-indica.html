What to know about Mangifera indica (mango) before using it medicinally

Mangifera indica, commonly known as mango, is a medicinal herb that has been revered for its numerous health benefits, which range from treating digestive disorders to reducing inflammation and improving antioxidant levels in the body.

In horticultural terms, the mango tree is a tropical evergreen that is widely cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions, requiring full sun and well-drained soil to thrive. From a botanical perspective, Mangifera indica belongs to the family Anacardiaceae and is characterized by its large, leathery leaves and showy, fragrant flowers.

The herb has a long history of use in traditional medicine, with ancient Ayurvedic texts referencing its properties and recommending it for various health conditions.

This article explains the medicinal, horticultural, botanical, and historical aspects of Mangifera indica.

What are the medicinal properties of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica helps with various health issues, including diarrhea, dysentery, and rheumatism due to its anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. The fruit's juice and pulp are used to treat these conditions. Its leaves and bark are used in traditional medicine to treat various ailments. The seed kernel oil is used in some medical applications. It is also used as a treatment for skin conditions and as a remedy for certain types of cancer.

The active constituents responsible for its medicinal properties include various phenolic compounds, such as flavonoids, phenolic acids, and terpenoids. These compounds possess antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities, which contribute to the plant's therapeutic effects. Flavonoids, specifically, have been found to have antiviral, antibacterial, and anti-allergic properties, making them useful in treating a variety of health conditions.

The parts of the plant most commonly used for medicinal purposes are the leaves, bark, fruit, and seed kernel. The leaves are used to treat fever, rheumatism, and skin conditions, while the bark is used to treat dysentery and diarrhea. The fruit is used to treat various health issues, including scurvy, anemia, and digestive problems. The seed kernel oil is used in some medical applications.

Improper use of Mangifera indica can cause side effects, including allergic reactions, such as skin rashes and itching, and gastrointestinal upset, including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Long-term consumption of large amounts of the plant's seed kernel oil can cause liver damage and kidney problems. It can also interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners and diabetes medications.

Precautions should be taken when using Mangifera indica medicinally, including avoiding its use in individuals with allergies to mango or its constituents. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should also avoid its use, as it may cause harm to the fetus or baby. Furthermore, the plant's seed kernel oil should be used in small amounts and under medical supervision, as it can cause adverse effects if used excessively.

What are the horticulural aspects of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica grow well in tropical and subtropical regions with temperatures above 25°C. The plant requires full sun, well-draining soil, and high humidity to thrive. It can tolerate drought but performs best with regular watering.

Planting tips for Mangifera indica include selecting a location with good air circulation and providing a support system for the tree as it grows. Plant the sapling in a hole twice the depth of the pot, mixing in organic matter and compost to improve soil fertility.

Harvesting mangoes typically occurs between 100 to 150 days after flowering, depending on the variety. The fruit is usually ready to pick when it turns yellow, red, or orange and has a sweet aroma. Avoid picking the fruit when it's green, as it may not be ripe yet.

Common pests and diseases affecting Mangifera indica include the fruit fly, mango hopper, and mango mealybug, as well as diseases such as anthracnose and powdery mildew. Regular pruning and monitoring can help prevent infestations and infections.

What are the botanical characteristics of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica is a tropical fruit tree species that belongs to the family Anacardiaceae.

Botanically, it is characterized by its evergreen or deciduous nature, with large, alternate, simple leaves that are ovate or elliptical in shape and have a pointed tip. The leaves are 10-30 cm long and 5-15 cm wide, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter green lower surface. The tree's bark is smooth, greyish-brown, and has a distinctive horizontal lenticel pattern.

Mangifera indica produces small, white, fragrant flowers that are borne in clusters at the tips of branches, typically measuring 5-10 cm in diameter and having five petals. Each flower contains many stamens and carpels, and is pollinated by insects and bees. The fruit, which is the mango, is a drupe with a single seed surrounded by a fleshy pericarp, typically yellow or orange when ripe and measuring 5-20 cm in length and 4-10 cm in width.

The tree can grow up to 35 meters tall, but is usually pruned to around 15 meters for easy harvesting of the fruit.

What is the taxonomy of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica belongs to the family Anacardiaceae, order Sapindales, class Magnoliopsida, division Magnoliophyta, and kingdom Plantae.

Specifically, it is a species of flowering plant in the genus Mangifera, which comprises around 70-80 species of mangoes and related genera.

What are the variants of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica has variants, such as Tommy Atkins, Kent, Haden, and Ataulfo, which are popular cultivars grown for their unique characteristics, flavor profiles, and growing conditions.

The Tommy Atkins variety is known for its sweet and slightly tangy taste, while the Kent variety is prized for its bright yellow skin and juicy flesh. Haden mangoes are renowned for their rich, buttery flavor and firm texture, whereas Ataulfo mangoes are celebrated for their vibrant orange-yellow color and delicate sweetness.

Other variants include Alphonso, Chaunsa, and Dasheri, each with its own distinct features and admirers among mango enthusiasts.

What is the geographic distribution of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica, also known as mango, is native to South Asia and is widely distributed throughout the tropical regions of the world.

It is primarily grown in India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Africa, Central America, and parts of South America.

The ideal climate for mango cultivation is in warm and humid regions with average temperatures ranging from 24°C to 37°C (75°F-99°F), making it well-suited for tropical and subtropical areas.

What is the life cycle of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica, the mango tree, has a life cycle that typically spans around 300 to 400 years.

The process begins with germination from seed, which takes approximately 1-2 weeks. The seedling grows into a sapling over several months, reaching about 6 inches in height within the first year.

It then requires regular pruning and care to promote healthy growth. As it matures, the tree produces its first flowers around 3-5 years after planting, followed by fruiting around 4-6 years later. The mango tree continues to produce fruit annually for several decades, with peak production typically occurring between 15-25 years of age.

With proper care and maintenance, a mango tree can continue to thrive and produce fruit for centuries.

What are the historical uses of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica, commonly known as the mango tree, has been a staple in many cultures for centuries.

Historically, its wood was used for building and furniture-making due to its durability and resistance to decay. The fruit itself was consumed fresh or dried, while the leaves were used in traditional medicine to treat various ailments.

In some Asian cultures, the bark and roots of the tree were employed as a natural dye and in rituals for spiritual purposes.

What are the mythological referencec of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica, the mango tree, has been revered in Hindu mythology for its divine qualities.

In the Rigveda, it is mentioned as "Amlaki", a fruit of the gods, associated with Lord Shiva and his consort Parvati.

The mango is also said to have been created by the god Brahma and is described in the Bhagavata Purana as a gift from the god Vishnu to his consort Lakshmi.

What are the symbolic meanings of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica, also known as the mango tree, has been imbued with rich symbolic meanings across various cultures.

In Hindu mythology, the mango is considered a gift from the gods, representing divine love and abundance. In Islamic tradition, the mango is seen as a symbol of paradise, where its juice is believed to flow like rivers in heaven.

The fruit itself represents fertility, prosperity, and good fortune, making it a popular motif in Indian art and architecture.

What are the historical texts of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica, commonly known as mango, has a rich and diverse history.

Historical texts such as the ancient Sanskrit text "Mango" by Susruta (circa 6th century BCE) and the Buddhist text "Maha-Samnipata" (circa 5th century CE) mention the fruit's cultivation and significance in Indian society.

The Persian physician Avicenna (980-1037 CE) also wrote about mangoes in his medical treatise, "The Canon of Medicine".

What are the historical artifacts of Mangifera indica?

Mangifera indica, commonly known as the mango tree, has a rich history dating back over 4,000 years.

The earliest recorded historical artifacts related to this fruit are from ancient India, where it was considered a sacred symbol of love and fertility.

Excavations at Mohenjo-Daro, a 2,500-year-old Indus Valley Civilization city, have uncovered mango stones and seeds, indicating its widespread cultivation during that period.
