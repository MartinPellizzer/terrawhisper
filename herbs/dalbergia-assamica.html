Dalbergia assamica

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Dalbergia assamica has pinnately compound leaves, small yellow flowers arranged in axillary panicles, and woody, oblong pods that contain several seeds. The leaflets are typically small and elliptical with entire margins. The tree structure is characterized by a spreading canopy and relatively smooth bark. Its reproductive structures feature delicate petals that contrast with the sturdy, legume-style fruit. The overall morphology reflects its adaptation to the subtropical forest environments where it grows.

      What are the herbal preparations of Dalbergia assamica?

      Dalbergia assamica is prepared primarily as a decoction, an infusion, or a paste derived from its bark, leaves, and roots. The bark is often boiled in water for extended periods to extract bioactive compounds for medicinal use. Leaves can be crushed into a topical paste to treat skin inflammations or steeped as a tea for internal consumption. In some traditional practices, the powdered root is mixed with honey or oils to create more concentrated therapeutic applications. These various forms allow the plant's medicinal properties to be targeted toward different types of ailments.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Dalbergia assamica can cause allergic contact dermatitis, skin rashes, and localized swelling upon direct physical contact with its tissues or sap. The plant contains various phytochemicals that may trigger hypersensitivity reactions in sensitive individuals. Prolonged exposure to the extracted oils or crushed parts of the plant often results in redness and itching. Some users may also experience irritation of the mucous membranes if the particles are inhaled or accidentally ingested. Monitoring for these dermatological responses is essential when handling the species.
