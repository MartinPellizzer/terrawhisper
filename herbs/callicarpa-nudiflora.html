Callicarpa nudiflora

What's the taxonomical classification of Callicarpa nudiflora?

Callicarpa nudiflora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it is further categorized under the order Lamiales. This plant is a member of the family Lamiaceae, falling into the genus Callicarpa, and is specifically identified by the species name nudiflora.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Callicarpa nudiflora contains various bioactive phytochemicals including flavonoids, terpenoids, tannins, and phenolic compounds that provide its medicinal properties. Researchers study these specific molecules to understand their potential for developing new pharmacological treatments.

What are the therapeutic actions of Callicarpa nudiflora?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various skin ailments, digestive disorders, and inflammatory conditions. It is often employed in traditional medicine to alleviate symptoms of rheumatism and joint pain. Some practitioners use preparations from the plant to manage stomach-related issues such as dysentery or indigestion. Additionally, topical applications are frequently applied to soothe skin irritations and minor wounds. The bioactive compounds within the plant are believed to provide these therapeutic effects.

What are the herbal preparations of Callicarpa nudiflora?

Callicarpa nudiflora is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical poultice using the leaves and bark of the plant. The leaves are frequently boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for treating various internal ailments. For external applications, the plant material is often crushed into a paste to serve as a soothing poultice for skin irritations. Some traditional practitioners also utilize extracts from the bark to formulate concentrated liquid tinctures. These different methods allow the bioactive compounds within the plant to be delivered effectively based on the specific health need.

What side-effects this plant can have?

While specific clinical data on this species is limited, many plants in the Verbenaceae family contain phytochemicals that trigger contact sensitivity in sensitive individuals. Direct contact with the leaves or stems may result in redness, itching, or a localized rash. If swallowed, the plant's chemical constituents might induce nausea, vomiting, or stomach cramps.
