Viola biflora

What's the taxonomical classification of Viola biflora?

Viola biflora belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic lineage further, it falls under the order Malpighiales and is a member of the family Violaceae. Finally, the plant is situated within the genus Viola, specifically identified by its species name, biflora.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Viola biflora has a perennial herbaceous habit characterized by creeping rhizomes and basal leaves that are typically cordate or reniform in shape with crenate margins. The foliage often features long petioles that emerge from a short stem structure. Its flowers are zygomorphic and usually appear in pairs, displaying a palette of pale violet, blue, or white petals. Each blossom possesses a prominent spur at the rear and subtle nectar guides on the lower petals to assist pollinators. Small, globose capsules develop from the ovaries to facilitate seed dispersal following pollination.

What are the pharmacological activities of Viola biflora?

This plant has anti-HIV, antimicrobial, cytotoxicity, haemolytic, and insecticidal pharmacological activities. Its bioactive compounds demonstrate the ability to inhibit viral replication and combat various bacterial and fungal pathogens. Research indicates that certain extracts exhibit significant cytotoxicity against specific cancer cell lines. The plant also shows measurable haemolytic properties when interacting with red blood cells. Additionally, its chemical constituents provide effective insecticidal actions against various pest species.

As discussed in a study, Viola biflora is a rich source of cyclotides, which are small plant proteins consisting of approximately 30 amino acid residues. These cyclotidic compounds exhibit potent cytotoxicity, insecticidal effects, anti-HIV, antimicrobial, and haemolytic activities. Researchers determined the sequences of 11 cyclotidic proteins, known as vibi A-K, through isolation and MS/MS sequencing. To correlate sequence with potency, vibi D, E, G, and H were analyzed using a fluorometric microculture cytotoxicity assay. The IC50-values for the bracelet cyclotidic proteins vibi E, G, and H ranged between 0.96 and 5.0 microM. In contrast, the Möbius cycloide vibi D showed no cytotoxicity at 30 microM.

    What side effects this plant can have?

    This plant can cause skin irritation, allergic dermatitis, or gastrointestinal distress if ingested. While many violets are considered non-toxic in small amounts, individual sensitivities to the salicylic acid or other compounds within the leaves can lead to redness and itching. Some people may experience an upset stomach or nausea after consuming the foliage. It is also possible for contact with the plant to trigger an allergic reaction in those with heightened sensitivities to the Violaceae family. Always consult a professional before handling or consuming wild flora to avoid these adverse reactions.
