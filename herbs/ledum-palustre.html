Lousewort

Ledum palustre

Lousewort (Ledum palustre) is a perennial herbaceous plant native to northern regions, known for its medicinal properties and historical use in treating respiratory and circulatory conditions.

Botanical Identification

Lousewort, scientifically known as Ledum palustre, belongs to the Ericaceae family. It is also called marsh tea or wintergreen in some regions. A perennial shrub native to northern regions, it has hairy leaves, red flowers, and a distinctive aromatic scent. It is sometimes referred to as "louse plant" in older herbal texts.

The following list summarize the taxonomy of plant:

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Magnoliopsida
  • Order: Ericales
  • Family: Ericaceae
  • Genus: Ledum
  • Species: Ledum palustre

Lousewort Identification.

Active Compounds

Lousewort contains essential oil, coumarin, and tannin as its main active compounds. These compounds work together to provide its medicinal effects, with essential oil contributing to antimicrobial properties and coumarin offering potential anti-inflammatory benefits. Tannins may support wound healing and astringent actions.

  • Essential oil
  • Coumarin
  • Tannin

Lousewort Uses.

Preparation Methods

Infusion: Used for respiratory ailments such as coughs and bronchitis.

Decoction: Used for digestive issues and as a general tonic.

Essential oil: Used for topical applications to relieve muscle pain and inflammation.

Safety Profile

Lousewort contains toxic compounds that can cause severe poisoning. It is contraindicated during pregnancy and lactation due to potential harm to the fetus or infant. Use with caution and only under professional supervision.
