Arundo donax

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Arundo donax has tall, perennial, rhizomatous stalks that reach heights of up to 6 meters and feature thick, woody stems with prominent nodes. These stems are hollow and cylindrical, often reaching diameters of several centimeters as they grow in dense clumps. The leaves are long, linear, and lanceolate, measuring up to 1 meter in length with a distinct midrib and parallel venation. Large, feathery panicles emerge at the top of the culms to produce numerous small, cream-colored flowers. The entire plant is characterized by a robust, grass-like appearance supported by a vigorous underground spreading system.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to the Mediterranean Basin, including regions across Southern Europe, North Africa, and Western Asia. While its natural range is centered around these warm climates, it has become widely established in many other parts of the world. It frequently colonizes riparian zones and wetlands in subtropical and temperate areas of the Americas, Australia, and South Africa. These invasive populations often thrive in disturbed habitats near water sources where they can spread rapidly. Consequently, its distribution has expanded globally due to human activity and its ability to adapt to various moist environments.

      What are the herbal preparations of Arundo donax?

      Arundo donax is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste derived from its rhizomes, stems, or leaves. The dried rhizomes are commonly boiled in water to create a concentrated decoction used for internal consumption. Leaves can be crushed into a fresh poultice to apply directly to skin irritations or wounds. Some traditional practices also involve using the powdered stem in medicinal washes. These various preparations allow for the extraction of specific bioactive compounds depending on the intended therapeutic use.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Arundo donax can cause skin irritation, allergic reactions, and physical abrasions due to its sharp, silica-rich leaves and tough stalks. Contact with the foliage often results in contact dermatitis or itching in sensitive individuals. The plant's rigid structure also presents a mechanical hazard, as the pointed edges can cause minor cuts or scrapes during handling. Furthermore, because it grows in dense, invasive thickets, it can create tripping hazards or hide obstacles in natural habitats. These physical and biological risks make careful handling essential when working near this species.
