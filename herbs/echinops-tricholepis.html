Echinops tricholepis

What's the taxonomical classification of Echinops tricholepis?

Echinops tricholepis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following the order Asterales, the plant is a member of the family Asteraceae. It is further defined by its genus, Echinops, and is specifically identified by its species name, tricholepis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Echinops tricholepis has a robust, perennial structure characterized by deeply lobed, grayish-green leaves and spherical, spiny flower heads that emerge from stiff, upright stems.

The foliage is typically covered in fine, silvery hairs that provide a soft texture to the serrated leaf margins. Its inflorescences are composed of numerous small, tubular florets densely packed within a protective layer of sharp, prickly bracts. These prickly globose heads can reach several centimeters in diameter and often turn a distinct shade of blue or purple during anthesis.

The stems are woody at the base and support the heavy floral structures throughout the growing season.

      What are the herbal preparations of Echinops tricholepis?

      Echinops tricholepis is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a paste for topical application. The dried roots and leaves are typically boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal consumption. In some traditional practices, the plant material is ground into a fine powder and mixed with water or oils to form a paste. This paste is applied directly to the skin to treat localized ailments or inflammations. These various methods allow the plant's bioactive compounds to be utilized for both systemic and external healing purposes.

      What side-effects this plant can have?

      Echinops tricholepis can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis, redness, or itching if the plant comes into direct contact with sensitive skin. The prickly texture of the leaves and stems may also lead to minor physical abrasions or skin irritation upon handling. Some individuals might experience gastrointestinal discomfort if any part of the plant is ingested accidentally. Because clinical studies on human toxicity are limited, the full range of potential internal side effects remains largely unknown. It is generally advisable to exercise caution and avoid direct contact to prevent unexpected sensitivities.
