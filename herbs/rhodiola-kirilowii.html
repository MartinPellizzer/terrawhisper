What's the taxonomical classification of Rhodiola kirilowii?

Rhodiola kirilowii belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Saxifragales. This plant is a member of the family Crassulaceae, which defines its broader botanical grouping. More specifically, it is identified within the genus Rhodiola, with its unique biological identity finalized as the species kirilowii.

What is the geographical distribution of this plant?

This plant is native to high-altitude regions of the Tibetan Plateau in China, specifically occurring in the provinces of Qinghai and Sichuan. It thrives in alpine meadows and rocky slopes located at elevations typically ranging from 3,500 to 4,500 meters above sea level. These specific habitats provide the cool, moist, and sun-exposed conditions necessary for its survival. The species is primarily restricted to these mountainous ecosystems due to its specialized adaptation to harsh alpine climates. Consequently, its distribution is highly localized within these specific Himalayan and Hengduan mountain corridors.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains salidroside, phenylpropanoids and their glycosides, phenylethanoids, polyphenols, and p-coumaric acid. These diverse chemical constituents contribute to the unique pharmacological profile of Rhodiola kirilowii. The presence of phenylpropanoids and their glycosides is particularly significant for its bioactive properties. Furthermore, the polyphenols and phenylethanoids work together to provide antioxidant benefits. The inclusion of p-coumaric acid also plays a role in the plant's medicinal efficacy.

According to a study published by "Central-European journal of immunology", Rhodiola kirilowii contains 15 identified polyphenols as detected by HPLC. Of these 15 polyphenols, 8 were present in the sera of mice that received 20 mg/kg of aqueous (RKW) or hydro-alcoholic (RKW-A) extracts. The hydro-alcoholic extract (RKW-A) resulted in higher concentrations of salidroside, kaempferol, chlorogenic acid, bFGF, and VEGF in the sera compared to the RKW group. While RKW sera contain salidroside and antiangiogenic catechins, they did not harm the proliferation of endothelial cells. The study suggests that RKW-A extract and its sera may contain other unidentified substances influencing endothelial cell proliferation.
