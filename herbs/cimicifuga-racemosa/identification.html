Botanical Identification of Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa)

To identify Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Scientific Classification

Black Cohosh, or Cimicifuga racemosa, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Ranunculales, family Ranunculaceae, genus Cimicifuga, and species Cimicifuga racemosa. This classification places it within the flowering plants and the buttercup family, which is characteristic of its morphological and chemical properties.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Black Cohosh is Cimicifuga racemosa, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It has several synonyms, including Actaea racemosa and Cimicifuga racemosa var. lutea. Common names include Black Cohosh, Bugbane, and American Bugbane, while regional and historical names vary across cultures and time periods.

Morphological Characteristics

Black Cohosh is a perennial plant with a rhizomatous root system. It grows up to 2 meters tall, featuring erect, hairy stems. Its compound leaves are trifoliate, with serrated edges. The plant produces a tall, branched inflorescence of small, greenish-white flowers. The fruit is a follicle containing numerous seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Black Cohosh can be distinguished by its tall, unbranched stems with smooth, hollow, and often red-tinged internodes. Its compound leaves have alternating, pinnately arranged leaflets with serrated edges. The raceme of white to pale green flowers is terminal, and the berries are red, globose, and clustered in groups of three.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Black Cohosh is native to eastern North America, particularly the northeastern United States and southeastern Canada. It thrives in temperate climates with cool, moist summers and cold winters. It prefers rich, well-drained soil and is typically found at elevations between 300 and 1,500 meters.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Black Cohosh blooms in late summer to early fall, typically between July and September, depending on the region. Seed formation occurs in late fall, with mature seeds developing by late October. For identification purposes, the most accurate harvest time is during the flowering period, when the plant's distinctive raceme of white flowers is visible.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa) is closely related to species within the genus Cimicifuga, which belongs to the Ranunculaceae family. It shares key morphological and genetic traits with other members of this family, such as compound leaves and showy flowers. Its closest relatives include Cimicifuga simplex and Cimicifuga dilatata, which are also native to North America.
