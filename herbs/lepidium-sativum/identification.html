Botanical Identification of Field Cress (Lepidium sativum)

To identify Field Cress (Lepidium sativum) accurately, examine its morphological characteristics, taxonomic classification, and diagnostic features.

Field Cress (Lepidium sativum) Monograph

Scientific Classification

Field Cress, Lepidium sativum, belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Spermatophyta, class Caryophyllopsida, order Brassicales, family Brassicaceae, genus Lepidium, and species Lepidium sativum. These classifications outline its place in the plant kingdom and help identify its botanical relationships and characteristics.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The scientific name of Field Cress is Lepidium sativum, first described by Linnaeus in 1753. It is also known by several synonyms, including Chenopodium sativum and Lepidium campestre. Common names include Field Cress, Garden Mustard, and Peppergrass, while regional names vary across Europe and Asia. Historically, it was referred to as "Cress of the Earth" in ancient texts.

Morphological Characteristics

Field Cress is an annual herb with a taproot system. It has erect, branching stems that are hairy and slightly swollen at the nodes. The leaves are alternate, lanceolate, and slightly hairy, with a distinctive white vein pattern. The inflorescence is a raceme of small white or yellow flowers with four petals. The fruit is a capsule containing several small, oblong seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Field Cress can be distinguished by its distinctive yellow flowers with four petals, heart-shaped leaves, and a strong, spicy scent. It has a branched stem and small, smooth, oval seeds. It is often confused with other mustard family plants but can be identified by its unique leaf shape and odor.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

Field Cress is native to temperate regions of Europe, Asia, and North Africa. It thrives in cool climates and prefers well-drained, slightly acidic soils. It commonly grows at low to moderate altitudes, often near roadsides, fields, and disturbed soils.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Field Cress blooms from late spring to early summer, typically between May and July. Seed formation occurs in late summer, around August to September. For identification purposes, the best time to harvest is during the flowering stage, when the plant is most distinct and the leaves are fully developed.

Phylogenetic Relationships

Field Cress (Lepidium sativum) is closely related to other members of the Brassicaceae family. It shares genetic and morphological traits with species like Arabidopsis thaliana and Sinapis arvensis. Its genus, Lepidium, includes plants with similar seed structures and flowering patterns. These relationships help in understanding its role in ecological and medicinal contexts.
