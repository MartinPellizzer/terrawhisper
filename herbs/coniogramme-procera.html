Coniogramme procera

What's the taxonomical classification of Coniogramme procera?

Coniogramme procera belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Polypodiinae, this plant follows the evolutionary lineage of the order Polypodiales. It is specifically situated within the family Pteridaceae and falls under the genus Coniogramme, ultimately being identified by its specific species epithet, procera.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Coniogramme procera contains diverse phytochemicals including flavonoids, tannins, saponins, alkaloids, and terpenoids that contribute to its biological activities. These secondary metabolites are primarily concentrated in the leaf extracts and serve as the foundation for its traditional therapeutic uses. Specifically, the presence of tannins and flavonoids provides significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Some studies also suggest that these compounds may exhibit antimicrobial effects against certain pathogens. This chemical profile supports the plant's application in various ethnobotanical remedies for treating skin ailments and internal inflammation.

What are the therapeutic actions of Coniogramme procera?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat skin infections, wounds, stomach aches, and respiratory ailments. Local communities often apply its crushed leaves topically to soothe inflammation and accelerate the healing of cutaneous lesions. When consumed as a decoction, the plant is believed to alleviate gastrointestinal distress and abdominal pains. It also plays a role in traditional medicine for managing symptoms related to coughs and other lung issues. These therapeutic properties make it a significant component of regional herbal practices.

What are the herbal preparations of Coniogramme procera?

Coniogramme procera is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste for various medicinal applications. The dried leaves are often boiled in water to create a liquid extract used for internal consumption. In some traditional practices, the crushed plant material is applied directly to the skin as a poultice to treat superficial wounds. These preparations are frequently utilized to address specific ailments such as digestive issues or skin irritations. The method of preparation typically depends on the specific therapeutic goal intended by the practitioner.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Coniogramme procera can cause skin irritation, allergic contact dermatitis, or respiratory distress if its spores or fine hairs are inhaled or touched. The plant contains microscopic structures that may trigger itching and redness upon contact with sensitive skin. Individuals with pre-existing respiratory conditions might experience coughing or wheezing if they encounter the plant in an enclosed space. Because specific toxicological studies on this fern are limited, caution should be exercised to avoid direct contact with its foliage. Monitoring for any swelling or discomfort is recommended if accidental exposure occurs.
