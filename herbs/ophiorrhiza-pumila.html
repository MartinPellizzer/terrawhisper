Ophiorrhiza pumila

What's the taxonomical classification of Ophiorrhiza pumila?

Ophiorrhiza pumila belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within the class Equisetopsida, it is situated in the subclass Magnoliidae and falls under the order Gentianaales. This plant is a member of the family Rubiaceae, which is further categorized into the genus Ophiorrhiza, specifically identifying it as the species pumila.

These blossoms are often pale in color and feature a delicate, elongated shape. The plant's overall growth habit is compact and low-growing, characteristic of many forest floor species.

As discussed in a study, the methanol extracts of Ophiorrhiza pumila contain camptothecin-related alkaloids. A natural 10-glycosyloxy camptothecin known as chaboside was found to accumulate in the tissues of O. pumila. Anthrachinones, which are regarded as phytoalexins, were present in the extracts of the hairy roots and calli of O. pumila but were not present in the differentiated plants. The activity of strictosidine synthase, a key enzyme in camptothecin biosynthesis, was detected in the protein extracts of the stems and roots of O. pumila. This enzyme activity was correlated with the pattern of strictosidine synthase mRNA expression in the plant.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains the medicinal compounds camptothecin, loganin, secologanin, anthraquinones, and strictosamide. These bioactive molecules are primarily responsible for the various pharmacological activities associated with the species. Camptothecin is well known for its potent anti-tumor properties, while the iridoid glycosides loganin and secologanin serve as essential precursors in the biosynthesis of complex alkaloids. The presence of anthraquinones often contributes to laxative or antimicrobial effects. Additionally, strictosamide plays a critical role in the metabolic pathways that produce diverse therapeutic substances.
