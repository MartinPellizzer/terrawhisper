Rubus amabilis

What's the taxonomical classification of Rubus amabilis?

Rubus amabilis belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the taxonomic order of Rosales and is a member of the family Rosaceae. Ultimately, its specific biological identification is defined by its placement within the genus Rubus, resulting in its unique species name, Rubus amabilis.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Rubus amabilis has arching, woody stems known as canes that are densely covered in fine, needle-like prickles and often feature a reddish hue. The leaves are typically trifoliate or occasionally pentafoliate, displaying serrated margins and a deep green color on the upper surface. On the undersides, the foliage often presents a lighter, sometimes pubescent texture that distinguishes it from similar species. Small, fragrant white flowers emerge from the axils of the leaves, characterized by five petals and numerous stamens. These flowers eventually develop into aggregate fruits that transition from green to a bright, edible red when fully ripe.

How is this plant cultivated?

This plant requires well-drained, acidic soil with consistent moisture and at least six to eight hours of direct sunlight daily to thrive.

Growers should plant it in a location that provides protection from harsh winds to prevent damage to its delicate canes. Regular mulching helps maintain the necessary soil humidity and suppresses competing weeds around the base. Pruning is essential during the dormant season to remove old wood and encourage vigorous new growth.

Proper irrigation schedules are critical to ensure the root system does not dry out during peak summer heat.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains medicinal compounds such as (-)-secoisolariciresinol-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranoside, pregnane glycoside, flavonoid glycosides, lignan glycosides, and triterpene glycosyl ester. These specific bioactive molecules contribute to the diverse pharmacological potential of the Rubus amabilis species. The presence of various lignan and flavonoid glycosides often relates to antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. Additionally, the triterpene glycosyl ester and pregnane glycoside components are significant for their unique chemical structures. Together, these secondary metabolites form a complex chemical profile used in studying the plant's therapeutic properties.

According to a study published by "Planta medica", Rubus amabilis Focke contains pregnane glycoside, lignan glycosides, triterpene glycosyl ester and flavonoid glycosides. From the aerial parts of Rubus amabilis, two new compounds were isolated, specifically 3-O-beta-D-glucopyranosyl-3 beta, 15 alpha-dihydroxypregn-5-en-20-one (1) and (-)-secoisolariciresinol-O-alpha-L-rhamnopyranoside (2). These two new compounds were found alongside six known compounds identified as 3-8. The structures of these 2 new compounds were elucidated using spectroscopic methods and chemical transformations.
