What's the taxonomical classification of Hedyotis auricularia?

Hedyotis auricularia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is categorized under the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Following its taxonomic descent, it is placed in the order Gentianales and the family Rubiaceae. Ultimately, the plant is defined by its specific genus, Hedyois, and its unique species designation, auricularia.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Hedyotis auricularia has small, fleshy, ear-shaped leaves that are typically green or slightly purplish and grow in a compact rosette pattern.

The leaves possess a thickened, succulent texture which helps the plant retain moisture in its natural habitat. These leaves often feature rounded margins and a slightly concave surface, giving them their distinct auricular appearance. The stems are relatively short and often remain obscured by the dense foliage of the basal rosette.

Tiny, inconspicuous flowers emerge from the center of the plant on slender stalks during its blooming period.

        What are the herbal preparations of Hedyotis auricularia?

        Hedyotis auricularia is prepared as a decoction, an infusion, or a topical paste depending on the intended therapeutic application. The dried roots and leaves are most commonly boiled in water to create a medicinal tea used for internal consumption. For external ailments such as skin irritations, the plant material is crushed into a fine consistency and mixed with a carrier to form a soothing poultice. Some traditional practitioners also use the juice extracted from fresh leaves for direct application to wounds. These diverse methods allow for the controlled extraction of the plant's specific bioactive compounds.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Hedyotis auricularia can cause localized skin irritation, redness, and swelling upon direct contact with the plant's tissues. Some individuals may experience an allergic reaction characterized by itchy rashes or small blisters where the skin touched the foliage. If the sap or plant material is accidentally ingested, it may lead to gastrointestinal distress such as nausea or stomach cramping. It is important to avoid touching the eyes or mucous membranes after handling the plant to prevent further irritation. Always wash hands thoroughly with soap and water after any exposure to minimize these risks.
