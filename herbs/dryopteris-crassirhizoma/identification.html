Botanical Identification of Cinnamon Fern (Dryopteris crassirhizoma)

Scientific Classification

The Cinnamon Fern, Dryopteris crassirhizoma, belongs to the kingdom Plantae. Its classification places it in the family Dryopteridaceae, genus Dryopteris, and species Dryopteris crassirhizoma. The division, class, and order are not provided in the data.

Botanical Nomenclature and Synonyms

The botanical name of the Cinnamon Fern is Dryopteris crassirhizoma, first described by the botanist John Lindley in 1833. It is also known by several synonyms, including Dryopteris filix-mas and Dryopteris carpathica, though these are now considered outdated. Common names such as Cinnamon Fern and Royal Fern reflect its appearance and historical use in herbal medicine.

Morphological Characteristics

The cinnamon fern is a clump-forming plant with a short, stout rhizome. It has a tall, unbranched stem that supports a single, broad, deltoid frond. The frond is divided into segments with a distinctive cinnamon-colored fertile zone at the base. The plant lacks true flowers and produces spores instead of seeds.

Diagnostic Identification Features

Cinnamon Fern can be distinguished by its dark brown, cinnamon-scented fronds with a pale, fuzzy underside. It has a tall, unbranched sterile stalk and a clustered, spore-bearing fertile frond. It grows in moist, shaded areas and is commonly found near streams or in woodlands. Its unique fragrance and frond structure help differentiate it from similar ferns.

Natural Habitat and Geographic Distribution

The Cinnamon Fern is native to eastern North America, including the eastern United States and southern Canada. It thrives in humid climates and prefers moist, shaded environments. It grows in rich, loamy soils and is commonly found in woodlands and stream banks. It typically grows at low to mid-elevation ranges, often near water sources.

Phenology and Growth Cycle

Cinnamon Fern blooms in late spring to early summer, producing spore-bearing fronds. Seed formation occurs in late summer to early fall. For identification purposes, harvest is best done in early spring before the fronds fully unfurl.

Phylogenetic Relationships

The Cinnamon Fern (Dryopteris crassirhizoma) is closely related to other Dryopteris species within the Dryopteris genus. It shares family traits with other ferns in the Athyriaceae family, such as rhizomatous growth and compound leaves. Its phylogenetic placement reflects its evolutionary ties to similar fern species with similar morphological and genetic characteristics.
