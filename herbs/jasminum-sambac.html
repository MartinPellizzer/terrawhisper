Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a fragrant flowering plant native to South and Southeast Asia, known for its sweetly scented white flowers and its use in traditional medicine for centuries.

What is Common Jasmine (Jasminum Sambac)?

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a flowering plant native to Southeast Asia, widely cultivated for its fragrant flowers used in herbal medicine, perfumery, and traditional remedies.

Botanical Identity and Classification

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a flowering plant species belonging to the genus Jasminum in the family Oleaceae, native to southern and eastern Asia.

Common Names and Synonyms

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is also known by several common names, including Arabian Jasmine, Persian Jasmine, and Night-Blooming Jasmine, and is occasionally referred to by its scientific name in herbal medicine contexts.

Plant Description and Morphology

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a shrub characterized by its fragrant, white, star-shaped flowers, opposite simple leaves with serrated margins, and a branched, woody stem that typically grows to a height of 1-3 meters.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly India, and is also found in parts of Southeast Asia, including Bangladesh, Nepal, and Sri Lanka.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The flowers, leaves, and seeds of Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) are used medicinally, with the flowers being the most commonly utilized part for their aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) contains a complex array of phytochemical compounds, including essential oils rich in linalool, benzyl acetate, and methyl benzoate, as well as flavonoids, alkaloids, and glycosides that contribute to its therapeutic properties in herbal medicine.

Pharmacological Properties

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) exhibits pharmacological properties including antispasmodic, sedative, and antidepressant effects, primarily due to its volatile oils and alkaloid compounds such as nardosinene and jasminine.

Mechanisms of Action

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) exerts its effects through the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, alkaloids, and essential oils, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties by interacting with cellular pathways and modulating enzyme activities.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Common Jasmine is used to treat insomnia, anxiety, digestive issues, skin infections, respiratory ailments, and menstrual disorders.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is typically prepared as an essential oil, tincture, or dried flower for use in herbal medicine, with each form offering distinct therapeutic properties and applications.

Dosage and Administration

The recommended dosage of Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) for herbal use typically ranges from 200 to 500 mg per day, depending on the form (such as dried flower, essential oil, or tincture), and should be taken under the guidance of a qualified healthcare practitioner.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in excess or improperly, Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, allergic reactions, and skin irritation, particularly in individuals with sensitivities to related plants.

Drug Interactions

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) may interact with certain medications, particularly those affecting the cardiovascular system or blood pressure, due to its potential effects on heart rate and blood pressure regulation.

Toxicity and Precautions

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses for medicinal purposes, but it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort or allergic reactions in some individuals, and excessive use may lead to toxicity due to its high concentration of volatile oils.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is typically cultivated in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil and partial to full sun exposure, requiring regular watering and pruning to maintain its fragrant blooms.

Processing and Extraction

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is typically processed by distilling its fragrant flowers to produce essential oil, which is then used in herbal medicine for its calming, antispasmodic, and antimicrobial properties.

Storage and Shelf Life

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) should be stored in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture to maintain its potency and extend its shelf life, which is typically around 1-2 years when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Jasminum sambac has primarily focused on its phytochemical composition, antimicrobial properties, and potential therapeutic applications in traditional medicine, with studies indicating its use in treating respiratory conditions, skin disorders, and as a mild sedative.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its calming properties, particularly in treating anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues, with historical references dating back to ancient Indian and Arabic medicinal practices.

Regulatory Status

The Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is not regulated as a pharmaceutical drug by the FDA but is often used in herbal medicine and aromatherapy, with its safety and efficacy largely determined by traditional use and anecdotal evidence.
