Scientific and Botanical Profile

Common Jasmine, with botanical name Jasminum sambac, is a flowering plant belonging to the Oleaceae family, known for its fragrant white flowers and aromatic properties.

Native to Southeast Asia, including regions such as India, China, and the Philippines, this species has also been naturalized in parts of the Middle East and South America. Morphologically, it is a deciduous shrub or small tree, typically growing up to 3 meters tall, with opposite, simple, and usually lance-shaped leaves, and clusters of small, white, star-shaped flowers that emit a strong, sweet fragrance. Common Jasmine is also referred to by several common names, including Jasmine Flower, Sambac Jasmine, Arabian Jasmine, White Jasmine, and Arborvitae Jasmine, reflecting its widespread use in perfumery and traditional medicine.

Its botanical profile highlights its importance in both horticulture and the fragrance industry, making it a popular ornamental and aromatic plant worldwide.

History and Cultural Relevance

Common Jasmine was used extensively in ancient cultures for its aromatic properties and therapeutic benefits, with historical records tracing its use back to Persian, Indian, and Chinese traditions.

In Ayurveda and Traditional Chinese Medicine, jasmine has been valued for its ability to balance emotions, reduce stress, and treat ailments like insomnia and digestive disorders. It plays a significant role in various cultural rituals, including weddings, religious ceremonies, and festivals, where its fragrance is believed to purify the air and enhance spiritual connection. Jasmine essential oil is still used today in aromatherapy to alleviate anxiety and promote relaxation, while its flowers are incorporated into skincare products for their skin-soothing properties.

The plant's enduring presence in both historical and modern practices highlights its deep-rooted cultural significance and continued relevance in holistic health and spiritual practices.

Chemical Composition and Nutritional Profile

Common Jasmine contains a complex array of chemical compounds, including essential oils, flavonoids, alkaloids, and terpenes, which contribute to its aromatic and medicinal properties.

The essential oil extracted from its flowers is rich in compounds like linalool, benzyl acetate, and methyl benzoate, which are known for their calming and antiseptic effects. Nutritional-wise, Common Jasmine is a good source of antioxidants, including vitamin C and various polyphenols, as well as minerals such as calcium, magnesium, and potassium. These compounds work synergistically in the body by modulating inflammatory responses, enhancing immune function, and promoting skin health through their antioxidant and antimicrobial activities.

Overall, the plant's chemical composition and nutritional profile make it valuable in both traditional and modern therapeutic applications.

Medicinal Properties and Health Benefits

Jasminum sambac has been widely recognized for its medicinal properties and diverse health benefits, particularly due to its rich composition of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, alkaloids, and essential oils.

It is known to support the respiratory system by acting as an expectorant and bronchodilator, aiding in the treatment of coughs and asthma. Additionally, it promotes skin health by reducing inflammation and acting as an antiseptic, making it beneficial for treating acne and skin infections. Compared to similar herbs like Cinnamomum zeylanicum or Eucalyptus globulus, jasminum sambac offers a more balanced profile of aromatic and anti-inflammatory properties, with a slightly higher potency in its essential oil content.

Its unique combination of calming effects on the nervous system and its ability to enhance digestion makes it a versatile herb with broader therapeutic applications.

Forms, Preparation and Usage

Jasminum sambac has been traditionally used for its aromatic and therapeutic properties, with various forms available including fresh flowers, dried tincture, powder, essential oil, and capsules.

To prepare jasminum sambac, it can be made into a tea by steeping dried flowers in hot water, or a decoction by simmering them for a longer period. It can also be used in an infusion for topical applications such as compresses or salves. The recommended dosage for adults is typically 1-2 grams of dried flowers per cup of tea, while children should only use it under medical supervision if deemed safe.

For optimal results, it is advised to use jasminum sambac no more than 3 times daily for a short duration, staying conservative to avoid potential side effects.

Safety, Side Effects and Contraindications

Jasminum sambac can be used cautiously as a medicinal plant, primarily for its calming and aromatic properties, but it is important to be aware of its potential safety concerns.

While generally considered safe in moderate amounts, it may cause side effects such as gastrointestinal upset, skin irritation, or allergic reactions in some individuals. There is limited research on its interactions with other drugs or herbs, but it is advisable to consult a healthcare provider before combining it with medications, especially those affecting the nervous system or blood pressure. Special populations, including pregnant and breastfeeding women, should avoid using jasminum sambac due to insufficient evidence on its safety during these periods, and individuals with chronic illnesses should also exercise caution.

To ensure safe use, it is recommended to use jasminum sambac in recommended dosages, consult a healthcare professional, and discontinue use if any adverse effects occur.

Growing, Harvesting and Storage

Jasminum sambac grows best in well-drained, loamy soil with a slightly acidic to neutral pH, in full sun to partial shade, and requires regular watering to maintain moist but not waterlogged conditions.

It thrives in temperate climates and benefits from mulching to retain soil moisture and suppress weeds. Regular pruning and fertilization during the growing season promote dense foliage and abundant flowering, while pest control measures such as neem oil can prevent infestations. The flowers should be harvested in the early morning when they are fully open but still fresh, using gentle plucking to avoid damaging the plant.

To preserve its potency, the harvested flowers should be dried in a cool, shaded area or using a dehydrator, then stored in airtight glass containers in a cool, dark place or refrigerated to maintain their aromatic and medicinal properties.
