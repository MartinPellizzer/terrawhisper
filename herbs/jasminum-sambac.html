Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac): Benefits, Uses, Dosage, and Safety in Herbal Medicine

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a flowering plant widely used in herbal medicine for its aromatic properties and therapeutic benefits, including its role in treating respiratory conditions, promoting relaxation, and enhancing skin health.

What is Common Jasmine (Jasminum Sambac)?

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a flowering plant native to southern Asia, widely cultivated for its fragrant white flowers and used in traditional herbal medicine for its calming, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties.

Botanical Classification and Taxonomy

The scientific name of Common Jasmine is Jasminum sambac. This plant belongs to the kingdom Plantae, division Magnoliophyta, class Eudicots, order Oleales, family Oleaceae, genus Jasminum, and species Jasminum sambac. It is commonly known as Jasmine in India, and as Common Jasmine or Arabian Jasmine in the Middle East. The synonyms for this plant include Jasminum officinale and Jasminum grandiflorum, with regional names such as Ylang-Ylang in Southeast Asia.

Plant Description and Morphology

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is a flowering shrub characterized by its fragrant white flowers, opposite simple leaves, and a creeping or erect growth habit, typically reaching heights of 1 to 3 meters.

Native Habitat and Distribution

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is native to the Indian subcontinent, particularly India, and is also found in parts of Southeast Asia, including Bangladesh, Nepal, and Sri Lanka.

Plant Parts Used Medicinally

The flowers, leaves, and seeds of Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) are used medicinally, with the flowers being the most commonly utilized part for their aromatic and therapeutic properties.

Phytochemical Composition

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) contains a variety of bioactive compounds including essential oils, flavonoids, alkaloids, and terpenoids, with its primary phytochemical constituents being the volatile compounds such as linalool, linalyl acetate, and methyl benzoate, which contribute to its aromatic properties and pharmacological activities.

Pharmacological Properties

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) possesses pharmacological properties that include antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and sedative effects, primarily due to the presence of compounds such as linalool, benzyl acetate, and flavonoids.

Mechanisms of Action

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) exerts its therapeutic effects through the presence of bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, alkaloids, and essential oils, which exhibit anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and sedative properties by interacting with various physiological pathways in the body.

Therapeutic Uses and Indications

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is traditionally used in herbal medicine to treat respiratory conditions such as bronchitis and asthma, as well as to alleviate symptoms of anxiety and insomnia due to its calming and sedative properties.

Preparation Methods and Forms

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is typically prepared as an essential oil, herbal tea, tincture, or dried flower for use in herbal medicine.

Dosage and Administration

The typical dosage of Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) for herbal use ranges from 200 to 600 mg per day, usually divided into two or three doses, and is typically administered in the form of a standardized extract or essential oil, with specific formulations and dosing guidelines recommended to be followed under the guidance of a qualified healthcare practitioner.

Safety, Side Effects, and Contraindications

When used in moderate amounts as part of herbal remedies, Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is generally considered safe, though it may cause mild gastrointestinal discomfort or allergic reactions in some individuals, and should be avoided by those with known sensitivities to the plant family.

Drug Interactions

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) may interact with medications that affect the liver or blood clotting due to its potential phytochemical properties, which could enhance or inhibit the metabolism of certain drugs.

Toxicity and Precautions

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is generally considered safe when used in recommended doses, but it may cause mild skin irritation or allergic reactions in some individuals, and excessive internal use can lead to gastrointestinal discomfort.

Cultivation and Harvesting

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) is typically cultivated in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil, requiring regular watering and pruning to promote flowering, with harvesting conducted primarily during the night when the flowers are in full bloom to preserve their fragrance and potency.

Processing and Extraction

The processing and extraction of Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) involve harvesting the fragrant flowers at peak bloom, typically during the night when their scent is most intense, followed by drying and distillation to obtain essential oils or aqueous extracts used in herbal medicine.

Storage and Shelf Life

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) should be stored in an airtight container in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain its potency and prevent moisture absorption, with a shelf life of up to 12 months when properly preserved.

Scientific Research and Evidence

Scientific research on Jasminum sambac has primarily focused on its aromatic compounds, such as jasmine ketones and linalool, which have been studied for their potential effects on mood, stress reduction, and as a natural preservative in pharmaceutical and cosmetic formulations.

Traditional and Historical Uses

Common Jasmine (Jasminum sambac) has been traditionally used in herbal medicine for its calming properties, particularly in treating anxiety, insomnia, and digestive issues, with historical applications dating back to ancient Indian and Middle Eastern practices.
