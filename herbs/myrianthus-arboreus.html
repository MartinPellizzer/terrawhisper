Myrianthus arboreus

What's the taxonomical classification of Myrianthus arboreus?

Myrianthus arboreus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this lineage, it is situated in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. Its taxonomic progression continues through the order Rosales and the family Urticaceae. Finally, the plant is identified by its specific genus, Myrianthus, and its unique species designation, arboreus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Myrianthus arboreus has a woody structure characterized by a small to medium-sized tree habit with spreading branches and simple, alternate leaves. The foliage typically consists of elliptical or oblong leaf blades that possess entire margins and distinct pinnate venation. Small, actinomorphic flowers are produced in clusters, often appearing in axillary inflorescences. These flowers feature numerous stamens that contribute to a somewhat brush-like appearance. The plant eventually develops small, fleshy fruits that serve as a primary dispersal mechanism.

    What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

    This plant contains a diverse array of medicinal compounds including alkaloids, flavonoids, glycosides, saponins, and tannins. These alkaloids often act on the central nervous system to provide potent therapeutic effects. The presence of flavonoids contributes significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties to the plant's chemical profile. Additionally, glycosides and saponins play critical roles in various physiological processes and cellular interactions. Finally, the tannins provide astringent qualities that can assist in wound healing and digestive health.

    As discussed in a study, Myrianthus arboreus was screened for its phytochemical content and antioxidant potential through four different types of extracts, which were methanolic, hydro-ethanolic, aqueous, and hydrolysed. Qualitative phytochemical tests were utilized to detect the presence of alkaloids, tannins, saponins, flavonoids, glycosides, and phenols within these extracts. Unlike Cussonia barteri, all extracts of Myrianthus arboreus contained the phytochemicals tested. The study utilized three quantitative methods, which were Folin, Ferric Reducing Antioxidant Power (FRAP), and diphenyl-1, 2-picryl hydrazyl (DPPH), to determine antioxidant potential. The highest antioxidant activities were observed in the hydrolysed extracts, while the aqueous extract showed the least activity.

      What health problems is this plant used for?

      This plant is used for treating acute and chronic wounds, bacterial infections, diarrhea, dysmenorrhea, and post-operative wounds. The medicinal properties of the species help promote healing in both recent injuries and long-standing skin lesions. It also functions as an effective agent to combat various bacterial infections. When applied or consumed appropriately, it can assist in managing digestive issues such as diarrhea. Additionally, the plant is utilized to alleviate the painful cramping associated with dysmenorrhea. Its restorative qualities are also particularly beneficial for the care of post-operative wounds.

      As discussed in a study, Myrianthus arboreus was evaluated through aqueous and ethanolic extraction for its antimicrobial activities against multidrug-resistant bacterial pathogens isolated from post-operative wounds. Preliminary phytochemical analysis of the leaves of the plant showed the presence of saponins, phenolics, and reducing sugars. The study utilized the agar well diffusion method to test the leaves against reference strains such as Escherichia coli ATCC 25922, Salmonella typhi ATCC 19430, Pseudomonas aeruginosa ATCC 27853, and Staphylococcus aureus ATCC 25923, along with a Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus strain and MDR clinical isolates. For Myrianthus arboreus, the highest activity was observed against S. aureus. These findings suggest that the plant exhibits antimicrobial activities against MDR bacterial pathogens and may be helpful in the management of acute and chronic wounds.

        What side effects this plant can have?

        This plant can cause severe skin irritation, contact dermatitis, and potential respiratory distress if its pollen or sap is inhaled or touched. Exposure to the sticky resin often results in intense redness, itching, and localized swelling on the affected area. Some individuals may experience an allergic reaction that manifests as hives or even anaphylaxis in extreme cases. If the plant's juices come into contact with the eyes, they can induce significant stinging and temporary blurred vision. It is crucial to wear protective clothing and gloves when handling any part of this species to avoid these complications.
