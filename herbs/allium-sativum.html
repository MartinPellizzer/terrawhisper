Garlic

Allium sativum

Garlic (Allium sativum) is a member of the onion family, widely cultivated for its pungent bulb and numerous health benefits, including its use in improving cardiovascular health and supporting the immune system.

Botanical Identification

Garlic (Allium sativum) is a bulbous perennial plant in the Amaryllidaceae family. Native to Central Asia, it is widely cultivated globally. Its botanical name is often used interchangeably with common names like "cloves" or "garlic bulbs" in herbal contexts. The plant features fleshy, white, bulbous roots and cylindrical, grass-like leaves.

The following list summarize the taxonomy of plant:

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Liliopsida
  • Order: Asparagales
  • Family: Amaryllidaceae
  • Genus: Allium
  • Species: Allium sativum

Active Compounds

Garlic contains essential oils, terpenoids, and flavonoids as its main bioactive compounds. These compounds work together to enhance its therapeutic effects, with essential oils contributing to antimicrobial properties and flavonoids offering anti-inflammatory benefits. The synergistic interaction between these compounds supports its use in herbal medicine.

  • Terpenoid
  • Flavonoid

Garlic Uses.

Preparation Methods

Culinary use: Used for digestive issues and as a flavoring agent in food.

Infusion: Used for respiratory conditions such as coughs and colds.

Decoction: Used for gastrointestinal issues and as an antiseptic.

Safety Profile

Garlic is generally safe when consumed in typical food amounts. However, it may cause gastrointestinal discomfort or interact with blood-thinning medications. Consult a healthcare provider before using high-dose supplements, especially for those with bleeding disorders or on medication.
