Cotoneaster harrovianus

What's the taxonomical classification of Cotoneaster harrovianus?

Cotoneaster harrovianus belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is classified within the phylum Streptophyta. As a member of the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae, it falls under the order Rosales. The plant is a representative of the family Rosaceae, specifically situated within the genus Cotoneaster, with its unique identification defined by the species harrovianus.

What are the morphological characteristics of this plant?

Cotoneaster harrovianus has prostrate, spreading woody stems and small, oval-shaped leaves that are dark green and glossy on the upper surface.

The foliage typically measures between 10 and 20 millimeters in length and features finely serrated margins. During the flowering season, tiny white blossoms emerge from the leaf axils to attract pollinators. These flowers eventually develop into small, round red berries that persist through the winter months.

The plant's compact, mat-forming growth habit makes it well-suited for groundcover applications.

        What side-effects this plant can have?

        Cotoneaster harrovianus can cause gastrointestinal distress, vomiting, and potential neurological symptoms if the berries are ingested by humans or animals. The plant contains cyanogenic glycosides, which are compounds that can release toxic hydrogen cyanide during digestion. While the foliage is generally considered less dangerous than the fruit, consuming any part of the shrub can lead to nausea or stomach pain. In pets, ingestion often results in lethargy, weakness, or a lack of coordination. It is essential to keep this plant away from small children and curious animals to prevent accidental poisoning.
