Elaeagnus angustifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeagnus angustifolia?

Elaeagnus angustifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Rosales and is a member of the family Elaeagnaceae. Ultimately, its taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Elaeagnus, specifically as the species angustifolia.

What parts of this plant are used medicinally?

Elaeagnus angustifolia contains medicinal compounds primarily located in its leaves, bark, and fruit. The leaves are often utilized in traditional remedies to treat inflammatory conditions and skin ailments due to their bioactive constituents. Extracts from the bark are sometimes applied topically or ingested to address various digestive or systemic issues. The fruit also provides nutritional and therapeutic value, often being used for its antioxidant properties. These various parts are processed into decoctions or ointments to harness their specific healing benefits.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

This plant contains flavonoids, terpenoids, phenolic acids, and alkaloids that contribute to its diverse therapeutic potential.

The presence of flavonoids provides significant antioxidant activity, helping to neutralize harmful free radicals in the body. Phenolic acids within the plant also offer anti-inflammatory properties that can aid in cellular protection. Additionally, the terpenoids present in the tissues are often associated with antimicrobial and insecticidal effects.

Finally, the alkaloids contribute to the complex pharmacological profile, potentially influencing various physiological processes.

What are the molecular targets of this plant?

Elaeagnus angustifolia targets enzymes such as cyclooxygenase, lipoxygenase, and various inflammatory cytokines to exert its therapeutic effects. The plant contains bioactive phenolic compounds and flavonoids that inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory mediators like nitric oxide and prostaglandin E2. These phytochemicals interact with signaling pathways, including the NF-kappaB pathway, to regulate cellular immune responses. Additionally, specific polyphenols found in the leaves and fruit may interact with antioxidant enzymes to mitigate oxidative stress within cells. By modulating these protein interactions, the plant demonstrates potential for managing inflammation and oxidative damage.

What are the therapeutic actions of Elaeagnus angustifolia?

Elaeagnus angustifolia has medicinal properties characterized by its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities. The plant contains various bioactive compounds, such as polyphenols and flavonoids, which help neutralize free radicals in the body. These chemical constituents contribute to its potential role in reducing oxidative stress and supporting cellular health. Some studies also suggest that extracts from the plant may possess inhibitory effects against certain bacterial strains. Consequently, it is often researched for its ability to mitigate inflammation and protect against microbial infections.

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including dysentery, diarrhea, and skin infections. The bark and leaves contain medicinal properties that can help soothe gastrointestinal distress. Some traditional practices also utilize the plant to address inflammation and minor wounds. It is often valued in folk medicine for its ability to manage digestive issues through its astringent qualities. These applications vary significantly depending on the specific part of the plant being processed.

What are the herbal preparations of Elaeagnus angustifolia?

Elaeagnus angustifolia is prepared as decoctions of the bark, infusions of the leaves, or topical oils derived from the seeds. Practitioners often boil the woody bark in water to extract tannins for medicinal use. The leaves are frequently steeped in hot water to create a soothing tea. Seed oils can be extracted through pressing for use in skin applications. These various forms allow for both internal consumption and external treatment depending on the desired effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Elaeagnus angustifolia can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis and respiratory irritation in sensitive individuals due to its pollen and leaf oils. While the plant is not typically classified as acutely toxic, prolonged exposure to its foliage can trigger itchy rashes or swelling on the skin. Some people may also experience sneezing or congestion if they inhale high concentrations of its airborne pollen. Because it is an invasive species, contact with dense thickets can also lead to physical irritation from scratchy branches. It is best to use protective clothing when working near these shrubs to minimize these potential sensitivities.
