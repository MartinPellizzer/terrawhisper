Elaeagnus angustifolia

What's the taxonomical classification of Elaeagnus angustifolia?

Elaeagnus angustifolia belongs to the kingdom Plantae and is categorized under the phylum Streptophyta. Within this group, it is classified in the class Equisetopsida and the subclass Magnoliidae. The plant follows the order Rosales and is a member of the family Elaeagnaceae. Ultimately, its taxonomic identity is defined by its placement within the genus Elaeagnus, specifically as the species angustifolia.

What medicinal compounds this plant contains?

Elaeagnus angustifolia contains high concentrations of flavonoids, polyphenols, and various terpenoids that contribute to its therapeutic potential. These bioactive compounds exhibit significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties when extracted from the leaves or fruit. Specific phenolic acids found within the plant help neutralize free radicals and may protect cells from oxidative stress. Some studies also suggest that the presence of triterpenoids provides antimicrobial benefits against certain pathogens. These chemical constituents make the plant a subject of interest for developing natural medicinal supplements.

What are the therapeutic actions of Elaeagnus angustifolia?

What diseases this plant treats?

This plant is used to treat various ailments including dysentery, diarrhea, and skin infections. The bark and leaves contain medicinal properties that can help soothe gastrointestinal distress. Some traditional practices also utilize the plant to address inflammation and minor wounds. It is often valued in folk medicine for its ability to manage digestive issues through its astringent qualities. These applications vary significantly depending on the specific part of the plant being processed.

What are the herbal preparations of Elaeagnus angustifolia?

Elaeagnus angustifolia is prepared as decoctions of the bark, infusions of the leaves, or topical oils derived from the seeds. Practitioners often boil the woody bark in water to extract tannins for medicinal use. The leaves are frequently steeped in hot water to create a soothing tea. Seed oils can be extracted through pressing for use in skin applications. These various forms allow for both internal consumption and external treatment depending on the desired effect.

What side-effects this plant can have?

Elaeagnus angustifolia can cause allergic skin reactions such as contact dermatitis and respiratory irritation in sensitive individuals due to its pollen and leaf oils. While the plant is not typically classified as acutely toxic, prolonged exposure to its foliage can trigger itchy rashes or swelling on the skin. Some people may also experience sneezing or congestion if they inhale high concentrations of its airborne pollen. Because it is an invasive species, contact with dense thickets can also lead to physical irritation from scratchy branches. It is best to use protective clothing when working near these shrubs to minimize these potential sensitivities.
